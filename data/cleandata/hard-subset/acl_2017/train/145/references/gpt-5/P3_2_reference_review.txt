Summary and contributions
This paper proposes probabilistic, multi-prototype word embeddings in which each word is represented as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm). Training uses a skip-gram–style, energy-based max-margin objective contrasting positive and negative word-context pairs. The energy between two words is the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel) between their distributions, which admits a closed form for Gaussian mixtures. The authors argue this enables multimodality for polysemy, better uncertainty modeling than unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014), and improved interpretability and entailment behavior. Empirically, they report gains on several word similarity benchmarks and on lexical entailment, and provide qualitative evidence of sense separation.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A scalable, closed-form training objective for mixture-of-Gaussians word embeddings using the expected likelihood kernel within a max-margin ranking framework.
2) Empirical demonstration that multi-modal (K=2–3) probabilistic embeddings can qualitatively separate senses and improve on several similarity and entailment benchmarks compared to unimodal Gaussian embeddings and (to a lesser extent) skip-gram.
3) Practical stability and training details (diagonal/spherical covariance, numerically stable log-sum-exp, sampling and optimization choices) enabling training on very large corpora.

Strengths
- Sound probabilistic formulation with tractable training. Using the expected likelihood kernel to compare Gaussian mixtures is principled and computationally convenient. The derivation is clear, and the implementation notes (diagonal/spherical covariances, log-parameterization, epsilon-stabilization, log-sum-exp trick) show good engineering care.

- Addresses a clear limitation of unimodal distributions. The paper convincingly argues why a single Gaussian is insufficient for polysemous words; the qualitative nearest neighbor analyses (e.g., rock, bank, cell) show crisp sense separation, and the reported reduction in inflated variances for polysemous words is sensible.

- Broad evaluation with multiple similarity metrics. Reporting results for expected likelihood, max cosine over components, and min Euclidean distance helps clarify how the representation is used downstream and shows that different tasks prefer different similarity measures.

- Entailment improvements and discussion of asymmetry. Although training uses a symmetric energy, the paper evaluates entailment with an asymmetric metric (KL), and reports improvements over unimodal Gaussians. The discussion around distributional inclusion and variance effects is insightful.

- Scalability and reproducibility. Training on billions of tokens with modest hardware and providing sufficient hyperparameter and optimization details (plus a promise of code) is a practical contribution that can enable reuse.

Weaknesses
- Limited and uneven baselines. While comparisons to skip-gram and Vilnis & McCallum are appropriate, several strong multi-prototype point baselines are missing or under-evaluated: Multi-Sense Skip-Gram (Neelakantan et al., 2014), Tian et al. (2014) on more datasets than SCWS, Reisinger & Mooney (2010), and more recent strong static baselines (e.g., GloVe, fastText) with comparable training data and dimensionality. The WordSim comparison to Huang (2012) is useful but dated and narrow. Without broader baselines, the incremental benefit of probabilistic mixtures over deterministic multi-sense methods remains unclear.

- Ambiguity around KL for mixtures. The paper states KL between Gaussian mixtures lacks a closed form (correct), yet for entailment uses d(f, g) = min_{i,j} KL(f || g) without specifying that this is computed between components (i.e., Gaussians) rather than mixtures. This is a crucial modeling/evaluation detail: taking the minimum pairwise component KL ignores mixture weights and risks optimistic entailment scores (picking the best-aligned pair). The method and its justification need to be made explicit, and compared against other mixture-to-mixture divergence approximations.

- Sense selection at test time may overstate utility. Many reported improvements rely on choosing the maximum similarity over component pairs, effectively an oracle sense selector without context. While this is common in multi-sense evaluations, the paper should also report context-aware scores (e.g., SCWS with AveSim weighted by mixture probabilities or with context-driven responsibilities) to demonstrate the model’s practical utility in realistic settings where sense disambiguation is required.

- Asymmetric training signal is absent. Entailment gains are shown only at evaluation time using an asymmetric metric; training uses a symmetric energy. It would strengthen the case to either (a) train with an asymmetric energy (e.g., an approximation to mixture KL or a distributional inclusion surrogate), or (b) show that the symmetric training objective is sufficient by ablations and analysis.

- Analysis of mixture behavior is thin. The paper asserts that one ξi,j term dominates as training proceeds, but provides no quantitative analysis of mixture component usage, weights, or collapse/duplication across words. Without regularization encouraging diversity or identifiability, mixtures may learn redundant components. Diagnostics such as entropy of mixture weights, component overlap, or coverage across vocabulary would help.

- Fairness and significance of improvements. Some reported gains are modest, dataset-dependent, and occasionally behind skip-gram (e.g., SimLex). It is unclear if all methods used matched dimensionality, window sizes, negative samples, and training corpora. No significance testing or confidence intervals are reported.

- Minor issues: The visualization link appears to reveal an IP address, which is potentially de-anonymizing. Several references are incomplete or contain placeholders. Notation is occasionally inconsistent.

Questions to authors
1) How is KL computed for entailment with mixtures? Is it the minimum KL over Gaussian component pairs, KL(Ni || Nj)? If so, why ignore mixture weights, and how sensitive are results to using weighted averages or other approximations (e.g., variational bounds)?

2) Can you report SCWS results with context-sensitive mixture responsibilities (e.g., using the context to weight components), in addition to MaxSim/AveSim? How does w2gm compare to MSSG (Neelakantan et al., 2014) on SCWS?

3) Did you observe component collapse or redundancy? Please provide statistics on mixture weight entropy, number of “active” components per word, and overlap between components. Any regularization or diversity constraints used?

4) How do results change with more negatives per positive pair, higher dimensionality (e.g., 200–300), and diagonal vs spherical covariances? Ablations would clarify the contribution of covariance modeling vs multiple means.

5) For fairness, were skip-gram and Vilnis & McCallum trained on the same corpora with matched hyperparameters? Can you include stronger contemporary baselines (GloVe, fastText, MSSG) trained under comparable settings?

6) Did you evaluate the model on contextual tasks beyond SCWS (e.g., word-in-context similarity or downstream tasks where uncertainty might help)? If not, can you comment on expected benefits?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and technically sound extension of probabilistic embeddings to mixtures with a tractable energy, with promising qualitative and some quantitative gains. Strengthening baselines, clarifying the KL entailment computation, and adding analyses of mixture behavior and context-aware evaluation would substantially improve the paper.
