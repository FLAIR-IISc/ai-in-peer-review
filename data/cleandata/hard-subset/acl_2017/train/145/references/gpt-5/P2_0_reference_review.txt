Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes probabilistic multi-prototype word embeddings where each word is represented as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm). Training follows a skip-gram-style, energy-based max-margin objective contrasting positive and negative context pairs. The similarity (energy) between two word distributions is the expected likelihood kernel (an inner product in L2), which admits a closed-form for Gaussian mixtures; the paper also provides stability/initialization tricks. Experiments on large corpora show qualitative capture of polysemy and improvements on several word similarity datasets and a lexical entailment task.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A scalable probabilistic multi-sense embedding: Moving from unimodal Gaussians (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) to Gaussian mixtures with an analytically tractable energy (expected likelihood kernel) and a max-margin objective. This yields an expressive distribution over senses with uncertainty.
2) Practical training design for mixtures: Spherical/diagonal covariance parameterization, numerically stable computation of the mixture kernel, and details enabling training on billions of tokens.
3) Empirical evidence: Qualitative interpretability for polysemous words, and quantitative gains over unimodal Gaussians and (reported) skip-gram on several similarity sets and improved entailment when using an asymmetric metric.

Strengths
- Sound, analytically tractable formulation: Using the expected likelihood kernel for GMMs yields a closed-form energy with clear gradients. The partial energy term decomposes neatly across component pairs and balances mean proximity and covariance terms. The training objective mirrors SGNS-style negative sampling but is adapted to distributions, which is a sensible and principled extension.
- Addresses a real limitation (polysemy) while preserving uncertainty: The mixture’s multiple modes resolve the “overly diffuse” covariance problem of unimodal Gaussians for polysemous words. The qualitative neighbors for rock, bank, cell, etc., convincingly show distinct senses, and the variance analysis supports reduced over-dispersion relative to w2g.
- Empirical improvements on several benchmarks: On WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, and RW, w2gm often outperforms unimodal Gaussians and reported skip-gram baselines, with sensible metric choices (max-cosine typically best; Euclidean sometimes better). The entailment results further show that exploiting asymmetry (via KL) helps, and w2gm > w2g consistently.
- Reproducibility and engineering detail: The paper discloses key implementation choices (negative sampling distribution, subsampling, diagonal/spherical covariances, constraints, stability constants, optimizer and learning schedule, input/output embeddings), ably supporting reproduction. The closed-form derivation for the kernel is provided.
- Interpretability: By retaining covariances and mixture weights, the model enables reasoning about uncertainty, specificity, and entailment (coverage), with intuitive geometric interpretations and visual examples.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and strength of baselines: Several comparisons, especially to skip-gram, rely on numbers copied from prior work (trained with potentially different corpora, preprocessing, dimensions). The paper trains w2gm on UKWAC+Wackypedia with D=50; it does not report same-data, same-dimension SGNS or GloVe baselines, nor stronger 300D baselines standard in the literature. This undermines the strength of the quantitative claims. Please provide apples-to-apples baselines.
- Entailment metric is heuristic for mixtures: The paper states KL between mixtures is not closed-form, yet uses min over component pairs of KL as the entailment score. This discards mixture weights and ignores cross-component coverage, making it a heuristic rather than a well-founded divergence between mixtures. The paper should justify this choice, analyze sensitivity, and compare to alternative asymmetric scores (e.g., expected pairwise KL weighted by mixture weights, or bounds).
- Limited ablations and analysis: Beyond a qualitative note for K=3, there is little study of:
  - Number of components K and mixture weight behavior (collapse vs useful splits).
  - Covariance structure (spherical vs diagonal) and its effect on performance/interpretability.
  - Energy choice (expected likelihood kernel vs other probability product kernels or alternatives).
  - Training hyperparameters (negative samples, margin) and their impact.  
  The improvements could be due to multiple factors; ablations would clarify what matters.
- Scalability evidence is thin: Claims of training “in days” on billions of tokens are not backed by wall-clock times, throughput, memory footprint, or complexity breakdown (the O(K^2 D) per pair mixture cost). A speed/scale comparison to SGNS/w2g would strengthen the practicality claim.
- Clarity and presentation issues: There are typos and inconsistencies (w2gm vs w2mg; some tables appear truncated; reference formatting is messy). The interactive demo URL risks anonymity. Some evaluation sections (e.g., SCWS) are described but not fully presented in the included text.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you provide same-corpus, same-dimension SGNS/GloVe baselines and multi-sense baselines (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014; Tian et al., 2014) to ensure fair comparison? How do results change at 200–300 dimensions?
- Mixture KL for entailment: Why choose min over componentwise KL rather than a mixture-aware asymmetric score (e.g., weighted min, expected min, or an upper/lower bound to mixture KL)? How sensitive are entailment results to this choice?
- Sense usage and collapse: Do mixture weights frequently collapse to a single dominant component for monosemous words? Can you report the distribution of effective K across the vocabulary and examples where K>1 is clearly beneficial?
- Computational cost: What are training times per epoch and memory usage vs w2g and SGNS? How does cost scale with K?
- Contextualization: At test time you use max over component pairs; do you consider contextualized similarity (select sense given a specific context), and if so, how does the model perform on SCWS with context-based disambiguation?

Overall evaluation
The paper addresses an important limitation of unimodal probabilistic embeddings with a principled, tractable mixture model and shows promising qualitative and quantitative gains. However, the lack of strictly comparable baselines, limited ablations, and heuristic entailment metric for mixtures weaken the empirical case. With stronger experimental grounding and analysis, this would be a solid contribution. As is, I find the idea technically sound and interesting, but the evaluation needs tightening to meet the ACL bar.
