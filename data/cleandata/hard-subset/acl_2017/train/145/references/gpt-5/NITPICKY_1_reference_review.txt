Summary and main contributions
- Proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm) rather than a single Gaussian or a point, to better capture polysemy and entailment. Parameters are learned with a max-margin ranking objective using an analytic energy based on the expected likelihood kernel (ELK), enabling tractable training and O(K^2D) similarity between two K-component embeddings.
- Presents practical training details (spherical/diagonal covariances, stabilized log-sum-exp, constrained parameterization) and claims scalability to billions of tokens.
- Empirical results suggest improved qualitative polysemy and competitive-to-better performance on several word similarity datasets and lexical entailment, versus word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.

Strengths
- Conceptually sound move from unimodal to multimodal probabilistic embeddings: mixtures are a natural way to model polysemy, and ELK gives a closed-form “inner product” suitable for mixtures, unlike KL.
- The energy/objective are well matched: ELK + margin loss yields a simple, numerically stable log-sum-exp structure; the paper provides stabilization tricks (epsilon in inverses, log-sum-exp shifting, log-parameterization).
- Qualitative examples are convincing: component-level neighbors for rock, bank, Apple, cell show clear sense separation that unimodal Gaussians fail to capture.
- Entailment evaluation uses an asymmetric divergence (componentwise KL) and shows consistent gains of mixtures over unimodal Gaussians; using a directional score is appropriate for entailment.
- The implementation section contains useful engineering details (subsampling, negative sampling, separate input/output embeddings, optimizer choice), which will help reproducibility if the code is released.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation fairness and completeness issues:
  - Many baseline numbers (sg*, w2g*) are taken from prior work with different corpora/hyperparameters. No strong apples-to-apples baselines trained under the same setup (same corpus, window size, dimension) are reported for word2vec or Vilnis & McCallum. This undermines claims of improvement.
  - The paper selects among multiple similarity metrics per dataset (ELK, max cosine, min Euclidean) and underlines the best per model/dataset. This amounts to post-hoc tuning on the evaluation sets and is not directly comparable to published baselines that use a fixed metric (usually cosine). A single, pre-specified metric should be used for all comparisons, with others relegated to ablations.
  - The SCWS section is incomplete: it references comparisons and metrics but provides no table or numbers. Table numbering is inconsistent (Table 5 is entailment; there is no SCWS table). This is a major omission.
  - No statistical significance tests, and improvements are often small. No ablations for K (beyond a brief note), covariance type, window size, dimension, number of negatives, or energy choice versus KL (for unimodal) are provided.
- Methodological clarity and potential pathologies:
  - The paper correctly notes KL between mixtures has no closed form, but later uses “minimum KL” for entailment. It should be made explicit that the score is min_{i,j} KL(N_i || N_j) and why this is appropriate and robust (vs soft-min or ELK-based asymmetric proxies).
  - The ELK energy can encourage variance collapse when means align (the -1/2 log det term grows as Σ→0). There is no explicit variance regularization other than numerical epsilons; no analysis of learned variance scales, collapse, or component redundancy is provided (a single anecdote for rock).
  - The paper claims diagonal covariances but then states “spherical case which we use in all experiments.” This should be made explicit up front; the implications (reduced expressiveness) should be discussed.
- Notation, math, and technical accuracy issues:
  - Initialization claim is incorrect: drawing each dimension from U[-sqrt(3/D), sqrt(3/D)] yields per-dimension variance 1/D, not 1. If the intent is E||μ||^2≈1, say so.
  - Several equations and symbols are inconsistent or malformed: missing “=1” in ∑ qi; inconsistent use of df,dg; an incorrect factor appears in the reduced ξi,j expression; typos in integrals; duplicate or garbled symbols.
  - Negative sampling description points to A.1 (derivation), but the details are in A.2; also says “across the whole document” rather than corpus. Window sizes differ between main experiments and some tables without clear justification.
- Novelty is incremental: This is a straightforward extension of Vilnis & McCallum: from single Gaussian to mixture and from KL to ELK for tractability. While reasonable, the paper lacks deeper theoretical or algorithmic innovations (e.g., principled handling of component identifiability, sense frequency calibration, or mixture-specific training signals).
- Reproducibility, anonymity, and presentation:
  - “Anonymous interactive visualization” links to a public IP/port, which risks deanonymization.
  - Claims of scalability (“billions of words in days on 12-core i7”) are not substantiated with runtime/throughput numbers; memory/time complexity with K^2 pairings is not discussed.
  - Numerous typos, inconsistent naming (w2gm vs w2mg), inconsistent capitalization, spelling (“Wackypedia”), and placeholder-like references with “?, year” and duplicated author names. The bibliography quality and several figure/table references do not meet ACL standards.

Questions to authors
- Please provide apples-to-apples baselines: train skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on the same corpus, window size, dimension, negatives, and report results with a single pre-specified metric (cosine) across all datasets. Include statistical tests.
- Provide the missing SCWS results (full table), clearly stating whether MaxSim or AveSim is used, and whether sense selection uses context (as SCWS provides).
- Clarify the entailment score: is it min_{i,j} KL(N_i || N_j)? Which direction is used for each pair, and how is asymmetry exploited in evaluation?
- Report ablations: K=1/2/3, spherical vs diagonal covariance, dimension D, window size, number of negatives, and energy choice. How do mixture weights correlate with sense frequency?
- How do you prevent variance collapse or component redundancy? Do you observe components collapsing or swapping? Any regularizers or priors tried?
- Runtime and memory: report tokens/sec, wall-clock time, and peak memory for K=2 and K=3. What is the per-batch complexity relative to skip-gram?

Additional comments
- Fix numerous notational and reference errors, ensure consistent model naming, and remove the non-anonymous demo link. Provide code for review if possible. Overall, the idea is promising, but the current draft’s evaluation and presentation issues make it hard to assess rigorously.
