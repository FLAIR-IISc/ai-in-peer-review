Summary and main contributions
- Proposal: Represent each word as a mixture of Gaussians (typically K=2), trained with a max-margin skip-gram-style ranking objective. The energy between word distributions is the expected likelihood kernel (inner product in L2 between densities), which has a closed form for Gaussian mixtures; log-energy is used in the loss.
- Practicalities: Spherical/diagonal covariances, simple reparameterizations for stability (optimize log-variance and logits for mixture weights), subsampling and negative sampling akin to word2vec, separate input/output distributions.
- Empirical claims: Better qualitative handling of polysemy; competitive to improved results on several word similarity datasets; improved lexical entailment when using KL at test time; reduced (over)variance compared to unimodal Gaussian embeddings.

Strengths
- Conceptually clear extension of Gaussian embeddings to mixtures: Using the expected likelihood kernel yields a fully analytic, scalable energy for mixtures, avoiding the intractability of KL for mixtures during training. This is an elegant, low-overhead way to introduce multi-prototype uncertainty.
- Qualitative sense separation looks convincing: Nearest-neighbor examples for bank, rock, Apple, cell, star show sensible sense clustering; the K=3 example for cell is a nice touch.
- Some quantitative gains: On multiple word similarity benchmarks (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW), the mixture model improves over unimodal Gaussian and sometimes over skip-gram. The use of cosine vs Euclidean vs ELK is discussed with task-specific observations.
- Training details are practical and reproducible: The paper provides initialization, optimization choices, numerical stability tricks, and subsampling/negative sampling settings; the choice of low-dimensional embeddings (D=50) and CPU-only training underscores efficiency.

Weaknesses
- Methodological gap around KL for mixtures: The paper states KL between Gaussian mixtures has no closed form (true), yet Table 5 uses KL as an entailment score and claims consistent improvements. The paper does not specify how KL is computed (Monte Carlo? bounds? moment matching?), over what pairing (componentwise min? mixture vs mixture?), or with what variance handling. This is a critical omission for a core result.
- Asymmetry not learned: The training energy (expected likelihood kernel) is symmetric, yet entailment fundamentally needs asymmetry. Using KL only at test time without training the model to be asymmetric undermines the claim that the learned distributions capture entailment; improvements may be incidental. No ablation on an asymmetric training objective is provided.
- Incomplete and inconsistent evaluation: SCWS is referenced as a key polysemy/homonymy benchmark and competitors (Huang, Tian, Chen) are cited, but the actual results table is missing. Table numbering skips (Table 4 absent). Entailment evaluation reports “best F1” without a dev set, which is optimistic; AP is fine, but there is no comparison to prior entailment baselines. Similarity comparisons omit strong modern baselines (e.g., GloVe, fastText) and standard 300D word2vec; dimensionality and corpora are not controlled across methods, limiting fairness.
- Covariance modeling and design choices are under-justified: The paper says diagonal covariances are used for efficiency, but then states spherical covariances are used in all experiments. This reduces expressiveness (no directional uncertainty) and undermines the argument about nuanced uncertainty and entailment. There is no ablation on K, covariance structure (spherical vs diagonal), number of negatives, margin, or effect of separate input/output distributions.
- Several technical and presentation inconsistencies/bugs: 
  - Negative sampling distribution reference misaligned (Section 3.3 points to Supplement A.1, which actually contains kernel derivation; word sampling is in A.2).
  - The Euclidean “similarity” is defined as min distance but used as a similarity in correlations without clarifying inversion or normalization.
  - Claim of “scalable to billions of words in days” is not substantiated with wall-clock time, memory usage, or throughput; only hardware is listed.
  - The visualization link includes a raw IP and a space in the URL; external websites in review can risk anonymity and are fragile.
  - Many typos and citation issues (e.g., “polysemys,” “compliments” vs “complements,” inconsistent author names, duplicate author entries, missing years, stray symbols, malformed references, e.g., “Collobert and JasonWeston.”, “Luong et al.” no year, random math fragment in references).
  - Notation fluctuates between diagonal and spherical derivations; use of brackets vs parentheses; minor equation formatting issues.

Questions to authors
- How exactly do you compute KL between mixtures for entailment? Please specify the estimator (closed-form per-component with mixture weighting? Monte Carlo?), the direction, and the min-over-components heuristic (if any), and provide computational cost and variance analysis.
- Given the symmetric training energy, why should the model learn distributional inclusion? Did you attempt training with an asymmetric energy (e.g., expected likelihood plus KL or an order-based objective)? Please provide an ablation or rationale.
- Please include the missing SCWS results with MaxSim/AveSim and comparisons to Huang (2012), Tian (2014), Chen (2014), including dataset/corpus/dimensionality controls. 
- Report training time, memory footprint, and convergence behavior; include ablations on K (beyond anecdotal K=3), covariance type (spherical vs diagonal), and number of negative samples. 
- Clarify whether all experiments use spherical or diagonal covariances, and why. If spherical, can you support the uncertainty/entailment claims with directional covariance experiments?
- For the “minimum Euclidean distance” metric, how is it converted to a similarity score for Spearman correlation? Please detail preprocessing and scaling.

Overall, while the idea is intuitive and some results are promising, the paper’s novelty is incremental and the accumulation of methodological omissions, missing results, and presentation issues is substantial. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
