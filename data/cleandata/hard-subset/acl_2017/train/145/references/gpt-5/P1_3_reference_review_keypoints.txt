- Proposes w2gm: represent each word as a mixture of Gaussians and train with a skip-gram-style max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel (closed-form for mixtures).
- Claims w2gm models polysemy, uncertainty, and entailment, with scalability and analytic tractability.
- Contribution: extends unimodal Gaussian embeddings to multi-modality with a closed-form, efficient energy function.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that multi-modality yields cleaner sense separation and improved performance on multiple word similarity datasets and a lexical entailment benchmark.
- Contribution: practical training recipe and stabilization tricks enabling scalable training (diagonal/spherical covariances, log-sum-exp stabilization, optimization choices).
- Strength: methodological elegance—expected likelihood kernel is closed-form, symmetric, and compatible with ranking objectives.
- Strength: clear qualitative sense separation shown by nearest-neighbor components for ambiguous words (e.g., rock, bank, cell, star, left).
- Strength: consistent quantitative improvements over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussians on several benchmarks (WS-353, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW) and entailment metrics.
- Strength: discussion and evidence that per-sense variances are reduced compared to unimodal Gaussians, addressing over-diffuseness.
- Strength: practical scalability demonstrated with implementation details and training on multi-billion-token corpora.
- Weakness: baseline comparisons are limited—omits strong baselines (300d SGNS/GloVe, MSSG/NPMI, recent multi-prototype models) and uses low dimensionality (50d) throughout.
- Weakness: central claims about uncertainty are weakened because best scoring on many datasets uses max cosine between means, largely ignoring covariances and mixture weights.
- Weakness: fixed global K (typically 2) with no mechanism for per-word adaptive number of senses or analysis of component specialization and collapse.
- Weakness: restricted covariance structure (diagonal/spherical) limits uncertainty modeling; no experiments on full/low-rank covariances or identifiability/stability across runs.
- Weakness: reproducibility/presentation issues—broken references/formatting, code only promised upon publication, vague training-time reporting, possible demo de-anonymization.
- Weakness: limited hyperparameter ablations (margin, negatives, window, dimension) and limited analysis of energy choice and number of negatives.
- Question: how does w2gm compare to strong multi-sense baselines (e.g., MSSG) on SCWS and WS-353 when trained on the same corpus with matched dimensionality?
- Question: ablation request—how much gain comes from multiple means vs. learned covariances (mixtures with fixed covariances; single-prototype with learned covariance; shared covariance across components)?
- Question: sensitivity analysis request—how sensitive are results to K, dimensionality, window size, negatives; per-word diagnostics on who benefits from K>1 and mixture weight distributions?
- Question: for entailment, did authors try other asymmetric measures compatible with mixtures (approximate mixture KL, expected likelihood asymmetrization) and how robust are gains across thresholds/corpora?
- Question: please report precise training time, throughput (tokens/sec), and memory footprint and compare to SGNS and unimodal Gaussians under the same setup.
- Question: does the expected likelihood kernel favor high-variance components despite log-det regularization; provide empirical evidence (covariance histograms) that variance inflation is avoided?
- Minor: fix reference formatting and typos.
- Minor: clarify whether the demo preserves anonymity.
- Minor: consider adding downstream extrinsic tasks to strengthen impact.
