Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- A mixture-of-Gaussians embedding for words (w2gm) trained with a skip-gram-style, max-margin ranking objective, where similarity between word distributions is the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel), which is closed-form for Gaussian mixtures.
- Empirical evidence that w2gm captures polysemy better than unimodal Gaussian embeddings (w2g) and yields moderate gains on several intrinsic benchmarks (word similarity; lexical entailment), plus practical training details for numerical stability and scalability.
- Qualitative analyses (nearest neighbors per component) and a visualization demo; some discussion of uncertainty modeling and how mixtures avoid inflated variances for polysemous words.

Strengths
- Methodologically coherent and tractable extension: Using the expected likelihood kernel yields an analytic, symmetric energy for mixtures, avoiding intractable KL terms between mixtures during training. The objective is simple and likely easy to reproduce.
- Qualitative evidence for polysemy: The per-component nearest-neighbor lists are convincing for canonical examples (rock, bank, cell), and the model’s behavior (variance not blown up to cover multiple senses) is plausible and aligns with the motivation.
- Intrinsic benchmark improvements: On several datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW), w2gm improves over the unimodal Gaussian baseline and often over reported word2vec numbers. The entailment experiment suggests asymmetric scoring (min KL over components) helps and w2gm > w2g.
- Practicality: Details on stability (log-sum-exp trick, epsilon regularization, diagonal/spherical covariances, parameterization of variances and mixture weights) and training choices are useful. Training on a multi-billion-token corpus with a simple CPU setup suggests the approach is not prohibitively expensive.
- Clarity: The paper is generally readable, with intuitive figures and explicit formulas. The supplementary material adds useful implementation specifics.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established baselines: Multi-prototype embeddings (e.g., Huang et al., Tian et al.) and probabilistic embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum) are well known; representing a word as a Gaussian mixture and using a probability product kernel is a fairly direct, incremental extension. The paper does not clearly differentiate itself from contemporaneous multi-sense embeddings (e.g., MSSG; Neelakantan et al., 2015), which are not discussed or compared to.
- Comparability and baseline fairness: Many reported word2vec (sg*) and w2g* numbers are taken from prior work, whereas w2gm/w2g are trained on a specific, very large corpus with particular preprocessing. It is unclear whether all baselines are trained under identical settings (corpora, dimensionality, window, negatives). Using only one negative sample (versus 5–15 commonly used) and D=50 (lower than standard 200–300) complicates fairness. No statistical significance testing is reported; some gains are modest.
- Evaluation scope: The paper relies mainly on intrinsic similarity/relatedness and lexical entailment. There are no downstream task evaluations (e.g., text classification, NER, parsing, MT, QA) to demonstrate practical utility, nor contextual/sense-specific evaluations beyond qualitative examples and a brief mention of SCWS. For a method whose central claim is better polysemy modeling, a robust evaluation on SCWS or SemEval WSD would be expected.
- Entailment methodology ambiguity: The text states using “minimum KL divergence, d(f,g)=min_{i,j} KL(f||g)” for mixtures. KL between mixtures lacks a closed form; the formula as written is unclear. If the authors actually compute min over Gaussian component pairs KL(N(μ_i,Σ_i) || N(ν_j,Λ_j)), that should be explicit. Moreover, the entailment objective is not used in training; only post-hoc scoring changes, limiting the strength of the entailment claim. Comparisons to stronger entailment baselines (e.g., order embeddings) are missing.
- Ablations and sensitivity: There is little analysis on K (only brief K=3 anecdote), covariance structure (spherical vs diagonal), number of negatives, dimensionality, window size, or the choice of energy. Since the training energy is the expected likelihood kernel but evaluation often uses cosine/min Euclidean, it is important to show whether the energy choice, variance learning, and mixture weights materially drive the gains.

Questions to authors
- Baselines and settings: Which baselines (word2vec, w2g) were retrained on the exact same corpus, preprocessing, dimension, window size, and number of negatives? If not, please provide controlled comparisons (same data/hyperparameters) and statistical significance.
- Number of negatives and dimensionality: Why use only one negative and D=50? How do results change with standard settings (e.g., 5–15 negatives, D=200/300)? Do gains over strong word2vec baselines persist?
- KL for entailment: Please clarify the exact scoring: is d(f,g)=min_{i,j} KL(N(μ_i,Σ_i) || N(ν_j,Λ_j))? If so, justify this heuristic for mixtures and discuss its asymmetry properties. Have you tried training with an entailment-aware/asymmetric objective?
- Comparison to multi-sense baselines: Please include comparisons to contemporaneous multi-sense embeddings (e.g., MSSG) on SCWS and WordSim-353 variants. Also report SCWS with MaxSim/AveSim and context integration details.
- Ablations: Provide sensitivity to K (1–3+), covariance type (spherical vs diagonal), similarity/energy choice (train-time and test-time), number of negatives, window size, and dimension. Which components of your design contribute most?
- Downstream tasks: Can you report results on at least one downstream task (e.g., sentiment classification, NER), ideally showing that mixtures materially help relative to strong point baselines under matched settings?
- Runtime and scalability: What are the precise training times and memory footprints for K=2 vs K=1 at D=50/200? How does K^2 component-pairing scale in practice, and did you consider pruning/approximation?

Overall, this is a clean and tractable extension with promising qualitative behavior and some intrinsic gains, but the novelty is incremental and the evaluation lacks rigorous, controlled comparisons and downstream validation. I encourage the authors to strengthen baselines, clarify entailment scoring, and broaden the evaluation.
