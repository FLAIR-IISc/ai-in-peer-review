Summary and claimed contributions
This paper extends probabilistic word embeddings by representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm), trained with a skip-gram–style, max-margin ranking objective. The key technical choice is to use the expected likelihood kernel (inner product in L2) as the energy between two distributions, yielding a closed-form expression even for mixtures. The model aims to capture polysemy (multiple modes), uncertainty (covariances), and to better support entailment (via asymmetric similarity like KL). Experiments on large corpora report qualitative sense separation and improvements on several word similarity benchmarks and a word entailment dataset over unimodal Gaussian embeddings and some prior multi-prototype baselines.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Method: A practical combination of Gaussian mixture embeddings with an expected likelihood kernel energy and a margin-based ranking loss, with implementation details for training at scale.
2) Empirical observations: Qualitative evidence of learned senses and reported gains on several word similarity datasets; some improvement on a lexical entailment dataset when using minimum KL over components.
3) Practicalities: Numerical stability tricks, diagonal/spherical covariance reduction, and initialization choices enabling training on large corpora.

Strengths
- Clear, motivated extension of Gaussian embeddings: Moving from a single Gaussian to a Gaussian mixture is a natural way to address polysemy. The expected likelihood kernel is an analytically convenient and sensible choice; the closed form for mixtures is a practical advantage over KL.
- Qualitative sense separation is plausible: The nearest-neighbor examples (e.g., bank: river bank vs. financial bank; rock: stone vs. music) are convincing and align with the paper’s motivation. The K=3 example for “cell” further illustrates the potential.
- Training details and stability: The paper provides concrete implementation details (diagonal/spherical covariances, log-parameterization, epsilon stabilizers, log-sum-exp trick, subsampling, negative sampling distribution), which are important for reproducing a mixture model that is otherwise easy to destabilize.
- Entailment directionality via KL: Using minimum KL over component pairs is a reasonable way to leverage asymmetry, and the reported trend that KL outperforms cosine on entailment is consistent with the intended semantics of inclusion.
- Scalability claims with simple optimizers: Using Adagrad and a single negative per positive with a large corpus is a practical recipe that many could reproduce.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and positioning are incremental: Representing words as mixtures and learning multiple prototypes are well-trodden (e.g., multi-sense skip-gram/EM-based approaches; unimodal Gaussian embeddings). The paper mainly combines known ingredients (mixtures + skip-gram-style training + ELK). The conceptual step from unimodal Gaussians (Vilnis & McCallum) to mixtures using a kernel that factorizes over pairs is straightforward, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself beyond that combination.
- Evaluation fairness and controls: The comparisons are not adequately controlled. Several baselines (sg*, w2g*) are copied from prior work with potentially different corpora, dimensionalities, and hyperparameters, while the proposed model is trained on a very large corpus (UKWAC+Wackypedia), at D=50, K=2. The paper does not include a strong, re-trained word2vec skip-gram baseline on the same data, same window, same dimension, and same preprocessing. Given that many improvements are small (often 1–3 Spearman points), these could fall within variance or stem from corpus/hyperparameter mismatches.
- Limited breadth of baselines for polysemy and entailment: For polysemy, comparisons to established multi-sense methods (e.g., nonparametric multi-sense embeddings, AdaGram, MSSG) are missing. For entailment, the evaluation uses a single dataset (Baroni et al., 2012) and does not compare to modern asymmetric embedding approaches or specialized hypernymy detectors; the protocol appears to tune the threshold on the same data for best F1, which risks optimistic estimates. No dev/test split is described.
- Methodological clarity gaps: 
  - The negative sampling description says sampling “across the whole document,” which is unusual versus the standard distorted unigram over the vocabulary; please clarify.
  - The model complexity and capacity vs. dimension are underexplored (only D=50 reported). It’s unclear how results scale with D and K; the paper asserts K=3 offers little benefit but provides limited evidence.
  - The objective trades off mean proximity and covariance magnitudes; without explicit regularization beyond what’s implicit in ξ, are there degenerate solutions (e.g., covariance inflation/deflation)? Empirical covariance distributions over training would help.
- Reproducibility and reporting: Code is promised “upon publication,” but no link is provided. Runtime/memory usage and training time are not quantified. Statistical significance is not reported; given the small gains on many datasets, confidence intervals or bootstrap tests over Spearman would be appropriate. Several references and tables contain formatting errors, suggesting the draft needs careful proofreading.

Questions to authors
1) Baselines: Can you provide a re-trained word2vec skip-gram baseline on exactly the same data, preprocessing, window size, negatives per positive, and dimension as w2gm? Similarly, include a re-trained unimodal Gaussian (w2g) baseline with identical settings. How do results change at D=100/300?
2) Sense modeling: How sensitive are results to K? Do you have a principled way to pick K per word (e.g., via nonparametric methods or model selection), and how often do words actually use multiple components (mixture weights)? Any WSD-style evaluation on SCWS with context-aware similarity?
3) Entailment protocol: Do you use a dev set to tune thresholds for F1, or is “best F1” selected on the test set? Could you report AP (threshold-free) and also compare to stronger asymmetric baselines on standard hypernymy benchmarks (e.g., BLESS, EVALution)?
4) Objective and regularization: Beyond the implicit -log det term inside ξ, do you use any explicit priors/regularizers on covariances or means? Can you report the distribution of learned variances and any evidence against degeneracy?
5) Efficiency: Please report training time, peak memory, and throughput as a function of K, D, and vocabulary size. How does O(K^2) pairing in the energy affect training at K>2?

Overall, while the direction is reasonable and some results are promising, the contribution strikes me as incremental, and the empirical evidence is not sufficiently controlled against strong baselines. I would need stronger, fairer comparisons and more rigorous evaluation (including statistical tests and additional baselines) to be confident in endorsing this work.
