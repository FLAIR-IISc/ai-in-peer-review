Summary and main contributions
- Proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm), extending unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) to capture polysemy and heavy-tailed uncertainty. Uses a maximum-margin, energy-based learning objective.
- Introduces the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel) as an analytic, closed-form energy for Gaussian mixtures, enabling efficient training with K^2 pairwise component interactions.
- Provides practical training details (stability tricks, diagonal/spherical covariances, initialization) and reports qualitative polysemy discoveries plus quantitative improvements on several word similarity datasets and a lexical entailment benchmark.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and modeling choice: Moving from unimodal to multimodal distributions addresses a central limitation of Gaussian embeddings for polysemy. The qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses for rock/bank/cell are convincing and intuitive.
- Analytic energy function: The expected likelihood kernel is a strong, principled choice with closed-form evaluation for Gaussian mixtures, avoiding the intractability of KL between mixtures. The derivation and the numerically stable log-sum-exp implementation are appropriate.
- Practicality and scalability: The paper presents implementable constraints (optimizing log variances, softmax for mixture weights) and stability fixes (epsilon in denominators; anchoring log-sum-exp), with a plausible training setup that can scale to billions of tokens. Separation of input/output distributions aligns with skip-gram practice.
- Quantitative results: On several similarity benchmarks (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, RW), w2gm achieves competitive or better correlations than unimodal Gaussians and often word2vec. The entailment experiment suggests that asymmetric measures (component-wise KL) help, and that mixtures better capture inclusion.
- Interpretability: The component-level analyses and the reported K=3 examples for cell are useful; the modeling choice affords a natural interpretation of senses and inclusion.

Weaknesses
- Missing or incomplete evaluations and unclear protocols:
  - SCWS (the dedicated polysemy dataset) is introduced but no results are actually reported in the paper body; only MaxSim/AveSim are defined. This is a major omission for a paper claiming improved polysemy modeling.
  - Entailment: KL for mixtures is intractable, but later an evaluation uses “min KL(f||g)” without clearly specifying that f and g are reduced to component Gaussians (i.e., min over i,j KL(Ni||Nj)). Please clarify exactly what is computed. Also, “best F1” implies threshold tuning on the test set; this is not a fair protocol unless done via cross-validation or a held-out dev set. Report AP (threshold-free) and a dev-tuned threshold for F1.
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Comparisons omit several strong and relevant baselines: GloVe (trained on same corpora), multi-sense skip-gram (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014), fastText/subword models, and context-sensitive embeddings (even as a reference). The comparison to Huang (2012) is dated, and the advantages of w2gm over competitive multi-sense methods remain unclear.
  - Dimension and hyperparameter parity: You fix D=50, one negative sample, and K=2. Many baselines commonly use D=200–300 and more negatives; please report sensitivity and ensure fairness (tuning baselines under the same compute budget).
- Reproducibility gaps:
  - The code is not provided; the interactive website link appears malformed (IP plus an extra space) and may be transient. Exact training time, hardware utilization, and memory footprint are missing (only “days” on 12-core CPU).
  - Handling of OOV pairs in similarity benchmarks is not described; coverage per dataset is not reported.
  - No variance across runs or statistical significance testing; many improvements are modest and may not be statistically reliable.
- Notational and methodological inconsistencies:
  - Diagonal vs spherical covariances: the main text and supplement mix these; the supplement says spherical used in all experiments, while Section 3.4’s discussion uses diagonal. Please unify and state explicitly what is used in all reported numbers.
  - Similarity metrics: Many reported nearest neighbors and some benchmark results are based on component mean cosine, not the proposed energy; conclusions about the benefit of modeling uncertainty would be stronger if evaluated with the expected likelihood kernel (already implemented) alongside cosine/Euclidean.
- Presentation and formatting quality:
  - Numerous typos, broken references, and LaTeX artifacts undermine clarity (e.g., “constrast,” “polysemys,” inconsistent capitalization of Word2Vec/WordSim; dangling equation fragments in references; duplicated author names; missing years “Luong et al.”; “w2mg” vs “w2gm”). Equation references sometimes omit parentheses. These issues are pervasive and would need thorough copy-editing.

Questions to authors
- SCWS: Please provide full SCWS results with MaxSim and AveSim (weighting by mixture probabilities), and compare against multi-sense baselines. How are contexts incorporated for SCWS scoring?
- Entailment: Precisely define KL computation for mixtures. Are you taking min over component-to-component Gaussian KLs? If so, why min and not a mixture-weighted or soft-min variant? Please report AP and dev-tuned F1 to avoid test-set thresholding.
- Baselines: Can you add GloVe, fastText, and MSSG (Neelakantan et al., 2014) baselines on the same corpora? Also report word2vec with competitive dimensions and negatives. How do results change with D=200–300 and 5–15 negatives?
- Ablations and sensitivity: Provide ablations on K (1, 2, 3), D, number of negatives, window size, and covariance type (spherical vs diagonal). Do mixture weights become skewed (e.g., one dominant component), and how does that correlate with polysemy?
- Reproducibility: Share code and training scripts, specify exact training time, memory, and corpus preprocessing. Report dataset coverage and significance tests for all correlations.

Additional comments (minor but numerous)
- Fix the broken/duplicated references and missing years; ensure consistent author formatting and proper venue pages.
- Correct “w2mg” to “w2gm” everywhere; “constrast” → “contrast”; “polysemys” → “polysemous words” or “polysemes.”
- Remove the space in the demo URL and consider a stable domain; demo access should not be a review dependency.
- In A.2, clarify notation dp, dq before use; ensure equation dimensions are explicit; use consistent equation numbering.
- In Table 2, define “sg*” and “w2g*” clearly; ensure that training corpora and hyperparameters are comparable; consider bolding tied bests consistently.

Overall: The idea is appealing and the kernel choice is technically sound, with promising qualitative and some quantitative evidence. However, missing key evaluations (SCWS), unclear entailment protocol, limited and potentially unfair baselines, and extensive presentation issues significantly weaken the submission. I lean toward rejection in its current form; with added experiments, stronger baselines, cleaned presentation, and clearer protocols, this work could be competitive.
