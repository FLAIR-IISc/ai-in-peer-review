Summary and claimed contributions
This paper extends probabilistic word embeddings by representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (GMM), trained with a max-margin ranking objective. The key technical choice is to use the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel) as an energy function, which has a closed form for Gaussian mixtures and thus enables scalable training. The model is intended to capture polysemy via multiple modes, reduce the variance inflation seen in unimodal Gaussian embeddings, and improve on similarity and entailment benchmarks. The authors provide qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses and report results on multiple word similarity datasets and a lexical entailment dataset.

As I see it, the main contributions, in order of importance, are:
1) A practical formulation of multi-prototype probabilistic embeddings via Gaussian mixtures trained with a closed-form energy (expected likelihood kernel) and max-margin objective, with implementation details for stability and scale.
2) Empirical evidence of small to moderate gains over unimodal Gaussian embeddings on several similarity datasets and lexical entailment, plus qualitative demonstrations of polysemy.
3) A set of training/engineering choices (diagonal/spherical covariances, stabilization tricks) that make the approach trainable on large corpora.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity with analytic tractability: Using the expected likelihood kernel provides a closed-form energy for mixtures, avoiding intractable KL between mixtures and enabling straightforward gradients. This is a principled and pragmatic choice for probabilistic embeddings.
- Qualitative handling of polysemy: The nearest-neighbor analyses show that different mixture components align with distinct senses for classic examples (bank, rock, cell), which is precisely the intended behavior and an improvement over unimodal Gaussians.
- Some improvements on benchmarks: On WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, and RW, the reported GMM results often improve upon the authors’ unimodal Gaussian reimplementation. Entailment results also improve when the asymmetric min-KL proxy is used.
- Scale and implementation details: Training on a multi-billion-token corpus with reasonable hardware, along with stability tricks (e.g., log-sum-exp-like stabilization for the energy, positivity via log-params, ε-regularization) are useful for the community. The promise to release code and an interactive visualization is positive for reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to established baselines: Modeling words as mixtures of Gaussians is a fairly direct extension of Vilnis & McCallum (2014). The energy-based objective and negative sampling setup closely mirror prior work. Expected likelihood kernels for distributions (Jebara et al., 2004) are well-known. The paper lacks a compelling methodological innovation beyond this straightforward combination.
- Limited and uneven empirical validation: Improvements are not universal (e.g., SimLex shows degradation; RW marginal). The best-performing similarity metric is often max cosine over means, which largely ignores covariance—undercutting the value of the distributional modeling. The expected likelihood kernel, the central modeling choice, is not consistently superior in evaluation. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Missing or weak comparisons to strong multi-sense baselines: Results against Huang et al. (2012) are limited to WordSim-353; comparisons to other established multi-sense models (e.g., Neelakantan et al. 2014; Tian et al. 2014; Chen et al. 2014) are either not shown or not done on shared datasets with matched settings. Without head-to-head comparisons on SCWS and other polysemy-targeted evaluations under controlled conditions, claims about superior polysemy handling are not convincingly substantiated.
- Ad-hoc entailment scoring with mixtures: The paper states KL between mixtures is intractable, yet uses “minimum KL” by taking min over component pairs. This ignores mixture weights and inter-component structure, and is a heuristic that can be gamed by any single close pair. It is unclear whether the observed entailment gains come from better modeling or from this aggressive max/min pairing. The asymmetry is welcome, but the metric needs stronger motivation and analysis.
- Evaluation protocol and fairness concerns: Dimensionality (D=50), window sizes, number of negatives (only one), and training corpora differ across compared systems (e.g., sg* and w2g* numbers from prior work). This raises questions about fairness. No ablations on K (only anecdotal K=3), covariance structure (spherical vs diagonal inconsistencies), mixture weights’ role, or number of negatives. Runtime/throughput vs word2vec/w2g is not quantified.
- Reporting and clarity issues: Section 4.4 (SCWS) references comparisons but does not present a complete table in the provided text. There are inconsistencies on whether diagonal or spherical covariances are used; references have placeholders/duplications; and some formulas and hyperparameters are only partially specified.

Questions to authors
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Please report results against strong multi-sense baselines (e.g., Neelakantan et al. 2014; Tian et al. 2014; Chen et al. 2014) on SCWS and other relevant datasets, trained on the same corpus with matched dimensions and hyperparameters. Include statistical significance.
  - How do your results change at standard dimensions (e.g., 200–300)? Do gains persist?
- Use of covariance and the ELK:
  - Why does max cosine over means often outperform the expected likelihood kernel? Can you provide analyses showing scenarios where covariance materially helps (beyond anecdotes)?
  - Can you quantify the degree to which mixture weights and covariances influence similarity rankings?
- Entailment metric:
  - Precisely define “minimum KL” for mixtures. Are you taking min over Gaussian component pairs KL(Ni||Nj)? Why ignore mixture weights? Have you tried other asymmetric proxies (e.g., expected KL under mixture weights, Monte Carlo estimates)?
  - Provide ablations comparing different asymmetric metrics and their impact on AP/F1.
- Model choices and ablations:
  - Clarify whether covariances are spherical or diagonal in the main experiments. Provide ablations across covariance structures, number of negatives, window sizes, and K values (beyond K=2), with training budgets held constant.
  - Report training time and throughput compared to word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on the same hardware and corpus.
- Polysemy and downstream tasks:
  - Beyond nearest neighbors, can you evaluate on sense-specific benchmarks (e.g., SCWS with standard evaluation protocols) and downstream tasks that should benefit from multi-prototype embeddings (e.g., WSD, contextual similarity)?
