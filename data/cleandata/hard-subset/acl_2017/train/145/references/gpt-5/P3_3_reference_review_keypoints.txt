- Proposal: Represent each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm) trained with a skip-gram-style, energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel to score word affinity.
- Contributions: Scalable, analytically tractable multimodal probabilistic embeddings; qualitative sense separation and improved similarity/entailment performance; practical training/stability recipes enabling large-scale training.
- Strength: Well-justified energy function—expected likelihood kernel generalizes inner products to densities and incorporates uncertainty via log-det and Mahalanobis terms.
- Strength: Captures polysemy unsupervisedly and scalably (K=2–3) with intuitive sense clusters for polysemous words.
- Strength: Empirical improvements over unimodal Gaussians and often word2vec on multiple similarity datasets and entailment benchmarks.
- Strength: Strong engineering/reproducibility details (covariance parameterizations, optimizer settings, numerical stabilizations, visualization).
- Strength: Thoughtful analysis of uncertainty—mixtures reduce per-dimension variance inflation seen in unimodal Gaussians.
- Weakness: Limited comparisons to other multi-sense methods (e.g., MSSG/Neelakantan et al.) and stronger entailment baselines, weakening empirical claims.
- Weakness: Ambiguity in KL-based entailment scoring—paper uses a “minimum KL” but does not explicitly define or justify the component-wise approximation.
- Weakness: Missing or incomplete evaluations (SCWS results absent, no context-sensitive training/disambiguation procedure) that limit claims about token-level sense disambiguation.
- Weakness: Sparse ablation and sensitivity analyses on key choices (K, covariance type, D, margin, negatives, input/output tying) and inconsistent choice of similarity metrics across datasets.
- Weakness: Potential unfairness of baselines—low embedding dimensionality (D=50) and unmatched training/hyperparameter settings may bias comparisons.
- Question: Please clarify the exact definition and justification of the “minimum KL divergence” used for entailment scoring.
- Question: How does w2gm compare to multi-sense skip-gram (Neelakantan et al., 2014) and other multi-prototype baselines when trained on the same data/dimensions?
- Question: Report training time, hardware, memory usage for K=2/3 and how runtime scales with K, D, and negatives; were more negatives tried?
- Question: How sensitive are results to covariance parameterization (spherical vs diagonal vs tied); do diagonal covariances help?
- Question: Do you have a token-level context disambiguation scheme (component selection) for context-sensitive tasks like SCWS; if so, report those results rather than MaxSim.
- Question: Are mixture weights pi interpretable / correlated with sense frequencies; any analysis of learned pi for polysemous words?
- Additional: Fix reference formatting/corrupt entries and ensure visualization link complies with anonymity policy for camera-ready submission.
- Additional: Report a single primary similarity measure across datasets (e.g., expected likelihood or MaxSim) to avoid per-dataset metric selection.
- Additional: Consider evaluating on modern hypernymy/entailment benchmarks and explicitly report directionality/asymmetric scoring.
- Overall: Solid, well-motivated extension of probabilistic embeddings to mixtures with promising results; main issues are comparison breadth, KL clarity, and missing ablations/context evaluations—lean accept after revisions.
