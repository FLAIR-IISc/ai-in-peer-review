- Proposal: represent words as mixtures of Gaussians (w2gm) to capture polysemy and uncertainty, trained with a skip-gram–style max-margin objective using an expected likelihood (probability product) kernel.
- Practical contributions: closed-form energy for mixtures, numerical/stability tricks, and training design (spherical/diagonal covariances, Adagrad, negative sampling) enabling large-scale training.
- Empirical claims: qualitative sense separation, quantitative gains on multiple word similarity datasets and lexical entailment tasks, plus analysis of variance behavior and a visualization tool.
- Main contributions summarized: scalable multi-prototype probabilistic embeddings, empirical evidence of improved sense separation and uncertainty modeling, and practical training/stability design for large corpora.
- Strength: sound, tractable probabilistic formulation with closed-form expected likelihood that balances means and covariances and shows understanding of geometry/optimization.
- Strength: clear qualitative demonstration of polysemy handling, with nearest-neighbor examples showing distinct senses per mixture component.
- Strength: empirical improvements over unimodal Gaussian and skip-gram on many benchmarks, and informative comparisons across similarity measures.
- Strength: implementational clarity and scalability choices are well-motivated, with supplementary derivations and promise of code/demo.
- Weakness: incremental novelty—direct extension of prior Gaussian embeddings and known kernels; conceptual leap is moderate, more practical than theoretical.
- Weakness: limited evaluation of context-sensitive sense selection—central promise of mixtures not convincingly tested (e.g., WSD, lexical substitution, SCWS not fully reported).
- Weakness: covariances/weights underutilized—use of spherical covariances and max-cosine metrics suggests gains mainly from means, with missing ablations and parameter-matched baselines.
- Weakness: entailment evaluation limited—comparison only to w2g, threshold-tuned metrics, heuristic min-KL scoring, no exploration of alternative asymmetric measures or statistical significance.
- Weakness: scalability/model-selection details missing—only small K evaluated (K=2/3), no per-word K selection, and no runtime/memory/throughput or scaling plots.
- Requested ablations/questions: report results with diagonal covariances and analyze effects on similarity/entailment.
- Requested ablations/questions: justify min KL for entailment (vs expectation/soft-min) and try other asymmetric distances (Bhattacharyya, Wasserstein).
- Requested ablations/questions: provide context-aware SCWS results and compare AveSim vs MaxSim against multi-prototype baselines.
- Requested ablations/questions: include parameter-matched baselines (higher-dim single Gaussian), fixed-variance baselines, shared covariances/weights, and statistical significance tests.
- Requested ablations/questions: report training time, memory, throughput vs skip-gram and w2g for K=1/2/3 and examine feasibility of per-word K selection.
- Overall assessment: solid, practical extension with meaningful qualitative gains and competitive results, but moderate novelty and underspecified/contextual evaluations—leaning toward acceptance if the reviewers’ concerns are addressed.
