Summary and Contributions:
- This paper proposes probabilistic multi-sense word embeddings by representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm), trained with a skip-gram-style max-margin objective. The key technical choice is to use the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel) as the similarity/energy between distributions, which remains closed-form even for mixtures.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) A tractable probabilistic multi-sense embedding: modeling words as Gaussian mixtures and training with a closed-form energy, enabling interpretable, sense-specific components and uncertainty.
  2) An effective training formulation and set of practical tricks: max-margin ranking objective, analytically computable energy for GMMs, diagonal/spherical covariance parameterization, and numerical stabilization details that enable large-scale training.
  3) Empirical evidence that mixtures improve over unimodal Gaussian embeddings on word similarity (several datasets) and lexical entailment, alongside qualitative demonstrations of polysemy.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean extension with analytic tractability: Moving from a single Gaussian to a Gaussian mixture is a natural way to model polysemy, and the expected likelihood kernel preserves closed-form computation even with mixtures. The derivations and partial-energy terms are sensible, and the log-det term curbs variance inflation.
- Clear, well-documented training recipe: The paper provides practical details (negative sampling distribution, subsampling, initialization, log-sum-exp stabilization, optimizing in log-variance space, softmaxed mixture weights) that improve robustness. These details aid reproducibility and indicate careful engineering.
- Qualitative multi-sense behavior: Nearest-neighbor analyses per component (e.g., rock, bank, cell) convincingly show distinct senses captured by different mixture components, addressing a core limitation of unimodal Gaussians (variance inflation and mean “pulling”).
- Broad evaluation with consistent improvements: On multiple similarity benchmarks, w2gm typically improves over unimodal Gaussians and often over word2vec, and lexical entailment benefits from asymmetric KL scoring at test time. The reported gains (e.g., WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, RW) and the observation that different metrics (cosine vs Euclidean) can be preferable per dataset show thoughtful evaluation.
- Scalability claims and simplicity: With K=2 and diagonal/spherical covariances, the method is computationally manageable. The training on ~3.5B tokens with straightforward hardware suggests practicality.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty relative to prior multi-sense embeddings and missing baselines: While probabilistic multi-sense is valuable, the idea of multi-prototype embeddings is well-studied (e.g., Reisinger & Mooney 2010; Huang et al. 2012; MSSG/Neelakantan et al. 2014; Tian et al. 2014). Some of these are not compared or only lightly referenced. Without direct comparisons to strong multi-sense baselines on standard datasets under matched settings, the empirical case is incomplete.
- Evaluation fairness and strength of baselines: Many reported numbers for skip-gram are borrowed from prior work, and dimensionality (D=50) is smaller than common 200–300-dim baselines. Negative sampling count and other hyperparameters differ. It is unclear if the gains persist against tuned, stronger baselines trained on the same corpora with matched settings. No significance testing is reported.
- Fixed number of senses and lack of analysis of usage: K is fixed globally (mostly 2). There is no mechanism to adapt K per word or to merge/split senses, and no analysis of the learned mixture weights (e.g., how often one component dominates, entropy distribution across vocabulary, collapse frequency). This limits the claim that the method robustly captures polysemy across the lexicon.
- Role of covariance vs multiple means not disentangled: The paper argues uncertainty and entailment advantages, yet covariances are restricted (diagonal/spherical) and there is no ablation to show how much of the improvement comes from having multiple means versus modeling covariances. A competitive deterministic multi-vector baseline might close much of the gap.
- Train/test objective mismatch and energy choices: Training uses the expected likelihood kernel, while entailment evaluation uses KL at test time. The paper does not explore whether training with an objective more aligned to entailment (or an asymmetric energy) would help, nor does it quantify the effect of energy choice on similarity tasks beyond reporting scores.

Questions to Authors:
- Baselines: Can you include strong multi-sense deterministic baselines (e.g., MSSG/Neelakantan et al. 2014; Reisinger & Mooney 2010) trained on the same corpora and dimensions? How do results change at 200–300 dims and with standard numbers of negative samples?
- Mixture usage: What is the distribution of mixture weight entropies across the vocabulary? How often do components collapse or remain balanced? On SCWS, how frequently does the model pick different components for different contexts of the same word?
- Ablations: 
  - Fix covariances to isotropic constants and compare to learnable covariances to isolate the contribution of uncertainty modeling.
  - Replace the mixture with multiple deterministic vectors per word and score with MaxSim to test whether mixtures outperform simpler multi-vector models under the same training objective.
  - Compare expected likelihood vs dot-product energy while keeping other settings fixed.
- K selection: Did you try K>2 systematically? Is there a criterion to adapt K per word (e.g., based on weight entropy or validation performance)? Any pruning strategy?
- Efficiency: Please report concrete training time, memory footprint, and throughput vs word2vec and unimodal Gaussians; how does cost scale with K (noting O(K^2) pair interactions)?
- Regularization: Beyond the implicit log-det term and epsilon stabilizers, do you apply explicit variance regularization or constraints to avoid variance inflation? Any observed pathologies?
- Anonymity and reproducibility: The visualization URL includes an IP; please ensure anonymity. Will you release code and pretrained models to support replication of all tables?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, technically sound extension of probabilistic embeddings to multi-sense with analytic training and promising results. Strengthening the empirical comparisons and analysis would substantially improve the paper’s impact.
