- Proposes probabilistic multi-sense word embeddings where each word is a Gaussian mixture (typically K=2) trained with a skip-gram–style, max-margin ranking objective using the expected likelihood kernel (ELK).
- Provides a practical, scalable training recipe (diagonal/spherical covariances, numerical stabilizations, negative sampling) with implementation details and initialization strategies.
- Shows empirical and qualitative evidence that mixtures capture polysemy, reduce per-component variance, and separate senses compared to unimodal Gaussians.
- Uses the expected likelihood kernel as a principled, analytically tractable energy for comparing mixture embeddings, with an interpretable decomposition into partial energies.
- Demonstrates competitive empirical results at modest dimensionality (D=50), outperforming unimodal Gaussians and matching/beating word2vec on many similarity benchmarks; improves lexical entailment with an asymmetric score.
- Training is scalable and reproducible: documented objective, negative sampling, subsampling, numerical stabilization, and feasible CPU-only training despite O(K^2) pairwise complexity.
- Model yields interpretable senses with uncertainty, mixture weights, and covariance-induced regularization; partial-energy view clarifies sense contributions.
- Novelty is incremental: Gaussian mixtures extend prior unimodal work (Vilnis & McCallum 2014) and core ingredients (skip-gram, max-margin, probability product kernels) are known—main contribution is integration and scaling.
- Test-time metric selection is inconsistent: many results use max cosine between means, ignoring learned uncertainty and ELK, risking metric selection bias.
- Baseline comparisons are incomplete or unfair: some word2vec numbers are taken from prior work and multi-sense baselines (e.g., MSSG, Reisinger & Mooney) are missing, making positioning unclear.
- Context-sensitive evaluation is weak: SCWS and polysemy benchmarks under-use context, relying on max-over-components rather than responsibility/posterior-based selection used by strong multi-sense methods.
- Entailment scoring is ambiguous: using min_{i,j} KL between components is a heuristic that ignores weights and cross-component mass and should be compared to ELK-based or Monte Carlo KL estimates.
- Limited ablations: little analysis of K, embedding dimensionality D, covariance structure (spherical vs diagonal vs full), or the effect of fixed vs learned variances.
- Requests clarification on metric selection: whether a single test-time similarity metric was fixed or best-per-dataset was reported, and asks for standardized ELK-based evaluation.
- Asks how context is incorporated at test time for SCWS and whether responsibility-based selection was tried and improves results.
- Requests parity baselines: word2vec, unimodal Gaussian, and MSSG results trained on the exact same corpora, vocabulary, dimensions, and window sizes.
- Seeks details on entailment KL: whether min_{i,j} KL ignores weights, which direction is used, and whether ELK-derived asymmetries or mixture-KL approximations were tried.
- Asks for ablations reporting sensitivity to K, D, window size, and covariance type, and specifically how diagonal vs spherical covariances affect performance and variance inflation.
- Requests efficiency details: exact training times and memory footprints for K=2 on the 3.5B-token corpus and runtime scaling with K.
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed extension of probabilistic embeddings with compelling qualitative results, but novelty is incremental and evaluation issues (metrics, baselines, context conditioning) need addressing before a strong accept.
