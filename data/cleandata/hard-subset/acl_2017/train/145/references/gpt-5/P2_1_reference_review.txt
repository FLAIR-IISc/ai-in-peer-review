Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes multimodal probabilistic word embeddings: each word is a mixture of K Gaussians (means, covariances, mixture weights). Training uses a skip-gram–style energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel) as a symmetric, closed-form energy between mixtures.
- It provides practical training details (diagonal/spherical covariances, stabilizing transforms, initialization, negative sampling) that enable scaling to billions of tokens.
- Empirically, the model captures multiple senses qualitatively and improves intrinsic performance on several word similarity datasets and on lexical entailment (using minimum KL at evaluation).

My view of the main contributions (strongest first)
1) A simple, tractable probabilistic multi-prototype embedding using Gaussian mixtures and the expected likelihood kernel, with an end-to-end training objective that scales.
2) Evidence that the learned mixtures reflect polysemy and help on several intrinsic benchmarks; qualitative analyses and an online demo.
3) Practical recipe for numerically stable, large-scale training of probabilistic embeddings.

Strengths
1) Clear, principled formulation with analytic energy. Moving from unimodal Gaussians to mixtures directly addresses the known limitation of Vilnis & McCallum (2014). Using the expected likelihood kernel yields a closed-form energy for mixture–mixture comparisons, avoiding approximations and enabling efficient learning. This is a technically clean choice with good justification.
2) Captures polysemy in an unsupervised manner. The qualitative neighbors for components of rock/bank/cell convincingly show distinct senses. This is an intuitive, interpretable benefit that deterministic single-vector or unimodal Gaussian embeddings struggle with.
3) Consistent improvements on several intrinsic benchmarks. On WordSim-353 (and subsets), MEN, MC, RG, YP, MTurk-287, and RW, the mixture model typically outperforms both skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian baselines; entailment improves when using asymmetric KL at evaluation. While gains are modest on some datasets, they are broad and consistent.
4) Practicality and reproducibility. The paper provides concrete implementation choices (covariance parameterization, subsampling/negative sampling, stability tricks, optimization details) and reports large-corpus training. These details are valuable for the community and increase confidence that others can reproduce the method.
5) Insight into uncertainty. The analysis that mixtures reduce over-diffuse covariances for polysemous words, together with the regularizing role of the log-det term in the energy, is a useful diagnostic and strengthens the modeling argument.

Weaknesses
1) Incremental novelty relative to prior art. Conceptually, “multiple embeddings per word” is well-trodden (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014; also MSSG/Neelakantan et al., 2014), and “probabilistic embeddings” are established (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014). Combining these with an expected likelihood kernel is natural. The contribution is more in careful engineering and an elegant energy choice than in a fundamentally new learning principle.
2) Evaluation breadth and baselines. The paper emphasizes intrinsic similarity and entailment; there are no extrinsic tasks (e.g., tagging, parsing, NLI, QA) to demonstrate end-task benefits of uncertainty and polysemy. Among multi-sense baselines, comparisons are limited (Huang, Tian, Chen/WordNet-assisted). Stronger and more recent multi-sense baselines (e.g., nonparametric or context-adaptive embeddings) are not reported. This makes it hard to gauge the true competitiveness of the approach beyond the included baselines.
3) Use (or non-use) of uncertainty at evaluation. For similarity, the best-performing metric is often maximum cosine similarity between component means, which largely ignores covariances and mixture weights. This undercuts the claimed value of modeling uncertainty; results with expected likelihood kernel are included but typically weaker. More analysis is needed to show when/why uncertainty helps (beyond the entailment setting).
4) Limited ablations and statistical rigor. Improvements are reported without variance across runs or statistical significance. Ablations isolating the contributions of mixtures vs. covariances vs. the energy choice (e.g., mixtures with fixed isotropic covariances; training with dot-product energy) would clarify what drives the gains. Sensitivity to D, margin m, window size, and negative sampling distribution is not explored.
5) Clarity and presentation issues. There are several citation/formatting glitches, incomplete references, and some duplicated or confusing phrasing (e.g., dataset attributions). While not fatal, they detract from polish and make it harder to verify claims.

How well the paper meets ACL reviewing criteria
- Originality: Moderate. The move to mixtures within a probabilistic embedding framework is a logical extension; the expected likelihood kernel is a known tool. The overall combination is coherent but not highly novel.
- Soundness/correctness: Good. The derivations are standard and correct; training objective is reasonable; numerical stability considerations are appropriate. The mismatch between training (symmetric energy) and entailment evaluation (asymmetric KL) is acknowledged and justified by tractability, but could be discussed more critically.
- Clarity: Generally clear, with helpful intuition and figures, but marred by reference/formatting issues and some missing details.
- Significance/impact: Moderate. The approach is simple, scalable, and shows consistent intrinsic gains and interpretability. Impact would be stronger with convincing extrinsic results and stronger baselines.
- Resources/reproducibility: Reasonable. Implementation details are extensive; code promise and demo help. Reported training scale is useful; more concrete runtime/memory numbers would help.

Questions to authors
1) How sensitive are results to K and to choosing spherical vs. diagonal covariances? Did you try per-word K (e.g., nonparametric sense induction) or model selection criteria?
2) Can you report variance over multiple runs and significance testing on the main benchmarks?
3) For similarity, why do metrics leveraging covariances underperform max-cosine? Can you show settings/datasets where uncertainty helps, or propose a similarity that better uses mixture weights/covariances?
4) How does w2gm compare to stronger multi-sense baselines such as MSSG (Neelakantan et al.) or AdaGram on SCWS and WordSim? Can you add these comparisons?
5) Can you provide extrinsic evaluations (e.g., as features in downstream tasks) to demonstrate practical gains from polysemy and uncertainty?
6) Entailment is evaluated with min KL but trained with ELK. Did you try incorporating an asymmetric training signal (e.g., when context suggests hypernymy) or a surrogate that correlates with KL to better align training and evaluation?
7) Please clarify throughput and memory: tokens/sec, hours on your hardware, and scaling with K and D.
