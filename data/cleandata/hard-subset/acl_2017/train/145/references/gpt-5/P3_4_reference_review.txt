Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm), extending unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) to capture polysemy and richer uncertainty. Training uses a skip-gram–style, energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel) as the similarity function, which remains closed-form for mixtures. Practical considerations (spherical/diagonal covariances, stable log-sum-exp computation, parameterization of variances and mixture weights) enable large-scale training.
- Empirically, the method qualitatively separates senses and quantitatively improves on several word similarity datasets and lexical entailment benchmarks. The authors also analyze variance shrinkage for polysemous words and provide a visualization tool.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A scalable probabilistic multi-prototype word embedding with Gaussian mixtures trained via a closed-form expected likelihood kernel in a max-margin setup.
2) Empirical evidence that mixtures yield better qualitative sense separation and competitive-to-better performance on multiple word similarity datasets and lexical entailment, with discussion on uncertainty/variance behavior.
3) Practical training/stability design that allows training on multi-billion-token corpora.

Strengths
- Sound, tractable probabilistic formulation: Using the expected likelihood (probability product) kernel for Gaussian mixtures yields a closed-form energy that naturally balances mean proximity and covariance magnitudes. The discussion of the ξi,j “partial energy” terms, the regularizing -log det term, and numerical stabilization demonstrates good understanding of the geometry and optimization.
- Clear qualitative value for polysemy: The nearest-neighbor analyses convincingly show sense separation (e.g., rock = {stone/music}, bank = {river/finance}), with a simple interpretation of mixture components as senses. The claim that mixtures mitigate the “variance bloat” of unimodal Gaussians for polysemous words is supported by reported variance magnitudes.
- Empirical improvements on several benchmarks: The mixture model outperforms unimodal Gaussian embeddings and skip-gram on many datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW) and improves lexical entailment when using asymmetric min-KL between components. The comparison across similarity measures (cosine, expected likelihood, Euclidean) is informative.
- Scalability and implementational clarity: The design choices (spherical/diagonal covariances, O(K^2) mixture energy, Adagrad, subsampling, negative sampling) are well-motivated and practical. The supplementary material provides derivations and training details, and the promise of code and an interactive demo is appreciated.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: The method is a relatively direct extension of Vilnis & McCallum (Gaussian → mixture) combined with a known kernel (PPK) and a standard ranking objective. Prior multi-prototype/deterministic sense embeddings (Huang et al., Tian et al., others) and alternative probabilistic/sense-aware methods are not comprehensively compared. The conceptual leap is moderate; the contribution is more in practical, scalable realization than new theory.
- Limited evaluation of sense use in context: Most reported gains are on type-level similarity; SimLex performance drops. SCWS is mentioned but not fully reported with context-aware sense selection. A central promise of mixtures—context-sensitive sense selection—is not convincingly evaluated (e.g., no lexical substitution, WSD, or contextual similarity tasks), and the best-performing metric is often maximum cosine over means, which largely ignores learned covariances and mixture weights.
- Underutilized covariance/weights and geometry: Experiments use spherical covariances (by authors’ admission), which weakens the potential of anisotropic uncertainty and inclusion geometry for entailment. Many improvements appear when using max cosine, suggesting that added parameters in means explain much of the gain, with limited evidence that covariance or mixture weights materially help. Ablations (fixed variance, removing weights, diagonal vs spherical) and parameter-matched baselines (e.g., higher-dimensional unimodal Gaussians) are missing.
- Entailment evaluation scope: The entailment results compare only w2g vs w2gm and hinge on a threshold chosen to maximize AP/F1; there is no comparison to stronger entailment baselines. The asymmetric scoring uses min KL across component pairs (Gaussian-to-Gaussian), which is reasonable but heuristic; alternative asymmetric measures (e.g., expected/soft-min KL, Bhattacharyya, Wasserstein) are not explored. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Scalability and model selection details: Although the approach is efficient, O(K^2) per pair limits larger K; only K=2 (occasionally 3) is evaluated, with a fixed global K and no per-word selection. Runtime/memory/throughput numbers and scaling plots (vs K and dimension) are not provided. Discarding words with <100 occurrences reduces vocabulary to ~314k; polysemy for rarer words remains unassessed.

Questions to authors
- Covariances: Were all main results obtained with spherical covariances? Please report results with diagonal covariances and analyze their effect on similarity and entailment, especially given claims about inclusion/uncertainty geometry.
- Entailment metric: For d(f||g) = min_{i,j} KL(Ni||Nj), why min and not an expectation or soft-min over mixture weights? Did you try asymmetric variants of the expected likelihood kernel, Bhattacharyya distance, or Wasserstein-based energies?
- Contextual disambiguation: Can you report SCWS with context-aware sense selection (e.g., posterior over components given the context window) and compare to multi-prototype baselines (Huang, Tian, Chen), including AveSim vs MaxSim?
- Ablations/parameter counts: How much of the gain is due to increased parameters? Please include baselines with (i) single Gaussian but doubled dimension, (ii) fixed variance, and (iii) mixtures but shared covariances/weights. Provide statistical significance where applicable.
- Efficiency and scaling: Provide training time, memory footprint, and throughput vs skip-gram and w2g for K=1/2/3; how does performance scale with K? Is per-word K (e.g., nonparametric prior or pruning) feasible?

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed extension of probabilistic word embeddings to mixtures with a tractable energy, showing meaningful qualitative gains and competitive quantitative results. The work is practically valuable and likely to interest the community. However, novelty is moderate, and the evaluation undersells the core promise of mixtures (context-sensitive disambiguation and uncertainty). Strengthening contextual evaluations, covariance ablations, and entailment comparisons would greatly improve the paper. I lean toward acceptance if space permits, contingent on addressing the above concerns in the camera-ready.
