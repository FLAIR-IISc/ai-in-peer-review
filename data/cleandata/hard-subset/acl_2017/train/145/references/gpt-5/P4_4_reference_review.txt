Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes a probabilistic multi-prototype word embedding model where each word is represented as a mixture of Gaussians (GMM). Training follows a skip-gram-style, energy-based max-margin objective that contrasts observed word–context pairs with negatives. The key technical choice is an energy function equal to the expected likelihood kernel (inner product in L2) between distributions, which has a closed form for Gaussian mixtures and enables efficient training. Practical stabilization details (diagonal/spherical covariances, log-parametrization, softmax mixture weights, numerically stable log-sum-exp) and large-scale training on 3.5B tokens are provided. Empirically, the model qualitatively separates senses of polysemous words and shows improved performance on several word similarity benchmarks and lexical entailment.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A probabilistic multi-sense embedding framework using Gaussian mixtures trained with a closed-form expected likelihood kernel within a max-margin objective, enabling analytic gradients and scalability.
2) A set of practical training and stabilization techniques that allow training GMM embeddings at web scale.
3) Empirical evidence that multi-prototype probabilistic embeddings yield better qualitative sense separation and improved performance on several word similarity datasets and lexical entailment.

Strengths
- Technically sound energy choice with closed form for mixtures. The expected likelihood kernel between GMMs is correctly derived and implemented efficiently (diagonal/spherical covariances), providing a principled, tractable probabilistic similarity that incorporates both means and covariances.
- Clear qualitative sense separation. Nearest-neighbor analyses for polysemous words (e.g., rock, bank, cell) show distinct clusters aligned with different senses, which the unimodal Gaussian baseline fails to capture. The provided visualization further supports interpretability.
- Quantitative gains on multiple benchmarks. The model achieves higher Spearman correlations than unimodal Gaussian embeddings and often than skip-gram on WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, and RW, and improves entailment AP/F1 when using an asymmetric KL-based score. The trend that mixtures outperform single-component Gaussians is consistent.
- Scalable and reproducible training procedure. The paper details windowing, subsampling, negative sampling, optimization choices (Adagrad), initialization, and stabilization steps, and trains on large corpora (UKWAC + Wackypedia), demonstrating practical viability.
- Entailment modeling benefits from probabilistic structure. Using (componentwise) asymmetric KL improves entailment over cosine, aligning with the intuition that distributional inclusion/asymmetry matters.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguity in KL computation for mixtures. Earlier the paper notes KL between GMMs lacks a closed form, yet entailment uses “minimum KL divergence” d(f,g) = min_{i,j} KL(f||g) without specifying whether KL is computed between component Gaussians N(µ_f,i, Σ_f,i) and N(µ_g,j, Σ_g,j). The exact definition and computational procedure should be stated unambiguously.
- Limited ablation on the role of covariances vs multiple means. The strongest similarity performance often comes from maximum cosine similarity over component means rather than the proposed expected likelihood kernel, suggesting that multi-prototype means may drive the gains. There is no ablation with mixtures of point embeddings (no covariances) or fixed covariances to quantify the added value of learned uncertainty.
- Potential covariance pathologies not analyzed. The objective structure (ξ includes −0.5 log det(Σ_f+Σ_g)) encourages small determinants for nearby pairs, which can favor covariance shrinkage; while the paper argues against large variances, it does not analyze or regularize against collapse to overly small covariances. No priors/penalties or empirical diagnostics are reported (e.g., variance distributions across training).
- Incomplete and uneven comparisons. Beyond Huang et al. (2012), comparisons to other multi-sense methods (e.g., Tian et al., Chen et al., and contemporary baselines) are limited or not reported across all datasets. The SCWS evaluation is introduced but the corresponding results table is missing. Word2vec and Gaussian baselines use different sources for some scores, and it is unclear if preprocessing, corpora, and hyperparameters are fully matched.
- Presentation and clarity issues. There are several typographical/formatting errors, inconsistent notation (e.g., w2g used to refer both to unimodal Gaussians and “our implementation of w2gm with one component”), and reference formatting glitches. Some claims (e.g., “heavy tailed uncertainty”) are not rigorously supported—Gaussian mixtures are heavier-tailed than a single Gaussian but remain light-tailed; this should be stated precisely.

Questions to authors
- Entailment scoring: Precisely define the “minimum KL divergence” used. Is KL computed between component Gaussians (closed-form) and then minimized over i,j? If so, please provide the explicit formula used and rationale for min vs other aggregations (e.g., soft-min or Earth Mover’s-like measures).
- Role of covariances: Can you add ablations comparing (a) mixtures of point embeddings (no covariances), (b) mixtures with fixed/spherical covariances, and (c) full learned covariances, to quantify the contribution of uncertainty modeling? Also, report which similarity metric is used at test time in each case.
- Stability and regularization: Do covariances tend to collapse to very small values during training? Please provide statistics of learned variances, any variance floors/priors used, and sensitivity to initialization v and margin m.
- Number of components: Provide a systematic study of K (e.g., K=1–4) on SCWS and key similarity datasets, including coverage: how many words actually utilize multiple components (by mixture weights or usage in nearest-neighbor pairs)?
- Evaluation completeness and fairness: Please include full SCWS results and comparisons to other multi-sense baselines (Tian et al., Chen et al.) under matched training data/hyperparameters where possible. For WordSim and other benchmarks, clarify corpus/preprocessing parity with baselines.
- Efficiency: Report wall-clock training times, memory footprint, and K^2 inference cost for the energy on your hardware, and discuss scalability trade-offs as K increases.
- Claims on heavy tails and entailment: Can you clarify the “heavy tailed uncertainty” claim and provide evidence (e.g., tail index estimates) or rephrase more conservatively? For entailment, have you considered other asymmetric measures that remain tractable for mixtures?
