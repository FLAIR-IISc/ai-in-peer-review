Summary
The paper proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (MoG) rather than a single point vector or a unimodal Gaussian. Training uses a skip-gram-inspired, energy-based max-margin objective with an energy function defined by the expected likelihood kernel (ELK), which has a closed form for Gaussian mixtures. The approach is designed to capture polysemy via multiple modes, reflect uncertainty via covariances, and support entailment-like inclusion. The authors provide training details for stability and scalability, and evaluate qualitatively (nearest neighbors per sense) and quantitatively (word similarity datasets and a lexical entailment task), reporting improvements over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A scalable probabilistic multi-prototype word embedding model using Gaussian mixtures, with each component intended to capture a distinct sense and uncertainty via covariances.
2) An analytic, tractable energy function (ELK) within a max-margin ranking framework tailored for mixtures, including practical stabilization tricks and an implementation that trains on billions of tokens.
3) Empirical evidence that the model better separates senses and improves on several intrinsic evaluations and a lexical entailment task compared to unimodal Gaussian and skip-gram baselines.

Strengths
- Clear modeling choice and tractable training: Extending Gaussian embeddings to mixtures is conceptually natural for polysemy. The use of the expected likelihood kernel yields a closed-form energy for mixtures and integrates uncertainty (means, covariances). The derivation and loss are clear and technically sound, with appropriate numerical stabilization (log-sum-exp, epsilon on covariances, optimization over log-variances and softmax weights).
- Demonstrated polysemy modeling: Qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses by component (e.g., bank, rock, cell) convincingly show disambiguated senses. The model addresses the known variance inflation issue of unimodal Gaussians for polysemous words by distributing variance across components.
- Competitive to improved results on intrinsic benchmarks: The model reports consistent gains over unimodal Gaussian embeddings and skip-gram on several word similarity datasets (e.g., WS-353, MEN, MC, RG, YP, RW). The mixture generally performs best with maximum cosine similarity, and Euclidean distance aligns with the training energy for some datasets (MC, RW). Results are plausible and in line with the intended strengths of the model.
- Entailment results and use of asymmetric metrics: For lexical entailment, the reported gains of the mixture model over the unimodal Gaussian, especially when using a (component-wise) KL-based score, align with the hypothesis that multimodality and uncertainty better capture inclusion relations.
- Implementation details and scalability: The paper provides useful training specifics (subsampling, negative sampling distribution, diagonal/spherical covariances, optimizer choice) and claims large-scale training. This increases reproducibility and practical value.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to prior Gaussian embeddings: The main technical novelty is moving from unimodal to mixture Gaussians and adopting ELK for mixtures. While important, this is a relatively direct extension of Vilnis and McCallum (2014). The energy function (probability product/ELK) is known, and the learning objective largely mirrors prior ranking objectives. The paper would benefit from deeper theoretical or algorithmic innovations beyond the mixture extension and practical stabilizations.
- Limited breadth of baselines and analyses: The evaluation lacks comparisons to strong multi-sense deterministic embeddings beyond Huang et al. (2012), e.g., Neelakantan et al. (2014) or other EM-/nonparametric-based multi-sense methods, which are natural baselines for polysemy. Additionally, ablations isolating the effect of ELK vs. other energies for mixtures, and comparisons to full-covariance variants, are missing.
- Ambiguity in KL computation for mixtures: The paper states KL for mixtures lacks a closed form and then uses “minimum KL divergence” for entailment. It appears this is computed between Gaussian components (min over component pairs), not the full mixtures, but this is not clearly specified. The choice of min over components (vs. weighted or other aggregations) for entailment should be justified and its impact analyzed.
- Evaluation scope: Intrinsic similarity and a single lexical entailment dataset are informative but limited. No downstream tasks (e.g., tagging, QA, NLI) or standard hypernym detection benchmarks are included. Sense-aware evaluation on SCWS is mentioned but detailed results are not clearly reported in the excerpt (metrics like AveSim/MaxSim are referenced but not comprehensively presented).
- Mixture modeling choices underexplained: The paper mostly fixes K=2, with anecdotal K=3 examples. There is limited analysis of sensitivity to K, how mixture weights correlate with sense frequency, or component identifiability. The selection of maximum similarity across components at evaluation time can overemphasize rare senses and ignores mixture weights; this trade-off is not thoroughly discussed.
- Expressiveness vs. efficiency trade-offs: The model restricts covariances to diagonal/spherical for tractability. While practical, this limits modeling of correlations in latent semantics. Claims about “heavy-tailed uncertainty” are not precise for Gaussian mixtures; mixtures can approximate heavier tails, but this should be clarified.

Questions to Authors
- KL for entailment: How exactly is the “minimum KL divergence” computed for mixtures? Is it min over component-pair KLs between Gaussians? If so, why choose min rather than a mixture-weighted or other aggregation? Please report sensitivity of entailment metrics to this choice.
- Baselines: Can you include comparisons to other multi-sense methods (e.g., Neelakantan et al., Reisinger & Mooney) and report SCWS with standard MaxSim and AveSim variants for a more complete picture?
- Sensitivity to K and mixture weights: How do results change with K=1,2,3,4? Do mixture weights align with sense frequency in corpora? Are components stable across random seeds?
- Energy function ablations: How does ELK compare to alternative symmetric energies or to approximations of KL in training (e.g., upper bounds) for mixtures? Any performance changes if the training loss uses cosine or Euclidean distance directly on means?
- Practicality: What are the exact training time, memory footprint, and parameter counts for the reported large-scale run (vocab size, K, D)? How does increasing K affect runtime and memory?
- Regularization: Beyond the implicit -log det term in ELK, did you use any explicit regularizers or variance floors/ceilings? How do these choices affect variance inflation/collapse and performance?
