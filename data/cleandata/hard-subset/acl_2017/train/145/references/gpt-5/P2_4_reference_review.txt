Summary
The paper proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm), rather than a single point vector or a single Gaussian, and training these probabilistic embeddings with a max-margin, energy-based objective. The key technical choice is an energy function given by the expected likelihood kernel (an L2 inner product between distributions), which has a closed-form for Gaussian mixtures, making training tractable. The model is trained at scale on UKWAC+Wackypedia and evaluated on intrinsic benchmarks (word similarity/relatedness, SCWS-like polysemy similarity, and lexical entailment). Qualitative examples show distinct senses captured in different mixture components.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A scalable probabilistic multi-prototype embedding model: Gaussian mixture word embeddings trained with an analytic expected-likelihood energy and max-margin objective, enabling efficient learning while capturing polysemy and uncertainty.
2) A practical training recipe for stable, large-scale learning of mixture embeddings: diagonal/spherical covariances, log-parameterization, log-sum-exp stabilization, subsampling/negative sampling, and separate input/output distributions.
3) Intrinsic evaluations indicating competitive or improved performance over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on several similarity datasets and improved asymmetry-aware entailment scores via a componentwise KL heuristic.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance with tractable formulation: Moving from unimodal Gaussians (Vilnis & McCallum) and deterministic multi-sense embeddings (e.g., Huang, Tian) to Gaussian mixtures is a natural way to model polysemy. Using the expected likelihood kernel yields closed-form energies even for mixtures, which is a neat and sound technical choice that avoids intractable mixture KL.
- Solid engineering for stability and scalability: The paper provides concrete, sensible details (diagonal/spherical covariances, positivity constraints via log-parameters, softmax mixture weights, epsilon-stabilization, log-sum-exp trick, subsampling and 3/4 negative sampling, Adagrad, separate input/output distributions). These choices make the approach reproducible and plausibly scalable; training on billions of tokens with K=2 seems feasible (K^2=4 pairwise terms).
- Empirical evidence of capturing polysemy: The nearest-neighbor analyses convincingly show sense separation (e.g., rock→{stone/basalt} vs {funk/punk}; bank→{river} vs {finance}; Apple→{fruit} vs {computers}). The reported reduction in variance per sense is consistent with the intuition that mixtures avoid the “overly diffuse variance” problem of unimodal Gaussians.
- Competitive to improved intrinsic results: On multiple similarity datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, RW), w2gm often outperforms skip-gram and the unimodal Gaussian baseline; on WS-353 it also surpasses Huang et al. The entailment experiment shows that incorporating asymmetry via a per-component KL improves AP/F1 and that w2gm > w2g, suggesting the mixture better captures inclusion relations.
- Reproducibility signals: Many hyperparameters and implementation choices are disclosed. The plan to release code is positive.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to prior art: Multi-prototype embeddings (Huang; Tian) and probabilistic word embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum) are established. This work’s primary novelty is combining mixtures with an analytically tractable energy. That’s meaningful but not a fundamentally new learning paradigm. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning vs. multi-sense skip-gram-style EM methods and other probabilistic variants (e.g., showing advantages beyond qualitative polysemy).
- Evaluation scope and baselines: The evaluation is mostly intrinsic. There are no downstream tasks (e.g., tagging, NER, QA, entailment classification beyond the lexical dataset) where uncertainty might matter. Baselines omit strong contemporaries like GloVe in comparable conditions and more recent multi-sense methods; dimensionality is relatively low (D=50), and fairness of comparison to reported baselines (which may use 200–300 dims) is unclear. Without controlled, state-of-the-art baselines and extrinsic tasks, the significance is harder to assess.
- Metric selection and potential cherry-picking: The paper reports results for several similarity metrics (expected likelihood, max cosine, min Euclidean) and highlights the best metric per dataset/model. This complicates fair comparison to baselines that use a fixed metric and risks overfitting to evaluation metrics. Statistical significance is not reported, and development/tuning protocols for choosing metrics are not specified.
- Entailment method is heuristic: Since mixture KL is intractable, the paper uses minimum pairwise Gaussian KL over components. This is reasonable but ad hoc. Alternatives (e.g., expected KL under mixture weights, variational bounds, asymmetric kernels) are not explored, and ablations are limited. More justification and analysis of asymmetry and directionality would strengthen claims.
- Expressivity vs. practice: Although the model allows full covariances, experiments appear to use spherical/diagonal covariances, limiting expressivity. The benefits of learned covariances (vs. point multi-sense embeddings) are not deeply analyzed. Moreover, sense selection at inference is via a max over component pairs; there is no context-conditioned sense disambiguation module, so the model remains context-agnostic at test time.
- Presentation/anonymity issues: References are messy with duplicated names and incomplete entries; table numbering and cross-references are inconsistent; some results (e.g., SCWS) are mentioned without complete tables. The “anonymous interactive visualization” link via a public IP is risky per anonymity policies.

Questions to authors
- Can you commit to a single, pre-specified similarity metric for evaluation (or a fixed protocol) and report all results under that metric, including statistical significance? If not, provide a development set and justify metric selection without peeking at test labels.
- How does performance scale with dimensionality (e.g., D=100/300) and with the number of components K? Please include systematic ablations (K=1,2,3) across tasks and report mixture weight distributions (do multiple components get used broadly?).
- What covariance structure was ultimately used (spherical vs. diagonal) in the main results? How sensitive are results to this choice? Any gains from diagonal over spherical?
- For entailment, please detail the exact KL computation (directionality, min over i,j, weighting), compare to reverse KL, and consider asymmetric alternatives to the expected likelihood kernel.
- Provide controlled comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., GloVe, multi-sense skip-gram) trained on the same data with similar dimensionality. Any extrinsic evaluations where uncertainty helps?
- The visualization link may violate anonymity; can you remove or fully anonymize it for camera-ready?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, technically sound, and practically detailed submission with promising qualitative and quantitative results, but the novelty is moderate and the evaluation could be stronger and fairer.
