- Proposes representing each word as a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) to capture multiple senses of polysemous words.
- Introduces a max-margin energy-based training objective using an expected likelihood kernel that is analytically tractable for GMMs.
- Demonstrates that the w2gm model can automatically discover word senses and outperforms unimodal Gaussian embeddings and word2vec on similarity and entailment tasks.
- Main contribution: multimodal probabilistic word embeddings using Gaussian mixtures to model multiple meanings.
- Main contribution: a practical, analytically tractable training procedure based on the expected likelihood kernel.
- Main contribution: empirical validation showing improved quantitative and qualitative performance on standard benchmarks.
- Strength: clearly identifies and addresses the limitation of unimodal probabilistic embeddings for polysemy with convincing examples.
- Strength: strong empirical results, with consistent improvements across multiple word similarity and entailment datasets.
- Strength: effective qualitative validation (examples and visualization) showing distinct learned sense components.
- Strength: technically sound derivation of the expected likelihood kernel and sufficient implementation details for reproducibility.
- Weakness: novelty claim overstated—prior multi-embedding works (Huang et al., Tian et al.) are not adequately acknowledged or contrasted.
- Weakness: lacks comparison to recent state-of-the-art contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT, ELMo), limiting relevance to current research.
- Weakness: insufficient ablation study on the number of mixture components (K), with K=2 used by default and minimal analysis of alternatives.
- Weakness: no computational complexity or training-time analysis to substantiate scalability claims.
- Question: how does performance vary with different numbers of Gaussian components (K=3,4, etc.), and why was K=2 chosen?
- Question: why were contextualized embeddings not included in comparisons, and how might the approach integrate with them?
- Question: request for detailed computational complexity and training time comparisons with standard methods like word2vec.
- Question: ask for quantitative evidence showing reduced variance for polysemous words (e.g., variance values).
- Overall: meaningful and well-validated contribution but needs revisions to address novelty, broader comparisons, and computational analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
