

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a Gaussian mixture model (w2gm) for word embeddings that captures multiple word senses through a multimodal distribution, as opposed to the unimodal Gaussian distribution used in prior work (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014). The authors claim their model is the first to capture multiple meanings in a probabilistic word embedding framework, using an expected likelihood kernel for training with a maximum margin objective. They demonstrate qualitative and quantitative improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. A Gaussian mixture model for word embeddings that can represent multiple word senses (with two components shown to be sufficient for most words)
2. An energy-based training objective using expected likelihood kernel with analytic tractability for Gaussian mixtures
3. Empirical evidence that their model outperforms prior probabilistic embeddings on word similarity and entailment tasks

## Strengths

1. **Clear Motivation for Multimodal Representations**: The paper effectively identifies the limitation of unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014) for polysemous words, where the variance becomes unnecessarily large to accommodate multiple meanings. The authors clearly articulate the problem and provide a compelling qualitative example (rock having separate components for stone and music).

2. **Technical Novelty in Energy Function**: The use of expected likelihood kernel for Gaussian mixtures as an energy function is a technically sound choice that maintains analytic tractability. The derivation in Section A.1 is thorough and well-explained, which is important for reproducibility.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation across multiple standard word similarity datasets (SimLex, WS, MEN, MC, RG, etc.) and demonstrates consistent improvements over both word2vec and the unimodal Gaussian embedding baseline. The qualitative examples (Table 1) effectively show how the model captures multiple word senses.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inaccurate Claim of Novelty**: The paper states "to the best of our knowledge, the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings." This is incorrect, as Huang et al. (2012) proposed a method using cluster centroids of context vectors to represent multiple word senses, and Tian et al. (2014) used an adapted skip-gram model with EM to learn multiple latent representations per word. The paper acknowledges these works but fails to clearly distinguish their approach from these prior methods. The authors' approach is a probabilistic extension of the multiple-prototype concept, not the first to capture multiple meanings.

2. **Technical Inaccuracy in KL Divergence Claim**: In Section 3.4, the paper states "unlike the expected likelihood kernel, KL divergence does not have a closed form if the two distributions are Gaussian mixtures." This is technically incorrect. KL divergence for two Gaussian mixtures doesn't have a simple closed form, but it's not that it "does not have a closed form" - it's just that the closed form is complex and requires numerical integration. This technical inaccuracy undermines the paper's credibility.

3. **Inconsistent Notation and Terminology**: The paper uses "w2g" to refer to both their implementation of a unimodal Gaussian embedding (with one mixture component) and the original Vilnis and McCallum model. This creates significant confusion. For example, they write "w2g refers to our implementation of w2gm model with one mixture component" but then use "w2g" to compare against their own unimodal model in Table 2. This inconsistent notation makes it difficult to follow the comparisons.

4. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper claims "our multi-prototype model substantially outperforms the model of Huang et al. (2012) on the WordSim-353 dataset" but provides no statistical significance test to support this claim. Without a significance test, it's impossible to determine if the reported improvements are meaningful or just due to random variation in the data.

5. **Unjustified Assumption of Diagonal Covariance**: The paper simplifies the covariance matrices to be diagonal for computational efficiency (Section A.2), but fails to justify this assumption. A diagonal covariance matrix implies independence between dimensions, which is unlikely to be true for semantic features. The paper should have either:
   - Provided evidence that the diagonal assumption doesn't significantly impact performance
   - Discussed the trade-offs of this simplification
   - Compared results with full covariance matrices

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify how your approach differs from Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) in terms of both methodology and theoretical guarantees? What specific aspect of your probabilistic Gaussian mixture model is novel compared to their multiple-deterministic-embeddings approach?

2. You state that "KL divergence does not have a closed form if the two distributions are Gaussian mixtures" but this is technically incorrect. What specific form of KL divergence were you referring to, and what is the basis for your claim that it doesn't have a closed form?

3. You claim your model is "capable of training on a corpus with billions of words in days" but don't provide any evidence of training time or computational resources used. Could you provide the training time for your experiments on the 3.5 billion token corpus?

4. The paper uses "w2g" for both your unimodal model and the original Vilnis and McCallum model, which creates significant confusion. How did you decide on this notation, and why not use different names for the two models?

5. You mention that "for words with more than 2 meanings, our model with K=3 does not generally offer performance differences to our model with K=2" but provide no analysis of why this is the case. Could you provide more insight into the relationship between K and performance, especially for words with more than two meanings?

## Additional Comments

1. There are numerous formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of subscripts, inconsistent use of vector notation with arrows, inconsistent spacing in equations).

2. In Table 2, the header "sg* w2g* w2g/mc w2g/el w2g/me w2gm/mc w2gm/el w2gm/me" has inconsistent formatting. The asterisks should be either consistently used or removed.

3. The paper claims "our model is able to automatically discover multiple meanings for words" but only provides a few qualitative examples in Table 1. A more comprehensive analysis of how the model discovers multiple meanings across a larger set of words would strengthen this claim.

4. The paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of how the mixture weights (p_w,i) are learned or how they relate to the semantic content of the components.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting extension to probabilistic word embeddings with a clear motivation, the numerous technical inaccuracies, inconsistent notation, and flawed claims of novelty significantly undermine the contribution. The paper's claim that it is "the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings" is demonstrably false given prior work by Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014), and the technical inaccuracy regarding KL divergence further erodes confidence in the paper's technical content. The formatting inconsistencies and lack of statistical significance testing also contribute to the paper's weakness. These issues are substantial enough that they overshadow the paper's methodological contributions.
