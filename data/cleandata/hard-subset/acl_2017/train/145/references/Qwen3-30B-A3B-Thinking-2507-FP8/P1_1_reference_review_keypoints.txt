- Proposes w2gm: representing words as Gaussian mixture models to capture multiple senses.  
- Uses an expected likelihood kernel energy function for analytic tractability and practical training.  
- Reports empirical gains over point and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on similarity and entailment tasks, with qualitative sense separation.  
- Addresses the limitation of unimodal embeddings for polysemy; qualitative examples (e.g., "rock", "bank") are convincing.  
- Method is well-motivated with clear theoretical derivation and a skip-gram/max-margin training procedure.  
- Strong empirical evaluation across benchmarks and qualitative visualizations show meaningful multi-sense learning.  
- Novelty claim is overstated; prior multi-prototype work (Huang et al. 2012, Tian et al. 2014) is not fully acknowledged.  
- Lacks comprehensive experimental comparison to other multi-prototype methods beyond limited WordSim-353 results.  
- No analysis of computational complexity, training time, or memory versus single-prototype methods like word2vec.  
- Limited exploration of number of mixture components K (mainly K=2; brief K=3 mention) and no guidance on choosing K.  
- No failure-case analysis for words with >2 meanings, rare multi-meaning words, or closely related senses.  
- Question: How does this method differ from and outperform Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014)?  
- Question: Provide details on the observation that K=3 offers little benefit and how K should be chosen.  
- Question: How does training time and memory scale compared to word2vec and other single-prototype models?  
- Question: Provide examples of words where the model fails and discuss its limitations.
