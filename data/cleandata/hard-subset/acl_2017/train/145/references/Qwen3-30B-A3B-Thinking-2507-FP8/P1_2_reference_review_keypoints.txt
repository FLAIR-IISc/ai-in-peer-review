- Proposes multimodal word embeddings using Gaussian mixture models to represent polysemous words.
- Claims three contributions: GMM for multiple meanings, an expected likelihood kernel for analytic tractability, and empirical gains on similarity/entailment tasks.
- Strong motivation: unimodal embeddings fail to capture polysemy and GMMs are a theoretically sound remedy.
- Empirical/qualitative results show GMM learns distinct senses (e.g., rock=stone/music) and outperforms word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings, notably on SCWS.
- Provides practical implementation details (diagonal covariances, numerical stability, initialization) enabling scalable training on large corpora.
- Limited novelty: approach is a natural extension of prior multiple-prototype methods (Huang 2012, Tian 2014) without clear differentiation.
- Missing comparison to contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT), leaving unclear relevance against current state-of-the-art for polysemy.
- Insufficient justification for choosing Gaussian mixtures over other multimodal or nonparametric representations; analytic tractability claim not fully compared.
- Vague computational claims: “billions of words in days” lacks concrete training time metrics on the described hardware.
- Asks how the GMM compares to contextualized models and whether it’s complementary or an alternative.
- Requests specific training time metrics on UKWAC and Wikipedia with the given hardware and comparisons to word2vec.
- Asks for counts of vocabulary words with three or more meanings and why K=3 gives little improvement.
- Overall assessment: solid, well-motivated contribution with compelling results and practical utility, but novelty and modern-baseline comparisons need strengthening.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to better differentiate from prior work and include comparisons to contextualized embeddings.
