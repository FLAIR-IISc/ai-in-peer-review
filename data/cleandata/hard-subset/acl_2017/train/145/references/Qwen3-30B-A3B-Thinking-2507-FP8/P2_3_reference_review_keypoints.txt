- Proposes multimodal probabilistic word embeddings where each word is a Gaussian mixture to capture multiple senses.
- Main contributions: novel multimodal probabilistic representation, expected-likelihood-kernel + max-margin training procedure, and comprehensive empirical improvements.
- Strength: Conceptual novelty—multimodal GMs address polysemy and avoid inflated variance in unimodal Gaussians.
- Strength: Practical implementation and scalability—diagonal covariances, numerical-stability fixes, and training on 3.5B tokens in days.
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation—multiple datasets, strong baselines, and qualitative examples showing distinct sense components.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—multi-prototype representations predate this work and the claim of being first probabilistic multisense is questionable.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of mixture components—no explanation for K=3 vs K=2 behavior and unclear mapping of components to true senses.
- Weakness: Weak evidence for “variance reduction” claim—demonstrated with a single example rather than systematic analysis.
- Weakness: Limited comparison to recent advances—omits contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT) and newer multi-prototype methods.
- Question: How does the probabilistic Gaussian mixture fundamentally differ from deterministic multi-prototype approaches and what specific advantages does it provide?
- Question: Can you provide systematic evidence of variance reduction across words and link it to performance gains?
- Question: Why does K=3 not generally outperform K=2—because most words have two senses or due to learning issues?
- Overall assessment: Well-motivated, scalable, and promising but somewhat incremental due to limited novelty and insufficient analysis.
- Recommendation: Weak Accept — meaningful contribution but needs stronger justification, broader comparisons, and deeper analysis.
