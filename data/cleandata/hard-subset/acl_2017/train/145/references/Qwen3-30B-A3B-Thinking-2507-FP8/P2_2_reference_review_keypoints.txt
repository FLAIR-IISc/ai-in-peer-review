- Proposes Gaussian Mixture (GM) word embeddings to model polysemy with multiple Gaussian components per word.  
- Contributions: novel multimodal embedding, energy-based training using the expected likelihood kernel, and comprehensive empirical gains on word similarity benchmarks.  
- Addresses a key limitation of single-vector/unimodal models by learning distinct components for different senses (e.g., "rock" → stone vs. jazz).  
- Strong empirical validation: achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple word similarity datasets, outperforming unimodal Gaussians and word2vec.  
- Solid theoretical foundation with a clear derivation of the expected likelihood kernel for analytic tractability with mixtures.  
- Practical and reproducible: provides detailed implementation notes on numerical stability, initialization, and training.  
- Novelty claim overstated: prior multi-prototype and probabilistic polysemy methods (e.g., Huang et al., Nalisnick & Ravi) are not sufficiently differentiated.  
- Limited analysis of mixture components: no systematic study linking components or weights to actual word senses or context distributions.  
- Inadequate comparison to recent multi-prototype/state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Chen et al., 2014), weakening claims of superiority.  
- Scalability claim unsubstantiated: asserts training on billions of words in days without runtime or complexity comparisons to word2vec/GloVe.  
- Question: provide systematic analysis showing how mixture components map to real word senses in large-scale corpora.  
- Question: clarify methodological and performance differences from other probabilistic polysemy approaches (e.g., Nalisnick & Ravi 2015).  
- Question: supply training time, computational complexity, and empirical comparisons to other embedding methods to support scalability.  
- Question: explain sensitivity to number of components K, report performance for different K values, and justify choice of K=2.  
- Overall: compelling, well-executed approach that improves polysemy modeling and yields strong results, but novelty is incremental and needs more comparison/analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
