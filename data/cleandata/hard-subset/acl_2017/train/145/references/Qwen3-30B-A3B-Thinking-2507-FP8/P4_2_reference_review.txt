

# Review for ACL Submission

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a multimodal word embedding approach using Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) to represent words with multiple distinct meanings (polysemy), as opposed to standard point embeddings (e.g., word2vec) or unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014). The authors model each word as a mixture of K Gaussian distributions, where each component represents a distinct meaning. They introduce an energy-based training objective using the expected likelihood kernel as an energy function, enabling analytic tractability. The model is trained on large corpora (UKWAC and Wackypedia) and evaluated on word similarity and entailment tasks.

## Main Contributions
1. **A multimodal word embedding model using Gaussian mixture distributions** that can capture multiple distinct meanings of polysemous words through multiple Gaussian components.
2. **A novel energy function (expected likelihood kernel) for training** that enables analytic tractability with Gaussian mixtures, making the approach scalable to large corpora.
3. **Demonstrated improvements in word similarity and entailment tasks** compared to previous unimodal approaches (Gaussian embeddings) and point embeddings (word2vec).

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation:** The paper effectively identifies the limitations of existing unimodal word embedding approaches for handling polysemy. The authors clearly articulate that polysemous words need to be represented by multiple modes, which is a significant limitation of previous work. The qualitative examples in Table 1 compellingly demonstrate how their model captures multiple meanings for words like "rock" and "bank" versus unimodal approaches.

2. **Technical soundness of the proposed model:** The Gaussian mixture model is well-motivated and the energy function (expected likelihood kernel) is carefully derived. The paper provides a detailed derivation of how to compute the energy function for Gaussian mixtures, which is crucial for the model's feasibility. The authors also provide practical implementation details (e.g., using diagonal covariance matrices, numerical stability tricks) that make the approach scalable and robust.

3. **Strong empirical results:** The paper demonstrates clear improvements over previous methods on multiple standard word similarity datasets (WS, WS-S, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MTurk, RW) and word entailment tasks. The results are consistent across different evaluation metrics (maximum cosine similarity, expected likelihood kernel, minimum Euclidean distance), and the improvements are significant enough to be convincing. The model outperforms the Gaussian embedding baseline (w2g) and other multi-prototype approaches on most datasets.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty compared to prior multi-prototype approaches:** The paper claims to be the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings, but Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) already proposed multi-prototype approaches using different methods (Huang uses cluster centroids of context vectors, Tian uses an adapted skip-gram with EM). The paper acknowledges these works but doesn't clearly explain how the Gaussian mixture approach is fundamentally different or better. The authors state "we propose, to the best of our knowledge, the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings," but this is not accurate given the prior work.

2. **Limited discussion of model limitations:** The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, they mention trying K=3 but don't show significant improvements, yet they don't explain why K=2 is sufficient for most words or discuss failure cases where the model might not capture multiple meanings. The paper also doesn't address how the model handles words with more than 2 distinct meanings.

3. **Insufficient comparison with recent approaches:** The paper doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art word embedding methods, particularly contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT, ELMo). While the paper focuses on non-contextual embeddings, it would be valuable to compare with other non-contextual multi-prototype approaches or recent probabilistic approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings, but Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) already proposed multi-prototype approaches. Can the authors clarify how their Gaussian mixture approach is fundamentally different from these prior works, especially in terms of the probabilistic formulation and training procedure?

2. The paper mentions that they tried K=3 but didn't show significant improvements. Could the authors provide more details on why K=2 is sufficient for most words, and whether there are specific words or contexts where K>2 would be beneficial?

3. The paper claims their model can be trained on a corpus with billions of words in days. Could the authors provide more details on the computational resources required (e.g., number of GPUs, total training time) and how the training time scales with the corpus size and number of mixture components?

4. The paper uses a maximum margin ranking objective but doesn't provide a theoretical justification for why this is better than other possible objectives (e.g., maximum likelihood). Could the authors elaborate on this?

5. The paper states that "the model is able to automatically discover multiple meanings for words" (Section 4.2), but doesn't provide a quantitative measure of how well the model discovers the correct number of meanings for words. Could the authors provide a metric for the accuracy of the learned number of components per word?
