

# ACL Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a Gaussian mixture (GM) model for word embeddings that captures multiple meanings of words through multimodal distributions. The authors claim to be the first probabilistic word embedding model that can capture multiple meanings, using a mixture of Gaussians with an expected likelihood kernel for training. The main contributions are:
1. A novel Gaussian mixture model for word embeddings that can represent multiple word senses
2. An energy-based max-margin training objective with the expected likelihood kernel
3. Demonstrated improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks compared to previous methods

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to polysemy**: The paper proposes a genuinely novel approach to modeling word polysemy using Gaussian mixtures, which is a meaningful extension beyond the unimodal Gaussian embeddings of Vilnis and McCallum (2014). The qualitative results in Table 1 clearly show that the model learns multiple distinct meanings for polysemous words.

2. **Theoretical foundation**: The use of the expected likelihood kernel for Gaussian mixtures is theoretically sound and provides a principled way to measure similarity between distributions. The derivation in Section 3.4 and Appendix A.1 is mathematically correct.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper evaluates on multiple standard word similarity datasets (WS, WS-S, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MTurk, RW) and shows consistent improvements over baselines on many of these datasets. The qualitative evaluation demonstrating multiple meaning components is particularly strong.

## Weaknesses

1. **Major methodological inconsistency in the main text**: The paper claims to use a full covariance matrix for the Gaussian components, but the implementation details (Section A.2) state they use a diagonal covariance matrix for computational efficiency. This is a critical simplification that significantly reduces the expressiveness of the model (from O(D^2) to O(D) parameters per component), yet this key detail is only mentioned in the supplementary material. This is a serious omission that undermines the claimed generality of the approach and misleads readers about the model's actual complexity.

2. **Overstated performance claims**: The paper claims "significant" improvements over baselines, but the actual numerical improvements are small and lack statistical significance testing. For example, on the SimLex dataset, their best model (w2gm/mc) scores 29.31 compared to word2vec's 29.39 (a difference of 0.08 points), which is negligible. On WordSim-353, the improvement over Huang et al. (2012) is 73.5 vs. 71.3 (a 2.2 point difference), which is modest. The paper should provide statistical significance tests to support their "significant" claims.

3. **Lack of scalability evidence**: The paper claims their model is "robust, straightforward, and scalable, capable of training on a corpus with billions of words in days." However, they provide no runtime or resource usage information to support this claim. They mention training on UKWAC (2.5 billion tokens) and Wackypedia (1 billion tokens), but don't state how long the training took or what computational resources were used. Without this information, the scalability claim is unsubstantiated.

4. **Inconsistent notation and mathematical errors**: 
   - In equation (3), they write "ξi,j ≡ logN(0; μ_f,i − μ_g,j, Σ_f,i + Σ_g,j)", but this is mathematically confusing. N(0; μ, Σ) typically denotes a Gaussian distribution with mean 0, but here it should be the Gaussian density evaluated at 0, which is N(0; μ, Σ) = (2π)^{-D/2} |Σ|^{-1/2} exp(-1/2 μ^T Σ^{-1} μ). The notation is incorrect and misleading.
   - The paper inconsistently uses index i for both distributions in the main text (f(x) = ∑_{i=1}^K piN(x; μ_f,i, Σ_f,i) and g(x) = ∑_{i=1}^K qiN(x; μ_g,i, Σ_g,i)), but in the supplementary material (A.1), they correctly use i for f and j for g. This inconsistency creates confusion about the model's structure.

5. **Inadequate comparison with Huang et al. (2012)**: The paper claims to be "the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings," but doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from Huang et al. (2012), which uses multiple deterministic embeddings. The paper should explicitly state how their probabilistic approach differs from and improves upon Huang's method, rather than simply citing it as an alternative.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you use a diagonal covariance matrix (Section A.2) instead of a full covariance matrix as described in the main text? How does this choice affect the model's ability to capture word polysemy?

2. For the word similarity improvements claimed in Table 2 (e.g., 73.47 vs. 65.49 on WS), can you provide statistical significance tests to support the claim that these differences are meaningful?

3. Can you provide the training time and computational resources used for training on the UKWAC and Wackypedia corpora to substantiate your scalability claims?

4. In Table 1, you show nearest neighbors for "rock 0" and "rock 1" but don't specify which similarity metric was used to determine these neighbors. Was it maximum cosine similarity, expected likelihood kernel, or minimum Euclidean distance?

## Additional Comments

- The bibliography has multiple formatting errors (e.g., "Vilnis and McCallum.,? 2014" should be "Vilnis and McCallum, 2014").
- The reference to "Figure 1" is included in the text, but the figure is not provided in the paper.
- The paper claims "we retain scalability and analytic tractability," but the expected likelihood kernel for Gaussian mixtures has O(K^2) complexity where K is the number of mixture components, which becomes expensive for larger K. The paper should discuss the computational complexity implications.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these flaws significant enough to warrant rejection. The paper presents an interesting idea with some strong qualitative results, but the methodological inconsistencies, unsubstantiated claims, and lack of key implementation details undermine the paper's credibility and contribution. The diagonal covariance approximation is a major simplification that should have been clearly stated in the main text, not hidden in the supplementary material. The performance improvements are overstated without statistical significance testing, and the scalability claims are unsupported by evidence. These issues collectively prevent me from recommending acceptance.
