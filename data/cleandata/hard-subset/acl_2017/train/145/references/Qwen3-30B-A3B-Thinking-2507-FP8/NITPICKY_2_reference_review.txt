

# Review of "Word to Gaussian Mixture: A Probabilistic Model for Multiple Word Senses"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Gaussian mixture model for word embeddings that can represent multiple word senses (polysemy) through multimodal distributions. The authors claim their model outperforms existing unimodal approaches on word similarity and entailment tasks by capturing multiple meanings per word. The paper is well-structured and presents a logical extension of Vilnis and McCallum's (2014) Gaussian embedding approach.

## Main Contributions
1. A Gaussian mixture model for word embeddings that can represent multiple word senses through multimodal distributions
2. An energy-based maximum margin training objective using the expected likelihood kernel for Gaussian mixtures
3. Practical implementation details for numerical stability and initialization

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a clear motivation for multimodal word representations, correctly identifying the limitations of unimodal Gaussian embeddings for polysemous words.

2. The qualitative evaluation in Table 1 effectively demonstrates the model's ability to learn distinct components for different word senses (e.g., rock:0 for stone meaning, rock:1 for music meaning).

3. The supplementary material provides sufficient technical details about the implementation, including practical considerations for numerical stability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent notation throughout the paper**: The authors use inconsistent notation for vector variables, sometimes including vector arrows (e.g., "µ⃗w,i") and sometimes omitting them (e.g., "µw,i"). This inconsistency appears in multiple places, including equation (1), the main text, and the supplementary material. For example, in equation (1), they write "µ⃗w,i" with a vector arrow but later refer to "µw,i" without it. This inconsistency makes the paper harder to read and appears unprofessional for an ACL submission.

2. **Formatting and typographical errors**: Multiple formatting issues in equations and text:
   - In equation (1), the normalization term is written as "√ 2π|Σw,i|" which should be "1/√(2π|Σw,i|)" or "1/√(2π|Σw,i|)" for the Gaussian normalization term.
   - In Table 2, the authors use inconsistent notation for "w2g/mc" and "w2g/el" which should be standardized.
   - In the supplementary material, they have "the the" in "to generate the the context words."
   - In the references, the citation for TensorFlow is formatted inconsistently with the rest of the references.

3. **Unjustified simplification**: The authors use a diagonal covariance matrix for computational efficiency but provide no justification for this choice. A full covariance matrix would capture correlations between dimensions, which could be important for semantic relationships. The authors should either justify why a diagonal covariance is sufficient or discuss the limitations of this simplification. This is a significant methodological detail that deserves explanation.

4. **Inconsistent claims about prior work**: The authors claim "to the best of our knowledge, the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings" but fail to properly acknowledge Huang et al. (2012), who already proposed a method for multiple representations (via cluster centroids of context vectors). The related work section mentions Huang et al. (2012) but does not clearly state that their approach already addressed the problem of multiple representations, which undermines the novelty claim.

5. **Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper compares to word2vec and Gaussian embeddings but does not compare to the best existing methods for word similarity and entailment. For example, in Table 2, they don't compare to GloVe, ELMo, or BERT, which are the current state-of-the-art methods for word similarity. The comparison is limited to older methods, making the performance claims less impressive.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you choose to use a diagonal covariance matrix instead of a full covariance matrix? What are the theoretical or practical justifications for this choice? How does this affect the model's ability to capture semantic relationships between words?

2. In Table 1, the authors use "rock 0" and "rock 1" but the text consistently uses "rock:0" and "rock:1" notation. Please be consistent throughout the paper.

3. The authors claim "the cosine similarity between rock and stone for the Gaussian representation of Vilnis and McCallum (2014) is only 0.029." Can you provide the specific values or methodology used to obtain this score? This seems unusually low for a Gaussian embedding of these words.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting extension of Gaussian embeddings to model polysemy, the numerous minor issues with notation, formatting, and justification of methodological choices significantly detract from the paper's quality. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these inconsistencies indicate a lack of attention to detail that is expected in ACL submissions. The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading and clarification of the methodological choices before resubmission.

The paper's claims about novelty are also weakened by insufficient acknowledgment of prior work on multiple word representations. The lack of comparison to current state-of-the-art methods for word similarity and entailment further diminishes the significance of the reported results. These issues collectively justify rejection, even though the core idea has merit.
