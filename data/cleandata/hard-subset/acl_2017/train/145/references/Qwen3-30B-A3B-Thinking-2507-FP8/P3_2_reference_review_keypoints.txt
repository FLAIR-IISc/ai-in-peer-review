- Proposal: Represent words as Gaussian mixtures (w2gm) to better capture polysemy than unimodal Gaussians or point vectors.  
- Energy function: Use an expected likelihood kernel with a closed-form energy enabling tractable training of Gaussian mixture embeddings.  
- Empirical validation: w2gm shows improvements over unimodal Gaussian embeddings and word2vec on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.  

- Strength: Clearly motivated—unimodal Gaussians pull means between senses, while mixtures can separate distinct meanings (supported by qualitative examples).  
- Strength: Technical contribution—the closed-form expected likelihood kernel addresses the intractability of using KL divergence on mixtures.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results—consistent gains on datasets like WS-R, MEN, MC and in entailment tasks.  

- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to prior multimodal approaches (Huang et al. 2012, Tian et al. 2014); novelty claim is overstated and comparisons are superficial.  
- Weakness: Limited analysis of component interpretability—no quantitative WSD evaluation or systematic coherence analysis, unclear handling of words with >2 senses.  
- Weakness: Scalability claims are unsupported—no concrete training times reported for the large corpora cited.  
- Weakness: No theoretical justification for choosing the expected likelihood kernel beyond its closed-form convenience.  

- Q: How does w2gm differ from Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) beyond the energy function—please provide thorough comparisons?  
- Q: Can you provide quantitative evaluation of component interpretability (e.g., WSD or neighbor coherence)?  
- Q: How does your method compare to Huang et al. when both are trained on the same corpus without external resources?  
- Q: How does performance scale with K (number of components) and what guidelines determine K for different tasks?  

- Conclusion: Valuable methodological contribution with convincing qualitative and empirical evidence, but needs clearer novelty claims, stronger comparisons to prior multimodal work, and better evidence for scalability and theoretical justification.
