- Summary: Proposes w2gm—representing words as Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy—trained with a max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel; shows qualitative sense separation and improved word similarity/entailment results.

- Contribution 1: Introduces multimodal word representations (Gaussian mixtures) to capture multiple meanings per word without external resources.

- Contribution 2: Uses the expected likelihood kernel as an analytically tractable energy function for Gaussian mixtures.

- Contribution 3: Provides empirical validation showing qualitative sense separation and quantitative gains on similarity and entailment benchmarks.

- Strength 1: Makes a compelling case for multimodal representations with clear qualitative examples separating senses of polysemous words.

- Strength 2: Practical, scalable implementation details (diagonal covariances, numerical stability) and closed-form energy computations.

- Strength 3: Strong empirical improvements across multiple datasets and evaluation metrics.

- Weakness 1: Insufficient novelty justification—insufficient differentiation from prior multi-prototype embedding work (Huang et al., Tian et al.).

- Weakness 2: Lacks comprehensive analysis of how the number of components K affects performance (insufficient K ablation).

- Weakness 3: Limited computational cost analysis—no training time or memory comparisons to baselines.

- Weakness 4: Ambiguous evaluation of "multiple meanings"—no systematic validation against gold-standard sense annotations.

- Weakness 5: Missing ablation studies to isolate contributions (e.g., expected likelihood vs. KL, mixture vs. single Gaussian).

- Question 1: How does this Gaussian mixture approach technically differ from Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014)?

- Question 2: Can you provide performance comparisons for K=2 vs. K=3 on standard datasets?

- Question 3: What are the training time and memory requirements compared to word2vec and w2g, and how does cost scale with vocabulary size?

- Question 4: Is there a principled way to choose the optimal evaluation metric per task given metric variability across datasets?

- Question 5: How would you quantitatively evaluate alignment of learned components with human-annotated word senses?

- Overall recommendation: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address comparisons to prior work, complexity and cost analyses, and more rigorous evaluation of multiple meanings.
