- Proposal: Represent words as Gaussian mixtures (GMMs) to model polysemy.  
- Method: Use an expected likelihood kernel as an analytic, tractable energy function for training mixtures.  
- Empirical claim: Shows quantitative and qualitative improvements on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.  

- Contribution: Introduces multimodal probabilistic word representations capturing multiple senses.  
- Contribution: Provides a closed-form expected likelihood kernel for Gaussian mixtures enabling efficient training.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates empirical gains and visualizations that illustrate polysemy capture.  

- Strength: Correctly identifies and addresses the limitation of unimodal Gaussian embeddings for polysemous words.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results, especially on SCWS entailment and polysemous-word benchmarks.  
- Strength: Theoretical derivation of the expected likelihood kernel is sound and practically useful.  

- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to prior multi-prototype work (e.g., Huang et al. 2012, Tian et al. 2014) and insufficiently positions novelty.  
- Weakness: Limited comparison to current state-of-the-art embeddings (e.g., GloVe, fastText, contextual models like BERT).  
- Weakness: Lacks justification for choosing Gaussian mixtures over other multimodal or nonparametric alternatives.  
- Weakness: Inadequate analysis of computational costs, memory, and training time relative to simpler embeddings.  

- Q: What is the training complexity and per-epoch time on large corpora (e.g., 2.5B tokens) compared to word2vec/GloVe?  
- Q: How does this approach concretely differ from and outperform Huang et al. (2012)?  
- Q: How is the number of mixture components K chosen in practice; were values >2 tried and what was the effect?  
- Q: Why choose the expected likelihood kernel over asymmetric metrics like KL or alternatives like Wasserstein?  

- Overall: Strong idea with compelling results but needs better prior-work comparison, broader baselines, justification of design choices, and computational analysis.  
- Recommendation: Minor Revision — promising contribution contingent on addressing the noted weaknesses.
