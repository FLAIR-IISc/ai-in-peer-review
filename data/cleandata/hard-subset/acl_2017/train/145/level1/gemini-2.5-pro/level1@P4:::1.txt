**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a new method for learning word representations by modeling each word as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). This approach extends prior work on single Gaussian embeddings to explicitly capture polysemy, where each component of the mixture is intended to represent a distinct word sense. The model parameters (component means, covariances, and mixture weights) are learned using an energy-based max-margin objective inspired by the skip-gram model. The authors propose using the Expected Likelihood Kernel (ELK) as the energy function, which measures the similarity between two GMMs and has a convenient closed-form solution. The paper provides both qualitative and quantitative evidence to demonstrate that the learned multimodal distributions can disentangle word senses and shows improved performance over single-vector and single-Gaussian baselines on word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is a novel probabilistic word embedding model that represents words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). This is a well-motivated extension of prior probabilistic methods that directly addresses the limitation of unimodal distributions in capturing polysemy.
2.  The paper introduces a tractable and scalable training framework for these GMM embeddings. The combination of a max-margin objective with the Expected Likelihood Kernel (ELK) as the energy function provides a closed-form objective that can be optimized with stochastic gradient descent on large text corpora.
3.  The work provides strong empirical results, including compelling qualitative examples of learned word senses (e.g., for "rock," "bank") and quantitative improvements on several standard word similarity and word entailment benchmarks.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper effectively identifies a key limitation of existing word embedding methods—their inability to handle polysemy. The proposed GMM representation is a logical and elegant solution to this problem, moving beyond capturing general uncertainty (as in single Gaussian models) to modeling distinct, multimodal semantics. The conceptual leap is significant and well-motivated.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is sound. The choice of the Expected Likelihood Kernel is well-justified as it renders the energy calculation between two GMMs analytically tractable, which is critical for the feasibility of the training procedure. The overall training objective, based on a max-margin criterion, is a standard and robust approach for this type of contrastive learning. The authors also provide important implementation details regarding numerical stability and parameter constraints, which strengthens the technical contribution.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The evaluation is thorough and persuasive. The qualitative analysis in Table 1 and the interactive visualization provide clear, intuitive evidence that the model successfully learns to assign different meanings of a word to different mixture components. This is supported by quantitative gains on a wide range of word similarity datasets and a word entailment task. The improvements over the authors' own implementation of a single-Gaussian model (w2g) effectively isolate the benefits of the multimodal representation.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out with helpful illustrations (Figure 1), the methodology is described systematically, and the experimental results are presented in a clear and understandable manner. This makes the paper's contributions easy to appreciate.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Selection of Component Number (K):** The number of mixture components, K, is a crucial hyperparameter, yet its selection is not adequately addressed. The experiments are conducted almost exclusively with K=2, with a brief mention of K=3. The paper does not provide a systematic analysis of how to set this value, nor does it discuss the implications of using a fixed K for all words in the vocabulary, including those that are unambiguous or have more than K senses. This omission leaves a critical aspect of the model under-explored.
2.  **Inconsistent Use of Model Information in Evaluation:** For the word similarity task, three different evaluation metrics are used, with the "maximum cosine similarity" between component means often yielding the best performance. This metric completely ignores the learned covariances and mixture weights, which are central to the proposed probabilistic representation. The fact that a non-probabilistic metric on a subset of the parameters performs best raises questions about the practical utility of the full GMM representation for this task and whether the primary benefit is simply learning multiple prototype vectors.
3.  **Missing Comparison on a Key Task:** The paper evaluates on the SCWS dataset, which is specifically designed for multi-prototype embeddings, and mentions several related models (Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2014). However, the table containing these results (referenced as Table 4) is missing from the submitted manuscript. A direct and thorough comparison on this benchmark is essential for properly contextualizing the proposed model's performance against other state-of-the-art multi-prototype methods.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you elaborate on the strategy for selecting K, the number of mixture components? Is K fixed for all words? Have you considered methods for adapting K on a per-word basis, and what is the model's behavior when K does not match the true number of senses for a given word?
2.  The maximum cosine similarity metric, which ignores the learned covariances and weights, often performs best on word similarity tasks. Does this imply that the main advantage of your model for these tasks stems from learning multiple mean vectors, rather than the full probabilistic density? Could you provide further analysis on the contribution of the covariance and mixture weight parameters to the performance on these benchmarks?
3.  The results for the SCWS dataset appear to be missing (the paper references a non-existent Table 4). Could you please provide these results and discuss how your model compares to other multi-prototype embedding models on this specialized task?
