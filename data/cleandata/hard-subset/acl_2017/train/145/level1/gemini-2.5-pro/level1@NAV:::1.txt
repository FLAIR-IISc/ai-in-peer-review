This paper proposes representing words as multimodal probability distributions using Gaussian Mixture Models (GMs), a direct extension of prior work on unimodal Gaussian embeddings. The goal is to better capture polysemy (multiple word meanings) and semantic relationships like entailment. The authors introduce a training method based on a max-margin objective, using the expected likelihood kernel as an analytically tractable energy function to measure similarity between word distributions. The model is evaluated on several word similarity and entailment benchmarks.

The primary contribution is the novel representation of words as Gaussian mixtures. This is an intuitive and powerful extension to probabilistic embeddings that directly addresses the key limitation of unimodal models in representing polysemous words. A second, enabling contribution is the use of the expected likelihood kernel, which makes training these complex distributions feasible within the established max-margin framework.

Strengths:
1.  The model is well-motivated and elegant. It logically extends existing work on probabilistic embeddings to address the clear shortcoming of unimodality. The qualitative results in Table 1, showing that different components of words like "rock" and "bank" capture distinct meanings (geology vs. music; geography vs. finance), provide compelling evidence that the model works as intended.
2.  The paper presents a strong and thorough empirical evaluation. The proposed model, w2gm, shows improved performance over both word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on a majority of the standard word similarity datasets (Table 2).
3.  The model demonstrates superior performance on the word entailment task (Table 5). This supports the authors' hypothesis that a more flexible density model, which avoids the artificially large variance of unimodal models for polysemous words, can better capture asymmetric semantic relationships.

Weaknesses:
1.  The choice of the number of components, K, is not well-justified. The experiments are conducted with a fixed K=2 for the entire vocabulary, which seems arbitrary. Many words are not polysemous, while others may have more than two distinct senses. The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis or discussion on how K could be chosen or adapted per word.
2.  The novelty is primarily in the representation, not the training objective. The max-margin framework is adopted directly from previous work on Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014), limiting the methodological contribution.
3.  The evaluation on polysemous words is incomplete. Section 4.4 discusses the SCWS dataset and relevant multi-prototype baselines, but the corresponding results are not presented in a table, making it difficult to assess the claims of competitive performance on this crucial task.
