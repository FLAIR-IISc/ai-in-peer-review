- Summary: Proposes representing words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to capture polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective using the Expected Likelihood Kernel (ELK), and shows qualitative and quantitative gains over unimodal baselines.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a novel probabilistic embedding that models each word as a multimodal GMM to explicitly represent multiple senses.

- Contribution 2: Presents a tractable, scalable training framework combining a max-margin objective with ELK for closed-form energy computation suitable for SGD on large corpora.

- Contribution 3: Provides empirical evidence (qualitative sense examples and quantitative gains) showing the multimodal model improves word similarity and entailment performance over single-vector and single-Gaussian baselines.

- Strength 1: Strong motivation and novelty—addresses polysemy limitation of prior embeddings by moving from unimodal uncertainty to explicit multimodal semantics.

- Strength 2: Technically sound—ELK makes GMM similarity tractable, the max-margin objective is appropriate, and implementation details on stability are provided.

- Strength 3: Comprehensive evaluation—clear qualitative examples, visualizations, and quantitative improvements across multiple benchmarks isolate benefits of multimodality.

- Strength 4: Clear presentation—the paper is well-written, well-organized, and effectively communicates motivation, methods, and results.

- Weakness 1: Choice of number of components K is underexplored—experiments mostly use K=2, with no systematic strategy or per-word adaptation discussed.

- Weakness 2: Evaluation inconsistency—the best word-similarity metric (max cosine between means) ignores covariances and weights, questioning the utility of the full probabilistic model for that task.

- Weakness 3: Missing comparison—results on the SCWS multi-prototype benchmark (and a referenced Table 4) are absent, preventing proper comparison to other multi-prototype methods.

- Question 1: How is K selected and fixed across vocabulary; have you considered per-word adaptive K and how does mismatch between K and true senses affect the model?

- Question 2: Can you analyze the contributions of covariances and mixture weights versus multiple mean vectors, given max-cosine (ignoring covariances/weights) often performs best?

- Question 3: Please provide the missing SCWS results and compare directly to other multi-prototype embedding models.
