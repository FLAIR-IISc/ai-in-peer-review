- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixture models to better capture polysemy and semantic relations.  
- Uses the expected likelihood kernel as an analytically tractable energy function within a max-margin training framework.  
- Evaluated on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.  
- Main contribution: novel multimodal probabilistic word representation extending unimodal Gaussian embeddings.  
- Enabling contribution: use of the expected likelihood kernel to make training feasible.  
- Strength: well-motivated, elegant extension with qualitative results showing components capture distinct senses (e.g., "rock", "bank").  
- Strength: strong empirical evaluation with w2gm outperforming word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on many similarity datasets.  
- Strength: superior performance on word entailment, supporting the benefit of flexible density models for asymmetric relations.  
- Weakness: fixed number of components (K=2) for all words is arbitrary with no sensitivity analysis or per-word adaptation.  
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty since the max-margin objective is adopted from prior work.  
- Weakness: incomplete evaluation on polysemous-word benchmarks—SCWS/multi-prototype results are discussed but not presented in a table.
