- Summary: Models words as Gaussian Mixture Models to capture polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective using the Expected Likelihood Kernel (ELK), and shows qualitative sense disentanglement and strong quantitative results versus baselines.

Contributions:
- Introduces GMM-based probabilistic word embeddings to represent multiple senses, uncertainty, and sense prevalence.
- Proposes a tractable learning framework using ELK within a max-margin objective, yielding closed-form, differentiable similarity between GMMs.
- Provides comprehensive empirical validation demonstrating sense disentanglement and strong performance on similarity and entailment tasks.

Strengths:
- Novel and conceptually elegant extension from point vectors to multimodal distributions, increasing representational capacity.
- Technically sound choice of ELK with solid derivations and implementation details for stable learning.
- Convincing qualitative nearest-neighbor examples showing effective sense separation.
- Strong quantitative gains over unimodal Gaussian embeddings and word2vec, notably on entailment tasks.

Weaknesses:
- No principled strategy for selecting K (number of components); fixed small K is likely suboptimal across vocabulary.
- Scalability and computational cost not thoroughly analyzed; energy computation scales as K^2 and training appears expensive.
- Evaluation uses multiple similarity metrics inconsistently and often ignores mixture weights, with little analysis of metric suitability.
- Crucial results (SCWS/multi-prototype comparisons, Table 4) are missing, preventing full assessment.

Questions to authors:
- How was K chosen; did you try per-word or nonparametric methods to adapt or learn K?
- Have you evaluated similarity metrics that incorporate mixture weights (e.g., expected similarity) rather than max/min that ignore p_i?
- Please provide the missing SCWS results and comparisons to prior multi-prototype baselines.
