- Models each word as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) to capture polysemy with means, covariances, and mixture weights.  
- Trains with a skip-gram-inspired, energy-based max-margin objective and uses the expected likelihood kernel for closed-form tractability.  
- Shows qualitative and quantitative evidence that GMM embeddings learn distinct senses and outperform word2vec and unimodal Gaussian baselines on similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Contribution: a novel, well-motivated probabilistic multi-sense embedding that also models uncertainty.  
- Contribution: a tractable training framework for GMMs via the expected likelihood kernel enabling gradient-based optimization.  
- Contribution: comprehensive empirical validation with strong results across benchmarks.  
- Strength: high novelty and conceptual soundness, providing interpretable multi-sense representations.  
- Strength: compelling qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses and consistent quantitative improvements over strong baselines.  
- Strength: technically sound methodology with detailed implementation and reproducibility information.  
- Weakness: missing quantitative evaluation on a dedicated polysemy benchmark (SCWS) despite claims about polysemy modeling.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of the fixed number of components (K=2) and no broad study or method for choosing K per word.  
- Weakness: training objective (expected likelihood) vs. evaluation metric mismatch — max cosine between means outperforms the expected likelihood, undermining the role of covariances.  
- Question: please provide SCWS results and comparisons to other multi-prototype models.  
- Question: how sensitive is performance to K, and do components collapse or capture nuanced senses for larger K?  
- Question: why does max cosine outperform expected likelihood, and does this imply covariances are unnecessary for similarity tasks?
