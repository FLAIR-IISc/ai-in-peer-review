- Introduces Word to Gaussian Mixture (w2gm), representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple senses.  
- Proposes an energy-based max-margin training objective using the expected likelihood kernel for analytic tractability with mixtures.  
- Provides comprehensive empirical evaluation showing improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks over existing methods.  

- Novel and well-motivated approach to polysemy with qualitative examples showing distinct mixture components for different senses.  
- Strong empirical validation: consistent performance gains across multiple standard datasets and useful qualitative analysis.  
- Theoretically sound energy function: expected likelihood kernel with closed-form solutions for Gaussian mixtures.  

- Paper lacks clear justification for why a probabilistic approach is necessary over deterministic multi-prototype methods and how covariances help.  
- Insufficient analysis of model complexity and scalability—no training-time comparison to standard methods like word2vec.  
- Limited exploration of the mixture component count K; mostly uses K=2 and offers little insight into when larger K helps.  

- How does the probabilistic formulation outperform deterministic multi-prototype methods and which tasks benefit from covariance uncertainty?  
- Please provide training-time comparisons and scaling behavior versus word2vec on comparable hardware/datasets.  
- Why does K=3 not improve results over K=2; is there a theoretical or empirical reason two components suffice?  

- Overall: strong, meaningful contribution addressing polysemy with solid theory and results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
