

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a multimodal word embedding approach using Gaussian mixtures (w2gm), where each word is represented by a mixture of Gaussian distributions rather than a single point vector or unimodal Gaussian. The primary contribution is the novel use of Gaussian mixture models to capture multiple distinct meanings (polysemy) of words, which unimodal approaches cannot effectively represent. The second contribution is the proposed energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel as the energy function, enabling analytic tractability for training. The third contribution is the empirical demonstration that the model outperforms unimodal alternatives (word2vec, Gaussian embeddings) on word similarity and entailment tasks while reducing the variance problem for polysemous words.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification and solution of a meaningful limitation in existing approaches**: The paper effectively identifies the key limitation of unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014) for polysemous words, where the distribution becomes overly diffuse to represent multiple meanings. The Gaussian mixture approach directly addresses this problem by allowing multiple modes, and the qualitative results in Table 1 provide strong evidence that the model successfully learns distinct meanings for polysemous words like "rock" and "bank."

2. **Strong empirical validation across multiple benchmarks**: The model demonstrates consistent improvements over unimodal alternatives across multiple word similarity datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW) using multiple evaluation metrics. The improvements on the WordSim-353 dataset (Table 3) are particularly significant, with w2gm achieving 73.5 Spearman correlation compared to 64.2 for Huang et al. (2012), a multiprototype model that uses external lexical resources. This shows the model's effectiveness without relying on external knowledge.

3. **Practical scalability and implementation details**: The authors provide thorough implementation details, including how they handle numerical stability, the use of diagonal covariance matrices, and training on large corpora (2.5B + 1B tokens). The paper demonstrates the model's scalability with training times on billion-word corpora in "days," which is a significant practical contribution for a novel embedding approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Modest performance gains with limited novelty justification**: While the model shows improvements over unimodal baselines, the gains are often marginal (e.g., 1-3% on most datasets) compared to the significant computational overhead of the Gaussian mixture approach. The paper claims to be the "first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings," but fails to adequately justify why this is novel given prior work by Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) who proposed similar multiprototype approaches using different methods. This weakens the contribution claim.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art polysemy models**: The paper only compares with word2vec and the Gaussian embedding model (w2g), but omits comparison with more recent and specialized polysemy models like those by Huang et al. (2012), Tian et al. (2014), and Chen et al. (2014). Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the improvements are truly significant or merely incremental over existing approaches.

3. **Lack of ablation studies on key hyperparameters**: The paper uses K=2 components throughout, but provides no ablation study on how performance changes with different values of K (e.g., K=1, K=2, K=3). The statement that "K=3 does not generally offer performance differences" is unsubstantiated without proper comparison, and the paper doesn't explain why K=2 is optimal or how to select K for new tasks.

4. **Unclear explanation for energy function choice**: The paper uses the expected likelihood kernel as the energy function but doesn't provide a thorough justification for why this particular choice works better than alternatives like negative KL divergence. The paper mentions that KL doesn't have a closed form for Gaussian mixtures, but doesn't explain why this is a critical advantage for the specific task of word embedding.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings. However, Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) proposed multiprototype approaches that also handle polysemy. Could you clarify the key differences between your approach and theirs, and specifically explain why your method is fundamentally different and superior?

2. Your model with K=2 components outperforms Huang et al.'s (2012) model on WordSim-353, even though their model uses external lexical resources (WordNet). Could you provide more details on why your unsupervised approach without external resources achieves better results?

3. The paper states that "K=3 does not generally offer performance differences" compared to K=2. Could you provide the exact performance numbers for K=3 across the word similarity datasets to substantiate this claim? This is important for understanding the optimal number of mixture components.

4. The energy function choice (expected likelihood kernel) is critical to your approach. Could you provide more evidence or analysis showing why this energy function is superior to alternatives like negative KL divergence, especially given that KL divergence is asymmetric and potentially more suitable for entailment tasks?

5. The paper mentions that the model "reduces the variances of each component for polysemous words." Could you provide quantitative evidence of this variance reduction (e.g., average variance per dimension for polysemous words across models) to support this claim?
