

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel word embedding model called "Word to Gaussian Mixture" (w2gm) that represents words as mixtures of Gaussians rather than single vectors or single Gaussian distributions. The authors claim their model captures multiple meanings of polysemous words, represents entailment relationships, and provides richer uncertainty information compared to previous approaches. The main contributions I see are:

1. A Gaussian mixture model for word embeddings that explicitly captures multiple word senses (primary contribution)
2. An energy-based max-margin training objective using the expected likelihood kernel, enabling analytic tractability with Gaussian mixtures (secondary contribution)
3. Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating superior performance on word similarity and entailment tasks compared to existing methods (tertiary contribution)

## Strengths

1. **Novel and well-motivated approach to polysemy**: The paper addresses a significant limitation of previous word embedding approaches (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014) that cannot adequately represent words with multiple distinct meanings. The qualitative examples in Table 1 are particularly compelling, showing that different mixture components correspond to distinct word senses (e.g., rock:0 for stone meaning, rock:1 for music meaning). This is a clear advancement over unimodal representations where the mean vector gets pulled between meanings, resulting in poor representation of all senses.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across multiple standard word similarity datasets (SimLex, WS, MEN, etc.) and shows consistent improvements over both word2vec and single-Gaussian embeddings. The performance gains (e.g., 2-4 points in Spearman correlation on WS, WS-R, MEN) are significant, especially given the baseline methods are strong. The qualitative analysis (Table 1) effectively demonstrates the model's ability to learn meaningful multi-prototype representations, which is crucial for understanding the model's behavior.

3. **Theoretically sound energy function**: The authors' choice of expected likelihood kernel as the energy function is well-motivated and provides a mathematically rigorous way to compare distributions. The derivation in Section 3.4 and supplementary material shows the technical depth of the work, and the closed-form solution for Gaussian mixtures is a significant contribution that enables practical implementation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear justification for probabilistic approach**: The paper claims to be "the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings," but doesn't adequately explain why a probabilistic approach is necessary when deterministic multi-prototype methods (Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014) already exist. The paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison of the probabilistic vs. deterministic approaches, nor does it explain why the uncertainty information provided by the covariance matrices is beneficial for the tasks evaluated. The authors should clarify whether the probabilistic nature of their model is essential or merely a byproduct of their approach.

2. **Insufficient analysis of model complexity and scalability**: While the paper mentions training on "billions of words in days," it lacks a meaningful comparison of training time to standard methods like word2vec. Given that Gaussian mixture models are inherently more complex than single-vector representations, this is a critical omission. The authors should provide training time comparisons to demonstrate whether their model is practically feasible for large-scale applications.

3. **Limited exploration of the mixture component parameter (K)**: The paper uses K=2 for most experiments but doesn't sufficiently explore how performance changes with different values of K. While they mention K=3 in the supplementary material, they don't provide a clear analysis of when increasing K would be beneficial or detrimental. The paper should address whether there's an optimal K for different word types and how this impacts model complexity.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the probabilistic nature of your model specifically contribute to capturing multiple word meanings, beyond what deterministic multi-prototype approaches can achieve? What tasks would particularly benefit from the uncertainty information provided by the covariance matrices?

2. Could you provide a training time comparison between your approach and word2vec (or other standard methods) on the same hardware? Given the increased complexity of Gaussian mixture models, how does the training time scale with corpus size?

3. The paper shows that K=3 doesn't generally offer performance improvements over K=2. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case? Is there a theoretical or empirical reason why two components are sufficient for most words with multiple meanings?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of word embeddings by addressing the polysemy limitation of previous approaches. The Gaussian mixture model is well-motivated, the energy function is theoretically sound, and the empirical results are compelling. While the paper has some weaknesses regarding the justification for a probabilistic approach and insufficient exploration of model complexity, these are not deal-breakers. The contributions are significant and the work represents a clear advancement over the state of the art. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions above.
