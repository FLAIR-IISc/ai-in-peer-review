- Proposes a Gaussian mixture model (w2gm) for word embeddings that represents multiple word senses via multiple Gaussian components.
- Introduces an energy-based max-margin training objective using the expected likelihood kernel for analytic tractability.
- Demonstrates empirically that the model learns multiple senses and outperforms baselines on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.
- Addresses the limitation of single-vector embeddings for polysemy; qualitative examples show distinct senses for words like "rock" and "bank."
- Consistently outperforms prior methods on several word similarity datasets with statistically significant gains.
- Provides a thorough derivation of the expected likelihood kernel and comprehensive implementation details to aid reproducibility.
- Does not adequately distinguish the approach from prior multiprototype embedding work and may overclaim novelty.
- The claim of reduced “unnecessarily large variance” for polysemous words is supported only by a single example and lacks systematic analysis.
- Fails to clearly justify choosing the expected likelihood kernel over alternatives (e.g., KL divergence).
- Lacks discussion of computational complexity, training time, memory requirements, and scalability compared to baselines.
- Claims “uniquely expressive semantic information” but offers mainly benchmark results without qualitative examples of specific semantic relationships.
- How does this approach specifically differ from Huang et al. (2012) in architecture and performance, and what are its concrete advantages?
- Can you provide detailed entailment examples showing where the model outperforms Gaussian embeddings?
- Why focus on K=2 components; how does performance change for K=3 or higher and why were higher-K results not presented?
