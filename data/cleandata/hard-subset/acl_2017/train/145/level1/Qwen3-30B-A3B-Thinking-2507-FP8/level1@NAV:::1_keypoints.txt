- Proposal: multimodal word embeddings using Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) to represent multiple word senses, extending Vilnis & McCallum (2014).
- Primary contribution: demonstration that GMMs capture multiple word senses (e.g., rock=stone vs music) better than unimodal Gaussians.
- Secondary contribution: energy-based max-margin objective with expected likelihood kernel for efficient training of expressive distributions.
- Tertiary contribution: empirical validation showing improvements on standard word similarity and entailment benchmarks.
- Strength: qualitative results (Table 1, Fig 1) clearly show sense separation with distinct Gaussian components.
- Strength: quantitative results (Table 2) show consistent gains over word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.
- Strength: entailment results (Table 5) substantially improve modeling of semantic relationships beyond similarity.
- Weakness: limited novelty—straightforward extension of prior work and not clearly distinguished from multi-prototype methods (e.g., Huang et al., 2012).
- Weakness: narrow experimental comparisons, omitting recent baselines like GloVe, ELMo, and contextual embeddings.
- Weakness: missing analysis of computational cost and scalability of the GMM approach, especially for larger K.
- Question: how does GMM performance compare to recent contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT) on downstream tasks?
- Question: what fraction of polysemous words does the model successfully disambiguate, and how does that affect overall performance?
- Question: provide a detailed analysis of when the multimodal representation gives the greatest benefit over unimodal approaches.
