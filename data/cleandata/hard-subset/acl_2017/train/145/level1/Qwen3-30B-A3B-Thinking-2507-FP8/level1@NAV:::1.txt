

This paper proposes a multimodal word embedding approach using Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) to represent words with multiple meanings, extending Vilnis and McCallum's (2014) unimodal Gaussian embedding. The primary contribution is the demonstration that GMMs can effectively capture multiple word senses (e.g., "rock" for stone vs. music), which unimodal approaches fail to represent adequately. The secondary contribution is the energy-based max-margin objective with expected likelihood kernel for training, enabling efficient learning of expressive distributions. The tertiary contribution is the empirical validation showing improvements on standard word similarity and entailment benchmarks.

Strengths: The qualitative results (Table 1 and Figure 1) compellingly demonstrate the model's ability to separate word senses, with distinct Gaussian components corresponding to different meanings. The quantitative results (Table 2) show consistent performance gains across multiple word similarity datasets, with the multimodal approach outperforming both word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings. The entailment results (Table 5) are particularly significant, as they demonstrate the model's ability to capture semantic relationships beyond simple similarity, with the multimodal approach substantially improving on entailment tasks.

Weaknesses: The novelty is limited, as the approach is a straightforward extension of prior work (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014) and does not sufficiently distinguish itself from existing multi-prototype embedding methods (e.g., Huang et al., 2012). The experimental comparison is narrow, omitting comparisons to more recent state-of-the-art embedding methods (e.g., GloVe, ELMo, or contextual embeddings). The paper also fails to analyze the computational cost of the GMM approach versus simpler methods, which is crucial for practical adoption, especially with larger K.

Questions to Authors: How does the performance of the GMM approach compare to recent contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT) on downstream NLP tasks? What is the fraction of polysemous words that the model successfully disambiguates, and how does this impact overall performance? Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of when the multimodal representation provides the greatest benefit compared to unimodal approaches?
