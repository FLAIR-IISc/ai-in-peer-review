- Proposes w2gm: multimodal word embeddings representing each word as a Gaussian mixture to capture multiple meanings (polysemy).
- Introduces an energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel for analytic tractability in training.
- Empirically outperforms unimodal baselines on word similarity and entailment tasks and reportedly reduces variance for polysemous words.

- Identifies unimodal Gaussian embeddings’ limitation for polysemy and shows mixtures learn distinct meanings qualitatively (e.g., "rock", "bank").
- Strong empirical validation across many benchmarks, notably higher Spearman on WordSim-353 than prior multiprototype work without using external lexical resources.
- Demonstrates practical scalability with implementation details and training on billion-token corpora within days.

- Performance gains are often modest (1–3%) relative to the added computational complexity, and novelty claims overlook prior multiprototype methods.
- Lacks comparisons with other recent polysemy models (Huang et al. 2012, Tian et al. 2014, Chen et al. 2014), making significance of improvements unclear.
- No ablation study on number of components K (uses K=2 throughout) and provides no substantiated justification for K selection.
- Insufficient justification for choosing the expected likelihood kernel over alternatives (e.g., negative KL); rationale and task-specific advantages are unclear.

- Clarify how your approach fundamentally differs from and improves upon prior multiprototype methods (Huang 2012, Tian 2014).
- Explain why your unsupervised K=2 model outperforms Huang et al. despite their use of external lexical resources.
- Provide exact performance numbers for K=3 (and other K) across datasets to substantiate the claim that K=3 offers no general differences.
- Supply analysis or evidence showing why the expected likelihood kernel is superior to alternatives like negative KL, especially for entailment tasks.
- Provide quantitative evidence of variance reduction for polysemous words (e.g., average per-dimension variance comparisons across models).
