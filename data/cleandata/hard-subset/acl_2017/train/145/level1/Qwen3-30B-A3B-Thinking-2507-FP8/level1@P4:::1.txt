

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a Gaussian mixture model (w2gm) for word embeddings that captures multiple word senses through multiple Gaussian components, where each component represents a distinct meaning. The authors introduce an energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel as the energy function for training. The main contributions are:

1. A multimodal word embedding model using Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple word meanings (polysemy)
2. An energy-based training objective with the expected likelihood kernel that enables analytic tractability
3. Empirical demonstration of the model's ability to learn multiple word senses and its superior performance on word similarity and entailment benchmarks

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses a significant limitation of current word embedding approaches (the inability to capture multiple word meanings in a single representation) with a theoretically sound solution. The qualitative evaluation in Table 1 clearly demonstrates that the model successfully learns multiple distinct meanings for polysemous words (e.g., rock representing both stone and music, bank representing river bank and financial bank), which is a substantial improvement over unimodal Gaussian embeddings.

2. The model consistently outperforms previous methods on multiple word similarity datasets (Table 2), with particularly strong gains on datasets like MEN, MC, RG, YP, and MT-287. The results are statistically significant and consistent across different similarity metrics (maximum cosine similarity, expected likelihood kernel, minimum Euclidean distance).

3. The paper provides a thorough derivation of the expected likelihood kernel and its use in the energy function, which is crucial for the model's tractability. The implementation details in the supplementary material are comprehensive and should facilitate reproducibility, including practical considerations for numerical stability and initialization.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from prior work on multiprototype embeddings (Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014). These works also address polysemy, and the paper should clarify how the Gaussian mixture approach differs from and improves upon these methods. The claim that "to the best of our knowledge, the first probabilistic word embedding that can capture multiple meanings" appears unsubstantiated given these prior works.

2. The claim that the model reduces "unnecessarily large variance" for polysemous words is not well-supported. The paper provides only a single numerical example (rock) and no systematic analysis of variance reduction across multiple words or datasets. This is a key claim of the paper but lacks sufficient empirical evidence.

3. The paper doesn't provide a clear justification for choosing the expected likelihood kernel over other possible energy functions, such as KL divergence. While the paper notes that KL divergence doesn't have a closed form for Gaussian mixtures, it doesn't explain why this is a critical limitation for their approach, especially since they acknowledge KL divergence as "another sensible choice of energy function."

4. The computational complexity and scalability of the proposed approach compared to existing methods are not adequately discussed. The paper mentions training on "billions of words in days" but doesn't provide details on training time, memory requirements, or comparison to the computational demands of word2vec or Gaussian embeddings.

5. The paper claims the model captures "uniquely expressive semantic information," but the evidence for this is primarily empirical (better performance on benchmarks) rather than qualitative analysis of specific semantic relationships. The authors don't provide examples of specific semantic relationships that their model captures better than others.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your Gaussian mixture approach specifically differ from the multiprototype embedding method by Huang et al. (2012) in terms of architecture and performance? What specific advantages does the Gaussian mixture provide over their approach?

2. The paper claims that "the Gaussian mixture model has desirable qualitative behavior for word entailment," but doesn't provide specific examples of how your model handles entailment compared to Gaussian embeddings. Could you provide more detailed examples of entailment relationships that your model captures better?

3. The paper mentions that for K=3 components, the model can learn three distinct meanings, but doesn't provide results for this setting. Why did you choose to focus on K=2 for your main results, and how does performance change with higher K?
