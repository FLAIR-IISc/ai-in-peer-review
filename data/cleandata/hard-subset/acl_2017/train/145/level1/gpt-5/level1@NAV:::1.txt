Summary: The paper proposes representing each word as a Gaussian mixture distribution to model polysemy, entailment, and uncertainty. Training uses an energy-based max-margin objective where similarity between two word distributions is the expected likelihood kernel (an inner product in L2), chosen for its closed-form with mixtures. The authors present practical stabilization/initialization details, train at web scale, and report qualitative sense separation and improved performance on word similarity and lexical entailment benchmarks over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A tractable, closed-form energy for multimodal probabilistic embeddings (expected likelihood kernel with a margin objective), enabling scalable learning of mixture embeddings.
2) An empirical study showing improved intrinsic performance (word similarity, entailment) and clear qualitative polysemy discovery, with code/visualization and large-scale training.
3) Practical training/stability insights (diagonal/spherical covariances, log-parametrization, determinant regularization, numerically stable log-sum-exp) that make mixture embeddings robust.

Strengths:
1) Expressivity and interpretability: The mixture formulation cleanly separates senses with component-specific neighbors (e.g., rock→{stone, basalt} vs {punk, indie}), addressing a core limitation of unimodal Gaussians; entailment behavior benefits from asymmetric KL.
2) Sound and efficient objective: The expected likelihood kernel yields closed-form energies and gradients for mixtures; the analysis of terms in ξi,j and the determinant regularization show clear design reasoning.
3) Solid empirical coverage and gains: Consistent improvements on many benchmarks (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, RW) and on entailment (higher AP/F1 with KL) indicate the approach is competitive beyond anecdotes.
4) Scalability and engineering: Training on 3.5B tokens with asynchronous Adagrad and careful numerical tricks demonstrates practicality; the public visualization increases transparency and utility.

Weaknesses:
1) Limited novelty beyond a natural extension: Moving from unimodal Gaussians to mixtures is expected; stronger conceptual advances (e.g., adaptive K, principled uncertainty calibration) would strengthen the contribution.
2) Intrinsic-heavy evaluation: No downstream tasks (e.g., NLI, QA, tagging) to show end-task benefits; SCWS and multi-sense comparisons are only partially covered.
3) Missing ablations: Sparse analysis of K (beyond anecdotal K=3), covariance parameterization, and the impact of the determinant penalty; no ELK vs KL training comparison.
4) Baseline fairness: The w2g baseline differs in hyperparameters; comparisons to other multi-sense methods (e.g., more recent variants) are incomplete; no significance testing.

Questions to authors:
- How is K chosen, and can K be learned per word (e.g., nonparametric prior or sparsity on mixture weights)?
- Do components collapse or duplicate for frequent words? Provide statistics on mixture weights and variance distributions.
- How sensitive are results to covariance forms (spherical vs diagonal) and the strength of the determinant regularizer?
- What are memory/runtime overheads vs skip-gram, and how do negative sampling and window size affect performance?
- Can you report results on at least one downstream task to validate transfer?
