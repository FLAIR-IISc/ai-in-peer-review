- Proposal: represent each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm) trained with a skip-gram-style, energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel (ELK).
- Contribution 1: Scalable probabilistic multi-prototype word embeddings with analytically tractable ELK similarity and max-margin training to handle polysemy and uncertainty.
- Contribution 2: Empirical evidence of more interpretable senses and improved performance on several word similarity and lexical entailment benchmarks versus skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.
- Contribution 3: Practical training/stability techniques (diagonal/spherical covariances, log-variance/logit optimization, numerical stabilization, negative sampling, Adagrad, separate input/output mixtures) enabling training at large scale.
- Strength: Conceptual expressivity and interpretability—mixtures capture distinct senses (e.g., rock, bank, cell) with convincing nearest-neighbor examples.
- Strength: Analytic, scalable energy function—ELK gives closed-form, symmetric similarity incorporating covariance structure and supports practical max-margin training.
- Strength: Solid engineering—careful implementation details and stability tricks that make large-scale training reproducible.
- Strength: Empirical gains—meaningful improvements on multiple benchmarks (e.g., WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG) and better entailment performance relative to unimodal Gaussians.
- Strength: Reduced variance pathology—mixtures yield sharper per-component variances for polysemous words compared to unimodal Gaussians.
- Weakness: Incomplete and uneven baselines—missing strong contemporary multi-sense and entailment-specific baselines (e.g., Neelakantan et al. 2014, order-embeddings).
- Weakness: Entailment metric is ad hoc—using min_{i,j} KL over components is not the KL between full mixtures and needs justification.
- Weakness: Mixed/limited gains—improvements are not uniform (SimLex-999 drops, modest gains on some sets), needing analysis of when mixtures help or hurt.
- Weakness: Limited extrinsic evaluations—no downstream tasks reported, weakening claims of utility beyond intrinsic metrics and interpretability.
- Weakness: Component identifiability and test-time usage—Max-over-components similarity may ignore mixture weights, risk component collapse, and needs ablation vs probability-weighted scoring.
- Question: How exactly is KL for entailment computed for mixtures and were mixture-level approximations (e.g., variational bounds) tried?
- Question: How sensitive are results to K (report quantitative trends for K=1,2,3 and nonparametric selection)?
- Question: Do mixture weights influence evaluation beyond MaxSim/MinDist—report AveSim (probability-weighted) comparisons?
- Question: Did you compare with Neelakantan et al. (2014) and other contemporary multi-sense baselines or explain incompatibilities?
- Question: What are computational costs vs skip-gram and unimodal Gaussians (training time, memory, scaling with K and D, K^2 partial-energy bottleneck)?
- Question: Can you provide error analysis for why SimLex lags and characterize when mixtures help (e.g., polysemy, concreteness, frequency)?
- Question: For entailment, compare to order-embeddings or hypernymy-specific methods and report hypernymy detection benchmarks (e.g., BLESS, EVALution)?
- Overall assessment: Well-motivated and technically sound advance with clear qualitative benefits and competitive quantitative results, but needs stronger baselines, clearer entailment justification, and more analysis/downstream evaluation to strengthen the case.
