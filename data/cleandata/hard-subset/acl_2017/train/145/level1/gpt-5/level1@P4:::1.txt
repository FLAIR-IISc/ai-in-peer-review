Summary
The paper proposes representing each word as a multimodal probability distribution, specifically a Gaussian mixture model (GMM), to capture multiple senses, encode uncertainty, and enable entailment-like inclusion relationships. Training uses a skip-gram-style, energy-based max-margin objective, where the energy between two word distributions is an expected likelihood kernel (inner product in L2) that is closed-form for GMMs. Practical choices (diagonal/spherical covariances, stability fixes, Adagrad) enable large-scale training on billions of tokens. Qualitative analyses show distinct modes for polysemous words; quantitative evaluations on several word similarity benchmarks and a lexical entailment task show improvements over word2vec skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A scalable probabilistic multi-sense embedding model that represents words as Gaussian mixtures, paired with an analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel as the training energy. This extends unimodal Gaussian embeddings to a multimodal setting and preserves closed-form computations needed for efficient learning.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach improves over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on multiple word similarity datasets and on lexical entailment, with qualitative demonstrations of polysemy.
3) Practical training/engineering contributions (initialization, constraints, stability tricks, diagonal/spherical covariances, and negative sampling design) that allow training on very large corpora.

Strengths
- Sound and tractable formulation: Modeling words as GMMs is a natural probabilistic generalization of point and unimodal Gaussian embeddings. The expected likelihood kernel yields a closed-form energy for mixtures, avoiding intractable KL terms. The objective (max-margin ranking) is standard and appropriate for contrastive learning. The derivation and stability considerations (log-sum-exp trick via centering at max ξi,j, covariance floors) are clearly described and technically sound.
- Clear evidence of polysemy capture: Nearest-neighbor analyses show well-separated senses for prototypical polysemous words (e.g., rock: geology vs. music; bank: river vs. financial). The component-wise nearest neighbors align with intuitive senses, and increasing K to 3 produces sensible tri-modal cases (e.g., cell: phone, jail, biology).
- Competitive to improved quantitative results: Across many word similarity benchmarks (WordSim-353, MEN, MC, RG, YP, RW), the GMM model often outperforms both skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian baselines, with the best metric varying by dataset. The model also improves on lexical entailment over the unimodal Gaussian baseline, especially when using asymmetric KL at evaluation.
- Scalability and reproducibility: Training details (subsampling, negative sampling, optimization, initialization) are provided, with a large training corpus (≈3.5B tokens). The claim of training in days on CPU with Adagrad and diagonal/spherical covariances suggests practicality; code release and an interactive demo further support reproducibility.
- Reduced variance for polysemous words: The mixture decomposition reduces the need for overly diffuse single Gaussians, yielding tighter per-sense covariances. This addresses a known limitation of unimodal Gaussian embeddings and is corroborated with example variance magnitudes.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: The core ingredients (multi-prototype embeddings; Gaussian embeddings; max-margin training; expected likelihood kernels) are known. The main novelty is their combination for probabilistic multi-sense embeddings with a tractable energy. However, comparisons and citations to strong multi-sense baselines such as MSSG/NP-MSSG (Neelakantan et al., 2014) are missing, which weakens the claim of advancing the multi-sense state of the art.
- Evaluation fairness and coverage: Several baselines use results from prior work (e.g., sg*, w2g*) that may differ in corpus, preprocessing, or dimensionality, and the proposed model’s embeddings are relatively low-dimensional (D=50). There is no comparison to strong 300d skip-gram/GloVe models trained on the same data. For entailment, comparison to more recent entailment/ordering embeddings (e.g., order embeddings) is absent. These issues make it difficult to assess absolute progress.
- Inconsistent use of uncertainty: While the model is probabilistic, the best-performing similarity metric is often maximum cosine similarity between component means, which ignores covariances. The expected likelihood kernel tends to underperform on similarity tasks. This suggests that much of the utility in similarity benchmarks comes from mean vectors rather than learned uncertainties; a deeper analysis or downstream tasks that benefit from uncertainty would strengthen the case.
- Limited analysis of number of senses and per-word adaptivity: The model fixes K (typically 2). There is no mechanism to infer K per word or to prune unused components, nor an analysis of how often multiple components are actually used across the vocabulary. The statement that K=3 seldom helps is anecdotal; more systematic evaluation (e.g., AIC/BIC proxies, sparsity of mixture weights, sense usage statistics) is missing.
- Entailment objective mismatch: The training energy is symmetric (expected likelihood kernel), while entailment evaluation relies on an asymmetric metric (KL). This mismatch suggests the model is not explicitly trained to capture asymmetry/inclusion; adding asymmetric training objectives or regularizers could better target entailment. No ablation is provided to quantify the contribution of covariances to entailment vs. means alone.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines: Can you include comparisons with multi-sense skip-gram (MSSG/NP-MSSG) trained on the same corpus and dimensionality? For entailment, can you compare against order embeddings or other asymmetric embedding methods?
- Use of uncertainty: Why does expected likelihood underperform cosine on several similarity datasets? Can you provide ablations quantifying the impact of covariances on similarity and entailment (e.g., fixing covariances, or randomizing them)?
- Number of components: Do mixture weights concentrate to effectively fewer senses for monosemous words? Can you report the distribution of effective senses (e.g., entropy of mixture weights) and per-word component utilization?
- Training vs. evaluation asymmetry: Have you tried asymmetric energies during training (e.g., approximated KL for GMMs, or alternative inclusion-based losses)? If so, how do results change?
- Fairness controls: Can you provide same-corpus, same-dimension comparisons against strong skip-gram/GloVe baselines and your unimodal Gaussian reimplementation, including runtime and memory footprints?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to K, D, margin m, and covariance initialization v? Is there a principled way to select K per word (e.g., nonparametric mixture, sparsity on mixture weights)?
