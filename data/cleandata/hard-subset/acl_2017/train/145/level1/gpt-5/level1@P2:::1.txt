Summary and contributions
This paper proposes representing each word as a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm) rather than a point vector or a single Gaussian, trained with a skip-gram-style, energy-based max-margin objective. The energy between two word distributions is the expected likelihood kernel (ELK), which is closed-form for Gaussian mixtures. The model aims to capture polysemy, asymmetric relations such as entailment, and represent uncertainty.

Key contributions (as I see them), strongest first:
1) A scalable probabilistic multi-prototype word embedding based on Gaussian mixtures, with an analytically tractable similarity (ELK) and max-margin training. This extends unimodal Gaussian embeddings to handle polysemy and richer uncertainty.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach yields more interpretable senses and improves on several word similarity and lexical entailment benchmarks compared to skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.
3) Practical training details (stability tricks, diagonal/spherical covariances, initialization, softmax mixture weights) that enable training on 3.5B tokens and a 314k vocabulary.

Strengths
- Conceptual expressivity and interpretability: Modeling words as mixtures directly addresses polysemy and sense uncertainty. The qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses convincingly show sense separation (e.g., rock→{basalt,…} vs {funk, punk,…}; bank→{river,…} vs {banking,…}). The K=3 “cell” example (cellphone/jail/biology) is illustrative of richer sense structure.
- Analytic, scalable energy function: Using the ELK provides a closed-form, symmetric similarity between mixtures. This makes the max-margin training practical while incorporating covariance structure. The paper carefully derives the partial energies and explains their roles (mean proximity vs covariance regularization via log-det).
- Solid engineering and stability considerations: The paper gives concrete, reproducible choices (diagonal/spherical covariances, optimizing log variances and mixture logits, epsilon-stabilized sums, numerically stable log-sum-exp over partial energies, subsampling, negative sampling U^(3/4), Adagrad with a learning rate schedule, separate input/output mixtures). These details matter for scaling beyond toy corpora.
- Empirical improvements where it matters: On several benchmarks the model outperforms baselines, often by a meaningful margin: WS (73.5 vs 71–72), WS-R (71.8 vs ≤68 for single-prototype), MEN (73.6 vs ~71–72), MC and RG (notably strong; e.g., MC up to 80.3 with Euclidean). On WordSim-353, it also beats Huang et al. (2012) multi-prototype. The entailment results show consistent gains of w2gm over w2g and that asymmetric metrics (min component KL) outperform cosine.
- Reduced variance pathology vs unimodal Gaussians: The analysis showing lower per-dimension variance for polysemous words (e.g., rock’s mixture components ~e^-2.5 vs unimodal ~e^-1.8) supports the claim that mixtures avoid variance inflation and yield sharper, more informative uncertainty.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and uneven baselines: While comparisons against skip-gram, Vilnis & McCallum (2014), and Huang et al. (2012) are included, other strong multi-sense baselines from the period are missing (e.g., multi-sense skip-gram (Neelakantan et al., 2014), sense embeddings with nonparametric senses, or order-embeddings/vectors specialized for entailment). For entailment, comparisons to methods designed for asymmetric relations (e.g., order embeddings) would better contextualize gains.
- Entailment metric is ad hoc relative to mixtures: The paper earlier notes that KL between mixtures is intractable. The entailment evaluation then uses min_{i,j} KL(N_i||N_j), i.e., component-wise KL. This is not the KL between the full mixtures, and effectively reduces to pairwise Gaussian comparisons with a max pooling decision rule. This discrepancy should be acknowledged and justified more clearly; otherwise the entailment claim risks being overstated.
- Mixed results and modest gains on some similarity sets: Improvements are not uniform. For example, SimLex-999 drops relative to the unimodal Gaussian variant and to skip-gram (25–32 vs 29–30+), and MEN/WS gains are modest. RW improvements are small and metric-dependent. The paper should analyze when mixtures help vs hurt (e.g., taxonomic similarity vs relatedness, concreteness, frequency).
- Limited extrinsic evaluations: The work focuses on intrinsic similarity and lexical entailment. No downstream tasks (e.g., NER, QA, STS, parsing, MT) are reported, which weakens claims of utility beyond interpretability and intrinsic scores. Given the training scale, at least one downstream comparison would strengthen significance.
- Component identifiability and usage at test time: Similarity scoring often takes max over component pairs, effectively ignoring mixture weights and the full distribution. This design risks sense “winner-takes-all” behavior, potential component collapse, and makes the role of learned mixture weights unclear. An ablation contrasting max vs probability-weighted comparisons would clarify the contribution of mixture modeling itself.

Questions to authors
- How exactly is the KL used for entailment computed for mixtures? Is it min over component pairs KL(N_i||N_j) with diagonal/spherical covariances? Did you try any mixture-level approximations (e.g., variational bounds) and how do results compare?
- How sensitive are results to K? You briefly show K=3 qualitatively; can you report quantitative trends for K=1,2,3 (and perhaps a nonparametric selection) on similarity/entailment?
- Do mixture weights pi influence evaluation beyond MaxSim/MinDist? Could you report AveSim (probability-weighted) on SCWS and other datasets and compare with MaxSim?
- Did you compare with Neelakantan et al. (2014) multi-sense skip-gram or other contemporary multi-sense baselines? If not, can you include them or explain incompatibilities?
- What are the computational costs vs skip-gram and unimodal Gaussians (training time, memory)? How do they scale with K and D? Any bottlenecks from computing all K^2 partial energies?
- Can you provide an error analysis explaining why SimLex lags and where mixtures help most (e.g., polysemy prevalence, concreteness, frequency bins)?
- For entailment, could you compare to order-embeddings (Vendrov et al., 2016) or hypernymy-specialized embeddings, and/or report hypernymy detection benchmarks (e.g., BLESS, EVALution)?

Overall assessment
The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and thoughtfully engineered. The probabilistic multi-prototype formulation plus ELK is a meaningful advance over unimodal probabilistic embeddings, with clear qualitative benefits and competitive to improved quantitative results. The main limitations are incomplete baselines (especially for multi-sense and entailment), mixed gains across datasets, and an ad hoc entailment scoring for mixtures. Addressing these would strengthen the case for acceptance.
