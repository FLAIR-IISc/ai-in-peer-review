## Review of "Multimodal Word Distributions"

This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, moving beyond point representations to model words as Gaussian mixtures. The core idea – capturing multiple word meanings and uncertainty through a mixture of Gaussians – is well-motivated and addresses limitations of existing methods like word2vec and single Gaussian embeddings. The authors propose an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel for learning these mixtures, demonstrating improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful application of Gaussian mixture models to word embeddings, coupled with a practical and analytically tractable learning objective. While probabilistic word embeddings have been explored before, this work offers a compelling combination of model expressiveness and training efficiency. A second contribution is the introduction of the expected likelihood kernel as an energy function, enabling closed-form computation and scalability. Finally, the empirical evaluation, demonstrating improved performance on several benchmarks, provides solid evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Sound Motivation:** The paper directly addresses the limitations of existing embedding methods in capturing polysemy and uncertainty. Representing words with distributions, specifically Gaussian mixtures, is a logical and promising approach.
2. **Analytical Tractability:** The choice of the expected likelihood kernel is a significant strength, allowing for efficient computation and scaling to large datasets. This is often a bottleneck in probabilistic models.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The results on word similarity and entailment demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over baselines, including word2vec and the previous Gaussian embedding approach. The qualitative examples further support the ability of the model to capture multiple meanings.
4. **Thoroughness:** The paper includes a detailed methodological description, implementation details, and ablation studies with different similarity measures, indicating a well-executed research effort.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation on Mixture Size:** While the paper experiments with K=2, a more comprehensive ablation study exploring the impact of different K values on performance would be beneficial. How does performance change with more components, and when does it plateau or degrade?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't fully explore the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters beyond the margin. Investigating the impact of learning rate, regularization, and the initialization scheme would enhance understanding.
3. **Comparison Scope:** While comparison to existing methods is present, expanding the comparison to more recent and sophisticated embedding techniques (e.g., contextualized embeddings like BERT or ELMo) would provide a stronger assessment of the proposed method’s relative performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the computational cost of training your model compared to word2vec or other embedding methods? Specifically, how does training time scale with vocabulary size and corpus size?
2.  What are the limitations of the expected likelihood kernel? Are there scenarios where other energy functions might be more appropriate?
3.  How do the learned Gaussian components correspond to known word senses (e.g., from WordNet)?  Is there a way to automatically interpret or label the different components?
