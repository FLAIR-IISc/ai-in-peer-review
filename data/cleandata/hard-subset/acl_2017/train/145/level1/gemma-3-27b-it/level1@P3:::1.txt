This paper introduces a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) for word embeddings, aiming to capture multiple meanings and uncertainty more effectively than single Gaussian or point-based embeddings. The authors propose an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel for learning the GMM parameters. The empirical evaluation demonstrates improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks compared to baselines.

**Contributions:**

1. **Multimodal Word Embeddings with GMMs:** The core contribution lies in representing words as Gaussian mixtures, allowing the model to represent polysemy and capture nuances beyond single-meaning representations. This is a reasonable extension to previous work on Gaussian embeddings.
2. **Expected Likelihood Kernel for Training:** The use of the expected likelihood kernel for defining the energy function within the max-margin framework is a well-motivated choice, enabling analytic tractability crucial for scaling to large datasets.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on standard word similarity and entailment benchmarks, showcasing performance gains over several baselines, including word2vec and a single Gaussian embedding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Motivation & Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing word embedding approaches in capturing polysemy and representing uncertainty, providing strong motivation for the proposed GMM-based approach.
2. **Technical Novelty (Kernel Choice):** The choice of the expected likelihood kernel is a clever way to combine the benefits of probabilistic modeling with efficient computation. This allows for a closed-form solution that enables training on large corpora.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation suite includes multiple datasets and various similarity/entailment metrics, providing a robust assessment of the proposed method's performance. The qualitative examples (nearest neighbors) are also helpful in demonstrating the model’s ability to capture different word senses.
4. **Practical Considerations:** The authors address several practical challenges such as numerical stability and initialization, demonstrating a focus on making the method usable in real-world scenarios. The inclusion of details regarding training parameters and implementation is beneficial for reproducibility.
5. **Performance Gains:** The reported performance improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks, while not always dramatic, are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, especially in comparison to the single Gaussian embedding baseline.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of GMM Application:** While the application of GMMs to word embeddings is a valid idea, it's not entirely novel. The concept of using multiple embeddings to capture different word senses has been explored in previous works (Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014). The core novelty resides in the specific energy function and learning framework, but the fundamental idea of multi-prototype representations is not new.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (K):** The performance seems somewhat sensitive to the choice of the number of mixture components (K). The experiments primarily focus on K=2, and the discussion of K=3 is limited. A more thorough exploration of the impact of K on performance and interpretability would strengthen the paper.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** It would be valuable to include an ablation study to dissect the contributions of different components of the proposed method. For instance, what is the performance gain from using the expected likelihood kernel compared to a simpler dot product? How much does the max-margin objective contribute to the overall performance?
4. **Entailment Dataset Concerns:** The entailment results reported are reasonable, but the dataset used (Baroni et al., 2012) is relatively small and might not be representative of all entailment scenarios. Larger and more diverse entailment datasets would provide a more convincing evaluation.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying specific cases where the model fails and analyzing the reasons for these failures could provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance to the initialization of the covariance matrices (Σw,i)? Do different initialization strategies significantly impact the learned embeddings?
2. Could you elaborate on the choice of the margin 'm' in the max-margin objective? Was a hyperparameter search performed to optimize 'm', and if so, what were the results?
3. Do you have any insights into why the expected likelihood kernel outperforms the cosine similarity for some datasets, while the opposite is true for others?
4.  You mention the subsampling and negative sampling techniques borrowed from word2vec. Have you experimented with different parameters for these techniques, and how do they affect performance?
5. The paper mentions a visualization website. Is it possible to include a few illustrative screenshots or examples in the paper itself to provide readers with a better sense of the model’s learned representations?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
* The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of existing approaches.
* The supplementary material (if available) would be helpful to review for more details on implementation and experimental settings.
* While the performance gains are encouraging, further investigation into the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters and a more detailed error analysis would significantly strengthen the paper.
