**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to word embeddings, representing each word as a Gaussian mixture distribution, allowing for multiple distinct meanings and uncertainty information. The authors introduce an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture model, using an expected likelihood kernel as the energy function. The proposed model, called Word to Gaussian Mixture (w2gm), is evaluated on various word similarity and entailment tasks, outperforming existing methods, including word2vec and Gaussian embeddings.

**Contributions**

1. **Multimodal word embeddings**: The authors propose a novel approach to word embeddings, representing each word as a Gaussian mixture distribution, allowing for multiple distinct meanings and uncertainty information.
2. **Energy-based max-margin objective**: The authors introduce an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture model, using an expected likelihood kernel as the energy function.
3. **Improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks**: The proposed model, w2gm, outperforms existing methods, including word2vec and Gaussian embeddings, on various word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Strengths**

1. **Expressive word representations**: The Gaussian mixture distribution allows for multiple distinct meanings and uncertainty information, making it a more expressive and flexible representation of words.
2. **Improved performance on polysemous words**: The authors demonstrate that w2gm can effectively capture multiple meanings of polysemous words, outperforming existing methods.
3. **Scalability**: The authors show that w2gm can be trained on large datasets, making it a scalable solution for word embeddings.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational complexity**: The authors note that the expected likelihood kernel can be computationally expensive to evaluate, which may limit the scalability of the model.
2. **Hyperparameter tuning**: The authors do not provide a thorough analysis of the hyperparameters used in the model, which may affect the performance of w2gm.
3. **Comparison to other multimodal embedding methods**: The authors do not provide a comprehensive comparison of w2gm to other multimodal embedding methods, which may be useful for understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameters used in the model, including the number of Gaussian components, the dimensionality of the embeddings, and the learning rate?
2. How does the performance of w2gm compare to other multimodal embedding methods, such as the method proposed by Huang et al. (2012)?
3. Can you provide more insights into the interpretability of the Gaussian mixture distribution, including how the different components of the mixture relate to different word meanings?
