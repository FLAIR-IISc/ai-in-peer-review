**Summary**

The paper proposes a novel approach to word embeddings by representing each word with a multimodal distribution formed from Gaussian mixtures. This approach allows for capturing multiple word meanings, entailment, and uncertainty information. The authors introduce an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture model. They evaluate their approach on various benchmark datasets, including word similarity and entailment tasks, and demonstrate its superiority over existing methods such as word2vec and Gaussian embeddings.

**Contributions**

1. **Multimodal Word Embeddings**: The paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings by representing each word with a multimodal distribution formed from Gaussian mixtures, allowing for capturing multiple word meanings, entailment, and uncertainty information.
2. **Energy-based Max-Margin Objective**: The authors propose an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture model, which is a significant contribution to the field of word embeddings.
3. **Improved Performance on Benchmark Datasets**: The paper demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach over existing methods on various benchmark datasets, including word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Strengths**

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed approach, including a clear explanation of the energy-based max-margin objective and the Gaussian mixture model.
2. **Experimental Evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation of their approach on various benchmark datasets, demonstrating its superiority over existing methods.
3. **Improved Performance on Polysemous Words**: The paper shows that the proposed approach can effectively capture multiple word meanings and uncertainty information, leading to improved performance on polysemous words.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational Complexity**: The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approach, which may be a concern for large-scale applications.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning**: The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how to tune the hyperparameters of the proposed approach, which may be a challenge for practitioners.
3. **Limited Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the proposed approach on a limited set of benchmark datasets, and it would be beneficial to see a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of tasks and datasets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the computational complexity of the proposed approach for large-scale applications?
2. Can you provide more guidance on how to tune the hyperparameters of the proposed approach?
3. How do you plan to extend the proposed approach to other NLP tasks, such as language modeling and machine translation?
