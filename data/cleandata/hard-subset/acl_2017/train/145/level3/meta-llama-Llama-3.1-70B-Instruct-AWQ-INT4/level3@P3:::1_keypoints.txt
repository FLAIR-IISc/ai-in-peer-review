- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixture distributions to capture multiple meanings and uncertainty.  
- Extends Vilnis and McCallum (2014) from unimodal to multimodal Gaussians to address polysemy.  
- Introduces an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel for training.  
- Reports superior performance on several benchmarks and descriptive learned representations.  
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and methods are well explained with effective tables/figures.  
- Experiments are correct and qualitative analyses align with expectations.  
- Thorough comparison with existing methods (e.g., word2vec skip-grams and Gaussian embeddings) but could be expanded.  
- Should clarify differences from Tian et al. (2014), which also uses mixture models for word senses.  
- Should include results from prior approaches in Tables 3 and 4 for direct comparison.  
- Raises a question why the w2gm model underperforms w2g on the SWCS analysis and asks for further explanation.  
- Strength: novel multimodal word distributions that represent multiple word meanings.  
- Strength: meaningful extension of prior Gaussian embedding work to handle polysemy.  
- Strength: thorough qualitative and quantitative evaluation and clear methodological exposition.  
- Weakness: comparisons to similar prior approaches are incomplete.  
- Weakness: lacks additional analysis/explanations for the SWCS performance drop.  
- Missing citations to add: Neelakantan et al. (2014); Li and Jurafsky (2015); Liu et al. (2015).  
- Suggested evaluations: add prior-approach results to Tables 3 and 4 and provide deeper insight into the SWCS results.
