- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple meanings, entailment, and uncertainty.
- Introduces a max-margin objective to train the Gaussian mixture word representations.
- Uses the expected likelihood kernel to measure similarity between word distributions, leveraging mean and covariance.
- Representation is more expressive than point embeddings by modeling multiple senses and uncertainty.
- Training via the max-margin objective and expected likelihood kernel is effective.
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on evaluated word similarity and entailment tasks.
- Evaluation is limited to a small set of tasks and datasets, possibly lacking generality.
- The model and kernel may be computationally expensive for large vocabularies.
- Lacks thorough analysis of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance.
- How was the optimal number of Gaussian components (K) chosen?
- Can you show insights into learned distributions for words with a single dominant meaning?
- What is the impact of using diagonal (anisotropic) covariances instead of spherical covariances?
- Please provide details on the subsampling method for balancing frequent vs. rare words.
- How does the approach handle out-of-vocabulary words or words with limited context?
