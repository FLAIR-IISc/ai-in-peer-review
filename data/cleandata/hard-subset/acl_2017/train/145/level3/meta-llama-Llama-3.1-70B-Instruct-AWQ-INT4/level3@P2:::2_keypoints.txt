- Proposes Gaussian mixture word embeddings to model polysemous words, using expected likelihood kernel and a max-margin learning objective.  
- Claims Gaussian mixtures capture multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty better than traditional embeddings.  
- Contribution: Introduces Gaussian Mixture Word Embeddings for multi-sense representation.  
- Contribution: Proposes a max-margin learning objective using the expected likelihood kernel for training.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates effectiveness on word similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Strength: Clearly motivates limitations of traditional word embeddings.  
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures provide more expressive representations than deterministic vectors.  
- Strength: Max-margin objective with closed-form similarity enables efficient training.  
- Strength: Paper is mostly well-written and easy to follow.  
- Weakness: Limited exploration of hyperparameters, e.g., number of Gaussian components.  
- Weakness: Evaluation is restricted to word similarity and entailment; lacks broader task/dataset coverage.  
- Weakness: No theoretical analysis of convergence properties or robustness to noise.  
- Question: Why was the number of Gaussian components k set to 2, what criteria determined this, and does increasing k hurt performance?  
- Question: How does the learned distribution behave for words with a single dominant meaning?  
- Question: Experiments use spherical covariances—was this for efficiency, and how would diagonal/anisotropic covariances affect results?  
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text.  
- Minor: Reference for Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
