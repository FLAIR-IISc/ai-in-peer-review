**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to word representation, using Gaussian mixtures to model polysemous words. The authors argue that traditional word embeddings, such as word2vec, are limited in their ability to capture multiple meanings of a word. In contrast, Gaussian mixtures can represent words with multiple distinct meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty. The authors propose a max-margin learning objective and use the expected likelihood kernel as a similarity measure to train the Gaussian mixture model. The paper presents experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. **Gaussian Mixture Word Embeddings**: The paper proposes a novel approach to word representation using Gaussian mixtures, which can capture multiple meanings of a word.
2. **Max-Margin Learning Objective**: The authors propose a max-margin learning objective that uses the expected likelihood kernel as a similarity measure to train the Gaussian mixture model.
3. **Effective Word Similarity and Entailment**: The paper presents experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Clearly Motivated Problem**: The paper clearly motivates the problem of word representation and the limitations of traditional approaches.
2. **Expressive Gaussian Mixtures**: Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors and can capture multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty.
3. **Efficient Training**: The max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity supports efficient training.
4. **Well-Written Paper**: The paper is mostly well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameters**: The paper does not provide a thorough exploration of hyperparameters, such as the number of Gaussian components.
2. **Limited Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the proposed approach on word similarity and entailment tasks, and does not consider other tasks or datasets.
3. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: The paper does not provide a theoretical analysis of the proposed approach, such as its convergence properties or robustness to noise.

**Questions to Authors**

1. **Why was the number of Gaussian components k set to 2, what criteria determined this, and does increasing k hurt performance?**
2. **How does the learned distribution look for words with only one dominant meaning?**
3. **All experiments use spherical covariances—was this purely for efficiency, and how would diagonal (anisotropic) covariances affect performance?**
