This paper proposes a novel approach to word representation by modeling words as Gaussian mixtures, allowing for the capture of multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty. The authors introduce a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure, enabling efficient training. The method is evaluated on word similarity and entailment tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Summary of the paper:**

The paper introduces a new approach to word representation by modeling words as Gaussian mixtures. This allows for the capture of multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty, which is not possible with traditional deterministic vector representations. The authors propose a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure, enabling efficient training. The method is evaluated on word similarity and entailment tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Main contributions:**

1. The paper proposes a new approach to word representation by modeling words as Gaussian mixtures, allowing for the capture of multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty.
2. The authors introduce a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure, enabling efficient training.
3. The method is evaluated on word similarity and entailment tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. The problem is clearly motivated and defined, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the limitations of traditional word representation methods.
2. Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors and can capture multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty, making them a more suitable choice for word representation.
3. The max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity supports efficient training, making the method scalable to large datasets.
4. The paper is mostly well-written, with clear explanations and concise language.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The number of Gaussian components (k) is set to 2, but the criteria for determining this value are not clearly explained. Increasing k may hurt performance, but this is not explored in the paper.
2. The learned distribution for words with only one dominant meaning is not explored in the paper.
3. All experiments use spherical covariances, which may be purely for efficiency. The effect of diagonal (anisotropic) covariances on performance is not explored.
4. Table 4 is not referenced in the text, and the reference for Luong et al. is missing the publication year.

**Questions to authors:**

1. Why was the number of Gaussian components (k) set to 2, and what criteria determined this choice? How does increasing k affect performance?
2. How does the learned distribution look for words with only one dominant meaning?
3. Was the use of spherical covariances purely for efficiency, and how would diagonal (anisotropic) covariances affect performance?
4. Can you provide more details on the training procedure, including the optimization algorithm and hyperparameter settings?

**Additional comments:**

The paper provides a novel approach to word representation and demonstrates its effectiveness on word similarity and entailment tasks. However, there are some limitations and unanswered questions that need to be addressed. The authors should provide more details on the training procedure and explore the effect of increasing the number of Gaussian components and using diagonal covariances. Additionally, the learned distribution for words with only one dominant meaning should be explored.
