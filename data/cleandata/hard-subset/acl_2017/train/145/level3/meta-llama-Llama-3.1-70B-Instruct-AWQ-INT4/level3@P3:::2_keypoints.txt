- Proposal: represent words as Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective and using the expected likelihood kernel for similarity.
- Contribution: introduce Gaussian mixtures as a more expressive word representation capturing multiple meanings and uncertainty.
- Contribution: develop an energy-based max-margin training objective for the Gaussian mixture model.
- Contribution: use the expected likelihood kernel as a closed-form similarity measure between Gaussians.
- Strength: problem is well-motivated with a clear discussion of limitations of existing embeddings.
- Strength: Gaussian mixtures capture multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty better than deterministic vectors.
- Strength: the max-margin objective and closed-form similarity enable efficient training.
- Strength: paper is mostly well-written with clear methodology and experiments.
- Weakness/question: unclear rationale for fixing the number of Gaussian components k = 2 and how performance changes with different k.
- Weakness/question: unclear how the model represents words with a single dominant meaning—does it still learn multimodal distributions?
- Weakness/question: use of spherical covariances is justified by efficiency, but the impact of diagonal (anisotropic) covariances on performance is unexplored.
- Minor issue: Table 4 is not referenced in the text.
- Minor issue: the citation to Luong et al. is missing a publication year.
- Suggestions: add more visualizations of learned distributions, especially for polysemous and single-meaning words.
- Suggestions: provide details on computational resources and training time.
- Suggestions: discuss limitations of the method and possible avenues for future work.
