This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings by representing each word as a Gaussian mixture distribution, allowing for multiple word meanings and uncertainty. The authors extend the work of Vilnis and McCallum (2014) from unimodal Gaussians to multimodal ones, addressing polysemy. The proposed energy-based max-margin objective and expected likelihood kernel energy function enable the learning of descriptive representations of words with superior performance on several benchmarks.

The paper is well-structured and clear, with a strong contribution in introducing multimodal word distributions to represent multiple word meanings. The experiments are correct, and the qualitative analysis matches expectations. The authors provide a thorough comparison with existing methods, including word2vec skip-grams and Gaussian embeddings.

However, the comparison against similar approaches could be extended. For instance, the authors could include a brief explanation of the differences from Tian et al. (2014), which also uses mixture models to split word representations. Additionally, the authors could include results from prior approaches in Tables 3 and 4 for comparison.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology and experiments. The use of tables and figures is effective in illustrating the results. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of their contributions and the implications of their work.

One question for the authors is why the w2gm model loses performance compared to the w2g model on the SWCS analysis. The authors may want to provide additional insights or explanations for this result.

In terms of contributions, the paper provides a novel approach to word embeddings, extending the work of Vilnis and McCallum (2014) to address polysemy. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including qualitative and quantitative analysis.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The introduction of multimodal word distributions to represent multiple word meanings
* The extension of Vilnis and McCallum (2014) to address polysemy
* The thorough evaluation of the approach, including qualitative and quantitative analysis
* The clear explanation of the methodology and experiments

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The comparison against similar approaches could be extended
* The authors could provide additional insights or explanations for the results on the SWCS analysis

The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including qualitative and quantitative analysis. The paper is well-structured and clear, with a strong contribution in introducing multimodal word distributions to represent multiple word meanings.
