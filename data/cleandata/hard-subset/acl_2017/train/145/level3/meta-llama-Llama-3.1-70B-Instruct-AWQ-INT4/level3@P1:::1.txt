**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to word embeddings, called Word to Gaussian Mixture (w2gm), which represents words as multimodal distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures. This allows the model to capture multiple word meanings, entailment, and uncertainty information. The authors introduce an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture, and propose an expected likelihood kernel as the energy function. The model is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, including word similarity and entailment tasks, and outperforms alternative methods such as word2vec and Gaussian embeddings.

**Contributions of the Work**

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. Introducing multimodal word distributions to represent multiple word meanings, which is a significant improvement over traditional point-based word embeddings.
2. Proposing an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture, which is a novel and effective approach to training word embeddings.
3. Evaluating the model on several benchmark datasets and demonstrating its superiority over alternative methods.

**Strengths of the Paper**

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow and understand. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and clearly explain the limitations of existing approaches. The proposed model is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

One potential weakness of the paper is that the comparison against similar approaches could be extended. For example, the authors could have included a more detailed comparison with Tian et al. (2014), which also uses mixture models to split word representations. Additionally, the authors could have included results from prior approaches in Tables 3 and 4 for comparison.

**Questions to Authors**

One question for the authors is why the w2gm model loses performance compared to the w2g model on the SCWS analysis. It would be helpful to understand the reasons behind this result and how it can be improved.
