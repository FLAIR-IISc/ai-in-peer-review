This paper proposes a novel approach to representing words as Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy, which is trained with a max-margin objective and uses the expected likelihood kernel as a similarity measure. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks, showcasing its ability to capture multiple meanings of words and uncertainty information.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) the introduction of Gaussian mixtures as a more expressive representation of words, allowing for the capture of multiple meanings and uncertainty information; (2) the development of an energy-based max-margin objective for training the Gaussian mixture model; and (3) the use of the expected likelihood kernel as a similarity measure, which can be evaluated in closed form.

The strengths of this paper are: (1) the problem is clearly motivated and defined, with a thorough discussion of the limitations of existing word embedding methods; (2) the use of Gaussian mixtures as a representation of words is more expressive than deterministic vectors and can capture multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty; (3) the max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity supports efficient training; and (4) the paper is mostly well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and experiments.

However, there are some weaknesses and questions that need to be addressed. One question is why the number of Gaussian components k was set to 2, and what criteria determined this choice. Increasing k may lead to overfitting, but it is unclear how the performance of the model would be affected. Another question is how the learned distribution looks for words with only one dominant meaning. It is possible that the model would still learn a multimodal distribution, but with one mode dominating the others.

Additionally, the paper uses spherical covariances for efficiency, but it is unclear how the performance of the model would be affected by using diagonal (anisotropic) covariances. The authors should provide more justification for their choice of spherical covariances and explore the impact of using diagonal covariances on the performance of the model.

Minor issues include the lack of reference to Table 4 in the text and the missing publication year for the reference to Luong et al.

Overall, this paper presents a novel approach to word embeddings that can capture multiple meanings and uncertainty information. While there are some weaknesses and questions that need to be addressed, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method and provides a clear explanation of the methodology and experiments.

Questions to Authors:

* Why was the number of Gaussian components k set to 2, and what criteria determined this choice?
* How does the learned distribution look for words with only one dominant meaning?
* How would the performance of the model be affected by using diagonal (anisotropic) covariances instead of spherical covariances?

Additional Comments:

* The paper could benefit from more visualizations of the learned distributions, particularly for words with multiple meanings.
* The authors should provide more details on the computational resources used for training the model and the time it took to train the model.
* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential avenues for future work.
