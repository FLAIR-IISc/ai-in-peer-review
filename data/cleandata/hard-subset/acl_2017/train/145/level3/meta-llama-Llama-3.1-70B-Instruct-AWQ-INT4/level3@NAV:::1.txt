This paper proposes a novel approach to word embeddings by introducing multimodal word distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures. The authors extend the work of Vilnis and McCallum (2014) from unimodal Gaussians to multimodal ones to address polysemy. The proposed model captures uniquely expressive semantic information and outperforms alternatives, such as word2vec skip-grams and Gaussian embeddings, on benchmark datasets.

**Summary of the paper**

The authors introduce a Gaussian mixture model to represent words with multiple distinct meanings. The model uses an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture. The energy function is based on the expected likelihood kernel, which is a generalization of an inner product between vectors to an inner product between distributions. The authors demonstrate that their model can capture multiple word senses and outperform other methods on word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Main contributions**

1. Introduction of multimodal word distributions formed from Gaussian mixtures to represent multiple word meanings.
2. Proposal of an energy-based max-margin objective to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture.
3. Use of the expected likelihood kernel as the energy function to capture the similarity between word distributions.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to word embeddings that can capture multiple word meanings.
2. The proposed model outperforms other methods on benchmark datasets.
3. The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their work.

**Weaknesses**

1. The comparison against similar approaches could be extended.
2. The authors could provide more details on the implementation and training of their model.

**Questions to authors**

1. Why does the w2gm model lose performance compared to the w2g model on the SWCS analysis?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the model?

**Suggested evaluation**

1. Include results from prior approaches in Tables 3 and 4 for comparison.
2. Provide more details on the evaluation metrics used and their justification.

**Additional comments**

1. The authors could provide more details on the computational complexity of their model and its scalability to large datasets.
