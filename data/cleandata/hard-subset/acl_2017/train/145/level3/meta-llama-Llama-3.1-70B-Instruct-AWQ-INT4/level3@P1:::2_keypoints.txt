- Proposes modeling words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple meanings, probability mass, and uncertainty.  
- Introduces a max-margin learning objective with a closed-form similarity measure for efficient training.  
- Evaluated on word similarity and entailment tasks, showing effectiveness.  
- Clearly motivated problem and thorough explanation of limitations of deterministic vectors.  
- Gaussian mixtures are more expressive than deterministic vectors and can model polysemy and uncertainty.  
- Max-margin objective with closed-form similarity enables scalable training on large datasets.  
- Paper is mostly well-written with clear and concise explanations.  
- The choice of number of Gaussian components (k=2) is unexplained and impacts are not explored.  
- Lack of analysis for words with a single dominant meaning and how their distributions look.  
- Experiments use spherical covariances only; effects of diagonal/anisotropic covariances are untested.  
- Table 4 is not referenced and a citation (Luong et al.) lacks a publication year.  
- Requests justification for k=2 and exploration of larger k and its performance effects.  
- Asks for visualization/analysis of learned distributions for single-meaning words.  
- Queries whether spherical covariances were chosen for efficiency and how diagonal covariances would affect results.  
- Requests more training details: optimization algorithm and hyperparameter settings.
