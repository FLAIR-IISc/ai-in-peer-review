**Paper Summary**

This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, representing each word as a multimodal distribution formed from Gaussian mixtures. This allows for the capture of multiple word meanings, entailment, and rich uncertainty information. The authors propose an energy-based max-margin objective to learn these distributions, which outperforms alternative methods on benchmark datasets such as word similarity and entailment.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Multimodal Word Distributions**: The authors introduce Gaussian mixture models to represent words, enabling the capture of multiple word meanings and uncertainty information.
2. **Energy-Based Max-Margin Objective**: The authors propose a novel objective function that leverages the expected likelihood kernel to learn the parameters of the Gaussian mixture model.
3. **Improved Performance on Benchmark Datasets**: The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms alternative methods on several word similarity and entailment datasets.

**Strengths**

1. **Clear and Well-Structured Paper**: The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach.
2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several benchmark datasets.
3. **Novel Contribution**: The authors introduce a new approach to word embeddings, which addresses the limitations of existing methods.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Comparison to Similar Approaches**: The authors could have extended their comparison to similar approaches, such as Tian et al. (2014), to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
2. **Lack of Explanation for Performance Drop**: The authors do not provide a clear explanation for why their approach loses performance compared to the Gaussian embedding model on the SWCS analysis.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Why does the Gaussian mixture model lose performance compared to the Gaussian embedding model on the SWCS analysis?
2. Can the authors provide more details on how their approach addresses the limitations of existing methods, such as Tian et al. (2014)?

**Additional Comments**

The authors should consider including a brief explanation of the differences between their approach and Tian et al. (2014), which also uses mixture models to split word representations. Additionally, the authors should include results from prior approaches in Tables 3 and 4 for comparison.
