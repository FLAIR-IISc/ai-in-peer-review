- Introduces w2gm: represents words as Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy, extending unimodal Gaussian embeddings.  
- Uses an energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel for analytic tractability and efficient learning.  
- Demonstrates improved performance on word similarity and entailment benchmarks and provides qualitative polysemy visualizations.  
- Novelty: multimodal Gaussian embeddings significantly improve representation of polysemous words.  
- Methodological rigor: well-motivated expected likelihood kernel and attention to numerical stability and initialization.  
- Comprehensive evaluation: quantitative and qualitative analyses with comparisons to baselines (word2vec, unimodal Gaussians) and multiprototype approaches.  
- Scalability: training scales to billions of tokens within days, offering practical advantages.  
- Limited comparison/discussion with Tian et al. (2014) and other multiprototype models.  
- Missing citations and discussion of Neelakantan et al. (2014) and Li & Jurafsky (2015).  
- Evaluation gaps: related approaches’ results omitted from key tables, hindering direct comparison.  
- Unexplained performance drop of w2gm vs w2g on SCWS, raising questions about robustness.  
- Q: Explain reasons for w2gm’s SCWS performance drop — metric or dataset issue?  
- Q: How does w2gm compare methodologically and empirically to Neelakantan et al. (2014) and Li & Jurafsky (2015)?  
- Q: Have you considered incorporating external lexical resources (e.g., WordNet) to improve polysemy modeling?  
- Conclusion: strong, novel contribution with promising results but needs more thorough related-work comparison and additional baselines; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
