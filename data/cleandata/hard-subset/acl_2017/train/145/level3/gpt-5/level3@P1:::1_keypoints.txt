- Proposes w2gm: represent words as mixtures of Gaussians to model polysemy, uncertainty, and asymmetric relations, trained with a max-margin energy objective using the expected likelihood kernel.
- Contribution: scalable multisense probabilistic embeddings replacing unimodal Gaussians with Gaussian mixtures to capture distinct senses and per-sense uncertainty.
- Contribution: principled, efficient energy function (expected likelihood kernel) matched to mixtures with closed-form energies enabling large-scale training.
- Contribution: empirical gains and qualitative evidence showing better multisense modeling and improved results over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian baselines on several benchmarks and entailment.
- Strength: Clear motivation that unimodal Gaussians diffuse variance across senses, and mixtures provide interpretable, tighter per-sense uncertainty.
- Strength: Methodologically sound and tractable—expected likelihood kernel, practical stabilization tricks, and careful training details are well described.
- Strength: Strong qualitative nearest-neighbor evidence that components capture distinct senses (e.g., rock, bank, Apple).
- Strength: Solid empirical performance on many word similarity datasets and improved entailment metrics using asymmetric KL with mixtures.
- Strength: Demonstrated scalability and reproducibility—trained on 3.5B tokens with implementation details provided.
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to key multisense baselines (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015) in discussion and tables.
- Weakness: Insufficient differentiation from Tian et al. (2014) — needs clearer conceptual and empirical contrasts regarding sense stability, mixture weights, and uncertainty benefits.
- Weakness: Mixed results and sensitivity to similarity metric (cosine/Euclidean/expected likelihood) raise concerns about metric choice and potential cherry-picking.
- Weakness: Training uses symmetric expected-likelihood but evaluation on entailment uses asymmetric KL, creating a training-testing objective mismatch that could be addressed.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of model capacity and sense allocation—sensitivity to K, component collapse/redundancy, and mixture weight distributions are underexplored.
- Question: Why does w2gm underperform w2g on SCWS—issue with scoring, sense selection, or domain mismatch?
- Question: Which similarity metric do the authors recommend for downstream use, and can a unified metric be reported across evaluations?
- Question: Did the authors try asymmetric training objectives (e.g., KL) to better align with entailment evaluation, and what are the computational barriers?
- Question: How sensitive is performance to K, do components collapse or become redundant, and how skewed are mixture weights across the vocabulary?
- Suggestion: Add and discuss the missing related works (Neelakantan et al., Li & Jurafsky, Liu et al.) and, if feasible, include their results in comparison tables.
- Suggestion: Clarify differences from Tian et al. (2014) both methodologically (probabilistic vs point senses; uncertainty) and empirically.
- Suggestion: Consider an ablation on covariance parameterization (spherical vs diagonal) to assess effects on similarity vs entailment.
- Closing: Addressing missing baselines and clarifying SCWS and Tian et al. comparisons would substantially strengthen the paper’s positioning and empirical case.
