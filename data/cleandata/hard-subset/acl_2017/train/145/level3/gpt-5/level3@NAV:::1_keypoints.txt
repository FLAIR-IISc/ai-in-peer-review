- Proposes w2gm: a tractable multimodal (Gaussian-mixture) word embedding model with an analytic expected-likelihood kernel to represent polysemy, uncertainty, and asymmetric relations.  
- Provides empirical evidence (qualitative sense separation and improved scores) showing benefits over word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings.  
- Includes practical training/engineering details enabling scalability to billion-token corpora.

- Conceptual advance: moving from unimodal to mixtures addresses variance-diffusion for polysemy and enables analytic, efficient training.  
- Strong qualitative analysis: clear sense disentanglement examples (e.g., rock, bank, cell) that align with linguistic expectations.  
- Solid empirical performance: consistent gains on many similarity datasets and lexical entailment using sensible metrics (e.g., KL).  
- Clear exposition and reproducible engineering choices (stability tricks, covariance options).  
- Demonstrated scalability with straightforward optimizers on large corpora.

- Missing comparisons to key multi-sense baselines and citations (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015) and their results should be included.  
- Needs explicit contrast with Tian et al. (2014) regarding mixture-of-senses via EM vs. probabilistic mixtures, training objectives, and inference.  
- Limited ablation studies (number of components K, kernel choice vs. KL, covariance structure, margin/negative sampling effects).  
- Mixed results on SCWS/SimLex; paper should discuss when mixtures help or hurt.

- Why does w2gm lose on SCWS relative to w2g—does MaxSim over-focus or are mixture weights underutilized?  
- How sensitive are results to K and mixture-weight initialization—do components collapse or proliferate?  
- Please add direct comparisons to Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), Liu et al. (2015) in the main tables.
