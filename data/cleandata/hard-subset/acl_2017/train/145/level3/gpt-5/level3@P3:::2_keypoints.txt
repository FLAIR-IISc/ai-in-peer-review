- Proposes w2gm: represent each word as a Gaussian mixture to capture polysemy, probability mass, and uncertainty, trained with a max-margin loss and an expected likelihood kernel (ELK) for closed-form similarity.
- Demonstrates qualitative sense separation and quantitative gains over word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on multiple word similarity datasets and lexical entailment.
- Provides a practical, scalable training recipe (negative sampling, numerical stability tricks, closed-form energies) and shows reduced variance for polysemous words versus unimodal Gaussians.
- Clear problem motivation and formulation: mixtures naturally model multiple senses and address limitations of point vectors and unimodal Gaussians.
- Expressive representation with principled uncertainty: mixtures allow multiple modes and anisotropy; ELK combines mean proximity and covariance structure; qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses are convincing.
- Efficient and tractable learning: ELK yields closed-form mixture energies, enabling scalable training on large corpora.
- Solid empirical evidence on standard benchmarks with competitive/superior Spearman correlations and improved entailment AP/F1 using asymmetric KL; useful ablations between similarity measures.
- Well written with detailed implementation notes aiding reproducibility (initialization, constraints, optimizer choices).
- Limited exploration of number of components K: mostly K=2, anecdotal K=3 examples, no systematic study of K’s effect on performance, cost, or component usage.
- Covariance restrictions undermine expressivity: main experiments use spherical covariances, diagonal only in supplement and not evaluated, leaving anisotropy benefits unquantified.
- Gains are moderate and uneven, with limited extrinsic evaluation; improvements aren’t universal and broader downstream tasks and statistical tests are missing.
- Ablations are thin: unclear how much gains come from mixtures vs energy/objective vs improved training; more controlled comparisons needed (K=1 vs K=2 with identical settings).
- Uncertainty evaluation is qualitative and anecdotal; lacks systematic analyses of calibration, component overlap, and sense allocations.
- Question: Why choose K=2 as default—report performance vs K, runtime vs K, and component usage entropy.
- Question: For monosemous words, do weights concentrate on one component or do components co-locate—provide weight entropy and inter-component distance stats.
- Question: Were spherical covariances chosen for efficiency/stability—provide diagonal vs spherical comparison for similarity, entailment, and runtime.
- Question: Any experiments training with ELK+KL or other divergences, and sensitivity to margin m?
- Question: Sensitivity to window size and negative sampling—effects on polysemy separation?
- Question: Do you observe component collapse or dead components, and was any regularization (e.g., Dirichlet priors) used?
- Additional: Reference Table 4 explicitly and fix Luong et al. RW dataset year in references.
- Suggested analyses to strengthen claims: report mixture weight entropy distributions, sense purity vs SCWS/WordNet, calibration plots relating covariance magnitude to semantic variability, and effect of K on SCWS.
