- Paper proposes modeling words as multimodal probability distributions (Gaussian mixtures) trained with an energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel to capture polysemy and uncertainty.

- Contribution: Scalable probabilistic multi-sense embeddings extending Vilnis & McCallum (2014) from unimodal to multimodal distributions with analytically tractable expected-likelihood energies.

- Contribution: Empirical evidence that multimodal distributions improve interpretability and entailment, and are competitive or superior on several similarity benchmarks with qualitative sense separation.

- Contribution: Practical training recipe (diagonal/spherical covariances, stabilized log-sum-exp, Adagrad) enabling stable, large-scale (billion-token) training.

- Strength: Clear, well-structured presentation with derivations and numerical-stability explanations; figures and tables effectively demonstrate sense separation.

- Strength: Sound probabilistic formulation—ELK gives closed-form, tractable energies for GMMs avoiding KL intractability and aligning with distributional objectives.

- Strength: Strong qualitative interpretability—component-wise nearest neighbors match known senses and polysemous words show reduced variances.

- Strength: Competitive empirical results—improvements over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussians on many similarity datasets and entailment; multiple similarity measures evaluated.

- Strength: Demonstrated scalability and engineering practicality for large corpora.

- Weakness: Incomplete empirical comparison—omits key multi-prototype baselines (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015) that should be included in tables and SCWS.

- Weakness: Limited discussion of differences vs. Tian et al. (2014); needs concrete comparison of objectives, inference, and trade-offs.

- Weakness: Missing ablations—more analysis needed on number of components K, covariance structure (spherical vs diagonal), and training energy choice (ELK vs approximate KL).

- Weakness: Mixed SCWS/contextual similarity results; requires deeper analysis of why w2gm sometimes underperforms w2g and whether context scoring or K causes issues.

- Weakness: Narrow task scope—no extrinsic downstream evaluation or comparison to context-sensitive multi-sense encoders to demonstrate broader impact.

- Question: Why does w2gm sometimes lose to w2g on SCWS—due to MaxSim/AveSim usage, mixture-weight estimation, small K, or lack of context-dependent component selection?

- Question: Please provide a concrete comparison to Tian et al. (2014) covering objective, learning algorithm, sense assignment, uncertainty modeling, and computational pros/cons.

- Question: How sensitive are results to K, and would nonparametric or data-driven K selection/pruning reduce redundant or collapsed components?

- Question: Any experiments or observations on diagonal vs spherical covariances regarding performance, interpretability, and stability?

- Question: Did the authors try approximate KL-based training for mixtures (variational or Monte Carlo) and what are feasibility/benefit considerations?

- Suggestion: Add and discuss missing citations (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015) and include their results in relevant tables.

- Suggestion: Include SCWS baselines from Tian et al., Neelakantan et al., and Li & Jurafsky for fair contextual-similarity comparison.

- Suggestion: Clarify handling of input/output mixtures at test time (tie, average, discard) and any performance impact for reproducibility.

- Overall recommendation: Strong, well-executed paper with impactful idea and solid evidence; would benefit from expanded related-work comparisons and deeper SCWS analysis to strengthen the final version.
