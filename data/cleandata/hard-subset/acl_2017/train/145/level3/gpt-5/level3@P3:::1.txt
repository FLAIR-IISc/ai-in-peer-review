Summary
This paper proposes representing each word as a multimodal probability distribution, specifically a Gaussian mixture, and learning these distributions with an energy-based max-margin objective that uses the expected likelihood kernel (probability product kernel) as the similarity (energy) function. The approach targets polysemy and uncertainty, aiming to capture multiple distinct meanings per word, retain entailment structure, and improve interpretability. The method scales to large corpora and is evaluated on word similarity, contextual similarity, and lexical entailment, with qualitative analyses showing clean sense separation.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A scalable, probabilistic multi-sense embedding: modeling words as mixtures of Gaussians and training them with an analytically tractable expected-likelihood energy and margin-based objective. This meaningfully extends Vilnis & McCallum (2014) from unimodal to multimodal distributions to address polysemy.
2) Empirical evidence that multimodal distributions improve interpretability and entailment, and are competitive or superior on several standard similarity benchmarks, with qualitative analyses (e.g., Table 1) that align with linguistic expectations.
3) Practical training recipe for stability and efficiency at scale (diagonal/spherical covariances, stabilized log-sum-exp for mixture energies, Adagrad schedule), enabling billion-token training.

Strengths
- Clear, well-structured, and readable presentation: The paper motivates the move from point and unimodal embeddings to mixtures, carefully derives the expected likelihood kernel for mixtures, and explains numerical stability steps. Figures and Table 1 compellingly demonstrate the core intuition (clean sense separation for rock, bank, cell).
- Sound and tractable probabilistic formulation: Using the expected likelihood kernel gives closed-form energies for mixtures, which is both elegant and practical, avoiding intractabilities of KL for GMMs. The objective aligns with distributional learning and is a natural extension of skip-gram ranking.
- Strong qualitative interpretability: The component-wise nearest neighbors align with known senses, and the observed variance reduction for polysemous words is consistent with the intended behavior (unimodal baselines diffuse variance to cover multiple senses).
- Competitive to strong empirical results: The mixture model improves over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussians on many word similarity datasets and on lexical entailment; the evaluation across multiple similarity measures (cosine, ELK, Euclidean) is thorough and informative.
- Scalability and engineering: Training on billions of tokens with simple diagonal/spherical covariances, stable numerics, and efficient optimization demonstrates practical viability.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete comparison to closely related multi-sense baselines: Beyond Huang et al. (2012), the empirical comparison omits several key multi-prototype methods, notably Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015). Including these in Tables 3 and 4 (and SCWS) would better situate the contribution.
- Limited discussion of differences vs. Tian et al. (2014): Tian also uses mixture-style sense induction (with an EM procedure over skip-gram). A succinct but concrete comparison—objective, inference (hard/soft sense assignment), uncertainty modeling, and computational trade-offs—would clarify novelty and practical advantages.
- Some evaluation gaps/ablations: The paper would benefit from ablations on (i) number of components K, beyond the brief K=3 note; (ii) covariance structure (spherical vs. diagonal) and its impact on results and entailment; (iii) training energy choice (ELK vs. approximate KL), at least in a small-scale study, to validate the design choice.
- Mixed results on contextual similarity: The SCWS results (as suggested by the text) indicate cases where w2gm does not outperform w2g. This should be analyzed more deeply (e.g., sense granularity mismatch, mixture-weight estimation, or context scoring method), since contextual similarity is precisely where multi-sense models often shine.
- Scope of tasks: The evaluation focuses on standard word-level benchmarks. An extrinsic task (e.g., downstream classification or entailment-driven inference) or comparison to context-sensitive multi-sense encoders would further substantiate impact.

Questions to Authors
- SCWS performance: Why does w2gm lose performance compared to w2g on SCWS in some settings? Is this due to the use of MaxSim/AveSim without explicit context disambiguation, mixture-weight estimation accuracy, or the small K? Could context-dependent component selection improve results?
- Relation to Tian et al. (2014): Please include a brief, concrete comparison detailing methodological differences (objective, learning algorithm, sense assignment, use of uncertainty), and expected pros/cons in practice.
- Component number and selection: How sensitive are results to K? Could a nonparametric or data-driven selection of K (or pruning) help? Do you observe redundant or collapsed components?
- Covariance structure: Did you try diagonal vs. spherical covariances on key tasks (similarity/entailment)? Any trade-offs between interpretability, stability, and performance?
- Training energy: Did you attempt approximate KL-based training for mixtures (e.g., variational bounds or Monte Carlo approximations)? A brief discussion of feasibility and expected benefits would be helpful.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Missing citations: Please add Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015), and discuss them in related work. Where possible, include their results alongside yours in Tables 3 and 4 for a fairer comparison.
- Baselines on SCWS: Include Tian et al. (2014), Neelakantan et al. (2014), and Li & Jurafsky (2015) numbers on SCWS, as they are canonical contextual similarity baselines.
- Clarify input/output distributions: You maintain separate input/output mixtures; a short note on whether you tie, average, or discard output parameters at test time, and any performance impact, would improve reproducibility.
- Author response: I have read the authors’ response. It is helpful, but a fuller comparative discussion and additional baselines would strengthen the final version.

Overall recommendation
A strong, well-executed paper with a clear and impactful idea, solid methodology, and convincing qualitative/quantitative evidence. With expanded related-work comparisons and a deeper analysis of SCWS behavior, this would be an excellent contribution.
