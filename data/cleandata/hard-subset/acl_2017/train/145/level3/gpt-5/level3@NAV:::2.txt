Summary and contributions
- Primary: Introduces a probabilistic multi-prototype word representation where each word is modeled as a Gaussian mixture; similarity is computed with the expected likelihood kernel (ELK), enabling closed-form energies within a max-margin objective. This extends unimodal Gaussian embeddings to capture polysemy with uncertainty.
- Secondary: Demonstrates scalable training and efficient inference due to the closed-form ELK and margin-based learning, with practical stability tweaks.
- Tertiary: Provides empirical evidence on word similarity and lexical entailment showing competitive or superior performance to skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings, alongside qualitative analyses that reveal distinct senses.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem setup: The limitations of point and unimodal Gaussian embeddings for polysemy are articulated compellingly, and the mixture formulation directly targets this gap.
- Expressivity and interpretability: Mixtures naturally encode multiple senses, uncertainty, and partial entailment (overlap/inclusion of mass). The qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses convincingly illustrate sense separation (e.g., bank, rock).
- Efficient learning via closed-form ELK: The expected likelihood kernel provides an analytically tractable, symmetric similarity that integrates means and covariances, making the max-margin objective practical at scale.
- Solid empirical results: On several similarity benchmarks (e.g., WS, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW) and lexical entailment, the method outperforms unimodal Gaussians and is competitive with or better than strong baselines; using KL for entailment is appropriate and beneficial.
- Writing and reproducibility: The paper is mostly well written, with useful implementation details and an interactive visualization that enhances interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Unjustified model capacity: The choice K=2 is presented as default without principled selection or ablation; it is unclear when increasing K helps or hurts, and whether overfitting or instability arises for larger K.
- Covariance restrictions: All experiments use spherical covariances; this limits anisotropy and may blunt the benefits of uncertainty modeling. The trade-off versus diagonal covariances is not quantified.
- Limited evaluation of uncertainty: While uncertainty is central, there is no calibration analysis (e.g., variance vs. frequency/sense ambiguity) or tasks where uncertainty directly matters.
- Intrinsic-task focus: Results are mainly on similarity and entailment; downstream tasks (e.g., WSD, NLI, QA) would strengthen claims about practical utility.
- Minor presentation issues: Table 4 is not referenced; the Luong et al. citation lacks a publication year.

Questions to authors
- Why fix K=2? What criteria guided this choice, and how do performance and training stability vary with K (e.g., K=1,2,3,4) across similarity and entailment?
- For monosemous words, do mixtures collapse (one dominant weight, tight covariance)? Please provide distributional summaries or examples.
- Were spherical covariances chosen purely for efficiency? What is the effect of diagonal covariances on accuracy, runtime, and stability?
- How sensitive are results to the margin m and to the choice of similarity at evaluation time (ELK vs. max-cosine/min-Euclidean)?
