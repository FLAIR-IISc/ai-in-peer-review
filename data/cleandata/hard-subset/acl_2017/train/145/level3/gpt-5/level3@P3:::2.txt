Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes representing each word as a Gaussian mixture distribution (w2gm), enabling multi-prototype embeddings that capture polysemy, probability mass, and uncertainty. Training uses a max-margin ranking loss over positive/negative contexts with an expected likelihood kernel (ELK) as a closed-form distributional similarity.
- It demonstrates qualitative sense separation and quantitative gains over word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on multiple word similarity datasets and lexical entailment.
- It provides a practical, scalable training recipe (negative sampling, stability tricks, and efficient closed-form energies), and shows reduced variance for polysemous words relative to unimodal Gaussians.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and formulation (primary). The limitations of point vectors and unimodal Gaussians for polysemy are articulated crisply. Modeling words as mixtures is a natural extension with clear semantic interpretations (modes = senses).
- Expressive representation with principled uncertainty. Mixtures allow multiple modes and encode anisotropy/uncertainty; the ELK energy combines mean proximity and covariance structure. The qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses for rock, bank, Apple, cell convincingly show sense separation.
- Efficient and tractable learning. Using ELK yields closed-form energies for mixtures, avoiding approximate KL between GMMs. Together with the max-margin objective and pragmatic numerical tricks, the method trains at scale (billions of tokens).
- Solid empirical evidence on standard benchmarks. The model achieves competitive or superior Spearman correlations on WS/WS-R/MEN/MC/RG/YP/MT-287/RW and improves entailment AP/F1 when using asymmetric KL. The ablation between similarity measures (cosine vs ELK vs Euclidean) is helpful.
- Generally well written and easy to follow. The methodology and implementation details (initialization, constraints, optimizer choices) are clearly documented, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited exploration of number of components K. Most results fix K=2, with only anecdotal K=3 examples and the claim that “limited words with >2 meanings” yield no gains. There is no systematic study of K’s effect on performance, training cost (O(K^2) pairwise terms), or component usage (e.g., weight entropy, collapse).
- Covariance restrictions undermine expressivity. All main experiments use spherical covariances; diagonal is relegated to the supplement and not evaluated. This likely undercuts the claimed uncertainty benefits; anisotropy may matter for entailment and fine-grained similarity. The efficiency/accuracy trade-off is not quantified.
- Gains are moderate and uneven, with limited extrinsic evaluation. Improvements over strong point baselines are not universal (e.g., SimLex), and the paper lacks extrinsic tasks (e.g., downstream tagging, QA, NLI) where polysemy modeling should help. The entailment gains, while consistent, would benefit from broader benchmarks and statistical testing.
- Ablations isolating the source of gains are thin. It is unclear how much improvement comes from mixtures vs the energy/objective vs better training details. A controlled comparison with K=1 and K=2 under identical settings using ELK is only partially visible; more ablations (e.g., ELK vs cosine energy during training, fixed vs learned covariances) would strengthen causal claims.
- Evaluation of uncertainty quality is qualitative. Apart from a variance anecdote for rock, there is no systematic analysis of uncertainty calibration, component overlap, or sense allocations on monosemous vs polysemous words.

Questions to authors
- Choice of K: Why was K=2 adopted as the default? What criteria (held-out performance, computational budget, sense usage statistics) guided this choice? Does increasing K (e.g., to 3–5) consistently help, plateau, or hurt due to overfitting/optimization? Please report performance vs K, runtime vs K, and component usage entropy.
- Monosemous words: For words with one dominant sense, do mixture weights concentrate on a single component (near-delta) or do components co-locate? Please include statistics on weight entropy and inter-component distances, and examples illustrating the learned distributions for monosemous words.
- Covariance structure: Were spherical covariances chosen purely for efficiency/numerical stability? How do diagonal covariances affect similarity and entailment, and what is the runtime impact? Given you already derive the diagonal case, a small-scale comparison (spherical vs diagonal) would be valuable.
- Energy function: You train with ELK due to closed form for mixtures, but evaluate entailment with KL. Have you tried training with an ELK+KL composite or using symmetric divergences (e.g., Jensen–Shannon) at test time? Any sensitivity to the margin m?
- Negative sampling and window size: How sensitive are results to window size (you report 5 vs 10 for entailment) and negative sampling distribution? Any differences in polysemy separation with narrower windows?
- Component collapse: Do you observe mode collapse or dead components? Any regularization (e.g., Dirichlet priors on mixture weights) needed?

Additional comments
- Please reference Table 4 explicitly in the text and ensure it is described; it appears unreferenced.
- The reference for Luong et al. (RW dataset) is missing the publication year; please fix.
- To strengthen claims about uncertainty and polysemy, consider reporting: (i) mixture weight entropy distributions, (ii) sense purity vs SCWS or WordNet senses, (iii) calibration plots relating covariance magnitude to semantic variability, and (iv) effect of K on SCWS.
