Summary and contributions
This paper extends probabilistic word embeddings from unimodal Gaussians to mixtures, learning multimodal (Gaussian-mixture) word distributions with an energy-based max-margin objective and an expected-likelihood kernel. The central idea is to represent polysemy explicitly while retaining uncertainty and entailment structure. Claimed contributions, as I see them:
1) A tractable multimodal probabilistic embedding model (w2gm) that captures multiple senses, uncertainty, and asymmetric relations via mixture covariances and a closed-form kernel energy.
2) Empirical evidence—qualitative sense separation and improved results on several similarity and entailment benchmarks—demonstrating the benefits over word2vec and unimodal Gaussians.
3) Practical training details that make the approach scalable to billion-token corpora.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance with clear motivation: Moving from unimodal to mixture distributions addresses the variance-diffusion problem for polysemous words in Gaussian embeddings; the expected-likelihood kernel makes training analytic and efficient.
- Strong qualitative analysis: Table 1 convincingly shows sense disentanglement (e.g., rock, bank, cell), aligning with linguistic expectations and illustrating entailment-like containment.
- Solid empirical performance: Consistent gains on many similarity datasets (WS/WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, RW) and lexical entailment, with sensible metric choices (KL for entailment).
- Well-written and well-structured: The exposition, derivations, and engineering choices (stability tricks, diagonal/spherical covariances) are clear and reproducible.
- Scalability: Demonstrated training on multi-billion-token corpora with straightforward optimizers and implementation details.

Weaknesses
- Comparative coverage: Missing key multi-sense baselines and citations—please add Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015)—and include their results in Tables 3 and 4 for fair comparison.
- Relation to Tian et al. (2014): Needs a brief but explicit contrast (e.g., mixture-of-senses via EM vs. probabilistic mixtures with uncertainty/entailment; training objectives and inference differences).
- Limited ablations: The paper would benefit from analyses on number of components K, kernel choice vs. KL, covariance structure (diagonal vs. spherical), and margin/negative sampling effects.
- Mixed results on SCWS/SimLex: w2gm does not uniformly outperform w2g; a discussion of when mixtures help or hurt would strengthen claims.

Questions to authors
- On SCWS, why does w2gm lose performance relative to w2g? Is MaxSim over-focusing on a single component, or are mixture weights underutilized?
- How sensitive are results to K and initialization of mixture weights? Do components collapse or proliferate?
- Could you add direct comparisons to Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), Liu et al. (2015) in the main tables?
