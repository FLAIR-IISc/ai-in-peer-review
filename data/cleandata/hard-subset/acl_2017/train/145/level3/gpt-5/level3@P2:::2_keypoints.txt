- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixture distributions to model polysemy and uncertainty more naturally than unimodal Gaussians or deterministic vectors.  
- Uses an energy-based max-margin training objective with the expected likelihood (probability product) kernel to obtain a closed-form similarity for mixtures and enable efficient training.  
- Claims empirical improvements on multiple intrinsic word similarity benchmarks and lexical entailment, plus interpretable multi-sense neighborhoods.  

- Strongly motivated: clearly articulates limitations of unimodal embeddings and shows qualitative examples where mixtures help (e.g., rock, bank, cell).  
- Probabilistically grounded and interpretable: mixture weights and covariances allow encoding sense salience, specificity, and inclusion relationships for entailment.  
- Training is sound and efficient: max-margin loss with expected likelihood yields analytic energies for mixtures and practical numerical/stability tricks are provided.  
- Good empirical results and interpretability: outperforms skip-gram and unimodal Gaussians on many benchmarks; uses asymmetric KL at test time for entailment and shows sensible nearest neighbors.  
- Well written with reproducibility details and interactive visualization aiding interpretation.  

- Incremental novelty: mainly extends unimodal Gaussian embeddings to mixtures and replaces KL with expected likelihood; lacks isolation of which components (means, covariances, weights) drive gains.  
- Fixed small K (mostly K=2) with no principled selection or per-word adaptive K, and only brief mention of K=3 without systematic study.  
- Covariance restriction to spherical form limits anisotropic uncertainty modeling; unclear whether gains come from multiple means or better uncertainty modeling.  
- Evaluation limited to intrinsic tasks and entailment with no extrinsic downstream tests, ablations, or statistical significance analyses; max-over-components test-time scoring may underuse mixture weights.  
- Computational cost and scalability claims lack quantitative analysis (memory, training time, parameter counts vs. baselines).  

- Why fix K=2 for most experiments — what criteria guided this choice and how do performance, stability, and memory scale with K?  
- For single-sense words, do mixtures collapse (overlapping means or degenerate weights) or does the extra component produce spurious neighbors?  
- Why use spherical covariances — was this purely for efficiency/stability, and do diagonal/full covariances improve results or cause failure modes?  
- Did you try weight-aware or expected-similarity scoring (using mixture weights) or context-conditioned sense selection instead of max-over-components, and how sensitive are results to metric choice?  
- What are training-time and memory costs compared to word2vec (per-word parameter count, total footprint, training time); sensitivity to margin and learning rate schedules?
