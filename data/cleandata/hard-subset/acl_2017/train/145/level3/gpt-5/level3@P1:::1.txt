Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes representing each word as a multimodal probability distribution, specifically a mixture of Gaussians (w2gm), to better model polysemy, uncertainty, and asymmetric relations such as entailment. Training uses an energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel for tractability.
- Main contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
  1) A scalable probabilistic multisense embedding that replaces unimodal Gaussian word embeddings with Gaussian mixtures, enabling distinct modes for different senses while preserving uncertainty modeling.
  2) A principled and efficient energy function (expected likelihood kernel) matched to mixtures, yielding closed-form energies and enabling robust training on very large corpora.
  3) Empirical evidence that the approach captures multiple senses qualitatively and improves over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian baselines on several word similarity datasets and on lexical entailment.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and well-justified design: The paper convincingly argues that unimodal Gaussians diffuse variance to cover multiple senses, leading to poorer representations for polysemous words. Using mixtures directly addresses this with interpretable modes and tighter per-sense uncertainty.
- Methodological soundness and tractability: Adopting the expected likelihood kernel yields a closed-form, symmetric energy well aligned with the mixture structure, unlike KL for mixtures. The training objective and practical stabilization details (diagonal/spherical covariances, log-parameterization, subsampling, negative sampling, softmax mixture weights) are carefully thought out and well described.
- Strong qualitative evidence of multisense modeling: The nearest-neighbor analyses (e.g., rock, bank, Apple, star, cell) cleanly split senses across components, with neighbors that match semantic expectations. This aligns with the core claim and is easy to interpret.
- Solid empirical performance and analysis: On many word similarity benchmarks (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW) the mixture model outperforms skip-gram and unimodal Gaussians. On entailment, the asymmetric KL scoring plus mixtures improves AP and F1 over unimodal Gaussians for both window sizes. The analysis of variance reduction for polysemous words is insightful and consistent with the qualitative findings.
- Scalability and reproducibility: Training on 3.5B tokens with simple hardware and the inclusion of implementation details (negative sampling distribution, subsampling, initialization, learning rates, margins) are valuable for adoption. The visualization and discussion of K=3 are nice additions.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparison to closely related multisense embeddings: Several strong baselines are missing in both discussion and tables. In particular, Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015) are not cited or compared. Including their results (or at least a discussion of differences in setup and trade-offs) is important to situate the contribution relative to multisense skip-gram, nonparametric sense induction, and context-selective embeddings.
- Insufficient differentiation from Tian et al. (2014): While Tian et al. also learns multiple embeddings per word using an EM-like assignment, the paper could more explicitly articulate how w2gm compares conceptually and empirically. For example, is sense separation more stable/consistent? Do mixture weights carry interpretable sense priors? Does uncertainty improve entailment beyond multiple point vectors?
- Mixed results and metric sensitivity: The method sometimes underperforms the unimodal Gaussian baseline (e.g., SimLex), and performance depends on the chosen similarity metric (cosine vs Euclidean vs expected likelihood). A more principled choice or justification for a single evaluation metric would reduce the appearance of metric cherry-picking and clarify when uncertainty-aware energies help.
- Asymmetric training vs testing mismatch: The model is trained with a symmetric expected-likelihood energy but evaluated on entailment with asymmetric KL. Exploring asymmetric training objectives (or combining symmetric + asymmetric energies) might further boost entailment performance and would strengthen the methodological story.
- Limited analysis of model capacity and sense allocation: The discussion on K focuses on K=2 with a brief note on K=3. A deeper analysis of sensitivity to K, component collapse or redundancy, and how mixture weights distribute across vocabulary would make the modeling claims more robust. Similarly, clarifying whether sense components get used consistently across contexts (without explicit EM-style assignments) would be helpful.

Questions to authors
- SCWS: In the SCWS evaluation, w2gm appears to lose ground to w2g in some settings. Can you explain why the mixture model underperforms there? Is it due to the scoring function, sense selection mechanism, or data-domain mismatch?
- Metric choice: Given the variability across cosine, Euclidean, and expected likelihood metrics, what metric do you recommend for downstream use? Could you report a unified metric choice across all evaluations for fair comparison?
- Asymmetry in training: Did you try training with an asymmetric objective (e.g., KL or an approximation) to better align with entailment evaluation? If not, what are the computational obstacles?
- K selection and stability: How sensitive is performance to K? Do you observe mode collapse or overly redundant components, and how often do mixture weights become highly skewed?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Please include and discuss the following missing related works: Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015). If feasible, add their results to the comparison tables (e.g., WordSim-353, SCWS) to more convincingly establish empirical gains.
- Clarify the differences from Tian et al. (2014), both methodologically (probabilistic distributions vs multiple point senses; uncertainty and entailment) and empirically.
- Consider a brief ablation on covariance parameterization (spherical vs diagonal) and its effect on similarity vs entailment.
- I have read the authors’ response. If you add the missing baselines and clarify the SCWS behavior and relation to Tian et al., the paper’s positioning and empirical case will be considerably stronger.
