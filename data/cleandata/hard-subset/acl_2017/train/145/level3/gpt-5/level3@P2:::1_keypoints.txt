- Proposes representing words as mixtures of Gaussians trained with an ELK-based max-margin objective to model polysemy, entailment, and uncertainty.

- Main contribution: probabilistic multi-prototype embeddings (Gaussian mixtures) with a tractable ELK training objective enabling uncertainty-aware similarity and asymmetric entailment scoring.

- Main contribution: practical, scalable training recipe (initialization, stability tricks, diagonal/spherical covariances, negative sampling, Adagrad) that trains on multi-billion-token corpora and yields interpretable components.

- Main contribution: empirical improvements over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on many word similarity datasets and entailment tasks, plus qualitative analyses and an interactive visualization.

- Strength: Conceptual clarity—extends unimodal Gaussian embeddings to mixtures while preserving analytic tractability via ELK.

- Strength: Strong qualitative evidence of sense separation (clean nearest-neighbor disambiguation and interactive demo).

- Strength: Competitive quantitative performance across standard similarity datasets and improved entailment when using KL at test time.

- Strength: Scalable and reproducible methodology with clear implementation details and promise to release code.

- Strength: Clear writing, good organization, and informative figures and tables.

- Weakness: Limited direct comparison to prior multi-sense/multi-prototype methods (Neelakantan et al., Tian et al., Li & Jurafsky, Liu et al.); missing head-to-head results in main tables.

- Weakness: Incomplete discussion of benefits/trade-offs versus deterministic multi-prototype approaches; needs clearer articulation of advantages of probabilistic mixtures and ELK.

- Weakness: Mixture model underperforms unimodal Gaussian on some SCWS settings without analysis of why context-sensitive benchmark results are worse.

- Weakness: Training objective/test metric mismatch for entailment—symmetric ELK used in training vs. asymmetric KL used at evaluation; asymmetric training ablations missing.

- Weakness: Limited systematic study of number of components K, selection criteria, component usage frequency, and covariance structure effects (spherical vs. diagonal).

- Question: Why does w2gm lose performance versus w2g on SCWS under some metrics—was context-conditioned component selection (rather than MaxSim/AveSim) tried?

- Question: Can you provide direct empirical comparisons to Tian et al., Neelakantan et al., Li & Jurafsky, and Liu et al. on SCWS and WS-353, and clarify the practical advantages of probabilistic mixtures?

- Question: Were asymmetric or inclusion-oriented training objectives (e.g., KL-based losses) experimented with, and how do they compare to ELK training?

- Question: How is K selected in practice, what fraction of words effectively use multiple components, and is there evidence of component collapse or dominance?

- Question: How sensitive are results to variance initialization, covariance parameterization (spherical vs. diagonal), margin, and learning-rate choices?

- Comment: Add missing citations and include results from contemporary multi-sense embedding work for fair comparison.

- Comment: Support or clarify the “heavy-tailed uncertainty” claim with empirical evidence or note it as an approximation via mixtures.

- Comment: Consider adding analogy or downstream task evaluations for broader validation or discuss reasons for omission.

- Post-rebuttal: Authors’ scalability clarifications and plans to add baselines help, but camera-ready should include quantitative comparisons to contemporary multi-sense methods.
