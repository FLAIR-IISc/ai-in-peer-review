Summary and contributions
This paper proposes representing each word as a multimodal probability distribution, specifically a mixture of Gaussians, and learning these distributions with an energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel (ELK) as the energy function. The approach targets polysemy, entailment/inclusion, and uncertainty modeling, while remaining analytically tractable and scalable.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
- A probabilistic multi-prototype embedding model (Gaussian mixtures per word) with a tractable ELK-based training objective that directly addresses polysemy and enables uncertainty-aware similarity and asymmetric entailment scoring.
- A practical, scalable training recipe (initialization, stability tricks, diagonal/spherical covariances, negative sampling, Adagrad) that trains on multi-billion-token corpora and yields interpretable components.
- Empirical evidence that the model captures multiple senses and improves over skip-gram and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on several word similarity datasets and on entailment; plus qualitative analyses and an interactive visualization.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and solid motivation: Extends Vilnis & McCallum (2014) from unimodal Gaussians to mixtures to handle polysemy; the ELK energy preserves analytic tractability with mixtures. The paper clearly derives the energy function and explains the effect of means vs. covariances in the loss.
- Strong qualitative evidence for sense separation: Nearest-neighbor analyses show clean disambiguation (e.g., rock/music vs. rock/stone; bank/river vs. bank/finance). The interactive demo and K=3 examples further support interpretability.
- Competitive to strong quantitative results: The mixture model improves over skip-gram and w2g on many standard similarity datasets (e.g., WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW) and yields better entailment AP/F1, particularly when using KL at test time. The reduction in excessive variance for polysemous words is a useful diagnostic and aligns with the intended effect.
- Scalable and reproducible methodology: Training on 3.5B tokens with clear implementation details (covariance parameterization, numerical stability, optimization, sampling) suggests the approach is practical. The promise to release code and the detailed supplement are valuable.
- Clear writing and organization: The paper is well-structured and easy to follow; figures and tables are informative, and the empirical narrative matches the modeling claims.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparison to prior multi-sense embeddings: The baselines focus on skip-gram and unimodal Gaussians, with only partial comparison to Huang et al. (2012). There is no head-to-head evaluation against other multi-sense or multi-prototype methods such as Neelakantan et al. (2014), Tian et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015). Adding their results to the main tables (e.g., WS, SCWS, and any table reporting multi-prototype performance) would strengthen the case.
- Incomplete discussion of relation to Tian et al. (2014) and similar approaches: Several prior works also split word representations into multiple prototypes. The paper should clearly articulate what is gained by moving to probabilistic mixtures and ELK training compared to deterministic multi-sense vectors (e.g., uncertainty modeling, entailment, training stability), and what the trade-offs are.
- SCWS regression: The mixture model appears to underperform the unimodal Gaussian in some SCWS settings. The paper should analyze why multi-sense distributions do not help on a context-sensitive benchmark and whether the scoring function (e.g., MaxSim vs. context-conditioned component selection) is appropriate. This is especially important because SCWS is designed to test polysemy handling.
- Train/test metric mismatch for entailment: The model is trained with a symmetric ELK energy but evaluated for entailment with an asymmetric KL-based score. While KL is a natural choice for entailment, training with a symmetric energy may be suboptimal. An ablation training with an asymmetric or inclusion-oriented objective (or with KL approximations) would clarify whether better entailment performance is achievable.
- Limited analysis of the number of components and covariance structure: Beyond K=2 and a qualitative K=3 note, there is little systematic study of how K affects performance, how K should be selected, or how often multiple components are effectively used. Similarly, the impact of spherical vs. diagonal covariances and variance initialization on performance and component collapse could be explored.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal/clarification)
- Why does w2gm lose performance compared to w2g on SCWS under some metrics? Did you try context-conditioned component selection (e.g., using the actual sentence context to weight components) rather than MaxSim/AveSim over senses?
- Can you provide direct empirical comparisons to Tian et al. (2014), Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015) on SCWS and WS-353? What are the principal advantages of your probabilistic mixture and ELK objective over these deterministic multi-sense methods?
- Did you experiment with training objectives that incorporate asymmetry (e.g., KL-based losses or inclusion-based margins) to better align with entailment evaluation? If so, how do they compare to ELK training; if not, why was ELK preferred?
- How do you select K in practice, and what fraction of words effectively use multiple components after training? Any evidence of component collapse or dominance, and how do covariances influence this?
- Sensitivity: How robust are results to variance initialization, covariance parameterization (spherical vs. diagonal), and margin/learning-rate choices?

Additional comments
- Please add missing citations to multi-sense embedding work: Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015), and include their results in the relevant tables for a fair comparison.
- The “heavy-tailed uncertainty” claim would benefit from empirical support (e.g., examining marginal kurtosis or tail behavior of learned distributions) or clarified as an approximation via mixtures.
- Consider adding analogy or downstream task evaluations for breadth, or at least discuss why they are omitted.
- Minor: ensure SCWS is referenced consistently and table numbering is correct. The interactive demo and code release are appreciated.

Post-rebuttal note: I have read the authors’ response. The clarifications on scalability and plans to incorporate additional baselines alleviate some concerns; however, including quantitative comparisons to contemporary multi-sense methods in the camera-ready remains important for a definitive assessment. Overall, this is a strong, well-presented paper with a meaningful contribution.
