- Proposal: Represent words as Gaussian mixtures to model multiple senses, uncertainty, and entailment, trained with a skip-gram-style max-margin objective.  
- Analytic similarity: Use the expected likelihood kernel (ELK) as a closed-form energy between mixtures for efficient training.  
- Empirical claims: Shows qualitative sense separation (e.g., rock, bank) and quantitative gains on word similarity and lexical entailment vs word2vec and unimodal Gaussians.  
- Contribution: Practical multimodal probabilistic embedding with tractable similarity and max-margin training that combines senses and uncertainty.  
- Contribution: Empirical evidence of improved performance, interpretable sense decompositions, and reduced variance for polysemous words.  
- Contribution: Engineering details (numerical stabilization, spherical covariances, negative sampling, Adagrad) enabling large-scale training.  
- Strength: Clear motivation and formulation diagnosing limitations of point and unimodal Gaussian embeddings for polysemy.  
- Strength: Expressiveness and interpretability with clean nearest-neighbor sense separation and K=3 homonym examples.  
- Strength: Analytic ELK energy avoids KL intractability; training scaffold is scalable and numerically stabilized.  
- Strength: Solid empirical results across multiple similarity datasets and consistent unimodal-Gaussian improvement on entailment.  
- Strength: Good writing and reproducibility with implementation details, visualization, and promised code release.  
- Weakness: Limited exploration of number of components K—default K=2 used mostly, no systematic study or model-selection criterion.  
- Weakness: Covariances restricted to spherical, limiting anisotropy and lacking comparison to diagonal covariances.  
- Weakness: Baselines limited—multi-prototype and other recent methods not compared across full suites and entailment.  
- Weakness: Improvements are sometimes modest and sensitive to similarity metric; no statistical significance reported.  
- Weakness: Training vs evaluation asymmetry—symmetric ELK used for training but asymmetric KL used at evaluation for entailment without ablation.  
- Questions: Why default K=2 and how do results vary with K; any validation curves or selection criteria?  
- Questions: Do mixture weights collapse for monosemous words; provide distributions of weights, covariances, and inter-component distances by frequency/polysemy.  
- Questions: Why spherical covariances; any experiments with diagonal covariances and cost/stability trade-offs?  
- Questions: Did you try asymmetric objectives (e.g., KL) in training to better align with entailment, and what were the trade-offs?  
- Questions: What are training time and memory overheads relative to word2vec and unimodal Gaussians for K=2/3; any implementation constraints?
