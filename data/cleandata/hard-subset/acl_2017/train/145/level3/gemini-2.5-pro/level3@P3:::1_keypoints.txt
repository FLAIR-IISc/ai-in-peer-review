- Introduces Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) word embeddings to explicitly model polysemy, using an expected likelihood kernel and a max-margin energy objective, and reports improved, interpretable senses.  
- Contribution: Proposes representing words as multimodal probabilistic distributions (GMMs) to capture multiple senses per word.  
- Contribution: Presents a scalable training method combining a max-margin energy objective with an analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel for GMMs.  
- Strength: Exceptionally clear and well-structured presentation that motivates the move from unimodal to multimodal distributions.  
- Strength: Strong qualitative analysis showing distinct, interpretable components for polysemous words (e.g., "rock" = geology vs. music).  
- Strength: Thorough quantitative experiments across multiple word similarity and entailment datasets with solid baselines and multiple similarity metrics.  
- Weakness: Insufficient experimental comparison to other multi-prototype methods (e.g., Neelakantan et al., Li & Jurafsky, Liu et al.).  
- Weakness: Inadequate differentiation from Tian et al. (2014); lacks clear discussion of model and objective differences.  
- Weakness: Missing citations and exclusion of key prior results from comparison tables, limiting completeness of evaluation on benchmarks like WordSim-353 and SCWS.  
- Question: Request to provide detailed SCWS results and comparisons with other multi-prototype models.  
- Question: Seeks explanation for why w2gm underperforms w2g on entailment with cosine similarity (72.9 vs. 73.0) and whether this is due to optimization or metric artifacts.
