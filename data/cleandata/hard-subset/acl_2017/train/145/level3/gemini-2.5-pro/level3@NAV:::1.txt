### Review

**Summary**
This paper presents a novel approach for learning word embeddings by representing each word as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM), which we refer to as `w2gm`. This method extends prior work on unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) to explicitly model polysemy, where each component of the mixture can capture a distinct word sense. The model is trained using an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel, which provides a tractable similarity measure between distributions. Experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate the model's ability to learn interpretable, multi-sense representations.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the novel modeling of words as multimodal Gaussian mixture distributions. This is an intuitive and principled extension of probabilistic embeddings that directly addresses the long-standing challenge of polysemy.
2.  A secondary contribution is the effective application of the expected likelihood kernel as an energy function for training these mixture models, which is both analytically tractable and well-suited for measuring the affinity between distributions.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper is exceptionally well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The motivation for moving from unimodal to multimodal distributions is compelling and logically presented.
2.  The core idea is strongly supported by excellent qualitative results. The nearest neighbor analysis in Table 1 provides convincing evidence that the model successfully disentangles distinct meanings for polysemous words like "rock" and "bank," a clear advantage over unimodal baselines.
3.  The experimental methodology is sound, and the quantitative results generally show that the proposed `w2gm` model outperforms both word2vec and its unimodal `w2g` counterpart on several standard benchmarks, validating the effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The comparison to other multi-prototype embedding methods is insufficient. While the paper compares against Huang et al. (2012), it would be significantly strengthened by including comparisons to more recent and directly related approaches that also use mixture models, such as Tian et al. (2014).
2.  The related work section is missing several key citations in this area, including Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015). Including these works and positioning the current paper with respect to them is crucial. Ideally, results from these methods should be included in Tables 3 and 4 for a more complete evaluation.
3.  The paper lacks a discussion of the differences between its approach and Tian et al. (2014), which seems highly relevant. A brief explanation of the methodological distinctions would help clarify the paper's specific novelty.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please elaborate on the key differences between your GMM approach and the mixture model used by Tian et al. (2014)? A brief discussion in the related work section would be very helpful.
2.  The proposed `w2gm` model shows strong performance overall but appears to slightly underperform the unimodal `w2g` model on the SimLex-999 similarity benchmark (Table 2). Do you have an intuition for why explicitly modeling multiple senses might be less beneficial for this specific task, which focuses on pure similarity rather than relatedness?
