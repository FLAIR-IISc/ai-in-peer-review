- Models words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to explicitly capture multiple senses with means, covariances, and mixture weights.  
- Trains with a max-margin energy-based objective using the expected likelihood kernel, yielding closed-form, differentiable similarity and scalable training.  
- Shows empirical improvements over word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on word similarity and entailment, with qualitative examples (e.g., "rock", "bank").  
- Well-motivated and expressive model that naturally handles polysemy and sense frequencies; qualitative results are convincing.  
- Elegant, efficient training framework: expected likelihood kernel enables closed-form energies and SGD-scale learning.  
- Paper is clearly written, structured, and compares fairly to point and unimodal probabilistic baselines.  
- Insufficient analysis/justification of number of components K (experiments mostly use K=2; no sensitivity study or validation-based choice reported).  
- No analysis of model behavior for predominantly monosemous words (whether one component dominates or components collapse).  
- Limited exploration of covariance structure—only spherical covariances used; diagonal/anisotropic covariances not empirically evaluated.  
- Question: How was K=2 chosen and were sensitivity experiments for K>2 (e.g., on SCWS) performed?  
- Question: For monosemous words, do mixture weights concentrate on one component or do means collapse?  
- Question: Were diagonal covariances tried, and what is the performance vs. computational cost trade-off?  
- Table 4 (SCWS evaluation) appears to be missing.  
- Reference for Luong et al. is missing the publication year.
