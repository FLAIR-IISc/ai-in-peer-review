- Introduces a novel word embedding that represents each word as a Gaussian Mixture Model to explicitly capture polysemy.  
- Trains model parameters (means, covariances, mixture weights) with an energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel as similarity.  
- Demonstrates qualitatively and quantitatively that the model learns distinct, semantically coherent sense representations and achieves strong results on standard similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Contribution: a principled multimodal probabilistic embedding (GMM) extending unimodal Gaussian embeddings to handle multiple word senses.  
- Contribution: a tractable and efficient training framework combining a max-margin loss with the expected likelihood kernel for GMMs.  
- Strength: paper is very clear, well-structured, and well-presented, with helpful illustrations (e.g., Figure 1).  
- Strength: method is a natural, well-motivated extension of prior unimodal probabilistic embeddings and uses an analytically tractable energy function.  
- Strength: solid experimental validation, with compelling qualitative examples and competitive quantitative results vs. word2vec and unimodal baselines.  
- Weakness: inadequate comparison to other multi-prototype/multi-sense embedding work (e.g., Neelakantan et al., Tian et al., Li & Jurafsky, Liu et al.), limiting assessment of novelty and state-of-the-art claims.  
- Weakness: inconsistent performance on the SCWS polysemy benchmark — multi-prototype model does not consistently outperform unimodal baseline, which undermines the central motivation.  
- Question: please explain why the multi-prototype GMM underperforms the unimodal model on SCWS and provide analysis.  
- Question: clarify key differences and advantages over other multi-prototype approaches, particularly Tian et al. (2014) that also uses a mixture-model framework.
