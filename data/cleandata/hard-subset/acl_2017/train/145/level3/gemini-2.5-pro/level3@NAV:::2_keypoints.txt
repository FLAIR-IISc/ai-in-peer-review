- Summary: Proposes word representations as Gaussian Mixture Models to model polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel for closed-form, differentiable similarity.

- Strength 1: GMMs naturally capture multiple senses, mixture weights, and uncertainty—more expressive than point vectors or single Gaussians.

- Strength 2: The max-margin + expected likelihood kernel framework is theoretically sound and enables efficient, scalable SGD training.

- Strength 3: Thorough empirical evaluation shows improved performance over strong baselines and qualitative components align with interpretable word meanings.

- Weakness 1: No justification or analysis for the chosen number of components (k=2); unclear how performance changes with different k and effects on monosemous words.

- Weakness 2: Use of spherical covariances limits modeling of anisotropic uncertainty; no ablation or discussion on using more expressive diagonal covariances.

- Question 1: Please explain the choice of k=2 and provide analysis of performance as k increases, especially for words with a single dominant meaning.

- Question 2: Was spherical covariance chosen solely for efficiency, and how would switching to diagonal (anisotropic) covariances affect performance?
