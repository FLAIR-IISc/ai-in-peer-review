- Proposes modeling words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to capture polysemy and uncertainty.  
- Trains GMM embeddings with a scalable max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel for closed-form similarity.  
- Demonstrates qualitative and quantitative gains over word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on similarity and entailment tasks.  

- Main contribution: expressive probabilistic word representation enabling multiple senses per word.  
- Main contribution: practical, scalable training with analytic energy function suitable for large corpora.  
- Main contribution: strong empirical results showing sense disentanglement and competitive benchmark performance.  

- Strength: clear motivation and problem formulation addressing polysemy in embeddings.  
- Strength: model expressiveness shown by distinct components for different senses (e.g., “rock”, “bank”, “Apple”).  
- Strength: well-written, clearly presented methodology and experiments.  

- Weakness: no systematic justification or analysis for choosing number of mixture components K (primarily K=2).  
- Weakness: limited evaluation of behavior on monosemous words and whether extra components are avoided or collapse.  
- Weakness: experiments use only spherical covariances with no ablation or discussion of diagonal/more expressive covariances.  

- Question: Why choose K=2; have you tried word-specific or learned K and how does performance vary with K (e.g., 3,4)?  
- Question: Can you show learned distributions (mixture weights, means) for clearly monosemous words to confirm no hallucinated senses?  
- Question: Why use spherical covariances exclusively; any experiments or discussion on diagonal covariances and trade-offs?  

- Additional: Section 4.4 mentions comparisons to other multi-prototype models but the corresponding results table appears missing.  
- Additional: Bibliography entry for “Luong et al.” is missing a publication year.
