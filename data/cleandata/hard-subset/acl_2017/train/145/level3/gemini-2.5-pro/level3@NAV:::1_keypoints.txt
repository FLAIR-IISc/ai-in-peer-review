- Novel approach: represent words as Gaussian Mixture Models (w2gm) to model polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective using an expected likelihood kernel.
- Contribution: multimodal GMM embeddings as a principled extension to probabilistic word embeddings addressing polysemy.
- Contribution: application of the expected likelihood kernel as an analytically tractable energy function for training mixture models.
- Strength: paper is well-structured, clearly written, and motivates the move from unimodal to multimodal embeddings.
- Strength: qualitative nearest-neighbor analysis shows the model disentangles distinct senses (e.g., "rock", "bank").
- Strength: sound experimental methodology; w2gm generally outperforms word2vec and unimodal w2g on several benchmarks.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to other multi-prototype methods, notably omission of Tian et al. (2014).
- Weakness: related work omits key citations (Neelakantan et al. 2014, Li & Jurafsky 2015, Liu et al. 2015) and lacks their results in evaluation tables.
- Weakness: paper lacks discussion clarifying methodological differences from Tian et al. (2014).
- Question: please elaborate the key differences between the GMM approach and Tian et al. (2014).
- Question: please explain why w2gm slightly underperforms unimodal w2g on SimLex-999, which focuses on pure similarity rather than relatedness.
