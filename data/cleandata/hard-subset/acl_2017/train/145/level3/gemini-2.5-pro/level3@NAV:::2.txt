**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel method for representing words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to explicitly model polysemy. The model is trained using an energy-based max-margin objective, where the similarity between word distributions is measured by the expected likelihood kernel. The primary contribution is the expressive word representation itself; using GMMs is a well-motivated step beyond single-point vectors or unimodal Gaussian embeddings, allowing a single word to capture multiple distinct semantic meanings, each with its own uncertainty. A second key contribution is the practical and efficient training framework. The choice of the expected likelihood kernel provides a closed-form, differentiable similarity score for GMMs, enabling straightforward optimization with stochastic gradient descent.

**Strengths**

1.  The problem of modeling polysemy is clearly motivated, and the proposed GMM representation is an intuitive and powerful solution. It is more expressive than deterministic vectors and single Gaussians, as it can naturally capture multiple word senses, their relative probabilities (mixture weights), and semantic uncertainty (covariances).
2.  The learning framework is theoretically sound and elegant. The combination of a max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel is well-suited for the task, as the closed-form solution for the kernel allows for efficient and scalable training, which the authors demonstrate on a large corpus.
3.  The empirical evaluation is thorough, with experiments on multiple word similarity and entailment benchmarks. The results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms strong baselines, including word2vec and single Gaussian embeddings, validating the practical benefits of the GMM representation. The qualitative analysis also compellingly shows that the learned components correspond to distinct, human-interpretable word meanings.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The paper lacks justification and analysis for key modeling choices, primarily the number of mixture components (k). The experiments are conducted with k=2, but there is no discussion of how this value was chosen or how the model's performance varies with different values of k. It is unclear if a larger k would improve performance on words with more senses or degrade it on monosemous words.
2.  The experimental evaluation is constrained by the exclusive use of spherical covariances. While this choice simplifies computation, it limits the model's ability to capture anisotropic uncertainty in the embedding space. An ablation study or discussion on the impact of using more expressive diagonal covariances would be necessary to understand the trade-off between model performance and computational efficiency.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the criteria for setting the number of Gaussian components to k=2? An analysis showing how performance changes as k increases would be valuable. Specifically, does performance degrade for words with only one dominant meaning as k increases?
2.  All experiments utilize spherical covariances. Was this choice made purely for computational efficiency? How do you expect performance to be affected by using more flexible diagonal (anisotropic) covariances?
