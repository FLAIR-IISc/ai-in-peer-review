- Proposes representing words as multimodal probability distributions (Gaussian mixtures) to explicitly model polysemy.  
- Each Gaussian component encodes a distinct sense via mean, covariance, and mixture weight.  
- Learns parameters with an energy-based max-margin objective inspired by skip-gram.  
- Uses the expected likelihood kernel as the similarity measure, which has a closed form for Gaussian mixtures and enables scalable training.  
- Shows qualitative and quantitative evidence that the model learns distinct senses and performs well on word similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Contribution: a novel probabilistic, multimodal word representation that directly addresses polysemy.  
- Contribution: a scalable learning framework using the expected likelihood kernel within a max-margin objective.  
- Contribution: strong empirical validation, including qualitative sense separation and competitive benchmark results.  
- Strength: paper is very clear and well presented, with good visual intuition (e.g., Figure 1).  
- Strength: high novelty and significance as an elegant extension of probabilistic embeddings to multiple modes.  
- Strength: convincing qualitative examples and strong quantitative performance, notably on entailment.  
- Weakness: limited comparison/discussion of other multi-prototype methods (e.g., Tian et al. 2014).  
- Weakness: missing important baselines (e.g., Neelakantan et al. 2014, Li & Jurafsky 2015, Liu et al. 2015) in experiments.  
- Weakness: unexplained underperformance of the multi-prototype model on the SCWS dataset relative to the single-prototype model.  
- Question: please explain the SCWS results—could metric choice (MaxSim/AveSim), optimization, or evaluation suitability account for the multi-prototype model's lower score?
