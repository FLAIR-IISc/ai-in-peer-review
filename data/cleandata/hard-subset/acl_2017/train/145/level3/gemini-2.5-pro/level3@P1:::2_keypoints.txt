- Proposes modeling words as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to capture polysemy, trained with a skip-gram max-margin objective.  
- Uses the Expected Likelihood Kernel for a closed-form similarity between GMMs enabling tractable training.  
- Demonstrates qualitative and quantitative gains over word2vec and single-Gaussian embeddings on similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Main contribution: principled extension from unimodal to multimodal probabilistic embeddings to represent multiple senses.  
- Main contribution: pairs expressive GMMs with an efficient, scalable training framework using a max-margin loss and ELK.  
- Main contribution: strong empirical validation, notably improved lexical entailment performance.  
- Strength: clear motivation and problem formulation addressing polysemy limitations in embeddings.  
- Strength: expressive and interpretable model—component means and covariances yield meaningful, sense-specific neighbors.  
- Strength: technically sound choice of ELK provides closed-form gradients for scalable optimization.  
- Strength: thorough experimental analysis including comparisons to strong baselines and internal ablations.  
- Weakness: experiments largely use K=2 without principled justification or sensitivity analysis for the number of components.  
- Weakness: lacks analysis of behavior on unimodal (non-polysemous) words—no evidence one component dominates or components collapse.  
- Weakness: uses spherical covariances for efficiency but provides no ablation or comparison to diagonal/full covariances to quantify trade-offs.  
- Question: provide justification/sensitivity analysis for choice of K and its effect on benchmarks and non-polysemous words.  
- Question: show how distributions look for unimodal words (mixture weights, component distances) to demonstrate handling of single-meaning words.  
- Question: clarify performance vs. cost trade-offs for spherical vs. diagonal/full covariances and report any experiments with diagonal covariances.
