This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, representing words as Gaussian mixtures rather than single points in vector space. The authors argue that this allows for modeling polysemy, capturing richer semantic information, and representing uncertainty more accurately. A max-margin objective, coupled with the expected likelihood kernel for efficient similarity calculation, is used to learn the parameters of these Gaussian mixtures. The method is evaluated on word similarity and entailment tasks, demonstrating performance improvements over existing techniques like word2vec and single Gaussian embeddings.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the development of a probabilistic word embedding model capable of representing multiple word senses simultaneously. Existing methods struggle to adequately represent polysemous words, often resorting to large variances to encompass multiple meanings, which leads to diluted semantic information. By utilizing a mixture of Gaussians, the authors provide a framework for capturing distinct meanings as separate modes within the distribution, offering a more nuanced and expressive representation. A secondary contribution is the application of the expected likelihood kernel within the learning objective. This allows for analytic tractability, facilitating efficient training even with the increased complexity of Gaussian mixtures. Finally, the empirical evaluation, showcasing improvements on standard benchmarks, validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper addresses a significant limitation of traditional word embeddings—their inability to represent multiple meanings effectively. The motivation for exploring probabilistic representations and specifically Gaussian mixtures is clearly articulated and grounded in the challenges of modeling semantic ambiguity. 
2. **Expressive Representation:** The use of Gaussian mixtures provides a compelling solution to the polysemy problem.  Representing words as distributions, rather than single points, inherently allows for capturing multiple meanings, uncertainty, and relationships between concepts (like entailment) in a way that deterministic embeddings cannot. The examples provided in Figure 1 effectively illustrate this advantage, showing how different modes of the distribution correspond to different senses of a word like “bank” or “rock.”
3. **Efficient Learning:** The choice of the expected likelihood kernel is crucial for practical implementation.  It enables a closed-form solution for calculating similarity between distributions, which is essential for scaling the max-margin learning objective to large datasets. The authors emphasize the importance of efficiency, stating they can train on corpora with billions of words in days.
4. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents comprehensive experimental results on a variety of benchmark datasets, consistently demonstrating performance improvements over both word2vec and previous Gaussian embedding approaches. The use of multiple similarity metrics (expected likelihood, cosine similarity, Euclidean distance) provides a more thorough evaluation of the embeddings.
5. **Clear and Mostly Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is described in detail, and the experimental setup is clearly outlined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Choice of K=2:** The authors consistently use K=2 Gaussian components. While they briefly mention exploring larger K, they don't provide a clear justification for choosing 2, nor do they systematically investigate the impact of varying K on performance. Is there a trade-off between model complexity and performance when increasing K? How does the model behave for words with more than two dominant meanings? This needs further investigation.
2. **Single Meaning Word Distributions:**  The paper focuses on polysemous words. It's unclear how the Gaussian mixture model behaves for words with a single, dominant meaning. Does the model still learn a mixture with one component having significantly higher probability than the others? Understanding this behavior is important for a complete picture of the model's capabilities.
3. **Spherical Covariance Assumption:** The authors opt for spherical (diagonal) covariance matrices for computational efficiency. However, this limits the model's ability to capture correlations between different dimensions of the embedding space. Investigating the impact of allowing diagonal (and potentially full) covariance matrices on performance is warranted. Would anisotropic covariances lead to more accurate representations, and at what computational cost?
4. **Unused Table:** Table 4 appears in the paper but isn't referenced in the text, making it unclear what information it is intended to convey.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What criteria were used to select the number of Gaussian components (K) as 2, and how does performance change with different values of K?
2. Can you describe the learned distribution for words with a predominantly single meaning? Does one component dominate, and what is the distribution of probabilities across components in such cases?
3. Was the spherical covariance assumption made solely for computational efficiency? If so, what is the expected performance impact of using diagonal or full covariance matrices, and would the performance gains justify the increased computational complexity?
