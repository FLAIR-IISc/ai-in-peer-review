- Novel method: represents words as Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy, richer semantics, and uncertainty.
- Learning: uses a max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel for efficient similarity computation.
- Primary contribution: a probabilistic embedding model that can represent multiple word senses simultaneously.
- Secondary contribution: analytic tractability via the expected likelihood kernel enabling scalable training.
- Empirical claim: shows improvements on word similarity and entailment benchmarks vs. word2vec and single-Gaussian embeddings.
- Strength — motivation: clearly addresses limitation of traditional embeddings in modeling multiple meanings.
- Strength — expressiveness: mixture components capture distinct senses (illustrated with “bank” and “rock”).
- Strength — efficiency: closed-form kernel allows training on very large corpora in reasonable time.
- Strength — results: consistent performance gains across multiple datasets and similarity metrics.
- Strength — clarity: paper is generally well-written with detailed methodology and experimental setup.
- Weakness — K choice: consistently uses K=2 without justification or systematic study of different K values.
- Weakness — single-sense behavior: unclear how mixtures behave for words with a single dominant meaning.
- Weakness — covariance assumption: spherical covariances limit modeling of dimension correlations; diagonal/full covariances not explored.
- Weakness — presentation: Table 4 appears in the paper but is not referenced or explained.
- Question: Why was K=2 selected and how does performance vary with other K values?
- Question: For single-meaning words, does one component dominate and what are typical component probability distributions?
- Question: Was the spherical covariance chosen only for efficiency, and what performance gains/costs would diagonal or full covariances bring?
- Additional: Luong et al. reference missing publication year.
- Additional: Clarify the purpose of Table 4 or remove it.
