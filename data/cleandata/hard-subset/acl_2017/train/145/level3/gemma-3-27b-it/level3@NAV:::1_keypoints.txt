- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple meanings and uncertainty, extending Vilnis & McCallum (2014).
- Introduces an energy-based max-margin objective and uses the expected likelihood kernel for tractable learning.
- Demonstrates that w2gm consistently outperforms skip-gram and single-Gaussian baselines on word similarity and entailment tasks.
- Qualitative examples (Table 1) show distinct Gaussian components correspond to different word senses.
- Main strength: more effective modeling of polysemy than previous unimodal approaches, supported by qualitative and quantitative results.
- Technical approach is well-motivated, building on existing work while remaining robust and scalable.
- Weakness: lacks thorough comparison to related mixture-model work, especially Tian et al. (2014).
- Recommends including results from Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015) in Tables 3 and 4 for stronger benchmarks.
- Observes an unexpected performance decrease of w2gm versus w2g on the SWCS analysis (Table 5) that needs explanation.
- Asks authors to explain why w2gm loses performance on SWCS and to detail methodological differences from Tian et al. (2014).
