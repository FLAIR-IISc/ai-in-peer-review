## Review of "Word to Gaussian Mixture"

This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, representing words as Gaussian mixtures (GMs) to model polysemy and uncertainty. The method is trained with a max-margin objective, leveraging the expected likelihood kernel for efficient similarity calculation. The core idea is well-motivated – deterministic word vectors struggle with representing multiple meanings – and the proposed solution offers a compelling avenue for richer semantic representation.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful application of Gaussian mixtures to the word embedding space, coupled with an efficient training procedure. Previous work utilized Gaussian distributions (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014), but suffered from issues related to representing multiple meanings. The GM approach demonstrably addresses this limitation, as evidenced by the qualitative examples and improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks. A second contribution is the practical implementation of the expected likelihood kernel, which enables analytically tractable training of the model. Finally, the empirical evaluation on established datasets provides a strong validation of the approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing word embedding methods in capturing polysemy and uncertainty, providing a strong rationale for the proposed GM approach.
*   **Expressive Power of Gaussian Mixtures:** The use of GMs is a significant improvement over single Gaussian distributions, allowing the model to represent multiple word senses and their associated uncertainty more accurately.
*   **Efficient Training:** The max-margin objective combined with the expected likelihood kernel results in a computationally efficient training procedure, enabling scalability to large corpora.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The comprehensive experimental evaluation on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, outperforming baseline methods in many scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Choice of *k*:** The paper sets the number of Gaussian components (*k*) to 2 without a clear justification. A more thorough exploration of *k* values and their impact on performance is needed. Increasing *k* might improve representation for some words but could also lead to overfitting; this needs investigation.
*   **Distribution Shape for Unimodal Words:** The paper does not discuss how the learned distribution looks for words with a dominant, single meaning. Does the model converge to a single, narrow Gaussian, or does it still exhibit a mixture with very low weights for secondary components?
*   **Spherical Covariances:** The choice of spherical covariances simplifies computation but may limit the model’s ability to capture more complex relationships between word features. Exploring diagonal (anisotropic) covariances, even with a performance trade-off, would provide valuable insights into the model’s expressiveness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What criteria were used to select *k*=2? Was any analysis performed to determine an optimal value for *k*, and how does performance change as *k* is varied?
2.  Can you provide visualizations or statistical descriptions (e.g., variance of component weights) of the learned distributions for words with a predominantly single meaning?
3.  What is the computational overhead associated with using diagonal covariances, and how much performance improvement could be expected compared to spherical covariances?
