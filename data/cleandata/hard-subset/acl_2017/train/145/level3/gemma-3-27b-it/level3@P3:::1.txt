This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple meanings and inherent uncertainty. It’s a strong and well-structured paper overall, clearly presenting the method and its advantages.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution lies in the introduction of multimodal (Gaussian-mixture) word distributions. This moves beyond the limitations of unimodal Gaussian representations (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) and deterministic point embeddings by allowing for a more nuanced representation of polysemous words and their associated semantic uncertainty. A secondary, but important, contribution is the proposed energy-based max-margin objective, tailored for learning these mixtures analytically using the expected likelihood kernel. Finally, the empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the method on word similarity and entailment tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The motivation for using Gaussian mixtures is clearly articulated, the methodology is described in a detailed and accessible manner, and the experimental setup is well-defined. Figures and tables are used effectively to illustrate key concepts and results.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** Representing words with Gaussian mixtures is a significant step forward in probabilistic word embedding models. Addressing the shortcomings of unimodal Gaussian embeddings, particularly concerning polysemy, is a valuable contribution.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed w2gm model over baseline methods (word2vec, w2g) on several benchmark datasets, including word similarity and entailment. The qualitative analysis (Table 1) offers compelling evidence that the model successfully captures different word senses.
4. **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the expected likelihood kernel and the discussion of implementation details (optimization constraints, stability, etc.) suggest a rigorous and thoughtful approach to the method's development. The use of Adagrad for optimization is well-justified.
5. **Thorough Evaluation:** The inclusion of multiple datasets and similarity/distance metrics provides a comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance. The ablation studies (varying the number of mixture components) are informative.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper acknowledges related work, the comparison to similar approaches – specifically those using mixture models – could be more extensive. The discussion of Tian et al. (2014) feels cursory, and doesn't fully articulate the differences in methodology and objective functions.
2. **Missing Citations:** The paper omits several relevant citations. Specifically, Neelakantan et al. (2014) on multi-sense embeddings, Li & Jurafsky (2015) on sense embeddings, and Liu et al. (2015) on contextualized word embeddings would strengthen the related work section and provide a more complete contextualization of the proposed method.
3. **Unexpected SWCS Analysis Result:** The authors mention that w2gm loses performance compared to w2g on the SWCS analysis, but don’t offer any explanation for this. This warrants further investigation and discussion.

**Questions for Authors:**

1. Why does w2gm lose performance compared to w2g on the SWCS analysis? What factors might contribute to this observed behavior?
2. Can you elaborate on the key differences between your approach and that of Tian et al. (2014), particularly regarding the learning objectives and the assumptions made about the mixture components?
