- Introduces multimodal word distributions using Gaussian mixtures to capture polysemy, entailment, and uncertainty.  
- Proposes an energy-based max-margin objective leveraging the expected likelihood kernel for tractable learning of these mixtures.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on word similarity and entailment benchmarks compared to baselines like word2vec and Gaussian embeddings.  

- Novel and sound extension of unimodal Gaussian embeddings; expected likelihood kernel is a clever, analytically tractable energy choice.  
- Thorough experimental evaluation with qualitative examples that illustrate the model’s ability to capture distinct senses.  
- Clear, well-written presentation with detailed implementation details aiding reproducibility.  
- Practicality addressed (numerical stability, initialization) and claimed scalability to training on billions of words in days.  

- Limited comparison to related work, particularly other mixture-model approaches such as Tian et al. (2014).  
- w2gm sometimes underperforms w2g on SCWS (Table 5), which is counterintuitive and unexplained.  
- Missing relevant citations and comparisons to Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015).  

- Why does w2gm occasionally perform worse than w2g on SCWS—dataset-specific or a general model characteristic?  
- What are the key differences and trade-offs between this method and Tian et al. (2014)’s mixture-model approach?  

- Suggest discussing computational cost of Gaussian mixtures versus simpler embeddings.  
- Recommend analysis of learned mixture weights (e.g., correlation with word frequency or linguistic features).  
- Interactive visualization website is a valuable and helpful addition.
