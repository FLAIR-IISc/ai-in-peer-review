## Review for ACL 2017

This paper introduces a novel approach to word representation learning, moving beyond point embeddings and unimodal Gaussian distributions to represent words as mixtures of Gaussians. The authors argue that this multimodal representation allows for a more nuanced capture of multiple word meanings, uncertainty, and relationships like entailment. They present an energy-based max-margin objective for learning these distributions and demonstrate superior performance on word similarity and entailment tasks compared to alternatives such as word2vec and Gaussian embeddings. Overall, this is a strong, well-structured, and clearly written paper with a significant contribution to the field.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Multimodal Word Distributions:** The core contribution is the proposal of representing words as Gaussian mixtures, enabling the capture of multiple distinct meanings and inherent uncertainty. This addresses a key limitation of previous distributional semantic models, particularly those based on single point embeddings or unimodal Gaussian distributions, which struggle with polysemy.
2. **Energy-Based Learning Objective:** The authors propose a well-motivated energy-based max-margin objective, leveraging the expected likelihood kernel for efficient computation with Gaussian mixtures. This provides a principled and scalable framework for learning the parameters of the mixture model.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides solid empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  Through extensive experimentation on standard benchmarks (word similarity and entailment), the authors demonstrate that their model outperforms existing methods, and offers a qualitative illustration of its ability to capture differing word senses.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Significance:** The concept of using Gaussian mixtures to model word distributions is a significant step forward in distributional semantics. By explicitly representing multiple meanings, the model has the potential to capture richer semantic relationships and improve performance on downstream tasks.
2. **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the energy-based objective and the choice of the expected likelihood kernel are well-justified and demonstrate a strong understanding of probabilistic modeling. The discussion of implementation details and considerations for numerical stability is particularly valuable.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly articulate their motivation, methodology, and results. The inclusion of illustrative examples (e.g., Figure 1 and Table 1) enhances understanding.
4. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements over baseline models on a variety of tasks. The qualitative analysis in Table 1 provides intuitive support for the model’s ability to capture distinct word meanings.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors commit to making their code publicly available, which is crucial for fostering reproducibility and further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** While the authors acknowledge related work, the comparison to approaches that *also* employ mixture models for word representation (e.g., Tian et al., 2014) is insufficient. A more detailed discussion of the differences between their approach and these existing methods would strengthen the paper. A crucial aspect is to show how their energy function and objective are different from existing models.
2. **Performance Drop in SCWS:** The results on the SCWS (Sense Word Comparison Task) dataset (Table 5) show a slight decrease in performance when moving from w2g to w2gm, which appears counterintuitive. While the authors might address this in their response, a clarification of this behavior is needed. This might be due to the complexity of the model or issues in hyperparameter optimization.
3. **Missing Relevant Citations:** The paper would benefit from including citations to more recent and relevant work on polysemy and word sense disambiguation.  Specifically, the authors should consider citing Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015) which all address related topics.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the key differences between your approach and that of Tian et al. (2014), given that both utilize mixture models for word representation? What specific aspects of your energy function and learning objective contribute to the performance gains observed in your experiments?
2.  Why does the w2gm model exhibit a slight performance decrease on the SCWS dataset compared to the w2g model? Could this be attributed to specific characteristics of the SCWS dataset or limitations of the current model configuration?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the number of Gaussian components (K)? Did you explore other values of K beyond 2 and 3, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

- The author's response has been read. The explanations and clarifications provided are helpful and address most of the concerns raised.
- Consider including a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed approach compared to existing methods.
- Exploring the interpretability of the learned Gaussian components further (e.g., through visualization or analysis of associated words) could add further value to the paper.



This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of distributional semantics and has the potential to inspire further research in this area. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper, particularly its novelty, technical soundness, and strong empirical results, outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance.
