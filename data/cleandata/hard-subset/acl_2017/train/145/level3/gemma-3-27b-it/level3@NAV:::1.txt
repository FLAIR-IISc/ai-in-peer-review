## Review of “Word to Gaussian Mixture”

This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, representing words as Gaussian mixtures (GMs) to capture multiple meanings and inherent uncertainty. The core idea – extending Vilnis & McCallum (2014)’s Gaussian embedding to a multimodal representation – is a strong contribution, effectively addressing the limitations of unimodal distributions for polysemous words. The proposed energy-based max-margin objective and the use of the expected likelihood kernel provide a principled and analytically tractable framework for learning these GMs. Experimentally, the results on word similarity and entailment tasks demonstrate that the w2gm model consistently outperforms both skip-gram and the original Gaussian embedding baseline, with qualitative analysis (Table 1) clearly illustrating the capture of distinct word senses.

The primary strength of this work lies in its ability to represent and model polysemy more effectively than previous approaches. The qualitative examples provided in Table 1 convincingly demonstrate that the learned Gaussian components correspond to different meanings of a word, a feat not achievable with single Gaussian embeddings. The quantitative results across multiple datasets reinforce this advantage. The technical approach, while building upon existing work, is well-motivated and executed, resulting in a robust and scalable learning procedure.

However, the paper could be strengthened by a more thorough comparison to related work. While the authors acknowledge Tian et al. (2014), which also utilizes mixture models for word representations, a detailed explanation of the differences between their approach and that of Tian et al. is missing. Furthermore, the comparison would be more compelling with results from Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015) included in Tables 3 and 4. These papers represent significant contributions to the field of polysemy modeling and would provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the proposed method.

Finally, the results on the SWCS analysis (Table 5) show a performance *decrease* with w2gm compared to w2g. A clarification on this unexpected behavior would be valuable. Why does moving to a multimodal representation negatively impact performance on this specific task?

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  Why does w2gm lose performance compared to w2g on the SWCS analysis?
2.  Please provide a detailed comparison of your approach to the mixture model used in Tian et al. (2014), highlighting the key differences in methodology and assumptions.
