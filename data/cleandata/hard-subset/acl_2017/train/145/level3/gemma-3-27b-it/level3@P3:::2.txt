This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture polysemy and uncertainty. The authors train these mixtures using a max-margin objective and employ the expected likelihood kernel for similarity calculations. Experiments demonstrate the method's effectiveness on word similarity and entailment tasks, outperforming alternatives like word2vec and simpler Gaussian embeddings.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Gaussian Mixture Representation for Polysemy:** The core contribution lies in representing words as Gaussian mixtures, offering a more expressive model than single Gaussian distributions or deterministic vectors. This allows for capturing multiple meanings, associated probabilities, and inherent uncertainty in word semantics.
2. **Efficient Max-Margin Learning with Expected Likelihood Kernel:** The authors successfully combine a max-margin objective with the analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel. This enables efficient training, even on large corpora, a significant practical advantage.
3. **Empirical Validation on Semantic Tasks:** The paper provides robust empirical validation on standard word similarity and entailment benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to established baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Motivation and Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing word embedding methods in representing polysemy and uncertainty. It establishes a strong motivation for exploring probabilistic, multimodal representations.
2. **Expressive Power of Gaussian Mixtures:** Representing words with Gaussian mixtures is a conceptually sound approach. Mixtures allow capturing multiple distinct meanings, offering a richer and more nuanced representation than single vectors or unimodal distributions. The ability to model probability mass and uncertainty is particularly valuable.
3. **Efficient Training via Max-Margin and Kernel:** The choice of a max-margin objective, combined with the closed-form expected likelihood kernel, is crucial. It enables efficient training, making the method scalable to large datasets. This is a significant practical advantage over methods requiring more computationally intensive training procedures.
4. **Solid Experimental Results:** The experiments on word similarity and entailment provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The improvements over baselines demonstrate the value of the Gaussian mixture representation and the training procedure.
5. **Generally Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the proposed methodology and understand the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Arbitrary Number of Gaussian Components (k=2):** The choice of *k*=2 for the number of Gaussian components feels somewhat arbitrary. The paper does not provide a clear justification for this value, nor does it explore the impact of varying *k*. A more thorough investigation into the optimal *k* for different word types would strengthen the analysis. Does increasing *k* lead to overfitting, or are there diminishing returns?
2. **Distribution Shape for Unimodal Words:** The paper doesn't adequately address how the learned distribution looks for words with a dominant, single meaning. Does the Gaussian mixture simply collapse into a single, high-variance Gaussian, or does it learn distinct components that represent subtle variations in context?  Understanding this behavior is important for a complete picture of the model's capabilities.
3. **Spherical Covariance Assumption:** The use of spherical covariances, while simplifying computations, raises concerns about potential performance limitations. The paper does not explore the impact of allowing diagonal (anisotropic) covariances.  Anisotropic covariances could potentially capture more complex relationships between dimensions, leading to improved performance, at the cost of increased computational complexity.
4. **Missing Table Reference:** Table 4 is presented but not referenced within the main text. This omission reduces the impact of the results presented in that table.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What criteria were used to select *k*=2 for the number of Gaussian components? How does performance change as *k* is increased or decreased?
2. Can you characterize the shape of the learned distribution for words with a predominantly single meaning? Does it resemble a high-variance Gaussian, or does it exhibit other characteristics?
3. How would allowing diagonal (anisotropic) covariances affect the performance of the model, and what would be the computational trade-offs?
4. Could you please clarify the relevance of the results shown in Table 4 within the broader context of the paper?

**Additional Comments:**
