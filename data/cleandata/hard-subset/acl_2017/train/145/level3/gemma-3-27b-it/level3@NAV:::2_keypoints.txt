- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy and uncertainty, trained with a max-margin objective and the expected likelihood kernel.
- Demonstrates that Gaussian mixtures improve over single Gaussians and deterministic vectors, with qualitative examples and better performance on word similarity and entailment tasks.
- Key contribution: applying Gaussian mixtures to embeddings plus an analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel for efficient training.
- Strength: clear motivation and problem definition addressing limits of existing embeddings for multi-sense words.
- Strength: increased expressive power—GMs can represent multiple senses and associated uncertainty more accurately than single Gaussians.
- Strength: efficient, scalable training via the max-margin objective combined with the expected likelihood kernel.
- Strength: comprehensive empirical validation showing improvements over baselines on established datasets.
- Weakness: choice of number of components (k=2) is unjustified; need exploration of different k and effects on overfitting/performance.
- Weakness: no analysis of learned distributions for predominantly unimodal words (whether secondary components shrink or persist).
- Weakness: spherical covariances may limit expressiveness; need investigation of diagonal/anisotropic covariances and associated trade-offs.
- Question: why was k=2 chosen and how does performance vary with different k values?
- Question: can authors provide visualizations or statistics (e.g., component weight variances) for single-meaning words?
- Question: what is the computational overhead and expected performance gain when using diagonal covariances instead of spherical?
- Additional comment: Table 4 is referenced nowhere in the paper.
- Additional comment: the Luong et al. reference is missing the publication year.
