- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture polysemy and uncertainty, trained with a max-margin objective and similarity via the expected likelihood kernel.
- Main contribution: Gaussian mixture representation enabling multiple meanings, associated probabilities, and uncertainty modeling.
- Main contribution: Efficient training by combining a max-margin objective with an analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel for scalability.
- Main contribution: Empirical validation showing improved performance on word similarity and entailment tasks over baselines like word2vec and single-Gaussian embeddings.
- Strength: Clear motivation and precise problem definition highlighting limits of existing embeddings for polysemy and uncertainty.
- Strength: Conceptual expressiveness—mixtures capture distinct meanings and uncertainty better than unimodal or deterministic vectors.
- Strength: Practical efficiency—closed-form kernel plus max-margin enables scalable training on large corpora.
- Strength: Strong experimental results that convincingly outperform established baselines.
- Strength: Paper is generally well-structured and clearly written.
- Weakness: Choice of number of components (k=2) appears arbitrary and lacks justification or ablation over k.
- Weakness: Unclear how distributions behave for predominantly single-meaning words (collapse to one high-variance Gaussian or retain distinct components).
- Weakness: Spherical covariance assumption may limit expressiveness; no exploration of diagonal/anisotropic covariances and trade-offs.
- Weakness: Table 4 is included but not referenced in the main text.
- Question: Why was k=2 chosen and how does performance change when k is varied?
- Question: How do learned distributions look for unimodal words—high variance vs multiple subtle components?
- Question: What would be the effect and computational trade-offs of allowing diagonal (anisotropic) covariances?
- Question: Please clarify the relevance and interpretation of the results in Table 4.
- Additional comment: Missing publication year for the Luong et al. reference.
- Additional comment: Consider adding visualizations of learned distributions for polysemous versus unimodal words.
