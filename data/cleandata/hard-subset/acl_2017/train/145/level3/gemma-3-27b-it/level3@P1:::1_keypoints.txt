- Proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture multiple senses, uncertainty, and richer semantic relationships.
- Introduces an energy-based max-margin learning objective using the expected likelihood kernel for efficient training of Gaussian mixtures.
- Shows empirical improvements over baselines (word2vec, unimodal Gaussian embeddings) on word similarity and entailment tasks, with qualitative examples of sense capture.

- Novelty: mixture-of-Gaussians addresses polysemy and advances distributional semantics.
- Technical soundness: well-motivated objective, justified kernel choice, and attention to numerical stability.
- Clear presentation: well-written paper with helpful figures and tables.
- Strong empirical results: consistent gains across benchmarks and intuitive qualitative analyses.
- Reproducibility: authors commit to releasing code.

- Limited comparison to prior mixture-model work (e.g., Tian et al., 2014); needs clearer distinctions in energy/objective formulation.
- Unexpected performance drop on SCWS when moving from w2g to w2gm requires clarification (model complexity or hyperparameters).
- Missing citations to relevant polysemy/WSD work (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015).

- How does this approach specifically differ from Tian et al. (2014) in energy function and objective to yield better results?
- Why does w2gm perform slightly worse on SCWS than w2g—dataset quirks or model configuration issues?
- How sensitive are results to the number of Gaussian components K, and were values beyond 2–3 explored?

- Reviewer found the authors’ response satisfactory for most concerns.
- Recommend adding a discussion of computational complexity relative to existing methods.
- Suggest further analysis/visualization to improve interpretability of learned Gaussian components.

- Overall recommendation: accept — significant, well-executed contribution with minor addressable weaknesses.
