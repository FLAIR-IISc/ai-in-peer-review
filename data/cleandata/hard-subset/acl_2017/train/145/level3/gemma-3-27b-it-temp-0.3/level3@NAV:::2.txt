## Review of "Word to Gaussian Mixture"

This paper proposes a novel approach to word embeddings, representing words as Gaussian mixtures to capture polysemy and uncertainty. The authors train these embeddings using a max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel for similarity calculation. The core idea is well-motivated, addressing limitations of existing methods like word2vec and single Gaussian embeddings in representing multiple word meanings. 

The main contribution of this work lies in the effective combination of Gaussian mixtures with a max-margin learning framework and the analytically tractable expected likelihood kernel. This allows for scalable training and provides a richer, more expressive representation of word semantics than simpler embedding methods. A second contribution is the demonstration, through experiments on word similarity and entailment tasks, that this approach outperforms existing methods, particularly in capturing nuanced meanings. Finally, the qualitative analysis and visualization (though the link was not tested during review) provide insights into how the learned distributions represent different senses of a word.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing word embedding techniques in handling polysemy and uncertainty, providing a strong rationale for the proposed approach.
*   **Expressive Representation:** Gaussian mixtures offer a significant advantage over deterministic vectors or single Gaussian distributions by allowing for multiple modes, capturing different meanings and associated uncertainties.
*   **Efficient Training:** The max-margin learning objective, coupled with the closed-form similarity calculation via the expected likelihood kernel, enables efficient training, a crucial factor for large-scale datasets.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on word similarity and entailment demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over baseline models.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Arbitrary Number of Components:** The choice of *k*=2 Gaussian components feels somewhat arbitrary. The paper doesn't provide a clear justification for this value, nor does it explore the impact of increasing *k* on performance. It’s unclear if performance plateaus or degrades with higher *k*.
*   **Distribution Shape for Unimodal Words:** The paper doesn't discuss how the learned distribution looks for words with a dominant meaning. Does the model still learn two components, with one having significantly higher probability mass? Understanding this behavior is important for assessing the model's generalizability.
*   **Spherical Covariance Limitation:** The use of spherical covariances, while simplifying calculations, may limit the model's ability to capture complex relationships between word dimensions. The impact of using diagonal or anisotropic covariances on performance is not explored.
*   **Missing Reference & Table Reference:** Table 4 is not referenced in the text, and the Luong et al. reference is missing a publication year.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What criteria were used to select *k*=2 for the number of Gaussian components? How does performance change as *k* is increased, and is there a point of diminishing returns?
2.  Can you characterize the learned distribution for words with a single dominant meaning? Does the model still learn two components, and if so, how is the probability mass distributed between them?
3.  What is the computational overhead of using diagonal or anisotropic covariances compared to spherical covariances? Do you anticipate significant performance gains from exploring these alternatives?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please add the publication year to the Luong et al. reference.
*   Please reference Table 4 in the main text.
