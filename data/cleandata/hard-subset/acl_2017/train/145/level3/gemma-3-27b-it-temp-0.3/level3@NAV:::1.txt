## Review of "Word to Gaussian Mixture"

This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings, representing words as Gaussian mixtures (GMs) rather than single Gaussian distributions or point vectors. The core idea – capturing multiple word meanings through distinct Gaussian components – is a logical extension of prior work and addresses a clear limitation of unimodal embeddings. The authors present a well-structured and clearly written paper, detailing the methodology, objective function, and experimental setup.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful application of multimodal Gaussian distributions to word embeddings, enabling a more nuanced representation of polysemous words and improved performance on tasks sensitive to semantic ambiguity like word similarity and entailment. The proposed energy-based max-margin objective, coupled with the use of the expected likelihood kernel, provides an analytically tractable and scalable learning procedure. The qualitative analysis (Table 1) convincingly demonstrates the model’s ability to capture distinct word senses.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear and Well-Structured:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow from motivation to methodology to experimentation.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** Extending Vilnis & McCallum (2014) to multimodal distributions is a significant step towards more expressive word representations.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline methods on several benchmark datasets, validating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The use of the expected likelihood kernel and the max-margin objective are well-justified and contribute to the model’s analytical tractability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates improvements over several baselines, the comparison could be more comprehensive. Specifically, a more direct comparison to Tian et al. (2014), which also employs mixture models for word representations, is needed. Including their results in Tables 3 and 4 would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Missing Citations:** The paper omits several relevant citations. Including Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015) would provide a more complete picture of the related work.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   The results in Section 4.6 show that w2gm loses performance compared to w2g on the SWCS analysis. Can you explain why this might be the case? Is it related to the specific characteristics of this dataset or a limitation of the proposed method?

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of word embeddings. The proposed method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and empirically validated. Addressing the weaknesses regarding comparative analysis and missing citations would further enhance the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance.
