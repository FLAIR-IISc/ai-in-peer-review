- Summary: Proposes Gaussian mixture word embeddings trained with a max-margin objective and expected likelihood kernel to better model polysemy, showing improvements on similarity and entailment tasks.

- Contribution: Introduces multimodal word representations using Gaussian mixtures to explicitly model multiple senses.

- Contribution: Uses a max-margin learning objective with closed-form expected-likelihood similarity for efficient training.

- Contribution: Demonstrates empirical gains on standard word similarity and entailment benchmarks.

- Strength: Clearly motivates the limitation of point and unimodal embeddings for polysemous words and the need for multimodal representations.

- Strength: Gaussian mixtures provide expressive, uncertainty-aware representations that capture multiple senses, supported by qualitative visualizations.

- Strength: Closed-form expected likelihood kernel enables scalable, efficient training on large corpora.

- Strength: Strong and consistent empirical improvements over word2vec and unimodal Gaussian embeddings across multiple datasets.

- Weakness: Arbitrary use of K=2 components without sufficient justification or empirical analysis of different K values.

- Weakness: Uses spherical covariances for efficiency but lacks exploration of diagonal/anisotropic covariances and their impact.

- Weakness: No analysis of model behavior for words with a single dominant meaning (whether components collapse or one gets negligible weight).

- Weakness: Minor presentation issues (Table 4 not referenced; missing year for Luong et al.).

- Question: Why was K=2 chosen and how does performance change for K=3,4?

- Question: For single-meaning words, does the model collapse to one meaningful component or retain two with low-probability noise?

- Question: Why choose spherical covariances over diagonal/full, and how would anisotropic covariances affect performance?
