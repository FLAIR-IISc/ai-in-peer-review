

# Review of "Multimodal Word Distributions with Gaussian Mixtures" (ACL 2017)

## Summary
This paper proposes representing words as Gaussian mixtures (GM) to model polysemy, trained with a max-margin objective and using the expected likelihood kernel as similarity measure. The authors demonstrate that their approach captures multiple meanings of words more effectively than point embeddings (e.g., word2vec) or unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis and McCallum, 2014), with improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel word representation approach using Gaussian mixtures to explicitly model multiple word senses (polysemy)
2. A max-margin learning objective with closed-form similarity for efficient training of multimodal word distributions
3. Demonstrated effectiveness on standard word similarity and entailment benchmarks

## Strengths
1. **Clear problem motivation and definition**: The paper effectively identifies the limitation of existing approaches in capturing polysemy. Traditional point embeddings and unimodal Gaussian embeddings cannot adequately represent words with multiple distinct meanings (e.g., "bank" as financial institution vs. river bank), as the mean vector gets pulled between meanings and the variance becomes unnecessarily large. The authors clearly articulate why multimodal representations are necessary for both semantic expressiveness and predictive performance.

2. **Expressive representation with uncertainty**: The Gaussian mixture model provides a more expressive representation than deterministic vectors or unimodal Gaussians. It can capture multiple meanings (each Gaussian component representing one sense), probability mass across different meanings, and uncertainty (via covariance matrices). The qualitative results in Table 1 and the visualization in Figure 1 compellingly demonstrate how the model learns meaningful multi-prototype representations for polysemous words.

3. **Efficient training with closed-form similarity**: The use of the expected likelihood kernel as the energy function provides a closed-form solution for similarity between Gaussian mixtures, enabling efficient training. The authors show the model can be trained on large corpora (billions of words) in a reasonable timeframe, addressing scalability concerns that often plague probabilistic models.

4. **Strong empirical results**: The model achieves significant improvements over existing approaches on multiple word similarity datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MTurk) and word entailment tasks. The results are consistent across different similarity metrics, with the Gaussian mixture model (w2gm) outperforming both word2vec and the unimodal Gaussian embedding (w2g).

## Weaknesses
1. **Unjustified choice of k=2 components**: The paper uses k=2 for all words without justification or analysis of how different k values affect performance. The authors should provide evidence for why k=2 is optimal, and whether increasing k would improve results (e.g., for words with three or more distinct meanings). The paper mentions that for k=3, "due to the limited number of words with more than 2 meanings, our model with K=3 does not generally offer performance differences," but this is not supported by empirical results.

2. **Spherical covariance choice without exploration**: The paper uses spherical covariances purely for efficiency but does not explore how diagonal (anisotropic) covariances would affect performance. This is a significant limitation since anisotropic covariances could better capture the varying semantic uncertainty in different dimensions of the embedding space.

3. **Lack of analysis for words with single dominant meaning**: The paper does not address how the model behaves for words with only one dominant meaning (e.g., "apple" primarily meaning fruit rather than the company). Would the model learn two components with one having very low probability, or would it effectively collapse to a single mode? This is important for understanding the model's general behavior.

4. **Minor technical issues**: Table 4 is not referenced in the text, and the reference for Luong et al. is missing the publication year, which are issues that should be addressed in the final version.

## Questions to Authors
1. What criteria determined the choice of k=2 for the number of Gaussian components? Did you experiment with higher values of k (e.g., k=3, 4)? If so, how did performance change with increasing k?

2. For words with only one dominant meaning (e.g., "cat" or "sun"), what does the learned distribution look like? Does the model effectively collapse to a single meaningful component, or does it maintain two components with one having very low probability?

3. Why was the spherical covariance matrix used instead of diagonal or full covariance matrices? How would using diagonal (anisotropic) covariances affect performance? The paper mentions spherical covariances are for efficiency, but it would be valuable to know if this choice impacts the model's effectiveness.
