- Introduces Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) for word embeddings to represent multiple meanings of polysemous words.  
- Proposes an energy-based max-margin objective using an expected likelihood kernel for analytic tractability with multimodal semantics.  
- Provides comprehensive empirical validation showing consistent improvements over unimodal Gaussian embeddings and word2vec on multiple word similarity and entailment tasks.  
- Strong conceptual contribution: extends unimodal Gaussian embeddings to naturally handle polysemy.  
- Qualitative evidence (Table 1) that the model learns distinct semantic components for words like “rock” and “bank.”  
- Theoretical development of the expected likelihood kernel is sound and well-motivated.  
- Experimental results (Table 2) show w2gm consistently outperforming w2g and word2vec across standard datasets.  
- Shows reduction in variance for polysemous words, supporting improved semantic uncertainty modeling (Section 4.5).  
- Lacks quantitative comparison with prior mixture-model embedding work (Tian et al. 2014, Neelakantan et al. 2014, Li & Jurafsky 2015, Liu et al. 2015).  
- No explanation for w2gm performing worse than w2g on the SWCS dataset (Table 4).  
- Limited analysis of the optimal number of mixture components (K); only brief mention of K=3 without impact analysis.  
- Missing direct comparison of training efficiency with other scalable approaches despite claiming fast training on billions of words.  
- Question: Why does w2gm underperform w2g on SWCS contrary to expectations?  
- Question: Can you clarify the relationship to Tian et al. (2014) and why their results weren’t included in the comparisons?
