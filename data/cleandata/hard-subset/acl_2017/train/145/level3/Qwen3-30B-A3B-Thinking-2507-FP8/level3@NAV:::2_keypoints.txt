- Proposes word embeddings as Gaussian mixtures to model polysemy via multiple meaning components.
- Introduces a max-margin energy-based training objective using the expected likelihood kernel for efficient, closed-form similarity.
- Demonstrates improved performance over point embeddings and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on similarity and entailment benchmarks.
- Main contribution: GM word embeddings explicitly capturing multiple word meanings.
- Main contribution: Theoretically grounded max-margin learning with closed-form similarity computations.
- Main contribution: Empirical evidence of superior performance on standard tasks.
- Well-motivated explanation of unimodal distributions’ failure for polysemous words.
- Strength: More expressive than point embeddings while remaining computationally tractable.
- Strength: Max-margin objective with expected likelihood kernel enables efficient training.
- Strength: Clear writing and thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets.
- Weakness: Unjustified fixed choice of k=2 components; no rationale for not using more components.
- Weakness: No analysis of behavior for words with a single dominant meaning.
- Weakness: Spherical covariance assumption appears arbitrary; no discussion of anisotropic/diagonal alternatives.
- Weakness: Fails to reference Table 4 in the text and omits publication year for Luong et al.
- Questions: Why was k=2 chosen and what criteria determined it?
- Questions: How do learned distributions look for words with a single dominant meaning?
- Questions: Was spherical covariance chosen purely for efficiency, and how would diagonal covariances affect performance?
- Questions: Does increasing k beyond 2 improve modeling for highly polysemous words?
