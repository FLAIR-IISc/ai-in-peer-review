- Proposes modeling words as multimodal Gaussian mixtures (w2gm) to capture polysemy, improving over unimodal Gaussians (w2g).
- Introduces an energy-based max-margin objective using the expected likelihood kernel that is analytically tractable for Gaussian mixtures.
- Provides extensive experiments showing w2gm improves on word similarity and entailment benchmarks versus strong baselines.

- Clearly motivated solution to polysemy with qualitative examples showing distinct components for different senses (e.g., "rock", "bank").
- Strong experimental validation across many standard datasets, with consistent improvements, especially on nuanced similarity tasks.
- Sound technical derivation and practical implementation details (diagonal covariances, stability, initialization) enabling large-scale training.

- Fails to adequately compare and position against Tian et al. (2014), which also uses mixture models for word representations.
- Omits recent relevant citations (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015) that address similar problems.
- Evaluation tables lack direct results from comparable prior approaches (Tian et al., Huang et al., Chen et al.), limiting completeness of comparisons.
- Unexplained drop in performance of w2gm versus w2g on the SWCS analysis (Table 4), contradicting other results and requiring clarification.

- Question: Why does w2gm lose performance compared to w2g on the SWCS analysis despite improvements elsewhere?
- Authors have addressed some concerns (e.g., differences from Tian et al.); remaining actions: add the missing citations and clarify the SWCS performance drop in the final version.
