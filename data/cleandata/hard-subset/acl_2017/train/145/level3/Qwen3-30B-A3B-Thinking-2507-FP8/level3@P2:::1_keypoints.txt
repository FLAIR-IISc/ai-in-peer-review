- Introduces Gaussian mixture word embeddings to model words as multimodal distributions, addressing polysemy beyond unimodal Gaussian embeddings.  
- Uses an energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel for tractable training and semantic/entailment representation.  
- Shows empirical gains over word2vec, unimodal Gaussian embeddings, and multi-prototype embeddings on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.  
- Principled extension that directly addresses unimodal variance inflation for polysemous words, with qualitative examples (e.g., "rock", "bank") showing distinct components.  
- Thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets with consistent superior performance and a visualization tool for exploring embeddings.  
- Methodologically sound: expected likelihood kernel gives closed-form energy, with implementation details for numerical stability and scalability.  
- Lacks detailed comparison with other mixture/multi-prototype approaches like Tian et al. (2014), weakening the novelty/advantage claims.  
- Misses important citations and comparisons to prior multi-prototype work (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015).  
- Does not explain why the mixture model (w2gm) underperforms the unimodal model (w2g) on the SCWS dataset.  
- Fails to sufficiently address handling of words with more than two meanings (K>2), despite brief mention of K=3.  
- Question: Why does w2gm lose performance vs. w2g on SCWS and is this a limitation for certain polysemy types?  
- Question: Please provide a detailed comparison with Tian et al. (2014) clarifying differences and advantages.  
- Question: Why are results from Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015) not included in Tables 2-3 for fair comparison?
