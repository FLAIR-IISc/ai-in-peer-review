

This paper proposes a multimodal word embedding approach using Gaussian mixtures (GM) to model multiple word senses, uncertainty, and entailment. The key contributions are: 1) A novel representation of words as Gaussian mixture distributions rather than single vectors, enabling capture of multiple meanings; 2) An energy-based max-margin objective with expected likelihood kernel for efficient training; 3) Demonstrated improvements over existing methods on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.

Strengths: The paper clearly identifies and addresses a significant limitation of current word embeddings: their inability to model polysemy effectively. By representing words as Gaussian mixtures, the model naturally captures multiple meanings (e.g., 'bank' as financial institution vs. river bank), which the authors demonstrate through qualitative examples. The expected likelihood kernel provides an elegant closed-form solution for similarity computation between distributions, enabling efficient training that scales to large corpora. The method outperforms both deterministic embeddings (word2vec) and unimodal probabilistic embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum) on multiple word similarity datasets (WS, WS-R, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MT-287, RW) and shows consistent improvements on word entailment tasks. The implementation details and ablation studies provide sufficient evidence for the model's effectiveness.

Strengths: The model's ability to reduce the variance problem for polysemous words is particularly noteworthy. For example, the word 'rock' in the unimodal Gaussian embedding has a much higher variance (e−1.8 ≈ 1.65) compared to the Gaussian mixture model (variance ≈ e−2.5 ≈ 0.82), which better aligns with the semantic reality of words with multiple distinct meanings. This technical insight demonstrates a deeper understanding of the problem than previous work.

Strengths: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology, thorough experiments, and thoughtful qualitative analysis. The interactive visualization of the embeddings (available at http://35.161.153.223:6002) is a valuable addition that helps readers understand the model's behavior. The authors also provide comprehensive implementation details and hyperparameter choices, making the work highly reproducible.

Weaknesses: The most significant weakness is the arbitrary choice of K=2 mixture components without sufficient justification. The paper states that "for words with multiple meanings, such as 'rock', the variance of the learned representation becomes unnecessarily large" but fails to explain why K=2 was selected as optimal. The authors should provide a thorough analysis of how performance varies with K, including whether higher values (e.g., K=3) could further improve results or if K=2 is sufficient for most words. The lack of this analysis weakens the claim that the model "automatically discovers multiple meanings."

Weaknesses: The paper uses spherical covariances throughout, which severely limits the model's expressiveness. The authors acknowledge this is "for computational efficiency" but provide no analysis of how diagonal (anisotropic) covariances would affect performance. This is a critical omission, as anisotropic covariances would better capture the varying uncertainty along different semantic dimensions. The paper should include an ablation study comparing spherical vs. diagonal covariances.

Weaknesses: The paper does not address how the model handles words with a single dominant meaning. For words like "the" or "and," which have essentially one meaning, the Gaussian mixture representation might introduce unnecessary complexity. The authors should provide evidence that the model adapts appropriately for such words, rather than forcing them into a multimodal structure.

Questions to Authors: 
1. What criteria determined K=2 as the optimal number of components? How does performance change when increasing K beyond 2?
2. How does the learned distribution for words with a single dominant meaning (e.g., "the", "and") differ from the multimodal case?
3. Was the use of spherical covariances purely for efficiency, and how would diagonal covariances affect performance and interpretability?

Minor issues: Table 4 is not referenced in the text, and the reference for Luong et al. is missing the publication year. These should be corrected for publication.
