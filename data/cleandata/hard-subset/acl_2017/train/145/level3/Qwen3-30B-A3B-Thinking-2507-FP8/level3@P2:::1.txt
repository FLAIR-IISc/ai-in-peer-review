

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a novel approach to word representation by modeling words as multimodal distributions using Gaussian mixtures, extending Vilnis and McCallum's (2014) unimodal Gaussian embedding framework. The primary contributions are:

1. A Gaussian mixture model for word embeddings that explicitly captures multiple distinct word meanings (polysemy) through multiple mixture components, addressing the limitation of unimodal representations which struggle to represent words with multiple meanings without excessive variance.

2. An energy-based max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel, which provides analytic tractability for training Gaussian mixture models while maintaining the ability to represent semantic relationships and entailment.

3. Comprehensive empirical validation showing that the proposed model significantly outperforms previous approaches (including word2vec, Gaussian embeddings, and multi-prototype embeddings) on standard word similarity benchmarks and word entailment tasks.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a clear, principled extension to existing work on probabilistic word embeddings. The Gaussian mixture approach directly addresses a well-known limitation of unimodal Gaussian embeddings (Vilnis & McCallum, 2014) for polysemous words, where the model's variance becomes unnecessarily large to accommodate multiple meanings. The qualitative results in Table 1 compellingly demonstrate that the model successfully learns distinct semantic components for polysemous words like "rock" and "bank."

2. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured, with comprehensive comparisons across multiple word similarity datasets (Table 2) and entailment tasks (Table 5). The model consistently outperforms previous approaches, with the Gaussian mixture model (w2gm) achieving the best results on most benchmarks. The authors also provide a useful visualization tool to explore the embeddings, enhancing the paper's accessibility.

3. The methodological contributions are well-motivated and technically sound. The use of the expected likelihood kernel provides a closed-form solution for energy computation with Gaussian mixtures, which is a significant practical advantage over alternatives like KL divergence. The authors also provide careful implementation details (Section A.2) that address numerical stability and scalability, making the approach more practical for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper lacks a detailed comparison with similar approaches that also use mixture models for multi-prototype word embeddings, particularly Tian et al. (2014). The authors mention this work but don't explain how their approach differs or why it might be superior, which weakens the contribution claim. The paper should include results from Tian et al. (2014) in Tables 2-3 for fair comparison.

2. The paper misses important citations to key prior work on multi-prototype embeddings, including Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015). These works present related approaches that should be acknowledged and compared against to better position the contribution.

3. The paper doesn't adequately explain why the Gaussian mixture model (w2gm) loses performance compared to the unimodal model (w2g) on the SCWS dataset (Table 4), which is a significant weakness. This is particularly puzzling given that the entire motivation of the paper is to address polysemy through multiple components.

4. The paper doesn't address how the model handles words with more than two meanings (K>2), despite briefly mentioning results with K=3 in Section 4.2. This is a limitation for words with three or more distinct meanings, which could be an important aspect of the model's applicability.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper mentions that w2gm loses performance compared to w2g on the SCWS dataset (Table 4). Could you explain why this occurs, and whether this is a limitation of the Gaussian mixture approach for certain types of polysemy?

2. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with Tian et al. (2014), explaining how your approach differs and why it might be superior for capturing polysemy?

3. Why does the paper not include results from Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015) for comparison in Tables 2-3, given that these works also explore multi-prototype embeddings?
