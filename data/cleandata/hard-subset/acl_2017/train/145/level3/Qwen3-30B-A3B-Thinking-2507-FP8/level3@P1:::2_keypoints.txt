- Represents words as Gaussian mixture distributions to capture multiple senses, uncertainty, and entailment.  
- Uses an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel for efficient training.  
- Demonstrates improvements over deterministic and unimodal probabilistic embeddings on word similarity and entailment benchmarks.  
- Effectively addresses polysemy with qualitative examples showing distinct senses (e.g., "bank").  
- Expected likelihood kernel provides closed-form similarity computation, enabling scalable training and strong empirical performance across many datasets.  
- Shows technical advantage in reducing variance for polysemous words (example: "rock" variance reduction).  
- Paper is well-written, includes thorough experiments, ablations, reproducible implementation details, and an interactive visualization.  
- Chooses K=2 mixture components arbitrarily without sufficient justification or analysis of larger K.  
- Uses spherical covariances throughout, which limits expressiveness and lacks comparison to diagonal (anisotropic) covariances.  
- Does not show how the model handles words with a single dominant meaning, risking unnecessary model complexity for such words.  
- Question: Why was K=2 chosen and how does performance change for K>2?  
- Question: How do learned distributions behave for single-meaning words (e.g., "the", "and")?  
- Question: Were spherical covariances chosen solely for efficiency, and how would diagonal covariances affect performance and interpretability?  
- Minor: Table 4 is not referenced in the text; Luong et al. reference missing publication year.
