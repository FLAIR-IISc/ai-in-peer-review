- Introduces multimodal Gaussian mixture embeddings to model multiple word meanings, extending Vilnis & McCallum (2014).
- Main contribution: a Gaussian mixture model that captures polysemy, entailment, and uncertainty better than point-based or unimodal Gaussian embeddings.
- Paper is very well-structured, clearly written, and technically strong.
- Methodology: energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel enabling analytical tractability.
- Qualitative results (Table 1) convincingly show distinct senses for polysemous words (e.g., “rock”, “bank”) that unimodal models miss.
- Quantitative results show consistent improvements on word similarity and entailment tasks and competitive performance with state-of-the-art.
- Strength: interpretable mixture components and reduced variance for polysemous words compared to unimodal approaches.
- Provides useful implementation details and discusses scalability.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to related mixture-model approaches, notably Tian et al. (2014).
- Missing important citations (Neelakantan et al. 2014; Li & Jurafsky 2015; Liu et al. 2015) that should be included in related work.
- Should add results from prior approaches in Tables 3 and 4 for fair comparison.
- Needs an explanation for why w2gm underperforms w2g on the SWCS analysis, which contradicts the main claim.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant contributions but requires minor revisions to strengthen related-work comparison and address missing citations.
