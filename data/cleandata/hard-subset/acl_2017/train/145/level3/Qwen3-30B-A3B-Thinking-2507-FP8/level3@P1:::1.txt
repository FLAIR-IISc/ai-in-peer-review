

This paper introduces a novel approach to word embeddings using multimodal Gaussian mixture distributions, addressing a key limitation of prior work that represented words with unimodal distributions. The primary contribution is the introduction of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) to represent multiple distinct meanings of polysemous words, extending Vilnis and McCallum's (2014) Gaussian embedding approach. A second significant contribution is the development of an energy-based max-margin objective with an expected likelihood kernel that enables analytic tractability while capturing multimodal semantics. The third contribution is the comprehensive empirical validation showing consistent improvements over alternatives on multiple word similarity and entailment tasks.

Strengths: The paper makes a strong conceptual contribution by recognizing that unimodal Gaussian embeddings cannot properly represent polysemous words, and proposing a natural extension to Gaussian mixtures to handle multiple meanings. The qualitative analysis in Table 1 provides compelling evidence that the model learns distinct semantic components for words like "rock" (stone vs. music) and "bank" (river vs. financial), which is not possible with unimodal representations. The paper's theoretical development of the expected likelihood kernel as an energy function is sound and well-motivated, providing the necessary mathematical foundation for the approach. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, with the multimodal model (w2gm) consistently outperforming both unimodal Gaussian embeddings (w2g) and word2vec on multiple standard word similarity datasets (WS, MEN, MC, RG, YP, MTurk, RW) as shown in Table 2. The reduction in variance for polysemous words (Section 4.5) further supports the model's ability to better capture semantic uncertainty.

Weaknesses: The most significant weakness is the lack of comparison with prior work that also uses mixture models for word embeddings, particularly Tian et al. (2014), which is mentioned in related work but not included in the quantitative evaluation. Including results from Tian et al. (2014), Neelakantan et al. (2014), Li & Jurafsky (2015), and Liu et al. (2015) in Tables 2-4 would strengthen the evaluation by providing a more complete picture of how this approach relates to the broader literature. A second major weakness is the counterintuitive result in Table 4 where w2gm performs worse than w2g on the SWCS dataset, with no explanation provided for this unexpected finding. The paper would benefit from a more systematic exploration of the optimal number of mixture components (K) for different words, as the current analysis only briefly mentions K=3 without demonstrating its impact on performance. Additionally, while the paper mentions training on billions of words in days, it lacks a direct comparison of training efficiency with other approaches.

Questions to Authors: Why does w2gm perform worse than w2g on the SWCS dataset, contrary to the model's design to handle polysemy? Could you clarify the relationship between your approach and Tian et al. (2014), which also uses mixture models to split word representations, and explain why their results aren't included in the comparative evaluation?
