

This paper proposes a novel approach to word embeddings by representing words as Gaussian mixtures (GM), enabling the modeling of polysemy through multiple meaning components. The authors introduce a max-margin energy-based objective with the expected likelihood kernel for efficient training, and demonstrate significant improvements over deterministic embeddings and unimodal Gaussian embeddings on word similarity and entailment tasks.

The main contributions are: (1) a Gaussian mixture model for word embeddings that explicitly captures multiple word meanings; (2) a theoretically grounded training method using max-margin learning with closed-form similarity; and (3) empirical evidence showing superior performance on standard benchmarks. The problem is well-motivated, with clear explanation of how unimodal distributions fail for polysemous words.

Strengths: The Gaussian mixture representation is a compelling solution to polysemy, offering more expressive power than point embeddings while maintaining computational tractability. The max-margin objective with the expected likelihood kernel enables efficient training with closed-form solutions. The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and thorough experimental validation across multiple word similarity datasets.

Weaknesses: The choice of k=2 components is not justified—why not use more components for words with more meanings? The paper lacks analysis of how the model handles words with a single dominant meaning, and the spherical covariance assumption appears arbitrary without discussion of anisotropic alternatives. The paper also fails to reference Table 4 in the text and omits publication year for Luong et al.

Questions to Authors: 1) What criteria determined the choice of k=2? 2) How does the learned distribution look for words with only one dominant meaning? 3) Was the spherical covariance choice purely for efficiency, and how would diagonal covariances affect performance? 4) Does increasing k beyond 2 improve results on polysemous words?
