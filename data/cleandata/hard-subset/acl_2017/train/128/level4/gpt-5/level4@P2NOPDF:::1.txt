This paper introduces a neural architecture that encodes structural linguistic information into a memory network for sequence tagging, specifically slot filling in dialogue NLU. Substructures (e.g., parse-tree nodes) are represented as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these embeddings is provided to an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The main contribution is a simple mechanism to “flatten” structured information into an array of vectors (the memory) and integrate it into a tagger. The idea is closely related to structured or syntax-based attention (e.g., attention over treeLSTM nodes); relevant prior work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. The substructure encoder resembles DCNN (Ma et al.), where a node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. While the architecture is not entirely novel, the approach is straightforward and practical compared to some earlier methods.

-----Weaknesses-----
I am not fully convinced by the empirical evaluation, largely due to missing details about the baselines. Points below are ordered by decreasing importance.

- The proposed model has two components: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baselines are TreeRNN and DCNN, which are typically used for sentence embeddings but can also yield node/substructure embeddings. It is unclear how these models are used to compute both parts in your setup. Please clarify how you extract and use node-level representations from these baselines and how they feed into the tagger.

- The model employs two RNNs: a standard chain-based RNN and a “knowledge-guided” RNN. The only difference in the latter appears to be the inclusion of a memory-derived “knowledge” vector in the input (Eqns. 5 and 8). Using separate parameters for these two RNNs seems unnecessary and primarily increases capacity. This raises concerns about fairness: what are the hyperparameters and sizes of all neural baselines? Parameter counts should be reported and matched as closely as possible.

- A reasonable baseline would feed additional linguistic signals as features to a single RNN (e.g., syntactic heads, NER tags, etc.) without a separate memory mechanism. This would help isolate the value of the memory network versus simple feature augmentation.

- Please discuss the model’s robustness to parser errors and, if possible, provide results under noisy parses.

Comments on the model:
- After computing substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute token-specific attention over substructures at each position. Why use a single, static attention distribution shared across all words? As implemented, the “knowledge” vector may act more like a global salience filter. This strengthens the case for the feature-based baseline mentioned above.

- Since the attention weight is the inner product between the sentence embedding and each substructure embedding, and both are produced by the same RNN/CNN family, does this bias the model toward substructures that most resemble the global sentence representation? Please clarify how this avoids degenerate behavior (e.g., overemphasizing certain frequent or broadly similar nodes).

- The paper claims the approach generalizes to different forms of “knowledge,” but the method appears to require that substructures be representable as word sequences. It is not obvious how to incorporate, for example, constituent trees without a clear linearization strategy. More discussion or examples would help.

Finally, I am hesitant about the term “knowledge.” In NLP, “knowledge” often refers to external/world knowledge (e.g., KBs), whereas here it is primarily syntactic—and arguably semantic if using AMR. Consider more precise terminology to avoid confusion.

-----General Discussion-----
This is a practical model that performs well on one dataset, but the core ideas are not particularly novel (see Strengths). For ACL, I would expect clearer takeaways or insights. More importantly, the current experimental description leaves key questions unanswered, making it difficult to assess the true contribution. Clarifications on baseline construction, parameter fairness, and ablations would significantly strengthen the paper.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The rebuttal did not address my main concern: whether baselines like TreeRNN were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. A second major concern remains: using two separate RNNs increases parameter count relative to baselines, potentially confounding comparisons. Given these unresolved issues, I am not changing my scores.
