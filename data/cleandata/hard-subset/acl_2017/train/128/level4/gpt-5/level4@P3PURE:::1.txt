Summary
This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging, specifically slot filling in the natural language understanding module of conversational systems. Substructures (e.g., a node in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

Strengths
- The main contribution is a simple mechanism to “flatten” structured information into an array of vectors (the memory) that is then connected to the tagger as auxiliary knowledge. The idea is related to structured/syntax-based attention (i.e., attention over nodes from TreeLSTM). Relevant prior work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation.
- The proposed substructure encoder resembles DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded based on a sequence of ancestor words.
- While the architecture is not entirely novel, the approach is simple and practical compared to prior work, which I appreciate.

Weaknesses
I am not fully convinced by the empirical results, primarily due to insufficient details about the baselines. The following comments are in decreasing order of importance.
- The proposed model has two main components: a sentence embedding and a substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baselines are TreeRNN and DCNN. These were originally designed for sentence embedding, but one can also extract node/substructure embeddings from them. It is unclear how these models are used to compute the two parts in your setup.
- The model uses two RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. The only stated difference in the knowledge-guided RNN is the additional “knowledge” vector from the memory in the RNN input (Eqns. 5 and 8). Having separate weights for the two RNNs seems unnecessary; the main effect appears to be increased capacity (i.e., more parameters). Furthermore, what are the hyperparameters and sizes of the baseline neural networks? The baselines should have comparable numbers of parameters.
- It would be reasonable to include a baseline that simply feeds additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as each word’s head, NER results, etc.
- Please comment on and, if possible, report results regarding the model’s sensitivity to parser errors.

Comments on the model
- After computing the substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute an attention distribution over them at each word. Is there a reason for using a static attention shared by all words? As implemented, the “knowledge” seems to act more like a filter to mark important words. This further motivates including the suggested feature-based baseline.
- Since the weight on a word is computed by the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and both embeddings are produced by the same RNN/CNN, doesn’t this imply that nodes/phrases most similar to the whole sentence receive higher weights—potentially favoring all leaf nodes?
- Although the paper claims the model generalizes to different types of knowledge, it appears that substructures must be representable as sequences of words. For example, it does not seem straightforward to incorporate constituent parses in this framework.
- I hesitate to call this “knowledge.” The term is potentially misleading, as it usually refers to world or external knowledge (e.g., a knowledge base of entities), whereas here it is primarily syntax—or arguably semantics if AMR parsing is used.

General Discussion
This paper presents a practical model that performs well on one dataset, but the core ideas are not very novel (see Strengths). For an ACL paper, there should be clearer takeaways. More importantly, the experiments are not convincing in their current presentation. Additional clarification is needed to better assess the results.

Post-rebuttal
The authors did not address my main concern: whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) are used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another major concern is the use of two separate RNNs, which gives the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. Accordingly, I am not changing my scores.
