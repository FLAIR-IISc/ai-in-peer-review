This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic information within a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot filling in the natural language understanding component of conversational systems. Substructures (e.g., nodes in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The main contribution is a straightforward way to “flatten” structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then connected to the tagger as auxiliary knowledge. The idea resembles structured/syntax-based attention (i.e., attention over tree nodes from a treeLSTM). Related work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder is similar to DCNN (Ma et al.): each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. While the overall architecture is not entirely novel, I appreciate the simple, practical approach relative to prior work.

-----Weaknesses-----
I am not fully convinced by the empirical results, primarily due to insufficient details about the baselines. Comments below are listed in decreasing order of importance.
- The proposed model comprises two main components: a sentence embedding and a substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baselines are TreeRNN and DCNN; although these are originally used for sentence embeddings, one can also obtain node/substructure embeddings from them. It is unclear how they are adapted to compute both components in this comparison.
- The model uses two RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. The only difference in the knowledge-guided RNN is the inclusion of a “knowledge” vector from the memory in the RNN input (Eqs. 5 and 8). It seems unnecessary to maintain separate weights for these two RNNs; the main effect is simply increased model capacity (i.e., more parameters). Moreover, what are the hyperparameters and sizes of the baseline neural networks? The baselines should have comparable numbers of parameters.
- It would be reasonable to include a baseline that directly feeds additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as the head of each word, NER outputs, etc.
- Are there any comments or results on the model’s sensitivity to parser errors?

Comments on the model:
- After computing substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute attention over them for each word. Is there a reason to use a single static attention for all words? As implemented, the “knowledge” appears to function more like a filter that flags important words. This further motivates including the feature-based baseline suggested above.
- Because the weight on a word is computed via the inner product between the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and both embeddings are produced by the same RNN/CNN, doesn’t this bias the model to assign higher weights to nodes/phrases similar to the whole sentence, i.e., potentially all leaf nodes?
- The paper claims the model generalizes to different kinds of knowledge, but the substructures must be representable as sequences of words. For example, it does not seem straightforward to incorporate constituent parses here.

Finally, I am hesitant to call this “knowledge.” The term is potentially misleading, as it typically refers to world/external knowledge (e.g., entity knowledge bases), whereas here it is primarily syntax, or perhaps semantics if AMR parsing is used.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper proposes a practical model that appears to perform well on one dataset, but the central ideas are not very novel (see Strengths). For an ACL paper, there should be more substantial takeaways. More importantly, the experimental section as presented is not convincing; additional clarifications are needed to properly assess the results.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors did not address my main concern: whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) are used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another major concern is the use of two separate RNNs, which gives the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. Therefore, I am not changing my scores.
