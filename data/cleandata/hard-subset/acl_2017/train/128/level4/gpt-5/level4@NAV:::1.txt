Review- This paper presents a neural architecture that encodes structural linguistic information into a memory network for sequence tagging, specifically slot filling in the NLU component of conversational systems. Each substructure (e.g., a parse-tree node) is represented as a vector (a memory slot), and at each time step a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is provided to an RNN as additional context for labeling.
-----Strengths-----
The main contribution is a simple mechanism to “flatten” structured information into an array of vectors (the memory) that is then connected to the tagger as auxiliary knowledge. This is reminiscent of structured/syntax-based attention (i.e., attention over treeLSTM nodes); related work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The substructure encoder is akin to DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. While the architecture is not entirely novel, I find the straightforward and practical formulation appealing relative to prior work.
-----Weaknesses-----
I am not fully persuaded by the empirical results, primarily due to insufficient details about the baselines. Points below are ordered by decreasing importance.
- The model consists of two components: a sentence embedding and a substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baselines (TreeRNN and DCNN) are typically used for sentence embeddings, but one can also obtain node/substructure embeddings from them. It is unclear how they are used to compute both parts in this work.
- The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. The only difference in the knowledge-guided RNN is the inclusion of a “knowledge” vector from the memory in the input (Eqn 5 and 8). Using separate parameters for the two RNNs seems unnecessary; the sole benefit appears to be increased capacity (i.e., more parameters). Additionally, what are the hyperparameters/sizes of the baseline neural networks? Parameter counts should be comparable.
- It would be reasonable to include a baseline that simply feeds additional knowledge as features to the RNN, e.g., head word of each token, NER outputs, etc.
- Any analysis/results on the model’s sensitivity to parser errors?
Comments on the model:
- After computing substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute attention over them conditioned on each word. Why use a static attention shared across all words? As implemented, the “knowledge” behaves more like a global filter highlighting important words, which motivates including the feature-based baseline suggested above.
- Since the weight on a word is computed via the inner product between the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and both embeddings are produced by the same RNN/CNN, wouldn’t nodes/phrases most similar to the whole sentence receive higher weights, potentially favoring leaf nodes?
- The paper claims the model generalizes to different forms of knowledge, but the substructures must be representable as sequences of words; using constituent parses as knowledge does not seem straightforward.
Finally, I hesitate to call this “knowledge.” The term usually denotes world/external knowledge (e.g., entity KBs), whereas here it is essentially syntax, or semantics if AMR parsing is used.
-----General Discussion-----
This is a practical model that appears effective on one dataset, but the core ideas are not very novel (see Strengths). For an ACL paper, there should be stronger takeaways. More importantly, the experiments are unconvincing as currently presented; clarifications are needed to better assess the results.
-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors did not address my primary concern: whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) are used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another major concern is the use of two separate RNNs, which increases the parameter count relative to the baselines. Consequently, I am not changing my scores.
