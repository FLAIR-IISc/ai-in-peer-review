This paper proposes a neural architecture that encodes structural linguistic information in a memory network for sequence tagging, specifically slot filling in conversational NLU. Substructures (e.g., parse-tree nodes) are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The main contribution is a simple way to “flatten” structured information into an array of vectors (the memory) that augments the tagger. The idea is related to structured/syntax-based attention (i.e., attention over tree-LSTM nodes); relevant prior work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder resembles DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. The architecture is not entirely novel, but the approach is simple and practical compared to prior work, which I appreciate.

-----Weaknesses-----
I am not convinced by the empirical results, primarily due to insufficient details about the baselines. Comments below are ordered by decreasing importance.
- The model has two main components: a sentence embedding and a substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baselines are TreeRNN and DCNN. Although these were originally used for sentence embedding, one can also extract node/substructure embeddings from them. It is unclear how you used these models to compute the two parts in your setup.
- The model uses two RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. The only difference in the knowledge-guided RNN is the addition of a “knowledge” vector from the memory to the RNN input (Eqns. 5 and 8). Having separate weights for the two RNNs seems unnecessary; it mainly increases capacity (i.e., more parameters). Also, what are the hyperparameters and sizes of the baseline neural networks? Parameter counts should be comparable across models.
- Please include a baseline that simply feeds additional knowledge as features to the RNN (e.g., each word’s head, NER results).
- Provide analysis or results on the model’s sensitivity to parser errors.

-----Comments on the Model-----
- After computing the substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute attention over them at each word. Why use a static attention for all words? As implemented, the “knowledge” appears to act more like a filter that marks important words, which further motivates the feature-based baseline suggested above.
- Since the weight on a word is computed by the inner product between the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and both embeddings are produced by the same RNN/CNN, doesn’t this imply that nodes/phrases similar to the whole sentence receive higher weights (e.g., all leaf nodes)?
- The paper claims the model generalizes to different kinds of knowledge, but the substructures must be represented as sequences of words. It does not seem straightforward to incorporate constituent parses in this framework.

Finally, I hesitate to call this “knowledge.” In NLP, this usually refers to world/external knowledge (e.g., knowledge bases), whereas here it is really syntax—or arguably semantics if AMR parsing is used.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper proposes a practical model that appears to work well on one dataset, but the core ideas are not very novel (see Strengths). For an ACL paper, there should be clearer takeaways. More importantly, the experimental evidence is unconvincing as currently presented; additional clarification is needed to properly evaluate the results.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors did not address my main concern: whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. A second major concern remains the use of two separate RNNs, which gives the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. I am therefore not changing my scores.
