- Proposes a memory-network architecture that encodes substructures (e.g., parse tree nodes) as vectors and feeds a weighted sum into an RNN for sequence tagging/slot filling.
- Encodes substructures by embedding sequences of ancestor words, resembling DCNN-style node encoders.
- Main contribution is a simple, practical method to “flatten” structured information into an array of vectors for auxiliary input to a tagger.
- Idea resembles structured/syntax-based attention and relates to prior work (Zhao et al., Liu et al., Eriguchi et al.).
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about how baselines were implemented and adapted.
- Unclear whether baselines like TreeRNN and DCNN were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and joint tagger.
- The model uses two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided), which may be unnecessary and simply increase parameter count versus baselines.
- Baselines should be matched in parameter count and hyperparameters; these details are not reported.
- A straightforward baseline feeding extra knowledge as features (e.g., head word, NER) is missing and should be included.
- No analysis of the model’s sensitivity to parser errors is provided.
- The model uses a single static attention over substructures for all words rather than a per-word attention, which is not justified.
- Computing attention by inner product of sentence and substructure embeddings may bias weights toward nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).
- Claiming generality to different kinds of “knowledge” is questionable because substructures must be representable as word sequences; constituent parses may not fit.
- The term “knowledge” is misleading here, as the paper mainly uses syntax/semantic structures rather than external/world knowledge.
- Overall: practical and performs on one dataset but lacks sufficient novelty and convincing experiments for a top-tier venue.
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not address main concerns about baseline usage and the extra parameters from two RNNs, so the reviewer’s score remains unchanged.
