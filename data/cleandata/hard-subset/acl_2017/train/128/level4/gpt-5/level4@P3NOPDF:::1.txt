Summary
This paper proposes a neural architecture for sequence tagging (slot filling in conversational NLU) that incorporates structural linguistic information via a memory network. Substructures (e.g., parse tree nodes) are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed to the tagger RNN at each time step as additional context. The substructure encoder resembles DCNN-style encodings (e.g., derived from sequences of ancestor words). The model also includes a sentence encoder and uses static attention over the substructures to form a “knowledge” vector.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: the paper offers a straightforward way to “flatten” structured information into an array of vectors that can be plugged into a tagger.
- Clear relation to structured/syntax-aware attention: the design resembles attention over tree nodes (e.g., tree-LSTM variants), connecting to prior work such as Zhao et al. (textual entailment), Liu et al. (NLI), and Eriguchi et al. (MT).
- Substructure encoding is intuitive and implementable (akin to DCNN; Ma et al.), and the overall architecture is easy to adapt to existing taggers.
- While not entirely novel, the approach is a sensible, practical alternative to heavier structured models.

Weaknesses and questions
The empirical evaluation is not convincing due to missing details and potentially unfair comparisons. Main concerns, in decreasing importance:
- Use of baselines and component mapping: The proposed model has two main parts—sentence embedding and substructure embedding. In Table 1, TreeRNN and DCNN are listed as baselines; both can produce node-level/substructure embeddings as well as sentence embeddings. It is unclear how these models are used to compute the two parts in your comparisons. Are their node embeddings used as memory slots? Are sentence and substructure encoders trained independently of the tagger or jointly? Please clarify the training setup and interfaces for each baseline.
- Two separate RNNs: The model uses a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN whose only difference is the inclusion of the “knowledge” vector in the inputs (Eqns. 5 and 8). Maintaining separate parameter sets for these two RNNs seems unnecessary and likely inflates capacity rather than isolating the contribution of structural knowledge. Please justify this design and provide ablations:
  - One-RNN variant (shared parameters) with/without the knowledge vector.
  - Parameter-matched baselines to ensure comparable capacity across models.
  Also report all hyperparameters and parameter counts for the proposed model and baselines.
- Missing feature-injection baseline: A strong and simple baseline is to feed readily available syntactic/semantic features directly to the RNN (e.g., head word, NER tags, dependency labels). This would test whether explicit features suffice without memory machinery.
- Sensitivity to parsing errors: How robust is performance to noisy parses? Please provide results with gold vs. automatic parses (if available) or a controlled noise analysis.
- Static vs. token-specific attention: After computing substructure embeddings, it is natural to attend over them conditioned on each token’s context. Why is the attention static for the entire sentence? As implemented, the “knowledge” vector appears to act as a global filter, which suggests that a simple feature baseline (above) could perform similarly. Please consider a token-conditioned attention variant and/or provide justification and empirical comparison.
- Similarity-driven weights: Since weights are computed via inner products between the sentence embedding and substructure embeddings, and both are produced by the same encoder family (RNN/CNN), won’t substructures that resemble the overall sentence (often shallow/leaf nodes or frequent phrases) be overweighted? Some analysis is needed to verify what is being attended to.
- Generality of “knowledge”: The claim that the model generalizes to different forms of knowledge seems overstated if substructures must be represented as word sequences. For example, using constituency parses directly may not be straightforward without linearization. Please clarify what classes of structure are truly supported and whether nonsequential structures require ad hoc conversions.
- Terminology: Referring to syntax-derived signals as “knowledge” is potentially misleading, as “knowledge” often denotes external/world knowledge (e.g., KB facts). Consider using “structural context” or “syntactic/semantic signals” to avoid confusion.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper
- Provide a clear, reproducible description of each baseline:
  - How TreeRNN/DCNN are used to obtain sentence vs. substructure embeddings.
  - Whether encoders are trained jointly with the tagger or frozen/pretrained.
  - Exact architectures, hyperparameters, and training regimes.
- Ensure parameter-count parity across all comparisons; report parameters and training time.
- Add the feature-injection baseline and a one-RNN variant.
- Include ablations for:
  - Static vs. token-conditioned attention over substructures.
  - Different substructure encoders (e.g., DCNN vs. alternatives).
- Analyze sensitivity to parser errors and provide qualitative attention visualizations to show what substructures are emphasized.
- Broaden evaluation beyond a single dataset or provide deeper analysis (e.g., per-slot F1, rare slot performance, error typology) to deliver clearer takeaways.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a practical and easy-to-implement method for incorporating structural information into sequence tagging. However, the novelty is modest relative to prior structured-attention and syntax-aware models, and the current experimental setup does not convincingly isolate the contribution of the proposed memory mechanism. With clearer baseline definitions, capacity-controlled comparisons, stronger ablations, and robustness analysis, the contribution would be much stronger.

Post-rebuttal
The rebuttal did not address my main concern: whether baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) are used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence encoder and the joint tagger. Additionally, the choice to use two separate RNNs appears to grant the proposed model a parameter advantage over baselines without justification or capacity matching. As these issues remain unresolved, I am not changing my scores.
