- Proposal: K-SAN extracts linguistic substructures, encodes them as memory vectors, attends over them to form a sentence representation, and injects it into an RNN tagger.

- Contribution 1: A simple end-to-end method to flatten structured analyses into substructure embeddings and attend over them for sequence tagging.

- Contribution 2: Empirical results on ATIS showing competitive/state-of-the-art F1, especially in low-resource settings, and robustness across dependency/AMR sources.

- Contribution 3: Qualitative attention visualizations indicating the model focuses on salient substructures even with parser noise.

- Strength: Practical, modular, and parallelizable architecture compatible with standard RNN taggers and multiple upstream analyses.

- Strength: Consistent and statistically significant gains on ATIS, notably in Small/Medium splits, indicating better generalization with limited data.

- Strength: Robustness to different knowledge sources—similar F1 across Stanford/SyntaxNet and AMR variants despite differing substructure counts.

- Strength: Attention visualizations provide intuitive explanations and suggest tolerance to noisy parses.

- Weakness: Baseline fairness unclear—Tree-RNN/DCNN adaptation and whether baselines match K-SAN’s two-RNN capacity are unspecified; parameter counts and hyperparameters missing.

- Weakness: Limited architectural novelty—approach closely resembles prior structured attention/memory and dependency-based encoders; positioning vs related work is weak.

- Weakness: Global sentence-level attention only; no token-wise attention tested, which may be more appropriate for sequence tagging.

- Weakness: Potential attention bias—inner-product scoring with shared encoders may favor globally similar or frequent/long substructures rather than token-aligned ones.

- Weakness: Sensitivity to knowledge quality not systematically quantified—no gold vs automatic parse comparisons, noise experiments, or stratified analyses.

- Weakness: “Knowledge” term is loose—method relies on linearized paths and often ignores relation labels, limiting scope beyond path-like structures.

- Question: Provide exact parameter counts and hyperparameters for all baselines and K-SAN variants; compare single-RNN+o vs joint two-RNN under matched capacity.

- Question: How were Tree-RNN and DCNN adapted—did they produce node/substructure embeddings and were they trained jointly with the tagger?

- Question: How does a parameter-matched chain RNN augmented with simple syntactic/semantic features perform?

- Question: Have you tried token-wise attention (p_i(t)) conditioning on current hidden state/token; how do accuracy, speed, and stability compare?

- Question: Can you report robustness vs parse/AMR quality (gold vs auto) or under controlled noise, and correlations with substructure count, sentence length, or parser confidence?

- Question: How would the method handle constituents or non-path graph fragments—do you require linearization and what maximum substructure length is used?

- Additional: Provide runtime and memory benchmarks including parsing/AMR cost to support efficiency/parallelizability claims.

- Additional: Clarify whether Min and Mkg are weight-tied and report effects of untied variants.

- Additional: Provide statistical testing details (number of runs, variance) for reported significant gains.

- Post-rebuttal: Main concerns about baseline parity and parameter control remain unresolved; lack of parameter-matched comparisons and feature-augmentation baseline prevents score change.
