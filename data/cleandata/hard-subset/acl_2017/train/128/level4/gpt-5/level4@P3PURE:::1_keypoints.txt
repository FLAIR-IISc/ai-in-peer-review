Paper proposes encoding parse substructures as memory vectors and feeding their weighted sum into an RNN for slot-filling sequence tagging.

Main contribution: a simple mechanism to “flatten” structured information into memory slots, related to structured/syntax-based attention.

Substructure encoder resembles DCNN, embedding nodes via ancestor-word sequences.

Approach is simple and practical but not highly novel.

Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about baselines.

Unclear how TreeRNN and DCNN baselines are used to compute sentence versus substructure embeddings in the experimental setup.

Using two separate RNNs with distinct weights may simply increase parameter count; baseline model sizes and hyperparameters are not reported for fair comparison.

Should include a baseline that feeds additional knowledge as features to the RNN (e.g., heads, NER).

Require analysis/reporting of model sensitivity to parser errors.

Why use a static attention over substructures shared by all words instead of per-word attention?

Inner-product weighting between sentence and substructure embeddings may bias towards nodes similar to the whole sentence, potentially favoring leaf nodes.

Model appears limited to substructures representable as word sequences, making incorporation of other structures (e.g., constituent parses) nontrivial.

Referring to these syntactic/semantic encodings as “knowledge” is misleading compared to external/world knowledge.

Paper performs well on one dataset but lacks clear takeaways and convincing experiments for an ACL venue.

Post-rebuttal: authors did not address key concerns about baseline usage and the extra RNN parameters; reviewer unchanged in scoring.
