Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The model extracts substructures from external linguistic analyses (e.g., dependency paths, AMR edges), encodes them as vectors in a “memory,” computes a sentence-level attention distribution over substructures, and injects the resulting knowledge-guided representation into an RNN tagger. As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, end-to-end mechanism to “flatten” structured analyses into substructure embeddings and attend over them to form a sentence representation for sequence tagging.
2) An empirical demonstration on ATIS that the approach yields competitive (often state-of-the-art) performance, especially in low-resource settings, and is reasonably robust across different knowledge sources (dependency, AMR).
3) Qualitative analyses (attention visualizations) suggesting the model can focus on salient substructures even with parser noise.

Strengths
- Practical, modular architecture. Encoding parse/AMR-derived substructures as memory slots and attending over them to form a sentence-level vector is straightforward to implement and integrate with standard RNN taggers. Compared to prior tree-structured encoders, this design is simple, parallelizable over substructures, and compatible with different upstream analyses.

- Strong results on ATIS, particularly in low-resource regimes. The K-SAN variants consistently improve over neural baselines (RNN, CNN-encoder + RNN tagger) and are reported as statistically significant in several settings. The gains in the Small/Medium splits indicate better generalization with limited data, aligning with the paper’s motivation.

- Knowledge source robustness. Using multiple parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and AMR (rule-based, JAMR), K-SAN achieves similar F1 scores across sources, despite differing numbers of extracted substructures. This supports the claim that the approach is not tied to one formalism and can leverage both syntactic and semantic structures.

- Insightful qualitative analysis. Visualizations show attention concentrating on origin, destination, and time expressions and their linking prepositions, even when dependency relations are partially incorrect. This offers an intuitive explanation for the model’s behavior and suggests some tolerance to noisy structure.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and clarity of baselines. It is unclear how Tree-RNN and DCNN were adapted for slot tagging: did those models provide both sentence and node/substructure embeddings to the tagger (to match K-SAN’s two-part design)? The proposed tagger uses two RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided), which increases capacity; no parameter-matched comparisons are reported. Hyperparameters and parameter counts for all baselines should be specified, and ablations controlling for capacity are necessary (e.g., single RNN with o vs joint model; matched hidden sizes).

- Limited architectural novelty relative to prior structured attention/memory work. The core idea—attending over structured units to form a representation for downstream prediction—has close precedents (e.g., syntax/structure-aware attention for NLI/entailment and tree-based encoders). The substructure encoder resembles dependency-based CNNs (Ma et al.). The contribution is mainly a practical recombination tailored to slot filling; a clearer positioning against prior structured attention and tree/memory architectures (e.g., Zhao et al., Liu et al., Eriguchi et al.) would strengthen the paper.

- Global (sentence-level) attention only. The model computes a single attention distribution over substructures and injects the same vector o at every time step. For sequence tagging, per-token attention is natural and could align substructures differently for different tags. The paper does not justify the static design nor compare against a token-wise attention variant. As designed, o acts more like a sentence-level filter/summary than token-conditioned guidance.

- Potential biases in the attention scoring. Attention is computed via inner products between u (sentence embedding) and mi (substructure embeddings), with shared encoders for both. This can favor substructures that are globally similar to the entire sentence (e.g., frequent or long leaf paths), not necessarily those aligned to the current token/tag decision. An analysis of attention distributions (e.g., vs. substructure length/frequency) or a token-conditioned attention ablation would clarify this.

- Sensitivity to knowledge quality not quantified. While the paper shows examples of robustness to parser errors, there is no systematic study. Comparisons with gold vs automatic parses (where feasible), perturbation/noise experiments, or stratification by parser confidence/sentence length would substantiate claims about error tolerance.

- Scope of “knowledge” and generalization claims. The method requires linearized substructures (paths) and ignores relation labels in most experiments, which may limit applicability to non-path structures (e.g., constituents) or richer graph relations. The term “knowledge” is used loosely; most experiments leverage syntactic/AMR analyses rather than external/world knowledge. This is more “structure-guided” than “knowledge-guided.”

Questions to authors
- Baseline parity and capacity control: What are the exact parameter counts and hyperparameters for all baselines and K-SAN variants? How does a single-RNN variant that injects o compare to the joint two-RNN tagger under matched capacity?

- Adaptation of structural baselines: How were Tree-RNN and DCNN used—only to produce sentence embeddings, or also node/substructure embeddings? Were their encoders trained jointly with the tagger? Please specify the pipeline.

- Feature-augmentation baseline: How does a strong chain RNN that receives simple syntactic/semantic features (e.g., head index/word, dependency label, NER) at each token perform when parameter-matched?

- Token-wise attention: Have you tried computing attention per time step p_i(t), conditioning on the current hidden state or token? If so, how does it compare in accuracy, speed, and stability?

- Robustness to structure quality: Can you report results vs. parse/AMR quality (gold vs auto if available) or under controlled noise? Any correlation with number of substructures, sentence length, or parser confidence?

- Generalization to other structures: How would you handle constituents or non-path graph fragments? Do you require linearization, and what is the maximum substructure length used?

Additional comments
- The efficiency/parallelizability claim would benefit from runtime and memory benchmarks vs baselines, including the cost of parsing/AMR.
- Please clarify whether Min and Mkg are always weight-tied and report the effect of untied variants.
- Statistical testing details (number of runs, variance) should be provided for all significant gains.

Post-rebuttal
The rebuttal did not resolve my main concerns about baseline parity and parameter control, particularly whether structural baselines provide substructure embeddings comparable to K-SAN and whether the two-RNN design inflates capacity relative to baselines. In the absence of parameter-matched comparisons and a feature-augmentation baseline, I am not changing my scores.
