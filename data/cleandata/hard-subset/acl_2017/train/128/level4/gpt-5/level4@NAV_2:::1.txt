This paper presents a neural architecture that encodes structured linguistic information into a memory-like component for sequence labeling, focusing on slot filling in NLU for dialog systems. Parse-derived substructures are embedded as vectors (memory slots), and an attention-weighted sum over these substructure embeddings is injected into an RNN at each time step as auxiliary context for tagging.

-----Strengths-----
The primary contribution is a straightforward mechanism to “linearize” structured signals into a bank of vectors (the memory) that can be plugged into a tagger as auxiliary knowledge. The approach resembles syntax-aware or structure-based attention (e.g., attention over tree nodes as in treeLSTM-style models); related lines include Zhao et al. for textual entailment, Liu et al. for NLI, and Eriguchi et al. for MT. The proposed substructure encoder is akin to DCNN (Ma et al.): each node is represented from a sequence of ancestor words. While the architecture is not fundamentally novel, the simplicity and practicality compared to prior structured attention methods are appealing.

-----Weaknesses-----
I am unconvinced by the empirical evidence, largely due to insufficient detail about baselines. Points below are ordered by decreasing importance.
- The model comprises two components: a sentence encoder and a substructure encoder. In Table 1, TreeRNN and DCNN are used as baselines; although they are designed for sentence encoding, one can also derive node/substructure embeddings from them. It is unclear how these baselines were adapted to compute both sentence and substructure representations in a comparable manner.
- The method employs two RNNs: a plain chain-based tagger and a knowledge-guided tagger. In the knowledge-guided variant, the only change is adding a “knowledge” vector to the RNN input (cf. Eqs. (5) vs. (8)). Having separate parameter sets for the two RNNs appears unnecessary and primarily increases model capacity (i.e., parameter count). Were the baseline neural models matched in size/parameters? Please report hyperparameters and parameter counts to ensure a fair comparison.
- A reasonable additional baseline would feed the same “knowledge” as simple features into a single RNN (e.g., dependency head of each token, NER tags), to test whether explicit features suffice without a memory/attention module.
- Please discuss and, if possible, quantify robustness to parsing errors.

Comments on the model:
- Given substructure embeddings, it is natural to compute attention per token rather than a single sentence-level attention shared across all time steps. With a global attention, the “knowledge” behaves more like a static filter highlighting salient parts; this strengthens the case for the feature-based baseline suggested above.
- Because attention weights are an inner product between the sentence embedding and substructure embeddings, and both are produced by the same encoder (RNN/CNN), won’t phrases most similar to the full-sentence representation tend to receive higher weights (potentially biasing toward many leaf nodes)?
- The paper claims knowledge generalization, but the method requires substructures to be encoded as word sequences; using constituent parses (or other non-sequential structures) is not obviously straightforward here.

Finally, calling this component “knowledge” is potentially misleading: it is primarily syntactic (or semantic, with AMR) structure rather than external/world knowledge as in KBs.

-----General Discussion-----
This is a practical model that performs well on a single benchmark, but the core ideas have limited novelty (see Strengths). For a top-tier venue, clearer takeaways and more rigorous comparisons are needed. As presented, the experimental evaluation is not fully convincing; further clarification is required to properly assess the contribution.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The rebuttal did not resolve the key concern about whether baselines like TreeRNN were used to obtain substructure embeddings independently of the sentence encoder and the joint tagger. A second major concern remains: employing two separate RNNs increases parameter count relative to baselines. Consequently, I am not adjusting my scores.
