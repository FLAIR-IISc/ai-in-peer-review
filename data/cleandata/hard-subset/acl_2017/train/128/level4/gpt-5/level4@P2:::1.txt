Summary and contributions
This paper presents a neural architecture for slot filling that represents linguistic structure as a set of “memory” vectors and integrates them with a sequence tagger via attention. For each utterance, substructures (e.g., paths in a dependency tree) are embedded and stored; a weighted sum of these embeddings is added to an RNN tagger as additional context. A joint tagger combines a standard (chain) RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
- A simple, practical abstraction that flattens structured linguistic information into a vector memory and connects it to a tagger via attention.
- A joint tagging architecture that fuses chain-based and knowledge-augmented representations.
- An empirical study on ATIS and a claim of generalization to different structure sources (dependency and AMR).

While the overall design is reminiscent of structured/syntax-based attention (e.g., attention over tree nodes or paths; cf. work in NLI, entailment, and MT), the proposal is clean and task-focused.

Strengths
- Simplicity and practicality: The idea of turning substructures into memory slots and attending to them is straightforward to implement and easy to plug into standard taggers. This makes the approach accessible and extensible.
- Structured attention formulation: The model offers a clear mechanism to attend over linguistically motivated substructures rather than over tokens alone, aligning with recent trends in syntax-aware attention while avoiding heavy tree machinery.
- Modular encoders and knowledge sources: The framework supports different substructure/sentence encoders (NN/CNN/RNN) and multiple knowledge sources (dependency and AMR), which increases applicability.
- Empirical results and interpretability: On ATIS, the model reports improvements and provides attention visualizations that highlight salient substructures (origin, destination, time), which are intuitively aligned with slot prediction.

Weaknesses
- Baseline clarity and fairness: The paper compares to TreeRNN and DCNN mainly as sentence encoders. However, both architectures can also produce node/substructure embeddings, making them natural structural baselines for the substructure component. It is unclear how (or whether) such node-level representations were used in baselines, and how the two parts (sentence vs. substructure embedding) were matched to ensure a fair comparison.
- Parameter count and dual-RNN design: The joint tagger uses two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided). The knowledge-guided RNN differs by only an added knowledge vector at the input. Maintaining separate weights likely increases capacity rather than isolating the contribution of knowledge. Without reporting parameter counts and matching baseline capacities, improvements are hard to attribute.
- Missing feature-augmentation baseline: A strong and relevant baseline is to feed simple structural features directly into a single RNN (e.g., dependency head, NER tags, or other parser-derived features). This would test whether the gains come from the attention/memory mechanism versus feature enrichment.
- Static (sentence-level) attention: The model computes a single attention distribution over substructures for the entire sentence, then injects this into every time step. A more natural alternative is token-level attention that can vary with position. An ablation comparing static vs. per-token attention is needed to justify the design and to rule out that the current mechanism acts mainly as a global filter of salient words.
- Sensitivity to parser errors and structural form: The approach relies on parsed substructures serialized as word sequences. The paper does not quantify robustness to noisy parses or demonstrate applicability to structures that are not easily linearized (e.g., constituents, richer graph fragments). This weakens the claim of broad generalization to “different knowledge.”

Questions to authors
- How were TreeRNN and DCNN instantiated for fair comparison? Did you extract node/substructure embeddings from these models and use them analogously to your memory slots? If not, why not?
- What are the exact parameter counts (or at least ranges) for all compared models, including the joint two-RNN tagger and baselines? Are baselines matched in capacity?
- What happens if you use a single RNN that concatenates the knowledge vector to the input (i.e., no separate knowledge-guided RNN)? Please report results.
- Can you provide results for a feature-augmentation baseline (e.g., adding dependency head indices/embeddings, NER, or other parser-derived features) to a standard RNN/CNN tagger?
- Did you try per-token attention over substructures (attention conditioned on ht or wt at time t)? If so, how does it compare to sentence-level attention?
- How sensitive is performance to parsing errors? Please include analyses where you perturb parses or compare across parsers with different error profiles.
- Can your method handle constituent trees or graph fragments that are not easily expressed as word sequences? If so, how are substructures encoded?

General discussion
The paper proposes a neat and usable formulation of structure-aware attention for slot filling and shows promising results on ATIS. However, the novelty is limited relative to existing structured attention and tree/path-based encoding work, and the experimental section lacks the baselines and capacity controls needed to convincingly isolate the benefit of the proposed memory/attention mechanism. With clearer baseline construction, matched parameterization, and ablations (per-token attention, feature-augmented RNN), the contribution would be significantly stronger.

Post-rebuttal
The rebuttal did not address my main concern: whether structural baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and combined fairly with the tagger. The use of two separate RNNs also remains a confound due to increased parameters relative to baselines. I am therefore not changing my scores.
