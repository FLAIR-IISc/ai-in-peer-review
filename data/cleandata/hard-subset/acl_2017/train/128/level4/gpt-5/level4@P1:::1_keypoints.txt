Paper proposes K-SAN: attend over encoded linguistic substructures stored in memory to produce a knowledge vector fed into an RNN tagger, plus a joint chain + knowledge-guided RNN for slot filling.

Contributions claimed: simple mechanism to flatten structured info into substructure embeddings integrated via attention into a tagger.

Contributions claimed: instantiation across knowledge sources (dependency parses, AMR) with evaluation on ATIS.

Contributions claimed: empirical gains, especially in low-resource settings.

Strength: simple, practical design that cleanly injects structure via memory+attention.

Strength: sensible substructure encoder using ancestor-word sequences (similar to DCNN).

Strength: empirical promise on ATIS with interpretable attention aligning to salient slots.

Weakness: unclear how baselines (TreeRNN, DCNN) are used to produce sentence vs. substructure embeddings, making comparisons hard.

Weakness: two-RNN design may only increase capacity; parameter counts/hyperparameters not reported and capacity-matched comparisons missing.

Weakness: missing feature-augmentation baseline that directly feeds “knowledge” features into RNNs to isolate attention/memory benefit.

Weakness: attention is static at sentence-level rather than token-level, limiting per-token focus and possibly weakening model.

Weakness: attention scoring (inner product between similarly produced embeddings) may bias toward substructures similar to the sentence embedding (e.g., leaf nodes).

Weakness: no analysis of sensitivity to parser errors despite reliance on parsed substructures.

Weakness: generalization claims to different knowledge sources are weak because substructure representation requires linearized word sequences and may not cover many structures.

Weakness: terminology—calling syntax/AMR structures “knowledge” is potentially misleading versus external/world knowledge.

Question: how exactly are TreeRNN and DCNN used to compute sentence and substructure embeddings in baselines and in the joint tagger?

Question: what are parameter counts and hyperparameters for all neural models, and are results robust under capacity-matched settings?

Question: how does a feature-augmentation baseline (e.g., head word, dependency head, NER) compare to K-SAN?

Question: why use single sentence-level attention instead of token-level attention per time step, and were time-step attentions tried?

Question: is there observed bias toward leaf nodes or substructures highly similar to the sentence embedding—any quantitative/qualitative analysis?

Question: how sensitive is performance to parser errors—any stratified results or experiments with injected noise?

Question: how would non-sequential structures (e.g., constituent trees) be incorporated when they don’t linearize into ancestor-word sequences?

General: model is practical and performs on one dataset but core idea is not novel and needs stronger methodological clarity and more convincing, fair experiments.

Post-rebuttal: authors did not resolve whether baselines produce independent substructure embeddings or address parameter inflation from two RNNs, so reviewer’s concerns and scores remain unchanged.
