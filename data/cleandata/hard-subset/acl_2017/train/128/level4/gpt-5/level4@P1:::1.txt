Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for sequence tagging, specifically slot filling in task-oriented dialogue. The model encodes linguistic substructures (e.g., nodes/paths in a parse tree) as vectors stored in a memory. A sentence embedding attends to these substructure embeddings to produce a “knowledge” vector, which is fed into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain-based RNN with a knowledge-guided RNN.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, practical mechanism to flatten structured information into substructure embeddings and integrate them via attention into a sequence tagger.
2) An instantiation of this idea across different sources of “knowledge” (dependency parses and AMR), with empirical evaluation on ATIS.
3) Evidence that the approach can help in low-resource settings.

While the overall architecture is not entirely novel, the simplicity and practicality are appealing relative to prior syntax-aware neural models.

Strengths
- Simple and practical design: Encoding substructures as memory slots and attending to them is a clean way to inject structure into a tagger. The approach relates to syntax-based attention (e.g., attention over tree nodes as in treeLSTM variants) and is conceptually close to prior work (e.g., Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on NLI, Eriguchi et al. on MT), but is streamlined for slot filling.
- Sensible substructure encoder: Using ancestor-word sequences to represent nodes is similar to DCNN (Ma et al.), which is a reasonable and effective design choice.
- Empirical promise: The model appears to work well on ATIS, with larger gains under data scarcity, and the attention visualizations are interpretable and consistent with salient slots (origin, destination, time).

Weaknesses
My main concerns are empirical and methodological, largely due to missing details about baselines and capacity control. Ordered by decreasing importance:
1) Baselines and component usage are unclear. The proposed model has two components: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. In Table 1, TreeRNN and DCNN are listed as baselines; although they are typically used for sentence embeddings, they can also yield node/substructure embeddings. It is unclear how these models are used to compute (a) sentence embeddings and (b) substructure embeddings in your setup, and how those feed into the tagger. Without this, the comparison is hard to assess.
2) Parameter parity and the two-RNN design. The knowledge-guided RNN differs from the chain-based RNN only by the addition of the “knowledge” vector to the input (Eq. 5 vs. 8). Maintaining two separate RNNs seems unnecessary and primarily increases model capacity. Please report hyperparameters and parameter counts for all neural baselines and ensure capacity-matched comparisons. Otherwise, improvements may reflect extra parameters rather than the proposed mechanism.
3) Missing feature-augmentation baseline. A strong and reasonable baseline would feed “knowledge” as features directly into the RNN (e.g., head word, NER tags, dependency heads) without the attention/memory mechanism. This would clarify how much the memory/attention buys over straightforward feature augmentation.
4) Static, sentence-level attention. The model computes a single attention distribution over substructures for the whole sentence. It seems natural to compute token-level attention (per time step) so the tagger can attend to different substructures for different words. As implemented, the “knowledge” vector may act more like a global filter that highlights important words; this further motivates the feature-augmentation baseline above.
5) Attention bias and similarity. Because attention weights are based on an inner product between the sentence embedding and substructure embeddings, and these embeddings are produced by the same encoder (RNN/CNN), substructures similar to the sentence embedding may dominate. In practice, this could overweight common or leaf-level nodes. Some analysis would help.
6) Sensitivity to parser errors. Results and discussion about robustness to parsing mistakes are missing. Since the method relies on parsed substructures, its sensitivity to noisy structures should be quantified.
7) Generalization claims and representation constraints. Although the paper claims generalization to different knowledge sources, the current substructure representation appears to require linearized sequences of words. It is not obvious how to handle structures like constituent parses that are not naturally represented as ancestor-word sequences.
8) Terminology. Calling syntax/AMR-derived structures “knowledge” is potentially misleading; in NLP, “knowledge” often refers to world or KB knowledge. Here, the method primarily integrates linguistic structure (syntax/semantics), not external factual knowledge.

Questions to Authors
- How exactly are TreeRNN and DCNN used to compute sentence and substructure embeddings in your baselines? Are substructure embeddings extracted independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger?
- What are the parameter counts and hyperparameters for all neural models, including your joint two-RNN architecture and each baseline? Are results robust under capacity-matched settings?
- How does a feature-augmentation baseline (e.g., head word, dependency head, NER) compare to K-SAN?
- Why use a single, sentence-level attention rather than token-level attention per time step? Did you try time-step attention, and what were the results?
- Do you observe a bias toward leaf nodes or substructures highly similar to the sentence embedding? Any quantitative or qualitative analysis?
- How sensitive is performance to parser errors? Can you report results stratified by parse quality or with injected noise?
- How would you incorporate non-sequential structures (e.g., constituent trees) that do not linearize naturally into ancestor-word sequences?

General Discussion
This is a practical model that performs well on a single dataset, but the core ideas are not especially novel. As an ACL paper, I would expect stronger methodological clarity and more convincing experiments, particularly around fair baselines and capacity control. With clearer comparisons—especially capacity-matched baselines and a feature-augmentation control—the contribution would be easier to assess.

Post-rebuttal
The rebuttal did not address my main concern: whether baselines like TreeRNN are used to compute substructure embeddings independent of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. The use of two separate RNNs also remains problematic, as it likely increases parameters relative to baselines. I am therefore not changing my scores.
