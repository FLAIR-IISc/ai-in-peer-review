- Proposes a memory-network style neural architecture for sequence tagging that encodes parse substructures as vectors and feeds a weighted sum as extra context to the tagger RNN (static attention/“knowledge” vector).  
- Conceptually simple and practical: flattens structured information into plug-in vectors for taggers.  
- Clearly related to prior structured/syntax-aware attention models (e.g., tree-LSTM variants) and positions itself relative to that literature.  
- Substructure encoding is intuitive and implementable (DCNN-like) and the overall architecture is easy to adapt to existing taggers.  
- Approach is sensible and practical, though novelty is modest relative to heavier structured models.  
- Empirical evaluation is unconvincing due to missing details and potentially unfair comparisons.  
- Unclear how baselines (TreeRNN/DCNN) are used to produce sentence vs. node embeddings and whether encoders are trained jointly or independently.  
- Using two separate RNNs (chain RNN + knowledge-guided RNN) seems unnecessary and likely inflates capacity; needs justification and ablation (shared-params one-RNN variant).  
- Must provide parameter-matched baselines and report all hyperparameters and parameter counts.  
- Missing simple feature-injection baseline (e.g., head word, NER, dependency labels) to test whether explicit features suffice.  
- No analysis of sensitivity to parsing errors—need results with gold vs. automatic parses or controlled noise.  
- Static, sentence-level attention is questionable; token-conditioned attention should be considered and compared.  
- Similarity-driven weighting (inner products between sentence and substructure embeddings) may overweight shallow/leaf nodes—requires attention analysis.  
- Claim of generality to different forms of “knowledge” is overstated if substructures must be linearized word sequences; clarify supported structure classes.  
- Terminology issue: calling syntax-derived signals “knowledge” is misleading; suggest “structural context” or “syntactic/semantic signals.”  
- Suggest providing reproducible baseline descriptions: how TreeRNN/DCNN produce embeddings, joint vs. frozen training, exact architectures/hyperparams.  
- Recommend ensuring parameter-count parity and reporting training time for fair comparisons.  
- Add feature-injection and one-RNN baselines.  
- Include ablations for static vs. token-conditioned attention and different substructure encoders.  
- Provide sensitivity-to-parser-errors analysis and qualitative attention visualizations to show emphasized substructures.  
- Broaden evaluation beyond a single dataset or add deeper analyses (per-slot F1, rare-slot performance, error typology).  
- Overall: practical and easy-to-implement method, but modest novelty and insufficient experimental isolation of the memory mechanism’s contribution.  
- Post-rebuttal: main concerns (baseline usage and two-RNN parameter advantage) were not addressed, so reviewer did not change scores.
