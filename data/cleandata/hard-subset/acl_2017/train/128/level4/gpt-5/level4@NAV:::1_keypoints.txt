- Main contribution: a simple mechanism that flattens structured linguistic information into a memory of substructure vectors used as auxiliary context for sequence tagging.
- Approach is reminiscent of structured/syntax-based attention and related prior work (e.g., Zhao et al., Liu et al., Eriguchi et al.).
- The substructure encoder is similar to DCNN where each node is embedded from its ancestor word sequence.
- The formulation is practical and straightforward, though not highly novel.
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about how baselines are implemented and compared.
- Unclear whether baselines (TreeRNN, DCNN) were used to compute both sentence and substructure/node embeddings independently.
- Using two separate RNNs (chain and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessary and likely inflates parameter count; baseline hyperparameters and parameter parity are not reported.
- A simple baseline that feeds extra knowledge as features (e.g., head words, NER) is missing and should be included.
- No analysis of the model’s sensitivity to parser errors is provided.
- Why use a static attention over substructures shared across all words instead of attention conditioned per word?
- The attention scoring (inner product between sentence and substructure embeddings) may bias towards nodes similar to the whole sentence, potentially favoring leaf nodes.
- Claim of generalizing to different forms of “knowledge” is questionable because substructures must be representable as word sequences, limiting applicability (e.g., constituent parses or non-sequential knowledge).
- The term “knowledge” is misleading here; the model injects syntax/semantic structure rather than external/world knowledge.
- Overall: practical and somewhat effective on one dataset, but limited novelty and unconvincing experiments; clarifications are needed for stronger takeaways.
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not address primary concerns about baseline usage and the extra-RNN parameter increase, so reviewer’s score remains unchanged.
