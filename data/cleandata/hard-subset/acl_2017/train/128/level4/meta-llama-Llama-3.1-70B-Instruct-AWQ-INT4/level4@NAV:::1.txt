This paper introduces a neural network architecture that incorporates structural linguistic knowledge into a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling in natural language understanding units within conversation systems. It encodes substructures, such as nodes in a parse tree, as vectors (memory slots) and feeds a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings into a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) at each time step as additional context for labeling.

**Strengths**

The primary contribution of this paper lies in its straightforward approach to "flattening" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then connected to the tagger as additional knowledge. This concept is similar to structured or syntax-based attention, such as attention over nodes from treeLSTM, and is related to the work of Zhao et al on textual entailment, Liu et al on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al on machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder bears resemblance to DCNN (Ma et al), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. Although the architecture may not be entirely novel, its simplicity and practicality are notable compared to prior work.

**Weaknesses**

I remain unconvinced by the empirical results, primarily due to the lack of details regarding the baselines. The following comments are ranked in order of decreasing importance:

- The proposed model consists of two main components: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. However, it is unclear how the baseline models, TreeRNN and DCNN, are utilized to compute these two parts, as they were originally designed for sentence embedding but can also be used for node/substructure embedding.
- The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one. The only distinction between the two lies in the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory in the RNN input (Eqn 5 and 8). It seems unnecessary to have separate weights for the two RNNs, as the primary advantage of using two RNNs is an increase in model capacity, i.e., more parameters. Furthermore, the hyperparameters and size of the baseline neural networks should be comparable in terms of the number of parameters.
- It would be reasonable to include a baseline that inputs additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as the head of each word or NER results.
- Any discussion or results on the model's sensitivity to parser errors would be beneficial.

**Comments on the Model**

- After computing substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute attention over them at each word. Is there a reason to use a static attention for all words? It appears that the "knowledge" acts more like a filter to mark important words, making it reasonable to include the aforementioned baseline.
- Since the weight on a word is computed by the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and the two embeddings are computed by the same RNN/CNN, does this not imply that nodes/phrases similar to the whole sentence receive higher weights, i.e., all leaf nodes?
- The paper claims that the model generalizes to different knowledge, but it seems that the substructure must be represented as a sequence of words, making it less straightforward to use constituent parse as knowledge.
- Finally, I hesitate to refer to this as "knowledge," as it is misleading and typically refers to world/external knowledge, such as a knowledge base of entities, whereas this is really just syntax or arguably semantics if AMR parsing is used.

**General Discussion**

This paper proposes a practical model that appears to work well on one dataset, but the main ideas are not particularly novel (see comments in Strengths). As an ACL paper, there should be more takeaways. More importantly, the experiments are not convincing in their current presentation, and some clarification is necessary to better judge the results.

**Post-rebuttal**

The authors failed to address my primary concern, which is whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) are used to compute substructure embeddings independent of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another major concern is the use of two separate RNNs, which gives the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. Therefore, I am not changing my scores.
