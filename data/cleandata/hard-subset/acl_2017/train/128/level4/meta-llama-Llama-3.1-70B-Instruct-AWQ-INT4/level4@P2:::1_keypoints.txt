- Proposal: K-SAN — a neural architecture that encodes structural linguistic substructures into a memory and uses attention-weighted sums as context for sequence tagging (slot-filling).
- Main contributions claimed: end-to-end learning, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability.
- Strength: simple and practical method to "flatten" structured information into memory slots for the tagger.
- Strength: approach resembles prior structured/syntax-based attention work and uses a DCNN-like substructure encoder (embedding nodes from ancestor word sequences).
- Strength: overall architecture is practical even if not entirely novel.
- Weakness: empirical results unconvincing due to insufficient details about how baselines were implemented and configured.
- Weakness: unclear how TreeRNN and DCNN baselines were used to produce both sentence and substructure embeddings.
- Weakness: use of two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessary and may only increase parameter count.
- Weakness: baseline models’ hyperparameters and parameter counts are not reported for fair comparison.
- Weakness: missing simple baselines that input additional knowledge as features into a single RNN (e.g., head word, NER).
- Weakness: no analysis or results on sensitivity to parser errors.
- Question: clarify how TreeRNN/DCNN were applied to compute substructure embeddings independently of the joint tagger.
- Question: justify the design choice of two separate RNNs versus shared parameters given the extra capacity.
- Question: provide experiments or discussion on robustness to parser errors.
- Question: include a baseline that feeds extra knowledge as features to the RNN.
- Question: report hyperparameters and model sizes for baseline neural networks for fair comparison.
