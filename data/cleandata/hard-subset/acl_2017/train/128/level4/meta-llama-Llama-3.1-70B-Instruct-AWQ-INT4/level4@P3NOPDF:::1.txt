This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling in conversation systems. The architecture encodes substructures, such as nodes in a parse tree, as vectors (memory slots) and feeds a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings into a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) at each time step as additional context for labeling.

**Strengths**

The main contribution of this paper is a simple method for "flattening" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then connected to the tagger as additional knowledge. This idea is similar to structured or syntax-based attention, such as attention over nodes from treeLSTM. Related work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder is similar to DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. Although the architecture may not be entirely novel, its simplicity and practicality are appealing compared to prior work.

**Weaknesses**

I have several concerns regarding the empirical results, primarily due to the lack of details about the baselines. My comments are ranked in order of decreasing importance.

1. The proposed model consists of two main parts: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baseline models (TreeRNN and DCNN) are originally used for sentence embedding, but it is unclear how they are used to compute the two parts. Specifically, it is not clear whether the baselines are used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger.
2. The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one. The only difference between the two RNNs is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory in the RNN input (Eqn 5 and 8). It seems unnecessary to have separate weights for the two RNNs, as the only advantage is an increase in model capacity (i.e., more parameters). Furthermore, the hyperparameters and size of the baseline neural networks are not provided, making it difficult to compare their performance.
3. It would be reasonable to include a baseline that inputs additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as the head of each word or NER results.
4. The paper does not discuss the model's sensitivity to parser errors, which is an important consideration.

**Comments on the model**

1. After computing substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute attention over them at each word. However, the paper uses a static attention for all words, which may not be optimal. This approach makes the "knowledge" act more like a filter to mark important words, which could be achieved by including additional features as mentioned earlier.
2. The weight on a word is computed by the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, which are computed by the same RNN/CNN. This may result in nodes or phrases similar to the whole sentence receiving higher weights, effectively prioritizing all leaf nodes.
3. The paper claims that the model generalizes to different knowledge, but it is unclear how this would work for representations that are not sequences of words, such as constituent parse trees.
4. The term "knowledge" may be misleading, as it typically refers to external knowledge, such as a knowledge base of entities. In this paper, it refers to syntax or semantics, depending on the parsing method used.

**General Discussion**

This paper proposes a practical model that performs well on one dataset, but the main ideas are not particularly novel (see comments in Strengths). As an ACL paper, I expect more takeaways. More importantly, the experiments are not convincing in their current form, and clarification is needed to better judge the results.

**Post-rebuttal**

The authors did not address my main concerns, specifically whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) are used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another major concern is the use of two separate RNNs, which gives the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. Therefore, I am not changing my scores.
