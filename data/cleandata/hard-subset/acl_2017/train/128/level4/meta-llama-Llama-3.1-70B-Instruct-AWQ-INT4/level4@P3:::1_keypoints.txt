1. Proposes a memory-network architecture that encodes parse substructures as vectors and feeds a weighted sum into an RNN for sequence tagging.  
2. Main contribution: a simple method to flatten structured linguistic information into a memory array for the tagger.  
3. Main contribution: a practical approach to incorporate structural linguistic knowledge into sequence tagging.  
4. Strength: substructure encoder resembles DCNN, embedding nodes from ancestor-word sequences.  
5. Strength: overall architecture is simple and more practical than prior work.  
6. Weakness: details are missing on how baselines (TreeRNN, DCNN) compute sentence and substructure embeddings.  
7. Weakness: using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessary and inflates capacity.  
8. Weakness: missing baseline that supplies additional knowledge as features to a single RNN.  
9. Weakness: no analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors.  
10. Weakness: attention over substructures is static across words instead of computed per word.  
11. Weakness: weight computation via inner product of sentence and substructure embeddings may bias toward nodes similar to the whole sentence.  
12. Weakness: representing substructures as word sequences complicates using constituent parses as knowledge.  
13. Weakness: calling syntax/semantics "knowledge" is misleading compared to external/world knowledge.  
14. Question: please detail how baselines compute substructure embeddings independently of the joint tagger.  
15. Question: why use two RNNs and how are parameter counts matched to baselines?  
16. Question: please include a baseline that inputs extra knowledge as RNN features.  
17. Question: how does the model handle parser errors and what is their performance impact?  
18. Question: justify the choice of static attention over per-word attention on substructures.  
19. Question: how do you prevent nodes similar to the whole sentence from receiving disproportionately high weights?  
20. Question: clarify how substructures are represented as word sequences and implications for constituent parses.  
21. Question: justify using the term "knowledge" for syntax/semantics rather than external knowledge.
