This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling in conversation systems. The architecture encodes substructures, such as nodes in a parse tree, as vectors (memory slots) and uses a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings as additional context in a recurrent neural network (RNN) at each time step for labeling.

**Strengths**
The main contribution of this paper lies in its simple approach to "flattening" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then connected to the tagger as additional knowledge. This idea is similar to structured or syntax-based attention mechanisms, such as attention over nodes from treeLSTM, and is related to the work of Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder bears resemblance to DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. Although the architecture may not be entirely novel, its simplicity and practicality are appealing compared to prior work.

**Weaknesses**
The empirical results are not entirely convincing, primarily due to the lack of details regarding the baselines. The following comments are ranked in decreasing order of importance:

* The proposed model consists of two main components: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. However, it is unclear how the baseline models, TreeRNN and DCNN, are used to compute these two parts, as they were originally designed for sentence embedding but can also be used for node/substructure embedding.
* The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one. The only difference between them is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory in the RNN input (Eqn 5 and 8). It seems unnecessary to have separate weights for the two RNNs, as the only advantage of using two RNNs is an increase in model capacity, resulting in more parameters. Furthermore, the hyper-parameters and size of the baseline neural networks should be comparable in terms of the number of parameters.
* A reasonable baseline to include would be one that inputs additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as the head of each word or named entity recognition (NER) results.
* The paper does not provide any comments or results on the model's sensitivity to parser errors.

**Comments on the Model**
* After computing the substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute an attention mechanism over them at each word. Is there any reason to use a static attention mechanism for all words? The current approach appears to use the "knowledge" as a filter to mark important words, making it reasonable to include the aforementioned baseline that inputs additional features.
* Since the weight on a word is computed by the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and the two embeddings are computed by the same RNN/CNN, it seems that nodes or phrases similar to the whole sentence receive higher weights, potentially resulting in all leaf nodes being weighted equally.
* The paper claims that the model generalizes to different types of knowledge, but it appears that the substructure must be represented as a sequence of words, making it unclear how to use constituent parse as knowledge in this framework.
* Finally, the term "knowledge" might be misleading, as it typically refers to world or external knowledge, such as a knowledge base of entities, whereas in this context, it represents syntax or arguably semantics if abstract meaning representation (AMR) parsing is used.

**General Discussion**
This paper proposes a practical model that performs well on one dataset, but the main ideas are not particularly novel (see comments in Strengths). As an ACL paper, it would be beneficial to have more takeaways. More importantly, the experiments are not convincing in their current form, and clarification is needed to better judge the results.

**Post-Rebuttal**
The authors did not address my main concern, which is whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) are used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another major concern is the use of two separate RNNs, which gives the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. Therefore, I am not changing my scores.
