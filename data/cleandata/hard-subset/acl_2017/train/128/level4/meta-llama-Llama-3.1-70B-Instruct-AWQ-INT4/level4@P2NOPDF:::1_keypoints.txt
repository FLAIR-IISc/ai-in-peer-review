- Main contribution: a simple method that flattens structured linguistic information into a memory of substructure embeddings fed as additional context to an RNN tagger.  
- Similarity to prior work: related to structured/syntax-based attention and node embeddings from treeLSTM/DCNN; not highly novel but practical and simple.  
- Strength: the substructure encoder resembles prior DCNN approaches and is appealing in simplicity.  
- Major empirical concern: baselines (e.g., TreeRNN, DCNN) are not clearly described or fairly compared.  
- Concern about architecture: two separate RNNs differ only by an added “knowledge” input, which may just increase parameter count rather than provide a principled benefit.  
- Need for fair comparison: hyperparameters and model sizes of baselines should be comparable in number of parameters.  
- Suggested baseline: include a model that inputs extra features (e.g., head words, NER) to the RNN to test if gains come from added features.  
- Parser robustness: request for analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors.  
- Attention design question: why use static attention over substructures rather than computing dynamic attention per word?  
- Weighting concern: using inner product between sentence and substructure embeddings may bias weights toward nodes similar to the full sentence, possibly favoring leaf nodes.  
- Generalization claim questioned: substructures must be represented as word sequences, so generalizing to arbitrary knowledge (e.g., constituents) is less straightforward.  
- Terminology issue: calling syntax-derived substructures “knowledge” is misleading compared to external/world knowledge.  
- Experimental verdict: model seems practical and works on one dataset, but lacks novel takeaways and convincing experiments for ACL standards.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not address main concerns about baseline embedding usage and the extra RNN parameters; reviewer keeps original scores.
