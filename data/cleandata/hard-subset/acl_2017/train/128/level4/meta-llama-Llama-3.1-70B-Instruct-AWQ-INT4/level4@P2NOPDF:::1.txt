This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling in natural language understanding units of conversation systems. The architecture encodes substructures, such as nodes in a parse tree, as vectors (memory slots) and feeds a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings into a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) at each time step as additional context for labeling.

**Strengths**

The main contribution of this paper is a simple method to "flatten" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then connected to the tagger as additional knowledge. This idea is similar to structured or syntax-based attention, such as attention over nodes from treeLSTM, and is related to the work of Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder is similar to the Deep Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN) of Ma et al., where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. Although the architecture is not entirely novel, its simplicity and practicality are appealing compared to prior work.

**Weaknesses**

I am not convinced by the empirical results, primarily due to the lack of details on the baselines. My concerns, listed in decreasing importance, are as follows:

1. The proposed model has two main components: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. However, it is unclear how the baseline models, TreeRNN and DCNN, are used to compute these two parts. Originally designed for sentence embedding, these models can also be used to obtain node/substructure embeddings.
2. The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one. The only difference between them is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory in the RNN input (Eqn 5 and 8). I believe it is unnecessary to have separate weights for the two RNNs, as the only advantage is an increase in model capacity, resulting in more parameters. Furthermore, the hyper-parameters and size of the baseline neural networks should be comparable in terms of the number of parameters.
3. It would be reasonable to include a baseline that inputs additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as the head of each word or Named Entity Recognition (NER) results.
4. I would appreciate any comments or results on the model's sensitivity to parser errors.

**Comments on the Model**

1. After computing the substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute an attention over them at each word. Is there a reason to use a static attention for all words? It appears that the "knowledge" is acting more like a filter to mark important words, making it reasonable to include the baseline suggested above, which inputs additional features.
2. Since the weight on a word is computed by the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and the two embeddings are computed by the same RNN/CNN, does this mean that nodes/phrases similar to the whole sentence receive higher weights, effectively prioritizing all leaf nodes?
3. The paper claims that the model generalizes to different knowledge, but I believe that the substructure must be represented as a sequence of words, making it less straightforward to use constituent parse as knowledge.
4. I hesitate to call the proposed component "knowledge," as it is misleading. Typically, knowledge refers to world or external knowledge, such as a knowledge base of entities, whereas in this context, it is really just syntax or, arguably, semantics if Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR) parsing is used.

**General Discussion**

This paper proposes a practical model that seems to work well on one dataset, but the main ideas are not particularly novel (see comments in Strengths). As an ACL paper, I expect more takeaways. More importantly, the experiments are not convincing in their current form, and some clarification is needed to better judge the results.

**Post-Rebuttal**

The authors did not address my main concern, which is whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) are used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another major concern is the use of two separate RNNs, which gives the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. Therefore, I am not changing my scores.
