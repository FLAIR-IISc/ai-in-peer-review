This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling in the natural language understanding unit of conversation systems. Substructures, such as nodes in a parse tree, are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) at each time step as additional context for labeling.

**Strengths**

The main contribution of this paper is a simple method to "flatten" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then connected to the tagger as additional knowledge. This idea is similar to structured or syntax-based attention, such as attention over nodes from treeLSTM. Related work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder is similar to DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. Although the architecture does not appear entirely novel, its simple and practical approach is preferable to prior work.

**Weaknesses**

I am not convinced by the empirical results, primarily due to the lack of details about the baselines. The following comments are ranked by decreasing importance:

1. The proposed model consists of two main parts: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baseline models (TreeRNN and DCNN) are originally used for sentence embedding, but it is unclear how they are used to compute the two parts. Specifically, it is not clear whether the node/substructure embedding from these models is used independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger.
2. The model uses two RNNs: a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one. The only difference between the two RNNs is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory in the RNN input (Equations 5 and 8). It seems unnecessary to have separate weights for the two RNNs, as the only advantage is an increase in model capacity, i.e., more parameters. Furthermore, the hyperparameters and size of the baseline neural networks should be comparable in terms of the number of parameters.
3. It is reasonable to include a baseline that inputs additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as the head of each word or NER results.
4. The paper does not provide any comments or results on the model's sensitivity to parser errors.

**Comments on the Model**

1. After computing the substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute attention over them at each word. Is there a reason to use a static attention for all words? As it is, the "knowledge" appears to act more like a filter to mark important words. Therefore, it is reasonable to include the baseline that inputs additional features.
2. Since the weight on a word is computed by the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and the two embeddings are computed by the same RNN/CNN, does this not mean that nodes/phrases similar to the whole sentence receive higher weights, i.e., all leaf nodes?
3. The paper claims that the model generalizes to different knowledge, but it seems that the substructure must be represented as a sequence of words, making it non-trivial to use constituent parse as knowledge.
4. Finally, I hesitate to call this "knowledge," as it is misleading. Typically, knowledge refers to world or external knowledge, such as a knowledge base of entities, whereas here it is really just syntax, or arguably semantics if AMR parsing is used.

**General Discussion**

This paper proposes a practical model that appears to work well on one dataset, but the main ideas are not very novel (see comments in Strengths). As an ACL paper, I expect more takeaways. More importantly, the experiments are not convincing as presented, and clarification is needed to better judge the results.

**Post-Rebuttal**

The authors did not address my main concern, which is whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) are used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another major concern is the use of two separate RNNs, which gives the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. Therefore, I am not changing my scores.
