- Proposes a simple memory-based method to flatten structured linguistic information into vectors and feed a weighted sum as additional context to an RNN tagger.
- Idea is similar to existing structured/syntax-based attention work (e.g., treeLSTM attention, Zhao et al., Liu et al., Eriguchi et al.) and the substructure encoder resembles DCNN (Ma et al.); novelty is limited but approach is simple and practical.
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about how baselines (TreeRNN, DCNN) are applied and whether their node embeddings are used independently of sentence embeddings and the joint tagger.
- Using two RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) with separate weights seems unnecessary and inflates model capacity; baselines should be matched in parameter count and hyperparameters.
- A basic baseline that supplies the RNN with additional features (e.g., head words or NER tags) is missing and should be included.
- No analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors is provided.
- Static attention over substructures for all words is questionable; per-word attention would be more natural and the current "knowledge" may act as a coarse filter rather than context.
- The inner-product weighting between sentence and substructure embeddings (computed by the same encoder) may bias weights toward nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).
- Claim of generalizing to different knowledge is weak because substructures must be represented as word sequences, making some knowledge types (e.g., constituent parses) non-trivial to incorporate.
- Calling the input "knowledge" is misleading—it's primarily syntax (or semantics with AMR), not external/world knowledge.
- Experiments are limited to one dataset, deliver few novel takeaways for an ACL paper, and need clarification to judge results.
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not address main concerns about baseline usage and the extra parameters from two RNNs, so the reviewer’s score remains unchanged.
