- Proposes a neural architecture that encodes parse substructures as vectors and uses a weighted sum as additional context for RNN sequence tagging (slot-filling).  
- Main contributions: simple flattening of structured info into vectors, a substructure encoder similar to DCNN, and a practical straightforward architecture.  
- Approach is related to prior work on structured/syntax-based attention and tree-LSTM attention mechanisms.  
- Empirical results are not fully convincing due to insufficient detail about how baselines compute sentence and substructure embeddings.  
- The model uses two RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) where the only difference is an added knowledge vector, raising concerns that gains may stem from extra parameters rather than design.  
- Hyperparameters and baseline model sizes are not provided; baselines should have comparable parameter counts.  
- A simple baseline that inputs extra features (e.g., head words, NER) is missing and should be included.  
- No analysis of sensitivity to parser errors is provided, which is important for syntax-based methods.  
- The model uses static attention over substructures rather than per-word attention, which may merely filter important words instead of providing contextualized attention.  
- The attention scoring may bias toward nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes) since embeddings are computed by the same encoder.  
- Representing substructures as sequences of words may limit applicability to certain knowledge types (e.g., constituent parses) and contradicts claims of generalizing to different knowledge.  
- Calling syntactic/AMR-derived structure “knowledge” is misleading, as it conflates internal linguistic structure with external/world knowledge.  
- Overall: practical and effective on one dataset but lacks novelty and stronger experimental takeaways expected for an ACL paper.
