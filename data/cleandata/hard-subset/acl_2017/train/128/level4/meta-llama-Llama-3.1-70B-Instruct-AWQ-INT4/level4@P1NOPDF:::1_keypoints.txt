- Proposes a neural architecture that stores structural linguistic substructures as memory vectors used as context for RNN sequence tagging (slot-filling).
- Main methodological contribution is a simple “flattening” of structured information into an array of vectors connected to the tagger.
- Approach is similar to prior structured/syntax-based attention work and the substructure encoder resembles DCNN; practical but not highly novel.
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to missing details about how baselines were implemented and used.
- Unclear whether TreeRNN/DCNN baselines produced node/substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and joint tagger.
- Uses two RNNs that differ only by an added knowledge vector, which may be unnecessary and mainly increases parameter count; baseline parameter parity is not addressed.
- Should include simpler baselines that add knowledge as input features to the RNN (e.g., head words or NER tags).
- Missing evaluation of model sensitivity to parser errors.
- Uses a static attention (same weights for all words) over substructures rather than per-word dynamic attention, which seems suboptimal.
- Inner-product weighting between sentence and substructure embeddings may bias weights toward nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).
- Claim that the model generalizes to different “knowledge” is questionable because substructures must be sequences of words, limiting other knowledge forms like constituent parses.
- Calling the syntax-derived representations “knowledge” is misleading since they are not external/world knowledge.
- Practical model that works on one dataset but lacks strong novelty and broader ACL-level takeaways; experiments need clarification.
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not resolve main concerns about baseline usage and the extra parameters from two RNNs, so scores remain unchanged.
