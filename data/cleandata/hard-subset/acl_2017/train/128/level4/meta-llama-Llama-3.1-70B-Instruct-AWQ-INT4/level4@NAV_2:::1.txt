This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling in the natural language understanding unit of conversation systems. The approach encodes substructures, such as nodes in a parse tree, as vectors and uses a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings as additional context for labeling in a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) at each time step.

The main contributions of this paper are three-fold. Firstly, it proposes a simple method to "flatten" structured information into an array of vectors, which can then be connected to the tagger as additional knowledge. This idea is similar to structured or syntax-based attention, such as attention over nodes from tree-LSTM, and is related to work by Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. Secondly, the proposed substructure encoder is similar to DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. Lastly, the architecture presents a practical and straightforward approach compared to prior work.

However, there are several weaknesses in the paper. The empirical results are not entirely convincing, primarily due to the lack of details about the baselines. For instance, the proposed model has two main parts: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baseline models are TreeRNN and DCNN, which are originally used for sentence embedding, but it is unclear how they are used to compute the two parts. Furthermore, the model uses two RNNs: a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one. The only difference in the knowledge-guided RNN is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory in the RNN input (Eqn 5 and 8). It seems unnecessary to have separate weights for the two RNNs, and the only advantage of using two is an increase in model capacity, i.e., more parameters. Additionally, the hyperparameters and size of the baseline neural networks are not provided, and they should have comparable numbers of parameters.

It is also reasonable to include a baseline that inputs additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as the head of each word or NER results. The paper does not provide any comments or results on the model's sensitivity to parser errors. 

Some additional comments on the model are also worth mentioning. After computing the substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute an attention over them at each word. However, the paper uses a static attention for all words, which seems to act more like a filter to mark important words. Since the weight on a word is computed by the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and the two embeddings are computed by the same RNN/CNN, it seems that nodes/phrases similar to the whole sentence get higher weights, i.e., all leaf nodes. The paper claims the model generalizes to different knowledge, but it seems that the substructure has to be represented as a sequence of words, which may not be straightforward for using constituent parse as knowledge.

Finally, the term "knowledge" might be misleading, as it usually refers to world/external knowledge, such as a knowledge base of entities, whereas in this paper, it is really just syntax or arguably semantics if AMR parsing is used.

Overall, this paper proposes a practical model that seems to work well on one dataset, but the main ideas are not very novel. As an ACL paper, there should be more takeaways, and the experiments are not convincing as presented. Some clarification is needed to better judge the results.
