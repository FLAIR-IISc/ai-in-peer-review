This paper introduces a neural network architecture leveraging a memory network to incorporate structural linguistic information for sequence tagging, specifically focusing on slot-filling within natural language understanding components of conversational systems. The approach encodes substructures – such as parse tree nodes – as vectors (memory slots), and integrates a weighted sum of these embeddings into an RNN at each time step to provide contextual information for tagging.

-----Strengths-----
The primary contribution of this work lies in its straightforward method for representing structured information as a vector array (the memory) and integrating it as supplemental knowledge into the tagger. This concept shares similarities with structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms, like those found in treeLSTM-based attention models. Relevant related work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder bears resemblance to the DCNN model (Ma et al.), embedding each node based on a sequence of ancestor words. While the architecture isn’t entirely new, its simplicity and practical applicability are appealing compared to existing approaches.

-----Weaknesses-----
The empirical results are not fully persuasive, largely due to insufficient detail regarding the baselines. Specific concerns, ordered by importance, are outlined below:

- Table 1 compares the proposed model against TreeRNN and DCNN, which are typically employed for sentence embedding. However, these models can also yield node/substructure embeddings. It’s unclear how these baselines were configured to produce comparable substructure and sentence representations for a fair comparison.
- The model employs two distinct RNNs – a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN – with the latter simply adding a “knowledge” vector from the memory to the RNN input (Eqn 5 and 8). The justification for separate weights for these RNNs is unclear; it appears to primarily increase model capacity (parameter count).  Information regarding the hyperparameter settings and sizes of the baseline networks is also missing, hindering a proper assessment of parameter comparability.
- Including a baseline that directly incorporates additional knowledge as features to the RNN – such as head words or NER tags – would strengthen the evaluation.
- The paper would benefit from a discussion of the model’s robustness to errors in the input parse trees, along with corresponding experimental results.
- Regarding the model itself: computing attention over the substructure embeddings at each word step seems a natural extension. The current static attention mechanism appears to function more as a filter, highlighting important words. This reinforces the need for the feature-based baseline suggested above.
- Given that substructure weights are calculated via the inner product of sentence and substructure embeddings (both generated by the same RNN/CNN), nodes or phrases similar to the overall sentence – potentially including leaf nodes – may receive disproportionately high weights.
- The claim of generalization to different knowledge sources is questionable, as the substructure representation currently relies on sequential word representations. Adapting the approach to utilize constituent parses, for example, isn’t immediately obvious.
- The term “knowledge” may be misleading. The model primarily utilizes syntactic (or potentially semantic, with AMR parsing) information, rather than external or world knowledge typically associated with the term.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper presents a practical model that demonstrates promising results on a single dataset. However, the core ideas are not particularly novel (as discussed in the Strengths section). For a conference like ACL, a more substantial contribution or novel insight is expected. Critically, the experiments, as currently presented, are not entirely convincing and require clarification for a more informed evaluation.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors did not adequately address my primary concern: whether the baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to generate substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. A further significant concern remains the use of two separate RNNs, which inherently provides the proposed model with a larger parameter space than the baselines. Consequently, I have not revised my assessment.
