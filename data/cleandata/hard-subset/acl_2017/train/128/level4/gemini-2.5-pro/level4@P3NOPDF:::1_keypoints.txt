- Proposes a memory-based sequence tagging model that encodes linguistic substructures (e.g., dependency nodes) as vector “memory” slots to provide context for slot-filling.

- Strength: Simple, practical method that “flattens” structured linguistic info into vectors, offering a lightweight alternative to complex structured-attention/TreeLSTM approaches; encoder resembles DCNN-style node embedding.

- Ambiguity in baseline setup: Paper does not clarify how TreeRNN/DCNN baselines were configured or whether they provided both sentence and substructure embeddings comparably.

- Questionable architecture choice: Using two separate RNNs (chain and knowledge-guided) appears to increase parameter count without justification, potentially giving an unfair advantage.

- Missing baseline: No simple feature-concatenation baseline (e.g., POS, syntactic head, NER appended to word embeddings) to isolate the benefit of the memory mechanism.

- Lack of robustness analysis: No evaluation of sensitivity to parsing/preprocessing errors despite reliance on automatic parse trees.

- Static vs. dynamic attention: Model uses a single static attention-weighted knowledge vector for all time steps; paper does not justify not using dynamic, per-token attention.

- Potential attention bias: Attention computed as inner product between substructure and sentence embeddings may favor nodes similar to the sentence (e.g., leaf nodes); no analysis of learned attention weights.

- Limited generalizability: Substructure encoder requires knowledge as word sequences, making it unclear how to handle other structures (e.g., constituency parses).

- Misleading terminology: Calling syntactic/AMR structure “knowledge” is imprecise; better termed “linguistic structure” or “syntactic context.”

- Main weakness: Empirical evaluation lacks crucial details, rigorous baselines, and parameter reporting, making results unconvincing; post-rebuttal did not resolve these concerns.
