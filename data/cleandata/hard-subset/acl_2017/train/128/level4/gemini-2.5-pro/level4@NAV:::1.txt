This paper presents a neural network architecture that encodes structural linguistic knowledge into a memory network for sequence tagging. The method is applied specifically to the task of slot-filling within natural language understanding systems for conversation. The proposed approach represents linguistic substructures, such as parse tree nodes, as vectors within a memory network. At each timestep, a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is provided to an RNN as additional context for the labeling task.

-----Strengths-----
The primary contribution of this work appears to be its straightforward method for "flattening" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which then provides supplementary knowledge to the tagging model. This concept is analogous to structured or syntax-based attention mechanisms, as seen in related work by Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. Moreover, the substructure encoder is analogous to the DCNN proposed by Ma et al., where node embeddings are derived from their ancestor words. While the overall architecture may not be entirely novel, its simplicity and practical application, in contrast to more complex prior approaches, are commendable.

-----Weaknesses-----
The empirical results are not fully convincing, primarily due to a lack of detail regarding the baseline implementations. The following comments are listed in order of decreasing importance.

-   The proposed model comprises two key components: a sentence encoder and a substructure encoder. In Table 1, the baseline models, TreeRNN and DCNN, were originally designed for sentence embedding. It is unclear how these models were adapted to generate embeddings for both the sentence and the substructures in the context of the joint tagging task.
-   The architecture employs two separate RNNs: a standard chain-based model and a knowledge-guided model. The only architectural difference is the addition of the "knowledge" vector to the input of the latter (Eqns 5 and 8). The motivation for using two distinct sets of weights is not well-justified; this design choice primarily serves to increase the model's parameter count. For a valid comparison, the paper must report the hyper-parameters and total parameter counts of the baseline models to ensure they possess comparable capacity.
-   The experiments would be strengthened by including a simpler baseline that incorporates structural knowledge directly as input features to a standard RNN, such as providing the headword or named-entity recognition results for each token.
-   The paper does not discuss the model's robustness or sensitivity to parsing errors, which is a critical factor for real-world application.

Comments on the model:
-   After computing the substructure embeddings, a dynamic attention mechanism calculated over these embeddings at each word's decoding step would seem more natural than the proposed static attention weight for all words. The paper should justify this design choice. As implemented, the static "knowledge" vector functions more like a global filter, which reinforces the need for the feature-based baseline mentioned previously.
-   The weighting scheme relies on the inner product between the sentence embedding and the substructure embeddings, with both being computed by similar RNN/CNN models. This may introduce a bias, potentially assigning higher weights to substructures (e.g., leaf nodes) that are most similar to the overall sentence representation.
-   The claim that the model generalizes to various knowledge sources is questionable. The proposed method requires knowledge to be represented as a sequence of words, and it is not immediately obvious how it could be adapted for other structures, such as constituent parses.
-   Finally, the use of the term "knowledge" is potentially misleading. In the NLP community, this term typically refers to external or world knowledge (e.g., from a knowledge base), whereas the information used here is purely syntactic or, if using AMR, compositional-semantic.

-----General Discussion-----
In summary, the paper presents a practical model that demonstrates promising performance on a single dataset. However, the core concepts lack significant novelty, drawing heavily on existing ideas. For a publication at a top-tier conference, a clearer and more impactful contribution is expected. Crucially, the experimental evaluation is currently unconvincing due to the issues raised above. Further clarification from the authors is necessary to properly assess the validity of the results.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The author response failed to address my primary concerns. Specifically, it remains unclear whether the baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN) were configured to compute substructure embeddings independently from the sentence embeddings for the joint tagging task. Furthermore, the concern regarding the increased parameter count of the proposed model, resulting from its use of two separate RNNs, was not adequately resolved. Consequently, my assessment of the paper remains unchanged.
