This paper proposes a neural architecture, Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), that incorporates structural linguistic knowledge into a sequence tagging model for natural language understanding (specifically, slot filling). The core idea is to encode linguistic substructures (e.g., paths in a dependency tree) into a set of vector representations, akin to a memory network. A sentence-level attention mechanism then computes a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings to produce a single, fixed "knowledge-guided" context vector. This vector is then supplied as an additional input to an RNN-based tagger at every time step to guide the labeling process.

-----Strengths-----
The paper's main contribution is a simple and practical method for "flattening" structured linguistic information into a format that can be easily integrated into a standard sequence tagging architecture. The proposed method encodes substructures into an array of vectors (the memory) and uses a simple attention mechanism to distill this information into a single context vector for the tagger. While the high-level idea bears similarity to prior work on structured or syntax-based attention (e.g., Zhao et al., 2017 on textual entailment; Liu et al., 2016 on natural language inference; Eriguchi et al., 2016 for machine translation), and the substructure encoder is reminiscent of DCNN (Ma et al., 2015a), the proposed architecture is straightforward to implement and apply. This practicality is a notable strength.

-----Weaknesses-----
I am not fully convinced by the empirical results, primarily due to a lack of detail regarding the baseline implementations and certain modeling choices. My concerns are listed below in decreasing order of importance.

1.  **Clarity of Baseline Comparisons:** The comparison to baseline models like Tree-RNN and DCNN lacks clarity. These models are not just sentence encoders; they also produce node- or substructure-level embeddings. It is unclear if the baselines were adapted to use this information in a comparable way to the proposed model, or if only their final sentence embedding was used. A fair comparison would require ensuring that the baselines leverage their structural information to a similar degree.

2.  **Model Capacity and Justification for Two RNNs:** The motivation for using two separate RNNs (a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one, combined in Eq. 11) is unclear. The only architectural difference between them is the addition of the static knowledge vector `o`. This design choice significantly increases the model's parameter count compared to a single RNN that receives the knowledge vector. Without a clear justification, this raises concerns about whether the performance gains are due to the architectural novelty or simply the increased model capacity. For a fair comparison, the baseline models should have a comparable number of parameters.

3.  **Missing a Simpler Feature-Based Baseline:** The experiments would be more convincing with the inclusion of a simpler baseline that incorporates structural information as features. For example, feeding the dependency head of each word as an additional feature into a single RNN tagger is a common and strong baseline that is missing here. Such a baseline would help isolate the contribution of the proposed attention-based knowledge integration.

4.  **Sensitivity to Parser Errors:** The paper does not analyze the model's sensitivity to parsing errors. Since the performance relies on correctly identified substructures, an analysis of how parser quality affects the final results would strengthen the paper's claims about robustness.

5.  **Static vs. Dynamic Attention:** The model uses a static, sentence-level attention mechanism, producing a single knowledge vector that is used for all words in the sequence. A more natural approach would be to compute a dynamic attention over the substructures at each time step of the RNN tagger. The authors should justify the choice of a static attention mechanism, as it seems to limit the model's ability to focus on different substructures for different words.

6.  **Potential Flaw in Attention Mechanism:** The attention mechanism itself may have a potential flaw. The attention weights (pi) are computed via an inner product between the whole sentence embedding (u) and each substructure embedding (mi), which are both generated by the same encoder (Mkg and Min are tied). This design might inherently assign higher weights to substructures that are most similar to the entire sentence, which could disproportionately favor longer paths or leaf nodes without necessarily capturing the most salient information for a specific tagging decision.

7.  **Generalization Claim:** The paper claims that the model generalizes to different types of knowledge. However, the proposed encoding scheme requires that knowledge be represented as a sequence of words. It is not obvious how this approach would naturally accommodate other forms of structural knowledge, such as constituent parse trees.

8.  **Misleading Terminology:** The use of the term "knowledge" is potentially misleading. In the NLP community, this term often refers to external or world knowledge (e.g., from a knowledge base). The information used here is primarily syntactic (or semantic, in the case of AMR), derived from the input sentence itself. Using more precise terminology like "structural guidance" or "syntactic guidance" would be more appropriate.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper presents a practical model that appears to perform well on the ATIS dataset. However, the core ideas are not entirely novel, and the experiments as presented are not fully convincing due to the issues raised above. Clarification on these points is needed to properly evaluate the paper's contributions.
