This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge within a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, focusing on slot-filling for natural language understanding in conversational systems. In this model, linguistic substructures (e.g., nodes in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors, or 'memory slots'. At each time step, a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into an RNN to provide additional context for the labeling task.

-----Strengths-----
The main contribution of this paper is a simple method for "flattening" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then used to provide additional knowledge to the sequence tagger. The core idea is conceptually similar to structured or syntax-based attention mechanisms, such as attending over nodes from a Tree-LSTM. Related work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. Furthermore, the proposed substructure encoder resembles the DCNN of Ma et al., where each node is embedded based on a sequence of its ancestor words. While the overall architecture is not entirely novel, the simple and practical approach is commendable compared to some prior work.

-----Weaknesses-----
I am not convinced by the empirical results, primarily due to a lack of detail regarding the baseline models. My concerns are listed below in order of decreasing importance.

- The proposed model consists of two main components: a sentence encoder and a substructure encoder. The baseline models listed in Table 1, TreeRNN and DCNN, were originally designed for sentence representation. However, they can also produce node or substructure embeddings. The paper does not clarify how these baseline models were adapted for the task—specifically, whether they were used to generate sentence embeddings, substructure embeddings, or both.

- The model employs two separate RNNs: a standard chain-based RNN and a 'knowledge-guided' RNN. The only architectural difference is the addition of the aggregated 'knowledge' vector to the input of the latter (Eq. 5 and 8). Using two separate sets of weights for these RNNs appears unnecessary. This design choice primarily serves to increase the model's parameter count, which could give it an unfair advantage over the baselines. The paper should report the hyperparameters and parameter counts for all models to ensure a fair comparison.

- A crucial baseline is missing: a standard RNN that incorporates linguistic knowledge simply as additional input features at each timestep (e.g., the syntactic head of each word, part-of-speech tags, or NER labels).

- The paper does not include any analysis of the model's sensitivity to parser errors, which is an important consideration for any syntax-dependent model.

Comments on the model:
- Given the set of substructure embeddings, a dynamic, per-word attention mechanism seems like a natural choice. Why does the model instead use a static attention weight for all words in the sequence? As designed, the 'knowledge' vector seems to function as a global filter highlighting important words, which further strengthens the case for including the feature-based baseline mentioned earlier.

- The attention weight for each substructure is computed via an inner product between the sentence embedding and the substructure's embedding. Since both embeddings are computed by the same underlying encoder (RNN/CNN), does this not bias the model to assign higher weights to substructures whose representations are most similar to the overall sentence representation? This could potentially favor trivial or overly broad substructures.

- The paper claims the model can generalize to different types of knowledge. However, the substructure encoder appears to require that knowledge be represented as a sequence of words (e.g., a dependency path). It is not clear how this method would apply to other common structures, such as constituent parse trees.

- Finally, the use of the term 'knowledge' is potentially misleading. In NLP, this term often refers to external or world knowledge (e.g., from a knowledge base). The information used here is purely linguistic (syntactic or semantic, in the case of AMR), not external.

-----General Discussion-----
In summary, this paper presents a practical model that performs well on a single dataset. However, the core ideas lack novelty, and the experimental evaluation is not convincing as presented. For an ACL paper, the work should offer stronger contributions and a more rigorous evaluation to allow for a proper assessment of its results. Clarification on the experimental setup is needed to better judge the reported results.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The author rebuttal did not address my primary concerns. Specifically, it remains unclear how the baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN) were configured to produce both sentence and substructure embeddings for the joint tagging task. Furthermore, the concern regarding the use of two separate RNNs, which likely gives the proposed model a parameter count advantage over the baselines, was not resolved. Consequently, I am maintaining my original scores.
