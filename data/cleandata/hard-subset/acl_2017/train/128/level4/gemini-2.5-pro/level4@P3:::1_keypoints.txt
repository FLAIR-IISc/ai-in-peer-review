- Introduces K-SAN: encodes parse-derived substructures into a knowledge memory and uses a global attention vector fed to an RNN tagger for slot filling.  
- Main contribution: a simple, practical method to flatten structured linguistic information into vector arrays compatible with sequential RNNs.  
- Shows the approach generalizes across structure sources, improving performance using dependency and AMR parses from multiple implementations.  
- Strength: architecturally simple and implementable compared to complex tree/graph neural nets.  
- Strength: achieves strong results on ATIS, with notable gains in low-data (Small/Medium) settings.  
- Strength: thorough ablation across four structural knowledge sources demonstrating consistent gains.  
- Weakness: unclear/fairness concerns about baseline implementations (DCNN/Tree-RNN) and whether node-level representations were used.  
- Weakness: joint RNN tagger doubles RNNs (increased capacity); paper doesn’t clarify if baselines matched parameter count.  
- Weakness: missing comparison to simpler structural baselines (e.g., concatenating headword embeddings to inputs).  
- Weakness: uses a static sentence-level attention vector for all time steps, which may be suboptimal for per-token tagging.  
- Weakness/question: attention computed as inner product between sentence and substructure embeddings may bias toward substructures similar to the whole sentence, down-weighting short but crucial phrases.  
- Question: please clarify exact DCNN and Tree-RNN baseline architectures and whether node-level outputs were used.  
- Question: were baseline models parameter-matched to the joint RNN tagger (e.g., doubled hidden size)?  
- Question: did you try the simple baseline of concatenating syntactic features (headword/dependency) to word embeddings?  
- Question: why use static sentence-level attention instead of dynamic attention recomputed per time step?  
- Question: how sensitive is performance to parser quality and how do parser errors affect results?  
- Question/suggestion: consider using a clearer term than “knowledge” (e.g., “structure-guided”) since it may be confused with external KBs.
