This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge using a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically for slot-filling in the natural language understanding (NLU) component of conversational systems. In this model, linguistic substructures (e.g., nodes in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors (i.e., memory slots). A weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is then fed into an RNN at each time step to provide additional context for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The main contribution of this paper is a simple method for "flattening" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then provided to the tagger as an additional knowledge source. This idea is similar to structured or syntax-based attention (e.g., attention over nodes from a TreeLSTM), and related to work by Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder is also reminiscent of DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of its ancestor words. Although the architecture is not entirely novel, I appreciate its simple and practical approach compared to prior work.

-----Weaknesses-----
The empirical results are not entirely convincing, primarily due to a lack of detail regarding the baselines. My comments are listed below in order of decreasing importance.

- The proposed model consists of two main components: a sentence encoder and a substructure encoder. The baseline models listed in Table 1, TreeRNN and DCNN, were originally designed for sentence embedding. While one can extract node/substructure embeddings from them, it is unclear how these models were adapted to compute both parts for the baseline experiments.
- The model uses two separate RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. The only difference between them is the addition of a "knowledge" vector to the input of the knowledge-guided RNN (Eqns. 5 and 8). It seems unnecessary to use two RNNs with separate weights for this purpose. The primary advantage of this design is an increase in model capacity (i.e., more parameters), which raises questions about the fairness of the comparison. Furthermore, what are the hyperparameters and model sizes of the baseline neural networks? The baselines should have a comparable number of parameters for a fair evaluation.
- A valuable baseline would be one that simply incorporates the additional knowledge as input features to a standard RNN tagger (e.g., using the syntactic head of each word, NER tags, etc.).
- The paper should include some analysis or results on the model's sensitivity to parser errors.

Comments on the model:
- After computing the substructure embeddings, a more natural approach would be to apply an attention mechanism over them at each decoding step. Is there a specific reason for using a static, globally-weighted sum for all words? As implemented, the "knowledge" vector acts more like a static filter highlighting important words, which further reinforces the need for the feature-based baseline suggested earlier.
- The weight for each substructure is computed via an inner product between the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding. Since both are computed using the same encoder, does this not mean that substructures most similar to the overall sentence will receive the highest weights? This could potentially bias the model towards certain structures, such as leaf nodes.
- The paper claims that the model generalizes to different types of knowledge. However, the proposed substructure encoder seems to require knowledge to be represented as a sequence of words. It is not straightforward how this method would incorporate other forms of structural information, such as a constituent parse tree.
- Finally, the use of the term "knowledge" could be misleading. This term typically refers to external or world knowledge (e.g., from a knowledge base). The information used here is more accurately described as linguistic structure (e.g., syntax from a dependency parse, or semantics from an AMR parse).

-----General Discussion-----
This paper proposes a practical model that appears to perform well on the chosen dataset. However, the core ideas are not entirely novel (as noted in the Strengths section), and the paper could offer more significant takeaways for the community. Most importantly, the experiments are not convincing in their current form. Clarification on the experimental setup is required to properly evaluate the paper's contributions.
