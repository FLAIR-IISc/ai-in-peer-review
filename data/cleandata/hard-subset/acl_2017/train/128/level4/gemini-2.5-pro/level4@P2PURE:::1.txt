This paper proposes a neural network architecture that incorporates structural linguistic knowledge into a memory network for sequence tagging. The primary application is slot-filling for natural language understanding in conversational systems. In this model, each linguistic substructure (e.g., a parse tree node) is encoded as a vector in a memory slot. A weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is then fed into an RNN at each time step, providing additional context for the labeling task.

-----Strengths-----
The paper's main contribution lies in its simple method for "flattening" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which then serves as an additional knowledge source for the tagger. This idea is analogous to structured or syntax-based attention mechanisms (e.g., attention over TreeLSTM nodes), and is related to prior work by Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. on machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder resembles the DCNN of Ma et al., where each node's embedding is derived from a sequence of its ancestor words. While the overall architecture is not entirely novel, its simplicity and practical approach are commendable in comparison to prior work.

-----Weaknesses-----
The empirical results are not fully convincing, primarily due to insufficient detail regarding the baseline models. My concerns are ranked below in decreasing order of importance.

- The proposed model has two key components: a sentence encoder and a substructure encoder. The baseline models listed in Table 1, TreeRNN and DCNN, were originally designed for sentence embedding. However, they can also produce node or substructure embeddings. The paper does not clarify how these baselines were adapted to compute both the sentence and substructure representations required for a fair comparison.

- The model employs two separate RNNs: a standard chain-based RNN and a "knowledge-guided" one. The only distinction is the addition of the knowledge vector to the input of the latter (Eq. 5 and 8). Using two separate sets of weights seems unnecessary, as its main effect is to increase the model's parameter count. This raises a critical question: what were the hyper-parameters and model sizes of the baselines? For a fair comparison, the baselines should have a comparable number of parameters.

- A crucial baseline is missing: one that incorporates similar structural information as simple features into a standard RNN (e.g., adding the syntactic head or NER tag for each word).

- The paper does not discuss the model's sensitivity to parsing errors, which is an important practical consideration.

Comments on the model:
- Given the computed substructure embeddings, a more natural approach would be to use a dynamic attention mechanism at each time step. Why was a static attention weight used for all words? As implemented, the "knowledge" vector seems to function more as a static filter for important words. This reinforces the need for the previously mentioned baseline, where such information is simply provided as input features.

- The weight for each substructure is an inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding. Since both are computed using the same encoder (RNN/CNN), does this not bias the model to assign higher weights to substructures (e.g., individual words/leaf nodes) that are most similar to the overall sentence representation?

- The paper claims the model generalizes to different types of knowledge, but the substructure representation appears to require a sequence of words. It is not straightforward how this method could incorporate other forms of structural knowledge, such as constituent parses.

- Finally, the term "knowledge" may be misleading. In NLP, this term typically refers to external or world knowledge (e.g., from a knowledge base). Here, it refers to internal linguistic structure (syntax, or semantics via AMR), and the terminology could be clarified.

-----General Discussion-----
In summary, the paper presents a practical model that performs well on a single dataset. However, the core ideas lack novelty, as noted in the Strengths section. For an ACL publication, the paper should offer more significant takeaways. Most critically, the experiments are unconvincing in their current form and require substantial clarification before the model's contributions can be properly evaluated.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors' rebuttal did not address the main concern regarding the baselines: specifically, whether models like TreeRNN were used to compute both substructure and sentence embeddings for a fair comparison with the proposed joint model. The second major concern about the use of two separate RNNs, which potentially gives the proposed model an unfair advantage in parameter count, also remains unaddressed. Consequently, I will not be changing my score.
