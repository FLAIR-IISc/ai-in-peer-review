- Proposes a simple method to “flatten” structured linguistic information into a memory array of substructure embeddings used as extra context for sequence tagging.  
- Work is related to prior structured/syntax-attention methods and resembles DCNN-style substructure encoding; overall approach is simple and practical but not highly novel.  
- Paper demonstrates good performance on a single dataset but empirical claims are undermined by unclear experimental details.  
- Unclear how baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN, DCNN) were adapted to produce both sentence and substructure embeddings for fair comparison.  
- The model uses two separate RNNs (chain and knowledge-guided), which may be unnecessary and inflate parameter count; baseline hyperparameters and model sizes are not reported for parity.  
- Missing a crucial baseline that injects structural information as simple input features (e.g., syntactic head, NER) into a standard RNN.  
- The paper lacks analysis of sensitivity to parsing errors, an important practical concern.  
- Uses a static attention/weighting of substructures across all time steps instead of a dynamic attention mechanism that would be more natural.  
- Weighting substructures by inner product of sentence and substructure embeddings may bias toward leaf nodes similar to the global sentence representation.  
- Claim of generalizing to different types of knowledge is weak because substructure embeddings seem to require sequences of words, making other knowledge forms (e.g., constituents) non-obvious to integrate.  
- Terminology is misleading: calling internal linguistic structure “knowledge” conflates it with external/world knowledge and should be clarified.  
- Overall, the paper’s ideas lack strong novelty for an ACL venue and experimental deficiencies prevent proper evaluation of contributions.  
- Post-rebuttal concerns remain unaddressed: unfairness of baselines (substructure/sentence embedding use) and the two-RNN parameter advantage, so reviewer did not change their score.
