- Proposes K-SAN, which encodes linguistic substructures as vectors and uses sentence-level attention to produce a single knowledge-guided context vector for an RNN tagger.  
- Strength: Simple, practical method to flatten structured linguistic information into a memory-like array of substructure embeddings with an attention-based distillation.  
- Strength: Architecture is straightforward to implement and integrate into standard sequence tagging pipelines.  
- Weakness: Baseline comparisons (e.g., Tree-RNN, DCNN) are unclear—uncertain whether baselines used node/substructure embeddings comparably or only final sentence embeddings.  
- Weakness: Using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) is not well motivated and increases parameter count; unclear if gains stem from architecture or larger capacity.  
- Weakness: Missing a simpler, strong feature-based baseline (e.g., adding dependency head as an RNN feature) to isolate attention-based benefits.  
- Weakness: No analysis of sensitivity to parser errors, despite reliance on parsed substructures.  
- Weakness: Static sentence-level attention may be suboptimal; dynamic per-time-step attention could better focus on different substructures for different words.  
- Weakness: Attention design may bias toward substructures most similar to the whole-sentence embedding (tied encoders), favoring longer paths or leaves rather than task-salient substructures.  
- Weakness: Claim of generalization to different knowledge types is weak because encoding requires knowledge as word sequences, limiting applicability to other structures (e.g., constituent trees).  
- Weakness: Terminology “knowledge” is misleading; the paper mainly uses syntactic/structural information derived from the sentence—terms like “structural guidance” would be clearer.  
- General: Paper performs well on ATIS but core ideas are not highly novel and experiments need clarification to be fully convincing.  
- Post-rebuttal: Authors did not adequately address main concerns about baseline comparability and the two-RNN justification; reviewer retains original scores.
