This paper proposes a neural network architecture for sequence tagging tasks, specifically for slot-filling in conversational language understanding. The core idea is to incorporate structural linguistic knowledge into the tagging process using a memory network. In this framework, linguistic substructures, such as nodes in a dependency parse tree, are encoded into vector representations, or "memory slots." At each time step, a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) to provide additional context for the labeling decision.

### Strengths

The paper's main contribution is a simple and practical method for "flattening" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which can then be supplied as external knowledge to a sequence tagger. This approach is conceptually similar to structured or syntax-based attention mechanisms seen in prior work on textual entailment (Zhao et al., 2015), natural language inference (Liu et al., 2016), and machine translation (Eriguchi et al., 2016). Additionally, the proposed substructure encoder, which embeds a node based on its ancestor words, shares similarities with the Dynamic Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN) of Ma et al. (2015). While the overall architecture may not be entirely novel, its simplicity and pragmatism are appealing compared to some more complex models in the literature.

### Weaknesses

The empirical results are not fully convincing, primarily due to a lack of detail regarding the baseline models. The following comments are ranked by decreasing importance.

1.  **Unclear Baseline Implementation:** The proposed model has two key components: a sentence encoder and a substructure encoder. The baseline models cited, TreeRNN and DCNN, were originally designed for sentence-level tasks. While their node/substructure embeddings could theoretically be repurposed, the paper does not specify how these models were adapted to compute both the sentence and substructure representations required for a fair comparison within the proposed framework. Without this clarification, it is impossible to verify that the baselines were implemented in a comparable and effective manner.

2.  **Model Architecture and Capacity:** The proposed model uses two separate RNNs: a standard chain-based RNN and a "knowledge-guided" RNN. The only architectural difference between them is the addition of the knowledge vector to the input of the second RNN (Equations 5 and 8). The motivation for using two distinct sets of weights is unclear. This design choice seems to primarily serve as a way to increase the model's capacity (i.e., add more parameters). This raises a critical question: what are the hyper-parameters and total parameter counts of the baseline models? To make a fair claim of superiority, the proposed model and the baselines should have a comparable number of parameters.

3.  **Missing Baselines:** A crucial baseline is missing from the experiments. It would be valuable to compare against a simpler model that incorporates linguistic knowledge as discrete features fed directly into the RNN at each time step (e.g., adding features for the headword of each token, its part-of-speech tag, or its NER tag). Such a baseline would help isolate the specific benefits of the proposed memory-based encoding over a more traditional feature engineering approach.

4.  **Robustness to Parser Errors:** The model's performance is dependent on the quality of an external parser. The paper includes no analysis or discussion of the model's sensitivity to parser errors, which is a significant practical consideration for real-world applications.

### Additional Comments on the Model

*   **Static vs. Dynamic Attention:** After computing embeddings for all substructures, a dynamic, per-token attention mechanism would seem like a more natural choice than the static attention mechanism used. A dynamic attention would allow the model to focus on different substructures for each word being tagged. As implemented, the static "knowledge" vector acts more like a global filter that marks important words for the entire sequence. This observation reinforces the need for the simpler feature-based baseline suggested above.

*   **Weighting Scheme:** The weight for each substructure is computed via an inner product between the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding. Since both embeddings are computed using the same encoder type (RNN/CNN), does this not imply that substructures that are representationally similar to the whole sentence will receive higher weights? This could introduce an unintended bias, for example, by systematically up-weighting all leaf nodes (individual words).

*   **Generalizability:** The paper claims the model can generalize to different types of knowledge. However, the proposed substructure encoder appears to require that the knowledge be represented as a sequence of words (e.g., a path in a dependency tree). It is not straightforward how this method would accommodate other common linguistic structures, such as constituent parse trees.

*   **Terminology:** The use of the term "knowledge" is potentially misleading. In the NLP community, this term often refers to external or world knowledge, such as that contained in a knowledge base of entities and relations. Here, the model incorporates internal linguistic information (syntax, or perhaps semantics via AMR). Using a more precise term like "structural linguistic information" would improve clarity.

### Summary and Conclusion

This paper presents a practical model that demonstrates strong performance on a single dataset. However, the core ideas are not entirely novel and draw heavily from existing work on structured attention. More importantly, the experimental evaluation is not convincing in its current form due to a lack of clarity on the implementation and parameterization of the baseline models. Without these details, it is difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed architecture.

### Post-Rebuttal

The authors' rebuttal did not address my primary concerns. There was no clarification on whether the baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently for use in the joint tagging model. Furthermore, the concern about the use of two separate RNNs, which gives the proposed model a potential advantage in parameter count over the baselines, was not resolved. Therefore, my evaluation of the paper remains unchanged.
