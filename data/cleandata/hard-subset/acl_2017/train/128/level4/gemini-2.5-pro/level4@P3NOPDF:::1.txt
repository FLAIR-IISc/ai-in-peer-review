This paper proposes a neural network architecture for sequence tagging that incorporates structural linguistic knowledge using a memory network. The primary application is slot-filling for natural language understanding in conversational systems. The core idea is to encode linguistic substructures, such as nodes in a dependency parse tree, into vector representations (memory slots). At each time step of a sequence labeling RNN, a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is provided as additional context for the tagging decision.

-----Strengths-----

The primary contribution of this work is a simple and practical method for incorporating structured linguistic information into a sequence tagging model. The proposed approach "flattens" this information into an array of vectors (the memory), which can be easily integrated as additional context for a tagger. This method offers a simpler alternative to more complex structured attention mechanisms, such as those built on TreeLSTMs (e.g., Zhao et al., Liu et al., Eriguchi et al.). While the overall architecture is not entirely novel—the substructure encoder, for instance, bears resemblance to the Dynamic Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN) of Ma et al., where each node is embedded from its ancestor words—the simplicity and practicality of the proposed model are commendable.

-----Weaknesses and Questions-----

The empirical evaluation is not convincing due to a lack of crucial details and potentially unfair comparisons. My concerns are listed below in decreasing order of importance.

1.  **Ambiguity in Baseline Implementation:** The proposed model has distinct components for sentence embedding and substructure embedding. The baselines cited, TreeRNN and DCNN, were originally designed for sentence-level tasks. While their internal node representations could be adapted for substructure embedding, the paper does not clarify how these models were actually configured. Were they used to generate *both* the sentence and substructure embeddings for the tagging task, or only one part? This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison.

2.  **Model Architecture and Parameter Count:** The model employs two separate RNNs: a standard chain-based RNN and a "knowledge-guided" RNN, which differ only by the addition of the aggregated knowledge vector to the input (Eqns. 5 and 8). The justification for using two distinct sets of weights is unclear. This design choice appears to be a straightforward way to increase the model's parameter count, potentially giving it an unfair advantage over the baselines. To ensure a fair comparison, the paper must report the hyper-parameters and total parameter counts for all models, demonstrating that any performance gains are not simply due to increased model capacity.

3.  **Missing a Key Baseline:** A crucial baseline is absent from the experiments. A much simpler way to incorporate linguistic knowledge is to directly featurize it and concatenate these features with the word embeddings at the input layer of a single RNN. For example, features like the part-of-speech tag, the syntactic headword, or a named entity tag for each token could be included. Comparing against such a baseline would help isolate the contribution of the proposed memory-based integration mechanism versus simply providing the raw linguistic information.

4.  **Robustness to Pre-processing Errors:** The model relies on automatically generated parse trees. The paper includes no analysis of the model's sensitivity to parsing errors. An evaluation of how performance degrades with less accurate parsers would strengthen the paper's claims about practical applicability.

5.  **Static vs. Dynamic Attention:** The model computes a single, static attention-weighted "knowledge" vector that is used for all time steps in the tagging RNN. A more intuitive approach would be to compute a dynamic attention over the substructure memory at each time step, allowing the model to focus on different syntactic components for different words. The paper should justify the choice of a static weighting scheme, especially since it resembles a simple information filter.

6.  **Potential Flaw in Attention Mechanism:** The attention weight for a substructure is determined by the inner product of its embedding and the overall sentence embedding. Since both embeddings are computed using similar network components, it is plausible that this mechanism will assign higher weights to substructures that are semantically similar to the entire sentence. This could disproportionately favor certain nodes (e.g., leaf nodes representing individual words) and may not be the intended behavior. An analysis of the learned attention weights would be insightful.

7.  **Limited Generalizability of Knowledge Representation:** The paper claims the model is generalizable to different types of knowledge. However, the proposed substructure encoder requires the knowledge to be represented as a sequence of words (e.g., a path in a dependency tree). It is not straightforward how this method would be applied to other common structural representations, such as constituent parse trees. This limitation should be acknowledged.

8.  **Misleading Terminology:** The use of the term "knowledge" is potentially misleading. In the NLP community, this term often refers to external, world knowledge (e.g., from a knowledge base). The information used here is purely linguistic structure (syntax or AMR-based semantics). Using a more precise term like "linguistic structure" or "syntactic context" would improve clarity.

-----General Discussion-----

In summary, this paper presents a practical and straightforward model for integrating linguistic structure into a sequence tagger. While the model appears to perform well on the chosen dataset, its core components are adaptations of existing ideas, limiting its novelty. The primary weakness of the paper is its empirical evaluation, which lacks the necessary detail and rigorous baselines to be convincing. For the paper to be acceptable, the authors would need to significantly strengthen the experimental section by addressing the concerns raised above.

-----Post-rebuttal-----

The author response did not adequately address the main concerns raised in this review. Specifically, there was no clarification on whether the baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute both sentence and substructure embeddings in a comparable manner to the proposed model. Furthermore, the concern about the proposed model having an unfair advantage due to the use of two separate RNNs (and thus, more parameters) was not resolved. Given that these are crucial issues for interpreting the validity of the empirical results, my assessment of the paper remains unchanged.
