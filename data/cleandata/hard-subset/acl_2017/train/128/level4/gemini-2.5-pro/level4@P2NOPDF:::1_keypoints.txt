- Proposes a practical method to "flatten" structured linguistic information into a memory array of substructure embeddings for sequence tagging.
- Incorporates dependency-tree substructures via a memory network feeding a weighted substructure embedding into an RNN per time step.
- Method is conceptually similar to prior structured/syntax-based attention and DCNN-style substructure encoders, so novelty is limited.
- Strength lies in simplicity and pragmatic design compared to more complex prior models.
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient detail about how baseline models were implemented and adapted for the task.
- Unclear whether TreeRNN/DCNN baselines were properly repurposed to compute both sentence and substructure embeddings for fair comparison.
- Architectural choice of two separate RNNs (one with a knowledge vector) is inadequately motivated and may simply increase parameter count.
- No reporting of hyperparameters or total parameter counts for baselines, preventing fair capacity-matched comparisons.
- Missing a crucial baseline that feeds discrete linguistic features (e.g., headword, POS, NER) directly into the RNN to isolate benefits of the memory encoding.
- No analysis of robustness to parser errors despite reliance on an external parser, a key practical concern.
- Static (global) attention over substructures seems suboptimal; dynamic per-token attention would be more natural and informative.
- Weighting via inner product with the sentence embedding may bias weights toward substructures similar to the whole sentence, potentially up-weighting leaf nodes.
- Generalizability claim is weak: the substructure encoder expects word sequences and may not extend cleanly to other structures like constituency parses.
- Terminology is misleading—calling syntactic/internal representations "knowledge" confuses this with external/world knowledge.
- Evaluation is limited to a single dataset and the authors' rebuttal failed to resolve major concerns about baselines and parameter advantages.
