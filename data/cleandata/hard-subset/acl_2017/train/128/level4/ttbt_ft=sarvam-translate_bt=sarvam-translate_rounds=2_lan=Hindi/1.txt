This research paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structured linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks (specifically, to fill slots in a natural language understanding unit in conversational systems). Sub-networks (like a node in a parse tree) are encoded as a vector (a memory slot), and the weighted sum of sub-network embeddings is fed into an RNN at each step as an additional reference for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
I think the main contribution of this paper is a simple way to "flatten" structured information into an array of vectors (memory), which are then associated with additional knowledge in the form of a tagger. This idea is similar to structured/syntax-based attention (i.e., attention to nodes from a tree LSTM); related works include Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed sub-network encoder is similar to a CNN (Ma et al.): each node is embedded from a sequence of parent words. The architecture doesn't seem entirely new, but I like the simple and practical approach compared to previous work.

-----Weaknesses-----
I'm not very convinced by the empirical results, mainly due to the lack of detail in the baseline. The comments below are sorted by decreasing significance.
- The proposed model has two main parts: sentence embedding and sub-network embedding. In Table 1, the baseline model is a tree-RNN and a CNN; they are basically used for sentence embedding, but one can easily take node/sub-network embedding from them. It's not clear how they are used to calculate the two parts.
- The model uses two RNNs: one chain-based and one knowledge-directed. The only difference between the knowledge-directed RNN is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory to the RNN input (equations 5 and 8). I find it completely unnecessary to have different weights for the two RNNs. The only advantage of using two is increased model capacity, i.e., more parameters.
Also, what are the hyper-parameters/sizes of the baseline neural network? They should have a comparable number of parameters.
- I also think it's appropriate to include a baseline that inputs additional knowledge as features for the RNNs, for example, the head of each word, NER results, etc.
- Any comments/results on the model's sensitivity to parser errors?
Comments on the model:
- After computing the sub-network embedding, it seems very natural to focus on them for each word. Is there any reason to use a fixed attention for all words? I think it is, "knowledge" is acting more like a filter to mark important words. Then it is appropriate to include the suggestion above, i.e., to input additional features.
- Since the weight for a word is calculated by the internal product of sentence embedding and sub-network embedding, and both embeddings are calculated by the same RNN/CNN, doesn't that mean that nodes/phrases similar to the entire sentence get a higher weight, i.e., all leaf nodes?
- The paper claims that the model has been generalized for different knowledge, but I think the sub-network should be represented as a sequence of words, for example, I don't find it straightforward to use component parse here as knowledge.
Finally, I'm hesitant to call it "knowledge". It's misleading because it's usually used to refer to the knowledge base of entities such as the world/external knowledge, while here it's really just syntax, or arguably semantics if AMR parsing is used.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper proposes a practical model that seems to work well on a dataset, but the main ideas are not very new (see comments in strengths). I think there should be more conclusions as an ACL paper. More importantly, the experiments are not as convincing as they are now presented. Some clarification will be needed to better assess the results.

-----Post-Rebel-----
The authors have not addressed my main concern, which is whether the baseline (such as a tree-RNN) is used to calculate sentence embedding and sub-network embedding independent of the combined tagger. Another major concern is the use of two different RNNs that give the proposed model more parameters than the baseline. Therefore, I am not changing my score.
