- Main contribution: practical method to "flatten" structured linguistic substructures into a memory array fed as additional context to a tagger.  
- Implementation: simpler and cleaner than prior structured-attention approaches, with a substructure encoder similar to DCNN.  
- Novelty: architecture not entirely novel — builds on existing ideas and offers engineering value rather than a major conceptual advance.  

- Primary empirical concern: insufficient baseline details—unclear how compared baselines produce the two components (sentence vs. substructure embeddings).  
- Two-RNN design: using separate chain-based and knowledge-guided RNNs seems unnecessary; effect could be achieved with one RNN and may simply increase parameter count.  
- Parameter/comparison gap: paper does not show baselines with comparable network sizes or provide parameter counts to justify the extra RNN.  
- Missing baseline: should include a simple feature-based baseline that inputs additional knowledge (e.g., head words, NER) to isolate architecture vs. extra information.  
- Parser robustness: paper fails to analyze sensitivity to dependency/AMR parser errors despite heavy reliance on parsing.  
- Attention limitation: attention uses a single static distribution across all words rather than word-specific dynamic attention, which seems suboptimal.  
- Embedding bias concern: scoring via inner product between sentence and substructure embeddings may overweight substructures similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).  
- Terminology issue: calling the inputs "knowledge" is misleading — method depends on syntactic/semantic structures, not external world-knowledge bases.  

- Overall evaluation: performs well on ATIS but lacks sufficient novelty for ACL and has inadequate experimental rigor.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not resolve main issues (baseline usage and parameter-count comparisons), so reviewer maintains original assessment.
