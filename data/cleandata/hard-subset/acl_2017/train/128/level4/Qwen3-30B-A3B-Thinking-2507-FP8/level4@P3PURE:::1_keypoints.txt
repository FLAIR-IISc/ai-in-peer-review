- Proposes a simple method to flatten structured linguistic information into a memory of substructure vectors used as extra context for sequence tagging.  
- Approach resembles prior structured/syntax-based attention and node-encoding methods (e.g., Zhao et al., Liu et al., Eriguchi et al., Ma et al.); practical simplicity is appreciated though novelty is limited.  
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient baseline details and unclear experimental comparability.  
- Unclear how listed baselines (TreeRNN, DCNN) were adapted to produce both sentence and substructure embeddings for fair comparison.  
- The dual-RNN architecture appears unnecessary: the knowledge-guided RNN only adds a memory vector and separate weights mainly increase parameter count without clear justification.  
- Paper lacks explicit matching of baseline hyperparameters/parameter counts to show fair comparisons.  
- Missing a simple baseline that inputs the same additional knowledge as conventional features (e.g., word heads or NER).  
- No analysis of the model’s robustness to parser errors or noisy substructure inputs.  
- Using a static (sentence-level) attention over substructures for all words seems suboptimal; per-word attention is more natural.  
- Node-weighting via inner product with the sentence embedding biases toward nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., many leaf nodes).  
- The claim that the model generalizes to different knowledge sources is questionable because substructure representations require word sequences and may not handle some formats (e.g., constituents) well.  
- Calling the input “knowledge” is misleading because it refers to syntactic/semantic structure (e.g., AMR), not external world knowledge or KBs.  
- Overall: practical and performs on one dataset but lacks strong novelty and more convincing, clarified experiments are needed for an ACL-level contribution.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not resolve major concerns about baseline independence for substructure embeddings or justify the dual-RNN/parameter increase; reviewer score remains unchanged.
