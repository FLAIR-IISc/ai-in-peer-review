- Proposes a simple method to "flatten" structured linguistic substructures into a memory array and integrate them into an RNN tagger for slot-filling.  
- Conceptually similar to prior syntax-based attention and substructure encoders (e.g., treeLSTM, DCNN); not entirely novel but practically straightforward.  
- Experimental results are unconvincing due to insufficient baseline details and unclear experimental comparisons.  
- Baseline ambiguity: unclear how compared models (TreeRNN, DCNN) were adapted to produce substructure embeddings versus their sentence-level use.  
- Redundant architecture: using two RNNs with separate weights appears unnecessary and mainly increases parameter count; comparable-parameter baselines are missing.  
- Missing simple baseline: should include a baseline that injects external features (heads, NER tags) directly into the RNN to isolate memory value.  
- Parser sensitivity: no analysis of how parser errors affect performance.  
- Attention design concern: using a static, global "knowledge" vector per word is suboptimal—per-word attention over substructures would be more natural.  
- Weight calculation concern: scoring substructures by inner product with the sentence embedding (from same encoder) may bias toward leaf nodes and undermine the intended design.  
- Generalization claim questionable: method depends on representing substructures as word sequences, making integration of other knowledge sources (e.g., AMR) unclear.  
- Terminology issue: calling syntactic/structural representations "knowledge" is misleading relative to usual use of the term.  
- Overall: idea is practical but lacks novelty and stronger theoretical/methodological insight expected for ACL; experiments need substantial clarification.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not show whether baselines produced substructure embeddings independently nor justify the two-RNN weight duplication, leaving original concerns unresolved.
