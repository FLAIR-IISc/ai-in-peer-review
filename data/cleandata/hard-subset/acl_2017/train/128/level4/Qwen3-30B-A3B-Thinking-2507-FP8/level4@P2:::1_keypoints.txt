- Summary: Introduces K-SAN, a memory-attention neural model that encodes linguistic substructures (e.g., dependency paths) to improve slot-filling sequence tagging.

- Contribution 1: Practical method for incorporating linguistic structure via a memory network, improving generalization with limited training data.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrated effectiveness on ATIS, especially with small training sets.

- Contribution 3: Works with different linguistic knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR) without schema changes.

- Strength 1: Significant F1 gains on very small data (1/40), showing strong generalization when labels are scarce.

- Strength 2: Relatively simple, practical architecture with clear implementation details, facilitating adoption.

- Strength 3: Joint RNN tagger effectively balances chain and knowledge signals, achieving SOTA on Large data and robust small-data performance.

- Weakness 1: Baseline comparisons (TreeRNN, DCNN) lack implementation detail, raising fairness concerns.

- Weakness 2: Dual-RNN design may simply increase capacity; authors don’t justify why two RNNs are needed over a single-RNN alternative.

- Weakness 3: Calling syntactic/semantic structures "knowledge" is misleading and overstates scope.

- Weakness 4: No analysis of sensitivity to parser errors despite heavy reliance on dependency parses.

- Question 1: How were baseline substructure embeddings configured—own embeddings or same as K-SAN—for fair comparison?

- Question 2: Why choose a dual-RNN architecture rather than a single RNN with additional knowledge input, and what evidence supports this choice?

- Question 3: How does performance change with a more accurate parser (e.g., SyntaxNet) to assess robustness to parsing quality?
