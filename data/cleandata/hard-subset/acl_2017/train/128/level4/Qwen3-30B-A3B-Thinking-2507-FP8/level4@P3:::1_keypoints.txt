- Proposes K-SAN: attention-based neural model that integrates linguistic structure (dependency, AMR) for slot filling, claiming end-to-end teacher-guided attention, cross-knowledge generalization, and efficiency via parallel substructure modeling.  
- Reviewer highlights strongest contribution: practical improvement in generalization on small datasets.  
- Reviewer’s second contribution: empirical robustness across different linguistic knowledge sources, though "generalization" claim is somewhat overstated.  
- Reviewer’s third contribution (efficiency) is weakly supported and not a primary novelty.  
- Strength: Strong empirical evidence on scarce data — statistically significant F1 gains (e.g., +5.1% Small, +2.4% Medium) with convincing attention visualizations.  
- Strength: Robust performance across dependency parsers and AMR, demonstrating flexibility to different linguistic representations.  
- Strength: Interpretable attention weights that align with semantic roles, offering practical insight over black-box baselines.  
- Weakness: Unclear baseline comparisons — unclear if Tree-RNN/DCNN use identical substructure encodings as K-SAN, risking capacity vs. architecture confound.  
- Weakness: Unjustified two-RNN design — extra parameters without ablation or parameter counts to show necessity vs. single-RNN alternative.  
- Weakness: Misleading "knowledge" framing — dependency/AMR are linguistic representations, not external knowledge bases; model may not handle non-sequence knowledge.  
- Weakness: No analysis of parser errors’ impact on K-SAN performance, despite dependency parsing being a prerequisite.  
- Question: Do Tree-RNN and DCNN baselines encode substructures the same way as K-SAN or only as sentence embeddings?  
- Question: What are parameter counts for K-SAN versus baselines to rule out capacity-driven gains?  
- Question: How would the model handle non-sequence knowledge (e.g., constituency trees); provide a concrete example?  
- Question: How do dependency parsing errors affect K-SAN’s attention and overall performance (e.g., Fig. 5 mislabeling)?  
- Additional comment: Core idea (attention over linguistic substructures) isn’t novel but is practical; framing as "knowledge-guided" is misleading and lack of baseline transparency undermines the contribution despite strong ATIS results.
