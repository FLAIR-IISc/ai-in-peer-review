

Summary: This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture for natural language understanding that incorporates linguistic structure through a memory network approach. K-SAN encodes substructures (e.g., dependency paths) as vectors in a memory module and uses attention over these vectors to inform slot-filling predictions. The model aims to better capture structured linguistic information beyond traditional flat sequence models.

Main Contributions (as reviewed):
1. A practical method for incorporating linguistic structure into sequence tagging through a memory network, demonstrating better generalization with limited training data
2. Demonstrated effectiveness on the ATIS benchmark, particularly with small training datasets
3. Generalization to different linguistic knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring schema modifications

Strengths:
1. The model shows significant improvements on the Small dataset (1/40 training data), with a 5.5% F1 point gain over baselines. This demonstrates clear generalization benefits when training data is scarce, a valuable contribution for real-world NLU systems where labeled data is limited.
2. The architecture is relatively simple and practical, with clear implementation details that could facilitate adoption in industry settings. The model's ability to work with different linguistic resources (dependency trees, AMR) without major architectural changes is particularly noteworthy.
3. The joint RNN tagger effectively balances chain-based and knowledge-guided information, showing robust performance across different training data sizes. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the Large dataset while maintaining strong performance on Small data.

Weaknesses:
1. The experimental comparison lacks sufficient detail about how baseline models (TreeRNN, DCNN) were implemented. It's unclear whether these baselines used their substructure embeddings independently or in the same way as K-SAN, making the comparison potentially unfair. The paper should clarify how the baseline models were configured for fair comparison.
2. The use of two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) appears primarily to increase model capacity rather than for architectural necessity. The authors should provide justification for why this dual-RNN approach is better than a single RNN with additional knowledge input, and demonstrate that the model isn't simply benefiting from more parameters.
3. The paper's use of "knowledge" is misleading, as it refers exclusively to linguistic structures (syntax/semantics) rather than external knowledge bases or world knowledge. This misnomer could confuse readers about the model's actual scope and contributions.
4. The paper fails to address the model's sensitivity to parser errors, which is particularly concerning given the heavy reliance on dependency parsing. The model's performance could be significantly impacted by parsing inaccuracies, yet no analysis of this issue is provided.

Questions to Authors:
1. How were the baseline models (TreeRNN, DCNN) configured to provide fair comparison for the substructure embedding component? Specifically, did they use their own substructure embeddings or were they modified to use the same embeddings as K-SAN?
2. Why was a dual-RNN architecture chosen instead of a single RNN with knowledge incorporated through additional input? What evidence supports that the dual-RNN approach is necessary rather than simply adding capacity?
3. How does the model perform when using a more accurate dependency parser (e.g., SyntaxNet instead of Stanford Parser)? Given the paper's reliance on parsing, this analysis would strengthen the claims about the model's robustness.

The paper presents a practical approach for incorporating linguistic structure into sequence tagging, with demonstrated improvements on the ATIS benchmark, particularly with limited training data. While the core idea of using structured information for attention is not entirely novel, the implementation is clear and the results on small datasets are compelling. However, the lack of detail on baseline implementation, the misleading use of "knowledge," and the unaddressed sensitivity to parser errors significantly weaken the paper's contribution claims. The model's performance on the Large dataset is strong, but the paper would benefit from addressing these concerns to better demonstrate the novelty and robustness of the approach.
