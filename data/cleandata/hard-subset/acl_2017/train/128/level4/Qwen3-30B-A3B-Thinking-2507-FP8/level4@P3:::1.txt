

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture for slot filling in natural language understanding (NLU) that incorporates linguistic structure (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs) via an attention mechanism. The authors claim three contributions: (1) end-to-end integration of prior knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention, (2) generalization across diverse knowledge sources (dependency relations, semantic graphs), and (3) efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling. As a reviewer, I see the strongest contribution as the *practical demonstration of improved generalization on small datasets*—a critical challenge in NLU. The second most significant contribution is the *empirical validation of robustness across knowledge sources*, though the "generalization" claim is somewhat overstated. The third contribution, efficiency, is weakly supported and not a primary novelty.

**Strengths**  
1. **Strong empirical evidence for generalization on scarce data**: K-SAN achieves statistically significant improvements (p < 0.05) over baselines on small datasets (1/40 training data), with F1 gains of 5.1% (Small) and 2.4% (Medium). This addresses a key practical limitation in NLU where labeled data is scarce. The attention visualization (Fig. 5) convincingly shows the model learns meaningful substructure salience even with limited training data, e.g., correctly identifying "morning" as an arrival time slot.  
2. **Robustness across knowledge sources**: The model achieves consistent performance across dependency parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and semantic graphs (AMR), with minimal variation in results (Table 2). This demonstrates the framework's flexibility beyond a single linguistic representation, a valuable trait for real-world systems.  
3. **Clear, interpretable attention mechanism**: The attention weights (Fig. 5) align with semantic roles (e.g., higher weights on "from seattle" for origin slots), providing intuitive insights into how the model leverages structure. This interpretability is a significant practical advantage over black-box baselines.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Unclear baseline comparisons**: The paper compares K-SAN to structural baselines (Tree-RNN, DCNN) but fails to clarify whether these baselines use *identical substructure encodings* as K-SAN. For example, Tree-RNN and DCNN are originally designed for sentence embedding, but it's unclear if they extract substructure embeddings *independently* for attention (as K-SAN does) or use them differently. Without this, the performance gains could simply reflect model capacity differences, not architectural innovation.  
2. **Unjustified two-RNN design**: The model uses separate chain-based and knowledge-guided RNNs (Eq. 8–11), but the only difference is adding a "knowledge" vector to the input. This increases parameters without clear justification, as the same effect could be achieved with a single RNN and a feature concatenation. The paper does not report parameter counts or ablate this design choice, making it unclear if the gains stem from the attention mechanism or extra capacity.  
3. **Misleading "knowledge" framing**: The paper treats dependency parses and AMR as "knowledge," but this is linguistically narrow (not external knowledge bases like Freebase). This misalignment with standard NLP terminology risks confusion. The authors claim generalization to "different knowledge," but the model still requires *sequence-based* substructures (e.g., paths in trees), making it inapplicable to non-sequence knowledge (e.g., constituent parses).  
4. **Lack of parser error analysis**: No experiments test how parser inaccuracies (e.g., Stanford's dependency errors in Fig. 5) affect performance. Given that dependency parsing is a prerequisite, this is a critical gap for real-world deployment.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How do Tree-RNN and DCNN baselines encode substructures for the knowledge-guided attention? Are they using the *same* substructure embeddings as K-SAN, or are they repurposed for sentence embedding only?  
2. What are the parameter counts for K-SAN vs. baselines (e.g., Tree-RNN, DCNN)? Without this, it's impossible to assess whether performance gains stem from the attention mechanism or model capacity.  
3. How would the model handle non-sequence knowledge (e.g., constituency trees where substructures aren't linear sequences)? Could you provide a concrete example?  
4. What is the impact of dependency parsing errors on K-SAN's performance? For instance, in Fig. 5, the parser mislabels "from" as a relation—does this degrade the model's attention?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper's core idea (attention over linguistic substructures) is not novel (e.g., Liu et al. 2013, Ma et al. 2015a), but the implementation is practical. However, the framing as "knowledge-guided" is misleading, and the lack of baseline transparency undermines the contribution. The results are strong for the ATIS dataset but require more rigorous comparison to be convincing for ACL.
