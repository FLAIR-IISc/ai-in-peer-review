

This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling within the natural language understanding component of conversation systems. Substructures (e.g., nodes in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The primary contribution lies in a simple method to "flatten" structured information into a vector array (the memory), which is then integrated with the tagger as supplementary knowledge. This approach resembles structured/syntax-based attention (e.g., attention over treeLSTM nodes), with related work including Zhao et al. (textual entailment), Liu et al. (natural language inference), and Eriguchi et al. (machine translation). The substructure encoder is analogous to DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. While not entirely novel, the architecture presents a straightforward and practical alternative to prior work.

-----Weaknesses-----
The empirical results are not fully convincing, primarily due to insufficient baseline details. Key concerns, ranked by importance:
- The model comprises sentence embedding and substructure embedding components. Table 1 lists TreeRNN and DCNN as baselines, which were originally designed for sentence embedding; however, it is unclear how these models are used to compute the two components (e.g., whether substructure embeddings are extracted independently).
- The model employs two RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided), differing only in the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory to the input (Equations 5 and 8). Using separate weights for the two RNNs appears unnecessary, as the sole advantage is increased model capacity (more parameters). Baseline hyperparameters and network sizes must be comparable to ensure fair parameter counts.
- A baseline that inputs additional knowledge (e.g., word heads, NER results) as features should be included for comparison.
- The model's sensitivity to parser errors is not addressed.

Comments on the model:
- After computing substructure embeddings, it is natural to compute word-specific attention over them. The static attention used for all words seems suboptimal; the current "knowledge" mechanism functions more like a filter for important words, justifying the inclusion of the feature-input baseline mentioned above.
- Since weights for words are computed via the inner product of sentence and substructure embeddings (both generated by the same RNN/CNN), nodes/phrases similar to the entire sentence receive higher weights—likely resulting in all leaf nodes being prioritized.
- The claim of generalization to diverse knowledge sources is questionable; the substructure must be represented as a sequence of words (e.g., using constituent parse as knowledge appears non-trivial).
- The term "knowledge" is misleading, as it typically denotes external world knowledge (e.g., knowledge bases), whereas here it refers solely to syntax (or potentially semantics if AMR parsing is used).

-----General Discussion-----
This paper proposes a practical model that performs well on one dataset, but the core ideas lack novelty (as noted in Strengths). For an ACL publication, stronger takeaways are expected. Crucially, the experiments are currently unconvincing due to insufficient detail. Clarification is required to properly evaluate the results.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors failed to address my primary concern: whether baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and joint tagger. A major unresolved issue remains the use of two separate RNNs, which increases parameter count compared to baselines. I maintain my original scores.
