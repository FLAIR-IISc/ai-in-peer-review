- Summary: Proposes K-SAN, a neural architecture that encodes linguistic substructures (dependency relations, AMR) as vector memories and integrates them via attention for slot filling.
- Contribution 1: Practical method to incorporate structured linguistic information into sequence tagging using memory networks.
- Contribution 2: Demonstrates generalization across different knowledge representations (dependency trees and AMR graphs).
- Contribution 3: Shows improved performance, especially with limited training data.
- Strength: Well-motivated, practical, and effective approach for leveraging linguistic structure in sequence tagging.
- Strength: Empirical gains on ATIS, notably strong in low-resource settings relevant to real-world applications.
- Strength: Can utilize different knowledge sources without requiring a specific schema, increasing applicability.
- Weakness: No clear comparison to baselines that use the same substructure embeddings as features, leaving the architectural benefit uncertain.
- Weakness: Uses two separate RNNs with different weights, increasing capacity without justifying whether gains stem from architecture or extra parameters.
- Weakness: Lacks analysis of sensitivity to parser errors despite reliance on external parsing tools.
- Weakness: Terminology misleadingly labels syntactic/semantic structures as “knowledge” rather than external/world knowledge.
- Question 1: Clarify how substructure embeddings for baselines (Tree-RNN, DCNN) were computed and whether they match K-SAN’s usage.
- Question 2: Explain how baseline hyperparameters (hidden sizes, parameter counts) were chosen to ensure fair comparison.
- Question 3: Provide results assessing robustness to parser errors (performance under different parsing inaccuracies).
- Question 4: Explain generalization to other structured knowledge types (e.g., constituency trees) beyond dependency and AMR.
