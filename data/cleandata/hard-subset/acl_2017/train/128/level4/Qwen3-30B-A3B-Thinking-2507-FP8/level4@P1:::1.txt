

This paper proposes a neural network architecture that incorporates structured linguistic knowledge into a memory network for sequence tagging tasks (specifically, slot-filling in natural language understanding modules for conversational systems). The approach encodes substructures (e.g., nodes in a parse tree) as vectors (memory slots) and feeds a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings into an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

Strengths
The main contribution of this paper is its practical approach to "flattening" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then connected to the tagger as additional knowledge. While the core idea resembles structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms (e.g., attention over nodes from Tree-LSTM), the implementation is notably simpler than prior work. The proposed substructure encoder bears similarity to DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. The architecture itself is not entirely novel, but the authors present a clean and practical implementation that could benefit from being more widely adopted in the community.

Weaknesses
I remain unconvinced by the empirical results due to insufficient baseline details. The following concerns are ranked by importance:

First, the model comprises two main components: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. Table 1 compares to baseline models (Tree-RNN and DCNN) that were originally designed for sentence embedding. However, it's unclear how these baselines would compute the two components. Specifically, Tree-RNN and DCNN could easily provide substructure embeddings independently of sentence embedding, but this isn't clearly addressed.

Second, the model employs two separate RNNs: a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one. The only difference in the knowledge-guided RNN is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory in the input (Equations 5 and 8). This design seems unnecessary, as the same effect could be achieved with a single RNN. The only advantage appears to be increased model capacity (more parameters), but the paper doesn't establish that the baselines used comparable network sizes or parameter counts.

Third, it would be more appropriate to include a baseline that simply inputs additional knowledge as features to the RNN (e.g., head words, NER results). This would better isolate the contribution of the proposed architecture from the additional knowledge itself.

Fourth, the paper should address the model's sensitivity to parser errors. Given that the approach relies heavily on dependency parsing, it's important to understand how robust the method is to parsing inaccuracies.

Fifth, the attention mechanism applies a single, static distribution across all words, which seems suboptimal. After computing substructure embeddings, it would be natural to compute word-specific attention over these substructures. The current approach appears to treat "knowledge" more as a filter to mark important words rather than as a dynamic attention mechanism.

Sixth, the embedding mechanism raises a concern: since weights are computed via the inner product of the sentence embedding and substructure embedding, and both are generated by the same RNN/CNN, substructures similar to the whole sentence will naturally receive higher weights (e.g., all leaf nodes). This might undermine the model's ability to distinguish relevant substructures.

Finally, the term "knowledge" is misleading. While the paper claims generalization to different knowledge sources, the approach fundamentally relies on syntactic or semantic structures (dependency trees or AMR graphs), not external knowledge bases containing world knowledge about entities. This misnomer could confuse readers.

General Discussion
This paper presents a practical model that performs well on the ATIS benchmark, but the main ideas lack sufficient novelty for an ACL paper. The approach builds on existing work with some clever implementation details, but the paper doesn't provide compelling new insights. More importantly, the experimental evaluation is insufficiently rigorous, particularly regarding baseline comparisons and parameter counts. Without addressing these concerns, it's difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method.

Post-rebuttal
The authors failed to adequately address my main concerns. Specifically, they didn't clarify whether the baselines (Tree-RNN, DCNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of sentence embedding and the joint tagger. They also didn't provide a proper parameter count comparison to justify the use of two separate RNNs. Therefore, I maintain my original assessment and will not change my score.
