

This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling in natural language understanding units of conversational systems. Substructures (e.g., nodes in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of substructure embeddings is fed into an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.  

-----Strengths-----  
The primary contribution is a simple method to "flatten" structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then connected to the tagger as supplementary knowledge. The approach resembles structured/syntax-based attention (e.g., attention over treeLSTM nodes), with related work including Zhao et al. (textual entailment), Liu et al. (natural language inference), and Eriguchi et al. (machine translation). The substructure encoder parallels DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. While not entirely novel, I appreciate the practical simplicity compared to prior work.  

-----Weaknesses-----  
I am unconvinced by the empirical results, primarily due to insufficient baseline details. Key concerns, ranked by importance:  
- The model comprises sentence embedding and substructure embedding components. Table 1 lists TreeRNN and DCNN as baselines, which were originally designed for sentence embedding but could also provide substructure embeddings. It is unclear how these baselines are adapted to compute the two components.  
- The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. The sole difference in the knowledge-guided RNN is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory in the input (Equations 5 and 8). Separate weights for the two RNNs appear unnecessary; the only advantage is increased model capacity (more parameters). Baseline hyperparameters and network sizes must be comparable (e.g., parameter counts).  
- A baseline that inputs additional knowledge as features (e.g., word heads, NER results) should be included.  
- The paper does not address the model’s sensitivity to parser errors.  

Additional comments:  
- After computing substructure embeddings, it is natural to compute attention over them per word. Using a static attention for all words seems suboptimal; the current "knowledge" mechanism functions more as a filter for important words, justifying the omitted feature-input baseline.  
- Since node weights are computed via the inner product of sentence and substructure embeddings (both generated by the same RNN/CNN), nodes/phrases similar to the whole sentence receive higher weights (e.g., all leaf nodes).  
- The claim that the model generalizes to different knowledge sources is questionable, as the substructure representation requires a word sequence (e.g., constituent parsing appears incompatible).  
- The term "knowledge" is misleading; it typically denotes external world knowledge (e.g., knowledge bases), whereas this model uses syntax (or semantics via AMR), not external knowledge.  

-----General Discussion-----  
This paper presents a practical model that performs well on one dataset, but the core ideas lack novelty (as noted in Strengths). For an ACL publication, stronger takeaways are needed. Crucially, the experiments are unconvincing as presented; clarification is required to fairly evaluate the results.  

-----Post-rebuttal-----  
The authors failed to address my primary concern: whether baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) compute substructure embeddings independently of sentence embedding and the joint tagger. They also did not resolve my major issue regarding the unnecessary dual-RNN architecture, which increases parameters without clear justification. I maintain my original score.
