

Summary: The paper proposes a neural network architecture that leverages structured linguistic knowledge (e.g., dependency relations, AMR graphs) to improve slot filling in natural language understanding systems. The authors introduce Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), which encode substructures from linguistic knowledge as vector memories and use attention mechanisms to integrate this knowledge into sequence tagging. The main contributions as I see them are: 1) A practical method for incorporating structured linguistic information into sequence tagging through memory networks; 2) The ability to generalize across different types of knowledge representations (dependency trees, AMR graphs); 3) A model that shows improved performance with limited training data.

Strengths: The paper presents a well-motivated approach to incorporating linguistic structure into sequence tagging that is both practical and effective. The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements on the ATIS benchmark, particularly with small training datasets, which is valuable for real-world applications where labeled data is scarce. The model's ability to leverage different knowledge sources (dependency parsing, AMR) without requiring specific schema is a practical advantage that could be beneficial for various NLU applications.

Weaknesses: The main weakness is the lack of clear comparison with baselines that use the same substructure embeddings as features, making it difficult to determine if the proposed architecture provides advantages beyond simply using substructure embeddings as additional features. The paper uses two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) with different weights, which increases model capacity without clear justification, making it unclear whether the performance gains are due to the knowledge integration or simply more parameters. The paper also fails to analyze the model's sensitivity to parser errors, which is critical since the method relies on external parsing tools. Additionally, the paper's use of "knowledge" is misleading as it primarily refers to syntactic and semantic structures rather than external knowledge bases or world knowledge.

Questions to Authors: 
1. Please clarify how the substructure embeddings used in the baseline models (Tree-RNN and DCNN) were computed and whether they were used in the same way as the proposed K-SAN model.
2. How were the hyperparameters (e.g., hidden layer sizes, number of parameters) for the baseline models determined to ensure fair comparison with K-SAN?
3. Could you provide results showing how the model performs with different parser errors to assess robustness to parsing inaccuracies?
4. How would the model generalize to other types of structured knowledge (e.g., constituency trees) beyond the dependency and AMR structures tested in the paper?
