- Proposes a memory-augmented neural tagger that encodes structural linguistic substructures into a vector memory and feeds weighted substructure embeddings as context to an RNN for slot-filling.  
- Strength: practical, simple, and implementation-efficient method to flatten structured information into a memory array for sequence tagging.  
- Strength: provides a clear, actionable approach to incorporate structural knowledge without complex models.  
- Major weakness: baseline ambiguity — unclear how TreeRNN/DCNN baselines produce or use substructure embeddings, preventing fair comparison.  
- Major weakness: redundant dual RNNs — using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) increases parameters unnecessarily; authors omit RNN sizes so parameter-fair baselines cannot be assessed.  
- Major weakness: missing baseline that injects substructure features directly into a standard RNN, which would isolate the memory mechanism’s value.  
- Major weakness: empirical evaluation lacks critical details and hyperparameters, undermining confidence in reported gains.  
- Concern: parser sensitivity is unaddressed; reliance on parser-derived structure risks performance degradation from parser errors.  
- Terminology issue: calling syntactic/structural information “knowledge” is misleading and confuses external KB-style knowledge with syntax.  
- Design critique: attention over substructures is static (same weights for all words), making the “knowledge” vector a global filter rather than a context-aware signal.  
- Unsupported claim: generalization to different “knowledge” types (e.g., AMR) is not demonstrated and unclear for non-span-based structures.  
- General assessment: idea is incremental (builds on existing attention/memory techniques) and lacks stronger theoretical insight or broader applicability for an ACL paper.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors failed to clarify baseline substructure derivation and justify dual-RNN parameter increase; results remain unconvincing.  
- Recommendation: Reject — marginal improvement with insufficient experimental rigor, lack of baseline transparency, parameter fairness issues, and misleading terminology.
