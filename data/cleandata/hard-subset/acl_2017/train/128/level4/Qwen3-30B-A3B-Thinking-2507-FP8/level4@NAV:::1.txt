

This paper introduces a neural network architecture that encodes structural linguistic knowledge into a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling in natural language understanding units of conversational systems. Substructures (e.g., nodes in a parse tree) are represented as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is incorporated as additional context into an RNN at each time step for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The primary contribution lies in a straightforward method to "flatten" structured information into a vector array (the memory), subsequently integrated with the tagger as supplementary knowledge. While conceptually related to structured/syntax-based attention (e.g., attention over treeLSTM nodes) and prior work by Zhao et al. (textual entailment), Liu et al. (natural language inference), and Eriguchi et al. (machine translation), the proposed substructure encoder resembles DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. The architecture lacks full novelty but presents a simple, practical approach relative to existing methods.

-----Weaknesses-----
The empirical results are insufficiently convincing, primarily due to inadequate baseline details. Key concerns, ranked by importance:
- The model comprises sentence embedding and substructure embedding components. Table 1 compares against TreeRNN and DCNN, originally designed for sentence embedding; it remains unclear how these baselines are adapted to provide both embedding components.
- The model employs two RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided), differing only in the inclusion of a "knowledge" vector in the input (Equations 5 and 8). The separation appears unnecessary, as it solely increases model capacity (more parameters) without clear benefit. Baseline networks should have comparable parameter counts.
- A baseline incorporating additional knowledge features (e.g., word heads, NER results) into the RNN should be included.
- Sensitivity to parser errors is not addressed.

Additional model comments:
- After computing substructure embeddings, dynamic attention over them per word would be natural; using a static attention for all words is questionable. This suggests the "knowledge" functions more as a filter, reinforcing the need for the baseline above.
- Given that word weights derive from inner products between sentence embeddings and substructure embeddings (computed by the same RNN/CNN), nodes/phrases similar to the full sentence (e.g., leaf nodes) would inherently receive higher weights.
- The claim of generalization to diverse knowledge sources is questionable, as the substructure representation requires a word sequence (e.g., constituent parsing appears non-trivial).
- The term "knowledge" is misleading; it typically denotes external world knowledge (e.g., knowledge bases), whereas this mechanism only utilizes syntax or limited semantics (e.g., via AMR parsing).

-----General Discussion-----
This work proposes a practical model effective on one dataset, but the core ideas lack novelty (as noted in Strengths). For an ACL publication, more substantial contributions are expected. Crucially, the experimental evaluation is unconvincing in its current form and requires clarification to assess validity.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors failed to address the primary concern: whether baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of sentence embedding and the joint tagger. The issue of unnecessary parameter increase from dual RNNs also remains unaddressed. Consequently, the review score remains unchanged.
