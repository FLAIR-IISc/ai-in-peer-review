This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structured linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks (specifically, slot-filling of natural language understanding units in conversation systems). Substructures (such as nodes in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors (memory slots) and fed into an RNN at each step as an additional context for weighted sum labeling of substrate embedding.

-----Strength-----
I believe the main contribution of this paper is "flattering" structured information into an array of vectors (memory) in a simple way, which is then attached to the tagger as additional knowledge. The concept is similar to structured/syntax-based attention (such as attention to nodes from tree LSTMs); related work includes Zhao et al.'s textual entailment, Liu et al.'s work on natural language inference, and Eriyuchi et al.'s work on machine translation. The proposed substrate encoder is similar to DCNNs (Ma et al.): each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. The architecture does not seem entirely new, but I prefer the simple and practical approach compared to previous work.

-----Weaknesses-----
I am not very satisfied with the empirical results, mainly due to the lack of detailed descriptions of the baselines. The comments below are sorted by importance.
- The proposed model has two main parts: sentence embedding and substrate embedding. In Table 1, the baseline models are tree RNNs and DCNNs, which are mainly used for sentence embedding, but one can easily take node/substrate embedding from them. It is not clear how these are used to calculate the two parts.
- The model uses two RNNs: one chain-based and one knowledge-directed. The only difference between knowledge-directed RNNs is that a "knowledge" vector is added to the RNN input from memory (actions 5 and 8). Having separate weights for the two RNNs seems completely unnecessary to me. The only advantage of using two is the increase in the model's capacity, i.e., more parameters.
Furthermore, what are the hyper-parameters/sizes of the baseline neural networks? Their number of parameters should be comparable.
- I think it is reasonable to include a baseline that inputs additional knowledge as a feature of the RNN, such as the head of each word, NER results, etc.
- Any comments/results on the model's sensitivity to parser errors?
Comments on the model:
- After computing substrate embedding, it seems very natural to compute their attention on each word. Is there any reason to use a static attention for all words? I think since it is working as a filter to identify "knowledge" important words. So it is reasonable to include the baseline proposed above, i.e., input additional features.
- Since the weight of a word is calculated by the internal product of sentence embedding and substrate embedding, and the two embeddings are calculated by the same RNN/CNN, does this mean that nodes/phrases like the whole sentence get higher weights, i.e., all leaf nodes?
- The paper claims that the model generalizes for different knowledge, but I think substrate should be represented as a sequence of words, such as using the component parse as knowledge here does not seem simple to me.
Finally, I hesitate to call it "knowledge". It is misleading because it is usually used to mean a knowledge base of entities like world/external knowledge, whereas here it is really syntax, or arguably semantics if AMR parsing is used.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper proposes a practical model that seems to work well on a dataset, but the core concepts are not very new (see comments in strengths). I think it should be more of a takeaway as an ACL paper. More importantly, the experiments are not convincing as they are presented now. Some clarification will be needed to better judge the results.

-----Revival-----
The authors have not addressed my main concerns, which are whether the baselines (such as tree RNNs) are used to calculate substrate embedding independently of sentence embedding and joint tagging. Another major concern is using two separate RNNs that give the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. So I am not changing my score.
