- Paper proposes a neural architecture that encodes parse substructures as memory vectors and injects a weighted sum of these embeddings into an RNN for sequence tagging (slot-filling).

- Strength: simple, practical method for representing structured information as an array of vectors and using it to enhance a tagger.

- Strength: approach resembles existing structured/syntax-based attention methods and substructure encoders (e.g., treeLSTMs, DCNN), making it a straightforward alternative to more complex prior work.

- Weakness: empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient detail about how baselines were implemented and adapted.

- Weakness: unclear whether baselines (TreeRNN, DCNN) produced separate substructure embeddings comparable to the proposed model, raising fairness concerns.

- Weakness: using two separate RNNs (chain RNN + knowledge-guided RNN) may be unnecessary and primarily increases model capacity; hyperparameters and parameter counts are not reported for fairness comparison.

- Weakness: missing baseline that directly adds linguistic features (e.g., word heads, NER tags) to the RNN input to serve as a simpler comparison.

- Weakness: no analysis of robustness to parser errors or performance with imperfect parses.

- Comment: applying dynamic attention over substructure embeddings at each word step seems more intuitive than the current static attention, which functions more like a global filter.

- Comment: the weighting mechanism (inner product between sentence and substructure embeddings) may overweight substructures similar to the overall sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).

- Comment: claim of generalizing to different knowledge types is questionable because the method requires substructures to be representable as word sequences, limiting incorporation of other knowledge sources.

- Terminology: calling the structured information “knowledge” is misleading; it is more accurately syntactic or semantic information derived from parsing.

- General: work is practical and shows positive results on one dataset but lacks groundbreaking contribution and broader insights expected for a top-tier ACL paper.

- Post-rebuttal: authors did not address core concerns about baseline substructure embeddings and the two-RNN capacity issue, so reviewer maintained original scores.
