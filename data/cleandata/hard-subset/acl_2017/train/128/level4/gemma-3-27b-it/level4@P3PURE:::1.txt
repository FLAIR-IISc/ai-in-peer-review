This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling within the natural language understanding component of conversational systems. Substructures (e.g., a node in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into a recurrent neural network (RNN) at each time step as additional contextual information for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The main contribution of this paper is a straightforward method for “flattening” structured information into an array of vectors (the memory) and integrating it as additional knowledge into a tagger. This idea is related to structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms (e.g., attention over nodes from TreeLSTMs). Relevant prior work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder resembles the Deep Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN) of Ma et al., embedding each node from a sequence of ancestor words. While the architecture isn't entirely novel, its simplicity and practicality are appealing compared to existing approaches.

-----Weaknesses-----
I find the empirical results unconvincing, primarily due to a lack of detail regarding the baselines. The following comments are ranked by decreasing importance.

- The proposed model comprises two main components: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. Table 1 presents TreeRNN and DCNN as baselines; however, these models are originally designed for sentence embedding. It’s unclear how they were adapted to compute substructure embeddings for a fair comparison.
- The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. The sole distinction between the two is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory to the input of the knowledge-guided RNN (Eqn 5 and 8). This appears unnecessary; maintaining separate weights for the two RNNs seems solely to increase model capacity, i.e., the number of parameters. The hyper-parameter settings and sizes of the baseline neural networks should be reported to ensure a comparable parameter count.
- Including a baseline that incorporates additional knowledge as features directly into the RNN would be reasonable. Examples include the head of each word, or Named Entity Recognition (NER) results.
- The paper would benefit from discussion, and ideally results, regarding the model’s sensitivity to parser errors.

Comments on the model:
- After computing the substructure embeddings, applying attention over them at each word seems natural. Is there a specific reason for using a static attention weight for all words? As currently implemented, the “knowledge” appears to function more as a filter to highlight important words, making the suggested baseline – incorporating additional features – particularly relevant.
- Since the weight assigned to a word is calculated using the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and given that both embeddings are computed by the same RNN/CNN, does this mean that nodes/phrases similar to the overall sentence receive higher weights, potentially favoring leaf nodes?
- The paper claims the model generalizes to different knowledge sources, but the substructure representation relies on a sequence of words. It’s not immediately apparent how to apply this approach to knowledge represented in other formats, such as constituent parses.
- The term "knowledge" is potentially misleading. It typically refers to world/external knowledge, such as knowledge base entities. In this work, it represents syntax, or potentially semantics if AMR parsing is used.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper presents a practical model that performs well on a single dataset, but the core ideas are not substantially novel (see comments in Strengths). I believe an ACL paper should offer more significant insights. Critically, the experiments, as currently presented, are not conclusive. Clarification is needed to properly evaluate the results.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors did not address my primary concern: whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another key concern remains the use of two separate RNNs, which introduces a parameter advantage for the proposed model compared to the baselines. Therefore, I am maintaining my original scores.
