- Proposes a neural architecture that encodes parse substructures as memory vectors and integrates a weighted sum into an RNN for slot-filling.
- Strength: simple, practical method for “flattening” structured information into a memory array and using it as supplemental knowledge for tagging.
- Relates to structured/syntax-based attention work and builds on prior models (Zhao et al., Liu et al., Eriguchi et al.); substructure encoder resembles DCNN ancestor-word embeddings.
- Architecture is not highly novel but is appealing for its simplicity and practicality.
- Empirical validation is unconvincing due to insufficient detail about baseline models.
- Unclear how TreeRNN and DCNN baselines were adapted to produce node/substructure embeddings for fair comparison.
- Uses two RNNs (chain and knowledge-guided) whose only difference is an added “knowledge” vector; justification for separate weights is unclear and likely increases parameter count.
- Missing hyperparameter details and baseline model sizes to verify parameter comparability.
- Should include a baseline that incorporates structural knowledge directly as RNN features (e.g., head words, NER tags).
- No analysis of model robustness to errors in the underlying parser.
- Rationale for using static attention over substructure embeddings across all words is unclear; current mechanism may simply act as a filter.
- Weighting via inner product of sentence and substructure embeddings may bias toward nodes similar to the sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).
- Claim of generality to different knowledge sources is questionable since substructure representations rely on sequential word representations.
- The term “knowledge” is misleading; the paper uses syntactic/semantic information rather than external/world knowledge.
- Overall contribution is incremental for a venue like ACL, and the experimental results need more clarification and stronger validation.
- Post-rebuttal concern remains: authors did not show whether baselines computed independent substructure embeddings, and the two-RNN design still raises parameter-count concerns.
