This paper introduces a neural network architecture that integrates structural linguistic information into a memory network for sequence tagging, specifically targeting slot-filling within natural language understanding components of conversational systems. The approach encodes linguistic substructures – such as nodes within a parse tree – as vectors (memory slots), and incorporates a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings as contextual information into a recurrent neural network (RNN) at each time step, aiding in the tagging process.

**Strengths:**

The primary contribution of this work lies in its straightforward method for representing structured information as an array of vectors (the memory) and utilizing it to enhance a tagger. This concept shares similarities with structured or syntax-based attention mechanisms, like attention over treeLSTM nodes. Related work includes contributions from Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. in machine translation. The substructure encoder bears resemblance to the Deep Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN) proposed by Ma et al., leveraging embeddings derived from sequences of ancestor words for each node. While the architecture isn’t radically novel, its simplicity and practical application are appealing compared to more complex prior approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

The empirical results presented are not entirely convincing, largely due to insufficient detail regarding the baseline models. The following points, listed in order of importance, detail my concerns:

*   The paper compares against TreeRNN and DCNN (Table 1). These models are typically employed for sentence embedding, but can also readily generate node or substructure embeddings. It is unclear whether and how the baselines were adapted to produce comparable substructure representations for a fair comparison. Specifically, it’s vital to understand if baselines generate separate embeddings for substructures and the overall sentence, or if they rely on a single embedding for both.
*   The model employs two separate RNNs: a standard chain-based RNN and a “knowledge-guided” RNN. The only distinction between these RNNs is the addition of a “knowledge” vector derived from the memory (Eqn 5 and 8) to the knowledge-guided RNN’s input. This separation appears unnecessary; maintaining separate weights for the two RNNs seems to primarily increase model capacity (number of parameters) without clear justification. Providing hyperparameter details and parameter counts for all neural networks, including the baselines, is crucial to assess fairness.
*   The inclusion of a baseline that directly incorporates linguistic features (e.g., word heads, named entity recognition (NER) tags) into the RNN input as additional features would provide a valuable point of comparison.
*   The paper lacks discussion regarding the model’s robustness to parser errors. An analysis of performance degradation with imperfect parse trees would strengthen the evaluation.

**Further Comments on the Model:**

*   Given the substructure embeddings, implementing attention over these embeddings at each word step seems intuitive. The current approach employs a static attention scheme across all words. This effectively treats the “knowledge” as a filter highlighting important words. Consequently, a baseline integrating features, as suggested above, would be particularly relevant.
*   The weighting mechanism, based on the inner product between sentence and substructure embeddings, raises a concern that nodes or phrases highly similar to the overall sentence (e.g., leaf nodes) may receive disproportionately high weights.
*   The claim of generalizing to different knowledge types is questionable. The current implementation requires substructures to be represented as sequences of words, making it less straightforward to incorporate knowledge sources like constituent parses. 

*Terminology:* The term "knowledge" may be misleading. While the model utilizes structural information, it’s more accurately described as syntactic or semantic information (depending on parsing method – e.g., AMR parsing) rather than external, world-based knowledge typically associated with knowledge bases and entities.

**General Discussion:**

This paper presents a practical model that demonstrates positive results on a single dataset. However, the core ideas are not particularly groundbreaking. For an ACL publication, a stronger contribution or broader insights would be expected. More importantly, the experiments, as currently presented, are not fully convincing and require clarification to accurately evaluate the results.

**Post-Rebuttal:**

The authors failed to address my core concern regarding the baselines, specifically whether TreeRNN (and similar models) were used to independently compute substructure embeddings separate from the sentence embedding and the overall tagging process. My second significant concern – the use of two separate RNNs leading to a larger model capacity compared to the baselines – also remained unaddressed.
