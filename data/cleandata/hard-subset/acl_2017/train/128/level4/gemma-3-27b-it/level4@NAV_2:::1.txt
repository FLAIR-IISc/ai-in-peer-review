This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks (in particular, slot-filling of the natural language understanding unit in conversation systems). Substructures (e.g., a node in the parse tree) are encoded as a vector (a memory slot) and a weighted sum of the substructure embeddings are fed into a RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The primary contribution of this paper lies in a straightforward method for integrating structured information into a neural network via a flattened vector representation (the memory), which is then utilized as additional knowledge within a tagger. This approach shares similarities with structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms found in related work, such as Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder resembles DCNN (Ma et al.) in its embedding of each node from a sequence of ancestor words. While the architecture isn't entirely novel, its simplicity and practicality offer a compelling advantage compared to existing approaches.

-----Weaknesses-----
I have reservations regarding the empirical results, largely due to a lack of detailed information regarding the baselines. The following points are ranked by decreasing importance:

- The proposed model comprises two key components: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baseline models (TreeRNN and DCNN) are fundamentally designed for sentence embedding; however, it’s readily apparent that node/substructure embeddings can be extracted from them as well. The methodology for leveraging these models to compute the two components within the comparison remains unclear.
- The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. The sole distinction in the knowledge-guided RNN is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from the memory to the RNN input (Eqn 5 and 8). This addition appears superfluous, offering no apparent benefit beyond increasing model capacity – i.e., the number of parameters.
- Including a baseline that directly incorporates additional knowledge as features to the RNN (e.g., the head of each word, NER results) would provide a valuable point of comparison.
- Further investigation into the model’s sensitivity to parser errors is needed.

Comments on the model:

- After computing the substructure embeddings, employing an attention mechanism *over* them at each word seems natural. Is there a rationale for using a static attention weight for all words? As it stands, the "knowledge" appears to function more as a filter to highlight important words. This reinforces the need for the baseline suggested above – incorporating additional features.
- Given that the weight assigned to a word is determined by the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and considering that both embeddings are computed using the same RNN/CNN, wouldn’t this prioritize nodes/phrases similar to the overall sentence, potentially favoring leaf nodes?
- While the paper asserts the model generalizes to different knowledge sources, the substructure representation inherently relies on a sequential arrangement of words. This raises questions about the straightforward application of, for example, constituent parsing as knowledge.

Finally, the term "knowledge" may be misleading. It typically refers to world/external knowledge such as a knowledge base of entities, whereas here it represents syntax, or potentially semantics if AMR parsing is used.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper presents a practical model demonstrating positive results on a specific dataset. However, the core ideas aren’t profoundly novel (as outlined in the Strengths section). For an ACL-level paper, more substantial takeaways are expected. Critically, the experiments, as currently presented, are not entirely convincing and require further clarification to allow for a thorough assessment.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors did not adequately address my primary concern: whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. A major concern remains the use of two separate RNNs, which inherently provides the proposed model with more parameters than the baselines. Consequently, I am maintaining my initial evaluation.
