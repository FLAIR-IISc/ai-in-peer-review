- Contribution: Proposes a simple method to flatten syntactic substructures into a memory array and incorporate them into an RNN tagger for slot-filling.
- Strength: Conceptually similar to structured/syntax-based attention and related work, but simpler and potentially more practical than complex alternatives.
- Weakness: Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient detail about baseline configurations.
- Baseline clarity: Unclear whether baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN, DCNN) were used to produce comparable substructure/node embeddings.
- RNN architecture concern: Using two separate RNNs (chain and knowledge-guided) is not justified and likely inflates parameter count without clear benefit.
- Model comparison: Hyperparameters and sizes of baseline networks are not reported, preventing fair complexity comparison.
- Missing baseline: No strong feature-based baseline (e.g., adding phrase head words or NER features directly to the RNN) is provided.
- Parser robustness: No analysis of sensitivity to parser errors or noisy parse trees.
- Static vs dynamic attention: Using static attention over substructures for all words is questionable; per-word (dynamic) attention is a natural extension.
- Embedding bias: Sharing the same encoder for sentence and substructure embeddings may bias attention toward nodes resembling the sentence or leaf nodes.
- Generalization claim weak: Method’s generalization to other knowledge sources is unclear because it requires substructures represented as word sequences.
- Terminology issue: Calling syntactic/semantic parse information “knowledge” is misleading given common NLP usage of the term.
- Overall assessment: Practical approach with positive single-dataset results but lacks strong novelty for a top-tier venue and needs clearer experimental validation.
- Post-rebuttal: Authors failed to resolve baseline and dual-RNN parameter concerns; reviewer’s original evaluation remains unchanged.
