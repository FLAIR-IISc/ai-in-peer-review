This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks (in particular, slot-filling within the natural language understanding unit of conversational systems). Substructures (e.g., a node in the parse tree) are encoded as vectors (a memory slot), and a weighted sum of the substructure embeddings is fed into an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

-----Strengths-----

I believe the primary contribution of this paper lies in its straightforward approach to integrating structured information into a neural network for sequence tagging. The method effectively “flattens” structural information into an array of vectors, representing a memory, which is then connected to the tagger as additional knowledge. This concept shares similarities with structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms (e.g., attention over nodes from treeLSTMs), and relates to work by Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder resembles DCNN (Ma et al.), where each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. While the architecture isn’t entirely novel, I appreciate its simplicity and practicality compared to previous, more complex approaches. A secondary, but notable contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of leveraging readily available linguistic structures, such as dependency parses and AMR graphs, for improving NLU performance.

-----Weaknesses-----

I have some concerns regarding the empirical evaluation, stemming from a lack of detailed information about the baselines. These concerns are ranked by decreasing importance.

*   The proposed model incorporates both sentence embedding and substructure embedding components. However, in Table 1, it’s unclear how the baseline models (TreeRNN and DCNN) are utilized to compute these two parts independently. These models are typically used for sentence embedding, but can readily provide node/substructure embeddings as well. The paper needs to clarify whether these baselines are being used to generate independent substructure embeddings and then integrated into the joint tagger.
*   The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. The sole difference in the knowledge-guided RNN is the addition of a “knowledge” vector from the memory into the RNN input (Equation 5 and 8). This seems unnecessary, as it primarily increases model capacity (i.e., introduces more parameters) without a clear justification. It’s unclear why separate weights are used for the two RNNs.
*   I recommend including a baseline that directly incorporates additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as the head of each word or Named Entity Recognition (NER) results. This would provide a stronger point of comparison.
*   What is the model’s sensitivity to parser errors? Results addressing this would be valuable.

Further comments on the model:

*   After computing the substructure embeddings, applying an attention mechanism *over* them at each word step seems more natural than using a static attention for all words. Currently, the “knowledge” seems to act more like a filter for identifying important words. The suggested baseline above (adding features) is relevant here.
*   Since the attention weight for each substructure is computed using the inner product of the sentence and substructure embeddings, and both embeddings are created using the same RNN/CNN, doesn't this naturally favor nodes/phrases similar to the entire sentence (e.g., leaf nodes)?
*   The paper claims the model generalizes to different knowledge sources, but the substructure must currently be represented as a sequence of words. It's not immediately clear how to effectively utilize constituent parses as knowledge in this framework.

Finally, the term "knowledge" might be misleading. It typically refers to world or external knowledge from a knowledge base (e.g., entities), whereas here it is primarily syntactic, or semantic if AMR parsing is used.

-----General Discussion-----

This paper presents a practical model that performs well on a single dataset, but the core ideas are not entirely groundbreaking (as discussed in Strengths). I believe a stronger ACL paper would offer more substantial takeaways. More importantly, the experiments, as currently presented, are not entirely convincing. Clarification regarding the baseline implementations and the rationale for the dual RNN structure is needed for a more thorough evaluation.

-----Post-rebuttal-----

The authors did not adequately address my primary concern: whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another significant issue, the use of two separate RNNs which gives the proposed model a parameter advantage, also remains unresolved. Therefore, I am maintaining my original assessment.
