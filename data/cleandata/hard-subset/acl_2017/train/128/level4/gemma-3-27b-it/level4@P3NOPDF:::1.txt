This paper introduces a neural network architecture designed to integrate structural linguistic knowledge into a memory network for sequence tagging, specifically focusing on slot-filling within natural language understanding components of conversational systems. The approach encodes linguistic substructures—such as nodes in a parse tree—as vectors (memory slots), and incorporates a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings into a recurrent neural network (RNN) at each time step to provide additional context for labeling.

**Strengths**

The primary contribution of this work lies in a relatively straightforward method for “flattening” structured information into a vector array (the memory) and utilizing this as supplemental knowledge for the tagger. This concept shares similarities with structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms, like those employing attention over treeLSTM nodes. Related work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder, leveraging sequential ancestor words to embed nodes, is reminiscent of the Deep Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN) architecture presented by Ma et al. While the architecture isn’t entirely novel, its simplicity and potential practicality are appealing compared to more complex approaches in the existing literature.

**Weaknesses**

The empirical results, as presented, are not entirely convincing, primarily due to insufficient detail regarding the baselines. The following points, ranked by decreasing importance, outline specific concerns:

*   **Baseline Configuration:** The baseline models presented in Table 1 (TreeRNN and DCNN) are typically employed for sentence embedding. However, these models could also provide node/substructure embeddings. The paper does not clarify whether, and how, these baselines were utilized to generate comparable substructure representations for evaluation. A clear delineation of how each model contributes to sentence *and* substructure embeddings is crucial.
*   **RNN Architecture:** The model employs two distinct RNNs: a chain-based RNN and a “knowledge-guided” RNN. The sole difference between these is the inclusion of a “knowledge” vector from the memory in the input to the latter (Equations 5 and 8). The justification for employing separate weights for these RNNs is unclear. This dual-RNN structure appears to primarily increase model capacity (parameter count) without clear benefits. The hyperparameter settings and sizes of the baseline neural networks should be reported to allow for a fair comparison of model complexity.
*   **Feature-Based Baseline:** A strong baseline would involve directly incorporating relevant linguistic knowledge as features into the RNN. For example, including head words of each phrase or named entity recognition (NER) results could provide a valuable comparative point.
*   **Parser Error Sensitivity:** The paper lacks any discussion or results regarding the model’s robustness to errors introduced by the parser. An analysis of performance degradation with noisy parse trees would be beneficial.

**Further Comments on the Model:**

*   **Static vs. Dynamic Attention:** After computing substructure embeddings, applying attention over them at each word seems a natural extension. The paper's use of static attention across all words is questionable. As currently implemented, the “knowledge” seems to function more as a filter identifying important words. Incorporating the suggested feature-based baseline would help clarify this.
*   **Embedding Similarity:** Given that both sentence and substructure embeddings are generated by the same RNN/CNN, and weights are determined by their inner product, nodes or phrases strongly resembling the overall sentence may receive disproportionately high weights (potentially favoring leaf nodes). This potential bias should be investigated.
*   **Generalization to Different Knowledge:** The claim regarding generalization to different knowledge sources is unclear, as the current implementation requires substructures to be represented as sequences of words. Extending the approach to utilize, for instance, constituent parses directly is not straightforward.
*   **Terminology:** The term “knowledge” is potentially misleading. In the context of NLP, “knowledge” often refers to external sources like knowledge bases. Here, it more accurately describes syntactic or potentially semantic information derived from parsing (e.g., via AMR).

**General Discussion**

This paper presents a practical model that demonstrates positive results on a single dataset. However, the core ideas are not particularly innovative. For an ACL paper, a more substantial takeaway is expected. Critically, the experimental setup requires clarification to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. More detailed information about the baselines and model configurations is needed for a thorough assessment.

**Post-Rebuttal**

The authors did not adequately address the primary concerns raised regarding the baseline configurations. Specifically, it remains unclear whether the baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN) were utilized to independently compute substructure embeddings for a fair comparison with the proposed joint tagger. The continued use of two separate RNNs, resulting in a model with a larger parameter count than the baselines, also remains a concern. Consequently, my initial assessment remains unchanged.
