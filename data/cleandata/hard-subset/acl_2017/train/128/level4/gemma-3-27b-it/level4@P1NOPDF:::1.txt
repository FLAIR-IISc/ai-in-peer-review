This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling in natural language understanding components of conversational systems. Substructures (e.g., a node in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The main contribution of this paper, in my opinion, is a simple method for “flattening” structured information into an array of vectors (the memory), which is then incorporated into the tagger as additional knowledge. This idea is similar to structured or syntax-based attention mechanisms (e.g., attention over nodes from treeLSTMs). Related work includes Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder resembles DCNN (Ma et al.): each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestor words. While the architecture isn’t entirely novel, I appreciate its simplicity and practicality compared to previous approaches.

-----Weaknesses-----
I find the empirical results unconvincing, largely due to a lack of detail regarding the baselines. The following comments are ranked by decreasing importance.

*   The proposed model comprises two main components: sentence embedding and substructure embedding. In Table 1, the baseline models – TreeRNN and DCNN – are originally designed for sentence embedding. However, node/substructure embeddings can easily be extracted from them as well. It’s unclear how these baselines were used to compute each component.
*   The model employs two RNNs: a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one. The only difference in the knowledge-guided RNN is the addition of a “knowledge” vector from the memory to the RNN input (Equations 5 and 8). I believe having separate weights for the two RNNs is unnecessary. The only advantage of this design appears to be increased model capacity – i.e., more parameters. Furthermore, what are the hyperparameters and sizes of the baseline neural networks? They should have a comparable number of parameters to the proposed model.
*   I also suggest including a baseline that directly incorporates additional knowledge as features to the RNN, such as the head of each word or NER results.
*   Are there any results or discussion regarding the model’s sensitivity to parser errors?

Comments on the model:

*   After computing the substructure embeddings, it seems natural to compute attention over them at each word. Is there a reason for using a static attention weight for all words? As it stands, the “knowledge” seems to function more like a filter for highlighting important words. Therefore, the baseline suggested above – incorporating additional features – would be particularly relevant.
*   Since the weight assigned to a word is computed via the inner product of the sentence embedding and the substructure embedding, and these embeddings are computed by the same RNN/CNN, doesn’t this mean nodes or phrases similar to the overall sentence receive higher weights (e.g., all leaf nodes)?
*   The paper claims the model generalizes to different knowledge sources, but the substructure must be represented as a sequence of words. It doesn't seem straightforward to use, for example, constituent parse trees directly as knowledge in this framework.
*   Finally, I hesitate to refer to this as “knowledge.” This is potentially misleading, as “knowledge” typically denotes world or external knowledge, such as a knowledge base of entities. Here, it refers to syntax, or arguably semantics if AMR parsing is used.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper presents a practical model that performs well on a single dataset. However, the core ideas are not particularly novel (see comments in Strengths). I believe an ACL paper should offer more significant takeaways. More importantly, the experiments, as presented, are not entirely convincing. Clarification is needed to better evaluate the results.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors did not address my primary concern: whether the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. Another significant concern remains the use of two separate RNNs, which inherently gives the proposed model more parameters than the baselines. Therefore, I am maintaining my original scores.
