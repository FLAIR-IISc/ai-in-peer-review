- Paper proposes a neural architecture that encodes parse substructures as memory slots and integrates a weighted sum of these embeddings into an RNN for sequence tagging (slot-filling).
- Strength: Simple and practical method for flattening structured information into a memory and using it as supplemental knowledge for tagging.
- Strength: Approach is related to existing structured/syntax-based attention work (e.g., treeLSTMs) and resembles DCNN-style substructure encoding.
- Weakness: Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient detail about baseline configurations and adaptations.
- Weakness: Unclear how TreeRNN and DCNN baselines were adapted to produce substructure embeddings for fair comparison.
- Weakness: Use of two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessary and may simply increase model capacity via extra parameters.
- Weakness: Hyperparameter settings and sizes of baseline networks are not reported, preventing comparison of model complexity.
- Weakness: A simple baseline adding knowledge as features (e.g., head words or NER tags) is missing.
- Weakness: No analysis of robustness to parser errors is provided.
- Model comment: Using static attention over substructure embeddings for all words is questionable; per-word attention would seem more natural.
- Model comment: Inner-product weighting between sentence and substructure embeddings may bias toward nodes similar to the sentence, potentially favoring leaf nodes.
- Model comment: Claim of generality to different knowledge types is weak because substructures are restricted to sequences of words.
- Terminology comment: Calling syntactic/AMR-derived representations “knowledge” is misleading compared to external world knowledge.
- General: Model is practical and performs well on one dataset but lacks novelty and convincing experimental validation for a conference like ACL.
- Post-rebuttal: Authors did not resolve concerns about baseline configuration or the extra-parameter effect of two RNNs; reviewer retains original scores.
