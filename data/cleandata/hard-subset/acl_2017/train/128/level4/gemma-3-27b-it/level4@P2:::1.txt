This paper proposes a neural network architecture that incorporates structural linguistic knowledge, represented as a memory network, for sequence tagging tasks – specifically, slot-filling within the natural language understanding component of conversational systems. Substructures (e.g., nodes in a parse tree) are encoded as vectors (memory slots), and a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings is fed into an RNN at each time step to provide additional contextual information for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The primary contribution of this work lies in a straightforward approach to integrating structured information into a neural network for sequence tagging. By "flattening" structural knowledge into an array of vectors representing a memory, and then incorporating this memory into the tagger, the model provides a practical means of leveraging syntactic or semantic information. While conceptually related to structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms found in prior work (e.g., Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation), the proposed method stands out for its simplicity. The substructure encoder, which embeds each node from ancestral words, is also reminiscent of the DCNN approach (Ma et al.). Although the architecture isn’t entirely novel, its pragmatic design is a strong point. The demonstrated improvements, even if modest, suggest a viable direction for enhancing NLU performance.

-----Weaknesses-----
The empirical validation of this work requires further clarification and strengthening. The following points, ordered by importance, represent key concerns:

1. **Baseline Comparison & Substructure Embedding Consistency:** The comparison to baseline models (TreeRNN and DCNN) is not sufficiently detailed. Both TreeRNN and DCNN are capable of producing node/substructure embeddings directly. It is unclear whether these baselines are using comparable substructure embeddings when utilized within the overall tagging framework. The paper needs to explicitly state how the baselines generate the substructure embeddings used in conjunction with their sentence embedding mechanisms. Are they independently extracting and embedding substructures in a manner comparable to the proposed K-SAN, or are they simply leveraging their sentence-level representations? 

2. **Redundancy of Dual RNNs:** The use of two separate RNNs – a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN – appears largely unnecessary. The only difference between them is the addition of a "knowledge" vector to the input of the knowledge-guided RNN. Introducing separate weights for the two RNNs merely increases model capacity without a clear justification. A single RNN, incorporating the knowledge vector directly, would likely achieve comparable performance with fewer parameters. A justification for maintaining separate RNNs is needed, or a demonstration that their performance significantly surpasses that of a single RNN.

3. **Lack of Feature-Based Baseline:** A crucial baseline is missing: a model that simply incorporates additional knowledge features (e.g., head words, NER tags) directly into the RNN input. This would help isolate the benefits of the proposed memory network architecture versus a simpler approach to knowledge integration.

4. **Sensitivity to Parser Errors:** The model’s robustness to errors in the underlying parser (dependency parser or AMR parser) has not been investigated. Parse errors can introduce noise into the substructure embeddings, potentially degrading performance. Analysis of the model's performance on instances with varying degrees of parsing accuracy would be valuable.

Additional concerns:

* The attention mechanism is static across all words. Exploring an attention mechanism that dynamically weights substructures for each word could potentially improve performance. As currently implemented, the “knowledge” functions more as a filter for important words, and a feature-based baseline, as mentioned above, might yield similar results.
* The inner product between the sentence and substructure embeddings may favor nodes/phrases similar to the overall sentence (i.e., leaf nodes). This potential bias should be addressed.
* The term “knowledge” is potentially misleading. The current implementation primarily utilizes syntactic and semantic structure rather than external, world knowledge. The paper should clarify this distinction.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper presents a practically oriented model that demonstrates improvements on a specific dataset. However, the underlying ideas, while sound, are not groundbreaking. To strengthen the contribution, the paper requires more compelling takeaways and, crucially, a more rigorous experimental evaluation. Further clarification regarding the experimental setup and baseline comparisons is needed to accurately assess the results.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors did not adequately address the core concern regarding the consistency of substructure embedding across the proposed model and the baselines (TreeRNN/DCNN). Specifically, it remains unclear whether the baselines independently extract and embed substructures in a comparable manner. The continued use of two separate RNNs, without a clear performance justification, and the associated increase in model parameters also remain problematic. Therefore, my assessment remains unchanged.
