This paper introduces a neural network architecture designed to incorporate structural linguistic knowledge into a memory network for sequence tagging tasks, specifically slot-filling within natural language understanding components of conversational systems. The approach encodes substructures, such as parse tree nodes, as vectors (memory slots), and integrates a weighted sum of these substructure embeddings into a recurrent neural network (RNN) at each time step to provide additional contextual information for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The primary contribution of this work lies in its straightforward method for “flattening” structured information into a vector array (the memory), and subsequently utilizing this memory as supplemental knowledge for the tagger. This concept shares similarities with structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms – like attention over nodes from treeLSTMs – as seen in related work by Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder bears resemblance to the DCNN model (Ma et al.), embedding each node based on a sequence of ancestor words. While the architecture isn’t entirely novel, its simplicity and practicality are appealing compared to existing approaches.

-----Weaknesses-----
I find the empirical results unpersuasive due to insufficient detail regarding the baselines. The following concerns are listed in decreasing order of importance. 
- The proposed model comprises sentence and substructure embedding components. However, Table 1 compares against TreeRNN and DCNN, which are typically employed for sentence embedding. It’s unclear how these models were adapted to generate substructure embeddings for a fair comparison.
- The model employs two RNNs – a chain-based one and a knowledge-guided one – with the knowledge-guided RNN differing only by the addition of a “knowledge” vector from the memory (Eqn 5 and 8). This separation and the use of distinct weights appear unnecessary, offering only an increase in model capacity (i.e., more parameters). Furthermore, the hyperparameter settings and sizes of the baseline neural networks are not reported, making a comparison of model complexity impossible.
- A baseline incorporating additional knowledge as features to the RNN—such as the head of each word or named entity recognition (NER) results—would be a valuable addition.
- The paper lacks any discussion or results regarding the model's robustness to parser errors.
Comments on the model:
- Following the computation of substructure embeddings, applying attention over them at each word seems natural. Is there a justification for utilizing a static attention mechanism across all words? As currently implemented, the “knowledge” appears to function more as a filter to highlight important words, suggesting the addition of the feature-based baseline mentioned above.
- Given that the weight for each word is determined by the inner product of the sentence and substructure embeddings, and that both embeddings are generated by the same RNN/CNN, it seems likely that nodes/phrases similar to the overall sentence will receive higher weights – potentially favoring leaf nodes.
- The claim that the model generalizes to different types of knowledge is questionable, as the substructure must currently be represented as a sequence of words, making the integration of constituent parses, for example, less straightforward.
- The use of the term “knowledge” is potentially misleading, as it commonly refers to external, world-based knowledge such as that found in a knowledge base of entities, whereas this work primarily utilizes syntactic (or potentially semantic with AMR parsing) information. 

-----General Discussion-----
This paper presents a practical model that performs well on a single dataset, but the core ideas are not particularly novel (as outlined in the Strengths section). For a conference like ACL, I would expect more significant insights. More critically, the experiments, as currently presented, are not entirely convincing. Clarification is needed to properly evaluate the results.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors did not address my central concern regarding the configuration of the baselines (e.g., TreeRNN) – specifically, whether they were used to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding and the joint tagger. A further unresolved issue is the use of two separate RNNs, which inherently provides the proposed model with a greater number of parameters compared to the baselines. Consequently, I am maintaining my original scores.
