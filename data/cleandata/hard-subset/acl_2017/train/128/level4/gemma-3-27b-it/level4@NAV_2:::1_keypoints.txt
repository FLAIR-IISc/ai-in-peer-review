- Proposes a simple memory-network architecture that encodes substructures as vectors and supplies a weighted sum of them to an RNN for sequence tagging (slot filling).
- Main contribution is a straightforward way to integrate structured syntactic information into a neural tagger via a flattened memory representation.
- Approach is similar to prior structured/syntax-based attention methods and its substructure encoder resembles DCNN; the novelty is limited but the method is simple and practical.
- Empirical results are questionable due to insufficient details about how baselines were implemented and compared.
- It is unclear whether baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN, DCNN) were used to produce separate substructure embeddings distinct from sentence embeddings in comparisons.
- The model uses two RNNs (chain RNN and knowledge-guided RNN); the only difference is adding the memory vector, which may simply increase parameters rather than provide real benefit.
- Missing baseline that directly feeds additional knowledge/features (e.g., head words, NER tags) into an RNN for a fair comparison.
- The paper lacks analysis of sensitivity to parser errors, which is important given reliance on parsed substructures.
- Using static attention weights over substructures for all words is questionable; dynamic per-word attention seems more natural.
- Computing attention as inner product between sentence and substructure embeddings (both from same encoder) may bias selection toward nodes similar to the sentence, possibly favoring leaf nodes.
- Claim of generalizing to different knowledge sources is weakened because substructure representations depend on sequential word arrangements, complicating use of other formalisms (e.g., constituency parses).
- Referring to syntax/AMR-derived substructures as “knowledge” is misleading since “knowledge” usually implies external/world knowledge rather than structural syntax.
- Overall: practical and shows positive results on a dataset but lacks strong novelty for ACL-level impact and the experiments are not fully convincing or well-justified.
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not resolve the primary concern about baseline usage; the extra RNN (hence extra parameters) remains a major unresolved issue, so the reviewer’s evaluation stands.
