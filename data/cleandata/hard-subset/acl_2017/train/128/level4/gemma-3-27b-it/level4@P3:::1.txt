This paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structural linguistic knowledge in a memory network for sequence tagging tasks (in particular, slot-filling of the natural language understanding unit in conversation systems). Substructures (e.g., a node in the parse tree) are encoded as a vector (a memory slot) and a weighted sum of the substructure embeddings are fed into an RNN at each time step as additional context for labeling.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is a practical approach to incorporating structured linguistic information into a neural sequence tagging model. Specifically, the method provides a mechanism for "flattening" structural information into a vector representation suitable for integration with an RNN-based tagger. While similar in spirit to structured attention mechanisms and previous work utilizing syntactic or semantic features, the proposed architecture offers a relatively simple and effective means of leveraging this information. A secondary contribution is demonstrating the model's robustness and generalization capability across different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) and parsing tools. Finally, the empirical results on the ATIS dataset show promising performance gains, particularly with limited training data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Simplicity:** The approach of encoding substructures as vectors and integrating them into a standard RNN architecture is relatively straightforward and easy to implement. This contrasts with more complex models requiring specialized network structures or training procedures.
2. **Generalization to Different Knowledge Sources:** The model’s ability to utilize both dependency parse trees and AMR graphs as knowledge sources demonstrates its flexibility and potential applicability to a wider range of NLP tasks and datasets. The results presented showing comparable performance with different knowledge sources is significant.
3. **Improved Performance with Limited Data:** The reported performance improvements, particularly when using smaller training datasets, suggest that the model effectively leverages prior knowledge to mitigate data scarcity issues. This is a valuable benefit in practical NLU applications where labeled data is often limited.
4. **Clear Presentation of the Architecture:** The paper clearly describes the K-SAN architecture, making it relatively easy to understand the flow of information and the role of each component. Figure 2 provides a useful visual representation of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to baseline models is not sufficiently detailed. The paper lacks crucial information regarding the architecture and hyperparameter settings of the baselines (TreeRNN, DCNN), making it difficult to assess the true contribution of K-SAN. It is unclear whether these baselines were optimized to the same degree as the proposed model. A fair comparison requires ensuring comparable model capacity and training procedures.
2. **Unjustified Use of Two RNNs:** The use of separate RNNs for chain-based and knowledge-guided processing appears largely arbitrary and lacks strong justification. The addition of a “knowledge” vector directly into the RNN input seems sufficient, and the introduction of separate weights unnecessarily increases model complexity without a clear benefit. The authors need to demonstrate a clear advantage of this architectural choice.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different components of the K-SAN architecture. For example, the effect of removing the knowledge-guided attention mechanism or using a simpler method for combining the substructure embeddings could provide valuable insights.
4. **Limited Analysis of Parser Errors:** The robustness of the model to parser errors is a crucial consideration, given the inherent imperfections of parsing tools. The paper only briefly mentions this issue and lacks any empirical analysis of its impact on performance.
5. **Misleading Use of "Knowledge":** The term "knowledge" is somewhat misleading, as the model primarily leverages syntactic and semantic *structure* rather than external world knowledge. While the use of AMR graphs partially addresses this, clarifying the scope of "knowledge" would improve the paper's clarity.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Please provide a detailed description of the baseline models used in the experiments, including their architectures, hyperparameter settings, and training procedures. Specifically, how are the TreeRNN and DCNN models adapted to compute substructure embeddings independently of the sentence embedding?
2. What is the justification for using separate RNNs for chain-based and knowledge-guided processing? Can you provide evidence that this architectural choice leads to significant performance gains compared to a simpler approach with a single RNN?
3. Have you investigated the impact of parser errors on the model's performance? Can you present any analysis or results related to this issue?
4.  Could you elaborate on how the model handles different types of substructures beyond dependency trees and AMR graphs? What modifications would be required to incorporate other forms of linguistic knowledge, such as constituent parse trees?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The suggestion to explore an attention mechanism over substructures at each word is a good one and could potentially improve the model’s performance.
*   The observation about the similarity between substructure and sentence embeddings potentially leading to biased attention weights is insightful.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of the model.
