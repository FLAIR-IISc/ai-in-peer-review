This paper introduces a neural network architecture designed to incorporate structural linguistic information into a memory network for sequence tagging, specifically focusing on slot-filling within natural language understanding components of conversational systems. The approach encodes substructures – such as parse tree nodes – as vectors (memory slots), and integrates a weighted sum of these embeddings into an RNN at each time step to provide contextual information for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
The primary strength of this work lies in its straightforward method for “flattening” structured information into a vector array (the memory), then leveraging this as supplemental knowledge for the tagger. This concept shares similarities with structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms (e.g., attention over treeLSTM nodes), and builds upon related research including the work of Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed substructure encoder bears resemblance to the DCNN model (Ma et al.), utilizing embeddings derived from sequences of ancestor words for each node. While the architecture isn’t groundbreaking, its simplicity and practicality are appealing compared to existing approaches.

-----Weaknesses-----
The empirical validation presented is unconvincing, primarily due to insufficient detail regarding the baseline models. Several concerns are detailed below, ordered by importance.
- The model comprises sentence and substructure embedding components. Table 1 presents TreeRNN and DCNN as baselines, however these are typically employed for sentence embedding. It is unclear how these models were adapted to generate substructure/node embeddings for a fair comparison.
- The model employs two distinct RNNs: a standard chain-based RNN and a “knowledge-guided” RNN. The sole distinction between the two is the addition of a “knowledge” vector derived from the memory (Equations 5 and 8). The justification for separate weights for these RNNs is unclear. The primary effect seems to be increased model capacity, and consequently, a larger parameter count.  Hyperparameter details and the size of the baseline networks are needed to confirm parameter comparability.
- Including a baseline that incorporates the structural knowledge directly as features into the RNN – such as head words or Named Entity Recognition (NER) tags – would be a valuable addition.
- The paper lacks any analysis of the model’s robustness to errors in the underlying parser.
Comments on the Model:
- Following substructure embedding computation, applying attention over these embeddings at each word seems intuitive. The rationale for using a static attention mechanism across all words is unclear. As currently implemented, the "knowledge" component appears to act more as a filter, highlighting important words, and the suggested baseline of incorporating features is relevant here.
- Given that weights are calculated via the inner product of sentence and substructure embeddings, both produced by the same RNN/CNN, nodes/phrases similar to the overall sentence context (e.g., leaf nodes) may receive disproportionately high weights.
- The claim that the model generalizes to different knowledge sources is questionable, as the substructure representation relies on sequential word representations. Adapting the approach to utilize, for example, constituent parses directly is not immediately apparent.
- The use of the term “knowledge” is potentially misleading. It typically refers to external, world-based knowledge (e.g., knowledge bases), whereas the information used here is more accurately described as syntactic, or perhaps semantic if AMR parsing is employed.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper presents a practical model that demonstrates performance on a single dataset, but the core ideas lack substantial novelty (see comments in Strengths). For a conference like ACL, a more significant contribution or insight is expected. More critically, the experimental results are not currently persuasive and require further clarification for accurate evaluation.

-----Post-rebuttal-----
The authors failed to address my core concern: whether the baseline models (e.g., TreeRNN) were used to compute independent substructure embeddings, separate from sentence embedding and the joint tagger. A continuing concern is the use of two separate RNNs which increases the model’s parameter count compared to the baselines. Consequently, I have not revised my assessment.
