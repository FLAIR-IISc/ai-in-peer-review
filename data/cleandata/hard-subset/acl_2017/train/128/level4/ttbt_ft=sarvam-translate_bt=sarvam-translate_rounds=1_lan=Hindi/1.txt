This research paper proposes a neural network architecture that represents structured linguistic knowledge in memory networks for sequence tagging tasks (specifically, slot-filling the natural language understanding unit in conversational systems). Sub-networks (such as a node in a parse tree) are encoded as a vector (a memory slot), and the weighted sum of the sub-network embeddings is fed into an RNN at each step as an additional context for labeling.

-----Strengths-----
I think the main contribution of this paper is a simple way to "flatten" structured information into an array of vectors (memory), which are then associated with the tagger as additional knowledge. This idea is similar to structured/syntax-based attention (i.e., attention on nodes from a tree LSTM); related works include Zhao et al. on textual entailment, Liu et al. on natural language inference, and Eriguchi et al. for machine translation. The proposed sub-network encoder is similar to a CNN (Ma et al.): each node is embedded from a sequence of ancestral words. The architecture doesn't seem entirely new, but I like the simple and practical approach compared to previous work.

-----Weaknesses-----
I'm not very convinced by the empirical results, mainly due to the lack of detail in the baseline. The comments below are sorted by decreasing significance.
- The proposed model has two main parts: sentence embedding and sub-network embedding. In Table 1, the baseline models are a tree-RNN and a CNN; they are originally used for sentence embedding, but one can easily take node/sub-network embedding from them. It's not clear how they are used to calculate the two parts.
- The model uses two RNNs: one chain-based and one knowledge-directed. The only difference between a knowledge-directed RNN is the addition of a "knowledge" vector from memory to the RNN input (equations 5 and 8). I find having separate weights for the two RNNs completely unnecessary. The only advantage of using two is increased model capacity, i.e., more parameters.
Also, what are the hyper-parameters/sizes of the baseline neural network? They should have a comparable number of parameters.
- I also think it's appropriate to include a baseline that inputs additional knowledge as features to the RNN, for example, the head of each word, NER results, etc.
- Any comments/results on the model's sensitivity to parser errors?
Comments on the model:
- After computing the sub-network embedding, it seems very natural to focus on them for each word. Is there any reason to use a fixed attention for all words? I think it is, the "knowledge" is acting more like a filter to mark important words. Then it is appropriate to include the suggestion above, i.e., inputting additional features.
- Since the weight on a word is calculated by the internal product of sentence embedding and sub-network embedding, and both embeddings are calculated by the same RNN/CNN, doesn't this mean that nodes/phrases similar to the entire sentence get a higher weight, i.e., all leaf nodes?
- The paper claims that the model is generalized for different knowledge, but I think the sub-network should be represented as a sequence of words, for example, I don't find it straightforward to use the component parse here as knowledge.
Finally, I'm hesitant to call it "knowledge." This is misleading because it is usually used to refer to world/external knowledge such as the knowledge base of entities, while here it is actually just syntax, or arguably semantics if AMR parsing is used.

-----General Discussion-----
This paper proposes a practical model that seems to work well on a dataset, but the main ideas are not very new (see comments in strengths). I think there should be more conclusions as an ACL paper. More importantly, the experiments are not as convincing as they are presented now. Some clarification will be needed to better assess the results.

-----Post-Ribal-----
The authors have not addressed my main concern, which is whether the baseline (such as a tree-RNN) is used to calculate sentence embedding and sub-network embedding independent of the combined tagger. Another major concern is the use of two different RNNs that give the proposed model more parameters than the baseline. Therefore, I am not changing my score.
