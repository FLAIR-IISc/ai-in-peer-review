- Paper proposes a neural architecture that embeds parse substructures into a memory and supplies their weighted sum as context for sequence tagging (slot filling).
- Main contribution is a simple, practical method of flattening structured information into memory vectors integrated into a tagger.
- Approach is conceptually similar to existing structured/syntax-based attention methods and relates to DCNN and other prior work, so novelty is limited.
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to lack of sufficient details about how baselines were implemented and compared.
- Unclear how baseline models (TreeRNN, DCNN) were used to compute both sentence and substructure embeddings.
- Using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) with distinct weights seems unnecessary and mainly increases parameter count.
- Baseline hyperparameters and model sizes are not reported, making fair comparison and parameter matching unclear.
- A stronger baseline would be an RNN augmented with additional knowledge features (e.g., heads, NER); this is missing.
- No analysis of the model’s sensitivity to parser errors, which could materially affect performance.
- Static attention over substructure embeddings for all words is questionable; per-word attention seems more intuitive.
- The scoring scheme (inner product of sentence and substructure embeddings) may bias weights toward nodes similar to the full sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).
- The framework appears to require substructures represented as word sequences, limiting its applicability to other knowledge forms (e.g., non-sequential constituents).
- Referring to substructure embeddings as “knowledge” is misleading since they represent syntactic/semantic structure rather than external/world knowledge.
- Overall, the model is practical and performs on one dataset but lacks substantial novel insights expected for an ACL paper.
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not adequately address concerns about baseline usage and increased parameters from dual RNNs, so reviewer did not change scores.
