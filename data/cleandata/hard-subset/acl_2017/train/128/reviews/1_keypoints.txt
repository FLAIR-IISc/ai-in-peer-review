- Proposes a neural architecture that encodes structural linguistic substructures as memory vectors for sequence tagging/slot-filling.
- Main contribution is a simple "flattening" of structured information into an array of vectors connected to a tagger, similar to structured/syntax-based attention.
- Substructure encoder is similar to DCNN (embedding nodes from ancestor word sequences); approach is practical though not highly novel.
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about how baselines were implemented and compared.
- Unclear whether TreeRNN and DCNN baselines were used to compute both sentence and substructure embeddings independently of the joint tagger.
- Using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessary and may only increase parameter count; comparable baseline sizes not reported.
- Should include a baseline that feeds additional knowledge features directly into the RNN (e.g., head words, NER) to control for simple feature effects.
- No analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors was provided.
- The model uses a static attention/filter over substructures for all words; unclear why per-word dynamic attention wasn’t used.
- Inner-product weighting of substructures with the sentence embedding may bias weights toward nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).
- Claim that the model generalizes to different kinds of "knowledge" is weak since substructures must be representable as word sequences, limiting applicability to some formalisms.
- Calling syntactic/substructure information "knowledge" is misleading because it typically denotes external/world knowledge (e.g., KBs).
- Overall: practical approach that works on one dataset but lacks novelty, strong takeaways, and convincing experimental validation.
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not address main concerns about baseline usage and extra parameters from two RNNs, so reviewer’s score remains unchanged.
