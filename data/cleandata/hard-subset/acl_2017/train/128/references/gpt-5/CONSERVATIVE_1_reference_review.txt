Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external structure (e.g., dependency or AMR) is decomposed into substructures, each embedded into a memory; an utterance embedding attends over these substructure embeddings; the resulting knowledge-guided vector is injected into an RNN/GRU tagger, optionally combined with a vanilla chain RNN. Experiments on ATIS show modest F1 gains over several baselines, with some robustness to parser choice and to using AMR instead of dependency trees.

Contributions, in order of strength:
1) An empirical demonstration that attending over parser-derived substructures can modestly improve slot filling on ATIS, particularly in low-resource regimes, and appears robust across different parsers and knowledge formalisms.
2) A simple, unified attention-over-substructures framework that can accept different knowledge resources (dependency, AMR) and different substructure/sentence encoders (NN/RNN/CNN).
3) Qualitative attention visualizations suggesting the model emphasizes salient spans even when parses contain errors.

Strengths
1) Conceptual simplicity and reasonable engineering: The architecture is a straightforward adaptation of memory/attention mechanisms to slot filling over knowledge-derived substructures, making it easy to reproduce and extend.
2) Consistent empirical gains over the authors’ chosen baselines: Across “Small/Medium/Large” ATIS splits, K-SAN variants improve F1, with the largest benefits in low-resource settings and significance reported (p<0.05) versus baselines. This is a useful practical observation for SLU practitioners with limited labels.
3) Knowledge-source robustness: The model works similarly with different dependency parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and with AMR graphs. This supports the claim that the framework is flexible to upstream knowledge choice and somewhat tolerant of parser noise.
4) Encoder and visualization analyses: Trying multiple encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) for substructures and presenting attention heatmaps adds some diagnostic depth beyond a single model variant.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to established attention/memory models: The core idea—embedding substructures as memory slots and attending with an utterance query—is very close to memory networks and attention pooling. Injecting a global “o” vector into an RNN tagger and linearly combining two RNN streams is also standard. The paper reads as an application of known mechanisms rather than a substantial methodological advance for sequence labeling.
2) Weak and outdated baselines; likely not SOTA: The evaluation omits established strong baselines in slot filling, notably BiLSTM-CRF (e.g., Huang et al. 2015; Lample et al. 2016) and joint models with CRF transitions and/or character features. Reported best F1 (~94.9) is below what many BiLSTM-CRF systems achieve on ATIS. Without comparisons to these, the “state-of-the-art” claim is not convincing.
3) Narrow and saturated evaluation: All results are on a single, small benchmark (ATIS). There is no evaluation on other public SLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MIT Movies, or broader NER benchmarks) to support generality, nor on noisy ASR outputs. Claims of generalization and robustness would be more credible with multiple datasets.
4) Missing ablations and analysis of alternatives: There is no ablation isolating the benefit of attention vs. simple averaging/max-pooling over substructures, or vs. word-level self-attention without external parses. The effect of using relation labels, the impact of the joint mixture weight α, or the choice to tie Min/Mkg are not examined. The claim of efficiency/parallelizability is not empirically supported (no runtime or scaling analysis).
5) “End-to-end” claim is overstated and reproducibility details are sparse: The approach depends on external, fixed parsers; no gradients flow to knowledge extraction, so the pipeline is not fully end-to-end. Training/variance reporting is limited (e.g., t-tests without stating number of runs/seeds; no standard deviations). AMR substructure extraction from a DAG is under-specified.

Questions to authors
1) Please compare against strong contemporary slot-filling baselines, especially BiLSTM-CRF (with/without character embeddings), and report whether K-SAN still yields gains when added to those models.
2) Provide ablations: (a) replace attention with uniform averaging over substructures; (b) use word-level self-attention without external parses; (c) include/deexclude relation labels in dependency/AMR; (d) remove the joint stream (i.e., only knowledge-guided RNN) and vary α beyond 0.5. How sensitive are results to these choices?
3) Clarify AMR substructure construction: In a DAG, how are root-to-leaf paths defined and sampled? Do you deduplicate overlapping paths? How do you handle reentrancies, and do you normalize for path length?
4) Report training variability (mean±std over multiple seeds) and details of the significance test (number of runs, test used). Also report runtime and memory, and the distribution of number of substructures per utterance for each knowledge source.
5) Can you integrate K-SAN with a CRF decoding layer on top of the RNN/GRU? If so, does knowledge-guided attention still provide improvements?
6) The paper claims efficiency and parallelizability. Please substantiate with empirical throughput/latency and scaling behavior as the number of substructures grows.

Overall, while the paper is well-motivated and shows promising gains on ATIS, the methodological novelty is incremental and the empirical validation is not yet at the rigor expected for publication (missing strong baselines, limited datasets, incomplete ablations). Strengthening the evaluation and clarifying the design/analysis would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
