Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes K-SAN, a neural architecture that computes attention over knowledge-guided substructures (e.g., dependency root-to-leaf paths or AMR fragments) to form a sentence-level representation, which is then injected into an RNN-based slot tagger (and a joint variant combining a chain RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN). Attention weights are learned end-to-end; the same encoder is used for substructures and the whole sentence, and different sequence encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) are explored.
- Empirically, K-SAN yields consistent gains over non-structural baselines and syntax/AMR-feature baselines on ATIS across small/medium/large training regimes, with the best model reaching F1 ≈ 95 on the full data. The method appears robust to the choice and quality of upstream parsers (Stanford vs SyntaxNet) and to semantic vs syntactic structures (AMR vs dependencies).
- The paper’s primary contributions are: (1) a simple, general attention-over-substructures mechanism for slot filling that can ingest heterogeneous structural knowledge; (2) an empirical demonstration that such guided attention improves slot-filling, especially in low-data settings; (3) a small-scale analysis suggesting attention focuses on salient substructures.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: The design—encode substructures, compute attention with the sentence embedding, sum to a knowledge-guided vector, and inject into a tagger—is straightforward, differentiable, and broadly compatible with different structure types (dependencies, AMR). This makes the idea easy to adapt beyond the specific task.
- Consistent empirical improvements: On ATIS, K-SAN variants improve over strong sentence-encoder baselines (CNN) and structural baselines (DCNN, Tree-RNN) across data sizes, with the largest gains in low-resource settings. The robustness to different parsers/graphs (Table 2) supports the claim that the approach is not brittle to the chosen knowledge source.
- Clear ablations across encoders and knowledge sources: The paper evaluates multiple encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) and multiple knowledge providers (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers). This triangulation strengthens the evidence that gains are due to the attention-over-substructures idea rather than a specific encoder or parser.
- Interpretable attention behavior: The qualitative visualization shows attention aligning with semantically important regions (origin/destination/time). Even when the parser errs, the model can still leverage partial structure. This supports the intended “teacher” role of knowledge.
- Sensible architecture choices for tagging: The joint tagger combining chain-based and knowledge-guided RNNs balances sequential dependencies with global structural cues, which is a reasonable design to mitigate parser noise.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and baselines: Results are only on ATIS. Strong contemporary baselines for slot filling (e.g., BiLSTM, BiLSTM-CRF, joint intent+slot models, character/subword features) are missing. Without comparisons to these, the strength of the claimed state-of-the-art is uncertain, and cross-dataset generalization (e.g., SNIPS, MultiWOZ domains, or other SLU corpora) is not demonstrated.
- Novelty is moderate relative to memory/attention literature: The core mechanism resembles memory networks/hierarchical attention—attend over a set of memory items (substructures) using a query (sentence embedding). The main novelty is the choice of “memory items” as knowledge-derived substructures for slot tagging. The claim of being the “first” end-to-end use of general knowledge as guidance is too strong given prior attention/memory models and syntax-aware neural encoders; the contribution is a targeted adaptation rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.
- Methodological details and ablations are incomplete: Important design choices are under-specified or unevaluated: the exact construction of AMR substructures; effect of using relation labels vs unlabeled paths; the role of tying Min/Mkg; the impact of alpha in the joint model; where dropout is applied; whether attention is single-hop vs multi-hop. An ablation removing attention (uniform averaging) or removing knowledge (random substructures) would clarify how much the attention itself contributes.
- Statistical testing and variance reporting: The t-test significance is reported but the protocol is unclear (number of runs, random seeds, what is averaged). No variance/confidence intervals are provided. Given small absolute gains in some settings (e.g., ~0.7–1.5 F1), variance matters.
- Claims about efficiency/parallelizability are not substantiated: The paper asserts potential efficiency and non-linear scaling with sentence length, but provides no runtime or memory comparisons, nor analysis of how the number/size of substructures affects cost or latency.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Baselines: Can you compare against BiLSTM and BiLSTM-CRF slot taggers and, if possible, joint intent+slot models, using the same lexical features? How do K-SAN gains look relative to these?
- Variance: How many random restarts were used for each result? Please report mean±std and clarify the significance testing setup.
- Ablations: (a) What is the performance if attention is uniform over substructures? (b) If Min and Mkg are untied? (c) If dependency/AMR edge labels are included? (d) Sensitivity to alpha; is α=0.5 optimal?
- Substructure construction: For AMR, what exact algorithm yields the substructures (paths from root to leaves? edge neighborhoods?), and how are re-entrancies handled?
- Computational cost: What is the inference/training overhead vs CNN/RNN baselines as a function of sentence length and number of substructures? Any batching or pruning strategies?

Overall assessment against ACL criteria
- Originality: Moderate. The idea of attention over external-knowledge-derived substructures applied to slot tagging is a reasonable and useful instantiation, but it builds closely on established attention/memory concepts and prior syntax-aware encoders.
- Quality: Solid but incomplete. The method is sound, and the empirical evidence is consistent, including robustness to different parsers. However, missing strong baselines, limited dataset scope, and absent ablations weaken the case.
- Clarity: Generally clear, with helpful figures. Some methodological details (AMR substructures, statistical protocol) need clarification.
- Significance: Promising for SLU, especially low-resource scenarios, and potentially generalizable. Demonstrated gains are modest; broader benchmarks and stronger baselines would increase impact.

If strengthened with additional baselines, variance reporting, ablations, and at least one more dataset, this would make a compelling contribution. As is, it is a borderline accept based on simplicity, generality, and consistent gains, but limited by evaluation breadth and novelty claims.
