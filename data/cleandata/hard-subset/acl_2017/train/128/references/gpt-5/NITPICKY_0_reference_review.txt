Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The core idea is to parse an utterance into a set of substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf dependency paths or AMR fragments), embed these into a memory, compute attention over substructures given a sentence embedding, and inject a resulting sentence-level “knowledge-guided” vector as an additional input into a recurrent tagger. A joint tagger combines a plain chain-based RNN with a knowledge-augmented RNN. Experiments on ATIS with different training set sizes show modest gains over baselines, with similar performance across dependency- and AMR-based knowledge.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A memory/attention framework over linguistically derived substructures to produce a sentence-level context vector for sequence tagging.
2) A joint architecture that blends a chain-based tagger and a knowledge-guided tagger.
3) An empirical study claiming generalization across different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR) on ATIS.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive idea: Treating parse-derived substructures as memory items and attending over them is a conceptually clean way to incorporate external structure without hand-engineered features. The architecture is simple to implement and end-to-end trainable.
- Some empirical gains on low-resource settings: On smaller ATIS training subsets, K-SAN yields consistent improvements over the provided baselines, suggesting potential robustness when data are scarce.
- Knowledge-source generalization: The model can consume different structured inputs (dependency trees from two parsers, and AMR from two parsers), with similar performance. This flexibility is appealing compared to feature-engineering approaches tied to a specific linguistic schema.
- Attention analysis: The visualization qualitatively suggests that the method can focus on semantically salient parts (origin, destination, dates), even in the presence of parser errors.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing memory/attention models and syntax-aware tagging: The mechanism—embedding substructures into a memory and scoring by u^T m_i with softmax—is a direct instantiation of memory networks/attentive readers. Injecting a single sentence-level context vector o to each timestep is not new and is arguably weaker than token-wise attention. Prior syntax-aware models (e.g., Tree-LSTM, dep-CNN) and per-token attention mechanisms are not sufficiently contrasted. A stronger novelty case is needed.
- Inadequate baselines and comparisons: The paper omits widely accepted strong baselines for slot filling such as BiLSTM-CRF and neural-CRF variants. Comparing mainly to Elman RNN/GRU and CNN-based encoders undercuts the “state-of-the-art” claim. It is unclear if the reported gains would hold against BiLSTM-CRF with character embeddings or contextualized encoders.
- Methodological gaps and ablations: 
  - The same sentence-level o is added to all timesteps; there is no per-token attention or token-conditioned knowledge use. An ablation comparing per-token attention vs global o is missing.
  - Tying Min and Mkg is asserted without justification; no ablation for untied parameters.
  - The joint model uses fixed α=0.5; no sensitivity analysis or tuning reported.
  - Dropping relation labels “for generalization” weakens the semantic signal; no experiment shows the impact of including labels.
  - No analysis of parser quality vs performance; only a single anecdotal example is shown.
- Claims about efficiency/parallelizability are unsubstantiated: The paper asserts that modeling time may not increase linearly because substructures are modeled separately, yet the number of substructures is O(n) and attention requires comparing all m_i. No runtime, memory, or throughput comparisons are provided, and parsing overhead is ignored.
- Experimental design and reporting issues:
  - ATIS only, single domain, single task (slots). No evaluation on larger or noisier datasets.
  - Dev set construction is unspecified; batch size, early stopping, and number of training runs/seeds are not reported.
  - Statistical significance is claimed (t-test p<0.05), but there is no mention of multiple runs or variance; a t-test on single runs is invalid.
  - It is unclear whether all baselines used the same embeddings, optimization, and regularization.
  - Attention visualization is qualitative; no quantitative correlation with tagging performance.
- Technical clarity issues:
  - Equation (8) adds Mo without defining its shape or relation to Mout; the notational overload of M* matrices is confusing.
  - Figure 2/4 diagrams are hard to parse; references to “top-left component” are ambiguous; several symbols in the figures are garbled.
  - The mapping from AMR graphs to token-aligned substructures is under-specified; token–concept alignment is nontrivial in AMR.
- Numerous formatting and referencing problems:
  - Many references are malformed (e.g., “Banarescu et al.,? 2013”, “Tur and Mori” vs “Tur and De Mori”), odd LaTeX artifacts (\Q2015\E), inconsistent capitalization and punctuation.
  - Typos and layout glitches (e.g., “ap- show”, “I-destO”, “v.s.”), missing spaces/hyphens, and footnotes/URLs inline.
  - Figures/tables are poorly formatted. These issues impede readability and suggest insufficient proofreading.

Questions to authors
- Novelty/positioning: How does K-SAN differ technically and empirically from (a) memory networks applied to sequence tagging with per-token attention, and (b) BiLSTM-CRF models augmented with syntactic features or syntax-aware encoders? Please add strong baselines and clarify novelty.
- Per-token conditioning: Why did you choose a single sentence-level o instead of conditioning attention on token-level hidden states to produce o_t? Have you tried token-conditioned attention and does it help?
- Parameter tying: What is the rationale for tying Min and Mkg? Please report results with untied encoders.
- Relation labels: What is the impact of including dependency/AMR relation labels in the substructure encodings?
- Parser robustness: Can you provide a systematic study of performance vs. induced parser noise (e.g., perturbation or cross-parser agreement) rather than a single example?
- Reproducibility: Please specify dev split, batch size, number of runs, seed control, early stopping, dropout placement, and release code. How were word embeddings trained “on in-domain data”? Are embeddings shared across models?
- Efficiency: Provide training/inference time and memory usage, including parsing overhead, compared to baselines.
- α sensitivity: How sensitive is the joint model to α? Did you tune it per dataset?

Given the combination of limited novelty against strong contemporary baselines, missing ablations, weak experimental breadth, and extensive presentation/formatting issues, I am not inclined to recommend acceptance in its current form.
