- Proposes K-SAN: attend over parser/AMR-derived root-to-leaf path encodings stored in memory to produce a sentence-level context vector that conditions an RNN slot tagger.  
- Joint tagger combines chain-only RNN and knowledge-guided RNN to mitigate parser noise.  
- Claims end-to-end, knowledge-source agnostic, and efficient design with interpretability via attention visualizations.  
- Contribution: memory/attention formulation using parse substructures as external “facts” for sequence tagging.  
- Contribution: empirical generalization across knowledge sources (two dependency parsers, two AMR parsers) with stronger gains in low-data regimes.  
- Contribution: interpretable attention analyses that identify linguistically salient substructures for slot prediction.  
- Strength: integrates structured linguistic information via attention rather than brittle hand-engineered features.  
- Strength: robustness to different syntactic/semantic parsers, supported by substructure counts and similar performance across resources.  
- Strength: consistent improvements on ATIS, especially in small-data settings (notable relative gains in Small/Medium).  
- Strength: attention visualizations provide useful diagnostics aligning with slot semantics even with imperfect parses.  
- Strength: pragmatic joint modeling (interpolation α) helps hedge against noisy or mismatched knowledge.  
- Weakness: novelty is incremental relative to existing memory/attention models; main novelty is using parser paths as facts.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to ATIS with dated/missing baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, attention BiLSTMs, contextualized pretraining).  
- Weakness: limited ablations—no tests of uniform attention, different α settings, untied weights, or alternative o injection methods.  
- Weakness: statistical robustness unclear—no multi-seed averaging, limited significance testing versus structural baselines.  
- Weakness: efficiency/parallelizability claims lack empirical runtime or memory benchmarks given many substructures.  
- Weakness: some architectural/implementation details under-specified (o integration with GRU, AMR substructure construction, use of relation labels).  
- Questions: Are taggers GRU-based or plain RNNs, and how exactly is o injected into GRU (gates, concat, init h0)?  
- Questions: What are results with uniform attention, learned vs fixed α, and untied Min/Mkg weights?  
- Questions: Were dependency/AMR labels used in encodings, and how do they affect cross-parser generalization?  
- Questions: How does K-SAN compare to strong BiLSTM-CRF and attention-based BiLSTM baselines, and to sentence-level context concatenation baselines?  
- Questions: Do gains generalize to other SLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MIT Restaurant/Movie)?  
- Questions: Can you provide empirical runtime and memory comparisons and breakdowns as a function of substructure count?
