Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a sequence tagging model that attends over substructures induced by external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs). Substructures are embedded, compared to a sentence embedding to produce an attention distribution, and aggregated into a global “knowledge-guided” representation that conditions an RNN slot tagger (with a joint variant combining chain-only and knowledge-guided paths). Experiments on ATIS show consistent gains over non-structural baselines and prior structure-aware encoders, with larger improvements in low-resource settings, and robustness across multiple parsers/knowledge types.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A general and end-to-end attention mechanism over knowledge-induced substructures for sequence tagging, moving beyond feature augmentation to learnable, selective use of structure.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach is robust to parser noise and generalizes across syntactic (dependencies) and semantic (AMR) sources, with particularly strong improvements in low-resource regimes.
3) A simple joint architecture that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided information and yields state-of-the-art performance on ATIS slot filling among the compared systems.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Attending over knowledge-induced substructures is a fresh idea for slot filling. Prior work typically injected knowledge as features or used full-tree encoders; here, knowledge plays a “teacher” role that guides which substructures matter, learned end-to-end. This is a clear and bold step beyond feature engineering and whole-tree encoders, and is closer in spirit to memory/attention models tailored to sequence labeling.

- Generality across knowledge sources: The same mechanism works with different parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and knowledge formalisms (dependency vs AMR), without relying on relation labels. Results are consistently good and similar across sources, supporting the claim of schema-agnostic guidance.

- Robust low-resource performance: The gains are most pronounced with limited training data (e.g., +2–3 F1 over the strongest baseline on Small/Medium), aligning well with the motivation that prior knowledge can act as an inductive bias when supervision is scarce.

- Practical, end-to-end integration: The attention is trained jointly with the tagger; no external feature pipelines beyond parsing are required at training time. Visualizations suggest the attention focuses on slot-relevant spans even under parse errors.

- Simple architecture that scales: Encoding multiple substructures and aggregating via a soft attention is conceptually simple and likely parallelizable (as argued), with tied encoders for substructures and sentences further simplifying the design.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: All results are on ATIS slot filling only. ATIS is small and saturated; improvements of ~1–2 F1 on the full set, while solid, are modest. Absence of tests on other SLU datasets or other sequence labeling tasks (e.g., SNIPS, MIT restaurant/movie, CoNLL NER) limits the paper’s impact and generality claims.

- Missing stronger baselines and ablations: No comparison to widely adopted BiLSTM-CRF (with/without character features), which has been a strong baseline for sequence tagging. Ablations are sparse: we do not see results for knowledge-only vs chain-only, attention vs uniform averaging over substructures, untied vs tied encoders, using relation labels, or per-token (contextual) attention vs a single global o. This makes it hard to isolate where the gains come from.

- “End-to-end” and “efficiency” claims overreach: The approach depends on external parsers whose errors and domain mismatch may affect performance; the system is not end-to-end from text without preprocessing. Efficiency/parallelizability is asserted but not quantified—no runtime, memory, or throughput comparisons vs baselines.

- Global conditioning only: The knowledge-guided vector o is injected uniformly at every time step, which ignores token-specific cues. A per-token attention conditioned on ht or wt might better exploit structure and yield larger gains; the current design choice is not justified or contrasted.

- Ambiguity about the unique role of knowledge: Performance is similar across very different knowledge sources and numbers of substructures; this could suggest the mechanism is acting as a strong sentence encoder irrespective of precise structure. A control using randomized trees/graphs or bag-of-chunks would help confirm that real linguistic structure (not just segmentation) drives improvements.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report results on a stronger neural baseline (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, optionally with char embeddings)? If not, can you compare to your best RNN-GRU+CRF decoding variant?

- How much does attention (Eq. 1–2) contribute beyond simple averaging/max-pooling over substructures? Please include an ablation removing attention or replacing it with uniform weights.

- Why use a single global knowledge vector o for the whole sequence rather than token-conditioned attention o_t? Did you try conditioning on ht or wt when computing attention, and what was the effect?

- What is the computational overhead (parse time excluded) and GPU runtime vs your best non-knowledge baseline? How does performance scale with the number of substructures?

- Did you try using dependency/AMR relation labels? If not, what prevented it, and do you expect further gains?

- How sensitive are results to parser quality/domain mismatch? Can you quantify performance when the parser is deliberately degraded or out-of-domain?

- Could you provide an ablation of the joint model (vary α or remove one branch) and the effect of tying vs untying Min/Mkg?

Overall assessment
This is a creative and promising direction: using attention to let external structure teach a tagger what to focus on is a meaningful conceptual advance for SLU. Despite some experimental limitations, the novelty and the consistent gains (especially in low-resource settings) make the submission attractive. Strengthening the empirical section with the requested ablations and a stronger baseline would further solidify the case. I am inclined to recommend acceptance.
