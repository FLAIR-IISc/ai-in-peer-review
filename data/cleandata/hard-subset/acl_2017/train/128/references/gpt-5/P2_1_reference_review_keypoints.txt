- Proposes K-SAN: an end-to-end model that attends over knowledge-derived substructures (dependency/AMR) to build a sentence representation for RNN slot tagging, combined with a joint tagger.
- Methodological contribution: introduces attention over knowledge substructures trained end-to-end (“knowledge as teacher”) rather than using static features.
- Empirical contribution: reports consistent F1 gains on ATIS across data regimes, especially in low-resource settings, and across multiple parsers.
- Analysis contribution: visualizations show attention focuses on semantically salient words/relations and is somewhat robust to parser errors.
- Strength: Conceptually clean integration of external structure via differentiable attention, extending memory/attention ideas to sequence labeling.
- Strength: Consistent empirical improvements, with largest gains in small/medium data and state-of-the-art numbers on the large split.
- Strength: Robust across different knowledge sources/parsers, suggesting schema-agnostic behavior.
- Strength: Simple, sensible architecture and training (tied encoders, single sentence vector, joint tagger) with practical advantages.
- Strength: Qualitative attention visualizations provide face validity for the mechanism.
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to ATIS; lacks multi-dataset testing (e.g., SNIPS, MIT Restaurant/Movie) to establish generality.
- Weakness: Baselines omit strong slot-filling models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, self-attentive encoders) and structural baselines (DCNN/Tree-RNN).
- Weakness: Missing ablations to isolate contributions: knowledge-only tagger, attention vs uniform averaging, labeled vs unlabeled edges, learned vs fixed α, and per-time-step o_t vs single o.
- Weakness: Reproducibility gaps—encoder details (depth, nonlinearity, bi-/uni-directional), hyperparameters, seed/variance, substructure linearization, and substructure counts per sentence are unspecified.
- Weakness: Efficiency/parallelizability claims unsubstantiated—no runtime/memory or batching strategy reported.
- Weakness: Methodological concerns about substructure design—root-to-leaf paths discard labels and may produce unnatural token orders; ignoring internal nodes may lose context.
- Weakness: Statistical testing/reporting issues—single-run results, t-test on F1 may be inappropriate; need mean±std and better significance testing (paired bootstrap/approximate randomization).
- Question: How exactly are root-to-leaf paths linearized, are labels/POS included, and how are duplicate tokens handled?
- Question: What is the distribution/average number of substructures per sentence for each knowledge source?
- Question: Why fix α=0.5 rather than learning it (globally, per-sentence, or per-time-step), and did you try dynamic per-time-step conditioning (o_t)?
- Question: How does K-SAN compare to BiLSTM-CRF and self-attentive encoders, and is performance significant vs DCNN/Tree-RNN?
- Question: What are results for ablations: remove attention (uniform), include dependency/AMR labels, or use only the knowledge-guided branch?
- Question: What is the training/inference overhead vs plain RNN/CNN baselines and how do you batch variable numbers of substructures?
- Overall: Originality moderate—neat application of attention over knowledge-derived substructures to SLU.
- Overall: Technically sound but some design choices lack justification and require ablations.
- Overall: Empirically promising but limited by single-dataset evaluation and baseline choices.
- Provisional recommendation: Weak accept (encourages attention-based knowledge integration) but borderline pending expanded evaluation and ablations.
