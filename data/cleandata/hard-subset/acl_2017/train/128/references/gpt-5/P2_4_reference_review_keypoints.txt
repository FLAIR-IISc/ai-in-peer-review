- Paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) that attend over parser/AMR substructures, aggregate a knowledge-guided sentence vector, and inject it into an RNN tagger with a joint chain+knowledge architecture.
- Main contributions: attention-over-substructures mechanism, empirical gains on ATIS (notably in low-resource settings), and evidence of generalization across syntactic/semantic parsers plus qualitative analyses.
- Strength: Conceptually clean, modular approach that leverages external structure without hard-coding and can down-weight noisy relations.
- Strength: Consistent empirical improvements on ATIS compared to CRF/RNN/CNN baselines, with largest gains in low-data regimes and statistical significance reported.
- Strength: Similar gains across different dependency and AMR parsers suggest robustness to knowledge source and parser variation.
- Strength: Qualitative attention visualizations focus on task-relevant substructures (e.g., origin/destination/time) and remain stable across data sizes.
- Strength: Training setup is well-documented and multiple encoder backbones are compared, supporting that the substructure-attention idea drives improvements.
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to ATIS, a small/saturated dataset, and lacks comparison to stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, bidirectional encoders, char features).
- Weakness: The “end-to-end” claim is overstated because K-SAN relies on precomputed parses/AMRs and does not learn or adapt the knowledge extraction component.
- Weakness: Missing ablations to isolate contributions—no uniform-average baseline, no whole-sentence structural encoder comparison, no tests on edge labels, and limited breakdown of joint vs. single-component performance.
- Weakness: Efficiency and parallelizability claims are unsupported—no runtime, memory, or scaling analyses compared to standard taggers.
- Weakness: Architectural choices need justification and exploration—global sentence embedding as attention key, weight tying, and use of unidirectional tagger are not empirically motivated.
- Question: Please report results against stronger baselines (BiLSTM-CRF, BiGRU taggers, character embeddings) to confirm gains hold.
- Question: Add ablations for attention vs. uniform weighting and random substructures to quantify attention’s contribution.
- Question: Evaluate sensitivity to knowledge noise by corrupting parses or limiting substructure availability.
- Question: Test whether including dependency/AMR relation labels improves performance compared to ignoring them.
- Question: Provide runtime/complexity comparisons versus standard taggers and scaling behavior with number of substructures.
- Question: Provide token-level error analyses to show which slot types or phenomena benefit most (e.g., long-distance dependencies, coordination).
- Overall: Moderately original and promising for low-resource settings, but scope and evidence are limited by dataset choice and missing key experiments.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept conditional on adding stronger baselines and critical ablations; otherwise lean reject due to limited empirical scope.
