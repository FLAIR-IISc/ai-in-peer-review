Summary
The paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses or AMR graphs) is decomposed into substructures (root-to-leaf paths). Each substructure and the sentence are embedded; an attention distribution over substructures is computed via inner product with the sentence embedding; a weighted sum produces a sentence-level “knowledge-guided” vector that is injected into an RNN tagger (and a joint model combining a chain-based and knowledge-guided RNN). Experiments on ATIS report F1 gains over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines and over dependency/Tree-RNN encoders, with larger gains in low-resource regimes. The method appears to work similarly across different parsers and knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency, rule-based/JAMR AMR). Visualizations of attention are provided.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A simple attention-over-knowledge-substructures mechanism that conditions a sentence-level vector on external structure and feeds it to a sequence tagger, applied to slot filling.
- An empirical study on ATIS showing modest but consistent gains, especially with limited training data, and that the approach is not highly sensitive to the specific parser/knowledge source.
- Qualitative attention analysis suggesting the model focuses on semantically salient parts even with noisy parses.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual framing: using “knowledge as a teacher” to guide attention over substructures is intuitive and aligns with memory/attention literature; the architecture is straightforward to implement.
- Empirical signal on ATIS: consistent improvements over the authors’ baselines, with statistically significant gains (per t-test) and more pronounced benefits in low-data settings.
- Knowledge source robustness: the method yields similar performance across two syntactic parsers and two AMR parsers, supporting the claimed generality to different structural resources.
- Qualitative interpretability: attention visualizations help diagnose what the model attends to and demonstrate some robustness to parsing errors.
- Practicality: the approach does not require dependency/AMR labels (only paths), which can simplify deployment across heterogeneous resources.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and baselines. ATIS is small and saturated; conclusions about generalization and robustness are weak without larger, diverse datasets (e.g., multi-domain SLU, noisy ASR transcripts). Strong, established baselines are missing: BiLSTM/biGRU with CRF decoding is standard for sequence tagging and often outperforms plain RNNs; character features and BiRNNs are also commonplace. The reported gains over relatively weak baselines may not hold against SOTA.
- Methodological gaps for token-level tagging. The attention produces a single sentence-level vector injected at all time steps. This global vector may be a blunt instrument for token-level decisions; per-token attention over substructures (conditioned on position) or token-context cross-attention would be a more appropriate comparison. The current design’s advantage over simply adding any sentence embedding is unclear.
- Insufficient ablations. Key components are not disentangled: (i) attention vs. uniform averaging of substructures; (ii) knowledge-free sentence vector plugged into the joint RNN in the exact same way (the “CNN Encoder-Tagger” is not architecturally identical); (iii) effect of tying Min/Mkg; (iv) the joint vs. knowledge-only RNN; (v) impact of using dependency/AMR edge labels. Without these, the source of gains remains ambiguous.
- Overstated claims about efficiency and error propagation. The paper claims efficiency/parallelizability and mitigation of error propagation, but provides no runtime/complexity analysis or controlled noise studies. Error propagation is still possible because the approach depends on parsed substructures; the robustness experiment is anecdotal.
- Statistical rigor and reproducibility. Significance testing details (number of runs/seeds, variance) and train/dev/test protocol are limited. Hyperparameter parity across baselines is unclear. No code or detailed training recipes are provided; 300 training iterations with Adam and a small hyperparameter sweep is sparse relative to modern practices.

Questions to Authors
- Can you compare against stronger established baselines (BiLSTM-CRF, BiGRU-CRF, with/without character CNNs), and a joint intent+slot model, using identical embeddings and training budgets?
- How much of the gain remains if you (a) uniformly average substructure embeddings (no attention), (b) use a knowledge-free sentence embedding (e.g., CNN/BiLSTM) injected identically, and (c) use per-token attention over substructures conditioned on token position?
- Why fix α at 0.5 in the joint model? Does learning α or using a learned gate improve performance?
- Do results hold on larger/more diverse SLU datasets and with ASR outputs (to validate robustness to noise and domain mismatch)?
- What is the precise construction and ordering of substructures (e.g., “show flights seattle from”)? Have you tried including dependency/AMR labels in the embeddings?
- Please report variance over multiple random seeds and clarify whether all baselines were reimplemented and tuned under the same settings. Any runtime comparisons to support efficiency claims?

Overall, while the idea is reasonable and results on ATIS are promising, the current empirical validation is too narrow and lacks comparisons to established strong baselines and informative ablations. Strengthening these aspects would substantially improve the paper’s credibility.
