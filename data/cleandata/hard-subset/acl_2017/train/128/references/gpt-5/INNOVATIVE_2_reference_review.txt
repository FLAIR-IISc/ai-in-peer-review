Summary
This paper introduces Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling in goal-oriented NLU. The key idea is to treat external linguistic/semantic structures (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs) as “knowledge” and encode their substructures as memory entries. An utterance embedding attends over these substructure embeddings to produce a knowledge-guided sentence representation, which conditions an RNN tagger. The model is trained end-to-end, learns attention over salient substructures, and is claimed to generalize to heterogeneous knowledge sources. Experiments on ATIS show consistent gains over non-structural and structural baselines, with larger improvements in low-data regimes; results hold across parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and knowledge types (dependency, AMR).

Main Contributions (as I see them)
1) A general, end-to-end framework that uses external structured knowledge as attention “teachers” for sequence tagging, by embedding substructures and learning attention over them to guide an RNN tagger.
2) Empirical demonstration that the approach is robust to knowledge source choice (syntax vs. semantics, different parsers) and particularly effective in low-resource settings.
3) A simple joint architecture that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided signals, improving robustness to noisy or imperfect structures.

Strengths
- Novel cross-pollination of memory/attention with structured NLP for sequence tagging: While memory networks/DMNs are established for QA, bringing the “knowledge as memory” idea to slot filling with substructure-level attention is a fresh, compelling direction. The use of root-to-leaf paths (or analogous AMR substructures) as attention units is intuitive and generalizable beyond a single formalism.

- Generality across knowledge sources is substantiated: The same machinery works with dependency trees (two parsers) and AMR (two parsers), with similar gains and without reliance on relation labels. This supports the central claim that K-SAN is schema-agnostic and robust to parser choice.

- Strong performance with low data and clear qualitative evidence: Gains are largest on 1/40 and 1/10 splits, a practically important regime for new domains. The attention visualizations align with task-relevant parts (origin, destination, time), including cases with parser errors, indicating the model learns to focus on useful substructures despite noise.

- End-to-end, simple integration with a standard tagger: The formulation allows gradients to flow through attention into the substructure encoders, and the joint RNN design mitigates error propagation from noisy knowledge. The implementation choices (shared encoders for sentences/substructures; plug-in of o into RNN/GRU) are clean and likely reproducible.

- Efficiency motivation and parallelizability: Modeling substructures independently and aggregating via attention is amenable to batching/parallel computation; while not empirically evaluated here, the design is compatible with efficient deployment.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope (single dataset): Results are only on ATIS, an overused and relatively small benchmark. No evaluation on more diverse SLU datasets (multi-domain, noisier user language) or larger-scale benchmarks limits claims of generality and scalability in real settings.

- Missing stronger modern baselines: The comparisons do not include BiLSTM-CRF or contemporary neural slot-filling architectures with CRF decoding, which are standard and strong. The best baseline here is a CNN encoder + RNN tagger; it’s unclear whether the claimed SOTA holds against BiLSTM-CRF or attention-augmented sequence taggers.

- Lack of ablation and sensitivity analyses: Key design choices (e.g., using root-to-leaf paths, tying Min/Mkg weights, including or excluding relation labels, choice of α in the joint model) are not dissected. An ablation removing attention (uniform pooling), or feeding only the chain tagger, would clarify where the gains come from.

- Efficiency claim not validated: The paper asserts potential efficiency/parallelizability but presents no runtime, memory, or complexity measurements versus baselines, nor scaling with sentence length/number of substructures.

- Treatment of knowledge quality is underexplored: While some noisy-parse examples are shown, there is no systematic study of robustness to knowledge errors, missing structures, or domain mismatch. Ignoring relation labels for “generalization” may leave performance on the table; this design trade-off is not empirically examined.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report results with a BiLSTM-CRF baseline and/or BiGRU-CRF, and, if feasible, integrate K-SAN with a CRF decoding layer? This would strengthen the empirical case.

- How sensitive is performance to α in the joint tagger? Have you tried learning α or gating the combination dynamically?

- What is the impact of including dependency/AMR relation labels in the substructure encoder? Does it help on any split?

- Why choose root-to-leaf paths specifically? Have you compared against other substructure definitions (e.g., all edge triples, shortest dependency paths between key tokens, AMR role-argument tuples)?

- Can you provide runtime statistics (per sentence) and scaling with the number of substructures, plus GPU utilization? This would substantiate the efficiency claim.

- How does K-SAN perform if the knowledge is partially missing or adversarially noisy? A controlled noise study would help quantify robustness.

Overall Recommendation
Leaning accept for novelty. Despite evaluation limitations and missing stronger baselines, the paper proposes a conceptually bold, general, and end-to-end way to use external structure as attention guidance for sequence tagging, with consistent gains and compelling low-resource benefits.
