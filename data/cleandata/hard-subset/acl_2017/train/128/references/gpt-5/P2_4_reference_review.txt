Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper introduces Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, it extracts knowledge-guided substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in a dependency tree or AMR edges), embeds each substructure, computes attention over them conditioned on a sentence embedding, aggregates a knowledge-guided sentence vector, and injects this vector into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain RNN with the knowledge-guided RNN.
- Contributions:
  1) A simple, general attention-over-substructures mechanism that can consume different external structures (dependency trees, AMR) and is trained end-to-end with the tagger.
  2) Empirical gains on ATIS over strong non-structural and structural baselines, especially in low-resource settings, with significance tests.
  3) Evidence of knowledge source generalization (similar gains across different parsers and semantic/syntactic sources) and qualitative attention analyses.

Strengths
- Clear, modular idea that leverages structure without hard-coding: The formulation—encode substructures, attend to them given the utterance embedding, aggregate, and feed to a tagger—is conceptually clean, easy to implement, and agnostic to the knowledge source. This addresses known issues with feature engineering (error propagation, mismatch) by learning the degree of reliance on each substructure. The joint tagger further hedges against noisy knowledge. This is novel in the slot-filling context relative to prior work that uses structures as features or full-tree encoders.
- Consistent empirical improvements, strongest in low-resource: On ATIS, K-SAN(CNN/RNN) outperforms CRF, RNN, and CNN-encoder baselines across small/medium/large settings, with the largest margins in low data (e.g., Small: 74.6 vs 73.6 F1 over the best non-structural baseline; Medium: 88.0 vs 85.5). This supports the claim that guided attention helps generalization when training data is scarce.
- Generalization across knowledge sources: The model shows similar gains whether the knowledge comes from two dependency parsers or two AMR parsers, despite very different substructure counts, suggesting the mechanism is robust to both syntactic and semantic structures and to parser variation.
- Qualitative analyses align with task-relevant cues: Visualizations indicate attention concentrates on origin/destination/time substructures and remains stable across data sizes, supporting the mechanism’s plausibility and the claim that the model can down-weight noisy relations.
- Sound training setup and statistical testing: The paper provides optimizer details, hyperparameter ranges, and marks significant improvements (p < 0.05). Multiple encoder backbones (NN/RNN/CNN) are compared, strengthening the evidence that the substructure-attention idea, not a particular encoder, drives gains.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and baselines: Results are only on ATIS, a small, saturated benchmark. Strong contemporary baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF for slot filling, bidirectional encoders, character features) are missing. The best baseline here (CNN encoder + RNN) underrepresents the current state of the art, making it hard to judge significance and generality. The absolute gains on the large set (roughly 1–1.1 F1 over CNN) may not hold against stronger models.
- “End-to-end” claim is overstated: The model is trained end-to-end conditional on precomputed parses/AMRs; it does not learn to induce or adapt the knowledge source. Errors from upstream parsers still propagate, and the system is not end-to-end differentiable w.r.t. knowledge extraction. This should be clarified and, ideally, complemented with experiments showing sensitivity to knowledge quality.
- Missing ablations to isolate what helps: Key components are not disentangled. For example:
  - No comparison to a variant without attention (uniformly averaging substructures) to prove the attention mechanism is the source of gains.
  - No comparison using whole-sentence structural encoders with the same tagger backbone to separate “attention-over-substructures” from “better sentence embedding.”
  - No ablation on using dependency/AMR edge labels (currently ignored), which could be informative.
  - Limited reporting on joint vs. knowledge-only vs. chain-only performance for each encoder.
- Efficiency claims not supported: The paper claims efficiency and parallelizability because substructures are modeled separately, but provides no runtime analysis, memory footprint, or scaling with sentence length/number of substructures. Given multiple encodings and attention steps, the practical overhead vs. a standard BiRNN tagger is unclear.
- Architectural details and choices need justification: The attention key is the global sentence embedding u, which can be biased or coarse; alternatives (e.g., token-level or contextual queries, multi-head attention, bi-directional encoders) are not explored. Weight tying between Mkg and Min is asserted but not justified empirically. The tagger appears unidirectional; stronger contextualization (BiGRU/LSTM) could change conclusions.

Questions to authors
- Can you report results with stronger baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, BiGRU taggers, character embeddings) and indicate whether K-SAN still yields gains?
- How much of the improvement is due to attention? Please add an ablation with uniform weighting over substructures, and another with randomly permuted substructures, to quantify the specific contribution of attention and the necessity of correct structures.
- How sensitive is K-SAN to knowledge noise? Provide experiments where parses are systematically corrupted or where only a subset of substructures is provided, and quantify performance degradation.
- Do dependency/AMR relation labels help? Please report a variant that includes labeled edges or small label vocabularies to test whether discarding labels leaves performance on the table.
- Can you provide runtime/complexity comparisons vs. a standard tagger, and how performance scales with the number of substructures?
- What are the token-level error categories where K-SAN helps most (e.g., long-distance slots, coordination)? A breakdown would help understand where structured attention is most beneficial.

Overall assessment
Originality: Moderate. Applying attention over parser/AMR-derived substructures to slot tagging is a neat, targeted extension of memory/attention ideas; similar ideas exist in classification but less so in NLU tagging. Quality: Solid but limited by dataset/scope and missing ablations. Clarity: Generally clear, though claims about end-to-end and efficiency should be tempered and better evidenced. Significance: Promising, especially for low-resource settings, but current evidence on a single benchmark and without stronger baselines tempers impact.

Recommendation: Borderline accept if additional experiments (stronger baselines and key ablations) can be provided; otherwise lean reject due to limited empirical scope.
