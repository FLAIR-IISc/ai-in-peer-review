Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes K-SAN, an attention-over-substructures architecture that uses external linguistic/semantic structure (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs) as “knowledge” to guide sentence representation learning for slot filling. Substructures (root-to-leaf paths) are embedded, an attention distribution over them is computed by matching with a sentence embedding, and the resulting knowledge-guided representation is fused with a recurrent tagger. A joint tagger combines chain-based and knowledge-guided signals.
- Empirical evaluation on ATIS shows consistent F1 improvements over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines and over tree-structured encoders, especially in low-data settings. The approach is robust across knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency, rule-based/JAMR AMR).
- The method is trained end-to-end (including attention over knowledge) and claims efficiency/parallelization benefits by operating on substructures.

In my view, the primary contributions are:
1) A general, end-to-end attention mechanism over external knowledge-derived substructures for sequence tagging (novel application of memory/attention ideas to NLU slot filling with parse/AMR paths).
2) Empirical evidence of robustness across different knowledge sources and under data scarcity, with state-of-the-art performance on ATIS at the time of comparison.
3) A simple joint RNN architecture that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided representations.

Strengths
- Clear methodological idea with plausible motivation: Using attention to selectively weight knowledge-derived substructures is a principled way to mitigate error propagation from noisy parses (by not committing to a single tree encoding) and to capture non-local relations relevant for slots (e.g., origin/destination). The inner-product attention over path embeddings is a natural and differentiable choice.
- Consistent empirical gains and robustness: On ATIS, K-SAN improves over strong non-knowledge baselines (CNN encoder + RNN tagger) by ~1–1.1 F1 on the full set and more in low-resource settings (Table 1). Gains persist across four different knowledge sources (Table 2), suggesting the approach is not brittle to parser choice or to whether knowledge is syntactic vs. semantic.
- Sensible architectural choices and ablations among encoders: Trying NN/RNN/CNN encoders for substructure/sentence embeddings and reporting which works best in different data regimes adds credibility. The joint tagger addresses potential brittleness by combining chain and knowledge streams.
- Qualitative attention analysis supports the mechanism: Figure 5 shows the model attends to substructures that align with slot-relevant relations even with incorrect parses and across training sizes, aligning with the claimed generalization benefits.
- End-to-end training with tied encoders: Tying Min and Mkg reduces parameters and enforces consistent encoding across sentence and substructures, which is a reasonable inductive bias and keeps the approach simple.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and modest absolute gains: Results are entirely on ATIS (small, relatively saturated). Improvements over the strongest baseline are modest on the large set (≈1 F1), and significance is only claimed versus “baseline” models, not versus structural baselines (DCNN/Tree-RNN). Broader validation (e.g., SNIPS, MIT Movie/Restaurant, multi-domain datasets) would strengthen claims of generality.
- Missing stronger modern baselines and modeling choices: No BiLSTM/BiGRU tagger, no CRF decoding on top of RNN, no character/subword features. These are standard for slot filling and often yield sizable gains. It is unclear whether K-SAN would still deliver improvements over a BiLSTM-CRF with contextualized encoders of that era. The comparison to memory-network-style architectures directly applied to tagging is also absent.
- Insufficient ablation of the attention/knowledge contribution: The paper does not report ablations such as (i) uniform averaging over substructures (no attention), (ii) self-attention over words without external knowledge, (iii) using full-tree encodings but with attention over nodes/edges, or (iv) using dependency labels. Such ablations are key to isolating where the gains come from (attention vs. knowledge vs. joint fusion).
- Claims of efficiency/parallelizability are not substantiated: There are no runtime, memory, or throughput results, nor analysis of how performance scales with the number of substructures. Table 2 lists max substructures per resource, but there is no empirical cost comparison vs. chain or tree encoders.
- Some methodological details are under-specified: The precise construction and ordering of root-to-leaf paths (e.g., example “show flights seattle from” is non-canonical), handling of duplicate tokens across paths, maximum path length, tokenization alignment for AMR concepts, and whether dependency/AMR labels were tried are unclear. These details matter for reproducibility and for understanding why semantic labels were excluded.

Questions to authors
- Substructure formation: Please clarify how root-to-leaf paths are linearized (token order, inclusion/exclusion of heads/dependents), how duplicates across paths are handled, and maximum path length. For AMR, how are concepts mapped back to surface tokens, and what happens with multi-word concepts?
- Ablations: Can you report (a) uniform averaging over substructure embeddings (no attention), (b) a self-attention over words baseline without external knowledge, and (c) the effect of including dependency/AMR relation labels? These would help isolate the role of the knowledge-guided attention.
- Stronger baselines: How does K-SAN compare to a BiLSTM-CRF tagger and to a memory-network variant that attends over words/spans rather than over parse paths?
- Runtime: Can you provide training/inference time and memory comparisons vs. chain RNN and tree encoders, and how cost scales with the number of substructures?
- Joint vs. single stream: What is the performance of knowledge-only and chain-only variants of your joint tagger, and how sensitive is α? Did you try BiGRU in the tagger?
- Generalization: Do the gains persist on another public SLU dataset (e.g., SNIPS or MIT Movie/Restaurant)? If not available, can you provide cross-validation-style robustness analysis within ATIS?

Overall assessment against reviewing criteria
- Originality: Moderate. The idea adapts memory/attention mechanisms to knowledge-guided substructures for tagging; incremental yet novel in this specific NLU setting.
- Soundness/correctness: Generally sound; the architecture and training are plausible. Missing ablations and stronger baselines reduce confidence in isolating the source of gains.
- Clarity: Mostly clear, but some implementation details of substructure extraction/encoding need clarification; references are somewhat messy.
- Significance: Limited by dataset scope and modest gains over strong baselines; promising as a direction if validated more broadly.
- Reproducibility: Partially reproducible given hyperparameters, but additional details (path construction, code) would help.
