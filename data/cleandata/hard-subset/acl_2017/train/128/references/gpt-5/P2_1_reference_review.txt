Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), an end-to-end model that uses external linguistic knowledge (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs) to define a set of substructures for an utterance, learns attention over these substructures to obtain a knowledge-guided sentence representation, and conditions an RNN slot tagger on this representation. A joint tagger combines a standard chain RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN.
- Main contributions:
  1) Methodological: An attention mechanism over knowledge-derived substructures that is trained end-to-end for sequence tagging, rather than using knowledge as static features. The idea of “knowledge as teacher” via attention is the core novelty for SLU.
  2) Empirical: Consistent gains on ATIS across data regimes (especially in low-resource settings) and across multiple knowledge sources (two dependency parsers, two AMR parsers), with significance vs strong non-knowledge baselines.
  3) Analysis: Visualization of attention over words and relations suggests the model focuses on semantically salient parts and is somewhat robust to parser errors.

Strengths
- Conceptually sound and timely use of attention to integrate external structure. Prior SLU work typically injected knowledge as features; here, knowledge determines a set of candidate substructures and the model learns where to attend. This is a clean, differentiable formulation (inner-product attention with softmax) that fits well with memory/attention literature and is a natural extension to sequence labeling, where such uses are rarer.
- Consistent empirical improvements, especially in low-resource regimes. On ATIS, K-SAN improves over CNN encoder and RNN baselines in Small and Medium subsets (e.g., K-SAN(CNN) 74.60/87.99 vs CNN 73.57/85.52 F1) and reaches state-of-the-art numbers on Large (94.86–94.97 vs 93.88). The effect is strongest when data are scarce, aligning with the motivation that external knowledge helps generalization.
- Robustness across knowledge sources. Results are similar across different dependency parsers and AMR parsers despite very different substructure counts, supporting the claim that K-SAN is schema-agnostic and can leverage both syntactic and semantic graphs. This reduces concern that gains are due to idiosyncrasies of a single parser.
- Sensible and simple architecture and training setup. Using tied encoders for utterance and substructures, a single vector o per sentence fed to the tagger, and a joint tagger that balances chain and knowledge-guided information (even with a fixed α) yields strong results without heavy engineering. This simplicity is a practical advantage.
- Qualitative analysis supports the mechanism. Visualizations indicate attention highlights origin/destination/time substructures, sometimes even when parsers err, providing face validity for the claim that the model learns to focus on salient parts.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and baselines for SLU. ATIS alone is not sufficient to establish generality; datasets like SNIPS or MIT Restaurant/Movie or multi-domain SLU would strengthen claims. Baselines omit standard strong slot-filling models such as BiLSTM-CRF and self-attentive sequence encoders. Without these, it is hard to quantify how much of the gain comes from knowledge vs stronger sequence modeling. Significance is reported only vs “baseline” models, not vs structural baselines (DCNN/Tree-RNN).
- Incomplete ablations weaken causal claims. Key ablations are missing: (i) knowledge-guided tagger alone vs the joint tagger, (ii) attention vs uniform averaging over substructures, (iii) using dependency/AMR edge labels vs unlabeled structures (authors assert labels may hurt generalization but this should be tested), (iv) learning α vs fixing it at 0.5, and (v) per-time-step conditioning with a dynamic o_t vs a single sentence-level o. These would isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism and design choices.
- Clarity and reproducibility gaps. Important details are not fully specified: exact encoder architectures (depth, non-linearities for NN/CNN/RNN), whether encoders are uni-/bi-directional, hidden sizes chosen per setting, early stopping/seed/variance across runs, how substructures are linearized (order, deduplication), and how many substructures per sentence are used at train/test. The efficiency/parallelizability claim is not substantiated by runtime/memory measurements. Citation formatting and some figures are rough, lowering polish.
- Methodological questions about substructure formation. Using root-to-leaf paths without relation labels may discard critical information and create unnatural token orders (“show flights seattle from”), whose linguistic plausibility is unclear. The choice to ignore internal (non-leaf) nodes to “reduce duplication” may also lose important contexts. These choices need justification and empirical support (e.g., compare with including labels or alternative substructure definitions like edges or short paths).
- Statistical testing and reporting. Using a t-test on F1 may not be the most appropriate; paired bootstrap or approximate randomization per sentence would be preferable. Results appear to be single-run; reporting mean±std over multiple seeds would increase confidence, especially given small margins on the Large set.

Questions to authors
- Substructure construction: How exactly are root-to-leaf paths linearized (node order, inclusion of POS/edge labels)? How are duplicate tokens handled across paths? What is the distribution and average number of substructures per sentence for each knowledge source?
- Joint tagger: Why fix α=0.5 rather than learn it (globally or per sentence)? Did you try per-time-step conditioning (o_t) instead of a single o?
- Baselines: How does K-SAN compare to a BiLSTM-CRF tagger and to a self-attentive encoder without external knowledge? Can you report significance vs DCNN/Tree-RNN?
- Ablations: What happens if you remove attention (uniform averaging), include dependency/AMR labels, or use only the knowledge-guided branch?
- Efficiency: What is the training/inference overhead vs the plain RNN/CNN encoder baselines? Any batching strategy over variable numbers of substructures?

Overall assessment
- Originality: Moderate. Applying attention over knowledge-derived substructures to SLU is a neat and plausible idea, more flexible than prior feature-augmentation or tree encoders.
- Technical soundness: Generally sound, but some design choices are under-justified and ablations are needed to validate them.
- Empirical significance: Promising but limited by single-dataset evaluation and baseline choices.
- Clarity: Reasonably clear high-level exposition but needs more detail and polish for full reproducibility.

Provisional recommendation: Weak accept if the goal is to encourage attention-based integration of external knowledge in SLU; otherwise borderline due to evaluation scope and missing ablations.
