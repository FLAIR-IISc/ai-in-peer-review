Summary and main contributions
The paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses or AMR graphs) is used to derive substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf paths). Each substructure and the sentence are embedded; attention weights are computed via similarity between the sentence embedding and substructure embeddings; a weighted sum yields a knowledge-guided representation that conditions an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain-based RNN with the knowledge-guided RNN.

Contributions as assessed:
1) A neural architecture that attends over knowledge-derived substructures to build a sentence representation for sequence tagging, integrated into an RNN tagger and trained with the tagging objective. This differs from prior “feature enrichment” approaches by learning attention over multiple substructures rather than using a single tree/sentence embedding or adding head-word features.
2) Empirical evidence on ATIS that K-SAN improves slot-filling F1 over strong non-knowledge and knowledge-based baselines, with larger gains in low-resource settings, and competitive state-of-the-art results in the full-data setting.
3) Demonstration that the framework generalizes across heterogeneous knowledge sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers) with similar gains, along with attention visualizations indicating interpretable focus on salient substructures.

Strengths
- Effective and consistent gains on a standard benchmark. On ATIS, K-SAN outperforms baselines including CRF, RNN, and CNN encoder-taggers, and structural baselines (DCNN, Tree-RNN). For example, with full training data, K-SAN (RNN encoder) reaches 94.97 F1 vs. 93.88 (CNN encoder-tagger) and 93.25 (DCNN). In low-resource settings, gains are larger (e.g., Small set: 74.60 F1 with K-SAN (CNN) vs. 73.57 CNN, 70.24 DCNN). Several improvements are statistically significant (t-test p<0.05).
- Knowledge-source robustness. Results are similar when using different parsers and knowledge types (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet dependency; rule-based AMR vs. JAMR), and all K-SAN variants substantially surpass the corresponding CRF-with-knowledge baselines. This supports the claim that the model does not rely on a particular schema and tolerates noise in the upstream parser.
- Clear architectural design and training objective. The model formalization (substructure extraction, embedding via Mkg/Min, attention via softmax over inner products, composition via weighted sum and Mout, integration into an RNN and a joint tagger) is well-specified and differentiable, facilitating end-task training.
- Interpretability via attention. Visualizations show higher attention on origin/destination/time phrases and their relations; notably, even with erroneous parses, attention still highlights informative regions, aligning with the observed robustness and providing diagnostic value.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing attention/memory frameworks. The architecture closely resembles memory networks/dynamic memory approaches, with “facts” replaced by parse-derived substructures and attention computed via content similarity. The main difference is the choice of memory items (parser-derived paths) and the sequence tagging application. Prior structural models and attention/memory methods reduce the incremental novelty.
- Incomplete comparisons to strong sequence labeling baselines. The evaluation omits widely used architectures such as BiLSTM-CRF (with or without character-level encodings), which have been strong on ATIS and related NLU tasks. Without these comparisons, the claimed state-of-the-art is not fully substantiated.
- Scope limited to a single dataset. All experiments are on ATIS; no results on additional NLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MIT datasets) or other slot-filling benchmarks. This constrains claims about generality and robustness, especially given ATIS’s small size and skewed label distribution.
- Missing ablations and analysis of key design choices. The paper reports only the joint tagger; it does not quantify the contribution of (a) attention vs. simple averaging over substructures, (b) the knowledge-guided branch vs. chain-only, (c) the choice of α in the joint model, or (d) using dependency/AMR edge labels. The claimed efficiency/parallelizability is not supported by runtime or complexity measurements (e.g., effect of number/length of substructures, encoder choices).
- “End-to-end” claim is overstated. The model is end-to-end conditioned on externally provided knowledge, but the knowledge extraction (parsing/AMR) is not learned jointly and can be a significant source of non-differentiable errors. The paper partially addresses robustness but does not quantify sensitivity to parser accuracy.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you add or report results against BiLSTM-CRF (with/without character embeddings) and/or LSTM-CRF with sentence-level features? This would contextualize the gains against stronger contemporaneous taggers.
- Ablations: What is the performance of (i) chain-only, (ii) knowledge-only, and (iii) joint with different α (e.g., tuned on dev) vs. fixed 0.5? How does replacing attention with uniform averaging over substructures affect results? What is the effect of untied Mkg/Min?
- Knowledge details: Do edge/label types (dependency relation names, AMR role labels) help if included? You mention dropping labels for generalization; a controlled comparison would clarify the trade-off.
- Efficiency: Please provide training/inference time, memory usage, and throughput comparisons vs. CNN encoder-taggers and DCNN/Tree-RNN, and scaling with max number of substructures. The current efficiency claim lacks empirical support.
- Robustness: Can you quantify sensitivity to parser quality (e.g., via controlled perturbations or using n-best parses), beyond a single qualitative example?
- Integration into GRU: For the GRU variant, how exactly is Mo injected into the gates? Please specify the equations and whether o is concatenated to inputs or added via separate affine transforms.
- Embeddings: Are word embeddings initialized in-domain and fine-tuned during training? Any gains from pretraining on larger corpora?

Overall, the paper presents a clear and practically effective method to leverage external linguistic structure via attention over substructures for slot filling, with solid gains on ATIS and encouraging robustness across knowledge sources. Addressing the missing comparisons, ablations, and efficiency analysis would strengthen the technical contribution and empirical claims.
