Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external structural analyses (dependency trees or AMR) are decomposed into substructures; each substructure and the utterance are embedded, an attention over substructures is computed via inner products with the utterance embedding, and a weighted sum (“knowledge-guided representation”) is injected into an RNN tagger at every time step. The joint model fuses a standard chain RNN with this knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS show modest F1 improvements over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines and over tree/D-CNN encoders; similar gains hold when swapping the structural source (Stanford/SyntaxNet parses, rule-based/JAMR AMR).

Main contributions (in order of strength)
1) A practical integration of parser-derived (or AMR-derived) substructures into a neural tagger via an attention-over-substructures mechanism, implemented end-to-end with the tagger.
2) An empirical study suggesting the approach is relatively robust across structural sources (dependency vs AMR; different parsers) and under limited data (ATIS 1/40, 1/10 splits).
3) A joint architecture that interpolates between a standard chain RNN and the knowledge-guided branch.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive mechanism: The memory/attention formulation over parse-derived substructures is straightforward to implement and differentiable end-to-end. The decomposition into root-to-leaf paths provides a simple way to define “units” to attend to.
- Empirical gains across multiple structural sources: The model improves over reported baselines with both syntactic and semantic structures and across two parsers each, lending some credence to the “generalization to different knowledge” claim.
- Robustness under data scarcity: On reduced ATIS training sets, K-SAN variants outperform CNN/RNN/CRF baselines, supporting the hypothesis that structure can guide learning with limited supervision.
- Qualitative analysis: Visualizations of attention weights on words/relations help illustrate that the model can focus on plausible substructures relevant to slots (origin/destination/date).
- Reasonable experimental hygiene: Hyperparameters, optimizer, and training details are given; statistical significance is reported (though see weaknesses below).

Weaknesses
- Limited and dated evaluation: Results are only on ATIS, a small, saturated benchmark. No evaluation on stronger, diverse datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MIT Movies/Restaurant, or multi-domain modern SLU datasets). The gains on the full ATIS set are modest (~+1 F1 over the best non-structured baseline), which is within the variance often seen on ATIS; a single dataset makes it hard to assess robustness or practical impact.
- Missing stronger baselines: The tagger is a unidirectional RNN/GRU without a CRF decoding layer; there is no comparison to standard, strong baselines such as BiLSTM-CRF (often with character encoders), or joint intent/slot models with CRF. CNN sentence encoders are not the state-of-the-art for slot filling. Without these baselines, it is unclear whether the proposed gains would persist.
- Ambiguous novelty and “end-to-end” claim: The approach is close in spirit to memory/attention models applied to syntactic/semantic structure. Prior neural work leveraged parse trees (Tree-LSTM, dependency-based CNNs), and structured representations as inputs. Here, attention is computed over substructures, but the “knowledge as a teacher” framing and “first end-to-end” claim feel overstated—parsers/AMR are external, fixed components, and the attention is over structures derived from the same input sentence rather than external knowledge bases.
- Methodological gaps and ablations: The joint model sets α=0.5; there is no sensitivity analysis. It is unclear how much each component contributes (chain-only vs knowledge-only vs joint). The decision to drop dependency labels is motivated by generalization but untested; results with labels vs without would be informative. The constant sentence-level knowledge vector o is injected at every time step; a token-conditioned attention (e.g., conditioning on ht or wt) may be more appropriate—an ablation would clarify.
- Significance and variance: Significance testing is reported, but details are missing (number of runs/seeds, whether tests are over runs or per-utterance F1). Given small absolute improvements, multiple runs with different seeds and confidence intervals are needed. Parameter-count matching across models is also not discussed, which could confound comparisons.
- Unsubstantiated claims on efficiency/parallelizability: The paper claims potential efficiency benefits, but provides no runtime or complexity analysis or wall-clock measurements.

Questions to authors
1) Strong baselines: Can you compare against BiLSTM-CRF (with and without character-level encoders) and a recent joint intent+slot model? If not, can you justify why the chosen baselines are representative of current state-of-the-art?
2) Variance and significance: How many random seeds were run per setting? Are the reported gains robust across seeds? Please report mean±std and confidence intervals on F1, and clarify the unit of your t-test.
3) Ablations: Can you report chain-only, knowledge-only, and joint results; sensitivity to α; and effects of including dependency labels? Also, does conditioning attention on token states ht improve results?
4) Generalization: Do gains hold on another public SLU dataset (e.g., SNIPS, MIT domains)? If not feasible, can you provide cross-validation splits or few-shot settings within ATIS to better support the data-scarcity claim?
5) Implementation details: Are parameter counts matched across models? Were hyperparameters tuned equally for baselines and K-SAN? How are substructure encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) capacity-normalized?
6) Efficiency: Can you provide training/inference time comparisons and memory usage versus the strongest baseline?

Additional comments
- The term “knowledge” here refers to sentence-local structure (parses/AMR), not external world knowledge; please clarify terminology.
- Some references are incomplete/garbled; please fix.
- The attention visualization example mentions incorrect parses yet helpful attention; quantitative robustness to parser noise would strengthen this claim (e.g., perturb parse quality and measure performance).

Overall, the mechanism is reasonable and potentially useful, but the experimental validation is too narrow and lacks comparisons to strong, established baselines. Strengthening the empirical section and providing thorough ablations and variance analyses would significantly improve the paper.
