Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The key idea is to derive substructures from external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses or AMR), embed both sentence and substructures, compute an attention distribution over substructures via inner products, and form a knowledge-guided sentence representation that conditions an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS show consistent F1 gains, especially in low-resource settings, and robustness across knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency parses; rule-based/JAMR AMR).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, end-to-end framework that attends over knowledge-derived substructures and integrates the resulting representation into sequence tagging.
2) Empirical evidence that structural attention improves slot filling and is relatively robust to parser errors and scarce data, generalizing across syntactic and semantic resources.
3) State-of-the-art-ish ATIS results with informative attention visualizations, using multiple encoders (CNN/RNN/NN) for knowledge encoding.

Strengths
- Conceptually clear and modular: The approach is an elegant bridge between external linguistic structure and neural attention/memory ideas, avoiding brittle feature engineering. Substructures-as-memory slots with attention is well-motivated, task-agnostic, and compatible with various knowledge sources.

- Consistent empirical gains, especially in low-data regimes: On ATIS, K-SAN outperforms baselines and structural encoders (DCNN, Tree-RNN) across small/medium/large splits, with the largest margins when training data is limited. This supports the claim that knowledge guidance helps generalization and reduces data hunger.

- Knowledge source generalization: The same mechanism works with both dependency trees and AMR, and across different parsers (Stanford/SyntaxNet; rule-based/JAMR), with similar performance and best results around 95 F1. This is a strong point for robustness and applicability.

- Interpretability through attention: The visualizations show attention focusing on origin/destination/time substructures, even in the presence of parser errors. This offers insight and suggests the model is learning meaningful structure rather than overfitting.

- Practicality: The design reuses standard components (CNN/RNN encoders, GRU tagger), trains end-to-end with cross-entropy, and should be easy to implement and extend. Tied Min/Mkg weights and a joint tagger make good engineering sense.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and baselines: Results are only on ATIS (a small, saturated benchmark). Strong contemporary baselines are missing, notably BiLSTM-CRF (with or without character embeddings) and attention-based taggers without external knowledge. The best non-structural baseline here (CNN encoder-tagger) is simpler than widely used BiLSTM-CRF; without such comparisons, it’s hard to situate the contribution.

- Insufficient ablations to isolate what matters: The paper varies the knowledge encoder (NN/RNN/CNN) but lacks critical ablations: attention vs simple averaging over substructures; knowledge vs random or shuffled substructures; joint vs single branch; injection point of o; using labeled vs unlabeled dependencies; and effect of tying Min/Mkg. Without these, the source of gains (attention, extra capacity, or knowledge) remains ambiguous.

- AMR integration details are under-specified: AMR concepts don’t align 1:1 with tokens; how are substructures constructed and embedded relative to surface words? Are concept labels used, and how is vocabulary handled? Figure 6 suggests concept-level sequences, but the text does not clarify alignment or preprocessing, making reproducibility and interpretation difficult.

- Efficiency and parallelizability claims are not substantiated: The paper claims potential efficiency due to separate substructure modeling, but provides no complexity analysis or runtime/memory measurements versus baselines. ATIS is too small to meaningfully assess efficiency.

- Margins are modest on the full set and significance reporting is thin: Improvements over the strongest baseline are around 1–1.5 F1 on the large set. While t-tests are referenced, details (test setup, variance across runs, seeds) are missing. Given the small dataset and potential variance, more rigorous reporting would help.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Can you provide results against a strong BiLSTM-CRF baseline (with/without character embeddings), and an attention-based sentence encoder without external knowledge? This would better quantify the incremental benefit of knowledge-guided attention.

- Ablations: 
  a) Replace attention with uniform averaging over substructures. 
  b) Use random/shuffled substructures. 
  c) Remove the joint branch (only knowledge-guided) and vary α. 
  d) Include dependency relation labels to test whether labeled structures help or harm generalization.

- AMR specifics: How are AMR nodes mapped to embeddings? Do you use concept strings, surface alignments, or a learned mapping? How do you handle concepts absent from the token vocabulary?

- Robustness to parser noise: Can you quantify performance as a function of parse quality (e.g., using gold parses, or injecting noise), beyond a single qualitative visualization?

- Efficiency: Please report training/inference time and memory versus the best non-knowledge baseline, and the number of substructures per sentence for each knowledge source.

Additional comments
- Clarify how the small/medium splits are constructed (random seeds, stratification) and whether results are averaged over multiple splits/runs.
- Provide per-slot F1 to illustrate which slot types benefit most from structural attention.
- Code and configuration release would materially aid reproducibility.

Overall recommendation
The paper presents a neat and generally useful way to inject external structure via attention into sequence tagging, with solid gains and good robustness across knowledge sources. However, the evaluation breadth and ablations are insufficient to unambiguously attribute the gains to knowledge-guided attention, and comparisons to stronger baselines are missing. With stronger baselines and ablations, this would be a clear accept; as is, it’s a borderline accept leaning positive due to conceptual clarity, robustness, and promising empirical results.
