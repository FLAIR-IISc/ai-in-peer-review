Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling in task-oriented NLU. Given an utterance, a parser (or other knowledge source) provides a set of substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf dependency paths or AMR subgraphs). Each substructure and the whole sentence are embedded; an attention distribution over substructures is computed by inner products with the sentence embedding; a weighted sum forms a knowledge-guided vector o, which conditions an RNN tagger. A joint tagger interpolates a plain chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS (small/medium/full splits) show consistent F1 improvements over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines and structural baselines (Tree-RNN, DCNN), and results are robust across knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependencies; rule-based/JAMR AMR). Visualizations suggest sensible attention.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A simple, effective attention mechanism over parser-derived substructures integrated into sequence tagging, trained end-to-end with the tagger. Primary novelty is the “knowledge as a teacher” view that supervises attention over substructures.
2) Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided attention improves slot filling, especially in low-resource settings, and generalizes across heterogeneous knowledge sources (syntax and semantics).
3) A practical, modular framework that can plug different encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) and different knowledge sources without tailoring.

Strengths
- Clear, general mechanism to inject structure via attention over substructures. The mapping from sentence to attention over substructures is simple, differentiable, and integrates cleanly with standard RNN taggers. This is easier to adopt than many tree-structured models and offers a principled alternative to “feature augmentation.”
- Consistent empirical gains, especially in low-resource regimes. On ATIS small/medium, K-SAN(CNN) improves substantially over strong CNN encoder baselines (e.g., 74.6 vs 73.6 F1 on small; 88.0 vs 85.5 on medium), with statistical significance reported. The low-resource improvements substantiate the central claim that knowledge can guide learning when data are scarce.
- Robustness across knowledge sources and parsers. Using SyntaxNet vs Stanford dependencies, and AMR (rule-based/JAMR), yields similar gains. This supports the generality claim: the method is not tied to a particular parser or to syntactic vs semantic relations.
- Analysis and interpretability. The attention visualizations align with intuitively salient regions (origin, destination, time), and even when the dependencies are noisy, the model appears to focus correctly. This makes the approach appealing for debugging and for motivating the use of external structure.
- Simple joint model that mitigates parser noise. Interpolation of the chain-based and knowledge-guided hidden states is practical and likely explains stability against poor parses.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and missing stronger baselines. ATIS is small and well-studied; no results on other NLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, multi-domain benchmarks) or on joint intent+slot tasks. Contemporary strong baselines (BiLSTM-CRF with character embeddings, CRF decoding, possibly self-attention or Transformers) are absent. Restricting to lexical features ensures fairness but limits the strength of the comparison and the claim of state-of-the-art.
- End-to-end claim is partial; knowledge is fixed and errors cannot be corrected. The parser/AMR graphs are external and frozen; gradients do not flow into the knowledge source. Thus, while the attention is learned end-to-end, the “teacher” is not. This limits robustness to severe parser noise and undercuts the “end-to-end” positioning.
- Ablations are insufficient to isolate where gains come from. Key missing ablations include: attention without knowledge (e.g., self-attention over words or n-grams), using the set of substructures without attention (uniform averaging), using dependency labels vs unlabeled relations, removing the joint interpolation (α=0.5 fixed), and removing Mout. Without these, it’s hard to attribute gains specifically to knowledge-guided attention vs added capacity/context.
- Efficiency and scaling claims are unsubstantiated. The paper asserts efficiency and parallelizability due to modeling substructures separately, but reports no runtime, memory, or complexity analysis, nor scaling experiments with longer inputs or more substructures. Table 2 shows variability in the number of substructures (e.g., 53 vs 8), yet no analysis of computational impact or performance as this number grows.
- Methodological details that affect reproducibility and significance are under-specified. The dev set split for ATIS is not described; the statistical test lacks details (variance across runs, random seeds); hyperparameter tuning space is narrow; and it’s unclear whether all baselines were re-implemented under identical settings. Some references are incomplete, and exact implementations of DCNN/Tree-RNN encoding for slot tagging are not fully specified.

Questions to authors
- Can you report results against a BiLSTM-CRF baseline (with and without character embeddings) and/or a word-level self-attention baseline, under the same lexical-feature constraint? This would better position the contribution.
- Please provide ablations: (a) no attention (uniform over substructures); (b) attention over surface n-grams instead of knowledge-derived substructures; (c) using dependency labels; (d) varying α beyond 0.5 or learning α; (e) with/without Mout, and with untied Min/Mkg.
- What is the precise construction and ordering of substructures? For dependency trees, do you linearize root-to-leaf paths by node order in the path? How do you handle repeated tokens across multiple paths? How is position encoded within a substructure?
- Can you quantify efficiency: average number of substructures per sentence per knowledge source, training/inference time, and memory footprint vs baselines? How does performance and speed vary with the number/length of substructures?
- How robust is K-SAN to severe parser noise? Can you simulate noise in the structures and report performance degradation? Conversely, can the model leverage richer structures (e.g., full tree with labels) to improve further?

Overall, this is a neat and practical idea with encouraging results and analysis. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and ablations would significantly improve the paper and clarify the source of gains.
