Summary
The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external linguistic structure (e.g., dependency trees or AMR graphs) is decomposed into substructures, each embedded into a vector. The model computes attention over these substructures conditioned on a sentence embedding, aggregates them into a knowledge-guided representation, and feeds this representation into an RNN-based tagger. A joint tagger combines a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS (with small/medium/full training splits) show consistent gains over non-structural and structural baselines, with particularly large improvements in low-data regimes. The approach appears robust to parser errors and generalizes across multiple structure sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency, rule-based/JAMR AMR).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, end-to-end attention mechanism over linguistically motivated substructures to derive sentence representations for sequence tagging.
2) Empirical evidence of robustness and generalization across different sources and types of linguistic structure (dependency and AMR) and across data sizes, with strong low-resource gains and competitive/superior results on ATIS.
3) A modular joint tagging architecture that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided signals.

Strengths
- Clear, modular architecture that integrates structure via soft attention. The model is easy to implement: substructure encoders (NN/RNN/CNN), attention via u^T m_i and softmax, aggregation, and a joint RNN tagger. It is trained end-to-end without hand-crafted features, addressing error-propagation concerns in feature-engineering approaches.

- Consistent improvements across data regimes, especially in low-resource settings. On ATIS, K-SAN(CNN) improves over strong non-structural baselines (CNN encoder-tagging) and structural baselines (DCNN/Tree-RNN), with statistically significant gains vs baselines (Table 1). Gains are most notable on Small and Medium splits, supporting the claim that structured attention helps generalization with scarce data.

- Generalization to different knowledge resources and parsers. The method works with both syntactic (dependency) and semantic (AMR) structures and different parsers (Stanford vs SyntaxNet; rule-based AMR vs JAMR), achieving very similar performance (Table 2). This supports the claim that K-SAN does not depend on a particular schema and is robust to variations and noise in upstream analysis.

- Qualitative analyses are informative. Attention visualizations show the model focuses on semantically salient substructures (origin, destination, dates), and appears resilient to parsing errors. This increases interpretability and helps explain why low-resource gains materialize.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and missing strong baselines. Only ATIS is used. There is no comparison to widely adopted stronger architectures for slot filling (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, character-enhanced models, joint intent-slot models), which often set higher bars on ATIS. The paper claims state-of-the-art but the statistical tests in Table 1 are only vs non-structural baselines, not vs structural models; and broader SOTA comparisons are absent. This weakens the empirical case.

- Insufficient ablation and sensitivity analysis. The reported results all use the joint tagger; the paper does not quantify: (a) the marginal contribution of the knowledge-guided branch vs chain-only under the same capacity, (b) the effect of attention (e.g., uniform/random attention, or removing attention), (c) the sensitivity to α in the joint objective, (d) the impact of using/ignoring relation labels, or (e) the effect of substructure construction choices. Such ablations are crucial to validate that attention over substructures is the key factor.

- Efficiency and parallelizability claims are not substantiated. The paper claims potential efficiency advantages because substructures are modeled separately, but provides no training/inference speed, memory, or complexity measurements, nor comparisons vs RNN/CNN baselines. Without evidence, the efficiency claim is speculative.

- Ambiguities in substructure construction and modeling details. The method extracts root-to-leaf paths but the token ordering and handling of repeated tokens, punctuation, and multi-word entities are not fully specified. Relation labels are discarded “for better generalization,” but it is plausible that labels (especially in AMR) add useful signal; this choice should be justified empirically. Tying Mkg and Min weights is mentioned but not motivated or compared to untied variants.

- Framing may overstate “knowledge” use. The current instantiation uses sentence-internal structure (parses/AMRs) as “knowledge.” While valuable, this differs from leveraging external KB facts; clarifying this distinction would avoid overclaiming and better position the contribution as structure-guided attention.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide ablations: (i) joint vs chain-only under equal parameter budgets; (ii) knowledge-guided branch alone; (iii) uniform attention over substructures; (iv) random substructures; (v) varying α; and (vi) using relation labels vs not? Which component drives the gains?

- How exactly are substructures linearized and embedded? For dependency paths like “show flights seattle from,” what is the ordering rule? How are repeated nodes/words handled? For AMR, how are concepts aligned to tokens, and how are edges traversed to produce substructures?

- How sensitive is K-SAN to parser quality? You compare Stanford vs SyntaxNet; can you quantify performance as a function of injected noise or parse quality metrics? Does the number of substructures correlate with performance?

- Can you report runtime and memory usage vs RNN/CNN baselines and structural baselines (DCNN/Tree-RNN) on ATIS, both training and inference?

- How does K-SAN compare to a BiLSTM-CRF tagger (with/without character embeddings) trained on the same features? If possible, please include this comparison to position your method against prevalent strong baselines.

- Have you tried the approach on additional datasets (e.g., MIT Restaurant/Movie, SNIPS, or CoNLL NER) to test generality beyond ATIS?

Overall assessment
The idea of attending over linguistically grounded substructures for sequence tagging is appealing and the empirical gains on ATIS—especially in low-resource settings and across multiple structure sources—are promising. However, the current submission lacks critical comparisons and ablations, and does not substantiate efficiency claims. With stronger baselines, thorough ablations, clearer specification of substructure construction, and runtime evidence, this work would be significantly strengthened. I lean toward rejection in its current form but see a clear path to a compelling paper.
