Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees or AMR graphs) is decomposed into substructures (root-to-leaf paths), each encoded into a vector. The model obtains a sentence embedding, computes attention over substructure embeddings via inner-product softmax, aggregates them, and injects the resulting knowledge-guided representation into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines the chain-based and knowledge-guided hidden states.
- Claimed contributions: (1) end-to-end learning of attention over knowledge-guided substructures for sequence tagging; (2) generalization across heterogeneous knowledge sources (syntax and semantics); (3) efficiency/parallelizability due to substructure decomposition.
- As I see it, the primary contributions are: (1) an application of single-hop memory/attention over syntactic/semantic substructures to slot filling with an integrated tagger, with empirical gains on ATIS; (2) a demonstration that heterogeneous external parses (dependency and AMR) yield similar improvements and that attention appears robust in low-data regimes; (3) a qualitative analysis of structural attention.

Strengths
- Clear, modular architecture that bridges memory/attention concepts and structured linguistic knowledge. The formulation—encoding substructures, attending via u^T m_i, and integrating into an RNN tagger—is simple, differentiable, and easy to adopt.
- Empirical improvements on ATIS over reasonably strong baselines, including CNN sentence encoders and structure-aware DCNN/Tree-RNN, with statistically significant gains and especially larger improvements in the low-resource setting. The joint tagger is a pragmatic design that stabilizes performance against noisy knowledge.
- Knowledge source agnosticism. The same mechanism works with multiple parsers (Stanford/SyntaxNet) and with AMR graphs, with comparable results despite very different numbers and granularity of substructures. This supports the claim of generality and robustness to knowledge mismatches.
- Interpretability via attention visualization. The analysis of attention highlighting origin/destination/time phrases, including cases with parsing errors, provides some evidence that the attention mechanism learns to focus on task-salient substructures rather than merely echoing the parse.
- Careful comparisons of different encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) for knowledge and sentence, and a consistent training recipe; reporting significance tests on multiple data regimes.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to memory/attention models. The core mechanism is a single-hop memory network with content-based attention over knowledge-derived “facts.” The novelty is primarily in the choice of memory items (dependency/AMR substructures) and the downstream task (slot tagging). Prior work in memory/structural attention and syntax-aware encoders is substantial; the paper would benefit from a sharper positioning and ablations that isolate what K-SAN adds beyond a standard memory network applied to parse-derived segments.
- Evaluation scope is narrow. Results are only on ATIS with lexical features, while current SLU practice includes stronger encoders (biLSTM/CRF, character/subword, contextual embeddings). The main gains over the RNN baseline on the full set are modest (~2 absolute F1 and ~1 over strong CNN), and it is unclear whether K-SAN still helps when paired with a competitive biLSTM-CRF or pretrained embeddings. No results on other datasets (e.g., Snips, multi-domain datasets) or on noisy ASR transcripts.
- Missing ablations to validate the role of “knowledge as a teacher.” Key controls are absent: (a) replace attention with uniform or random weights over substructures; (b) remove knowledge but keep the same architectural capacity (extra input vector learned from the sentence) to ensure gains are not from capacity alone; (c) use the dependency/AMR edge labels to test whether labels help or hurt; (d) learn α instead of fixing 0.5, or use a gating mechanism. Without these, claims about knowledge-guided attention and reduced error propagation are not fully substantiated.
- Claims of efficiency/parallelizability are not supported with measurements. Runtime, parameter counts, and scaling with number of substructures are not reported. In practice, building and encoding many substructures per sentence can be nontrivial, and the claim that time may not increase linearly with sentence length is unconvincing without empirical evidence.
- External supervision confound. The method relies on external parsers (PTB-trained dependency parsers, AMR parsers), which introduce additional supervision and potentially domain mismatch. While this is reasonable, the paper should more explicitly discuss fairness against baselines that do not benefit from such resources, and ideally compare to baselines augmented with the same knowledge as features in equally strong neural architectures (e.g., biLSTM-CRF + syntactic features).

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the number and construction of substructures? Have you tried alternative segmentations (e.g., all dependency edges, n-gram paths, or graph neighborhoods) and a uniform attention baseline?
- What happens if you use dependency/AMR edge labels in the encoder? Do labels improve robustness and disambiguation?
- How would K-SAN perform when integrated into a biLSTM-CRF tagger and/or with character/subword features or pretrained embeddings? Do the gains persist?
- Can α in the joint tagger be learned (globally or per token) or replaced with a gate? Did you try multi-hop attention?
- Do you have runtime/memory statistics and parameter counts compared to the strongest baselines?

Additional comments
- Please clarify how substructures are linearized (order of tokens along paths), whether word order is preserved, and whether repeated tokens occur across multiple substructures. The examples in Figure 3 are slightly confusing.
- The claim about reduced error propagation needs empirical support with noisy parses; consider a controlled experiment degrading parses.
- Consider reporting results on ASR hypotheses to strengthen the SLU relevance.
- Minor: detail dev split construction for ATIS, and ensure baselines were tuned comparably. Include code or more hyperparameter details for reproducibility.

Overall: A solid, well-executed adaptation of memory/attention to knowledge-guided substructures for slot filling with encouraging results and interpretable behavior. However, the conceptual novelty is moderate and the evaluation lacks breadth and critical ablations. I lean toward borderline acceptance if the authors can address the ablations and strengthen comparisons; otherwise, this is a strong workshop/short paper.
