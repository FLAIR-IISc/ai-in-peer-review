- Proposes K-SAN: attention over parser/AMR-derived substructures to create a knowledge-guided sentence representation injected into an RNN tagger.  
- Joint tagger mixes chain RNN and knowledge-guided RNN to hedge between plain and structured signals.  
- Main contribution: general, end-to-end mechanism to incorporate external linguistic/semantic structure into sequence tagging.  
- Empirical claim: K-SAN improves slot filling on ATIS, especially in low-resource settings, and is robust across different parsers and knowledge types.  
- Interpretability: attention visualizations highlight relevant substructures and appear to mitigate parser error propagation.  
- Modular design: any substructure encoder and any parser/knowledge source can be used; model is end-to-end differentiable.  
- Consistent gains reported, with statistically significant improvements on Small/Medium splits and SOTA on Large for ATIS.  
- Robustness evidence: comparable performance using different dependency parsers and AMR parsers despite varying substructure counts.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to ATIS (single-domain), lacking multi-domain or additional datasets to support generality.  
- Weakness: missing stronger baselines such as BiLSTM-CRF or RNN/GRU+CRF, making competitiveness unclear.  
- Weakness: insufficient ablations (joint vs. knowledge-only, attention vs. averaging, use of relation labels, sensitivity to substructure counts/encoders).  
- Weakness: efficiency/parallelizability claims lack runtime, complexity, or memory comparisons despite potentially many substructures.  
- Weakness: methodological details under-specified (substructure token ordering, handling variable counts, encoder hyperparameters, GRU integration).  
- Asks for comparisons to RNN/GRU+CRF and BiLSTM-CRF or discussion of CRF impact and K-SAN integration into BiLSTM-CRF.  
- Requests ablation of attention vs. simple averaging/max pooling over substructures.  
- Asks how sensitive results are to the joint-mixing parameter α and whether it is learned or tuned.  
- Queries whether dependency/AMR relation labels were used in encodings and their impact on performance/generalization.  
- Requests clarification on substructure construction: word order, repeated node handling, batching/padding/memory limits.  
- Requests training/inference time and memory comparisons vs strongest baselines and scaling with number of substructures.  
- Overall assessment: promising, well-motivated approach with useful low-resource gains, but currently a borderline accept pending stronger baselines, broader datasets, ablations, and efficiency/reproducibility details.
