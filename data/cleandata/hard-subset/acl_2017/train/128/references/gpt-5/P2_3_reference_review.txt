Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, it extracts substructures from external knowledge (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in dependency trees or AMR edges), encodes each substructure into a vector, computes an attention distribution over substructures conditioned on the sentence embedding, aggregates them, and feeds the knowledge-guided representation into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines standard chain-based and knowledge-guided hidden states.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) A task-specific formulation of attention over knowledge-derived substructures for sequence tagging, trained end-to-end with the tagger. This differs from prior work that used linguistic knowledge as fixed features or whole-structure encoders (Tree-LSTM/DCNN) without selective attention.
  2) Empirical evidence that the approach is robust to small training data and generalizes across knowledge sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers) on ATIS, with consistent improvements over non-knowledge baselines.
  3) A simple, modular architecture (supporting NN/CNN/RNN encoders for substructures) and qualitative attention analyses illustrating where the model focuses.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent modeling idea with explicit mechanism to mitigate whole-structure brittleness. Prior structural models build one sentence vector from an entire parse; by attending to substructures, K-SAN can downweight noisy parts of imperfect parses. This is a principled response to error propagation and mismatches between KBs and data; it is also supported by visualizations showing attention concentrating on origin/destination/time spans even under parsing errors.
- Consistent empirical gains over non-structural baselines across data sizes, particularly in low-resource regimes. On ATIS, K-SAN(CNN) notably improves F1 by roughly +1–2 points over a strong CNN encoder baseline, with larger gains in small/medium settings. This supports the claim that external structure acts as an effective inductive bias when labeled data are scarce.
- Knowledge-source generality. The same architecture works with both syntax (Stanford and SyntaxNet dependencies) and semantics (rule-based AMR and JAMR). Results are very similar across these resources and consistently outperform non-knowledge baselines, suggesting the approach is not tied to a specific parser or representation.
- Architectural flexibility and reasonable design choices. Supporting multiple encoders (CNN/RNN/NN) for substructures and tying weights of Mkg/Min simplify the system while enabling competitive performance; the joint tagger design sensibly balances chain-based and knowledge-guided representations, improving robustness to noisy knowledge.
- Clarity and analysis. The paper lays out the pipeline, equations, and training setup clearly, includes attention heatmaps, and provides significance tests for major comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and baselines. ATIS is a single, relatively small and saturated benchmark; improvements on the large set are modest (~1 F1). Stronger modern baselines for slot filling (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, character or subword features, contextual embeddings) are absent. Without comparisons to state-of-the-art sequence taggers, the significance beyond the chosen baselines is hard to assess.
- “End-to-end” claim is overstated. While the network is trained end-to-end given the knowledge-derived substructures, the approach depends on external parsers at both train and test time. This introduces a non-trainable, error-prone component in the inference loop. The paper should clarify this limitation and avoid implying parser-free end-to-end operation.
- Missing ablations critical to understanding where gains come from. The paper reports only joint tagger results; it does not show the standalone chain-based vs knowledge-guided RNN vs joint, nor an attention ablation (e.g., uniform averaging over substructures), nor the effect of tying Mkg and Min. These are important to justify that attention over substructures, rather than added capacity or sentence-level features, drives the gains.
- Efficiency claim lacks evidence. The paper asserts efficiency and parallelizability (since substructures are modeled separately) but provides no complexity analysis or runtime/latency measurements. This matters in SDS scenarios and for fair comparison to alternatives (e.g., Tree-LSTM, DCNN).
- Underutilization and underanalysis of knowledge. Dependency labels are ignored “for better generalization,” but this decision is not empirically justified; using labels could strengthen the guidance. Similarly, the choice of root-to-leaf paths as the substructure set is not motivated or compared to alternatives (e.g., dependency arcs, paths between key entities, or AMR role labels). A deeper error analysis quantifying sensitivity to parser quality (beyond one example) would strengthen the robustness claim.

Questions to authors
- Please report ablations: (a) chain-based RNN only, (b) knowledge-guided RNN only, (c) joint, and (d) no attention (uniform averaging of substructures). How much does each component contribute?
- How sensitive is performance to the number and type of substructures? Why choose root-to-leaf paths? Have you tried including dependency/AMR labels, or alternative substructure definitions (e.g., shortest paths between detected entities)?
- What is the test-time overhead versus baselines? Please include average parsing time and model inference time per utterance, and end-to-end latency comparisons to Tree-RNN/DCNN/BiLSTM-CRF if possible.
- How robust is K-SAN to parser noise quantitatively? For example, can you bucket test utterances by parser confidence or induced parse quality and report F1 by bucket?
- The joint tagger uses α=0.5. Is this tuned per dev set? How does performance vary with α? Does the optimal α change with data size or knowledge source?
- Could you add a stronger tagging baseline (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) and/or a second dataset to better establish generality and significance?

Overall assessment
- Novelty: Moderate. Applying attention over knowledge-derived substructures to slot filling is a reasonable and useful twist on memory/attention models; however, the core components are standard.
- Technical quality: Sound, but missing key ablations and efficiency evidence.
- Clarity: Good.
- Significance: Limited by scope (single dataset, modest gains vs strong baselines not shown). The knowledge-source generalization is a plus.
- I lean toward borderline/weak accept if ablations and stronger baselines can be added; otherwise, weak reject due to limited empirical breadth and missing analyses.
