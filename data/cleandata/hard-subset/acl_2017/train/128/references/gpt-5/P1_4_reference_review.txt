Summary and contributions
This paper introduces Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling in task-oriented NLU. The model parses an input utterance into a set of knowledge-guided substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in a dependency tree, or paths in AMR), encodes each substructure into a vector, computes attention over these substructures conditioned on the utterance embedding, aggregates them into a knowledge-guided sentence representation, and injects this representation into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a plain chain RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS show gains over several baselines, particularly in low-resource settings, and demonstrate similar improvements across different knowledge sources (dependency trees from Stanford/SyntaxNet and AMR from two parsers).

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple and general mechanism to incorporate external linguistic/semantic structure into sequence tagging via attention over substructures, learned end-to-end.
2) Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided structural attention improves slot filling, especially with limited training data, and is robust to parser choice and knowledge type (syntax vs. AMR).
3) Interpretability via attention visualizations; and a practical joint architecture that hedges between chain and knowledge-guided models.

Strengths
- Clear and general idea: Treat knowledge as a teacher that guides attention over linguistically motivated substructures. The design is modular (any encoder for substructures, any parser/knowledge source) and end-to-end differentiable. It is an intuitive bridge between structured NLP signals and neural attention/memory ideas without handcrafting features.

- Consistent gains, strongest in low-resource regimes: On ATIS, K-SAN (especially with CNN encoders) outperforms RNN and CNN baselines and structural baselines, with statistically significant improvements on Small and Medium splits, and reaches state-of-the-art results on the Large split. The low-data gains are practically important for many NLU deployments.

- Knowledge-source agnosticism and robustness: The method works comparably with dependency parses from different toolkits and with AMR graphs from two parsers, despite very different substructure counts. This supports the claim that K-SAN does not require a specific schema and can leverage heterogeneous knowledge resources.

- Interpretability: The attention visualizations highlight intuitively relevant substructures (origin, destination, dates), and the model appears to maintain reasonable attention even with parser errors, supporting the claim that attention mitigates error propagation compared to hard features.

- Simple integration with sequence tagging: The joint RNN combines chain-based and knowledge-guided hidden states with a single mixing parameter, is easy to train, and appears to stabilize against noisy knowledge.

Weaknesses
- Limited experimental scope: Evaluation is only on ATIS (single-domain, relatively small, long saturated). While useful for proof-of-concept, more diverse datasets (multi-domain SLU, e.g., SNIPS or multiWOZ slot tagging analogs, or at least another public SLU corpus) are necessary to substantiate generality. The paper claims robustness and generalization; cross-dataset evidence would strengthen this substantially.

- Missing stronger baselines: For sequence labeling, BiLSTM-CRF (and RNN-CRF) were established by 2015–2016 as strong baselines for IOB tagging. The paper compares to Elman/GRU RNNs without CRF decoding, and to CNN/DCNN/Tree-RNN encoders feeding an RNN. Without a CRF baseline and a BiLSTM architecture, it is hard to gauge the competitiveness of K-SAN relative to standard practice. At minimum, RNN/GRU + CRF should be included; ideally BiLSTM-CRF.

- Ablation depth is insufficient: Results are reported mostly for the joint tagger with α fixed at 0.5. Important ablations are missing: (i) knowledge-guided tagger alone vs. joint; (ii) attention vs. uniform averaging over substructures; (iii) impact of using relation labels (dependency labels, AMR relation types), which the paper omits “for generalization” but may be informative; (iv) sensitivity to the number/length of substructures and to encoder choice beyond simply swapping NN/RNN/CNN.

- Efficiency claims unsubstantiated: The paper claims efficiency and parallelizability because substructures are modeled separately, but there are no runtime/complexity analyses or wall-clock comparisons versus baselines or structural encoders. Given that substructures can be numerous (e.g., up to 53), evidence is needed to support the efficiency claim.

- Methodological clarity gaps: Several design choices are under-specified: the exact token ordering inside substructures (examples suggest root-to-leaf order but not formalized), handling of variable numbers of substructures (padding/truncation; max memory size), encoder hyperparameters (e.g., number of CNN filters), and training details for tied encoders Min/Mkg. The integration into GRU gating is mentioned but not fully specified beyond adding Mo; clarity would aid reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Can you add a comparison to RNN/GRU+CRF and BiLSTM-CRF? If not, can you discuss why CRF decoding would not change conclusions? Have you tried plugging K-SAN into a BiLSTM-CRF?

- What is the impact of attention versus simple averaging or max pooling over substructures? Please provide an ablation.

- How sensitive is performance to α in the joint tagger? Do you learn α or tune it per dataset?

- Did you try incorporating dependency/AMR relation labels in the substructure encodings? If so, how does it affect performance and generalization?

- Please clarify substructure construction and encoding: exact word order within a path, whether intermediate non-leaf nodes contribute multiple times, and how you handle variable counts (e.g., batching, memory size limits).

- Can you report training/inference time and memory usage versus the strongest baseline, and how runtime scales with number of substructures?

Overall assessment
A solid, well-motivated idea with clean implementation and promising gains, particularly in low-resource settings and across knowledge sources. However, the empirical evaluation is narrow (ATIS only) and misses strong modern baselines and key ablations. With additional comparisons (CRF/BiLSTM-CRF), broader datasets, and ablation/efficiency evidence, this work would be a strong accept. In its current form, I lean toward a borderline accept, contingent on addressing the experimental gaps.
