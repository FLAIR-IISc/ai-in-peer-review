Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external “knowledge” (e.g., dependency parses or AMR graphs) is decomposed into substructures. Each substructure and the input sentence are embedded; attention over substructures (via dot-product softmax) produces a knowledge-guided sentence representation that is injected into an RNN slot tagger. A joint tagger balances a chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS show modest gains over CNN/RNN baselines and parse-aware encoders, with larger gains in low-resource settings. The approach is claimed to be end-to-end, general across knowledge types, and efficient.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An architecture that attends over parser/graph-derived substructures and integrates the resulting representation into a sequence tagger, evaluated on slot filling.
2) Empirical evidence that such substructure attention yields improved F1 on ATIS, particularly with limited training data, and appears robust across different knowledge sources (dependency vs. AMR, different parsers).
3) Qualitative visualizations of attention over words/relations illustrating some robustness to parser errors.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea and reasonably well-motivated: Using attention to select salient substructures addresses a known issue with flat sequence models ignoring non-local relations. The separation of Mkg/Min and joint tagger is sensible and easy to implement.
- Low-resource gains: Improvements are more pronounced in Small/Medium subsets (e.g., K-SAN(CNN) 74.6 vs. 73.6 F1 over the best non-knowledge baseline on Small; larger gains vs. CRF/RNN), supporting the claim that structural priors can help when data is scarce.
- Knowledge-source generalization: Results are comparable across Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency parses and AMR produced by two parsers, suggesting the method is not brittle to a particular structural formalism or parser (albeit on a single domain).
- End-to-end training with attention: Unlike feature-augmentation methods, the attention mechanism lets the model decide which substructures matter; visualizations are informative and show attention on origin/destination/time spans.

Weaknesses
- Limited and dated evaluation: Only ATIS is used. ATIS is small, nearly saturated, and idiosyncratic. Strong, established baselines for slot filling (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with character embeddings; joint intent/slot models; CRF decoding over RNN emissions) are missing. The reported best Large-set F1 (≈95) is only ~1 point above a simple CNN encoder-tag baseline and may not hold against stronger baselines.
- Incomplete ablations: It is unclear what portion of the gain comes from (a) additional capacity, (b) sentence-level context vector o irrespective of attention, or (c) the attention over substructures. Missing ablations include: no-attention (uniform averaging), using random/shuffled structures, using only u (no h), untied Mkg/Min, feeding concatenated substructure embeddings, and removing Mout. The joint vs. single tagger contribution is also not isolated.
- Statistical rigor and variance: The paper marks † for significance vs. baselines, but does not state the number of runs/seeds, variance, or whether significance holds vs. structure-aware baselines. ATIS results are known to vary by >0.5 F1 across seeds; without reporting mean±std over multiple runs, conclusions are fragile.
- Efficiency claims unsubstantiated: The paper claims efficiency/parallelizability, but provides no training/inference time, memory footprint, or latency comparisons. In practice, running an external parser/AMR system can dominate latency and reduce deployability in SLU pipelines; this is not discussed or measured.
- Dependence on external parsers and domain shift: Although the authors claim robustness to parser errors, there is no systematic analysis of performance under parser noise, out-of-domain parses, or ASR errors. The paper uses text transcripts and in-domain embeddings; SLU robustness under realistic speech/ASR conditions is not evaluated.
- Fairness and baseline configuration: Some baselines (e.g., CRF with syntactic heads, DCNN/Tree-RNN) may be underpowered or not state-of-the-art for slot filling. The CNN encoder-tag baseline is strong, but the absence of CRF decoding on top of RNN/CNN, character-level inputs, or bidirectional encoders weakens the empirical case.

Questions to authors
- Strong baselines: Can you compare against a BiLSTM-CRF tagger (with/without char embeddings) and a BiLSTM with CRF decoding using the same word embeddings and training protocol? Also, a joint intent/slot BiLSTM with CRF is a common baseline.
- Ablations: Please provide ablations for (i) no attention (uniform p_i), (ii) random/shuffled substructures, (iii) using only u vs. h+u, (iv) untied Mkg/Min, (v) removing Mout, and (vi) single knowledge-guided tagger vs. joint tagger, all with multiple seeds.
- Variance and significance: How many runs per setting? Please report mean±std F1 and proper significance testing vs. the best non-knowledge and best structure-aware baselines.
- Parser dependence: What is the sensitivity to parser quality? E.g., deliberately degrade parses or use out-of-domain parsers. How does K-SAN perform if only partial parses are available? What is the impact of using gold parses (if available) to bound the potential?
- Efficiency: Please report training/inference time per sentence and memory vs. your best baseline, including parsing time for dependency/AMR. How many substructures are typically attended (entropy of p_i), and is there a way to sparsify attention for efficiency?
- Generalization: Beyond ATIS, can you evaluate on another SLU dataset (e.g., SNIPS) or a different tagging task (NER/slot filling in another domain) to substantiate the generalization claim? How does the method fare with ASR hypotheses rather than gold transcripts?
- Hyperparameters: How is α chosen and how sensitive are results to α? How sensitive are results to the number and length of substructures and to the encoder type (CNN/RNN/NN) beyond what is shown?

Overall, the idea is reasonable and potentially useful, but the empirical validation is too narrow and lacks strong baselines and ablations. Strengthening the experimental section along the lines above would make the contribution more compelling.
