Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external structure (e.g., dependency trees or AMR graphs) is decomposed into substructures; attention over substructure embeddings yields a sentence-level representation that conditions an RNN tagger (including a joint variant that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided signals).
- Empirical evaluation on ATIS shows modest but consistent improvements over several baselines, with larger gains in low-resource settings. The method is demonstrated with multiple knowledge sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers), suggesting some robustness to the choice of external structure.
- The paper provides qualitative attention visualizations and argues the approach is end-to-end with respect to learning attention over knowledge-guided substructures.

Strengths
- Clear idea of using knowledge-derived substructures as an attention memory: The formulation of attention over root-to-leaf paths (or AMR paths) is straightforward, modular, and general across sources of structure. This is a more targeted integration than simply feature augmentation and avoids hand-crafting.
- Consistent gains on ATIS across data regimes: K-SAN improves over the authors’ baselines and prior structural encoders, with the largest relative improvements on the small/medium subsets, supporting the claim that external structure can help in low-data regimes.
- Knowledge source generalization: The method works similarly across different parsers (Stanford vs SyntaxNet) and across syntactic vs semantic graphs (dependency vs AMR), which is encouraging for robustness to noisy or heterogeneous structure extraction.
- Interpretable analysis: Attention visualizations align with intuitively important parts (origin/destination/time), offering some qualitative support that the model learns meaningful structure.
- Modular architecture: The separation of Mkg/Min/Mout and the joint tagger makes the approach pluggable with different encoders (NN/RNN/CNN), which is practical.

Weaknesses
- Limited and dated evaluation: Experiments are conducted only on ATIS, a small and saturated benchmark. There is no evaluation on stronger, more diverse datasets (e.g., SNIPS, multi-domain NLU, more recent multi-intent/slot corpora), making it difficult to assess generality. Claims of state-of-the-art are not compelling without broader testing.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations: There is no comparison to standard BiLSTM/BI-GRU + CRF sequence taggers, character-level models, or self-attention/hierarchical sentence encoders without external knowledge. A self-attention over tokens baseline (no parser) would be a direct test of whether “attention” or “knowledge” drives gains. Similarly, no CRF layer is used on top of the RNNs, which is a standard for slot filling.
- “End-to-end” claim is overstated: The approach depends on external parsers/AMR systems at both train and test time. While attention weights are learned end-to-end, the reliance on non-differentiable upstream tools introduces the same error propagation the paper aims to mitigate. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to parser errors beyond a single anecdote.
- Incomplete methodological validation: Important ablations are missing: (a) remove attention and average substructures; (b) vary α in the joint tagger; (c) untie Mkg/Min; (d) use relation labels vs unlabeled paths; (e) restrict/expand the number of substructures; (f) feed o only at t=1 vs every timestep. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to the proposed components.
- Small absolute gains and unclear statistical rigor: Improvements over a CNN encoder-tagging baseline on ATIS-large are around 1 point F1. The paper reports t-tests but provides no variance across multiple random seeds, no standard deviations, and conducts multiple comparisons. Reproducibility is not established.
- Efficiency and parallelizability claims not substantiated: There is no runtime or memory analysis, no scaling with sentence length/substructure count, and no comparison to the cost of running external parsers. The claim that modeling time may not increase linearly is unproven.
- Baseline tuning and fairness concerns: Details on hyperparameter tuning, dev splits, and embedding initialization across models are sparse. Strong baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with pre-trained embeddings) could alter conclusions; using only in-domain embeddings may depress baselines unevenly.

Questions to authors
- Can you compare against BiLSTM/BI-GRU + CRF (with and without char embeddings), and a self-attention sentence encoder that uses no external parsers? This is critical to isolate the contribution of external knowledge vs stronger architectures.
- Please report results with multiple random seeds (mean±std) and clarify the significance testing setup (number of runs, correction for multiple comparisons).
- How sensitive is K-SAN to parser/AMR accuracy? Provide a controlled study where you perturb the structures or vary parser quality, and quantify the impact on F1.
- Provide ablation studies: (i) no attention (uniform over substructures), (ii) untied Mkg/Min, (iii) varying α, (iv) using relation labels vs unlabeled paths, (v) limiting/expanding substructure count, (vi) feeding the knowledge vector only once vs at each timestep.
- Evaluate on at least one additional NLU dataset (e.g., SNIPS or a multi-domain dataset). Do results hold in domains with different syntax/semantics?
- Runtime/memory: What is the inference latency including parsing/AMR generation? How does training/inference time scale with number of substructures? Please provide empirical measurements.
- Implementation details: Are RNNs bidirectional? If not, why? What is the dev split used for tuning? Will you release code and preprocessing scripts including parsers and substructure extraction?
