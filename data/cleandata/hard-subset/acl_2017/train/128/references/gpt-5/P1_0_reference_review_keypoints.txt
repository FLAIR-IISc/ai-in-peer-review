- Proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) that compute attention over parser-derived substructures to condition an RNN tagger for slot filling.
- Uses embeddings of substructures and the full sentence to compute attention weights and form a knowledge-guided vector o that influences tagging.
- Introduces a joint tagger that interpolates outputs of a plain chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN.
- Shows consistent F1 improvements on ATIS (small/medium/full splits) over CRF/RNN/CNN and structural baselines, with notable gains in low-resource settings.
- Demonstrates robustness across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependencies; rule-based/JAMR AMR).
- Provides attention visualizations that align with intuitive salient tokens (e.g., origin, destination, time), aiding interpretability.
- Frames novelty as “knowledge as a teacher” supervising attention over substructures in an end-to-end trained tagger.
- Presents a modular framework allowing different encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) and knowledge sources without task-specific tailoring.
- Simple, differentiable mechanism to inject structure via attention that is easier to adopt than many tree-structured models.
- Empirical gains are statistically significant on low-resource ATIS splits, supporting the claim that external knowledge helps with scarce data.
- Joint interpolation of chain-based and knowledge-guided states helps mitigate parser noise and stabilizes performance.
- Evaluation is limited to ATIS and omits other NLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, multi-domain) and joint intent+slot tasks.
- Missing stronger contemporary baselines (BiLSTM-CRF with char embeddings, CRF decoding, self-attention/Transformers) under comparable settings.
- “End-to-end” claim is partial because external knowledge sources (parsers/AMR) are fixed and not updated by training.
- Ablation studies are insufficient: missing comparisons to self-attention, uniform averaging of substructures, labeled relations, fixed vs learned interpolation, and removal of Mout.
- Efficiency and scaling assertions lack empirical support: no runtime, memory, or complexity analyses despite variability in substructure counts.
- Reproducibility is hindered by under-specified methodological details (dev split, statistical testing details, hyperparameter tuning, baseline implementation parity).
- Requests: report BiLSTM-CRF and word-level self-attention baselines under same lexical constraints for better positioning.
- Requests: provide specific ablations — (a) uniform substructure averaging, (b) attention over surface n-grams, (c) use of dependency labels, (d) varying/learning α, (e) with/without Mout and untied Min/Mkg.
- Requests: clarify construction/ordering/position encoding of substructures, handling of repeated tokens across paths, and linearization choices.
- Requests: quantify efficiency — avg. substructures per sentence, training/inference time, memory vs baselines, and performance vs number/length of substructures.
- Requests: evaluate robustness to parser noise (simulate noise) and whether richer structures (full labeled trees) can further improve performance.
- Overall: a neat, practical idea with encouraging results and interpretability, but needs stronger empirical validation, ablations, and clearer methodological details to substantiate claims.
