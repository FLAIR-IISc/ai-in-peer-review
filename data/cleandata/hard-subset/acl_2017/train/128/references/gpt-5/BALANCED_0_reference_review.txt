Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The core idea is to extract substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in a dependency tree or AMR edges) as “knowledge,” embed them alongside the sentence, compute attention over substructures, and inject a knowledge-guided sentence representation into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger blends a standard chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS across data sizes show consistent F1 improvements over several baselines; the model also appears robust to different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency parses and two AMR parsers).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Methodological: A simple, end-to-end mechanism to use external structured knowledge by attending over substructures instead of treating knowledge as fixed features. This design directly addresses error propagation and overcommitment to full-tree parses by soft-selecting salient fragments.
2) Empirical: Evidence of robustness to data scarcity and to heterogeneous knowledge formats, with consistent gains over neural baselines on ATIS.
3) Analysis: Visualizations of attention and comparisons across knowledge sources that offer some interpretability and insight.

Strengths
1) Clear and modular architecture that is easy to implement and train end-to-end. The formulation—encode substructures, compute attention with a sentence embedding, aggregate, and feed into an RNN—is elegant and general (works with CNN/RNN encoders and various parsers).
2) Good idea to attend to substructures rather than entire parses. This addresses a common pain point: structured features often overfit or propagate parse errors. The model’s soft attention provides a graceful way to exploit partial, noisy, or mismatched structures.
3) Robust improvements under low-resource settings. On Small and Medium ATIS subsets, K-SAN (especially with CNN encoding) gives meaningful F1 gains over strong sentence-encoder baselines, supporting the claim that external structure helps generalization when data is scarce.
4) Knowledge generalization demonstrated across syntax and AMR and across different parsers. Results are consistently strong and similar across knowledge sources, suggesting the approach is not tethered to a specific parse formalism or tool.
5) Interpretability via attention visualizations. The figures illustrate that the model focuses on intuitively relevant substructures (origins, destinations, times), and appears resilient even when parses are imperfect.

Weaknesses
1) Limited evaluation scope and missing strong modern baselines. The results are only on ATIS; there is no evaluation on other SLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, multi-domain datasets) or related sequence tagging tasks (e.g., NER). Several widely adopted baselines are missing: BiLSTM-CRF, character-level features, and contextual pretraining (ELMo/BERT/SpanBERT), as well as joint intent-slot models. It is unclear how K-SAN compares to these stronger baselines or whether it is complementary to them.
2) Insufficient ablations to isolate where gains come from. Missing analyses include: uniform vs learned attention; attention over random/perturbed substructures; effect of including dependency/AMR edge labels; sensitivity to α in the joint tagger; tied vs untied Mkg/Min weights; token-conditioned vs sentence-global knowledge vector o. Without these, it’s hard to attribute the improvements to the proposed attention mechanism rather than to generic sentence-embedding enrichment.
3) Efficiency/parallelizability claims are not substantiated. There are no training/inference time comparisons, memory usage reports, or latency breakdowns versus baselines. The number of substructures can be large; runtime complexity and batch-time behavior should be quantified to support the claims.
4) Design choices for knowledge extraction and representation are under-justified. The use of only root-to-leaf paths, exclusion of non-leaf nodes, and ignoring labeled relations are plausible but not empirically motivated. The potential trade-off between richer labels (more specific guidance) vs generalization is not explored, and the impact of parse quality is only superficially addressed.
5) The integration of knowledge is sentence-global, not token- or span-conditional. Injecting a single vector o into the RNN at all time steps may be suboptimal for token-level tagging compared to conditioning attention on the current token or hidden state. This limits the model’s ability to align specific substructures to specific tags.

Questions to authors
1) Substructure extraction: Please clarify precisely how root-to-leaf paths are constructed and deduplicated. Are words repeated across paths? How many substructures per sentence on average, and how does this scale for different knowledge sources?
2) Why ignore relation labels? Did you try using labeled dependencies/AMR roles, and what was the trade-off in performance and robustness?
3) Is the knowledge-guided vector o static for the entire sentence? Did you try conditioning attention on the current token or hidden state to obtain token-specific knowledge vectors? If so, how did it affect performance and efficiency?
4) Ablations: Please provide results for (a) uniform attention, (b) random or corrupted substructures, (c) untied Mkg/Min parameters, (d) varying α, (e) removing the joint tagger, (f) multi-hop attention vs single hop.
5) Efficiency: Can you report training/inference time and memory against CNN/RNN baselines, and how cost scales with number of substructures?
6) Stronger baselines: How does K-SAN compare to BiLSTM-CRF, BiLSTM-CRF with dependency features, and models with character-level and contextualized embeddings? Are gains additive when combined?
7) Generalization: Have you tested on additional SLU datasets or cross-domain settings to validate robustness beyond ATIS? How does domain mismatch between parser training data and SLU domain affect performance?

Overall assessment
The paper proposes a neat, general mechanism to bring structured knowledge into neural sequence tagging via attention over substructures, and demonstrates consistent improvements and interpretability on ATIS with multiple knowledge sources. However, for ACL standards, the empirical evaluation is too narrow and lacks comparisons to current strong baselines and thorough ablations that would convincingly validate the claimed advantages and design choices. I lean toward rejection in the current form, but I believe the idea is promising. Addressing the missing baselines, expanding to additional datasets, adding token-conditioned attention variants, and providing detailed ablations and efficiency measurements would substantially strengthen the work.
