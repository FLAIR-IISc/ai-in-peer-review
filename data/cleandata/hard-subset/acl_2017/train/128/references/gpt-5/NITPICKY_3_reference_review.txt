Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external structured “knowledge” (e.g., dependency trees or AMR graphs) is decomposed into substructures (root-to-leaf paths). The model encodes the sentence and each substructure, computes an attention distribution over substructures via inner products, aggregates them into a knowledge-guided sentence vector, and injects this vector into a recurrent slot tagger. A “joint” tagger balances a plain chain RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS (small/medium/full) show gains over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines and two “structural” encoders (DCNN, Tree-RNN), with similar performance across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependencies, rule-based/JAMR AMR).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple attention-over-substructures mechanism that treats parse/AMR paths as a per-sentence memory and integrates the resulting vector into an RNN slot tagger, yielding strong performance on ATIS and some robustness under low-resource settings.
2) An experimental study suggesting the approach is relatively agnostic to the structured source (syntax vs. AMR, different parsers) and that attention helps focus on salient substructures.

Strengths
- Conceptually clear and practical: Casting substructures as a memory and learning attention over them is intuitive, easy to implement on top of existing encoders, and potentially useful beyond slot filling.
- Competitive empirical results: On ATIS, K-SAN achieves higher F1 than CRF/RNN/CNN and the provided “structural” baselines. Gains are especially pronounced in low-data regimes (Small/Medium), supporting the claim that structure helps regularize.
- Knowledge-source agnosticism: The method works with both dependency paths and AMR-based paths, and across two parsers per source, with very similar performance. This indicates robustness to imperfect structures and parser variability.
- Attention analyses: The qualitative visualizations are consistent with the intuition that the model pays more attention to origin, destination, and time expressions, even when parses are noisy.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and positioning: The core mechanism (attention over a set of sentence-specific memories) is very close to memory networks and hierarchical/structured attention ideas. Several related works (e.g., Hierarchical Attention Networks, attention over syntactic units) are not cited or contrasted. The claim “first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion” is too strong.
- “Knowledge” is intra-sentence structure: The method does not actually retrieve external factual knowledge; it re-weights substructures extracted from the same input. This is more “linguistic structure guidance” than “knowledge as a teacher.” The terminology risks overselling and obscures scope.
- Missing ablations and clarity on architecture: 
  • No comparison between joint vs. knowledge-guided-only tagger; α is fixed at 0.5 and not learned; no sensitivity analysis. 
  • No ablation of attention (e.g., uniform averaging over substructures), no effect of tying Mkg and Min, no use of dependency labels vs. unlabeled, no removal of Mout. 
  • For GRU integration, equations are omitted; Eq. (8) uses “Mo” without defining M or clarifying shapes; it looks like adding a constant bias per time step—this needs disambiguation.
- Evaluation scope: Only ATIS is used; no multi-domain or larger datasets (e.g., SNIPS or other SLU corpora), and no stronger contemporary baselines like BiLSTM-CRF, BiLSTM with character embeddings, or joint intent/slot models widely used for ATIS. It’s hard to judge generality and true state-of-the-art status.
- Statistical rigor and reproducibility: 
  • Significance marks (†) are reported but the number of random runs, seeds, and exact test setup (paired vs. unpaired, variance across runs) are not specified. 
  • For Small/Medium subsets, it is unclear how subsampling was done and whether results are averaged over multiple samples. 
  • Dev set construction is not described. No code or detailed recipe is provided.
- Efficiency claim not supported: The paper claims “efficiency and parallelizability,” yet reports no runtime, memory, or scaling analyses with respect to number of substructures, sentence length, or parser choice. Table 2 lists “Max #Substructure” but does not connect it to computational cost.
- Substructure construction for DAGs: For AMR (a DAG), the method of deriving “root-to-leaf paths” is under-specified. How are multiple parents handled? Are paths duplicated across reentrancies? How are concept nodes linearized? This matters for reproducibility and fairness vs. dependency trees.
- Attention visualization mismatch: The model computes attention over substructures, yet Figure 5 shows word-level attention as well. The mapping from substructure attention to word-level attention is not defined.
- Writing and formatting issues: Numerous typos/grammar issues, inconsistent notation, and poorly formatted figures/tables (e.g., “ap-plied,” “with need of fewer parameters,” “v.s.”). References contain many malformed entries (missing years, question marks, broken author names). This detracts from professionalism.

Questions to authors
1) Please provide ablations: (a) joint vs. knowledge-guided-only, (b) learned α vs. fixed α, (c) uniform weights over substructures (no attention), (d) effect of tying vs. not tying Mkg and Min, (e) using dependency labels vs. unlabeled paths, (f) removing Mout.
2) Clarify Eq. (8): Is Mo a matrix-vector product (M times o) or a learned bias? Provide full GRU equations showing how o is injected into gates/states.
3) For AMR: precisely define the algorithm to extract substructures from a DAG, including handling reentrancies, variable nodes, concept labeling, and edge ordering. How do you linearize paths?
4) Provide stronger baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, BiLSTM+char, joint intent+slot with CRF) and, if possible, additional datasets to demonstrate generality beyond ATIS.
5) Report variance across multiple random seeds and clarify the significance testing protocol. For Small/Medium splits, report whether results are averaged over multiple random subsamples.
6) How is word-level attention in Figure 5 computed from substructure attention? If it’s inferred heuristically, please describe the mapping.
7) Can α be learned per sentence or per time step? Did you try gating o dynamically instead of a fixed mixture?
8) Please substantiate efficiency claims with wall-clock, memory, and complexity analyses vs. number of substructures and sentence length; discuss parser cost.

Overall, while the idea is appealing and results are promising, the combination of limited novelty, missing ablations, under-specified details (especially for AMR), weak baselines, and issues in presentation currently prevents me from recommending acceptance.
