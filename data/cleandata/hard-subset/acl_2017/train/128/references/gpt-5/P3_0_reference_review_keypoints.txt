- Paper summary: Proposes K-SAN, which embeds linguistic/knowledge substructures into a memory, attends over them conditioned on a sentence vector, and injects the resulting knowledge-guided vector into a joint chain + knowledge RNN slot tagger.

- Claimed contribution 1: A general end-to-end framework that attends over explicit linguistic/knowledge substructures and integrates that representation into sequence tagging.

- Claimed contribution 2: Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided attention improves slot filling (notably in low-resource settings) and generalizes across syntactic/semantic resources and parsers.

- Claimed contribution 3: Analysis suggesting the model focuses on salient substructures even with noisy parses.

- Strength — conceptual simplicity/general applicability: Treating root-to-leaf dependency paths or AMR subpaths as memory entries with tied encoders is a clean, broadly applicable idea.

- Strength — empirical gains, esp. low-resource: Consistent F1 improvements on ATIS over non-structural and structural baselines, largest gains in Small/Medium data regimes.

- Strength — knowledge robustness: Results stable across two dependency parsers and AMR parsers despite large differences in substructure counts.

- Strength — interpretability: Attention visualizations align qualitatively with slot-relevant regions and are stable across data sizes.

- Strength — comparisons/significance: Multiple baselines and significance tests lend credibility to the reported gains.

- Weakness — limited evaluation/saturated dataset: Experiments only on ATIS (small/saturated); missing broader SLU datasets (SNIPS, MIT) and cross-domain tests.

- Weakness — missing stronger baselines: Key modern baselines absent, notably BiLSTM-CRF (with/without char features) and joint intent-slot models.

- Weakness — incomplete ablations/sensitivity: No analysis of α in joint tagger, no chain-only vs knowledge-only, no random/uniform attention or tied vs untied encoder effects.

- Weakness — under-specified architecture: Details lacking for NN/RNN/CNN encoders (pooling/aggregation), exact GRU injection equations, and attention regularization/peaking behavior.

- Weakness — efficiency claims unsubstantiated: No complexity analysis, wall-clock timings, or memory comparison despite potentially many substructures and attention overhead.

- Weakness — reliance on external parsers: Using external parser outputs undermines the “end-to-end” and “teacher” framing and may propagate parser errors.

- Weakness — reporting/reproducibility gaps: Sparse details on hyperparameter tuning, dev splits, run variance, code/data release, and inconsistent citations/formatting.

- Question — substructure encoding: How are dependency root-to-leaf paths defined (labels/words/repeats) and how are variable-length substructures aggregated for the “NN” and RNN/CNN encoders?

- Question — GRU integration & alternatives: Exact GRU equations showing where o enters, and results comparing additive injection vs concatenation [wt; o].

- Question — ablation requests: Performance of chain-only, knowledge-only, joint with varying α, random/uniform attention, use of dependency/AMR labels, and tied vs untied Min/Mkg.

- Question — stronger baselines: Comparison to BiLSTM-CRF (with/without char embeddings) and joint slot-intent models on ATIS.

- Question — efficiency/scale: Report training/inference time and memory vs best non-structural baseline and DCNN/Tree-RNN, and scaling with number of substructures.

- Question — robustness to parse quality: Quantify performance under artificial parse perturbations (dropped edges, randomized paths).

- Question — generalization: Evaluate on at least one additional SLU dataset to support claims about data-scarce robustness and domain generalization.
