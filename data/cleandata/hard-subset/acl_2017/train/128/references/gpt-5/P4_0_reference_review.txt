Summary
The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for sequence tagging in spoken language understanding (SLU), specifically slot filling. Given external structural knowledge (e.g., dependency parses or AMR graphs), K-SAN extracts substructures (root-to-leaf paths), encodes each substructure and the whole sentence into vectors using a shared encoder (NN/RNN/CNN), computes an attention distribution over substructures via inner product with the sentence vector, aggregates a knowledge-guided representation, and injects it into a recurrent tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS show consistent gains over baselines, with larger improvements in low-resource settings and robustness across different knowledge sources (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet parses; rule-based vs. JAMR AMR).

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A general, end-to-end attention mechanism that uses external linguistic/semantic substructures as guidance to form sentence representations tailored for sequence tagging, differing from prior feature-augmentation approaches by learning attention over substructures jointly with the tagger.

2) Empirical evidence that the approach improves slot filling, particularly under data scarcity, and remains robust across heterogeneous knowledge sources (syntactic dependency trees and AMR graphs from different parsers).

3) A simple joint RNN tagger that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided signals, mitigating sensitivity to noisy or imperfect structural knowledge.

Strengths
- Effective and consistent gains, especially in low-resource settings: On ATIS, K-SAN(CNN) improves F1 over the strongest non-knowledge baseline (CNN encoder + RNN) from 73.6 to 74.6 on Small and from 85.5 to 88.0 on Medium; on Large, K-SAN(RNN) reaches 95.0 vs. 93.9. Improvements are statistically significant against baselines (as reported) and also exceed structural baselines (DCNN 93.25, Tree-RNN 92.28).

- Robustness to knowledge source variation: Results with different parsers and knowledge types are similar (e.g., K-SAN(CNN) 94.86 with Stanford dependencies, 95.00 with SyntaxNet, 94.85–94.89 with AMR), supporting the claim that the method generalizes to multiple forms of external structure and tolerates parser noise.

- End-to-end use of structure via attention rather than feature injection: The model learns which substructures are salient for the task and integrates them smoothly into the tagger. This addresses error propagation issues common with static feature enrichment and aligns with the provided attention visualizations that highlight origin/destination/time substructures.

- Architectural simplicity and flexibility: The framework supports multiple encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) for substructures, ties weights between sentence and substructure encoders for parameter efficiency, and integrates the knowledge-guided vector into standard RNN/GRU taggers with minimal engineering.

- Clear problem setup and training details: The paper specifies encoders, objective, optimizer (Adam), hyperparameters, and evaluation protocol across varying training set sizes, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: All experiments are on a single, small benchmark (ATIS) and a single task (slot filling). Claims about generalization and robustness would be stronger with additional datasets (e.g., multi-domain SLU, larger or noisier corpora) and tasks (e.g., intent detection, joint modeling).

- Baseline coverage and fairness: Comparisons omit strong contemporary sequence tagging baselines such as BiLSTM-CRF, character-augmented models, and word-level self-attention without external knowledge. The reported statistical significance is only with respect to non-knowledge baselines; significance versus DCNN/Tree-RNN is not reported.

- Insufficient ablation analyses: The paper does not isolate the contributions of key design choices (e.g., attention over substructures vs. uniform/bagging, weight tying between Min and Mkg, inclusion/exclusion of dependency/AMR relation labels, the value of α in the joint tagger, injecting o into GRU gates vs. hidden bias). Without these, it is difficult to attribute gains to the proposed attention mechanism specifically.

- Efficiency claims unsubstantiated: While efficiency and parallelizability are claimed, there are no empirical measurements of runtime, memory footprint, or scaling with number of substructures, nor comparisons to DCNN/Tree-based models.

- Some methodological details are unclear or under-specified: The construction and ordering of substructure sequences (e.g., “show flights seattle from”), whether edge labels are ever used, and the exact way o is integrated into GRU gating are not fully specified. The development split on ATIS for hyperparameter tuning is not described.

- Incremental novelty relative to memory/attention literature: The mechanism is a straightforward attention over substructure embeddings akin to memory networks; the main novelty lies in applying it to knowledge-guided substructures for sequence tagging. This is useful but methodologically modest.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Ablations: (a) Replace attention with uniform averaging over substructures; (b) no weight tying between Min and Mkg; (c) vary α in the joint tagger; (d) use top-k attended substructures vs. all; (e) add/remove dependency/AMR relation labels. How do these affect performance?

- Baselines: Can you report results against BiLSTM-CRF (with/without char embeddings), and a word-only self-attention encoder, to contextualize gains? Also report a “no-knowledge” version of your architecture where attention is over n-gram spans extracted from the sentence.

- Integration details: Precisely how is o injected into GRU (which gates, additive vs. concatenation), and is o constant across time steps or recomputed per token? Does injecting o at each time step versus only at t=0 change results?

- Substructure construction: How are root-to-leaf paths linearized (word order), and are edge labels ever used? What is the average number/length of substructures on ATIS, and is there any cap on memory size?

- Efficiency: Provide wall-clock training/inference times and memory usage versus DCNN and Tree-RNN. How does complexity scale with the number of substructures?

- Data splits and tuning: What is the development set used for hyperparameter selection on ATIS, and is it disjoint from test? Are reported p-values computed against both non-knowledge and structural baselines?

- Generalization: Have you tried multi-domain SLU datasets or joint intent+slot tasks? If not, what limitations prevent such evaluation?
