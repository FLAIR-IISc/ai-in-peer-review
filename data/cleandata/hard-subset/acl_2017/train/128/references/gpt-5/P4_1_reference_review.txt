Summary
The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for sequence tagging in goal-oriented NLU (slot filling). Given an utterance, external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees or AMR graphs) is used to decompose the sentence into substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf paths). Each substructure is encoded into a vector (via NN/RNN/CNN encoders), and an attention distribution over substructures is computed by matching a sentence embedding with substructure embeddings. A knowledge-guided sentence representation is formed as the attention-weighted sum and injected into an RNN-based tagger. A joint tagger combines a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS (small/medium/full training subsets) show consistent improvements over non-structural baselines and competitive or better results than structural baselines. The approach generalizes across different knowledge sources (dependency and AMR, different parsers). Attention visualizations are provided.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A general, end-to-end differentiable architecture that attends over knowledge-guided substructures and integrates this representation into sequence tagging via a joint RNN tagger.
2) Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided attention yields consistent gains, especially under data scarcity, and that the mechanism is robust across multiple knowledge sources (dependency parses and AMR).
3) Qualitative analysis (attention visualizations) illustrating that the model focuses on semantically salient substructures even with imperfect parses.

Strengths
- Methodological generality and simplicity: The model abstracts knowledge to substructures and uses a unified attention mechanism over these units. It is compatible with multiple encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) and knowledge types (syntax and semantics), and is fully trainable end-to-end without handcrafted feature engineering.
- Consistent performance gains: On ATIS, K-SAN variants outperform strong non-structural baselines (CRF, RNN, CNN-encoder) across small, medium, and large data settings. Gains are largest in low-resource regimes (e.g., up to ~2–3 F1 in Small/Medium), supporting the claim that structured guidance improves generalization when data are scarce.
- Competitiveness with structural baselines: Results are on par with or better than dependency-aware CNNs (DCNN) and Tree-RNN encoders, suggesting that selective attention over substructures is more effective than encoding entire structures uniformly.
- Knowledge source robustness: Comparable results across different parsers (Stanford vs SyntaxNet) and between syntax (dependency) and semantics (AMR) indicate the approach is not tied to a single resource or schema, supporting the generalization claim.
- Clear training and evaluation setup: The paper specifies datasets, metrics, optimization details, model variants, and statistical significance testing (versus baselines), aiding reproducibility and interpretation.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: All experiments are on a single dataset (ATIS) and a single task (slot filling). Broader validation (e.g., other SLU datasets such as SNIPS/MIT, or related sequence labeling tasks) is absent, limiting conclusions about generalizability beyond ATIS.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations: Comparisons do not include widely used BiLSTM-CRF architectures (potentially state-of-the-art for slot filling), character-level features, or modern self-attention over tokens without external knowledge. Ablations isolating the contribution of structural attention versus added capacity are lacking (e.g., attention over surface n-grams, random or linear substructures, using full-tree encodings without attention).
- Causality of “knowledge as a teacher” not firmly established: Since u and substructure embeddings are computed from the same sentence, improvements may arise from structured self-attention rather than leveraging external knowledge per se. The paper does not quantify the benefit over purely intra-sentential attention that ignores parses, nor the impact of including/excluding relation labels.
- Efficiency claims unsubstantiated: The paper claims efficiency/parallelizability, yet provides no runtime, memory, or scaling analysis with respect to sentence length or number of substructures. Table 2 lists maximum substructures, but there is no empirical overhead comparison to baselines.
- Hyperparameter and design sensitivity underexplored: The joint tagger uses a fixed α=0.5 without sensitivity analysis; encoder choice (CNN vs RNN) yields different best results across data sizes, but no explanation or analysis is provided. Robustness to parser errors is shown only via a single qualitative example.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- Can you provide ablations to disentangle the effect of external knowledge from structured self-attention? Specifically: (a) attention over fixed surface n-grams or random substructures; (b) full-tree encoding without attention; (c) token-level self-attention without parses; (d) including dependency/AMR relation labels.
- How does K-SAN compare against a strong BiLSTM-CRF baseline (optionally with character CNN/LSTM) on ATIS? If not available, please comment on expected differences.
- What are the training/inference time and memory costs relative to non-structural baselines, and how do they scale with the number of substructures?
- How sensitive is performance to the mixing weight α in the joint tagger and to the number/definition of substructures (e.g., using different path definitions, pruning thresholds)?
- Have you evaluated on additional SLU datasets or other sequence labeling tasks to substantiate generalization claims? If not, could you provide preliminary results or discuss applicability?
