Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture for slot filling that attends over a set of linguistically motivated substructures extracted from external knowledge sources (primarily dependency parses, but also AMR). Each root-to-leaf path (or analogous AMR path) is encoded into a vector (via NN/RNN/CNN), stored in a memory, and attended using an utterance embedding; the weighted sum is transformed into a sentence-level context vector o that conditions an RNN-based slot tagger. A joint tagger combines a chain-only RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN to mitigate parser noise. Experiments on ATIS under three training sizes show consistent (though modest) gains over several baselines and structural encoders, with visualizations of learned attention. The method is claimed to be end-to-end, general with respect to knowledge sources, and efficient.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A memory/attention-based formulation that leverages parse-derived substructures as external “knowledge” to produce a sentence-level guidance vector for sequence tagging, trained end-to-end.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach generalizes across different knowledge resources (two dependency parsers, two AMR parsers) and helps more in low-data regimes.
3) Interpretable attention analyses identifying salient substructures for slot prediction.

Strengths
- Conceptual integration of structure via attention: Rather than concatenating hand-engineered features, the model encodes substructures and learns an attention distribution over them. This is a clean instantiation of memory/attention ideas tailored to NLU, and it avoids brittle feature pipelines and some error propagation.
- Knowledge-source generality: Experiments with both syntactic (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and semantic (AMR via Rule-based and JAMR) sources show similar improvements, suggesting the mechanism is agnostic to the schema and robust to parser variation. The analysis of substructure counts (Table 2) and performance parity across resources supports the claim.
- Consistent improvements and low-resource gains: On ATIS, K-SAN variants outperform baselines across Small/Medium/Large settings, with the largest relative gains in low-data scenarios (e.g., +2.5–3.5 F1 over RNN on Small/Medium). This aligns with the motivation that external structure helps generalization when supervision is scarce.
- Interpretability: The attention visualizations highlight origin/destination/time substructures that align with slot semantics, even when the dependency parse is imperfect. This is a useful diagnostic relative to opaque sentence encoders.
- Sensible joint modeling: The joint RNN (Eq. 9–11) that interpolates chain and structure-informed hidden states is a pragmatic design to hedge against noisy or mismatched knowledge, and appears important given parser errors.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to memory/attention literature is incremental: The mechanism closely mirrors memory networks/dynamic memory models—encode a set of facts (here, substructures), attend with an utterance query, take a weighted sum—and then condition a downstream predictor. The main novelty is the choice of “facts” (parser paths) and the downstream task (slot tagging), but the architectural advances beyond prior attention/memory frameworks are limited.
- Experimental scope is narrow and baselines are dated: Results are only on ATIS, a small and saturated benchmark. Stronger and widely adopted baselines for slot filling are missing, notably BiLSTM-CRF (often with char/subword features), attention-based BiLSTM taggers, and contextualized pretraining (ELMo/BERT-style, if contemporaneous). Reported SOTA for ATIS often exceeds 95 F1; the paper reaches 94.9–95.0 without comparing to those. This limits claims of state of the art.
- Limited ablations to isolate where gains come from: There is no ablation of the attention mechanism (e.g., uniform attention over substructures), the joint interpolation α, or alternative ways to inject o (e.g., init hidden state vs additive bias vs gating). The paper also ties Min and Mkg weights but does not show the effect of untied weights. It is thus hard to attribute gains specifically to knowledge-guided attention versus simply providing a sentence-level vector.
- Statistical analysis and variance: Significance is reported only against “baseline models,” not against structural baselines (DCNN/Tree-RNN). It is unclear whether results are averaged across multiple random seeds; ATIS is sensitive to initialization and small hyperparameter changes. Effect sizes on Large are modest (~1.0 F1 over the best baseline), so robustness reporting would help.
- Efficiency claim is unsubstantiated: The paper claims efficiency/parallelizability and non-linear growth with sentence length, yet attention still requires scoring each substructure and substructure counts can exceed token counts (Table 2 shows up to 53). No training/inference time or memory benchmarks are provided.
- Some architectural and implementation details are under-specified: It is stated that GRUs are used, but equations and injection of o (Eq. 8) are shown for plain RNN; how o enters GRU gates is deferred as “similarly.” The choice not to use dependency/AMR labels may harm expressivity; at least an ablation including labels would be informative. The construction of AMR “substructures” (paths) and tokenization/lexicalization details are only briefly sketched.

Questions to authors
- Architecture specifics: In the reported results, are the taggers GRU-based or simple RNNs? Precisely how is o injected into GRU (which gates, add vs concat)? Did you try initializing h0 with o instead of (or in addition to) adding Mo at each step?
- Ablations: What is the performance with uniform attention over substructures (i.e., no learned attention)? What is the performance of the joint model with different α, and with α learned? What happens if Min and Mkg are not tied?
- Knowledge labels: Did you experiment with using dependency relation labels or AMR edge labels in the substructure encoding? If so, how did they affect generalization across parsers and performance?
- Baselines: How does K-SAN compare to a strong BiLSTM-CRF (possibly with character CNN/LSTM) and to attention-based BiLSTM taggers trained under the same constraints (no external pretraining)? If you control for sentence-level context vectors (e.g., CNN/LSTM utterance encoders concatenated at each timestep), do you still see gains?
- Generalization: Have you evaluated on other SLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MIT Restaurant/Movie)? Do the gains from knowledge-guided attention persist across domains with different slot schemas?
- Efficiency: Can you provide empirical runtime and memory comparisons versus the strongest baselines, and a breakdown as a function of the number of substructures?

Overall, the idea of treating parse-derived substructures as an attentionable memory for slot tagging is appealing and well-motivated, and the results on ATIS are encouraging. However, to merit publication at ACL, the work would benefit from stronger baselines, broader evaluation, and ablations that more directly validate the claimed contributions and clarify the role of knowledge-guided attention.
