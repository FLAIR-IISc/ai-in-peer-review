- Proposes K-SAN: extract root-to-leaf parser substructures, embed them, attend over them conditioned on sentence embedding, and inject knowledge-guided representation into an RNN tagger with a joint tagger combining chain and knowledge-guided paths.
- Shows higher F1 on ATIS across low-, medium-, and full-resource settings, with larger gains in low-resource regimes and attention visualizations highlighting semantically salient parts.
- Demonstrates robustness across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependencies and AMR parsers) with comparable gains.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated formulation of sentence-specific memory/attention over linguistically grounded substructures trained end-to-end.
- Consistent empirical improvements, especially substantial in low-data settings; competitive with or superior to structure-aware encoders.
- Mechanism generalizes across syntactic and semantic parsers, suggesting schema-agnostic applicability.
- Conceptually simple and modular using standard components, facilitating implementation and adoption.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is limited relative to existing attention/memory models; main novelty is using parser-derived substructures as memory, which is incremental.
- Missing strong baselines and crucial ablations (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, character features, uniform averaging, random structures, edge labels, different substructure granularities, impact of joint tagger).
- Clarify Equation (8) and exact mechanism for integrating o into GRU gates; current presentation hampers reproducibility.
- Claims of efficiency and parallelizability are unsubstantiated; no complexity analysis or runtime comparisons provided.
- Evaluation is narrow (only ATIS), and absolute gains on full data are modest, so external validation is needed.

Questions for authors:
- Please clarify Equation (8): is ht = φ(Wwt + Uht−1 + M o)? How exactly is o integrated into GRU gates (provide equations)?
- What is the dev split and hyperparameter tuning protocol for ATIS; are results averaged over multiple seeds?
- How many substructures per sentence are used on average per knowledge source and is there pruning or a cap?
- Have you evaluated BiLSTM-CRF or GRU-CRF baselines, and how does K-SAN compare in parameters and conceptually?
- Please provide ablations for attention vs. uniform averaging, use of edge labels, and tying vs. not tying Mkg and Min.
- Have you explored multi-hop attention over substructures and, if so, what are the results?
- Can you report training/inference time and memory comparisons to substantiate efficiency claims?

Additional comments:
- Correlating parser quality (UAS/LAS or AMR Smatch) with performance would strengthen robustness claims.
- The “teacher” framing is misleading; discuss extending memory to cross-sentence or KB-derived substructures for clarity.
- Standardize notation (e.g., Mout vs. Mo) and specify matrix/vector dimensions for reproducibility.

Overall recommendation:
- Borderline accept: sensible, empirically helpful method especially in low-data scenarios with good cross-parser generalization, but novelty, clarity, baselines, and evaluation scope need strengthening.
