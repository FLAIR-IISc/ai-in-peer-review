Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The key idea is to treat linguistic/knowledge structures (e.g., dependency parse paths, AMR subgraphs) as substructures xi, embed them into a memory, attend over them conditioned on the sentence embedding u, form an attention-weighted representation h, and inject a knowledge-guided vector o into a recurrent slot tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS across three training sizes show consistent F1 improvements over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines and structural baselines (DCNN/Tree-RNN), and similar gains with different parsers (Stanford/SyntaxNet) and with AMR vs dependencies.

As I see them, main contributions:
1) A general, end-to-end framework that attends over explicit linguistic/knowledge substructures and integrates the resulting representation into sequence tagging.
2) Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided attention improves slot filling, especially in low-resource settings, and generalizes across syntactic and semantic resources and parsers.
3) An analysis suggesting the model learns to focus on salient substructures even with noisy parses.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: Treating root-to-leaf dependency paths or AMR subpaths as memory entries and attending over them is a clean, broadly applicable idea. The architecture is resource-agnostic (syntax/semantics), and the same pipeline works with different encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) whose weights are tied for sentence and substructure encoding.

- Consistent empirical gains, especially low-resource: On ATIS, K-SAN variants improve over strong non-structural (CNN encoder) and structural (DCNN/Tree-RNN) baselines, with the largest gains on Small/Medium settings. This supports the claim that knowledge guidance helps generalization.

- Knowledge robustness: Results are stable across two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers, despite large differences in number of substructures. This is a valuable test of the “teacher knowledge” hypothesis and reduces concern about parser overfitting.

- Interpretability: The attention visualizations qualitatively align with slot-relevant regions (origin/destination/time) and show similar patterns across data sizes, supporting the claim that the model learns meaningful structural focus.

- Solid comparisons and significance: Multiple baselines, state-of-the-art structural alternatives, and significance tests provide reasonable evidence that the gains are real.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and missing stronger baselines: ATIS is small and saturated. The paper lacks evaluation on additional SLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MIT restaurant/movie) or cross-domain setups. Key modern baselines are absent, notably BiLSTM-CRF (with or without character-level features) and joint intent-slot models that are standard in SLU. Without these, it is hard to position the absolute strength of K-SAN.

- Incomplete ablations and sensitivity: The joint tagger uses α=0.5 without analysis. Ablations isolating the contribution of attention over substructures are missing (e.g., chain-only vs knowledge-only, random or shuffled substructures, uniform attention, using dependency labels vs not, effect of tied vs untied Min/Mkg). It is unclear whether gains come from adding any global sentence vector o or specifically from knowledge-guided attention over substructures.

- Architectural clarity and rigor: Several details are under-specified. For the “NN” encoder with linear activation, how are variable-length substructures aggregated (bag/avg vs sum vs position-aware)? For RNN/CNN encoders, what pooling/last-state scheme is used? Injecting o via ht = φ(Mo + Wwt + Uht-1) may act as a learned global bias; for GRU “added into gating similarly” is vague—please specify exact equations. No regularization on the attention distribution is described; are there collapse/peaking issues?

- Efficiency/parallelizability claims unsubstantiated: The paper asserts improved efficiency and parallelizability, but provides no complexity analysis, wall-clock timings, or memory footprint vs baselines. The number of substructures can be large (up to 53), and the attention requires computing uT mi for all i; the practical overhead is not quantified.

- Use of external parsers blurs “end-to-end” claim and “teacher” narrative: Although the model is trained end-to-end with respect to SLU loss, it relies on external parsers trained on PTB; errors can propagate. The “knowledge as a teacher” framing suggests a supervisory signal, but the attention is learned only from slot labels, not from knowledge supervision. This weakens the novelty claim relative to existing memory/attention models over external structures.

- Reporting and reproducibility: Hyperparameter tuning is briefly mentioned, but dev split, early stopping, and variance across runs are not detailed. Code/data release is not discussed. Some citations and reference formatting are inconsistent.

Questions to authors
1) Substructure definition and encoding: For dependencies, do you always use root-to-leaf paths including only surface words, ignoring labels? How do you handle repeated words across paths? For the “NN” encoder, how are variable-length inputs combined before the linear layer? For RNN/CNN, which pooling/aggregation do you use?

2) Integration into GRU: Please provide exact GRU equations showing where o enters (reset/update/candidate). Did you try concatenation [wt; o] vs additive injection Mo? How sensitive are results to these choices?

3) Ablations and sensitivity: What is the performance of (a) chain-only, (b) knowledge-only, (c) joint with various α, and (d) uniform/random attention over substructures? Did using dependency/AMR edge labels help or hurt? What happens if Min and Mkg are untied?

4) Stronger baselines: How does K-SAN compare to BiLSTM-CRF (with/without character embeddings) on ATIS? If unavailable, can you add these with comparable tuning?

5) Efficiency: Please report training/inference time and memory vs the best non-structural baseline and vs DCNN/Tree-RNN, and examine scaling with number of substructures.

6) Robustness to parse quality: Beyond parser choice, can you quantify performance as you artificially perturb parses (drop edges, randomize some paths) to understand sensitivity?

7) Generalization: Have you evaluated on another SLU dataset to support the general claim of robustness to data scarcity and domain mismatch?

Overall, this is an interesting and promising direction—attending over explicit linguistic/semantic substructures for sequence tagging. With stronger baselines, clearer architectural details and ablations, and broader evaluation, it would be a solid contribution.
