Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The core idea is to treat external structured knowledge (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs) as a set of substructures (root-to-leaf paths) stored in a memory; the model learns an attention distribution over these substructures conditioned on the input sentence and fuses the resulting knowledge-guided representation with an RNN tagger. The approach is trained end-to-end. Experiments on ATIS show consistent gains over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines and structure-aware encoders (DCNN/Tree-RNN), with especially strong improvements in low-resource settings and robust performance across multiple knowledge sources and parsers.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A novel, general architecture that uses external structured knowledge as an attentionable memory of sentence substructures, integrated end-to-end into a sequence tagger. Compared to prior work that adds knowledge as fixed features or full-tree encodings, this design lets the model learn where to attend in the structure.
2) Empirical demonstration that the approach is robust to parser errors and generalizes across knowledge formats (dependency trees and AMR) and encoders (NN/RNN/CNN), delivering state-of-the-art ATIS results and large gains in low-data regimes.
3) Interpretability via attention over substructures, providing insight into which syntactic/semantic elements drive tagging decisions.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and scope: Casting knowledge as a “teacher” that guides attention over composable substructures is a clean, general idea that extends memory/attention models to sequence tagging with structured guidance. Unlike prior structural encoders that compress whole trees, K-SAN selectively aggregates salient parts, which is a meaningful advance.
- Consistent, sizable gains, especially in low-resource settings: On ATIS-small and -medium, K-SAN (CNN encoder) achieves 74.60/87.99 F1, outperforming strong baselines (CNN encoder–tagger 73.57/85.52) and structural models (Tree-RNN/DCNN). Improvements are statistically significant and practically important for low-resource NLU.
- Generalization across knowledge sources: The model performs similarly with dependencies (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and AMR (rule-based, JAMR) despite very different graph topologies and substructure counts, indicating the framework is not tied to a specific parser or knowledge schema.
- End-to-end training and robustness to parser noise: Attention visualizations show the model learns to focus on origin/destination/time-related substructures even when parsers make errors. This helps alleviate error propagation often seen in feature-based approaches.
- Interpretability without heavy engineering: The substructure-level attention is easy to inspect and potentially useful for debugging and downstream trust, with minimal additional feature crafting.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: All results are on a single, relatively small benchmark (ATIS). The claim of generality would be much stronger with additional domains/datasets (e.g., other SLU corpora or contemporary slot-filling benchmarks). This limits external validity.
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations: The paper lacks comparisons to BiLSTM-CRF or contemporary joint modeling baselines that were competitive for sequence tagging. Ablations isolating (i) attention vs. uniform averaging over substructures, (ii) knowledge vs. extra parameters, and (iii) single-chain vs. joint tagger are needed to attribute gains precisely to knowledge-guided attention.
- Efficiency claims not substantiated: The paper claims parallelizability and non-linear growth in modeling time with sentence length, but provides no runtime, memory, or throughput measurements vs. baselines or vs. full-tree encoders.
- Underexplored design choices: Relation labels are dropped “for generalization,” but this choice likely discards useful signal. A variant that incorporates edge labels (or learned label embeddings) would clarify the trade-off. Similarly, only single-hop attention is considered; multi-hop or iterative reading might improve performance and deserves discussion.
- Reporting and reproducibility: While many hyperparameters are given, code, parameter counts, and capacity-matched baselines are not provided. Given the architectural complexity (dual encoders, memory, joint RNN), parameter count differences could partially explain gains.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide ablations for: (a) removing attention (uniform averaging over substructures), (b) removing external knowledge (same tagger capacity), and (c) using only chain-based vs. only knowledge-guided RNN, to quantify the incremental contribution of each component?
- How does K-SAN compare to a BiLSTM-CRF baseline on ATIS with similar parameter counts? If not included, please justify and discuss expected performance.
- Do relation/edge labels help? Please report results when incorporating dependency/AMR labels (e.g., via label embeddings) and comment on generalization across knowledge sources.
- Can you report runtime and memory overhead relative to chain RNNs and DCNN/Tree-RNN? How does performance scale with the number of substructures?
- How sensitive is performance to parse quality? E.g., train/test using different parsers; add noise to parses; or compare gold vs. automatic parses if available.
- Did you try multi-hop attention or iterative refinement over memory? If so, what was the impact?

Additional comments
- The visualization is a nice touch; adding quantitative correlation between attention peaks and token-level F1 could strengthen the interpretability claim.
- The idea seems broadly applicable beyond slot filling (e.g., NER, relation extraction); a brief discussion would increase the paper’s impact.

Recommendation
Borderline accept leaning accept. Despite limited evaluation breadth and missing ablations, the central idea—end-to-end attention over knowledge-guided substructures for sequence tagging—is novel and promising, with clear gains in low-resource settings and robustness across knowledge sources. With stronger baselines and ablations, this could be a solid contribution.
