Summary
The paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling in goal-oriented NLU. Given an utterance, external knowledge (primarily dependency trees and also AMR graphs) is used to produce a set of substructures (root-to-leaf paths). Each substructure and the entire sentence are encoded (via NN/RNN/CNN), attention weights over substructures are computed by matching with the sentence embedding, and a knowledge-guided sentence representation is formed via weighted sum. This representation is combined with an RNN tagger (including a joint architecture that blends chain-based and knowledge-guided RNNs). Experiments on ATIS with varying training sizes show consistent gains over CRF, vanilla RNN, CNN encoder-taggers, and tree/dependency-aware encoders, and show similar gains across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependencies, rule-based/JAMR AMR). Visualizations suggest interpretable attention.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, end-to-end attention-over-substructures mechanism that uses external linguistic structure to guide sentence representation for sequence tagging, yielding consistent ATIS gains and improved data efficiency.
2) Empirical demonstration that the method is robust to the choice/quality of knowledge resource (different parsers and syntactic vs semantic graphs) and to limited training data.
3) An interpretable framework that highlights salient substructures, with qualitative evidence.

Strengths
- Clear, modular architecture that integrates knowledge as soft attention rather than hard features. The attention mechanism over substructures is a principled way to mitigate parser noise and avoid error propagation typical of feature augmentation. The weighted combination is differentiable and trained end-to-end, unlike many prior knowledge-injection approaches.

- Consistent improvements across data regimes and knowledge sources. K-SAN (especially with CNN encoder) outperforms strong encoder baselines and structure-aware DCNN/Tree-RNN on ATIS, with statistically significant gains in most settings and larger improvements in low-resource conditions. The robustness across parsers (Stanford vs SyntaxNet) and across syntactic vs semantic (AMR) structures supports the generality claim.

- Interpretability and qualitative analysis. Attention visualizations show that the model places higher weights on substructures aligned with origin/destination/time slots, even when parsing is imperfect. This is a useful property for NLU systems.

- Simple to implement and potentially broadly applicable. The proposed pipeline (construct substructures, encode, attend, combine with tagger) is straightforward and could be adapted to other structured knowledge sources without schema changes.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and missing strong baselines. Results are only on ATIS; no evaluation on other slot-filling benchmarks or multi-domain datasets. Stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF sequence taggers, possibly with character features) are absent. This makes it hard to judge absolute competitiveness and isolates gains primarily relative to older baselines.

- Incomplete ablations. The paper reports encoder variants (NN/RNN/CNN) but lacks: a) no-attention baseline (uniform or max over substructures), b) effect of using only chain RNN vs only knowledge-guided RNN vs the joint architecture, c) effect of tying vs untied Min/Mkg weights, d) using dependency labels/AMR role labels vs words only, e) sensitivity to number/quality of substructures (e.g., random or corrupted parses). These ablations are needed to attribute gains to the attention mechanism and to the knowledge signal.

- Architectural details and clarity. It is unclear if the tagger is bidirectional (seems unidirectional), and there is no CRF decoding layer; both are common for slot filling and could change conclusions. Equation (8) suggests a constant Mo added at every timestep; the dimensionality and whether o is recomputed per sentence or per token deserves clarification. The claim that modeling time may not increase linearly is not substantiated and seems counterintuitive given per-substructure encoding and attention.

- Efficiency and scalability claims unverified. No runtime or memory analysis, nor discussion of how substructure counts scale with sentence length or knowledge source (AMR often yields fewer but richer components). The parallelizability claim should be supported with measurements.

- Overstated generalization claim. While the method does not require a fixed schema, it does require access to structured analyses (dependency/AMR) at inference time, which may be costly or domain-mismatched. The experiments still rely on in-domain embeddings and a single domain (ATIS), limiting the strength of the generalization argument.

Questions to Authors
- Can you compare to stronger sequence tagging baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, with/without character embeddings) and report whether K-SAN still adds gains when stacked onto them?

- Please provide ablations: attention vs uniform/max pooling over substructures; joint vs guided-only vs chain-only; tied vs untied Min/Mkg; and the effect of using dependency/AMR edge labels.

- Is the RNN tagger bidirectional? If not, what is the effect of using BiRNN and adding a CRF layer on top? Where exactly is o injected (all timesteps, concatenation vs addition), and what is its dimensionality relative to W and U?

- How sensitive is performance to parser noise? Can you report results with systematically perturbed parses and with no parses (zero substructures) to quantify reliance on knowledge quality?

- Can you substantiate efficiency claims with wall-clock/runtime and memory comparisons, and report average/max number of substructures per sentence across knowledge sources?

Additional comments
- Reporting standard deviations or confidence intervals over multiple runs would strengthen the significance claims.
- A brief error analysis (where K-SAN helps/hurts relative to baselines) would be valuable.
- Clarify the data splits, dev set construction, and whether hyperparameters were tuned separately per dataset size.
