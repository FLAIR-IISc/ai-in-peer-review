Summary and claimed contributions
This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, the model extracts a set of knowledge-guided substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf paths from a dependency tree or edges from an AMR graph), embeds each substructure, attends over them based on a sentence embedding, and produces a knowledge-guided sentence representation. This representation is injected into a recurrent tagger, with a joint architecture balancing chain-based and knowledge-guided signals. Experiments on ATIS show gains over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines and over syntax-aware sentence encoders (DCNN, Tree-RNN), with especially strong improvements in low-resource settings. The authors also report comparable gains when swapping different parsers and knowledge types (dependency vs. AMR), arguing generality.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, end-to-end attention mechanism over knowledge-derived sentence substructures used to guide a sequence tagger, yielding consistent improvements and robustness under data scarcity.
2) Demonstration that the mechanism is agnostic to the knowledge source (dependency parses, AMR graphs, different parsers), with similar gains across sources.
3) A joint tagger that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided signals, mitigating sensitivity to noisy parses.

Strengths
- Clear and flexible architecture that integrates external structure via attention. The formulation (embedding substructures, softmax attention against a sentence vector, and a joint RNN tagger) is simple, differentiable end-to-end, and easy to port across knowledge sources. Tying the encoders Mkg/Min is a sensible regularization choice.
- Consistent empirical gains, particularly in low-resource regimes. On ATIS, K-SAN improves over strong non-knowledge baselines (CNN encoder–tagger) by 1–2 F1 on the Large set and more on Small/Medium, with significance reported. The low-resource gains substantiate the central claim that external structure acts as a useful teacher when labeled data is scarce.
- Generalization across knowledge sources and parsers. The model yields similar improvements whether using dependency trees (Stanford or SyntaxNet) or AMR (JAMR or rule-based), despite large differences in the number of substructures. This supports the claimed agnosticism to the schema and robustness to parser/domain mismatch.
- Interpretability. The attention visualizations illustrate that the model focuses on semantically salient substructures (origin, destination, time), even with parser errors, providing qualitative evidence that the attention mechanism learns useful structure.
- Joint modeling reduces brittleness. The joint tagger that mixes chain and knowledge-guided hidden states is a pragmatic design that likely explains the robustness to noisy knowledge, and it is validated empirically.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing attention/memory models. The core mechanism—storing substructure embeddings and attending to them with a sentence query—closely mirrors memory networks and attention-based sentence encoders. The paper’s distinction (substructures as memory slots derived from parses) feels incremental; more positioning against contemporaneous structured/semantic attention work is needed. The claim of being “the first” end-to-end model to use general knowledge as guidance seems overstated given prior memory/attention literature (albeit not on slot filling).
- Evaluation scope is narrow and misses stronger baselines. All results are on ATIS, a small, single-domain dataset. State-of-the-art slot filling baselines at the time include BiLSTM(-CRF) and CRF decoding; the paper uses unidirectional GRU without CRF, and does not compare to BiLSTM-CRF or joint intent-slot architectures. This weakens the strength of the empirical claim and makes it hard to assess competitiveness beyond ATIS.
- Insufficient ablations to isolate where gains come from. Important components are not ablated: joint vs. knowledge-only vs. chain-only within K-SAN; effect of α; tying vs. untied Mkg/Min; attention vs. uniform averaging over substructures; use of dependency/AMR labels; number/selection of substructures. Without these, it is unclear whether the gains are due to attention, additional sentence-level context o, or regularization from the joint architecture.
- Claims of efficiency/parallelizability are not substantiated. The paper argues substructures can be modeled separately and implies better-than-linear scaling, but provides no time/memory benchmarks, complexity analysis, or throughput comparisons to baselines.
- Some methodological clarity gaps. Details on the construction of AMR substructures (beyond an example) are sparse; it is unclear how substructures are ordered, truncated, or normalized across variable sizes. Feeding the same sentence-level o at each time step is plausible, but a discussion of per-token attention or token–substructure alignment could strengthen the design rationale.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide ablations for: (i) joint vs. knowledge-only K-SAN; (ii) varying α; (iii) tying vs. untied Mkg/Min; (iv) replacing attention with uniform averaging over substructures; (v) using dependency/AMR relation labels?
- How are AMR substructures generated in general (algorithmic description), and how sensitive are results to that choice? Do you consider only root-to-leaf paths, or also other graph walks?
- How does K-SAN perform with stronger baselines such as BiLSTM-CRF (with and without CRF decoding) or bidirectional encoders? Could K-SAN be combined with a CRF layer?
- Can you report training/inference time and memory usage vs. RNN/CNN baselines and DCNN/Tree-RNN?
- Did you explore token-level conditioning (e.g., computing attention keyed by token context rather than a single sentence vector) to allow position-specific use of knowledge?

Additional comments
- The “first” claim should be toned down or more precisely scoped (e.g., for slot filling).
- Consider evaluating on an additional dataset (e.g., SNIPS or a multi-domain NLU corpus) to strengthen generality claims.
- Reference formatting has several inconsistencies/typos; please clean up for camera-ready.
- Public release of code and parsing scripts would aid reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper presents a neat and potentially useful idea with solid gains on ATIS and good evidence of generality across knowledge sources. However, the novelty over existing attention/memory paradigms is limited, the evaluation is narrow and lacks strong baselines, and key ablations/efficiency claims are missing. Addressing these points could make the work much more compelling.
