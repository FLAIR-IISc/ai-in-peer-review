Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. An utterance is parsed into substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in a dependency tree; analogous paths in AMR). Each substructure is embedded; an utterance embedding attends over these substructures via softmax of inner products; the resulting knowledge-guided sentence vector is injected into an RNN/GRU tagger. A joint tagger linearly combines hidden states from a plain chain RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS (small/medium/full) compare against CRF, RNN, CNN and structure-aware encoders (DCNN, Tree-RNN), show consistent gains, and demonstrate “knowledge generalization” across parsers (Stanford/SyntaxNet) and AMR (rule-based/JAMR). Visualizations illustrate attention.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple structural attention mechanism over parser-derived substructures to augment an RNN tagger for slot filling.
2) An empirical study showing robustness across different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR) and better gains in low-resource settings.
3) A joint architecture to balance chain-based and knowledge-guided information, with attention visualizations.

Strengths
- Conceptually clear and modular: The decomposition into knowledge encoding, attention over substructures, and a tagger is easy to instantiate with different encoders (CNN/RNN/NN) and different knowledge sources (syntax/AMR). The tied-weights encoder is a reasonable simplification.
- Robustness to data scarcity and parser choice: Gains are largest on small data; results are stable across four knowledge sources (two syntactic parsers, two AMR parsers). This supports the “knowledge as guidance” hypothesis.
- Interpretable attention: Visualizations qualitatively align higher attention with salient origin/destination/time substructures, even under parser noise, which is desirable in SLU.
- Empirical improvements over listed baselines: K-SAN(CNN/RNN) outperforms CRF/RNN/CNN and Tree-RNN/DCNN on ATIS; the full-data F1 (≈95) is competitive with older literature on ATIS.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior memory/attention and tree-structured models: The method reduces to a single-hop memory/attention over substructure embeddings using a dot-product with a sentence embedding (akin to Memory Networks/DMN), then injecting a global vector into a token-level RNN. In contrast, prior tree-LSTM/Tree-RNN/DCNN already integrate structure, and attention has been used widely. The key novelty—attending over parser-derived substructures for sequence tagging—feels incremental without deeper architectural advances (e.g., token-aligned structured attention, multi-hop, or differentiable parsing).
- Evaluation scope and baselines: Results are only on ATIS (saturated, small, single-domain). Strong contemporary baselines are missing: BiLSTM-CRF, BiLSTM with character embeddings, CRF decoding to enforce IOB constraints, or recent joint intent/slot models. It is unclear whether the “RNN baseline” and K-SAN use the same recurrent unit (GRU vs simple RNN), or whether bidirectionality is used. Without these, the practical significance is hard to judge.
- Insufficient ablations and sensitivity analyses: No ablation of (i) tied vs untied Min/Mkg, (ii) using dependency/AMR relation labels, (iii) attention vs uniform/learned scalar weights, (iv) joint tagger vs knowledge-guided alone, (v) α in Eq.(11) (fixed at 0.5), (vi) random structures or gold parses to quantify reliance on structure quality. The claim that the approach is robust to parser noise is not quantified. The claim that CNN is best on small sets and RNN on large is observational without analysis.
- “End-to-end” and efficiency claims are overstated: Parsing/AMR extraction is an external, non-differentiable step; thus the system is not end-to-end with respect to raw text. No runtime/complexity or parallelization results are provided; the number of substructures scales with sentence length and tree branching (Table 2 lists maxima but no cost analysis). The statement that modeling time “may not increase linearly” is unsubstantiated.
- Methodological/detail issues and clarity: Several details are under-specified: number of CNN filters and layers for Min/Mkg; whether encoders are bi-directional; dropout placement (input/recurrent?); early stopping/validation split and seed control; handling of OOV/casing; use of pre-trained vs randomly initialized embeddings; whether the tagger is bi-directional; whether CRF decoding is used (appears not). Statistical significance (t-test) is reported but the protocol (over random restarts? folds?) is not described. The attention is defined over substructures; how the “word-level attention” in Figure 5 is derived is unclear. Section 4.2 mentions “previous turns,” but the task is single-turn ATIS—this is inconsistent. Finally, many equations/notations are sloppy (e.g., Mo vs o, reuse of U/V, inconsistent bolding), and references are highly inconsistent/incomplete (numerous “?”, malformed author names, missing venues), which is below ACL standards.

Questions to authors
- Stronger baselines: How does K-SAN compare to BiLSTM-CRF (with/without char embeddings) on ATIS under the same training regime? Is your tagger bidirectional? If not, why?
- Ablations: Please report (a) untied Min/Mkg, (b) use of dependency/AMR relation labels, (c) removing attention (uniform averaging) or replacing with learned scalar weights, (d) knowledge-guided tagger without the joint combination, (e) sensitivity to α, (f) random substructures and gold parses (if available) to quantify reliance on structure quality.
- Significance protocol: How were t-tests computed (number of random seeds, variance estimates)? What is the validation split? Are results averaged over runs?
- Implementation details: Number of CNN filters and layers for Min/Mkg? Are encoders bi-directional? Where is dropout applied? How are embeddings initialized and updated? Do you enforce IOB constraints (e.g., CRF layer)?
- Efficiency: Provide training/inference time and memory vs baselines as a function of sentence length and number of substructures; clarify parallelization strategy.
- Attention granularity: Have you tried token-aligned structural attention (e.g., attention over substructures incident to the current token) instead of injecting a single global o at each time step? This may better localize slot decisions.
- Generalization: Any results beyond ATIS (e.g., SNIPS, multi-domain SLU) to support robustness claims?

Overall, while the idea is reasonable and results are promising on ATIS, the limited novelty, incomplete evaluation, missing strong baselines/ablations, and numerous presentation/formatting issues make this submission below ACL acceptance standards in its current form. I recommend rejection and encourage a revised version addressing the above.
