Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external structural knowledge (e.g., dependency trees or AMR graphs) is decomposed into substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf paths), embedded, and stored as a memory. A sentence embedding attends over these substructure embeddings to produce a knowledge-guided representation, which is injected into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN.
- The approach is evaluated on ATIS with different training set sizes. K-SAN with various encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) outperforms CRF, RNN, CNN sentence-encoder baselines, and structural baselines (DCNN, Tree-RNN), with larger gains in low-resource settings. The method appears robust to the choice of parser/knowledge source (Stanford vs SyntaxNet; AMR parsers).
- The paper provides qualitative attention visualizations and a knowledge generalization study (dependency vs AMR).

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A practical architectural instantiation of attention over knowledge-guided substructures for sequence tagging, combining memory/attention ideas with external linguistic structures in a way that is trainable end-to-end with the tagger.
2) Empirical evidence that such knowledge-guided attention improves ATIS slot filling, especially in low-resource regimes, and is robust to different parsers/knowledge types.
3) A simple, general recipe to convert heterogeneous structures (dependency trees, AMR graphs) into comparable substructures for attention.

Strengths
- Clear, modular architecture bridging external structures and sequence tagging. The design (Min/Mkg shared encoders; inner-product attention over substructure memory; sum and Mout; injection into RNN and joint combiner) is simple enough to reproduce and provides a concrete way to exploit structure without hand-crafted features.
- Robust improvements across data scales and knowledge sources. On ATIS, K-SAN consistently improves F1 over strong non-structural baselines (e.g., CNN sentence encoder) and structural baselines (DCNN, Tree-RNN). Gains are strongest in Small/Medium (e.g., 73.57→74.60 and 85.52→87.99 with CNN encoder), supporting the “knowledge helps when data is scarce” claim, and remain positive on Large (93.88→94.86/94.97).
- Generalization across parsers and semantic formalisms. Results with Stanford/SyntaxNet dependencies and rule-based/JAMR AMR are similar, despite different substructure counts, indicating the method does not hinge on a specific parser or formalism.
- Qualitative interpretability. Attention visualizations align with salient spans (origin, destination, times), and show resilience to parser errors, lending credibility that the model focuses on meaningful substructures rather than spurious signals.
- Conceptual novelty relative to prior SLU work. Prior systems typically inject parse-derived features or use tree-structured encoders over full trees. K-SAN’s selective attention over substructures offers a different trade-off (avoid committing to whole-tree composition; mitigate error propagation), which is empirically supported.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope. Only ATIS is used. ATIS is small, nearly solved, and idiosyncratic. Without evaluation on additional SLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MIT Restaurant/Movie), it is hard to assess generality. Cross-domain results would substantively strengthen the claims.
- Missing comparisons to stronger modern taggers. BiLSTM-CRF (possibly with character embeddings) is a de facto strong baseline for slot filling; CRF decoding often boosts performance. The paper compares to Elman-RNN/GRU and CNN sentence encoders but omits LSTM/BiLSTM-CRF, making it difficult to position K-SAN against state-of-the-art sequence taggers.
- “End-to-end” claim is overstated. The approach depends on external parsers/AMR tools trained on different data (PTB, etc.) and is not jointly trained with them. The network is end-to-end conditioned on fixed knowledge, but the pipeline is not end-to-end from text. This should be clarified and framed accordingly.
- Incomplete ablations and architectural details. Key design choices are not dissected: effect of attention vs. uniform averaging; contribution of knowledge-guided RNN vs. chain RNN vs. joint (the joint is reported, but not the standalone knowledge-guided variant); using/tieing Min/Mkg; sensitivity to α (fixed at 0.5). The GRU integration is hand-wavy (“can be added into gating”), lacking precise equations. An ablation table would help isolate what drives gains.
- Efficiency/parallelizability claim is not substantiated. The paper claims potential efficiency because substructures are modeled separately but provides no runtime, memory, or scaling analysis with respect to sentence length or number of substructures. Empirical measurements or complexity analysis would be needed.
- Underuse of structural labels. Dependency/AMR relation labels are ignored “for generalization,” which may discard valuable information. A variant using labels (or showing that including them hurts generalization) would clarify the trade-off.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Please report results with a BiLSTM-CRF baseline (with/without character embeddings) on ATIS and, if possible, another SLU dataset. How does K-SAN compare or combine with CRF decoding?
- Can you provide an ablation study: (a) chain RNN vs knowledge-guided RNN vs joint; (b) attention vs uniform averaging over substructures; (c) untied vs tied Min/Mkg; (d) varying α and learning α?
- How exactly is o injected into GRU? Please include full equations for gates and state updates with Mo terms.
- What is the distribution of substructure counts/lengths across sentences for each knowledge source? Do longer paths disproportionately receive attention due to inner-product magnitudes?
- Did you try using relation labels (dep/AMR) or edge-role embeddings? If so, what was the effect?
- How is the dev set constructed on ATIS? Are hyperparameters selected once per training-size setting?

Additional comments
- The visualization example states the parser is incorrect yet attention helps; adding a quantitative stress test (e.g., randomly perturb a percentage of edges) would bolster the robustness claim.
- Please clarify whether word embeddings are trained from scratch on ATIS or pre-trained and fine-tuned; and whether OOV handling/character information is used.
- Minor: Some references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted.
