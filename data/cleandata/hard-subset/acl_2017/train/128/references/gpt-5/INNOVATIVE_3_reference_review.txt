Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The key idea is to treat external linguistic structure (e.g., dependency or AMR) as a set of substructures, embed them into a “knowledge memory,” and learn an attention distribution over these substructures conditioned on the current utterance. The resulting knowledge-guided representation conditions an RNN tagger, with a joint architecture that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided signals. The model is trained end-to-end. Experiments on ATIS show consistent gains, particularly in low-resource settings, and comparable improvements across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependencies, rule-based/JAMR AMR). Visualizations suggest interpretable attention over salient substructures.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Conceptual: A general, end-to-end mechanism that uses external structured knowledge as an attention space over substructure embeddings for sequence tagging, rather than as fixed features. This “knowledge as a teacher” framing is novel in NLU slot filling.
2) Generalization and robustness: Evidence that diverse knowledge types (syntax and semantics) from different parsers yield similar gains, and that the approach is relatively robust to parser errors, with larger improvements under data scarcity.
3) Empirical result: New SOTA or near-SOTA on ATIS slot filling, with interpretable attention visualizations.

Strengths
- Novel knowledge–attention integration: The paper moves beyond feature augmentation by making prior structure a first-class, learnable attention mechanism over substructures. This is a clean and broadly applicable idea that can plug into many sequence models.
- Knowledge-agnostic design: The same pipeline works with dependency trees and AMR graphs, without relying on labels or schema-specific engineering. The comparable gains across four knowledge sources support the claim of generality.
- Robust low-resource gains: Improvements are most pronounced on small/medium data, which is practically important in NLU. This supports the paper’s premise that guided attention mitigates data scarcity and parser mismatch.
- End-to-end and interpretable: The attention visualization aligns with semantically salient parts (origin/destination/time), even when parses are imperfect, offering both interpretability and internal validation of the mechanism.
- Simple, modular architecture: The design choices (embedding substructures with NN/RNN/CNN, softmax attention, joint tagger) are straightforward and easy to replicate/extend, making the contribution useful for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: ATIS is small and near-saturated; no results on other slot-filling datasets (e.g., SNIPS, multi-domain datasets) or languages. The paper would be much stronger with at least one additional domain to validate generality and robustness claims.
- Missing comparisons to stronger baselines: No BiLSTM-CRF or contemporary self-attention/Transformer-style encoders for slot filling, which are standard strong baselines. It’s unclear whether the observed gains would hold over such models or if o could similarly benefit them.
- Ablation depth: Important ablations are missing: (a) remove attention (uniform over substructures); (b) use whole-tree/sentence encoding without substructure selection; (c) include dependency/AMR labels vs. excluding them; (d) random/noisy structures to quantify robustness. These would clarify where the gains come from.
- Incomplete efficiency evidence: The paper claims efficiency/parallelizability because substructures are modeled separately, but provides no training/inference time or memory metrics, nor analysis of scaling with number/size of substructures.
- Clarity of knowledge encoding: The exact construction and ordering of substructure sequences (e.g., “show flights seattle from”), handling of directionality and multiword nodes, and the effect of ignoring relation labels are under-specified. This makes reproduction and interpretation harder.

Questions to Authors
- Substructure construction: How exactly are paths linearized (word order, direction, inclusion of roots/leaves), and how are duplicates handled? Did you try including dependency/AMR edge labels or directions, and what was the impact?
- Robustness controls: What happens with random substructures or shuffled parses? Can you quantify sensitivity to parser error rates? Any ablation with uniform attention vs. learned attention?
- Stronger baselines: Have you integrated o into a BiLSTM-CRF tagger? How does K-SAN compare to self-attention over words (no external knowledge) or to memory networks adapted for tagging?
- Efficiency: What is the average/max number of substructures per sentence across knowledge sources, and how does training/inference time compare to the plain RNN? Any batching/parallelization details?
- Joint weighting: How sensitive is performance to α in the joint tagger? Did a learned α or gating outperform the fixed 0.5?
- AMR specifics: How do you align AMR nodes to surface tokens for substructure embeddings? Do you include concept normalization or named-entity expansion?

Overall, this paper introduces a compelling and broadly useful idea—using external structure as an attention space over substructures, trained end-to-end—for sequence tagging. Despite limited evaluation breadth and some missing ablations, the conceptual novelty and promising low-resource gains merit acceptance.
