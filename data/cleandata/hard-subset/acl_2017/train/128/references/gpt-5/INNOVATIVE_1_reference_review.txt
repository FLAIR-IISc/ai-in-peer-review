Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The key idea is to treat external linguistic structure as a “teacher” that guides attention over substructures derived from a parse (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in dependency trees or edges in AMR). Substructures are embedded, attended, and aggregated into a knowledge-guided sentence representation, which is then combined with a standard RNN tagger; a joint tagger blends chain-based and knowledge-guided representations. The model is trained end-to-end, with tied encoders for sentences and substructures. Experiments on ATIS show gains over baselines, especially in low-resource settings, and similar gains across multiple knowledge sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers).

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A novel, end-to-end architecture that uses external linguistic structure to induce attention over substructures for sequence tagging, rather than injecting knowledge as static features.
2) Demonstration that the proposed mechanism generalizes across different knowledge schemas (syntactic trees and AMR graphs) and parser variants, with robustness to parser errors.
3) Empirical improvements on ATIS slot filling, most pronounced with limited training data, with insightful attention visualizations.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: “Knowledge as teacher” instantiated as attention over parse-derived substructures for sequence tagging is a fresh and compelling direction. Prior work in SLU largely fed knowledge as features or used tree encoders wholesale; selectively attending to salient substructures and combining that with token-level RNNs is a clear step forward.

- End-to-end and schema-agnostic integration of knowledge: The same mechanism works with dependency trees and AMR graphs, and with different encoders (CNN/RNN/NN) for substructures. The tied sentence/substructure encoder and differentiable attention enable learning what parts of the structure to trust without hand-engineering. This is attractive for practical SLU where parsers and schemas vary.

- Strong gains in low-resource regimes and robustness to noisy parses: On 1/40 and 1/10 splits, K-SAN yields statistically significant improvements over strong neural baselines. The attention visualizations show the model focusing on origin/destination/time despite incorrect dependencies, supporting the claimed robustness.

- Clean, modular architecture with interpretable components: The decomposition into knowledge encoding, attention, and a joint tagger makes the contribution easy to extend. The joint RNN design that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided information is sensible and empirically effective.

- Knowledge generalization study: Reporting results across two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers, with comparable performance despite different numbers of substructures, strengthens the generality claim.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and saturated benchmark: All results are on ATIS only, which is small and well-trodden. Improvements on the full set over strong neural baselines are modest. Absence of additional datasets (e.g., other SLU corpora) weakens the empirical case for broad impact.

- Missing strong baselines and ablations: There is no comparison to BiLSTM-CRF (a de facto standard for slot filling), nor to word-level self-attention/transformer encoders, nor to hierarchical sentence embeddings concatenated to the RNN at each step (a closer ablation to K-SAN without structure-guided attention). The paper reports only the joint tagger; ablations of α, knowledge-only vs chain-only, attention vs uniform averaging over substructures, and use of dependency labels would clarify where gains come from.

- “Knowledge” is sentence-local parsing rather than external KBs: Despite the title/positioning, all experiments use structure extracted from the input utterance (syntax/AMR). No results with external knowledge graphs (entities/relations beyond the sentence) are provided. The framing risks overclaiming generality; demonstrating at least one external KB scenario would better substantiate the claim.

- Efficiency/parallelizability claim unsubstantiated: The paper asserts that modeling substructures separately improves efficiency, but provides no runtime, memory, or complexity measurements, nor scaling studies with number/length of substructures.

- Clarity gaps and reproducibility details: It is unclear how substructure word order is formed (e.g., “show flights seattle from”), whether dependency labels were tried and why omitted, whether the knowledge-guided representation o is recomputed per sentence once or per timestep, and how sensitive results are to encoder choice and hyperparameters. The tied weights assumption (Min = Mkg) is stated but not justified via ablation.

Questions to authors
- Substructure construction: How exactly are tokens ordered within each substructure for dependency trees and AMR? Did you try including relation labels or edge directions, and with what effect?

- Attention ablations: What happens if you average substructures uniformly (no attention), or attend over words directly (no structure)? How sensitive are results to α in the joint model?

- Baselines: Can you report BiLSTM-CRF and a word-level self-attention/transformer baseline, as well as a “sentence embedding + RNN” baseline that mirrors K-SAN but without structure-guided attention?

- Computation: Is the knowledge-guided vector o fixed per sentence and injected at every timestep, or timestep-dependent? What is the per-sentence runtime and memory overhead vs the strongest neural baseline, and how does it scale with number of substructures?

- External knowledge: Have you tried K-SAN with substructures derived from an external KB (e.g., entity/relation chains linked from the utterance)? If not, can you clarify scope and rename claims accordingly?

- Reproducibility: Will you release code, preprocessing (parsers, AMR tools), and the exact hyperparameters? What is the average/maximum number of substructures per sentence for each knowledge source?

Overall assessment
This is a bold and promising idea—using structured knowledge to guide attention over substructures for sequence tagging—implemented cleanly and shown to help in low-resource settings and across knowledge schemas. Despite evaluation limitations and missing baselines/ablations, I find the conceptual novelty and potential impact compelling and am inclined to recommend acceptance, provided the authors address the key empirical gaps.
