- Proposes K-SAN: attend over knowledge-derived substructures (dependency/AMR), embed them and sentence, and condition an RNN tagger with a joint chain+knowledge architecture.  
- Main contributions: end-to-end structural attention framework; empirical evidence of structural attention improving slot filling and robustness across parsers and scarce data; strong ATIS results with attention visualizations and multiple encoders.  

- Strength: Conceptually clear and modular bridge between external linguistic structure and neural attention/memory, avoiding brittle feature engineering.  
- Strength: Consistent empirical gains on ATIS, especially in low-data regimes, indicating improved generalization.  
- Strength: Robust across knowledge sources and parsers (dependency/AMR; Stanford/SyntaxNet/JAMR).  
- Strength: Interpretability via attention visualizations that highlight meaningful substructures.  
- Strength: Practical design using standard components, end-to-end training, and reasonable engineering choices (tied weights, joint tagger).

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to ATIS, a small/saturated benchmark, and missing strong baselines like BiLSTM-CRF and attention-based taggers without external knowledge.  
- Weakness: Insufficient ablations to isolate the contributions of attention vs averaging, knowledge vs random substructures, joint vs single branch, injection point, labeled vs unlabeled dependencies, and tied weights.  
- Weakness: AMR integration and alignment details are under-specified, harming reproducibility and interpretation.  
- Weakness: Efficiency and parallelizability claims are unsubstantiated due to lack of complexity/runtime/memory comparisons.  
- Weakness: Modest margins on the full set (~1–1.5 F1) with thin significance reporting and unclear variance across runs/seeds.

- Question: Please compare to a strong BiLSTM-CRF baseline (with/without char embeddings) and an attention-based encoder without external knowledge.  
- Question (ablations): a) Replace attention with uniform averaging over substructures. b) Use random/shuffled substructures. c) Remove the joint branch and vary α. d) Test using dependency relation labels.  
- Question: Clarify AMR specifics — how are AMR nodes mapped to embeddings, are concept strings or surface alignments used, and how are out-of-vocab concepts handled?  
- Question: Quantify robustness to parser noise (e.g., performance with gold parses or injected noise) beyond qualitative visuals.  
- Question: Report training/inference time, memory vs best non-knowledge baseline, and average number of substructures per sentence per knowledge source.

- Additional: Clarify how small/medium splits are constructed and whether results are averaged over multiple splits/runs.  
- Additional: Provide per-slot F1 to show which slot types benefit most from structural attention.  
- Additional: Release code and configurations to aid reproducibility.

- Overall recommendation: Conceptually neat and promising with robustness and interpretability, but evaluation breadth, critical ablations, and stronger baselines are needed; borderline accept leaning positive pending stronger experiments.
