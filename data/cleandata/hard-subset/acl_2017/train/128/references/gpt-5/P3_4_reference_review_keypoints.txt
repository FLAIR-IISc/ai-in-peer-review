- Proposal: K-SAN encodes parser-derived substructures, attends over them using the sentence embedding, and injects the aggregated knowledge representation into an RNN tagger with a joint chain/knowledge tagger.  
- Claimed contributions: end-to-end attention over knowledge substructures for tagging, generalization across heterogeneous parsers, and efficiency via substructure decomposition.  
- Primary contribution (reviewer): single-hop memory/attention applied to parse substructures for slot filling with empirical gains on ATIS and qualitative structural-attention analysis.  

Strengths
- Clear, modular, differentiable architecture bridging memory/attention concepts and structured linguistic knowledge.  
- Empirical improvements on ATIS over strong baselines, especially in low-resource settings; joint tagger stabilizes performance with noisy knowledge.  
- Knowledge-source agnosticism: similar gains using multiple parsers and AMR despite different substructure granularities.  
- Interpretability via attention visualizations that focus on task-salient substructures.  
- Careful comparisons of encoders and consistent training, with significance testing across data regimes.  

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: core mechanism is a single-hop memory network; novelty mainly in choice of memory items and task application.  
- Narrow evaluation: only ATIS with lexical features; unclear gains with stronger encoders (biLSTM-CRF, pretrained embeddings) or other datasets/ASR noise.  
- Missing crucial ablations (uniform/random attention, capacity controls, use of edge labels, learnable α/gating, multi-hop) to isolate the contribution of knowledge-guided attention.  
- Unsupported efficiency claims: no runtime, parameter counts, or scaling measurements; substructure building/encoding costs unreported.  
- External supervision confound: reliance on external parsers introduces extra supervision/domain mismatch and lacks fair baselines using the same knowledge.  

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the number and construction of substructures and to uniform attention baselines?  
- Do dependency/AMR edge labels help when included in the encoder?  
- Does K-SAN still help when integrated with biLSTM-CRF, character/subword features, or pretrained embeddings?  
- Can α be learned or gated, and was multi-hop attention tried?  
- Do you have runtime/memory/parameter comparisons to strongest baselines?  

Additional comments
- Clarify substructure linearization, token order, and repeated tokens across substructures (Figure 3 confusion).  
- Provide empirical evidence on robustness to noisy parses to support reduced error-propagation claims.  
- Consider reporting results on ASR hypotheses to strengthen SLU relevance.  
- Clarify ATIS dev split and hyperparameter tuning; include code or more hyperparameter details for reproducibility.  

Overall verdict
- Solid, interpretable adaptation of memory/attention to parse substructures with encouraging ATIS results, but moderate conceptual novelty and insufficient breadth/ablations; borderline acceptance pending stronger ablations and comparisons.
