Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees or AMR graphs), K-SAN extracts substructures (root-to-leaf paths), embeds them, computes attention over them conditioned on a sentence embedding, and produces a single knowledge-guided sentence vector o, which is fed into a (GRU-based) RNN tagger. A joint variant blends a chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN via a fixed α.
- Empirical results on ATIS (small/medium/full training subsets) show modest gains over baselines and prior “structural” encoders, with the largest gains in low-data regimes. The model appears robust across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency, rule-based/JAMR AMR).
- Ancillary contributions include visualizations of attention and a brief study of knowledge source generalization.

Strengths
- Clear and broadly applicable idea: attending over knowledge-derived substructures and integrating the resulting representation into a tagger. The design is simple, end-to-end trainable, and can consume different structure providers (syntax/semantics).
- Consistent improvements on ATIS in small/medium data settings, which supports the claim that externally guided structure can improve generalization under scarcity. The reported gains (e.g., K-SAN(CNN) 74.60 vs best CNN 73.57 on small; 87.99 vs 85.52 on medium) are non-trivial in relative error reduction.
- The knowledge-source robustness experiment (dependency vs AMR; different parsers) is valuable: performance stability despite different structural inputs supports the generality claim.
- The attention visualizations are informative and suggest the model focuses on semantically salient regions (origin, destination, temporal expressions), even with imperfect parses.
- The framework ties to memory/attention literature and is conceptually extensible to other structural sources (knowledge graphs, grammars), which could be useful beyond slot filling.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing attention/memory models: the mechanism (softmax over dot products of a sentence vector with a set of key vectors, weighted sum to form a context) is essentially a standard single-hop memory/attention over a set, applied to substructures. The step from prior dependency/AMR sentence encoders to this attention pooling is incremental, and the paper does not demonstrate why a single global sentence-level o is preferable to token-level attention or more expressive models (multi-hop, per-token structural attention).
- Evaluation scope and baselines are dated/weak. ATIS alone is insufficient; no evaluation on larger/more diverse SLU datasets. Stronger standard baselines are missing: BiLSTM-CRF (with/without char embeddings), CRF decoding on top of RNNs, token-level self-attention, or joint intent-slot models. The “state-of-the-art” claim on ATIS at 94.9 F1 is questionable given widely reported ≥95 F1 with stronger architectures. The CRF “structural” baseline uses only dependent heads—substantially weaker than modern neural CRF taggers.
- Methodological ambiguities and potential leakage:
  - “Represent words as their embeddings trained on the in-domain data”: unclear whether test utterances were used to train embeddings (leakage). Clarify whether embeddings are trained on train only; whether they are updated during training.
  - Dev set composition is unspecified; ATIS typically lacks a standard dev split—how was tuning done for each subset?
  - Statistical testing: t-test on a single test set F1 is inappropriate without multiple runs; number of random seeds/runs not reported; no variance reported.
  - Training details are under-specified: where is dropout applied (embeddings, recurrent, Mout)? Early stopping? Gradient clipping?
- Claims not supported by evidence:
  - “Efficiency and parallelizability”: no runtime/memory analysis or training/inference speed comparisons. The number of substructures is still O(n), and attention adds cost; the claim that time “may not increase linearly” is unsubstantiated.
  - “Avoid error propagation” and “robust to poor structures”: while some robustness is shown, the method still relies on parses/AMR. No controlled noise experiments or ablations support these claims.
- Modeling/design concerns and missing ablations:
  - The knowledge-guided vector o is global and injected identically at each time step. This discards token-specific structural cues; per-token attention over substructures or gating conditioned on wt would be more principled.
  - Tying Mkg and Min is asserted without justification; no ablation of tying vs untied.
  - Ignoring dependency/AMR relation labels removes informative signals; no comparison with labeled variants.
  - α is fixed at 0.5; no tuning/ablation of α or demonstration that the joint model outperforms its components consistently.
  - Equation (7) and decoding ignore tag dependencies; no CRF layer atop the RNN; yet the paper compares to CRF.
- Presentation/formatting issues that hinder reproducibility:
  - Many citation/reference errors (placeholders “? \Q2015\E”, malformed author names, missing venues/years), numerous typos and broken figure references. Notation inconsistencies (e.g., reuse of M, Mo vs Mout; Eq. (1) softmax notation ambiguous; substructure example “show flights seattle from” unclear ordering). These detract from clarity and polish.

Questions to authors
- Data splits and leakage: Did you train word embeddings using only the training portion for each subset, or all in-domain data including test? Are embeddings fine-tuned during training?
- Dev set: How is the dev set constructed for hyperparameter tuning in each of the three training-size conditions? Do you use the standard ATIS validation split or carve one from training?
- Statistical testing and stability: How many independent runs per configuration? Report mean±std over seeds. How was the t-test conducted on F1?
- Architecture ablations: Results for (i) chain-only, (ii) knowledge-only, (iii) joint with tuned α, (iv) tied vs untied Mkg/Min, (v) using labeled dependencies/AMR relations, (vi) per-token structural attention rather than a single global o?
- Baselines: Why not compare against BiLSTM-CRF, character-aware models, or RNN-CRF hybrids? Can you add such baselines to substantiate the “state-of-the-art” claim?
- Substructure construction: Clarify token ordering within a substructure path (“show flights seattle from” appears odd). How are AMR substructures defined exactly? Do you normalize or include edge labels?
- Efficiency: Provide complexity analysis and wall-clock comparisons vs baselines. What is the average/95th percentile number of substructures for dependency vs AMR on ATIS?
- Implementation details: Where is dropout applied? Any gradient clipping? What are the dimensions of Mout and Mo? Do you use early stopping? Code availability?
