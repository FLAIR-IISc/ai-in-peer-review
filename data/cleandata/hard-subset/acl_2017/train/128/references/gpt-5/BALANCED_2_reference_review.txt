Summary and Claimed Contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling in goal-oriented spoken language understanding. The core idea is to treat external linguistic knowledge (e.g., dependency trees or AMR graphs) as a “teacher” that induces attention over substructures (root-to-leaf paths or graph fragments). Substructure embeddings are stored in a “knowledge memory,” and attention-weighted combinations are fused with an RNN-based tagger via a joint model. Experiments on ATIS show gains over several baselines, with particularly strong improvements in low-resource settings; the approach also shows similar benefits when swapping knowledge sources and parsers.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, interpretable architecture that integrates knowledge-guided substructure attention with a neural tagger, enabling the model to focus on salient structural pieces for sequence labeling.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach is robust across knowledge sources (dependency vs. AMR; different parsers) and data regimes, with consistent gains—especially in low-resource settings.
3) Analysis and visualizations of attention over substructures/words, suggesting the model learns meaningful alignments that correspond to slots.

Strengths
- Clear, modular design that links knowledge and attention: The formulation—encode substructures, compute attention via u^T m_i, aggregate, and fuse with an RNN tagger—is conceptually clean and easy to extend. The joint model that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided streams is a sensible way to hedge against noisy parses.

- Robust improvements across data sizes and knowledge sources: K-SAN variants (particularly CNN encoder) outperform CRF/RNN/CNN baselines on ATIS, with statistically significant gains in most settings. The method appears robust to parser choice (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet) and to semantic vs. syntactic knowledge (AMR vs. dependencies), with similar performance across sources despite large differences in substructure counts. This supports the generality claim.

- Benefits in low-resource regimes: The gains are most pronounced on the Small and Medium ATIS subsets, an important practical scenario. The attention mechanism over knowledge-guided substructures seems to compensate for data scarcity, as corroborated by stable attention patterns across training sizes.

- Interpretability: Visualizations of attention over both relations and words align with intuitive cues (origin/destination/time), and even when parses are imperfect the model still extracts useful structure. This provides a tangible advantage over black-box sentence encoders.

- Thorough implementation details: The paper reports model choices (GRU, tied encoders), training setup (Adam, dropouts, dimensions), and shows variants with different encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) and a joint tagger, improving reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope and missing strong baselines: Results are only on ATIS, a small and saturated benchmark. The paper does not compare against widely adopted sequence labeling baselines such as BiLSTM-CRF (Huang et al., 2015) or strong joint intent-slot models with CRF decoding, which are standard for slot filling. Without these, it is hard to assess the true competitiveness of K-SAN.

- Incremental novelty relative to existing attention/memory and structure-aware models: The method adapts known memory/attention ideas to parse-derived substructures for tagging. Prior structural encoders (Tree-LSTM/Dependency-CNN) already exploit syntactic structure; the novelty is more in the use of attention over substructures as a memory than in a fundamentally new learning principle. Given this, broader evaluation and stronger baselines are needed to justify significance.

- “End-to-end” claim is overstated: The approach depends on non-differentiable external parsers/AMR systems; only the neural parts are trained end-to-end. In practice, performance hinges on parser quality and domain match (PTB-trained parsers on ATIS). The paper does not quantify sensitivity to parse errors beyond anecdotal examples.

- Efficiency/parallelizability claims lack evidence: The paper asserts potential efficiency because substructures are modeled separately, but reports no runtime, memory, or scalability analyses (e.g., vs. sentence length or number of substructures). Table 2 lists maximum substructures, but there is no empirical validation that K-SAN is faster or more parallelizable than alternatives.

- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses: Key design choices are not dissected. Examples:
  • Effect of attention vs. uniform/learned pooling over substructures.
  • Impact of tying Mkg and Min weights; these encode different sequence types and lengths.
  • Sensitivity to α in the joint model; performance with only the knowledge-guided stream vs. only chain-based.
  • Use of relation labels in dependencies/AMR (the paper avoids them for generality, but reporting their effect would be informative).
  • Comparison to using the whole tree/graph embedding without attention.

Questions to Authors
- Could you include comparisons to BiLSTM-CRF and a BiLSTM baseline, and optionally a CRF decoder on top of your tagger? How do results change?
- How sensitive is K-SAN to parser/AMR errors quantitatively? For instance, can you perturb structures or use out-of-domain parsers to report robustness curves?
- Why are Mkg and Min weights tied? Did you try untied encoders, or sharing only some layers? Please provide ablations.
- How does performance vary with α in the joint model? What happens if you remove the chain-based or the knowledge-guided stream?
- Can you report runtime/memory and throughput vs. sentence length and number of substructures, to substantiate the efficiency claim?
- Did you test incorporating dependency/AMR relation labels? If not, can you comment on feasibility and expected impact?
- Are word embeddings fixed or fine-tuned? Any gains from pretraining on larger corpora?

Overall Assessment
The paper presents a neat and interpretable way to inject external structure into slot filling via attention over knowledge-guided substructures, with consistent gains on ATIS and robustness across knowledge sources. However, the empirical scope is narrow and omits strong baselines; efficiency claims are unsubstantiated; and several important ablations are missing. I lean toward rejection in its current form, but the work is promising. Strengthening the experimental section (additional datasets, stronger baselines, ablations and efficiency analyses, and robustness to parsing noise) would significantly improve the paper and could make a compelling case for acceptance.
