Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling in goal-oriented spoken language understanding. The approach extracts substructures from external knowledge sources (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs), encodes each substructure and the utterance into vectors, computes attention over substructures via a softmax on inner products with the utterance vector, aggregates them into a knowledge-guided representation, and feeds this into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain-based RNN representation with the knowledge-guided one. Experiments on ATIS show consistent F1 gains over neural and feature-based baselines across small, medium, and full training sets. The method appears robust to different knowledge sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers) and to parser errors.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A neural architecture that integrates external structured knowledge into sequence tagging via attention over knowledge-derived substructures, trained end-to-end. This is a concrete and technically sound adaptation of memory/attention mechanisms to slot filling.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach improves performance and data efficiency on ATIS, with consistent gains over non-structural and structural baselines, and robustness across different knowledge sources (dependency vs. AMR; different parsers).
3) A joint tagging architecture that mitigates noisy knowledge by balancing chain-based and knowledge-guided representations.

Strengths
- Clear, modular architecture with end-to-end training: The components (knowledge encoder, attention over substructures, combination with RNN tagger) are well specified mathematically (Equations 1–4, 8–11), and the model is a natural, technically sound extension of attention/memory models to sequence tagging with external knowledge.
- Consistent gains across data regimes, especially low-resource: On ATIS-small and -medium, K-SAN (CNN encoder) improves F1 over strong neural baselines (e.g., CNN Encoder-Tagger) by ~1–2.5 points, with significance reported (p < 0.05). This supports the claim that leveraging external structure aids generalization under data scarcity.
- Robustness to knowledge source and parser choice: Comparable gains are obtained using different dependency parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and AMR parsers (rule-based, JAMR), despite large differences in the number of substructures. This suggests the method is not tied to a particular parsing technology and can handle noisy structures.
- Reasonable analysis: The attention visualization qualitatively aligns with salient semantic regions (origins, destinations, times) and appears relatively stable across training sizes, supporting the claim that the model focuses on task-relevant substructures even with limited data.
- Joint model design for noise tolerance: Combining chain-based and knowledge-guided hidden states is a sensible way to hedge against unreliable external structures and appears to contribute to robustness.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: Results are only on ATIS. Broader validation on additional SLU datasets or domains (e.g., multi-domain SLU benchmarks) is necessary to substantiate generality claims. The current empirical scope limits the strength of the conclusions.
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations: The paper omits comparisons to commonly used strong sequence tagging baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF decoding, character-aware models). Ablations are limited: there is no systematic analysis of the contribution of attention vs. uniform averaging, the effect of the joint model vs. knowledge-only, or the impact of using dependency labels. This makes it hard to isolate what drives the gains.
- Efficiency/parallelizability claim unsubstantiated: The paper claims potential efficiency and parallelizability because substructures are modeled separately, but provides no complexity analysis, runtime measurements, or resource usage to support this. In practice, encoding multiple substructures and computing attention adds overhead.
- Some implementation details are under-specified: The exact linearization of substructures (e.g., the ordering in root-to-leaf sequences) is unclear; injection of o into GRU gating is only described at a high level; the number of training runs and variance for significance testing are not specified; and the choice and tuning of α (set to 0.5) are not justified empirically.
- Modest absolute gains on the full-data setting: While statistically significant, improvements over the best baseline on ATIS-large are ~1 point F1. Without broader benchmarks and stronger baselines, it is difficult to assess practical impact at scale.

Questions to Authors
- How many random seeds/runs were used for each reported number and for the t-tests? Please report means and standard deviations.
- How exactly are substructures linearized for encoding (e.g., token order along root-to-leaf paths)? Did you try including dependency/AMR edge labels, and what was the effect?
- Can you provide ablations isolating: (a) joint model vs. knowledge-only vs. chain-only; (b) attention vs. uniform averaging over substructures; (c) different α values?
- How is o incorporated into GRU gating in practice? Please provide the precise equations.
- What is the computational overhead (training and inference time, memory) relative to the best non-knowledge baseline? How does it scale with the number of substructures?
- Did you evaluate with a CRF output layer or BiLSTM encoders? If so, how does K-SAN interact with these stronger tagging architectures?
- For AMR, how do you handle graph properties such as reentrancies and non-tree structures when constructing substructures? Is there any loss of information compared to dependencies?

Overall, the paper is clear and technically sound, with a reasonable adaptation of attention over external structural knowledge to slot tagging and consistent gains on ATIS. The main limitations are the narrow empirical scope, missing comparisons/ablations, and lack of evidence for the efficiency claim.
