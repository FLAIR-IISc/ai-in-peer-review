Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an external structure (e.g., dependency tree or AMR), the model extracts a set of sentence-specific substructures (root-to-leaf paths), embeds them, computes an attention distribution over these substructures conditioned on a sentence embedding, and forms a knowledge-guided representation that is injected into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN.
- Empirically, K-SAN achieves higher F1 than strong baselines on ATIS under low-, medium-, and full-resource settings, with larger gains in low-resource settings. The attention visualizations suggest the model assigns higher weights to semantically salient parts.
- The framework is shown to generalize across different knowledge resources (Stanford and SyntaxNet dependency trees; two AMR parsers) with comparable gains, suggesting robustness to the source of structure.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a sentence-specific memory/attention over linguistically grounded substructures. Unlike prior “feature augmentation” approaches that append parser-derived features to a tagger, K-SAN integrates structured signals with a differentiable attention mechanism trained end-to-end. This architectural choice is well-motivated and technically sound.
- Consistent empirical improvements, especially in low-data regimes. On ATIS-small and ATIS-medium, the K-SAN encoders (particularly CNN) substantially outperform non-knowledge baselines and are competitive or superior to structure-aware encoders (DCNN, Tree-RNN). The robustness to parser errors shown in the visualization is encouraging.
- Knowledge-source generalization. The same mechanism applies to syntactic (dependencies) and semantic (AMR) structures produced by different parsers, with similar performance despite differing numbers of substructures. This supports the claim that the method is schema-agnostic and not tuned to a specific parser.
- Conceptual simplicity and modularity. The method is easy to implement with standard components (CNN/RNN encoders, dot-product attention, sum pooling), and the joint tagger combines complementary information from chain-based and knowledge-guided paths. This increases potential for adoption.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing attention/memory models and structure-aware encoders. The core mechanism is a single-hop memory over sentence-internal units; analogous ideas have been explored in memory networks and attention for classification. The main novelty here is using parser-derived substructures as the memory for sequence tagging, which is incremental. A more thorough positioning against prior memory/attention models (e.g., multi-hop, self-attention over spans) is needed.
- Incomplete baselines and ablations. Strong sequence tagging baselines from the same era (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF; LSTM/GRU with character features; joint intent-slot with CRF or attention) are missing. The reported gains over CNN Encoder-Tagger and RNN could shrink against a BiLSTM-CRF. Key ablations are absent: no comparison to (i) uniform averaging over substructures (no attention), (ii) random or perturbed structures, (iii) using edge labels vs. not, (iv) including non-root-to-leaf paths or different substructure granularities, (v) removing the joint tagger to quantify its contribution.
- Clarity issues in the knowledge injection into the RNN. Equation (8) uses “Mo” additively: ht = φ(Mo + Wwt + Uht−1). It is unclear whether this is a matrix times o (M o) or a scalar parameter Mo; if the former, dimensions should be specified, and if the latter, it reduces to a sentence-level bias. Similarly, the mention of integrating o into GRU gating “similarly” lacks details. These are important to reproducibility.
- Claims of efficiency and parallelizability are not substantiated. The paper claims modeling time may not increase linearly because substructures are modeled separately, but there is no complexity analysis or runtime comparison. In practice, encoding multiple substructures and computing attention adds overhead; evidence is needed.
- Narrow evaluation scope. All experiments are on ATIS, which is small and domain-specific. The method’s generality would be better supported with additional datasets (e.g., SNIPS, multi-domain SLU, or a standard NER benchmark) and with noisy ASR hypotheses to stress robustness. The absolute improvements on the full ATIS set are modest (~1 F1), so external validation is important.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify Equation (8). Is the intended form ht = φ(Wwt + Uht−1 + M o), where M is a learned projection and o is the knowledge-guided vector? How exactly is o integrated into GRU gates (please provide equations)?
- What is the exact dev split and hyperparameter tuning protocol for ATIS? Is there a fixed validation split and are results averaged across multiple random seeds?
- How many substructures per sentence are used on average for each knowledge source, and what is the distribution? Is there any pruning or maximum cap during training?
- Have you evaluated a BiLSTM-CRF baseline and a GRU-CRF baseline? If not, can you comment on how K-SAN compares conceptually and in parameter count?
- Can you report ablations on (i) attention vs. uniform averaging, (ii) using dependency/AMR edge labels, and (iii) the effect of not tying Mkg and Min?
- Do you observe benefits from multi-hop attention over substructures, or is single-hop sufficient? If explored, please report.
- Can you provide training/inference time comparisons to the strongest baseline and a memory footprint comparison to substantiate the efficiency claim?

Additional comments
- The attention visualization is helpful; adding a quantitative analysis correlating parser quality (e.g., UAS/LAS or AMR Smatch) with performance would strengthen the robustness claim.
- The motivation frames knowledge as a “teacher,” but the memory holds sentence-internal structures produced by a parser, not external facts. A brief discussion of extending the memory to cross-sentence or KB-derived substructures would clarify the generality of the claim.
- Please standardize notation (e.g., distinguish Mout vs. Mo vs. M o) and specify dimensions for all matrices/vectors for reproducibility.

Overall recommendation: Borderline accept. The idea of parser-guided substructure attention for slot filling is sensible and empirically helpful, especially in low-data scenarios, and the generalization across knowledge sources is a plus. However, the novelty is moderate, clarity and ablations are lacking, and the evaluation is narrow. Strengthening baselines and ablations would considerably improve the paper.
