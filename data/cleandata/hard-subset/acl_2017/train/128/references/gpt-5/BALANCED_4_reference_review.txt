Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture that uses external linguistic knowledge (e.g., dependency parses or AMR graphs) to define sentence substructures. The model embeds these substructures, attends over them conditioned on the current utterance embedding, and produces a knowledge-guided sentence representation which augments an RNN slot tagger. The approach is trained end-to-end. Experiments on ATIS show consistent F1 gains over several baselines across small/medium/large training regimes and across different knowledge sources. The paper also provides attention visualizations.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, end-to-end attention mechanism over knowledge-derived substructures for sequence tagging, integrated into an RNN tagger with a joint architecture (chain-based and knowledge-guided).
2) Empirical evidence that external knowledge can help slot filling, especially in low-resource regimes, and that the method generalizes across syntactic (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and semantic (AMR) resources.
3) Qualitative analysis via attention visualizations indicating robustness to parser errors.

Strengths
- Clear, general mechanism for leveraging structure as attention: The design (encode substructures, attend with inner product, aggregate into a sentence vector, inject into RNN) is conceptually clean, end-to-end differentiable, and broadly applicable to multiple knowledge formats. This addresses common feature-engineering pitfalls and avoids error-prone pipelining.

- Consistent gains across data regimes and knowledge sources: On ATIS, K-SAN improves over strong baselines. For example, with all training data, K-SAN (RNN/CNN encoders) reaches 94.86–94.97 F1 vs. 93.88 for the CNN Encoder-Tagger; in medium data, 87.99 vs. 85.52; in small data, 74.60 vs. 73.57. Table 2 shows the method is robust across Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency parses and two AMR parsers, with comparable performance despite different substructure counts.

- Robustness to parsing noise: The visualization in Fig. 5 illustrates that the model can learn to focus on semantically salient parts (origin/destination/time) even when dependency analyses are imperfect. This supports the claim that attention over multiple substructures can mitigate parser errors.

- Practical generality: The approach does not require relation labels (though they could be used) and ties the substructure and sentence encoders, reducing engineering burden. The same machinery works with syntactic and semantic graphs, suggesting portability.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and modest absolute gains on a single small benchmark: Results are only on ATIS. While statistically significant, improvements over a strong non-structural baseline (93.88 to 94.86/94.97 F1) are relatively small on the full data. To substantiate broad impact, evaluation on additional NLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MIT Restaurant/Movie, MultiATIS, or other slot-filling tasks) and possibly other sequence labeling tasks (NER) would strengthen claims.

- Missing stronger modern baselines and ablations: There is no comparison to bidirectional LSTM/GRU with CRF decoding, character-level features, or self-attention/transformer-style encoders that can model long-distance dependencies without external parses. Ablations are also lacking: (i) uniform/random attention over substructures, (ii) self-attention over spans without external knowledge, (iii) using dependency/AMR edge labels, (iv) removing the joint architecture (α), (v) removing the Mout nonlinearity, and (vi) untied vs. tied encoders. Such studies are needed to isolate what K-SAN specifically contributes beyond richer sentence encoding.

- Claims about efficiency/parallelizability not empirically supported: The paper asserts that modeling substructures separately improves efficiency/parallelism, but provides no runtime, parameter count, memory, or throughput measurements, nor complexity analysis vs. baselines. This weakens the efficiency claim.

- Substructure design choices are under-justified: Using only root-to-leaf paths and ignoring dependency/AMR labels may omit valuable relational information and interactions (e.g., sibling/ancestor relations, labeled edges). The paper neither motivates this choice nor compares against alternatives (e.g., path sets including labeled edges; graph neural encoders over full structures).

- Limited analysis of knowledge quality and causality: Although Table 2 shows similar results across parsers, there is no controlled sanity check (e.g., random trees/graphs, shuffled edges) to demonstrate that the model truly exploits structure rather than surface word co-occurrence. Likewise, the impact of parse accuracy (gold vs. predicted) is not explored.

Questions to Authors
- How does K-SAN compare to a strong BiLSTM-CRF baseline (with and without character features)? Could you report ATIS numbers for those settings?
- What is the performance gap between K-SAN and a self-attention or transformer encoder that uses no external knowledge but can attend non-locally?
- Did you try incorporating dependency/AMR relation labels in substructure encodings? If so, how did they affect performance?
- Can you provide ablations for: uniform/random attention over substructures; removing the joint blending (α); untied Mkg/Min; and replacing external knowledge with randomly generated trees?
- Can you quantify efficiency (train/inference time, parameters) and scalability as the number/size of substructures grows?
- How sensitive is performance to errors in parsing (e.g., across parser confidence strata), and to the maximum number/length of substructures?

Additional Comments
- The approach is promising and well-presented. Extending evaluation to additional datasets and adding thorough ablations and stronger baselines would substantially strengthen the paper.
- Consider experimenting with graph neural networks over full dependency/AMR graphs, or path encoders that include edge labels, to test whether richer structure yields further gains.
- Reporting few-shot and cross-domain results would better support the “knowledge as teacher” narrative.
