Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses or AMR graphs) is decomposed into substructures (root-to-leaf paths). The model encodes each substructure and computes an attention distribution over them conditioned on an utterance embedding, aggregates a knowledge-guided sentence vector, and injects it into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS show gains, particularly in low-resource settings, and the approach appears robust to different knowledge sources.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple attention-over-substructures mechanism that integrates external syntactic/semantic structures into a sequence tagger in an end-to-end training pipeline (excluding the parser).
2) Empirical study showing consistent improvements on ATIS across multiple encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) and across diverse knowledge sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers).
3) Qualitative analysis via attention visualizations suggesting the model can focus on salient substructures even with parser errors and limited data.

Strengths
1) Conceptually clean and potentially general mechanism: Framing external knowledge as a set of substructure memories and using attention over them is an intuitive, modular design. It does not depend on a specific schema (labels are even omitted) and is adaptable to dependency or AMR inputs, which supports the generalization claim.

2) Consistent empirical gains and low-resource benefits: On ATIS, K-SAN improves over non-knowledge baselines; the CNN encoder variant yields sizable gains in the Small/Medium splits. This is a realistic and important regime for many NLU applications.

3) Knowledge source robustness: Comparing four knowledge sources (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet dependencies; rule-based vs. JAMR AMR) with similar performance bolsters the claim that the approach is not brittle to the choice of parser or to syntactic vs. semantic structures.

4) Useful diagnostics: The attention visualizations are informative and show the model can highlight origin/destination/time substructures, even when parses are imperfect, giving some interpretability.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior attention/memory models and structured sentence encoders: The design—encode facts/substructures, compute attention with an utterance query, do a weighted sum, and inject into a tagger—mirrors memory networks and “structured attention” ideas. Prior work already integrates syntax into neural encoders (e.g., Tree-RNN/DCNN). The paper lacks a clear technical leap (e.g., no multi-hop reasoning, no per-token conditioning, no use of relation labels) and reads as an incremental adaptation of existing mechanisms to slot filling.

2) Incomplete and outdated experimental baselines and scope: ATIS alone is insufficient in 2024 to substantiate broad claims. Missing strong, standard baselines:
- BiLSTM/GRU-CRF taggers with character/subword features.
- Self-attention over words without external knowledge to isolate the value of knowledge vs. attention itself.
- Token-conditioned knowledge attention (per-token queries) vs. the single sentence-level vector.
Reported SOTA (≈95 F1) is not competitive with modern slot-filling results on ATIS (often ≥95–96 with BiLSTM-CRF/transformers). The gains over a competitive neural baseline are modest on the Large split.

3) Methodological and notation ambiguities:
- Eq. (8): ht = φ(Mo + Wwt + Uht−1). Dimensions are unclear; Mo appears as a matrix times o but the multiplication is omitted. Is o added as a bias, or via a learned projection M o?
- The knowledge-guided vector o appears fixed per sentence and injected identically at every time step. This may be suboptimal for slot tagging; no ablation of per-token conditioning is provided.
- The construction and ordering of substructures (root-to-leaf paths) are insufficiently specified. Examples show unnatural token orders (“show flights to francisco san”), and the omission of relation labels discards key information. Why do non-leaf nodes have no substructures? What is the impact?
- Tying Min and Mkg weights is asserted but not justified; an ablation without tying is needed.

4) Claims not supported by evidence and statistical rigor:
- Efficiency/parallelizability claims are not measured (no runtime, memory, or scaling analysis). In practice, encoding all paths can be costly and duplicative.
- “End-to-end” is overstated since the parser/AMR components are external and non-differentiable; the model is end-to-end only downstream of the parser.
- Statistical tests: t-test significance is reported but without number of runs, seeds, or variance; no confidence intervals. The sampling procedure for Small/Medium splits is under-specified (random seeds, stratification), and the dev set definition is unclear.

Additional issues (minor but numerous):
- Reference formatting is inconsistent and often incorrect (missing years, punctuation, author names; e.g., “Celikyilmaz and Hakkani.Tur.”).
- Figures/tables have formatting artifacts; equations and variable naming are sometimes inconsistent (e.g., mixing bold/tilde notation, subscript spacing).
- The choice to ignore dependency/AMR edge labels for “better generalization” is plausible but should be validated with a comparison including labels.

Questions to Authors
1) Clarify Eq. (8): how exactly is o integrated (additive bias, linear projection M o, concatenation)? Provide dimensions and an ablation of each choice.

2) Why is o sentence-level and shared across all time steps? Did you try per-token knowledge attention with token-specific queries u_t? Any effect on performance?

3) How are root-to-leaf paths linearized (ordering of tokens), and why are non-leaf nodes excluded? What is the average number/length of substructures, and how much token overlap exists across paths?

4) Please add stronger baselines: BiLSTM/GRU-CRF (with/without char features) and a self-attention/tagger without external knowledge. How do K-SAN gains compare?

5) Provide training variance across multiple seeds, details for Small/Medium splits, dev set selection, and runtime/memory comparisons.

Recommendation
While the idea is appealing and results are positive on ATIS, the novelty is limited, experimental baselines are insufficient, and technical clarity/issues (equation consistency, substructure construction) and unsupported claims weaken the submission. I lean toward rejection unless the above concerns are addressed with stronger baselines, clearer methodology, and broader evaluation.
