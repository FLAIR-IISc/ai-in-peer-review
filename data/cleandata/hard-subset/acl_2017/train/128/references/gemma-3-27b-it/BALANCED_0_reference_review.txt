This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue state tracking, aiming to improve natural language understanding by incorporating external knowledge into the learning process. The core idea is to use knowledge (dependency parses in the presented experiments) to guide the attention mechanism of a recurrent neural network, enabling it to focus on important substructures within the input utterance. The primary contribution is an end-to-end neural network approach that leverages knowledge to guide attention and generate sentence representations for sequence tagging. A secondary contribution lies in the method’s generality, accommodating various knowledge types like dependency relations, knowledge graph relations, and handcrafted grammar parsing output. Finally, the authors claim efficiency and parallelizability due to the separate modeling of substructures.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core NLU Challenge:** The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in NLU – incorporating prior knowledge effectively. Traditional approaches often treat knowledge as additional features, potentially leading to generalization and error propagation issues. K-SAN's attempt to directly integrate knowledge into the learning process with an attention mechanism is a valuable direction.
2. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments on the ATIS dataset are well-designed, utilizing different training data sizes to demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method.  The comparisons against strong baselines, including both traditional and recent deep learning approaches, provide convincing evidence of K-SAN’s effectiveness. The consistent improvements across different data sizes are particularly encouraging.
3. **Demonstrated Generalization to Knowledge Sources:** The experiments showcasing the model’s performance with different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency parsers, rule-based/JAMR AMR parsers) convincingly demonstrate the flexibility and generalizability of the K-SAN framework.  Maintaining performance across varied knowledge representations is a significant strength.
4. **Insightful Attention Analysis:** The visualization of attention weights in Figure 5 provides a qualitative understanding of how the model leverages the knowledge-guided structures.  Seeing that the model focuses on relevant parts of the sentence and corresponding knowledge substructures enhances trust in the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of knowledge guidance and attention is a reasonable approach, neither knowledge guidance in neural networks nor attention mechanisms themselves are novel. The contribution feels more incremental than breakthrough. The paper would benefit from more thoroughly positioning itself within the existing literature and clearly articulating what makes K-SAN uniquely valuable beyond its combination of existing techniques.
2. **Dependency on Parsing Accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently tied to the quality of the external knowledge source (e.g., the dependency parser or AMR parser).  While the paper demonstrates robustness across different parsers, inaccuracies in parsing will inevitably introduce noise and potentially degrade performance. This dependency is acknowledged but could be explored further (e.g., investigating methods to make the model more robust to parsing errors).
3. **Lack of Ablation Study on Knowledge Types:** The paper shows performance with different knowledge *sources* but doesn’t provide a comprehensive ablation study to evaluate the relative contribution of different *types* of knowledge (e.g., dependency vs. AMR).  It would be valuable to understand which knowledge representations are most beneficial and whether combining them yields further improvements.
4. **Somewhat Superficial Explanation of Attention Mechanism:** While the attention mechanism is central to the approach, the explanation of how it operates and why it’s effective remains somewhat superficial. A more detailed discussion of the attention weights and their interpretation would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to errors in the dependency parsing or AMR parsing? Have you experimented with noisy knowledge sources (e.g., by introducing random errors into the parsing trees) to quantify this sensitivity?
2. Can you elaborate on the trade-offs between using different knowledge encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN)? Why does CNN appear to perform best on smaller datasets, while RNN excels on larger datasets?
3. Have you explored techniques to incorporate uncertainty estimates from the knowledge source (e.g., parser confidence scores) into the attention mechanism?
4.  The paper mentions error propagation as a limitation of feature-based approaches. How does K-SAN mitigate error propagation *specifically*? Is there a mechanism that allows the model to downweight or ignore incorrect knowledge signals?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are generally well-designed and informative.
*   The writing is clear and concise.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of K-SAN compared to baseline methods. While the paper claims efficiency, a quantitative analysis would be helpful.
*   The authors should consider providing error analysis to identify specific types of errors that K-SAN still struggles with.



Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of spoken dialogue understanding. While the core ideas are not entirely novel, the effective combination of knowledge guidance and attention mechanisms, coupled with a thorough empirical evaluation, makes this work valuable. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed with further experimentation and analysis. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the reviewer’s questions and incorporating the suggested improvements.
