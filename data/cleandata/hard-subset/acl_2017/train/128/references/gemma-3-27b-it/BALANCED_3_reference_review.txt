This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue systems’ natural language understanding (NLU) task. The core idea is to leverage external knowledge, such as dependency parses or AMR graphs, to guide attention mechanisms within an RNN framework, enhancing semantic tagging (intent determination and slot filling). The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization across different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability due to substructure modeling. 

I believe the most significant contribution is demonstrating the *potential* of integrating structural knowledge directly into the attention mechanism of an RNN for sequence tagging. While previous work often used knowledge as additional features, this approach attempts to learn *where* to focus attention based on the knowledge structure, which is a promising direction. The empirical results, especially the gains on the smaller datasets, suggest that K-SAN can effectively leverage knowledge when data is limited. A second contribution is the systematic exploration of different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) and encoding methods within the K-SAN framework. This demonstrates the flexibility and robustness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Attention Guidance:** The primary strength lies in the novel idea of using knowledge to directly *guide* the attention mechanism within a neural network, as opposed to simply incorporating knowledge as additional features. This is a potentially powerful approach to inject structural information into the model.
2. **Robustness and Generalization:** The experimental results demonstrate that K-SAN consistently outperforms baseline models across different dataset sizes. The model also shows good generalization ability when tested with different types of knowledge (dependency parses vs. AMR graphs) and encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN). This robustness is a significant advantage.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a clear experimental setup and a comprehensive comparison against several baselines, including strong structural models like DCNN and Tree-RNN. The results on the ATIS dataset are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements, particularly in low-data scenarios.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation behind the proposed approach. The authors effectively highlight the limitations of existing methods and explain how K-SAN addresses these limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Gain Over Strong Baselines on Large Dataset:** While K-SAN consistently outperforms baselines, the performance improvement on the *large* dataset is relatively small. This suggests that when sufficient training data is available, the benefits of knowledge guidance become less pronounced. It would be valuable to investigate *why* this is the case. Perhaps the model struggles to disambiguate noisy or inaccurate knowledge structures when a large amount of data is present.
2. **Dependency on Parser Accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently dependent on the accuracy of the external parser used to generate the knowledge structures. The authors acknowledge this but do not fully address the potential impact of parser errors. The visualization in Figure 5 shows an example of an incorrectly parsed dependency tree, and it is unclear how the model handles such cases. This is a crucial aspect to investigate, as real-world dialogue systems will encounter noisy and ambiguous utterances.
3. **Substructure Definition and Complexity:** The definition of “substructures” based on dependency tree paths or AMR graph paths feels somewhat arbitrary. There’s a lack of justification for why these specific substructures are chosen and how they relate to the underlying semantic meaning. Further exploration of different substructure definitions or hierarchical representations could potentially improve performance.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** It would be helpful to include an ablation study to assess the contribution of each component of the K-SAN architecture. For example, what is the performance gain achieved by incorporating the attention mechanism versus simply concatenating the knowledge embeddings with the input embeddings?  How much does each type of knowledge encoder (NN, RNN, CNN) contribute?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to errors in the external parser? Have you conducted any experiments to quantify the impact of parser accuracy on the overall system performance?
2.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind the specific substructure definition used in your experiments?  Have you explored alternative substructure definitions, such as using shorter paths or focusing on specific types of dependency relations?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the attention weights learned by the model? Are there any patterns or insights that can be gained from examining which substructures the model is focusing on for different types of utterances?
4.  Regarding the relatively small gains on the large dataset, do you have any hypotheses about why the benefits of knowledge guidance diminish as the amount of training data increases?  Could techniques like knowledge selection or filtering be used to mitigate this issue?

**Additional Comments:**

* The provided code repository would be helpful for the community to reproduce and extend your work.
* The discussion of related work is thorough but could be slightly more focused on the intersection of deep learning and knowledge representation for NLU.
* The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the weight α in the joint RNN tagger) seems somewhat arbitrary.  A more systematic hyperparameter tuning procedure might yield further improvements.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to integrating knowledge into neural networks for NLU. While some weaknesses remain, the authors have presented a compelling case for the benefits of knowledge-guided structural attention. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses and answer the questions raised above.
