The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue understanding, aiming to integrate external knowledge into a neural network architecture to improve slot filling performance. The core idea is to use dependency parsing (or other structural knowledge sources) to identify substructures within a sentence and learn attention weights over these substructures to enhance the sentence representation. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization across different knowledge types, and efficiency due to parallel substructure modeling.

While the idea of incorporating knowledge into attention mechanisms is reasonable, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from superficial novelty to inconsistent experimental setup and presentation. The primary contribution, end-to-end learning, feels incremental; attention mechanisms have been used with knowledge before, and the specific application to slot filling doesn't represent a paradigm shift. The claimed generalization across knowledge types is also weakly demonstrated—experiments largely focus on dependency parsing, and the benefit of AMR is not clearly established. The alleged efficiency gains are not substantiated with runtime comparisons.

**Strengths:**

1. **Motivation:** The problem of leveraging external knowledge for NLU is well-motivated, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The authors correctly identify the limitations of simply adding knowledge features to neural networks.
2. **Clear Architecture:** The overall architecture of K-SAN is reasonably well-explained and illustrated in Figure 2. The flow of information from knowledge encoding to attention weighting to final slot tagging is understandable.
3. **Comprehensive Baseline:** The inclusion of several baselines, including both traditional methods (CRF) and more recent deep learning approaches (CNN, Tree-RNN), allows for a relatively thorough comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core concept of attention weighted knowledge integration is not novel. Previous work (cited by the authors) has explored similar ideas, albeit with potentially different implementations. The K-SAN architecture appears to be a relatively straightforward extension of existing methods. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant architectural innovation.
2. **Inconsistent Experimental Setup & Reporting:** The experimental details are often vague and inconsistent. For example, the different knowledge encoding models (NN, RNN, CNN) are tested without a clear justification for their selection. The impact of the various hyperparameters (hidden layer sizes, dropout rates) is not thoroughly analyzed. The use of different parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) for dependency trees is mentioned but not systematically compared. Table 2 is particularly concerning – results with SyntaxNet and JAMR are shown but lack detail on parser configuration and performance.
3. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks proper ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of K-SAN. For instance, what is the performance gain specifically from the attention mechanism *over* simply concatenating the knowledge embeddings?  Without this, it’s impossible to know if the attention mechanism is genuinely beneficial or just adding complexity.
4. **Overstated Claims:** The claim of "significant improvement" in Table 1 is misleading. While K-SAN achieves better F1 scores than some baselines, the gains are often marginal, particularly on the larger datasets. The statistical significance test (t-test) is mentioned but the p-values are not consistently reported, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the results.
5. **Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor errors and inconsistencies in formatting and notation. The figures are occasionally unclear. The writing quality is generally adequate but could be improved. For instance, the explanation of Figure 3 is repetitive.
6. **Dependence on Parser Quality:** The model’s performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the dependency parser.  The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately address the impact of parsing errors on the results. The example in Figure 5 highlights a parsing error and implicitly acknowledges the issue.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of K-SAN with existing approaches that also utilize attention mechanisms and external knowledge? What specifically differentiates your approach and justifies its novelty?
2.  Please provide a comprehensive analysis of the impact of the knowledge encoding models (NN, RNN, CNN) on performance. Why did you choose to test all three, and what insights did you gain from comparing them?
3.  Can you provide ablation study results demonstrating the contribution of the attention mechanism specifically? What is the performance of the model *without* the attention weights?
4.  Please clarify the experimental setup for the SyntaxNet and JAMR parsers used in Table 2. What versions were used, what were the configurations, and how did you evaluate their accuracy on the ATIS dataset?
5.  Provide the p-values for all statistical significance tests reported in Table 1.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors should consider conducting experiments on a larger and more diverse dataset to demonstrate the generalizability of their approach.
*   Investigate the robustness of K-SAN to noisy or incomplete knowledge sources.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. Despite addressing a relevant research problem, the paper's lack of substantial novelty, inconsistent experimental details, and overstated claims prevent it from meeting the standards for acceptance at ACL. The paper requires significant revisions, including a more thorough evaluation, a clearer justification of its contributions, and improved presentation, before it can be considered for publication.
