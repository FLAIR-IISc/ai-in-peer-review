This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue state tracking, aiming to integrate external knowledge into sequence tagging models. The core idea is to leverage dependency parse trees (or other structured knowledge sources) to guide attention mechanisms within a recurrent neural network framework. The authors claim three primary contributions: end-to-end learning of knowledge-guided attention, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

The most significant contribution of this work is the *idea* of explicitly guiding attention mechanisms with external knowledge structures, rather than simply using knowledge as additional features. This is a genuinely novel approach to integrating symbolic knowledge into neural networks for sequence labeling tasks. While attention mechanisms are common, using a predefined structure to *shape* that attention is a conceptually interesting departure. The demonstrated ability to utilize diverse knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) showcases a degree of flexibility.  The empirical results, while not overwhelmingly superior, demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines, suggesting the approach has merit.

Further strengthening the paper is the thorough experimentation. The authors conduct a reasonably comprehensive set of experiments across different data sizes and knowledge sources. The ablation studies, while not explicitly labeled as such, are implied through the comparisons between different encoder types (NN, RNN, CNN). This provides some insight into the effectiveness of the different components.

However, the paper falls short of being truly impactful due to a lack of in-depth analysis and methodological concerns. The improvements over baselines, while statistically significant in some cases, are not dramatic. This raises questions about the practical benefit of the added complexity of K-SAN. The experiments are largely confined to the ATIS dataset, limiting the generalizability of the findings. While the authors *mention* generalization, they don’t push the boundaries by testing on significantly different domains.

A significant weakness is the relatively superficial exploration of the knowledge integration. The authors primarily use dependency parsing, a well-established technique.  While they explore AMR, the results don’t indicate a clear advantage over dependency trees, and the discussion of AMR’s potential is brief. A deeper dive into the *quality* of the knowledge and its impact on performance would be valuable. For example, analyzing cases where the parser makes errors and observing the model's robustness to these errors.  The visualizations, while present, are somewhat uninformative without a more detailed qualitative analysis.

Finally, the paper lacks a clear discussion of computational cost. The authors claim efficiency due to parallelizable substructure modeling, but this is not quantified.  Comparing the training and inference time of K-SAN to the baselines would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors the parser makes and how K-SAN handles these errors? Are there specific substructure patterns that consistently lead to errors?
2.  What is the computational overhead of K-SAN compared to the baselines (training time, inference time)?
3.  Beyond ATIS, have you experimented with K-SAN on other dialogue datasets with different characteristics (e.g., more complex dialogues, different domains)? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the challenges of applying K-SAN to these datasets?
4.  The paper mentions potential for error propagation. Can you provide examples of how errors in the knowledge source (e.g., incorrect dependency parses) impact the final slot filling accuracy?
