This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for goal-oriented spoken dialogue systems’ Natural Language Understanding (NLU) module. The core idea is to integrate external knowledge (dependency relations in this work) into a recurrent neural network framework via an attention mechanism, guiding the network to focus on important substructures within the input utterance. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution is the novel architecture, K-SAN, which effectively incorporates structural knowledge into an RNN-based sequence tagging model. This addresses a recognized limitation of previous work that treated knowledge as simply additional features. The ability to generalize to different knowledge sources (dependency parses, knowledge graphs, etc.) is a secondary but valuable contribution, broadening the applicability of the method. The claim of efficiency/parallelizability appears less substantial given the inherent sequential nature of RNNs, but the modular structure could potentially offer some optimization opportunities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Key Limitation:** The paper directly addresses the limitations of feature-enrichment approaches to knowledge integration in NLU, specifically poor generalization and error propagation. By using knowledge to *guide* attention within the network, K-SAN aims to mitigate these issues. The idea is sound and logically motivated.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the ATIS dataset demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art structural models. The gains are particularly significant in low-data settings (Small dataset), indicating the benefit of knowledge guidance when training data is limited.
3. **Generalizability Demonstrated:** The experiments with different knowledge sources (Stanford and SyntaxNet dependency parsers, rule-based and JAMR AMR parsers) clearly demonstrate the flexibility of the K-SAN architecture and its ability to leverage diverse forms of linguistic and semantic knowledge. This supports the claim of generalization.
4. **Comprehensive Ablation Study:** The various experiments performed – different encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN) and the comparison of different parsers – provide a good understanding of the components contributing to the overall performance.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup. The visualizations in Figure 2 and 5 are helpful in understanding the attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination of knowledge guidance and attention is innovative, the individual components (RNNs, attention mechanisms, dependency parsing) are well-established in the field. The primary novelty lies in the *integration* of these components, but a more thorough discussion of related work that combines attention and external knowledge in other NLP tasks would be beneficial.
2. **Dependence on Parser Quality:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently tied to the accuracy of the external knowledge source (in this case, the dependency parser). While the experiments show robustness across different parsers, the potential for errors in the parser to propagate to the NLU model is not fully addressed. The authors acknowledge this but could discuss potential mitigation strategies (e.g., using noisy knowledge, incorporating confidence scores).
3. **Efficiency Claim Weakly Justified:** The claim of efficiency/parallelizability is not convincingly supported. The core RNN component is inherently sequential, and while the substructure modeling might offer some parallelization opportunities, the overall speedup is not quantified.
4. **Lack of Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the visualization of attention weights in Figure 5 is helpful, a more in-depth analysis of *what* the model is attending to and *why* would strengthen the paper. For example, are there specific dependency relations that consistently receive higher attention? Is the attention aligned with linguistic intuition?
5. **Limited Scope of Knowledge Types:** The experiments primarily focus on syntactic knowledge (dependency relations) and semantic knowledge (AMR graphs). Exploring other knowledge sources (e.g., ontologies, common-sense knowledge) could further demonstrate the generality of the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to the quality of the dependency parser (e.g., parser accuracy, coverage)? Have you investigated methods for handling noisy or incomplete knowledge?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the attention weights learned by the model? What specific linguistic patterns or substructures consistently receive higher attention?
3. The paper focuses on end-to-end learning. Have you experimented with pre-training the knowledge encoding module separately before fine-tuning the entire model?
4.  Regarding the claim of efficiency, can you provide a more quantitative comparison of the training and inference time of K-SAN compared to the baselines?



**Additional Comments:**
