This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue systems’ natural language understanding (NLU) component, specifically slot filling. The core idea is to integrate external knowledge – in this case, dependency parse trees – into a recurrent neural network via an attention mechanism, guiding the network to focus on salient substructures of the input utterance. The authors claim three primary contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization across different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Integration of Structural Knowledge:** The most significant contribution is a viable approach to incorporate structural information from dependency parses (and potentially other knowledge sources) into a neural network for slot filling. The attention mechanism allows the model to dynamically weight the importance of different substructures, a nuanced approach compared to simply using parse features.
2. **Demonstrated Generalization to Different Knowledge Sources:** The experimental results showing comparable performance across dependency trees obtained from different parsers (Stanford and SyntaxNet) and different knowledge types (dependency trees and AMR graphs) demonstrates the model's flexibility and adaptability.
3. **Empirical Validation on a Standard Dataset:** The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments on the ATIS dataset, showing improvements over strong baselines, particularly in low-data regimes.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional NLU approaches and the potential benefits of integrating knowledge. The problem of leveraging structural information in a learnable way is well-defined.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed K-SAN architecture is well-reasoned and logically presented. The use of attention to dynamically weight substructures is a sensible approach. The comparison of different knowledge encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN) is valuable.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are thorough, covering different data sizes and baselines. The ablation study with different knowledge sources strengthens the claims. The attention visualization provides qualitative insights into the model's behavior.
4. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently positive results, especially in the small data settings, support the claim that K-SAN effectively leverages knowledge to improve performance and generalization. The improvements over existing methods are statistically significant.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide ample details about the experimental setup and dataset, increasing the potential for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of structural attention and knowledge integration is effective, the individual components (RNNs, attention mechanisms, dependency parsing) are well-established in the literature. The novelty lies in the specific application and integration, but a stronger claim of novelty is made in the paper.
2. **Dependency on Parsing Accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently dependent on the quality of the external knowledge (dependency parses in this case). While the results demonstrate robustness across different parsers, errors in parsing can still propagate and negatively impact performance. This is acknowledged, but could be further investigated with noisy parsing data.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't fully explore the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters. While the chosen hyperparameters yielded good results, it's unclear how robust the performance is to changes in these parameters. The impact of the `α` parameter in the joint RNN tagger isn’t thoroughly analyzed.
4. **Presentation of Results:** Table 2 feels a bit redundant and doesn't provide significant new insights beyond the main results in Table 1. Streamlining the presentation of experimental results would improve clarity.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis would provide valuable insights into the types of errors K-SAN still makes and potential areas for improvement. What kinds of slots are still difficult to fill, and why?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to errors in the dependency parsing? Have you experimented with noisy or imperfect parse trees to evaluate the robustness of K-SAN?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the `α` parameter in the joint RNN tagger? What is the optimal range of values, and how does it affect performance?
3. Could you provide some examples of failure cases where K-SAN makes incorrect predictions, and explain why the model might have failed in those cases?
4. The paper focuses on dependency parses. Are there plans to explore other types of knowledge sources, such as semantic role labeling or knowledge graphs (beyond AMR)?

**Additional Comments:**

* The claim of "efficiency and parallelizability" feels somewhat weak. While modeling substructures separately might offer some potential for parallelization, this isn't explicitly demonstrated or quantified in the paper.
* Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of K-SAN compared to the baselines.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a well-motivated and effective approach to integrating knowledge into neural networks for NLU. The experiments are thorough and the results are compelling. Despite some minor weaknesses, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance.
