## Review for "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel neural network architecture for spoken dialogue state tracking (specifically, NLU encompassing domain classification, intent determination, and slot filling). The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge (dependency parse trees in this work) to guide attention mechanisms within an RNN, effectively telling the network *where* to focus its attention during sequence tagging.  The authors claim contributions in end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge sources, and efficiency/parallelizability due to the structural decomposition.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Principled Integration of Structural Knowledge:** The paper presents a clear and well-motivated approach to inject structural knowledge into a sequence tagging model *via* attention. Unlike previous approaches that treat knowledge as additional features, K-SAN uses it to actively shape the network’s focus.
2. **Demonstrated Robustness:**  The experiments consistently show performance gains, particularly in low-data scenarios, indicating the method’s ability to generalize and leverage knowledge when training data is scarce.
3. **Empirical Validation Across Knowledge Sources:** Showing the method works with both dependency trees *and* AMR graphs demonstrates a degree of flexibility and confirms the core principle isn't tied to a specific parsing technique.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of using knowledge to *guide* attention is a strong and intuitive concept. The paper convincingly argues the limitations of previous feature-based approaches and clearly articulates the benefits of a more integrated approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experiments on the ATIS dataset demonstrate consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, especially in the low-data settings.  The improvement in the small data setting is particularly encouraging.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to several baselines, ablation studies (implicitly, through different encoding methods for K-SAN), and experiments with different knowledge sources.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the K-SAN architecture.  The diagrams (Figures 2 and 3) are helpful in understanding the key components.
5. **Demonstrated Generalization:** The successful application of the method to both dependency trees and AMR graphs illustrates the broad applicability of the proposed framework.


**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Components:**  While the *combination* of knowledge and attention is novel, the individual components (RNNs, attention mechanisms, dependency parsing) are well-established. The core innovation lies in *how* these components are integrated.  The paper could better highlight the nuance of this integration, perhaps contrasting K-SAN more explicitly with existing attention-based sequence taggers.
2. **Dependency on Parser Accuracy:** While the authors acknowledge this, the performance of K-SAN is inherently tied to the accuracy of the external parser. Errors in parsing will likely propagate through the network.  A deeper analysis of the impact of parsing errors would be valuable (e.g., a controlled experiment varying parser accuracy). The fact that the parser incorrectly parsed the example in Figure 5, yet the model still benefited, hints that K-SAN is somewhat robust, but more systematic investigation is needed.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention hyperparameter tuning, but the details are limited.  It would be helpful to understand which hyperparameters were most critical for performance and how sensitive the model is to their settings.  More detailed reporting on the hyperparameter search process would improve reproducibility.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Understanding the *types* of errors K-SAN still makes would provide valuable insights for future work.  Are there specific slot types or linguistic phenomena that remain challenging?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of parser accuracy on K-SAN’s performance?  Have you experimented with noisy parsing outputs?
2.  What were the key hyperparameters and their optimal values? How sensitive is the model to variations in these hyperparameters?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis? What types of errors does K-SAN still make, and are there any patterns or systematic biases?
4.  The paper mentions efficiency and parallelizability.  Could you provide some empirical evidence to support these claims (e.g., training time comparisons)?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The related work section is comprehensive.
*   The choice of the ATIS dataset is appropriate for evaluating NLU components for dialogue systems.
*   The reference to JAMR is a little ambiguous (appears to be a typo).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach to incorporating knowledge into neural sequence tagging.  The experimental results are compelling, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of K-SAN on a standard benchmark dataset. While the individual components are not entirely new, the integration of knowledge through attention is a significant contribution. I recommend **accepting** this paper, with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
