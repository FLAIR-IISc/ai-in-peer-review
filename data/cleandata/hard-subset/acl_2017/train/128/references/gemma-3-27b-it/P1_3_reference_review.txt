## Peer Review: Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel neural network architecture, Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), for improving Natural Language Understanding (NLU) in goal-oriented spoken dialogue systems. K-SAN aims to address the limitations of traditional approaches and previous deep learning methods by explicitly incorporating external knowledge (specifically, dependency parse trees in the presented experiments) to guide attention mechanisms. The model learns to focus on salient substructures within an utterance, enhancing semantic tag prediction for tasks like intent determination and slot filling. The authors claim three primary contributions: (1) end-to-end learning of attention guided by knowledge, (2) generalization to different knowledge sources beyond specific schemas, and (3) efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Integration of Structural Knowledge:** The paper presents a convincing framework for integrating readily available linguistic structures (dependency parses) into a neural network architecture for NLU. The attention mechanism guided by these structures demonstrably improves performance, particularly in low-data scenarios.
2. **Demonstrated Generalization Across Knowledge Sources:**  While the primary experiments use dependency parses, the authors convincingly argue for and demonstrate the adaptability of K-SAN to other knowledge representations like AMR graphs, showcasing the model's versatility.
3. **Empirical Validation on a Standard Benchmark:** The authors provide comprehensive experimental results on the ATIS dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of K-SAN compared to several strong baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** K-SAN consistently outperforms strong baselines on the ATIS dataset, particularly when training data is limited. The gains are statistically significant and demonstrate a clear benefit from incorporating knowledge-guided attention.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the shortcomings of existing NLU methods (reliance on large datasets, ignoring linguistic structure) and positions K-SAN as a solution to these problems. The motivation for explicitly guiding attention with external knowledge is sound.
3. **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand. The architecture of K-SAN is clearly explained with a helpful diagram (Figure 2), and the experimental setup is detailed.
4. **Demonstrated Flexibility:** The successful application of K-SAN to both dependency parses *and* AMR graphs is a significant strength, illustrating the model's potential for wider applicability. The results in Table 2 highlight this important aspect.
5. **Effective Attention Visualization:** Figure 5 offers useful insights into *how* K-SAN is leveraging the knowledge structures, visualizing the attention weights assigned to different substructures and providing qualitative evidence of the model's reasoning process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of structural knowledge and attention is effective, the individual components (RNNs, attention mechanisms, dependency parsing) are well-established in the field. The paper’s novelty lies primarily in the integration, rather than the invention, of these components.  The authors should more explicitly acknowledge this and focus on the unique contribution of their integrative approach.
2. **Dependency on Parser Quality:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently tied to the quality of the external parser. While the authors explore different parsers, they don’t extensively analyze the impact of parser errors on the final NLU performance. A more thorough error analysis would be beneficial. A parser error, as noted in the caption of Figure 5, doesn't significantly degrade performance, but a systematic study is missing.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments explore different hidden layer sizes and dropout rates, but a more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the results. It is unclear how robust K-SAN is to variations in these parameters.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study on Attention Mechanism:** While the attention mechanism is central to the proposed method, a more detailed ablation study to understand the contribution of each component of the attention mechanism (e.g., different scoring functions, knowledge encoding methods) is missing.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Parser Error Analysis:**  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of parser errors on K-SAN's performance? Specifically, what types of parsing errors are most detrimental, and how does K-SAN handle them?
2. **Hyperparameter Robustness:**  How sensitive is K-SAN's performance to changes in key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, embedding dimension)? Could you present a plot or table illustrating the performance variation across a range of hyperparameter values?
3. **Attention Mechanism Ablation:**  What is the relative contribution of different components within the attention mechanism (e.g., the knowledge encoder (NN, RNN, CNN), the scoring function)?  Can you perform an ablation study to quantify the impact of each component?
4. **Computational Complexity:** While the paper mentions efficiency, could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of K-SAN compared to standard RNN-based approaches, particularly for long utterances?

**Additional Comments:**

* The use of "teacher" in the title is slightly misleading. Knowledge is a guiding source, but the model still learns through gradient descent. A more descriptive title might be "Knowledge-Guided Attention Networks for NLU."
* The discussion of related work is comprehensive, but could benefit from explicitly contrasting K-SAN with recent approaches that also integrate knowledge graphs into neural networks (beyond just feature enrichment).
