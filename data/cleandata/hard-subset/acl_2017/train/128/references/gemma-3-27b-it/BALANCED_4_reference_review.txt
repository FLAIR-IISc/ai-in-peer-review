This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue state tracking, aiming to improve neural network performance by incorporating external knowledge in an end-to-end fashion. The core idea is to use knowledge (dependency parse trees in the presented experiments) to guide the attention mechanism within a recurrent neural network, allowing the model to focus on relevant substructures of the input utterance. The authors claim three primary contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability due to substructure modeling.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a relevant problem:** Integrating external knowledge into NLU models is a crucial step towards building robust and generalizable dialogue systems. The paper directly tackles the limitations of previous approaches that treat knowledge as additional features, which can suffer from poor generalization and error propagation.
2. **Novel approach to knowledge integration:** The K-SAN framework is a conceptually sound and potentially effective way to integrate knowledge. Guiding attention with structured knowledge, rather than simply concatenating features, allows the model to learn more nuanced relationships between the input utterance and the external knowledge source.
3. **Empirical validation:** The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments on the ATIS dataset, demonstrating that K-SAN outperforms several strong baselines, including both feature-based and structural models. The ablation studies exploring different knowledge encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN) are valuable and informative. The results suggest that the method is effective even with limited training data.
4. **Generalization capability demonstrated:** The experiments demonstrating generalization across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency parsers, rule-based/JAMR AMR parsers) strengthens the claim of broad applicability, which is a significant advantage of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the core idea:** While the specific implementation of K-SAN is novel, the idea of using attention mechanisms to focus on relevant parts of the input is not new. The key contribution is arguably *how* the knowledge is used to guide this attention, but this could be more clearly articulated and contrasted with existing attention-based methods in NLU. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss related work that leverages attention for similar purposes, potentially understating the existing landscape.
2. **Dependency on parsing accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently tied to the accuracy of the external knowledge source (e.g., the dependency parser). While the paper demonstrates robustness to some extent, a significant parsing error could negatively impact the performance. The experiments do not explicitly analyze the correlation between parsing accuracy and K-SAN performance. Furthermore, the paper lacks a discussion of error analysis – identifying *types* of errors where K-SAN fails and whether these failures are linked to parsing errors.
3. **Lack of detailed explanation of substructure creation:** The description of how substructures are created from the dependency tree (or AMR graph) is somewhat vague.  It would be helpful to provide more concrete examples of the substructures that are learned and how they relate to the semantic meaning of the utterance. The paper mentions avoiding duplicated information from non-leaf nodes but doesn't explain how this is determined or if it’s a critical hyperparameter.
4. **Performance gains are moderate:** While statistically significant, the performance improvements over strong baselines (particularly the CNN Encoder-Tagger) are not massive. The paper needs to further highlight *where* K-SAN excels – for which types of utterances or slot-filling tasks does it provide the most significant benefit?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the criteria used to select substructures from the dependency tree, and how this selection impacts the performance?
2. How does the performance of K-SAN vary with the accuracy of the external parser? Have you conducted any experiments to measure this correlation?
3. Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the attention weights learned by K-SAN on specific example utterances? This would help to understand which substructures the model is focusing on and whether this aligns with linguistic intuition.
4. How does the computational cost of K-SAN compare to the baselines, especially considering the added complexity of knowledge encoding and attention calculation?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach.
*   The notation in Section 3.2 could be clarified.
*   Consider adding an ablation study to investigate the contribution of each component of K-SAN (e.g., knowledge encoding, attention mechanism).
*   It would be valuable to see experiments on other datasets beyond ATIS to assess the generalizability of the approach.
