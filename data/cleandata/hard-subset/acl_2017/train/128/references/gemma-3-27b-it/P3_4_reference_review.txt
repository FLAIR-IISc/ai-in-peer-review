## Review for "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel neural network architecture designed to incorporate external knowledge into spoken dialogue state tracking (specifically, NLU components like intent detection and slot filling). The core idea is to use dependency parse trees (or other structured knowledge sources) to guide attention mechanisms within an RNN framework, allowing the model to focus on relevant substructures of the input utterance. The authors claim three primary contributions: end-to-end learning of attention guided by knowledge, generalization to different knowledge sources, and efficiency/parallelizability.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel and potentially effective way to integrate structural knowledge into sequence tagging models.** While knowledge integration isn't new, the K-SAN architecture offers a compelling approach to *how* this integration happens, moving beyond feature engineering and towards a more learned, attention-based approach.
2. **Demonstrated effectiveness on a standard benchmark.** The paper shows consistent improvements over strong baselines on the ATIS dataset, suggesting the approach has practical merit.
3. **Empirical validation of knowledge source flexibility.** The results showing similar performance across different knowledge sources (dependency trees from different parsers, AMR graphs) suggest the model is indeed adaptable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional NLU pipelines (reliance on large datasets, ignoring structural information) and motivates the need for a more knowledge-aware approach.
2. **Well-Designed Architecture:** The K-SAN architecture appears thoughtfully designed. The use of attention mechanisms to weigh the importance of knowledge-guided substructures is a logical and promising approach.  The modularity allows for flexibility in knowledge encoding.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a solid experimental setup, comparing against several relevant baselines and performing ablation studies to demonstrate the benefits of different components of their model.  The varying dataset sizes are a good way to demonstrate generalization ability.
4. **Demonstrated Generalization to Knowledge Sources:**  The fact that the approach works reasonably well with different knowledge formats (dependency trees vs. AMR) is a significant strength. This is not a method tightly coupled to a specific parsing technology.
5. **Strong Performance Improvements:** Consistently outperforming strong baselines on ATIS is a positive sign, and the gains are more pronounced on smaller datasets, suggesting K-SAN’s ability to learn from limited data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** The individual components of K-SAN (RNNs, attention mechanisms, dependency parsing) are not novel in themselves. The contribution lies in the *combination* and application to this specific NLU problem. While the combination is well-motivated, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how it differs from existing approaches that also combine deep learning with linguistic structures (e.g., tree-LSTMs).
2. **Dependency on Parser Quality:** While the paper demonstrates robustness to *different* parsers, the performance is still ultimately dependent on the quality of the initial parse tree. Errors in the parsing can propagate through the system. The authors acknowledge this but could provide more analysis of the impact of parsing errors.  A more robust approach might involve learning to jointly optimize the parsing and tagging steps.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study on Knowledge Encoding:**  The paper shows results for different *types* of knowledge encoding (NN, RNN, CNN) but doesn’t provide a detailed ablation study on the impact of different hyperparameters within those encoding networks.  What’s the sensitivity of performance to embedding dimensions, network depth, etc.?
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:**  While quantitative results are presented, a qualitative error analysis would be valuable.  What types of errors does K-SAN still make? Are there specific linguistic phenomena that it struggles with? Providing concrete examples would help understand the model's limitations and guide future work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does K-SAN compare to recent work that directly incorporates pre-trained linguistic knowledge (e.g., BERT, ELMo) into NLU models?  Do you see K-SAN as complementary to these approaches, or as an alternative?
2.  You mention the potential for error propagation from the parser. Could you elaborate on strategies for mitigating this issue? Have you considered methods for learning to "ignore" or down-weight unreliable parse trees?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of K-SAN compared to the baseline RNN models? While you claim parallelizability, a more quantitative comparison would be helpful.
4.  Can you provide some example instances where K-SAN correctly tags slots that the baseline models fail on, and explain *why* K-SAN is able to succeed in these cases?




**Additional Comments:**

* The formatting is generally good, but some figure captions are a bit sparse.
* The writing is clear and concise, making the paper easy to follow.
* The inclusion of the ablation study comparing different knowledge encoders is helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a promising approach to knowledge-guided NLU. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and application are well-motivated and demonstrably effective. The weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed through further experimentation and analysis. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
