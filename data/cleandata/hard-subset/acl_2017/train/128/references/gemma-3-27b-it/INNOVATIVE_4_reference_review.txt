## Review

This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue NLU, aiming to integrate external knowledge into sequence tagging via an attention mechanism. The central idea—using knowledge to *guide* attention rather than simply augmenting features—is the most significant aspect of this work. The authors demonstrate improvements over strong baselines, particularly in low-data settings, and show generalization across different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Novel Attention Guiding Mechanism:** The concept of explicitly using knowledge structures to direct the attention of a neural network during sequence tagging is a genuinely novel approach. This moves beyond typical feature-engineering with knowledge and towards a more integrated learning process.
2.  **Empirical Demonstration of Benefit in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The consistent performance gains, especially with limited training data, provide compelling evidence for the value of knowledge integration. This is a practical and valuable finding.
3. **Generalizability across knowledge sources:** Demonstrating consistent performance using dependency trees *and* AMR graphs strengthens the argument that K-SAN isn’t tied to a specific linguistic representation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Bold Idea & Clear Motivation:** The paper tackles a core problem in NLU – effective knowledge integration – with a relatively unexplored approach. The motivation for guiding attention is sound, acknowledging the limitations of naive feature concatenation.
2.  **Solid Experimental Setup:** The authors use a standard benchmark (ATIS) and compare against a range of strong baselines, including recent deep learning approaches. The ablation studies (different knowledge encoders) are helpful.
3.  **Robust Performance:** The results consistently show K-SAN outperforming baselines, with gains being most pronounced when data is scarce. This suggests the model genuinely learns something valuable from the knowledge, rather than overfitting.
4. **Generalization to Diverse Knowledge Sources:** Showing comparable performance with dependency parses and AMR graphs suggests the underlying approach isn't brittle and can adapt to different knowledge representations.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the architecture (K-SAN) is clearly explained with a helpful diagram.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the paper mentions attention, the analysis is superficial. Providing more detailed visualizations or quantitative analyses of *what* the model is attending to would strengthen the claims about knowledge-guided attention. For instance, showing attention distributions for correctly vs. incorrectly classified examples could be insightful.
2.  **Dependency on Parser Quality:** The performance relies on the accuracy of the dependency parser (or AMR parser). While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough investigation of the impact of parsing errors on downstream performance would be beneficial. The experiment with slightly different parsers helps but isn’t exhaustive.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Knowledge-Augmented Models:**  There is a lack of comparison with current approaches that *directly* incorporate knowledge graph embeddings into the sequence tagging process. While the authors cite such work, they do not benchmark against them directly, making it difficult to assess the relative advantage of K-SAN.
4. **End-to-End Training Concerns:** While the authors claim end-to-end training, the pre-processing step of obtaining dependency parses or AMR graphs adds complexity. The potential for error propagation *from the parser* to the NLU model isn't fully addressed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more detailed analyses of the attention weights learned by K-SAN? Specifically, can you show examples of what the model attends to in successful and unsuccessful cases?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to errors in the dependency parsing (or AMR parsing)? Have you experimented with noisy parsing outputs to assess robustness?
3.  Could you compare K-SAN against a recent, strong baseline that directly incorporates knowledge graph embeddings into the sequence tagging process? (e.g., a model that uses pre-trained entity and relation embeddings).
4. How do the results change when using different window sizes for the CNN encoder?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The ablation study comparing different knowledge encoders (NN, RNN, CNN) is valuable, but it would be helpful to understand *why* CNN consistently performs best with larger datasets.
* The paper would benefit from discussing the limitations of using syntactic knowledge alone and exploring the potential benefits of incorporating semantic knowledge (e.g. from knowledge graphs) directly into the attention mechanism.



**Overall:**

This is a strong submission with a genuinely novel idea. While there are some areas for improvement (particularly regarding attention analysis and comparison to more recent knowledge-augmented models), the core contribution – knowledge-guided attention – is significant and the empirical results are compelling. The paper’s boldness and potential to influence future research in knowledge-aware NLU outweigh its minor flaws. I recommend **Accept**.
