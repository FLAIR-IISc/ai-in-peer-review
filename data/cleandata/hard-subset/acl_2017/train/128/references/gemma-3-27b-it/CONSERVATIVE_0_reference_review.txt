This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken language understanding, specifically slot filling in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge, in the form of dependency parses or similar structures, to guide the attention mechanism of an RNN-based sequence tagger. The authors claim three primary contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge sources, and efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

The most substantial contribution is the attempt to integrate structured knowledge directly into the attention mechanism of a neural network for slot filling. While knowledge integration into NLU is not novel, the approach of using knowledge to *guide* attention—rather than simply adding features—is a reasonable direction to explore. The empirical results, while not overwhelmingly superior, do demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly with limited training data. The demonstration of relatively consistent performance across different knowledge sources (dependency trees from different parsers, and AMR graphs) also suggests a degree of robustness.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed architecture. The experimental setup is reasonable, using a standard benchmark dataset (ATIS) and comparing against relevant baselines. The ablation study examining different knowledge encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN) provides some insight into the relative strengths of each approach. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence to justify the claimed novelty and benefits. The performance gains, while statistically significant in some cases, are modest. The authors do not adequately establish *why* K-SAN outperforms the baselines. Is it truly the guided attention, or simply the incorporation of structural information in some form? The improvements are also heavily dependent on the size of the training data, with the largest gains observed in the small data regime, suggesting the method primarily addresses data scarcity rather than providing a fundamental advance in NLU. 

A significant weakness is the limited analysis of the attention mechanism itself. The authors visualize attention weights (Figure 5), but this is a qualitative illustration rather than a quantitative analysis. A more thorough examination of *where* the model is attending, and *why*, would strengthen the argument that the knowledge guidance is effective. The authors state that the model "pays correct attention to salient substructures," but do not provide concrete evidence to support this claim beyond the visualization. The observation that performance isn't drastically affected by the choice of parser is interesting, but not deeply explored.

Another concern is the reliance on pre-trained dependency parsers and AMR parsers. The performance of K-SAN is therefore indirectly tied to the quality of these parsers. While the authors acknowledge this, they don’t sufficiently address the potential for errors in the knowledge source to propagate and negatively impact performance. The robustness across different parsers is a positive sign, but a more rigorous evaluation, perhaps with intentionally noisy knowledge sources, would be beneficial. 

Finally, the claim of “efficiency” through parallelizable substructure modeling feels weak. While the substructures are processed independently, the overall complexity likely remains dominated by the RNN processing of the sequence. A more detailed analysis of computational costs would be needed to support this claim.



1.  Can the authors provide a quantitative analysis of the attention weights learned by K-SAN? Specifically, are the attention weights for knowledge-guided substructures consistently higher for semantically relevant parts of the utterance?
2.  How sensitive is K-SAN to errors in the dependency parses or AMR graphs? Have the authors considered any methods for mitigating the impact of parsing errors?
3.  The authors claim generalization across different knowledge sources. Can they provide an analysis of the types of knowledge that are most effective for K-SAN? Are there knowledge sources that consistently lead to worse performance?
4.  What is the computational overhead of K-SAN compared to a standard RNN-CRF model? Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the efficiency gains?



Additional Comments: The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison to other knowledge integration techniques, such as incorporating knowledge graph embeddings as features. The related work section is somewhat superficial. The phrasing "to our knowledge" is used excessively.
