This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken language understanding, specifically slot filling in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge, in the form of dependency parses or similar structural representations, into an RNN-based sequence tagger via an attention mechanism. The authors claim three main contributions: 1) end-to-end learning of attention guided by knowledge, 2) generalizability to different knowledge sources, and 3) efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

A minor contribution of this work is the demonstration that incorporating *any* form of structured knowledge can yield modest gains in slot filling performance, especially when training data is limited. The exploration of different knowledge encodings (NN, RNN, CNN) and the comparison with baselines that do and do not utilize structural information provide some incremental insight into the value of knowledge integration. The experimental setup, while relying on the standard ATIS dataset, is reasonably thorough in its comparison to existing methods.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. Firstly, the proposed method lacks substantial novelty. Attention mechanisms and the incorporation of external knowledge into neural networks are well-established techniques in NLP. The K-SAN architecture feels like a fairly straightforward application of these existing concepts, rather than a truly innovative approach. The claim of “end-to-end learning” is overstated; the knowledge structure itself requires a separate parsing step.

Secondly, the performance gains reported are modest and not consistently significant across all experimental conditions. While the authors report improvements, these often appear small relative to strong baselines. The improvements are most apparent with limited training data, which suggests the model is primarily mitigating data sparsity rather than demonstrating a fundamentally superior understanding capability.  The sensitivity analysis in Table 2 regarding different knowledge sources further emphasizes this point - performance doesn’t drastically change.

Thirdly, the paper doesn’t adequately address potential issues related to the quality of the external knowledge. Dependency parsing is not perfect, and errors in the parse tree could propagate into the model, potentially degrading performance. The authors briefly acknowledge this but don’t offer a robust solution or analysis of how the method handles noisy knowledge. The visualizations in Figure 5 actually *show* incorrect dependency parses, yet the model seems to function reasonably well despite them – this raises questions about what the model is actually learning.

Finally, the motivation for the specific architectural choices, particularly the different knowledge encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN), is not clearly articulated. Why does CNN perform best on smaller datasets, while RNN excels with more data? This is left unexplored, hindering understanding of the method’s behavior.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of parsing errors on the model’s performance? Have you explored any methods for handling noisy or inaccurate knowledge?
2.  What is the computational cost of the parsing step, and how does it affect the overall efficiency of the K-SAN system compared to methods that do not require external knowledge?
3.  Why are the gains from using K-SAN more pronounced with smaller training datasets? Is this simply due to regularization, or is there a more fundamental reason?
4.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind experimenting with different knowledge encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN), and provide a more in-depth analysis of their respective strengths and weaknesses within the K-SAN framework?



**Additional Comments:** The citation formatting is inconsistent throughout the paper. The discussion of related work is somewhat superficial and could benefit from a more critical engagement with the literature.
