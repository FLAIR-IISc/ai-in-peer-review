This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken language understanding, specifically slot filling in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge, represented as linguistic structures (dependency trees in the main experiments), to guide attention mechanisms within an RNN-based sequence tagger. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning of knowledge-guided attention, generalization to different knowledge sources, and efficiency/parallelizability.

The most significant contribution of this work is the *idea* of explicitly using external knowledge to *guide* attention within a neural network for sequence tagging. While attention mechanisms are commonplace, and knowledge integration is not new, framing knowledge as a “teacher” to direct attention is a conceptually interesting approach. The authors correctly identify the limitations of simply adding knowledge as features and propose a more integrated method. The experimental validation, though not exhaustive, suggests this approach can be beneficial, especially in low-data scenarios.

Further strengths include the exploration of different knowledge encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN) and the demonstration of generalization across knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs). The comprehensive experimental setup with varying dataset sizes is also a positive aspect, highlighting the potential robustness of K-SAN. The analysis showing attention weights aligned with intuition (focusing on key phrases and relationships) is compelling.  The authors also provide a thorough related work section, contextualizing their contribution well.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the methodological novelty is somewhat limited. The K-SAN architecture builds heavily on established components (RNNs, attention mechanisms). The primary innovation is the application of these to a knowledge-guided setting, but the architectural choices themselves aren't groundbreaking.  Second, the experimental results, while positive, aren’t overwhelmingly convincing. The performance gains, particularly on the larger dataset, are modest.  A more comprehensive comparison against stronger baselines (e.g., more recent transformer-based models) is needed. Third, the knowledge parsing step (dependency parsing, AMR parsing) introduces an external dependency and potential error source. While the authors attempt to mitigate this, a more detailed error analysis on the impact of parsing errors would be valuable. Finally, the explanation of parallelizability is rather vague; a more concrete analysis of computational complexity would strengthen this claim.

Despite these weaknesses, I believe the core idea presented is strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper proposes a conceptually novel approach to knowledge integration in NLU, and the initial results are promising. The potential for further development and improvement is high. I’m willing to overlook the moderate performance gains and methodological limitations given the potential impact of this direction.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of parsing errors on the performance of K-SAN? Specifically, how does the model handle noisy or inaccurate dependency trees/AMR graphs?
2.  What is the computational complexity of K-SAN compared to a standard RNN-based slot filler, particularly as the size of the input sentence and the complexity of the knowledge graph increase? Could you provide a more concrete analysis of the parallelizability claim?
3. The paper mentions the possibility of using handcrafted grammars as knowledge. Could you provide a brief example of how this would be implemented and how it might differ from using dependency parses or AMR graphs?
4. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the weighting factor α in the joint RNN tagger?
