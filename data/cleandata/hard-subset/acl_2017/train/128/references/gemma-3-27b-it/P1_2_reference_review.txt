## Peer Review for "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Network (K-SAN) for Natural Language Understanding (NLU), specifically targeting spoken dialogue systems. The core idea is to leverage external knowledge (dependency parsing in the experiments) to guide attention mechanisms within an RNN-based sequence tagging model. The authors claim to address limitations of previous approaches that treat knowledge as merely additional features, aiming for end-to-end learning, generalization across knowledge types, and efficiency. The paper demonstrates improvements on the ATIS dataset, highlighting the benefits of the knowledge-guided attention, especially with limited training data.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Principled Integration of Knowledge via Attention:** The paper presents a clear and relatively straightforward approach to integrate structural knowledge (from dependency parsing) into a neural sequence tagging model *via* an attention mechanism. This goes beyond simply adding features and attempts to learn *where* and *how much* to attend to different substructures.
2. **Empirical Validation on a Standard NLU Task:** The experiments on the ATIS dataset demonstrate a consistent improvement over strong baselines, showing the practical effectiveness of the K-SAN approach. The analysis showing improvements with smaller datasets is particularly valuable.
3. **Demonstrated Generalization across Knowledge Sources:** The exploration of different knowledge representations (dependency trees from different parsers, AMR graphs) and the comparable performance suggests a degree of flexibility and generalization capability of the K-SAN framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing NLU approaches and the potential benefits of integrating knowledge in a more intelligent way. The motivation for moving beyond feature enrichment is well-explained.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of the standard ATIS dataset, comparison with strong baselines (including both feature-based and structural models), and the ablation studies with different knowledge sources provide a convincing empirical evaluation.
3. **Effective Attention Visualization:**  Figure 5 provides a helpful visualization of the attention weights, demonstrating that the model is indeed attending to semantically relevant substructures, and that this attention remains fairly consistent even with limited data.
4. **Comprehensive Ablation Studies:** Investigating different encoding mechanisms (NN, RNN, CNN) for knowledge representation shows a nuanced understanding of the design choices and their impact on performance.
5. **Demonstrated Robustness to Data Scarcity:** The consistent performance gains, particularly on the smaller datasets, demonstrate the utility of incorporating prior knowledge when data is limited.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of knowledge integration and attention is effective, the individual components (RNNs, attention mechanisms, dependency parsing) are well-established. The core innovation lies in the specific application and integration, rather than in fundamentally new algorithmic breakthroughs.
2. **Dependence on Parser Quality:** The performance is inherently tied to the accuracy of the dependency parser. While the authors show robustness across parsers, noisy or inaccurate parsing can negatively impact results. This dependency should be more explicitly acknowledged and mitigated (e.g., through more robust parsing or learning to discount unreliable structures).
3. **Overstated Claims of Generalization:** The claim of generalization across *any* knowledge type feels strong, given that all experiments rely on structured representations.  Applying this to completely unstructured knowledge sources (e.g., free-text descriptions) would likely require significant modifications and may not be as effective.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the overall performance is reported, a more detailed error analysis would be valuable. Understanding *where* the model still struggles and *why* could provide insights for future improvements.  What types of errors does K-SAN still make?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Parser Sensitivity:**  How sensitive is the K-SAN performance to errors in the dependency parsing? Have you explored techniques to mitigate the impact of parsing errors, such as weighting substructures based on parsing confidence scores?
2. **Knowledge Source Selection:**  What criteria would you use to select the most appropriate knowledge source (e.g., dependency parsing, AMR graphs) for a given NLU task? Are there guidelines or heuristics that can be used to guide this selection?
3. **Unstructured Knowledge:** Could the K-SAN framework be adapted to incorporate unstructured knowledge sources (e.g., textual descriptions)? If so, what modifications would be necessary? What are the potential challenges?
4. **Computational Cost:**  While the paper mentions efficiency, can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of K-SAN compared to the baselines, particularly as the input sequence length increases?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The related work section is quite comprehensive.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the current approach and potential avenues for future research. For example, could this approach be combined with pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT)?
