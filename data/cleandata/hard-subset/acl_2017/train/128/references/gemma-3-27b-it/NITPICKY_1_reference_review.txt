This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken language understanding, specifically slot filling in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge, in the form of dependency parses or similar structural representations, into an RNN-based sequence tagger via an attention mechanism. The authors claim three primary contributions: 1) end-to-end learning of knowledge-guided attention, 2) generalizability to different knowledge sources, and 3) efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

While the idea of integrating knowledge into neural sequence models is not novel, the attempt to do so *directly* via attention, rather than as feature engineering, is a reasonable direction. The experimental results on the ATIS dataset demonstrate some improvement over strong baselines, particularly with limited training data.  The exploration of different knowledge sources (dependency trees vs. AMR graphs) is also a positive aspect.

However, numerous issues prevent a positive recommendation. The presentation is plagued by minor, yet pervasive, flaws that suggest a lack of meticulousness. The writing is often verbose and repetitive, and the figure captions are incomplete (e.g., Figure 1 lacks a clear explanation of the 'S'). More importantly, the methodological details are frequently vague. The description of the knowledge encoding module is particularly hand-wavy ("different types…can be applied in the same way"), and the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the α parameter in the joint RNN tagger) lacks justification. The ablation studies are insufficient; it's unclear what portion of the performance gain comes from the attention mechanism *specifically* versus simply incorporating structural information in any way.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Evaluation & Ablation:** The paper fails to demonstrate convincingly that the attention mechanism is *essential* to the observed performance gains.  Simply showing that K-SAN performs better than baselines is insufficient. A key experiment is missing: a version of K-SAN without the attention mechanism (i.e., simply concatenating or averaging the encoded knowledge representations) should be included to assess the value of the attention component. Further ablation studies exploring different knowledge encoding architectures (NN, RNN, CNN) independently would provide valuable insights.

2. **Insufficient Methodological Detail:** The description of the knowledge encoding process is overly abstract. What specific representations are used for the substructures? How are they embedded? The claim of “generalizability” to different knowledge sources is undermined by the lack of detail regarding how these sources are processed.  The authors state different RNN types are used in knowledge encoding, but do not adequately explain the rationale behind these choices or how they impact performance. The hyperparameter tuning process is similarly vague.

3. **Minor Flaws and Inconsistent Formatting:** The paper contains numerous minor errors, including grammatical issues, inconsistent notation (e.g., use of both ~s and s to represent the utterance), and incomplete figure captions. While individually minor, their cumulative effect detracts from the paper’s credibility. For instance, the description of equation (1) refers to "pi can be viewed as attention distribution" which reads awkwardly and feels incomplete. Similarly, the use of "†" to denote significance is inconsistent with standard academic practice. The inconsistent use of acronyms (e.g. "RNN" being fully spelled out in some sections and not in others) is also distracting.

4. **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of knowledge and attention is a sensible approach, the core ideas are not groundbreaking. Existing work has already explored both knowledge integration and attention mechanisms in NLP. The paper doesn't sufficiently highlight what *specifically* distinguishes K-SAN from prior approaches beyond the end-to-end learning claim, which, as noted above, isn't convincingly demonstrated.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a detailed explanation of the knowledge encoding process, including the specific representations used for substructures, the embedding method, and the rationale for choosing different architectures (NN, RNN, CNN).
2.  Please present results for an ablation study where the attention mechanism is removed from K-SAN. How does performance change?
3.  Can you provide more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and criteria used to select the optimal values?
4.  Why did you choose to not utilize the dependency relation labels in your experiments? Would including them potentially impact the performance of K-SAN?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are not formatted consistently.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of K-SAN compared to existing methods.
*   The use of "teacher" in the paper's title and introduction is metaphorical and doesn’t accurately reflect the model’s functionality.



Overall, while the core idea of K-SAN is reasonable, the lack of rigorous evaluation, insufficient methodological detail, and numerous minor flaws make it difficult to recommend acceptance. A major revision addressing these issues would be necessary. I recommend **rejection**.
