This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue state tracking, aiming to improve slot filling by incorporating external knowledge into a recurrent neural network architecture. The authors claim three primary contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge sources, and efficiency/parallelizability.

In my assessment, the core contribution lies in the attempt to integrate structured knowledge into an attention mechanism within a neural network for slot filling, rather than simply using knowledge as additional features. The demonstration of reasonable performance across multiple knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) is a positive aspect. However, the gains over strong baselines are modest, and the claims of significant novelty are overstated. The efficiency argument is not convincingly demonstrated; simply stating that modeling time *may* not increase linearly doesn't constitute evidence.

**Strengths:**

1. **Integration of Knowledge and Attention:** The central idea of guiding attention mechanisms with external knowledge is conceptually sound and addresses a limitation of previous approaches that treat knowledge as static features. The proposed architecture attempts a more dynamic interaction between the input and the knowledge source.
2. **Generalization Across Knowledge Sources:** The ability to apply the framework to both syntactic (dependency trees) and semantic (AMR graphs) knowledge is a benefit, indicating some robustness to the representation of the external knowledge. The results demonstrate that the method isn’t overly reliant on a specific knowledge format.
3. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments on the ATIS dataset are well-conducted with reasonable baselines and ablation studies. The use of different training data sizes helps assess the model's data efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement:** While K-SAN demonstrates improvements over baselines, particularly on smaller datasets, the performance gains are relatively small, especially on the full dataset. This suggests the method doesn’t represent a substantial leap forward. The best results (94.86-94.97 F1) are only marginally better than existing approaches.
2. **Overstated Novelty:** The claim of “end-to-end learning” is somewhat misleading. While the attention mechanism is learned, the knowledge itself (dependency parses, AMR graphs) is *pre-computed* using external tools. This isn't truly end-to-end in the sense of jointly learning the knowledge representation and the slot filling model. The reliance on external parsers introduces potential error propagation, a point acknowledged by the authors but not adequately addressed.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* K-SAN still struggles and *why* would provide valuable insights. For example, are there specific slot types or utterance structures where the model consistently fails? A qualitative examination of errors is missing.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** More recent and sophisticated slot filling models, such as those leveraging pre-trained language models (BERT, RoBERTa), are not included in the comparison. Evaluating against these more powerful baselines is crucial to accurately assess the contribution of K-SAN.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and parallelizability of K-SAN compared to the baseline RNN models, perhaps with runtime measurements?
2.  The paper mentions potential errors in the external parsers propagating to the model. Have you investigated the impact of parser accuracy on K-SAN's performance? Is there a way to mitigate this issue, such as incorporating confidence scores from the parser?
3.  What types of knowledge representations *beyond* dependency trees and AMR graphs have you explored or considered? Are there limitations to the types of knowledge that can be effectively integrated into the K-SAN framework?
4.  Can you provide specific examples of utterances where K-SAN significantly outperforms the baselines, and explain *why* the knowledge-guided attention is beneficial in those cases?



**Additional Comments:**

The visualizations of the attention weights (Figure 5) are helpful but could be improved by highlighting the specific substructures that are driving the attention. The formatting of Table 2 is slightly unclear; please clarify which parser was used for each knowledge source.
