This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken language understanding (SLU), aiming to incorporate external knowledge into a neural network architecture to improve slot filling performance. The core idea is to use dependency parsing (or other knowledge sources) to create “substructures” and then learn attention weights over these substructures to generate a more informed sentence representation. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability.

I find the most significant contribution, if validated, to be the attempt to move beyond simply *adding* knowledge as features to a neural network and instead *integrating* it into the network’s attention mechanism. However, the presented validation is insufficient to convince me of the novelty or substantial benefit of this approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Problem Relevance:** The task of SLU is undeniably important, and improving performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios, is a valuable goal. The motivation for leveraging external knowledge to aid in generalization is sound.
2. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the K-SAN architecture in a relatively clear manner. The diagrams are helpful in understanding the flow of information.
3. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors evaluate their approach on the standard ATIS dataset, with varying amounts of training data, and compare against a range of established baselines. The consideration of different knowledge sources (dependency trees vs. AMR graphs) is also commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the idea of guiding attention with external knowledge is potentially interesting, the core mechanism – attention over substructures – feels largely derivative of existing attention mechanisms in neural networks. The authors do not sufficiently differentiate K-SAN from established attention-based sequence tagging models. The claim of “end-to-end learning” is not particularly distinguishing; many SLU models are trained end-to-end.
2. **Limited Performance Gains:** The performance improvements reported, while statistically significant in some cases, are relatively modest. The gains are most pronounced on the smallest training set (Small), suggesting the method might be more effective when data is scarce, but this isn’t convincingly demonstrated. The results on the larger datasets are less compelling. A more substantial improvement would be needed to justify the added complexity of the K-SAN architecture.
3. **Baseline Selection & Reporting:** The baseline results are reported without sufficient detail. For example, the exact configuration of the CRF Tagger isn't specified (feature set beyond dependency trees). Furthermore, the performance of established methods like BERT or other pre-trained language models, which are now commonplace in SLU, is absent. Failing to compare against these stronger baselines weakens the claims of superiority.
4. **Knowledge Source Impact:** The results in Table 2 show minimal performance difference between different knowledge sources (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet dependency parsers, rule-based vs. JAMR AMR parsers). This suggests the specific knowledge source is less important than the general mechanism of knowledge guidance, which again reduces the novelty. The influence of parsing *error* on performance is not discussed. Figure 5 illustrates a case where the dependency parse is incorrect, yet the model still benefits—this is a concerning point that requires further investigation.
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of different knowledge encoding models (NN, RNN, CNN) is presented, but a deeper analysis of hyperparameter tuning for each configuration is missing. Was a consistent search strategy employed? Are the reported results the best achievable for each model?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does K-SAN compare to state-of-the-art SLU models based on pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa)? Please provide a direct comparison.
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of parsing accuracy on the performance of K-SAN? How robust is the model to noisy or incorrect knowledge graphs?
3. What is the computational overhead of K-SAN compared to the baseline models? Is the claimed "efficiency and parallelizability" substantiated with empirical measurements?
4.  The ablation studies lack depth. Specifically, what is the contribution of the attention mechanism itself? Does removing the attention component and simply averaging the substructure embeddings result in similar performance?
5. Provide further explanation on why the model performs comparatively better with a smaller dataset.



**Additional Comments:**

* The citation format is inconsistent (e.g., "Banarescu et al\.,? 2013"). Please standardize the formatting.
* While the inclusion of the code is appreciated, it would be helpful to provide more detailed instructions on how to reproduce the results.



Overall, this paper presents a reasonable but ultimately unconvincing contribution to the field of SLU. While the idea of knowledge-guided attention is potentially promising, the limited performance gains, lack of comparison to stronger baselines, and insufficient analysis of the knowledge source's impact prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above and provide stronger evidence to support their claims.
