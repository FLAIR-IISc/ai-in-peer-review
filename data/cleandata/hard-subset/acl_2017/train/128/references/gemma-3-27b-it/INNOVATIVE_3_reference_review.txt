This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken language understanding, specifically slot filling. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge, represented as linguistic structures (dependency trees in the main experiments), into an attention mechanism within a recurrent neural network framework. The authors claim three key contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge sources, and efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

The most significant contribution of this work lies in the *attempt* to directly integrate structured knowledge into the attention mechanism of a neural network for sequence tagging, rather than simply using knowledge as additional features. While the approach isn't radically different in its components (RNNs, attention), the framing – treating knowledge as a "teacher" guiding attention – is a conceptually interesting shift. The demonstrated ability to leverage diverse knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without significant architectural changes suggests a degree of flexibility. The empirical results, while not groundbreaking, show consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly on smaller datasets.

Strengths:

1. **Novel Conceptual Framing:** The idea of knowledge *guiding* attention, rather than simply being *added* as features, is a noteworthy contribution. This encourages the network to actively learn which aspects of the knowledge structure are most relevant for the task, potentially leading to more robust and interpretable models.
2. **Flexibility with Knowledge Sources:** The ability to utilize different types of knowledge (dependency parses, AMR graphs) without major modifications is a strength. This demonstrates the generalizability of the K-SAN framework and its potential for adapting to various NLP tasks and resource settings.
3. **Empirical Improvements:** The consistent, statistically significant improvements over competitive baselines, especially in low-data scenarios, demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed approach. This is crucial; the conceptual novelty needs to translate to tangible performance gains.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the K-SAN architecture and experimental setup. The figures are helpful in visualizing the process.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** The underlying building blocks (RNNs, attention) are well-established. The core innovation is the *application* of attention to knowledge-derived substructures, which is incremental. The paper doesn't introduce fundamentally new neural network architectures or learning algorithms.
2. **Dependency on Parsing Accuracy:** The performance is intrinsically linked to the quality of the external knowledge sources (e.g., dependency parser). While the authors demonstrate robustness across different parsers, parsing errors inevitably propagate and can negatively impact performance. This isn't adequately addressed. The visualization in Figure 5 highlights this, showing incorrect parses still utilized by the model.
3. **Insufficient Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the authors claim knowledge "guides" attention, a deeper analysis of the learned attention weights is missing. Are specific substructures consistently receiving higher attention? What types of substructures are most informative? A more thorough examination of the attention mechanism would strengthen the claims.
4. **Evaluation Limited to ATIS:** The evaluation is confined to a single benchmark dataset (ATIS). While ATIS is a standard testbed, its limited scope raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other, more complex dialogue domains.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned attention weights? Specifically, what types of substructures consistently receive the highest attention, and how does this vary across different utterances and knowledge sources?
2. How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to parsing errors? Have you explored techniques for mitigating the impact of noisy or inaccurate knowledge sources?
3. Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with incorporating knowledge-guided attention compared to standard attention mechanisms? Does the claimed efficiency hold in practice, especially with larger knowledge graphs or more complex utterances?
4. Do you have any preliminary results on other dialogue datasets to demonstrate the generalizability of K-SAN?
