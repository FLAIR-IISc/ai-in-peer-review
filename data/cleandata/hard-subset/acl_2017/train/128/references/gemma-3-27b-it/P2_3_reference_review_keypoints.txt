- Summary: Proposes K-SAN, a neural model that uses external structured knowledge (e.g., dependency parses, AMR) to guide attention over utterance substructures for slot filling, claiming end-to-end learning, generality, and efficient parallel substructure modeling.

- Contribution 1: Presents a practical framework to integrate structural knowledge into a neural slot-filling model beyond feature augmentation.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates consistent performance gains on ATIS, especially in low-data settings, versus strong baselines.

- Contribution 3: Shows the approach is robust to different parsers/knowledge formats (Stanford vs SyntaxNet, dependency vs AMR), though differences are small.

- Strength 1: Clear motivation and problem definition highlighting limits of data-only approaches and benefits of structured knowledge.

- Strength 2: Solid experimental validation with standard benchmark, ablations, and statistically significant improvements.

- Strength 3: Practical and implementable architecture with enough detail for replication and ability to handle multiple knowledge formats.

- Strength 4: Attention visualizations that provide interpretability and insight into how knowledge guides the model.

- Weakness 1: Core components (RNNs, attention, dependency parsing) are established techniques; novelty is mainly in their combination.

- Weakness 2: Claim of broad generality is overstated—performance depends on knowledge quality and robustness to noisy/parsing errors is not thoroughly tested.

- Weakness 3: Lacks detailed error analysis to show where the model still fails (specific slot types or complex constructions).

- Question 1: How sensitive is K-SAN to errors in the underlying knowledge (parse accuracy) and can a self-correcting mechanism be integrated?

- Question 2: What is the concrete computational overhead (training/inference time) compared to standard RNN slot fillers?

- Question 3: Have experiments been done with other knowledge sources (e.g., Wikidata, ConceptNet), and what were the results?

- Additional comment 1: Some figures (e.g., Figure 2) are cramped and hard to read.

- Additional comment 2: Introduction could be more concise.

- Overall assessment: Solid, well-executed paper with practical contributions and good empirical support; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
