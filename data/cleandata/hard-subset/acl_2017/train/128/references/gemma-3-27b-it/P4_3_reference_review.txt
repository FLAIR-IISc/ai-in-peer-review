This paper proposes a knowledge-guided structural attention network (K-SAN) for natural language understanding in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge, specifically dependency parse trees, to guide the attention mechanism of an RNN-based sequence tagger. The authors claim three main contributions: 1) end-to-end learning of attention guided by knowledge, 2) generalization to different knowledge types, and 3) efficiency and parallelizability.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the exploration of integrating structural knowledge into an attention-based neural network for slot filling. While previous works have used knowledge as features, this paper attempts a more direct integration through an attention mechanism, which is a reasonable approach. The demonstration that different knowledge sources (dependency parses, AMR graphs) can be used without significant architecture changes also showcases a degree of flexibility. The experimental results on the ATIS dataset, while not drastically superior to the state-of-the-art, are competitive and show consistent improvements, especially with limited training data, supporting the claim of better generalization.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Problem:** The paper addresses a core challenge in spoken dialogue systems – robust natural language understanding, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The reliance on external knowledge to augment understanding is a well-motivated approach.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:**  The K-SAN architecture is logically sound. Using knowledge structures to guide attention provides a principled way to incorporate linguistic information into the neural network. The use of RNNs with GRU cells is a standard and appropriate choice for sequence tagging.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the ATIS dataset provide evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies exploring different knowledge encoding methods and the comparison with existing baselines strengthen the results. The consistently positive results across different training data sizes suggest the model's ability to generalize.
4. **Flexibility with Knowledge Sources:** The ability to use different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) without major architectural changes is a valuable feature. This showcases the method’s adaptability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** While the specific implementation of K-SAN is novel, the concept of incorporating external knowledge into neural networks for NLU is not entirely new. Several existing works, as mentioned in the related work section, have explored similar ideas. The attention mechanism itself is a well-established component in neural networks.  The step from using knowledge as features to using it to *guide* attention is incremental rather than transformative.
2. **Performance Gains are Moderate:** The performance improvements over the state-of-the-art are relatively modest. While statistically significant in some cases, the gains are not dramatic enough to suggest a paradigm shift in NLU performance.  It's unclear if the added complexity of the K-SAN architecture justifies the gains in accuracy.
3. **Dependency on Parser Quality:** The performance of the model is inherently tied to the quality of the dependency parser. Errors in parsing will propagate to the attention mechanism and potentially degrade performance. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth analysis of the impact of parser errors would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Providing examples of cases where K-SAN excels and where it fails, and relating those to the knowledge structures, would provide a deeper understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of parser errors on the performance of K-SAN? Specifically, how does the accuracy of the dependency parser correlate with the slot filling F1 score?
2.  Can you provide examples where K-SAN makes correct predictions that baseline models fail on, and explain *how* the knowledge-guided attention mechanism contributes to the improved performance in those specific cases?
3.  The paper mentions efficiency and parallelizability as a contribution. Could you provide quantitative evidence of this, such as training time comparisons with other models of comparable size and complexity?
4.  How sensitive is the model to the choice of knowledge encoding method (NN, RNN, CNN)? Are there specific types of knowledge or datasets where one encoding method consistently outperforms others?
