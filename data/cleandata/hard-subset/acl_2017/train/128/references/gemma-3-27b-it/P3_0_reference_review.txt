## Review of "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper presents Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach to spoken language understanding (SLU) that integrates external knowledge (specifically dependency parse trees, but generalizable to other structures like AMR graphs) into a recurrent neural network framework using an attention mechanism. The core idea is to let knowledge "guide" the network's attention, allowing it to focus on relevant substructures within the input utterance. The authors demonstrate improved performance on the ATIS dataset, particularly in low-data scenarios, and showcase the method’s adaptability to different knowledge sources.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Knowledge Integration via Attention:** The most significant contribution is the proposed mechanism for integrating structured knowledge into a neural network via an attention mechanism. This allows the model to dynamically weigh the importance of different knowledge-guided substructures, offering a more nuanced approach than simply using knowledge as additional features.
2. **Robustness to Data Scarcity:** The experiments demonstrate a clear benefit of K-SAN in low-data regimes. This is important for SLU, where labeled data is often limited and expensive to obtain.
3. **Generalizability to Different Knowledge Sources:** The demonstrated ability to work with different types of structured knowledge (dependency trees, AMR graphs) expands the applicability of the framework and highlights its conceptual strength.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Attention Mechanism:**  The core contribution – the attention mechanism guided by knowledge structures – is well-motivated and provides a clear advance over previous approaches that treated knowledge as static features. The idea of letting knowledge act as a “teacher” guiding attention is a compelling concept.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates improvements over strong baselines, particularly when training data is limited. The results on the ATIS dataset are statistically significant and provide convincing evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach.
3. **Thorough Evaluation & Ablation:** The authors provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons with various baselines, analysis of different knowledge encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN), and experiments with different knowledge sources (dependency trees vs. AMR). This thoroughness strengthens the claims made in the paper.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and the experimental setup.  The figures are helpful in illustrating the key concepts.
5. **Demonstrated Generalizability:**  Showing performance with both dependency trees *and* AMR graphs significantly increases the perceived applicability and robustness of the method.


**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Knowledge Structures:** While the authors demonstrate generalizability to different knowledge *sources*, the knowledge structures themselves are limited to parse-tree-like representations. Exploring other forms of knowledge (e.g., ontologies, frame nets) could further broaden the scope and impact of the work.  The paper doesn’t delve into the limitations of relying solely on syntactic/semantic structures derived from parsing.
2. **Dependence on Parser Accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently tied to the quality of the external parser (dependency or AMR). Errors in parsing can propagate through the model, potentially diminishing the benefits of knowledge integration. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t fully address the impact of parser errors through robust error analysis or mitigation strategies.  The experiments showing results *despite* parser inaccuracies (Figure 5) are interesting, but a more quantitative assessment of this impact would be valuable.
3. **Lack of Detailed Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the authors visualize attention weights, a more detailed analysis of *what* the model is attending to and *why* would be insightful. For example, are there specific dependency relations or AMR concepts that consistently receive higher attention for certain slot types?  This kind of analysis could provide deeper understanding of the model's behavior and potentially lead to further improvements.
4. **Computational Complexity:** While the paper claims efficiency due to modeling substructures separately, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the approach (compared to standard RNNs) would be beneficial.  The impact of the number of substructures on runtime and memory usage should be clarified.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to parser error rates? Could you provide a quantitative analysis of the impact of parser accuracy on the final slot filling results?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the attention weights learned by the model?  Are there specific dependency relations or AMR concepts that are consistently associated with higher attention for certain slot types?
3.  What is the computational complexity of K-SAN compared to a standard RNN-based SLU model? How does the number of substructures affect runtime and memory usage?
4.  Have you explored incorporating other forms of knowledge beyond dependency trees and AMR graphs, such as ontologies or frame nets?  If so, what were the results? If not, what challenges would need to be addressed to integrate these types of knowledge sources?




**Additional Comments:**

*  The related work section is comprehensive and well-organized.
*  The code should be released to facilitate reproducibility.
*  Consider adding a discussion of potential real-world applications and limitations of K-SAN in the conclusion.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to knowledge integration in SLU. The proposed K-SAN model demonstrates promising results, particularly in low-data scenarios, and its generalizability to different knowledge sources is a significant strength. While there are some weaknesses related to parser dependence and a need for more in-depth analysis, the overall contribution is substantial. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
