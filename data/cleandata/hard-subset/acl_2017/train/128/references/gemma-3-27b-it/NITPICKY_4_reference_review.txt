The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue state tracking, aiming to incorporate external knowledge to improve sequence tagging performance. The core idea is to use dependency parsing (or other structural information) to guide attention mechanisms within an RNN framework. The authors claim three primary contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability.

While the motivation is sound and the idea of explicitly guiding attention with external knowledge is appealing, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues that, taken together, lead me to recommend rejection. The presentation is verbose and lacks conciseness, obscuring the core technical contributions. Numerous minor details detract from the overall quality.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The proposed K-SAN architecture feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The idea of incorporating external knowledge into neural networks for NLU is well-established (see Related Work). While the attention mechanism guided by structure is a reasonable approach, it’s not fundamentally new. The authors fail to convincingly demonstrate *how* their approach surpasses existing methods that leverage knowledge as features. Simply stating "knowledge plays the role of a teacher" is insufficient justification.

2. **Experimental Rigor and Reporting:** The experimental setup is insufficiently detailed. The hyperparameters (e.g., embedding size, hidden layer sizes, dropout rates) are mentioned but without a clear rationale for their selection. The selection of the Stanford dependency parser feels arbitrary; a comparison against SyntaxNet, also readily available, would have been valuable. The ablation studies are minimal. The authors claim CNN encoding performs best on smaller datasets, but provide no analysis *why*. The significance testing (t-test) is mentioned but not fully presented – p-values are reported only with a dagger (†) without the specific values hindering proper evaluation. The results in Table 2, showing knowledge generalization, are also not properly explained. What does 'max #Substructure' signify? It appears to correlate with performance, but there’s no discussion.

3. **Presentation and Clarity:** The paper is excessively verbose. The Introduction, in particular, contains a lengthy overview of SDS and NLU that, while providing context, adds little to the specific contribution of this work. The figures (especially Figure 2) are cluttered and difficult to parse. The notation is inconsistent. For instance, 's' represents both a sequence of words and a single utterance. The writing style is often awkward and imprecise. Phrases like “poor generalization comes from the mismatch” are vague and unhelpful.

4. **Minor Technical Flaws:** The description of the "Joint RNN Tagger" (Section 4.3) lacks sufficient detail. The purpose of the α parameter is stated, but no justification for its value (0.5) is given. The claim of efficiency/parallelizability in the introduction is not substantiated. The architecture presented in the paper does not appear to lend itself to significant parallelization advantages over standard RNN-based approaches. The description of the Knowledge Encoding Module in section 3.1 states, “Here we do not utilize the dependency relation labels in the experiments for better generalization, because the labels may not be always available for different knowledge resources.” This seems like a poor design choice. Dependency relation *labels* provide valuable semantic information that should be leveraged if available. Dismissing them outright hinders potential performance gains.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation:** The problem addressed – incorporating external knowledge into NLU – is a relevant and important one. The authors correctly identify the limitations of simply treating RNNs as “flat” structures.

2. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The related work section provides a reasonably thorough overview of prior research in knowledge-based NLU and attention mechanisms.

3. **End-to-End Approach:** The end-to-end nature of the proposed model is a positive aspect, although its benefits are not clearly demonstrated.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a detailed justification for the selection of hyperparameters used in your experiments.
2.  Could you clarify the meaning of "max #Substructure" in Table 2 and explain its relationship to the observed performance?
3.  What specific computational advantages does K-SAN offer in terms of parallelizability compared to standard RNN-based approaches? Provide evidence.
4.  Why was the decision made to exclude dependency relation labels during knowledge encoding? Could you provide experiments that demonstrate its benefits for generalization?
5.  Please provide the full results of the statistical significance testing, including specific p-values, for all comparisons.



**Additional Comments:**

The paper requires substantial revision to address the identified weaknesses. A more concise presentation, more rigorous experimentation, and a clearer articulation of the novel contributions are essential for acceptance. The current version does not meet the standards for publication in ACL.
