This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken language understanding, specifically slot filling in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The authors aim to move beyond simply using knowledge bases as feature sources and instead integrate knowledge directly into the model's attention mechanism. The core idea is to leverage external knowledge (dependency parses in this work) to guide attention over different substructures of the input utterance, improving sequence tagging performance.

The primary contribution of this work is the *concept* of explicitly guiding attention mechanisms with external knowledge structures within a neural network for sequence tagging. While attention mechanisms are commonplace, and knowledge integration into NLU is not novel, the idea of *using* knowledge to dictate *where* the network focuses its attention is a potentially impactful direction. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of this approach on a standard NLU task (ATIS) showing improvements, particularly in low-data scenarios. The experiments with different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) is also a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The core idea – knowledge-guided attention – is genuinely interesting. It moves beyond feature engineering with knowledge and proposes a more integrated approach. This is the strongest point of the paper.
2. **Strong Results in Low-Data Settings:** The reported performance gains, especially when training data is limited, are encouraging. This suggests the model is effectively leveraging external knowledge to compensate for insufficient training examples. This is a crucial benefit for real-world applications where labeled data is often scarce.
3. **Generalizability Across Knowledge Sources:** Demonstrating that the approach works with different types of knowledge structures (dependency trees and AMR graphs) strengthens the claim that K-SAN isn't tied to a specific knowledge representation.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the K-SAN architecture is clearly explained with the help of the figure. The experimental setup is sufficiently detailed.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Attention:** While the paper *claims* knowledge guides attention, there’s insufficient analysis to demonstrate *how* or *why* this guidance is effective. Visualizations (like Figure 5) are a good start, but more quantitative analysis of the attention weights is needed. Specifically, are attention weights on knowledge-aligned spans significantly higher? How does attention change with different knowledge sources?
2. **Dependence on Parser Quality:** The effectiveness of K-SAN is fundamentally tied to the accuracy of the external knowledge source (e.g., the dependency parser). The paper acknowledges this, but doesn't adequately address the impact of parsing errors. The example in Figure 5 *shows* a parsing error, yet the model still benefits. Is the model robust to such errors, or are improvements largely contingent on high-quality parsing?
3. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Attention Mechanisms:** The baseline comparisons are somewhat dated. While relevant, comparing K-SAN against more recent, state-of-the-art attention mechanisms (e.g., transformers, or more sophisticated self-attention variants) would provide a stronger benchmark.
4. **Incremental Improvement on Large Datasets:** The gains on the large dataset are marginal. While statistically significant, the improvement is small, suggesting the benefits of knowledge-guided attention may diminish with sufficient training data.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the attention weights? Specifically, can you quantify the degree to which knowledge-aligned spans receive higher attention compared to non-aligned spans?
2. How robust is K-SAN to errors in the external knowledge source (e.g., parsing errors)? Have you experimented with noisy knowledge or different parsing tools?
3. How do the results compare when K-SAN is benchmarked against more recent attention mechanisms, such as transformers?
4. Could you elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the weighting factor α in the joint RNN tagger)? Were these values carefully tuned, and how sensitive are the results to these choices?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper's strength lies in its conceptual novelty. The authors have identified a promising direction for integrating knowledge into neural NLU models. While the experimental validation isn’t exhaustive, the preliminary results are encouraging. I believe the paper warrants acceptance, despite its flaws, as the core idea is significant and could stimulate further research. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses raised above in future work.
