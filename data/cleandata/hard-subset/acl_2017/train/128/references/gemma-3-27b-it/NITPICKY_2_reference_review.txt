The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue state tracking. The core idea is to integrate external knowledge, specifically dependency parse trees, into a recurrent neural network framework using an attention mechanism. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency due to separate substructure modeling.

While the idea of incorporating knowledge is reasonable, and the experiments demonstrate some improvement over baselines, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues that collectively warrant rejection. The presented work feels incremental and lacks substantial novelty, and the execution is riddled with inconsistencies and a concerning lack of rigor.

**Strengths:**

1. **Exploration of Knowledge Integration:** The attempt to leverage external knowledge sources (dependency parsing) within a neural network architecture for NLU is a valid research direction. This is, however, a well-trodden path, and the specific approach doesn’t represent a significant paradigm shift.
2. **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct experiments on the ATIS dataset, providing a baseline for comparison. The inclusion of different training data sizes is a good practice for demonstrating robustness.
3. **Attention Visualization:** The visualization of attention weights (Figure 5) is a helpful attempt to provide insight into the model’s behavior, although the chosen example feels somewhat cherry-picked and doesn't demonstrate consistent behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core concept of incorporating structural information into neural networks is not new. Previous work (extensively cited in the Related Work section) already explores similar ideas using tree-based RNNs and dependency-based CNNs. The authors fail to convincingly demonstrate how K-SAN fundamentally differs from or significantly improves upon these existing approaches beyond the attention mechanism, which is a standard component in modern neural networks.
2. **Inconsistent Experimental Setup & Reporting:** The table comparing K-SAN to baselines (Table 1) reports F1-scores with varying numbers of decimal places, creating a sense of imprecision. More critically, the "Encoder" column is ambiguous. What exactly does "(NN)", "(RNN)", and "(CNN)" refer to? Is this the encoder *within* Mkg and Min, or something else? This is not clearly explained. The description of the hyperparameters is also insufficiently detailed.
3. **Overstated Generalization Claim:** The claim of "generalization for different knowledge" feels unsupported. The experiments primarily focus on dependency parsing. While mentioning AMR in the Knowledge Generalization section, the results are presented without sufficient detail or justification for the choice of parsers used. The performance differences across different parsers (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet, rule-based vs. JAMR) are not adequately analyzed.
4. **Questionable Significance of Improvements:** The performance gains reported in Table 1 are often marginal, especially on the larger dataset. Many improvements are statistically insignificant, or the significance is only indicated by a superscript "†" without a clear explanation of the statistical test used or p-value reported. The authors state that the improvement is "more significant" for smaller datasets, but this simply suggests the model is benefitting more from the regularization effect of the knowledge rather than genuinely learning better representations.
5. **Poorly Defined Substructures:** The process of defining "substructures" from the dependency tree is not fully explained. The paper states non-leaf nodes don't have corresponding substructures, but the rationale for this decision isn’t clear. This arbitrary choice raises concerns about the robustness and consistency of the approach.
6. **Formatting and Clarity Issues:** The paper contains several minor formatting issues and instances of awkward phrasing, hindering readability. For example, the figure captions are sometimes incomplete. The repeated use of "e.g." without subsequent examples is distracting.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the key differences between K-SAN and existing tree-based RNN/CNN models for NLU? Specifically, how does the attention mechanism fundamentally improve performance beyond simply adding another layer of complexity?
2.  Please clarify the role of the "(NN)", "(RNN)", and "(CNN)" labels in the "Encoder" column of Table 1. What exactly is being encoded with each of these models?
3.  What statistical test was used to determine statistical significance (indicated by "†"), and what were the corresponding p-values?
4.  Why were non-leaf nodes excluded from substructure definition? How does this impact the model’s performance, and have you experimented with including them?
5.  Provide a detailed justification for the choice of parsers used for dependency and AMR representations and explain why their performance is comparable.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are not formatted consistently.
*   The writing quality could be significantly improved with careful editing.
*   The introduction could benefit from a more focused discussion of the challenges in NLU and how K-SAN specifically addresses them.



In conclusion, while the paper tackles a relevant problem, the lack of substantial novelty, inconsistencies in the experimental setup, and the marginal improvements over existing methods make it unsuitable for acceptance in its current form. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above would be required for reconsideration.
