- Proposes K-SAN, an attention-based RNN model that uses external structural knowledge (dependency parses) for NLU in spoken dialogue systems.  
- Claims end-to-end learning, generalization across knowledge types, and efficiency; shows improvements on ATIS, especially with limited data.

- Introduces a principled attention-based method to integrate structural knowledge into sequence tagging rather than treating it as extra features.  
- Provides empirical validation on ATIS with consistent improvements and notable gains on small-data settings.  
- Shows comparable performance using different knowledge representations (different parsers, AMR), suggesting flexibility.

- Clearly motivates the need to move beyond feature enrichment and defines the problem well.  
- Uses a solid experimental setup with standard dataset, strong baselines, and ablation studies.  
- Includes effective attention visualizations demonstrating meaningful and stable focus on substructures.  
- Evaluates different encoders (NN, RNN, CNN) for knowledge representation.  
- Demonstrates robustness and clear benefits when training data is scarce.

- Core components (RNNs, attention, dependency parsing) are not novel; novelty lies in their specific integration.  
- Model performance depends on parser quality; noisy parses can hurt results and mitigation strategies are needed.  
- Claims of generalization to “any” knowledge type are overstated given experiments focus on structured representations.  
- Lacks detailed error analysis identifying remaining model failure modes.

- How sensitive is K-SAN to dependency parsing errors, and have confidence-weighting or mitigation techniques been tried?  
- What criteria or heuristics should guide selection among knowledge sources (dependency trees vs. AMR) for a task?  
- Can K-SAN be adapted to unstructured knowledge (e.g., free text), and what modifications or challenges would that entail?  
- Please provide a more detailed computational-cost comparison with baselines, especially as sequence length grows.

- Related work coverage is comprehensive and the paper is well-written.  
- Recommend adding a limitations discussion and future directions, such as integration with pre-trained models (e.g., BERT).
