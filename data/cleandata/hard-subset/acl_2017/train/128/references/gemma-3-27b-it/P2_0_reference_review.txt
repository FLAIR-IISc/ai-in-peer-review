## Review for "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

**Summary:** This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural network architecture designed to incorporate external knowledge (specifically, dependency parse trees, but extensible to other knowledge sources) into spoken language understanding (SLU) tasks, specifically domain classification, intent determination, and slot filling. The core idea is to use knowledge to *guide* attention mechanisms within an RNN framework, effectively allowing the model to focus on relevant substructures within an utterance. The authors claim contributions in end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Integration of Structural Knowledge:** The paper demonstrates a compelling method for integrating structural knowledge, represented as dependency parses, into a neural network architecture for SLU.  The attention mechanism guided by this structure demonstrably improves performance, particularly in low-resource settings.
2. **Demonstrated Generalization across Knowledge Sources:** The experiments showing comparable performance with different knowledge sources (dependency trees from different parsers, and AMR graphs) highlight the flexibility and potential robustness of the K-SAN approach.
3. **Practical SLU Improvement:**  The empirical results on the ATIS dataset show consistent improvements over strong baselines, showcasing the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The paper consistently outperforms several baselines, including strong neural network architectures. The gains are particularly noticeable in the "Small" data setting, demonstrating the benefit of knowledge guidance when data is limited.  This is a critical advantage in real-world SLU applications where labeled data is often scarce.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of previous approaches that treat knowledge as merely additional features, leading to potential error propagation and generalization issues. The idea of using knowledge to *guide* attention is a logical and effective solution.
3. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a thorough explanation of the K-SAN architecture, including the knowledge encoding module, the attention mechanism, and the RNN tagger. The diagrams are helpful in understanding the model’s flow.  The ablation studies through different knowledge encoders (NN, RNN, CNN) are valuable.
4. **Robustness to Knowledge Source:** The ability to use different knowledge representations (dependency trees from different parsers and AMR graphs) highlights the flexibility of the approach and suggests it's not overly reliant on the quality of any single knowledge source.
5. **Attention Visualization:** The visualization of attention weights (Figure 5) provides valuable insight into *how* the model is utilizing the knowledge, building confidence in its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of knowledge-guided attention and RNNs is well-executed, the individual components (RNNs, attention mechanisms, dependency parsing) are not novel. The primary contribution lies in the synergistic integration of these existing techniques. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but should be acknowledged.
2. **Dependence on Parsing Accuracy:** Although the authors show robustness to *different* knowledge sources, the performance is still inherently tied to the quality of the initial parsing.  Errors in the dependency parse tree will propagate through the model, potentially diminishing its benefits. The discussion around this is limited.  It would be beneficial to analyze performance on examples where parsing is known to be incorrect.
3. **ATIS Dataset Limitation:** The evaluation is limited to the ATIS dataset. While ATIS is a common benchmark, it is a relatively simple domain. It would be helpful to see results on more complex and diverse datasets to assess the generalizability of K-SAN.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but doesn’t provide much detail about the process.  A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Parsing Error Analysis:** Could you provide some analysis of the impact of parsing errors on the performance of K-SAN? Specifically, can you identify examples where incorrect parsing leads to degraded performance, and discuss potential strategies to mitigate this issue (e.g., incorporating uncertainty into the attention mechanism)?
2. **Computational Cost:** While the paper claims efficiency, a more quantitative comparison of training and inference time with the baselines would be helpful.
3. **Generalization to Other Knowledge Types:** You mention the model can generalize to different types of knowledge.  Could you elaborate on the challenges and potential modifications needed to integrate other knowledge sources beyond dependency trees and AMR graphs (e.g., knowledge graphs like Wikidata or Freebase)?
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:**  Could you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process? What search space was used, and what metrics were prioritized?




**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sentences are a bit dense.
* The related work section is comprehensive and provides good context for the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of spoken language understanding. The K-SAN architecture is a clever and effective way to integrate external knowledge into a neural network model, leading to improved performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the synergistic integration and the demonstrated generalization are significant.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
