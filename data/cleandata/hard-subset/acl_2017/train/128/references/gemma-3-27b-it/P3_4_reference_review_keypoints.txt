- Proposes K-SAN: a knowledge-guided structural attention network that uses dependency/AMR structures to guide attention for intent detection and slot filling.  
- Claims three contributions: end-to-end learned knowledge-guided attention, generalization across knowledge sources, and efficiency/parallelizability.  
- Novelty lies in integrating structural knowledge via attention rather than hand-engineered features.  
- Shows consistent improvements on the ATIS benchmark, especially pronounced with smaller training datasets.  
- Demonstrates adaptability across different knowledge sources/parsers (dependency trees, AMR).  
- Clear motivation highlighting limitations of traditional NLU pipelines and need for knowledge-aware methods.  
- Architecture is well-designed and modular, using attention to weight important knowledge-guided substructures.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive with multiple baselines, ablations, and varying dataset sizes.  
- Gains suggest K-SAN is data-efficient and beneficial when labeled data is scarce.  
- Core components (RNNs, attention, parsing) are not individually novel; main novelty is their combination and application.  
- Performance depends on parser quality; parsing errors can propagate through the system.  
- Lacks detailed ablation on hyperparameters of knowledge encoders (embedding size, depth, etc.).  
- Missing qualitative error analysis and concrete examples of K-SAN successes/failures.  
- Asks how K-SAN compares or complements pre-trained models like BERT/ELMo.  
- Requests strategies for mitigating parser error propagation and learning to down-weight unreliable parses.  
- Requests quantitative comparison of computational cost versus baseline RNNs despite claimed parallelizability.  
- Requests example instances where K-SAN outperforms baselines with explanations.  
- Minor presentation notes: some sparse figure captions; overall clear writing and helpful encoder ablation.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper, recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.
