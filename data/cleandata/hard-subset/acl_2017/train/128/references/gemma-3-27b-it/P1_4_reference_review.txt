## Peer Review: Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

**Summary:**

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue state tracking, aiming to improve natural language understanding by incorporating external knowledge into a neural network architecture.  The core idea is to use knowledge (represented as dependency trees in the experiments) to guide the attention mechanism, allowing the model to focus on relevant substructures within the input utterance. The authors claim three main contributions: 1) end-to-end learning of attention guided by knowledge; 2) the ability to generalize to different knowledge types; and 3) efficiency and parallelizability due to the structural approach.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical framework for integrating structured knowledge into a sequence tagging model:** The paper presents a concrete and relatively straightforward approach to incorporating dependency parse information into a neural network for NLU.  While not entirely novel (see weaknesses), it's a well-executed integration that demonstrates a clear benefit.
2. **Empirical validation on a standard NLU dataset:** The authors thoroughly evaluate K-SAN on the ATIS dataset, demonstrating improvements over strong baselines. The ablation studies related to knowledge source (dependency parser) and encoder types (NN, RNN, CNN) are valuable.
3. **Demonstration of generalization across knowledge sources:** Showing comparable performance with different parsers (Stanford and SyntaxNet) and a different knowledge representation (AMR graphs) provides evidence for the claim of knowledge-agnosticism.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper effectively outlines the challenges of NLU, particularly the need to leverage external knowledge to address data sparsity and improve generalization. The limitations of previous feature-based approaches are clearly articulated.
2. **Sound Methodology:**  The K-SAN architecture is logically structured and reasonably well-explained. The use of attention mechanisms, guided by knowledge-derived substructures, is a plausible approach to improve sequence tagging.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on the ATIS dataset consistently show performance gains over competitive baselines, especially with smaller training sets. This suggests that the knowledge-guided approach is particularly beneficial when data is limited.
4. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors conducted several experiments varying the encoder type and knowledge source, providing a solid empirical foundation for their claims.  The ablation studies are particularly helpful in understanding the contributions of different components.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The figures are informative and aid in understanding the proposed architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of incorporating linguistic structure into neural networks isn't entirely novel. Several prior works (cited in the paper) have explored similar avenues, such as dependency-based CNNs and tree-structured RNNs.  The main contribution feels more like a refined implementation and thorough empirical evaluation rather than a breakthrough idea.
2. **Dependency on Parser Accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN is intrinsically tied to the quality of the dependency parser. While the authors show robustness across different parsers, parsing errors can still propagate and negatively impact the results.  This dependence isn't adequately discussed. A more in-depth analysis of the impact of parsing errors would strengthen the paper.
3. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** While quantitative results are presented, the paper lacks a deeper qualitative analysis of *how* K-SAN makes its decisions. Providing examples of attention weights for specific utterances would help illustrate the benefits of knowledge guidance and provide insights into the model’s behavior.
4. **Limited Scope of Knowledge Sources:** Although the paper shows the flexibility of using different knowledge sources, the experiments are primarily focused on syntactic information (dependency trees and AMR). Exploring other forms of knowledge (e.g., knowledge graphs) could further broaden the applicability of K-SAN.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Parser Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of parsing errors on the performance of K-SAN? Did you observe any patterns in the types of errors that had the most significant impact?
2. **Attention Visualization:** Can you provide examples of attention weights learned by K-SAN for different utterances, highlighting how the model focuses on relevant substructures based on the knowledge guidance?
3. **Computational Complexity:** While you mention parallelizability, can you provide a more concrete analysis of the computational complexity of K-SAN compared to standard RNN-based models, especially as the sentence length increases?
4. **Knowledge Representation Exploration:**  Have you explored alternative knowledge representations beyond dependency trees and AMR graphs, such as knowledge graphs (e.g., Wikidata, ConceptNet)?  If so, what were the results? If not, why not?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The notation in the paper could be slightly improved for clarity (e.g., consistently using subscripts for indices).
* The references could be more thoroughly checked for formatting consistency.
* The link to SyntaxNet in Table 2 is broken and should be corrected.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of spoken dialogue state tracking. While the core idea isn't entirely novel, the authors provide a practical framework for integrating structured knowledge into a neural network and demonstrate its effectiveness through comprehensive experiments.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
