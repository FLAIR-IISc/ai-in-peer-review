This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken language understanding (SLU), specifically targeting domain classification, intent determination, and slot filling. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge, such as dependency parses or AMR graphs, to guide attention mechanisms within a recurrent neural network framework. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization across different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability.

I view the primary contribution as an attempt to integrate structural knowledge into a neural network architecture for SLU. The exploration of different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) is a positive aspect, demonstrating a degree of flexibility. The secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that incorporating *some* structural knowledge can improve performance, particularly in low-data scenarios. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Results:** The paper provides a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation on the ATIS dataset. The results consistently show improvements over strong baselines, particularly with limited training data. The performance gains, while not dramatic, are statistically significant and suggest a benefit from the knowledge integration.
2. **Exploration of Knowledge Sources:** The authors investigate both syntactic (dependency trees) and semantic (AMR graphs) knowledge sources, demonstrating the adaptability of the K-SAN framework. The relatively consistent performance across these sources strengthens the argument that the architecture is robust to the specific type of knowledge used.
3. **Attention Visualization:** The visualization of attention weights (Figure 5) is helpful in understanding how the model is leveraging the structural information. It suggests that the model is, in some cases, focusing on relevant portions of the input sentence based on the provided knowledge. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the integration of knowledge is a reasonable direction, the fundamental approach – using attention mechanisms within an RNN – is not novel. Many previous works have explored attention for SLU and knowledge integration in NLP. The key innovation, the *knowledge-guided* attention, feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The authors do not convincingly demonstrate why this approach is fundamentally superior to simply including structural features as input to a standard RNN.
2. **Dependence on Parser Quality:** The performance of K-SAN is intrinsically linked to the accuracy of the underlying parser (dependency or AMR). The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t adequately address the potential for error propagation from imperfect parses. The example in Figure 5 highlights an incorrectly parsed sentence, yet the model still appears to benefit from the (flawed) structure. This raises concerns about the robustness of the approach. A more thorough analysis of the impact of parsing errors would be valuable.
3. **Insufficient Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The baseline models, while adequate, are not state-of-the-art. Comparing against more recent and powerful SLU models (e.g., transformer-based models) would provide a clearer picture of the K-SAN’s relative performance and its true contribution. The claim of “state-of-the-art” performance feels overstated given the baseline choices.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of each component of K-SAN. For example, how much does the knowledge encoding module contribute? How sensitive is the performance to the choice of RNN cell type (GRU vs. LSTM)? How does the performance vary with different attention mechanisms?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to the accuracy of the dependency parser or AMR parser? Can you provide a quantitative analysis of the impact of parsing errors on the final slot filling accuracy?
2.  Could you compare K-SAN against more recent and powerful SLU models, such as those based on transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa)?
3.  Please provide a more detailed ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component of K-SAN, including the knowledge encoding module, the attention mechanism, and the choice of RNN cell type.
4.  Why are the dependency/AMR relations *not* used as labels in the training process? Would incorporating these labels improve performance?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and well-organized. The presentation of the model architecture (Figure 2) is helpful. The paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of related work, particularly regarding attention mechanisms and knowledge integration in NLP.
