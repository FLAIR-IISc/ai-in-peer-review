This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for natural language understanding in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge, such as dependency parses or AMR graphs, as guidance for an attention mechanism within a recurrent neural network, aiming to improve sequence tagging performance (intent determination and slot filling). The authors claim three main contributions: (1) end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance via attention, (2) generalization across different knowledge types, and (3) efficiency and parallelizability due to separate modeling of substructures.

**Contributions:** The most significant contribution of this work is the exploration of integrating structural knowledge into a neural network attention mechanism for sequence tagging in NLU. While previous work has used knowledge as features, this paper’s approach of using knowledge to *guide* attention is a notable distinction. The demonstration that this approach works with different knowledge representations (dependency parses and AMR graphs) is a valuable empirical result. Finally, the extensive experimentation and comparisons against multiple baselines provide a solid evaluation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Knowledge Guidance:** The primary strength lies in the novel approach of using knowledge to *guide* the attention mechanism. This shifts away from simply adding knowledge as features and towards a more integrated learning process where the network learns which parts of the input, informed by knowledge, are most important.
2. **Generalization Across Knowledge Sources:** The ability to utilize both dependency parses and AMR graphs demonstrates the flexibility and generalizability of the K-SAN framework. This is a key advantage, as access to specific, high-quality knowledge sources can vary. The experiments clearly show that the method isn't tied to a single type of knowledge representation.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation, comparing K-SAN against a variety of strong baselines, including CRFs, RNNs, CNNs, and other structural approaches. The ablation studies and the analysis with different dataset sizes (Small, Medium, Large) provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in low-resource scenarios.
4. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the K-SAN architecture. The diagrams (Figures 2 and 3) are helpful in understanding the knowledge encoding and attention mechanisms.
5. **Empirical Results:** The achieved performance is consistently better than the baseline methods on all dataset sizes. The result on the small dataset is particularly convincing, implying the usefulness of incorporating external knowledge when data is limited.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the paper presents a visualization of the attention weights in Figure 5, the analysis of these weights is limited. A more in-depth discussion of *what* the model is attending to and *why* would strengthen the paper. For example, analyzing attention patterns for specific slot types or challenging examples could reveal valuable insights into the model’s behavior.
2. **Dependency on Parser Accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently dependent on the accuracy of the external parser (dependency or AMR). While the authors acknowledge this, the impact of parsing errors is not thoroughly investigated. Experimenting with noisy or imperfect parses would provide a more realistic assessment of the model’s robustness.
3. **Complexity:** The K-SAN architecture is relatively complex compared to some of the baselines. While the efficiency claim is made, the overhead of the knowledge encoding and attention mechanism isn't explicitly quantified. A more detailed analysis of the computational cost would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Transformers:** The comparisons lack recent state-of-the-art transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) which are commonly used in NLU tasks. Including such a comparison would put the performance of K-SAN into better context.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the attention weights learned by K-SAN? Specifically, what types of substructures (dependency relations, AMR concepts) receive the most attention for different slot types?
2. How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to errors in the external parser (dependency or AMR)? Have you experimented with noisy or imperfect parses to assess the model's robustness?
3. Can you provide a quantitative comparison of the computational cost (training time, inference time) of K-SAN with the baseline methods and, ideally, with a transformer-based model?
4. Are there specific cases where the K-SAN model fails, and can these failures be attributed to limitations in the knowledge representation or the attention mechanism? Providing example failures would be helpful.

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list appears to be incomplete. Some references are mentioned in the text but do not have complete bibliographic information.
* It would be helpful to include a discussion of the limitations of the K-SAN approach and potential directions for future research. For example, exploring alternative knowledge representations or attention mechanisms.
