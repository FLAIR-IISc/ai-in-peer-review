## Peer Review: Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel neural network architecture, Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), designed to improve natural language understanding (NLU) for goal-oriented spoken dialogue systems. The core idea is to integrate external knowledge, specifically linguistic structures obtained via dependency parsing (though adaptable to other knowledge sources), into an attention mechanism that guides the network’s focus during sequence tagging (intent determination and slot filling). The authors claim three main contributions: 1) end-to-end learning with knowledge as guidance, 2) generalization to different types of knowledge, and 3) efficiency and parallelizability.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Integration of Structural Knowledge:** The paper demonstrates a reasonably effective way to integrate structural knowledge from dependency parsing (and potentially other sources) into a neural network for slot filling. The attention mechanism guided by this knowledge appears to improve performance, particularly with limited training data.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a fairly thorough experimental evaluation on the ATIS dataset, including comparisons to several strong baselines and ablation studies. The experiments across different data sizes are particularly valuable.
3. **Demonstrated Generalizability of Knowledge Source:** The results demonstrate that the approach is not tied to a specific knowledge source, with comparable performance using different parsing tools and semantic graph representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Performance Gains, Especially in Low-Resource Settings:** The experimental results consistently show that K-SAN outperforms baseline models, particularly when training data is scarce. This is a significant benefit for real-world applications where labeled data is often limited. The gains are statistically significant in several experiments (indicated by the '†' notation), strengthening this argument.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches (feature enrichment being prone to error propagation) and convincingly motivate their proposed solution. The analogy of "knowledge as a teacher" is effective in conveying the core idea.
3. **Flexibility of Knowledge Integration:** The demonstrated ability to utilize different forms of knowledge (dependency trees, AMR graphs, different parsers) is a strong point. This makes the framework adaptable to a variety of scenarios and resources. The ablation study regarding parser choice confirms this.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The comparison to a comprehensive set of baselines, including both traditional and recent neural network approaches, provides a strong validation of the proposed model. The systematic variation in data size adds further robustness to the evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination of structural attention and knowledge integration is effective, the individual components (attention mechanisms, RNNs, dependency parsing) are well-established. The novelty lies primarily in the specific way these components are combined, and the contribution in that regard feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Dependence on Parser Accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently tied to the accuracy of the external parser. While the experiments demonstrate robustness to *different* parsers, the results don't adequately address the impact of parsing *errors*.  The example in Figure 5 illustrates a case of incorrect parsing, yet the model still benefits, but the extent of this benefit isn’t quantified. A more detailed analysis of performance in relation to parsing accuracy would be valuable.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Limited Analysis:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but provides limited details about the process.  It would be helpful to understand the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters, particularly those related to the attention mechanism. The effect of α in the joint RNN tagger is not discussed in depth.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, there is a lack of detailed error analysis. Identifying specific types of errors that K-SAN still struggles with would provide valuable insights for future work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Parser Error Impact:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of how parsing accuracy affects the performance of K-SAN? Specifically, have you explored the correlation between parsing error rate and slot filling accuracy?
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:**  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process?  What ranges were explored for the key hyperparameters, and how was the best configuration selected?
3. **Ablation Study - Attention Mechanism:**  What is the impact of removing the knowledge-guided attention mechanism entirely?  What performance degradation is observed, and how does this compare to simply using the sentence embedding without attention?
4. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide examples of errors that K-SAN still makes, and discuss the potential reasons for these errors? Are there specific types of slots or utterances that are particularly challenging?

**Additional Comments:**

* The figures are generally clear and helpful, but could benefit from slightly larger fonts for readability.
* The references are correctly formatted.
* The writing is clear and concise.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of spoken language understanding. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the effectiveness of the approach, combined with the thorough evaluation and demonstrated generalizability, make it a worthwhile addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions raised above.
