## Review: Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

**Summary:**

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel neural network architecture designed to incorporate external knowledge into spoken dialogue understanding (specifically, semantic slot filling). The core idea is to use knowledge (represented as dependency parse trees in the experiments) to guide attention within the network, focusing on relevant substructures within the input utterance. The model aims to address limitations of previous approaches that treat knowledge as mere features, instead integrating it directly into the learning process.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Integration of Structural Knowledge:** The paper presents a compelling method for directly incorporating structural knowledge (dependency parses) into a neural network architecture for semantic slot filling. This is achieved through an attention mechanism that learns to weight different substructures based on their relevance to the task.
2. **Demonstrated Generalization Across Knowledge Sources:** The authors successfully demonstrate that the proposed framework is adaptable to different knowledge representations (dependency trees, potentially others), showcasing its flexibility and broader applicability.
3. **Empirical Validation and State-of-the-Art Results:** The paper provides solid experimental results on the ATIS dataset, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance, particularly in low-data scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty & Insightful Approach:**  The central idea of using knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention is novel and insightful.  It addresses a common problem in NLU where simply adding knowledge as features can be suboptimal due to feature engineering and error propagation.  The K-SAN architecture offers a more elegant and potentially more robust way to leverage external knowledge.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results are convincing. The improvements over strong baselines, particularly in the low-data settings, demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed approach.  The ablation studies in Table 2 highlighting the robustness to different knowledge sources are also very strong.
3. **Well-Motivated and Clearly Presented:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation behind the work, the proposed architecture, and the experimental setup.  The diagrams (Figures 2 and 3) are particularly helpful in understanding the model.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation using multiple dataset sizes and explore the use of different knowledge sources, lending credibility to their findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependency on Parsing Accuracy:** While the authors acknowledge this, the performance of K-SAN is still fundamentally tied to the quality of the dependency parsing.  Errors in the parse tree can introduce noise and potentially mislead the attention mechanism. The results don't show a comprehensive sensitivity analysis to parsing errors.
2. **Limited Exploration of Knowledge Types:** Although the authors demonstrate generalization to different parsers, the paper primarily focuses on dependency trees. Exploring other knowledge sources (e.g., knowledge graphs, ontologies) in more detail would further strengthen the work.  The impact of differing graph *density* or *quality* isn't explored.
3. **Complexity of Architecture:** K-SAN introduces additional complexity compared to simpler models. While the performance gains justify this complexity, a clearer analysis of the computational cost and scalability of the model would be beneficial.  Is the gain in performance worth the increase in training time and model size?
4. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** While quantitative results are strong, a qualitative analysis of the model's errors could provide valuable insights into its strengths and weaknesses.  What types of errors does K-SAN still make, and can these be attributed to limitations in the knowledge representation or the attention mechanism?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of parsing errors on the model's performance? Specifically, have you experimented with noisy or imperfect parse trees to assess the robustness of K-SAN?
2.  What is the computational cost of K-SAN compared to the baseline models, in terms of training time and inference time?
3.  Can you provide examples of cases where K-SAN significantly outperforms the baseline models, and explain how the knowledge-guided attention mechanism contributes to this improvement?
4.   You mention the possibility of using other types of knowledge. What are the main challenges you anticipate in adapting K-SAN to incorporate knowledge from a knowledge graph, for instance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-cited and relevant.
*   The code availability would be a valuable addition to the submission.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach to incorporating knowledge into neural network models for spoken dialogue understanding. The empirical results are convincing, and the paper is clearly written and well-organized. While there are some weaknesses, they are not critical, and the authors have the potential to address them in a revision. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
