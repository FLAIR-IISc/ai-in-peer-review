This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken language understanding, specifically slot filling in goal-oriented dialog systems. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge (dependency parses in the presented experiments) into a recurrent neural network through an attention mechanism, guiding the network to focus on relevant substructures of the input utterance. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability due to substructure modeling.

A primary contribution, as I see it, is the attempt to integrate structured knowledge into a neural network *without* relying solely on feature engineering. Many prior approaches treat external knowledge as additional features. K-SAN aims for a more direct integration via the attention mechanism, which is a conceptually sound approach. The empirical results demonstrate a modest but consistent improvement over strong baselines, suggesting the idea has merit. However, the magnitude of improvement doesn’t fully justify the complexity added.

The exploration of different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) and the demonstration of relatively consistent performance across them is a second, noteworthy contribution. This suggests the approach isn’t overly reliant on the specific type of knowledge used, increasing its potential applicability.

Finally, the extensive experimental evaluation on the ATIS dataset, with variations in training data size, provides a fairly thorough assessment of the model's performance and robustness. This is valuable for the community.

However, there are numerous weaknesses. First, the presentation is surprisingly verbose for a technical paper. The introduction repeats information and lacks conciseness. The description of the model (Section 3) feels excessively detailed, bordering on tedious, with many equations that could be simplified or moved to the appendix. 

Second, the claimed "efficiency and parallelizability" is not substantiated. The paper states modeling time *may* not increase linearly, but there's no evidence presented to support this claim – no timing results or analysis of computational cost. 

Third, the improvement over the baselines, while consistent, is not substantial, particularly on the larger datasets. The gains appear marginal given the increased model complexity. A more thorough ablation study is needed to understand which components of K-SAN are truly contributing to the performance gains. For instance, is the attention mechanism genuinely beneficial, or are the knowledge-guided substructures sufficient on their own?

Fourth, the experimental setup contains several inconsistencies. The number of substructures used varies significantly between experiments (53 vs. 19), without clear justification. The impact of this variation isn’t discussed. Also, the choice of hyperparameters (dropout rates, hidden layer sizes) is not adequately motivated. Were these values tuned systematically, or were they chosen arbitrarily?

Fifth, the paper lacks a critical discussion of limitations. The performance is heavily reliant on the accuracy of the external parsers. What happens when the parser makes errors? The authors briefly mention error propagation, but don't present any analysis of this issue.

Sixth, several minor formatting and stylistic issues are present throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, awkward phrasing, typos). While not major, these detract from the overall professionalism of the submission.

Finally, the visualization in Figure 5, while intended to illustrate attention weights, is difficult to interpret due to the cluttered layout and lack of clear labeling.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of K-SAN compared to the baseline models? Specifically, what is the runtime complexity, and how does it scale with the length of the input utterance?
2. Please provide a more rigorous ablation study, systematically evaluating the contribution of each component of K-SAN (e.g., attention mechanism, knowledge-guided substructures, different embedding methods).
3. How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to errors in the external parser? Can you quantify the impact of parser errors on the final results?
4. Please explain the rationale behind the choice of hyperparameters used in the experiments. Were these values tuned systematically?
5. Why does the number of substructures vary so significantly across experiments, and how does this variation affect the performance of the model?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is incomplete and contains several errors (e.g., missing author names, unclear publication venues).
*   The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of the connection between the attention mechanism and the concept of "knowledge as a teacher." The analogy feels somewhat weak.
* The use of (†) to denote statistically significant results is unconventional. A clearer indication of p-values would be preferable.



Overall, while the idea behind K-SAN is promising, the paper suffers from a lack of conciseness, insufficient analysis, and modest improvements over existing methods. The numerous minor flaws further detract from its overall quality. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above and provide a more compelling demonstration of the benefits of their approach before resubmission.
