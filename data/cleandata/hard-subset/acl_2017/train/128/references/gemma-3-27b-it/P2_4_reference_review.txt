## Review for "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural network architecture designed to incorporate external knowledge into spoken dialogue understanding (specifically, semantic slot filling). The core idea is to use dependency parsing (or other structured knowledge sources) to guide the attention mechanism within an RNN, allowing the model to focus on relevant substructures within the input utterance. The authors claim three main contributions: 1) end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, 2) generalization to different knowledge types, and 3) efficiency and parallelizability.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical approach to integrating structured knowledge into a sequence tagging model:** The paper provides a relatively straightforward and effective method for leveraging dependency parses (or similar structures) to improve slot filling performance. While knowledge integration isn’t novel *in concept*, the attention-guided mechanism offers a more elegant solution than simply using parse features.
2. **Demonstrated robustness across knowledge sources:** The experiments showing comparable performance with different knowledge types (dependency trees from different parsers, AMR graphs) are a positive signal, suggesting the method isn’t overly reliant on a specific knowledge format.
3. **Empirical results on a standard benchmark:** The paper provides a clear evaluation of K-SAN on the ATIS dataset, demonstrating improvements over strong baselines.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional NLU approaches and the potential benefits of incorporating external knowledge, particularly for handling unseen data. The motivation for addressing error propagation and poor generalization is strong.
2. **Effective Integration of Knowledge:** The attention mechanism guided by the knowledge structures is a well-designed and intuitive approach.  It allows the model to dynamically focus on potentially relevant parts of the input, rather than relying on pre-defined features.  The visualization in Figure 5 nicely illustrates this.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The K-SAN model consistently outperforms the baselines, particularly in the low-data scenarios, showcasing its ability to generalize. The gains are statistically significant as indicated in Table 1.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:**  The authors evaluate their model on a standard benchmark (ATIS) and perform ablation studies by experimenting with different knowledge sources (parsers and graph representations) demonstrating the flexibility of the proposed method. 
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The figures and tables are clear and informative.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Concepts:** While the *combination* of techniques is sensible, the individual components (RNNs, attention mechanisms, dependency parsing) are well-established. The novelty lies primarily in the specific way these components are integrated, which feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Dependency on Parsing Quality:** Although the paper attempts to address this with generalization across parsers, the performance of K-SAN is still inherently tied to the accuracy of the underlying parser.  The example in Figure 5 explicitly points out parsing errors; a more thorough analysis of the impact of parsing errors on performance would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems to vary depending on the encoder type (NN, RNN, CNN) and dataset size, suggesting the model is sensitive to hyperparameter tuning.  More discussion of the hyperparameter search and the reasons behind the differing performance would be helpful.
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent NLU Models:** The baseline models, while relevant, are somewhat dated.  A comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art NLU models (e.g., BERT-based approaches) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of K-SAN’s performance.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Parsing Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of how parsing errors affect the performance of K-SAN? Specifically, what types of parsing errors are most detrimental, and are there strategies to mitigate their impact?
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:**  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process?  What strategies were used, and how sensitive is the performance to the specific choices of hyperparameters for different encoder types?
3. **Computational Complexity:** While you mention parallelizability, can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of K-SAN compared to other models, particularly in terms of training and inference time?
4. **Comparison to BERT/Transformer-based approaches:** Have you experimented with incorporating K-SAN into a more modern architecture, such as one based on transformers (e.g. BERT)? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors should consider adding a discussion of the limitations of their approach, such as its dependence on the availability of high-quality parsers or knowledge bases.
*   The sentence "poor generalization comes from the mismatch between knowledge bases and the input data" is a bit vague. Providing a concrete example would be helpful.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of spoken dialogue understanding. While the individual components aren't particularly novel, the combination is effective and well-motivated. The empirical results are promising, and the authors demonstrate the robustness of their approach. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
