This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue natural language understanding (NLU). The core idea is to leverage external knowledge (dependency parsing in the presented experiments) to guide attention mechanisms within a recurrent neural network, aiming to improve slot filling performance, especially in low-data scenarios. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency due to parallelizable substructure modeling.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** Integrating external knowledge into neural NLU models is a crucial and ongoing research area. The problem of leveraging structured information beyond simple feature engineering is well-motivated, and the potential to improve generalization, particularly in data-scarce environments, is significant. 
2. **Novelty of the Approach:** The idea of using knowledge to *guide* attention within an RNN, rather than simply adding knowledge-derived features, is a valuable contribution. The framework offers a more integrated approach to knowledge incorporation, potentially allowing the model to learn more complex relationships between the utterance and the external knowledge source.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough evaluation on the standard ATIS dataset, with comparisons to several strong baselines, including both feature-based and neural approaches. The ablation study with different knowledge resources (dependency trees from different parsers and AMR graphs) further strengthens the validity of the results. The experiments varying training data size convincingly demonstrate the benefit of K-SAN in low-resource settings.
4. **Clear Presentation and Implementation Details:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the K-SAN architecture. The authors provide sufficient details regarding the implementation, allowing for potential reproducibility. The visualizations of the attention weights are helpful in understanding the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gain Over Strong Baselines:** While the results show improvement over many baselines, the overall performance gain, especially on the large dataset, is relatively modest. The improvement is more pronounced in the small and medium data regimes, suggesting the benefit is mainly derived from enhanced generalization rather than inherent representational power. A more detailed analysis of *where* K-SAN excels (e.g., specific slot types, challenging utterances) would be beneficial.
2. **Dependency on Parser Quality:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently linked to the accuracy of the external knowledge source (the dependency parser in this case). While the experiments show some robustness across different parsers, errors in parsing can negatively impact the model's performance. The paper doesn’t sufficiently address strategies for mitigating the impact of noisy or imperfect knowledge. The visualization in Figure 5 shows an example of incorrect parsing, and the authors acknowledge this, but further discussion is needed.
3. **Complexity of the Model:** K-SAN introduces additional complexity compared to standard RNN-based approaches. The benefits of this complexity need to be clearly justified, especially given the relatively modest performance gains observed.  A more detailed analysis of the computational cost and training time compared to simpler models would be helpful.
4. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Presenting examples of utterances where K-SAN significantly outperforms the baselines, and conversely, cases where it fails, would provide valuable insights into the model's strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper mentions the potential for error propagation from the knowledge source. Could you elaborate on potential methods for mitigating this issue, such as incorporating confidence scores from the parser or using a more robust knowledge representation?
2. How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to the choice of embedding dimension for the knowledge-guided substructures? Did you perform an ablation study on this parameter?
3. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost (training time, inference time) of K-SAN compared to the baseline RNN models?
4. Can you provide examples of failure cases where K-SAN performs worse than the baseline?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation in Section 3.2 could be made more consistent (e.g., clarifying the dimensionality of vectors).
*   The abbreviation “RNN” is used inconsistently throughout the paper (sometimes referring to a general type of network, other times to a specific model).
*   The reference list is very extensive and includes multiple references to the same authors. It could be streamlined.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising approach to integrating external knowledge into neural NLU models. The K-SAN architecture is novel and well-motivated. However, the relatively modest performance gains and the dependency on parser quality raise some concerns. Despite these weaknesses, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worth considering for publication. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
