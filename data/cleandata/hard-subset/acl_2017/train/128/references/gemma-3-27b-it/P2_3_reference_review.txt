## Review for "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural network architecture designed to incorporate external knowledge (specifically, dependency parse trees but generalized to other structured knowledge) into a slot filling pipeline for goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core idea is to use this knowledge to guide attention mechanisms, allowing the model to focus on salient substructures within the input utterance. The authors claim three main contributions: 1) end-to-end learning of knowledge-guided attention, 2) generality across different knowledge sources, and 3) efficiency through parallelization of substructure modeling.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical framework for integrating structural knowledge into a neural slot-filling model.** The paper provides a concrete implementation of how to leverage dependency parses (and potentially other structured information) within a deep learning framework for NLU, moving beyond simply using knowledge as additional features.
2. **Demonstrated effectiveness of knowledge guidance on a standard benchmark.** The experimental results on the ATIS dataset show consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly in low-data scenarios.
3. **Exploration of knowledge source generalization.** The authors show that the approach is robust to different parsers (Stanford vs SyntaxNet) and potentially different knowledge types (dependency trees vs AMR graphs) although the performance difference isn't dramatically shown.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches relying solely on large datasets and feature engineering, and presents a compelling argument for incorporating structured knowledge.
2. **Solid Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, utilizing a standard benchmark (ATIS) and comparing against strong baselines. The ablation studies and analysis across different dataset sizes demonstrate the benefits of the K-SAN approach, particularly its robustness to data scarcity.  The results show statistically significant improvements.
3. **Practical & Implementable Approach:** The proposed K-SAN architecture is relatively straightforward to understand and implement, and the authors provide sufficient detail for replication.  The ability to handle different knowledge formats (dependency parses, AMR graphs) is a practical advantage.
4. **Attention Visualization:** The inclusion of attention visualizations (Figure 5) provides valuable insight into how the model is leveraging the knowledge-guided structures, increasing confidence in the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of knowledge guidance and attention mechanisms is a valuable contribution, the individual components (RNNs, attention mechanisms, dependency parsing) are well-established in the literature. The paper doesn’t introduce fundamentally new algorithmic building blocks.
2. **Overstated Generalization Claim:** The claim of complete generality across *all* knowledge sources feels somewhat overstated. While the framework *can* accommodate different knowledge representations, the performance is dependent on the quality of the underlying knowledge.  Poorly parsed trees or inaccurate AMR graphs will likely degrade performance. More thorough testing with noisy/imperfect knowledge sources would strengthen this claim. The performance difference between AMR graphs and dependency trees is minimal.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results demonstrate improved performance, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the model still struggles (e.g., specific slot types, complex sentences) would help identify areas for future improvement.  



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding knowledge quality:** How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to errors in the underlying knowledge source (e.g., inaccurate dependency parses)? Have you investigated the impact of parse accuracy on the final slot filling performance?  Could a self-correcting mechanism for knowledge be integrated?
2. **Computational cost:**  You mention efficiency. Can you provide more concrete details about the computational overhead of K-SAN compared to a standard RNN-based slot filler (e.g., training time, inference time)?
3. **Beyond Dependency Parses:**  You mention AMR graphs. Have you experimented with other types of knowledge bases (e.g., knowledge graphs like Wikidata or ConceptNet)?  If so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

* The formatting of some figures (e.g., Figure 2) is a little cramped, making it difficult to read the labels.
* The introduction could be slightly more concise.  



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a practical and effective approach to incorporating structured knowledge into neural slot filling. While the individual components aren't groundbreaking, the combination and experimental validation demonstrate a worthwhile contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses mentioned above.
