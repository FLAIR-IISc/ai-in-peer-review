This paper proposes a Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Network (K-SAN) for spoken dialogue state tracking. The core idea is to incorporate external knowledge (dependency parsing in the presented experiments) to guide the attention mechanism within a recurrent neural network, aiming to improve sequence tagging performance (domain classification, intent determination, and slot filling). The authors claim three main contributions: (1) end-to-end learning of attention guided by knowledge, (2) generalization to different knowledge sources, and (3) efficiency and parallelizability due to separate modeling of substructures.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing Knowledge Integration:** The paper tackles a crucial problem in NLU: effectively leveraging external knowledge. Existing approaches often treat knowledge as additional features, which can suffer from generalization and error propagation issues. K-SAN attempts to integrate knowledge more directly into the learning process via attention, which is a promising direction.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on the ATIS dataset demonstrate consistently better performance than several strong baselines, including CNN-based and tree-structured RNN approaches. The improvements are particularly noticeable on smaller datasets, highlighting the benefits of knowledge integration when data is scarce. The ablation study regarding different knowledge encoders (NN, RNN, CNN) is well executed.
3. **Demonstrated Generalization:** The results with different parsing tools (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet) and knowledge formalisms (dependency trees vs. AMR graphs) showcase the model’s ability to generalize to diverse knowledge sources. This is a significant strength, as it reduces reliance on specific parsers or knowledge base formats.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Architecture:** While the knowledge-guided attention mechanism is a valuable contribution, the underlying RNN architecture is fairly standard. The novelty primarily lies in *how* knowledge is incorporated rather than a fundamentally new network design. This limits the overall impact of the work.
2. **Dependence on Parser Quality:** Although the paper demonstrates generalization across different parsers, the performance is still inherently tied to the accuracy of the external knowledge source. The analysis in Figure 5 shows the model functioning despite parser errors, but the model’s potential is clearly constrained by these errors. A more robust approach to handling noisy or incomplete knowledge would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Ablation on Attention Mechanism:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of K-SAN overall, it lacks a thorough ablation study specifically focused on the attention mechanism. A clearer understanding of *how* the knowledge-guided attention improves performance—e.g., by focusing on relevant words or relations—would strengthen the analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper states that the number of substructures may be less than the number of words in the utterance. What is the impact of this reduction on the model's performance? Is there a systematic way to determine an optimal threshold for including substructures?
2. The experiments primarily focus on the ATIS dataset. How well does K-SAN generalize to other NLU datasets with different characteristics (e.g., more complex sentence structures, less structured knowledge)?
3. The attention visualization in Figure 5 is helpful, but it doesn’t provide quantitative insights. Could you provide metrics (e.g., precision, recall) for the attention weights to quantify how accurately the model focuses on relevant substructures?

**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of related work is comprehensive and well-organized.
* The code availability would improve the reproducibility and impact of the research.
* Consider adding a section discussing the computational cost of K-SAN compared to other approaches.
