

# Peer Review for ACL

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel neural network architecture for natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. The key innovation is using external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses or semantic graphs) as a "teacher" to guide the attention mechanism of the model, rather than simply incorporating knowledge as additional features. The model learns to automatically focus on salient substructures within the input sentence that are most relevant for slot filling, addressing the error propagation problem common in previous knowledge-augmented approaches.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Novel attention guidance mechanism**: The paper presents a fundamentally different approach to incorporating external knowledge by using it to guide attention rather than as additional features. This addresses the error propagation issue in prior work where errors in knowledge extraction propagate to the NLU model.

2. **Demonstrated generalization across knowledge sources**: The authors effectively show that their model generalizes well across different types of knowledge representations (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring specific schema adaptation.

3. **Strong performance on small datasets**: The empirical results demonstrate that K-SAN particularly excels on smaller training datasets, showing better generalization capabilities than existing approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful problem in NLU**: The paper correctly identifies the error propagation problem in previous knowledge-augmented NLU approaches, where errors in knowledge extraction (e.g., from dependency parsers) propagate to the NLU model. This is a real issue in practical NLU systems, and the authors' solution of using knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention rather than as additional features is a meaningful contribution to addressing this problem.

2. **Strong empirical results on small datasets**: The paper demonstrates significant improvements, particularly on the Small dataset (1/40 of training data), where K-SAN achieves 74.60 F1 compared to the best baseline's 73.57. This shows the model's ability to generalize better with limited data, which is crucial for real-world applications where labeled data is scarce.

3. **Effective demonstration of model flexibility**: The experiments with different knowledge sources (dependency trees from Stanford and SyntaxNet, AMR graphs from rule-based parser and JAMR) convincingly show that K-SAN generalizes well across different knowledge representations without requiring major architectural changes.

4. **Clear visualization of attention mechanisms**: The attention visualization in Figure 5 effectively demonstrates that the model is learning to focus on relevant parts of the input, providing intuitive evidence that the proposed mechanism works as intended.

5. **Well-structured and clearly written**: The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the model architecture, experiments, and results. The comparison with relevant baselines is thorough, and the evaluation methodology is appropriate for the task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the attention mechanism**: While the paper presents a novel application of attention to NLU, the attention mechanism itself is not particularly innovative. The core idea of using an attention mechanism to weight different substructures is similar to existing approaches in other NLP tasks (e.g., attention in machine translation or sentence classification). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate K-SAN from other attention-based models, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the approach.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art models**: The paper compares against relatively older baselines (CRF, RNN, DCNN, Tree-RNN), but doesn't include comparisons with more recent models that also use attention mechanisms (e.g., transformer-based models, BERT, or other recent NLU architectures). This limits the assessment of the true standing of K-SAN in the current NLU landscape.

3. **Insufficient analysis of computational efficiency claims**: The authors claim that "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words," but they don't provide any runtime analysis, scalability tests, or comparisons with other approaches to substantiate this claim. Without this evidence, the claim remains unsubstantiated.

4. **Limited evaluation beyond ATIS**: The paper only evaluates on the ATIS benchmark dataset. While ATIS is a standard NLU benchmark, the paper doesn't address whether the approach generalizes to other domains or datasets. This is a significant limitation for a model claiming to be a general solution for NLU.

5. **Lack of analysis of error cases**: The paper mentions that the model can benefit from incorrect dependency parsing (as shown in Figure 5), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of error cases where the knowledge guidance is incorrect. This is important because the model's performance might be highly sensitive to the quality of the external knowledge source.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that K-SAN can "avoid error propagation" compared to previous approaches. However, the results in Table 2 show that K-SAN with Stanford dependency parsing (which may contain errors) still outperforms CRF with Stanford dependency parsing. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how K-SAN handles incorrect knowledge (e.g., from a faulty dependency parser) compared to other approaches?

2. The paper doesn't compare with recent transformer-based approaches for NLU. How would K-SAN's performance compare with state-of-the-art models like BERT or other transformer-based architectures on the ATIS dataset?

3. The paper claims "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words," but doesn't provide any evidence for this. Could you provide runtime analysis or scalability results showing how K-SAN's computational complexity compares to other approaches?

4. The paper uses only the ATIS dataset. Have you evaluated K-SAN on other NLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MultiWOZ) to demonstrate its generalizability beyond ATIS?

5. In Figure 5, the model appears to correctly identify the salient substructures even when the dependency parser makes errors. Could you provide more examples of this phenomenon and analyze when the model is able to overcome errors in the external knowledge source?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and presents a potentially useful approach to NLU. However, the novelty claim is somewhat overstated given that attention mechanisms are now standard in NLP. The authors would benefit from more clearly differentiating their approach from other attention-based models and providing a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods. The claim about efficiency would be much stronger with supporting evidence.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that addresses a real problem in NLU with a plausible solution, but the novelty and significance could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons and analysis.
