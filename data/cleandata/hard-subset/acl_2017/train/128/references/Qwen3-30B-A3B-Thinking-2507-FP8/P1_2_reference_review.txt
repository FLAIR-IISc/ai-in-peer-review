

# Peer Review: Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

## Summary

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural network architecture for Natural Language Understanding (NLU) tasks, particularly slot filling. The model integrates external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses, semantic graphs) to guide attention mechanisms when learning sentence representations. Instead of treating input as flat sequences, K-SAN identifies knowledge-guided substructures (e.g., paths from root to leaves in dependency trees) and learns to weigh these substructures through an attention mechanism. The resulting knowledge-guided representation is then used to improve slot tagging performance. The authors evaluate K-SAN on the ATIS dataset with different training data sizes and demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, particularly on smaller datasets. They also show the model's flexibility with different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Integrated knowledge-guided attention mechanism**: The paper's most significant contribution is the approach of using external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention in a more integrated, end-to-end manner rather than as additional features. This addresses the error propagation issue in prior knowledge-augmented NLU approaches.

2. **Robust performance with limited training data**: The model demonstrates particularly strong performance on smaller datasets (1/40 of training data), suggesting better generalization from limited data by leveraging structured external knowledge, which is a practical and valuable contribution for real-world NLU systems.

3. **Flexibility across different knowledge representations**: The model's ability to work effectively with various knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring specific schema adaptations demonstrates good generalizability and practical utility.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation with consistent improvements**: The paper demonstrates clear and consistent improvements over multiple baselines across different dataset sizes (Small, Medium, Large). The F1 improvements of 1-2% on Small, 1-2% on Medium, and 0.5-1% on Large datasets are statistically significant (p<0.05), which is particularly valuable for NLU systems that often operate with limited training data. The results on the Small dataset are especially compelling, showing the model's ability to generalize from scarce data.

2. **Practical and well-motivated approach**: The "knowledge as a teacher" concept is well-motivated by the limitations of prior work that used knowledge as additional features. The paper clearly articulates the problem of error propagation from incorrect feature extraction and demonstrates how K-SAN avoids this issue through its end-to-end learning approach.

3. **Demonstrated flexibility across knowledge sources**: The evaluation with different knowledge resources (Stanford dependency parser, SyntaxNet, rule-based AMR, JAMR) shows consistent performance improvements across all sources. This demonstrates the model's robustness and practical utility for different NLU applications without requiring knowledge schema adaptation.

4. **Insightful attention visualization**: Figure 5 provides valuable insights into how the model learns to focus on important substructures. The visualization shows that even with incorrect dependency parsing (as noted in the caption), the model still correctly identifies important semantic elements, which strongly supports the authors' claims about the model's robustness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work**: While the paper claims novelty in "knowledge as a teacher," the approach is quite similar to prior work on memory networks (Weston et al., 2015; Xiong et al., 2016) that use external knowledge to guide attention. The paper cites these works but doesn't sufficiently differentiate K-SAN's contribution. The core idea of using external knowledge to guide attention isn't sufficiently novel, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with these memory network approaches.

2. **Lack of comprehensive ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to understand what components of the model are most important. For example, it doesn't compare the performance when using only the knowledge-guided attention versus the full model, or how different knowledge encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN) affect performance. Without these, it's difficult to determine which aspects of the architecture are truly contributing to the improvements.

3. **Limited discussion of failure cases and sensitivity to knowledge quality**: The paper acknowledges that the dependency relations in Figure 5 were incorrectly parsed, but doesn't discuss how the model handles such cases or how sensitive it is to errors in the external knowledge source. This is a significant limitation, as real-world systems will often have imperfect external knowledge (e.g., from a dependency parser), and understanding the model's robustness to these errors is crucial for practical deployment.

4. **Comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares to relatively older baselines (e.g., CRF, Tree-RNN) but doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods for slot filling, such as transformer-based architectures or more advanced sequence labeling approaches. This makes it difficult to assess how competitive K-SAN is with the current state of the art.

5. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: While the paper provides strong empirical results, it lacks a strong theoretical justification for why the specific architecture (with the particular attention mechanism and combination of knowledge-guided structures) should work better than other approaches. The connection between the proposed architecture and the linguistic properties of the data could be more thoroughly discussed.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that they don't use dependency relation labels in the experiments. Why is this the case, and would using the labels improve performance? How would the model need to be adjusted to incorporate these labels?

2. The paper claims that K-SAN is "efficient and parallelizable" because substructures are modeled separately. However, the paper doesn't provide any computational complexity analysis or empirical evidence of speed improvements. Can the authors provide more details on the efficiency claims, such as training time comparisons with baselines?

3. The paper uses a dependency parser to generate knowledge-guided substructures. How sensitive is the model to errors in the dependency parsing? Would using a more accurate parser (e.g., SyntaxNet instead of Stanford) lead to better performance, and how much would the performance improve?

4. The paper mentions that the number of substructures from dependency trees (53) is larger than from AMR graphs (8). How does the model handle this difference in the number of substructures, and would it be beneficial to normalize the number of substructures across different knowledge sources?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and the experiments are well-designed, but the novelty claims need to be more carefully positioned against prior work on memory networks and attention mechanisms. The authors should more clearly articulate what makes K-SAN different from these prior approaches. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential directions for future work.

The attention visualization in Figure 5 is particularly strong and provides valuable insight into the model's internal workings. This is one of the paper's strongest elements, as it helps validate the authors' claims about the model's ability to learn meaningful attention from external knowledge.

The work is relevant and timely for NLU systems that need to handle limited training data and benefit from external knowledge sources, making it a potentially valuable contribution to the field. With some revisions to address the novelty concerns and provide more comprehensive analyses, this paper could make a strong contribution to ACL.
