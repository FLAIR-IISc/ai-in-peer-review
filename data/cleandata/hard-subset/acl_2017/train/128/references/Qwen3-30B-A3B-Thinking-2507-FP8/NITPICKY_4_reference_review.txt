

# ACL Review: Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a model for natural language understanding (NLU) that uses external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs) to guide attention mechanisms for sequence tagging. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning using knowledge as a teacher, generalization across different types of knowledge representations, and efficiency/parallelizability.

## Strengths

1. **Empirical results on small datasets**: The model shows significant improvements over baselines on the smallest training set (1/40 of data), suggesting better generalization capabilities when data is scarce, which is valuable for practical NLU applications.

2. **Multiple knowledge source testing**: The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness with different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) and show consistent performance improvements, suggesting robustness to different types of structural information.

3. **Clear visualization of attention**: The attention visualization in Figure 5 effectively demonstrates how the model uses knowledge-guided substructures to focus on important parts of the input sentence.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty**: The paper's claim that "this is the first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion" is fundamentally flawed. The related work section cites Liu et al. (2013) and Chen et al. (2015), which explicitly use parse trees as knowledge for language understanding. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from these prior works. The authors cite "feature enrichment based approaches" as having limitations, but their method appears to be a variation of these same feature enrichment approaches rather than a fundamentally different end-to-end framework.

2. **Critical methodological gaps**: The paper contains serious omissions in model description that make the approach difficult to understand and reproduce. Specifically:
   - In Equation (1), "pi = softmax(uTmi)" computes attention between the entire utterance vector u and substructure vectors mi, rather than between current word embeddings and knowledge representations. This is a fundamental misunderstanding of how attention mechanisms should work in sequence tagging tasks.
   - Section 4.2 introduces "Mo" in the RNN formulation without explanation (what is Mo? How is it learned? What is its size?). This critical component is not defined, making the model description incomplete.
   - The paper states that "non-leaf nodes do not have corresponding substructure" (Section 3.1), but this contradicts the dependency tree structure described. If substructures are built from root-to-leaf paths, non-leaf nodes are part of those paths and should be included in substructures.

3. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with baselines from 2013-2015 (e.g., Mesnil et al., 2015) but fails to compare with more recent state-of-the-art NLU models. The field has advanced significantly since 2015, with transformer-based models (BERT, etc.) now being standard. Without comparison to these, the claim of "state-of-the-art performance" is unsupported.

4. **Inconsistent experimental setup and claims**: The paper claims "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words" (Section 1) but provides no evidence for this claim. The experiments are conducted on the ATIS dataset, which has short sentences, so the efficiency claim is not properly validated. Additionally, the paper inconsistently states that dependency trees come from "Stanford dependency parser" and later mentions "SyntaxNet parser" without explaining the difference or why both were used.

5. **Formatting and presentation issues**: The paper contains numerous minor but significant formatting inconsistencies:
   - In Figure 1, the IOB format is shown as "O O O O B-origin O B-dest I-destO" (with an extra trailing "O" that doesn't match the standard IOB format).
   - The references section contains inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Banarescu et al.,? 2013" with a question mark).
   - The paper inconsistently uses "NLU" without spelling it out on first use.
   - The paper states "Small: 1/40 set" but doesn't specify what the total training set size is, making it hard to understand the scale of the experiment.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach differ from Liu et al. (2013) and Chen et al. (2015), which explicitly used parse trees as knowledge for language understanding? The paper claims to be "the first" to use knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion, but these prior works did similar things.

2. In Equation (1), the attention is computed between the entire utterance vector u and substructure vectors mi. Shouldn't the attention be computed between current word embeddings and knowledge representations? How does this formulation affect the model's ability to perform sequence tagging?

3. In Section 4.2, you introduce "Mo" in the RNN formulation without explanation. What is Mo? Is it a learned matrix? How is it initialized? What is its size? This critical component is not defined.

4. The paper claims "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words," but provides no evidence. Have you tested the model on longer sentences where this claim might be validated?

5. The paper compares with baselines from 2013-2015, but the NLU field has advanced significantly since then. How does your model compare with more recent state-of-the-art approaches, such as those using BERT or other transformer-based models?

## Overall Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting idea and shows some empirical improvements, the fundamental flaws in the claimed novelty, critical methodological gaps in the model description, and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art work outweigh the paper's positive contributions. The paper makes a strong claim of novelty that is not supported by the related work, and the model description contains critical omissions that make it impossible to properly evaluate or reproduce the approach. The minor formatting issues and inconsistencies further undermine the paper's quality. These issues are substantial enough to warrant rejection, even though the paper addresses a relevant problem in NLU.
