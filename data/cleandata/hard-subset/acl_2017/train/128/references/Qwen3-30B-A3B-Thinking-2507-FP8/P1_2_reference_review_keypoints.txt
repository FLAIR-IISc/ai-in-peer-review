1. Proposes K-SAN, a neural architecture that uses external knowledge to guide attention for slot filling.  
2. Represents sentences via knowledge-guided substructures (e.g., dependency paths) weighted by attention.  
3. Evaluated on ATIS with varying training sizes, showing consistent improvements, especially on small data.  
4. Claims end-to-end "knowledge as a teacher" approach to reduce error propagation from feature-based methods.  
5. Empirical results show statistically significant F1 gains across Small, Medium, and Large datasets.  
6. Approach is practically motivated and addresses limitations of prior knowledge-as-features methods.  
7. Demonstrates flexibility across knowledge sources (dependency parsers, AMR) without schema changes.  
8. Attention visualizations indicate the model focuses on meaningful substructures even with parser errors.  
9. Novelty is limited relative to prior memory-network and attention work and lacks clear differentiation.  
10. Missing ablation studies to isolate the impact of knowledge-guided attention and different encoders.  
11. Insufficient analysis of sensitivity to noisy or incorrect external knowledge sources.  
12. Comparisons omit recent state-of-the-art sequence models (e.g., transformer-based approaches).  
13. Lacks strong theoretical justification linking the architecture to linguistic properties.  
14. Unclear why dependency relation labels were not used and how they might be incorporated.  
15. Efficiency and parallelizability claims lack computational complexity analysis or timing comparisons.  
16. Unclear sensitivity to parser quality and whether better parsers would yield significant gains.  
17. No discussion of how differing numbers of substructures across knowledge sources are handled or normalized.  
18. Paper is well-written with solid experiments but needs clearer positioning against related work.  
19. Attention visualization is a particularly strong and supportive empirical element.  
20. With clearer novelty claims and more comprehensive analyses, the paper could be a strong ACL contribution.
