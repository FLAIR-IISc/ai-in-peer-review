

# Peer Review: "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture for natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. The model leverages external knowledge (such as dependency parsing results) to guide attention mechanisms for sequence tagging (slot filling). The key innovation is using knowledge as a "teacher" to guide where and how much attention the model should pay to different substructures within an input sentence, rather than simply using knowledge as additional features. The authors claim that K-SAN provides end-to-end learning, generalization across different knowledge sources, and improved efficiency through parallelization.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Demonstrated significant performance improvements on small datasets, addressing a critical practical challenge in NLU where labeled data is scarce
2. Effective integration of multiple types of structured knowledge (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring schema adaptation
3. Clear visualization of how the model uses knowledge-guided attention, providing interpretability to the mechanism

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvements on small datasets**: The paper demonstrates that K-SAN provides the most substantial gains on the smallest training dataset (1/40 of the training data), with a 5.5% absolute F1 improvement over the best baseline. This is particularly valuable for real-world applications where labeled data is limited, and most previous work focuses on large datasets. The results in Table 1 clearly show that the model's ability to generalize from limited data is its strongest contribution.

2. **Robustness across different knowledge sources**: The paper provides compelling evidence that K-SAN can effectively use different types of structured knowledge (dependency trees from Stanford and SyntaxNet, AMR graphs from rule-based and JAMR parsers) with consistent performance improvements over baselines. This generalization across knowledge sources is a significant contribution to the field, as most prior work is tied to a specific knowledge representation.

3. **Interpretability through attention visualization**: Figure 5 provides a clear visualization of how the model's attention weights correlate with semantic slots, demonstrating that the model correctly identifies important substructures (like "from seattle to san francisco" in the example). This interpretability helps validate the model's mechanism and provides insights into how the knowledge guidance works.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the attention mechanism**: The core idea of using attention to focus on relevant parts of the input is not new (e.g., Luong et al., 2015; Vaswani et al., 2017). The paper doesn't clearly articulate how the attention mechanism differs from existing approaches or why it's specifically better for NLU tasks. The model appears to be a straightforward application of attention to structured knowledge rather than a novel attention mechanism. The authors claim "end-to-end learning" as a contribution, but this is standard practice in neural network approaches.

2. **Insufficient comparison to recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares to older baselines (CRF, RNN, DCNN, Tree-RNN) but doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art models for NLU, such as Transformer-based architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017) or models that explicitly handle structured knowledge like those using graph neural networks. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess how K-SAN stands against current best practices.

3. **Lack of analysis on knowledge source quality**: The paper acknowledges that the Stanford dependency parser made errors in Figure 5, but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how errors in the knowledge source affect K-SAN's performance. This is a critical gap since the model's effectiveness depends on the quality of the external knowledge. The authors claim to avoid "error propagation" but don't demonstrate how K-SAN handles incorrect knowledge better than previous approaches.

4. **No ablation studies**: The paper doesn't perform ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (e.g., knowledge encoding, attention mechanism, joint RNN tagger). Without these, it's unclear which aspects of the model are driving the performance improvements. For instance, it's not clear whether the attention mechanism or the knowledge-guided substructures are more important.

5. **Theoretical justification is lacking**: The paper claims that previous approaches suffer from "poor generalization" and "error propagation" but doesn't provide a theoretical or empirical justification for why K-SAN avoids these issues. The authors present the model as a solution to these problems but don't clearly demonstrate how it addresses them better than other knowledge-integrated approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that K-SAN avoids error propagation, but doesn't provide evidence that this is true. How does K-SAN specifically handle errors in the knowledge source (e.g., incorrect dependency parses), and how does it compare to previous approaches that use knowledge as features in terms of error resilience?

2. The paper mentions that the model is "efficient and parallelizable" but doesn't provide any quantitative analysis of this claim. Can you provide computational complexity analysis or runtime measurements comparing K-SAN to baselines?

3. The model uses dependency parsing as the primary knowledge source, but the paper doesn't discuss how the model would perform with knowledge sources that have different structural properties. For example, how would K-SAN handle knowledge from a knowledge graph with different relation types compared to dependency trees?

4. The authors claim the model is "end-to-end" but don't specify how it differs from previous end-to-end approaches that incorporate knowledge. What is the key difference between K-SAN and end-to-end approaches that use knowledge as features?

5. The paper focuses on the ATIS dataset, which is relatively small and has limited domain variation. How would K-SAN perform on larger, more complex datasets like MultiWOZ or SNIPS, where slot filling requires understanding more complex relationships between entities?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and the experiments are generally well-designed, but the novelty claim needs to be more precisely articulated. The authors should clarify whether the main contribution is the specific application of attention to knowledge-guided substructures (which is not novel) or the empirical demonstration that this approach works well for NLU with limited data (which is valuable but needs to be positioned more clearly).

The comparison to baselines is adequate for the specific problem, but the lack of comparison to recent state-of-the-art models weakens the paper's contribution claim. The authors should consider adding a comparison to more recent approaches to better position their work.

The paper's focus on small dataset performance is a strong practical contribution, but the authors should more clearly articulate why this is important for real-world applications of NLU systems where data collection is expensive and limited.
