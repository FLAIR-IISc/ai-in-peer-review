

# Review of "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes K-SAN, a neural network architecture for natural language understanding (NLU) that incorporates external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs) to guide attention during slot filling. The authors claim the model is "end-to-end," generalizes to different knowledge types, and is efficient/parallelizable. As I see it, the actual contributions are:

1. A model that integrates knowledge-guided attention into NLU slot filling, with empirical evidence of improved performance, especially with limited data.
2. Demonstration that different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR) can be used with the same model architecture.
3. Visualization of attention weights to show how the model focuses on relevant substructures.

## Strengths

1. **Data Scarcity Context**: The paper effectively demonstrates that the model performs better with limited training data (small dataset), which is a valuable contribution for real-world NLU applications where labeled data is scarce. The results on the small dataset (1/40 training data) show more significant improvements over baselines.

2. **Knowledge Generalization**: The paper successfully shows the model can work with different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) without requiring knowledge schema modifications, demonstrating good generalization properties. This is a meaningful contribution to the field.

3. **Attention Visualization**: The attention visualization in Figure 5 provides valuable insights into how the model processes linguistic structures, helping to explain the model's behavior and why it outperforms baselines on certain examples.

## Weaknesses

1. **Format and Presentation Errors**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies throughout that would be unacceptable in a top-tier ACL publication. References use inconsistent formatting (e.g., "et al\.,? 2013" instead of "et al., 2013"), and there are numerous minor typos (e.g., "I-destO" in Figure 1 instead of "I-dest O"). The paper also uses inconsistent notation (e.g., "Mkg" vs. "Mkg" in different sections). These are minor errors, but as a perfectionist reviewer, I find the sheer number of them unacceptable for a paper claiming to be a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Misrepresentation of "End-to-End" Learning**: The paper claims to be the "first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion," but the dependency parsing is performed using a separate, pre-trained Stanford parser that is not integrated into the training process. The knowledge parsing step is not part of the model being trained, so the model is not truly end-to-end. This misrepresentation of the model's capabilities is a significant flaw in the paper's claims.

3. **Marginal Performance Gains**: The improvements over baselines are marginal (1-1.5 F1 points), and the paper fails to provide sufficient context for whether these improvements are meaningful in the NLU community. For example, on the Large dataset, the best K-SAN model (94.97) only improves over the best baseline (93.88) by 1.09 points, which is not a substantial improvement for a new model.

4. **Unsubstantiated Efficiency Claim**: The paper lists "Efficiency and parallelizability" as a key contribution but provides no evidence or analysis to support this claim. The paper states "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words in the input sentence" but offers no empirical evidence or computational complexity analysis to back this up. This is a significant gap in the paper's claims.

5. **Technical Ambiguities**: The paper contains several technical ambiguities that make it difficult to reproduce the model. For example, the phrase "the weights of Mkg and Min are tied together based on their consistent ability of sequence encoding" is vague and lacks technical justification. The equation for the sentence representation (Equation 3: o = Mout(h+ u)) is confusing because h is already a sum of weighted substructure vectors, making the addition of u to h conceptually unclear.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be "end-to-end" but uses a separate dependency parser. Could you clarify how the dependency parsing is integrated into the end-to-end training process, or acknowledge that this is not truly end-to-end?

2. The paper states that "K-SAN (RNN) performs best on the Large set (95% on F1)" but the table shows 94.97, not 95%. Could you clarify this discrepancy?

3. The paper claims to "utilize general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion" but only evaluates dependency trees and AMR. Could you explain why the paper doesn't evaluate knowledge graph-specific relations or handcrafted grammars as mentioned in the introduction?

4. In Equation (3), the paper states "o = Mout(h+ u)" but h is already a weighted sum of substructure vectors. Could you clarify why we are adding u (the input embedding) to h rather than combining them in another way?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a model that works with different knowledge sources and shows modest improvements in NLU performance, the numerous minor flaws in presentation, the misrepresentation of "end-to-end" learning, the marginal performance gains, and the unsubstantiated efficiency claim are significant enough to warrant rejection. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find the formatting inconsistencies and technical ambiguities particularly troubling in a paper claiming to make a significant contribution to the field. The paper would need to be substantially revised to address these issues before it could be considered for publication in ACL.
