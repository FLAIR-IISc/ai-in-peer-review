

# Peer Review: "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach to natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. The authors address a key limitation in existing approaches: previous work typically treats external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses) as additional features rather than as a guiding mechanism for attention. K-SAN instead uses knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention to relevant substructures in the input utterance, learning this guidance end-to-end.

My assessment of the main contributions (in order of strength):

1. **Novel integration of knowledge as attention guidance**: The paper presents a meaningful shift from treating knowledge as additional features to using it as a direct attention guide, which avoids error propagation from knowledge extraction and enables better generalization, particularly with limited training data.

2. **Demonstration of improved performance with limited data**: The model shows significant gains on small datasets, which is highly relevant for practical NLU applications where labeled data is scarce.

3. **Flexibility across knowledge sources**: The model's ability to work with different knowledge representations (dependency parses, AMR graphs) demonstrates its generalization capability across different linguistic resources.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful limitation in NLU approaches**: The paper correctly identifies that existing knowledge-based approaches suffer from two key problems: poor generalization due to knowledge-data mismatches, and error propagation from knowledge extraction. K-SAN's approach of using knowledge as a direct attention guide rather than as features effectively addresses these limitations, as evidenced by the improved performance on small datasets where knowledge mismatch is more likely.

2. **Strong empirical results with clear practical relevance**: The experiments on the ATIS dataset show consistent improvements over baselines, especially on the small dataset (74.60% vs 73.57% for CNN baseline). This is highly relevant for real-world applications where labeled data is limited. The 1.03 F1-point improvement on the small dataset (with only 1/40 of training data) is particularly significant.

3. **Effective visualization of attention mechanisms**: Figure 5 provides clear evidence that the model learns to focus on relevant substructures (e.g., origin, destination, time), which supports the paper's claims about knowledge-guided attention. The attention patterns remain consistent across different dataset sizes, demonstrating robustness.

4. **Demonstrated generalization across knowledge sources**: Table 2 shows that K-SAN performs well with different knowledge resources (dependency trees from Stanford vs. SyntaxNet, AMR from Rule-Based vs. JAMR), suggesting the approach is robust to variations in knowledge representation. This is a strong point for the model's practical applicability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to existing attention mechanisms**: While the application to NLU is novel, the core mechanism of using external information to guide attention is not new. The paper builds on memory networks (Weston et al., 2015) and attention mechanisms (Bahdanau et al., 2015), and the "knowledge as teacher" concept is essentially a specific application of these existing ideas. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from the broader literature on attention-guided models.

2. **Inadequate comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares against older baselines (e.g., Tree-RNN from 2015) but doesn't compare with more recent NLU approaches that use transformer architectures or other modern attention mechanisms. For example, the paper doesn't compare against models like BERT or other contextualized embeddings, which have become standard in NLU. This weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art performance."

3. **Lack of analysis on knowledge quality impact**: The paper mentions in Figure 5 that the dependency parser made an error but doesn't analyze how the model handles such errors. The paper claims robustness, but doesn't provide evidence of how performance degrades when knowledge is incorrect or incomplete, which is critical for real-world applications.

4. **Overstated efficiency claims**: The paper claims "efficiency and parallelizability" but provides no evidence for this. The architecture doesn't seem to offer significant computational advantages over standard RNNs, and the paper doesn't provide runtime comparisons or scalability analysis to support this claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the model perform when the external knowledge (e.g., dependency parser) makes errors? The paper notes in Figure 5 that the parser made an error, but doesn't analyze how this affects performance or whether the model can compensate for such errors.

2. How does K-SAN compare with recent transformer-based NLU models (e.g., BERT fine-tuned for slot filling) that have become standard in the field? The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art performance" needs to be evaluated against these more recent approaches.

3. The paper claims the model is "efficient and parallelizable" but provides no evidence. Could you provide runtime comparisons or scalability analysis to support this claim?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a meaningful approach to integrating knowledge into NLU models by using it as a direct attention guide rather than as additional features. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements, especially on small datasets, which is highly relevant for practical applications. However, the novelty of the core mechanism is limited, and the paper doesn't adequately compare with recent state-of-the-art approaches.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses regarding novelty, comparison with recent approaches, and the efficiency claims in their revision. The paper has significant potential but needs to better position itself within the current NLU landscape and provide more thorough comparisons with recent work.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding how to better integrate structured knowledge into neural NLU models, and with appropriate revisions, it could be a strong contribution to ACL.
