

# ACL Review: "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes K-SAN (Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks), a model that leverages external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses) to guide attention mechanisms in neural networks for natural language understanding (NLU) tasks. The authors claim to use knowledge as a "teacher" to guide where and how much attention the model should pay to different substructures in the input sentence.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **Knowledge-guided attention mechanism**: The model integrates external knowledge to guide attention over substructures rather than using knowledge as additional features.
2. **Generalization across knowledge types**: The model can work with different knowledge sources (dependency relations, AMR graphs, etc.) without requiring schema changes.
3. **End-to-end training**: The model learns to use knowledge guidance without requiring manual feature engineering.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to knowledge integration**: The paper presents an interesting alternative to the common practice of using knowledge as additional features. The idea of using knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention is conceptually novel and potentially valuable for NLU tasks where structural information is important. The approach addresses the error propagation problem mentioned in the paper, which is a valid concern in NLU pipelines.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The authors conducted thorough experiments across multiple data sizes (Small, Medium, Large) and with different knowledge resources (dependency trees, AMR graphs). The ablation study on different encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN) for the knowledge representation provides useful insights into the model's behavior.

3. **Strong results on data-scarce scenarios**: The paper demonstrates that K-SAN shows significant improvements on the Small dataset (1/40 training data), with F1 scores of 74.60 (K-SAN CNN) versus 73.57 (CNN baseline), which is a meaningful improvement (1.03 points) in a challenging low-resource setting. This suggests the model could be valuable for applications with limited training data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical inconsistencies and ambiguities**: The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. For example:
   - Figure 3 shows "substructures" that are not actual dependency paths (e.g., "show flights from seattle" is listed as a substructure, but dependency paths would be from root to each leaf, not arbitrary phrases).
   - The paper claims "the maximum number of substructures for the dependency tree is larger than the number in the AMR graph (53 and 25 v.s. 19 and 8)" but Table 2 shows SyntaxNet (25) produces fewer substructures than Stanford (53), contradicting the statement.
   - In Section 3.1, the paper states "the number of substructures may be less than the number of words in the utterance, because non-leaf nodes do not have corresponding substructure." This is incorrect - in dependency parsing, each word is a leaf, so the number of substructures should equal the number of words.
   - The attention visualization in Figure 5 shows "slot names" (e.g., "flight_stop") as the attention weights, not dependency relations as claimed.

2. **Marginal improvements with no clear significance**: While the paper claims "significant improvements" (with †), the actual numerical improvements are marginal (e.g., 94.86 vs 93.25 for DCNN on Large dataset is only 1.61 points). The paper doesn't provide p-values for most comparisons, and the "significance" claim is questionable given the marginal gains. The paper also states "the proposed model presents the state-of-the-art performance" but the results (94.86 F1) are below commonly reported state-of-the-art results for ATIS (typically 95-96%).

3. **Inadequate explanation of key design choices**: The paper lacks justification for several important design decisions:
   - "The weights of Mkg and Min are tied together based on their consistent ability of sequence encoding." This is vague and unsupported by evidence.
   - The joint RNN tagger uses α = 0.5, but the paper doesn't explain why this value was chosen or whether it was tuned.
   - The attention mechanism uses a simple dot product (u^T mi) but doesn't justify why a more sophisticated attention mechanism (e.g., with a learned weight matrix) wasn't used.

4. **Inaccurate claims and misleading statements**: 
   - The paper claims "the semantic concepts in AMR were leveraged to benefit multiple NLP tasks (Liu et al., 2015)" but Liu et al. (2015) is about abstractive summarization, not AMR.
   - The paper states "CNN (Kim, 2014) performs better compared to DCNN (Ma et al., 2015a) and Tree-RNN (Tai et al., 2015), even though CNN does not leverage external knowledge when encoding sentences" - this is confusing because the comparison is between models with and without external knowledge, but the CNN baseline isn't using external knowledge as claimed.
   - The paper claims "the dependency relations are incorrectly parsed by the Stanford parser" in Figure 5, but then says "our model is still able to benefit from the structural information" - this is contradictory, as incorrect parsing would likely hurt performance rather than help.

5. **Formatting and writing issues**: The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent terminology, and formatting problems. For example:
   - The reference list has inconsistent formatting (some with "et al." and some without).
   - The description of the model (Section 3.2) refers to "Mkg" and "Min" without defining them.
   - The paper uses "knowledge-guided substructures" and "knowledge-guided structural attention" interchangeably, creating confusion.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Section 3.1, you state "the number of substructures may be less than the number of words in the utterance, because non-leaf nodes do not have corresponding substructure." This is incorrect. In dependency parsing, each word is a leaf node, so the number of substructures (paths from root to leaves) should equal the number of words. Can you clarify this point?

2. In Table 2, you report "Maximum #Substructure" as 53 for Stanford dependency parser and 19 for Rule-Based AMR parser. However, in the text, you state "the maximum number of substructures for the dependency tree is larger than the number in the AMR graph (53 and 25 v.s. 19 and 8)". The SyntaxNet numbers (25 and 8) don't match the claim that dependency trees have more substructures. Can you clarify this discrepancy?

3. You claim that "K-SAN (CNN) achieves the state-of-the-art performance on the large dataset" (94.86 F1). However, standard results for ATIS typically report F1 scores of 95-96% for state-of-the-art models. Can you provide a comparison to recent state-of-the-art results on the ATIS benchmark?

4. In Figure 5, the attention visualization shows "slot names" (e.g., "flight_stop") as the attention weights, but the paper claims it's showing dependency relations. Can you clarify what the attention weights are actually highlighting?

5. You state that "the weights of Mkg and Min are tied together based on their consistent ability of sequence encoding." What specific evidence or experiments led to this decision, and why wasn't this tied weight configuration compared to separate weights in your experiments?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper proposes an interesting concept, the numerous technical inconsistencies, ambiguous explanations, marginal results, and misleading claims significantly undermine the contribution. The paper contains too many minor flaws that collectively make it difficult to assess the validity and novelty of the work. The authors would need to thoroughly revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication. The marginal improvements over baselines and lack of statistical significance for most comparisons further reduce the paper's impact.
