- Proposes K-SAN: uses external structured knowledge to guide attention for sequence tagging in NLU.  
- Frames knowledge as a "teacher" to direct attention to important substructures rather than as simple features.  

- Claims significant gains on small-data regimes, improving generalization when labeled data is scarce.  
- Demonstrates integration of multiple structured knowledge types (dependency trees, AMR) without schema changes.  
- Provides attention visualizations to interpret how knowledge guides slot predictions.  

- Strong empirical improvements on very small training sets (e.g., +5.5% F1 on 1/40 data).  
- Robust performance across different parsers and knowledge sources, suggesting generalization.  
- Attention visualizations show the model focuses on semantically relevant substructures.  

- Attention mechanism novelty is limited; resembles existing attention methods and lacks clear differentiation.  
- Comparisons omit recent SOTA models (e.g., Transformers, GNNs), weakening claims of superiority.  
- No systematic analysis of how noise/errors in external knowledge affect model performance.  
- Missing ablation studies to isolate the impact of model components (knowledge encoding, attention, RNN tagger).  
- Lacks theoretical or empirical justification for claims about avoiding poor generalization and error propagation.  

- How does K-SAN handle incorrect knowledge inputs and compare in error resilience to feature-based approaches?  
- Can you provide quantitative runtime or complexity evidence supporting claims of efficiency and parallelism?  
- How would K-SAN handle differently structured knowledge sources (e.g., heterogeneous knowledge graphs)?  
- What specifically differentiates K-SAN from prior end-to-end knowledge-integrated approaches?  
- How does the model scale to larger, more diverse datasets (e.g., MultiWOZ, SNIPS)?  

- Paper is well-written and experiments are generally solid, but novelty needs clearer articulation.  
- Emphasize empirical value on small-data NLU, but add comparisons to modern baselines and component analyses.
