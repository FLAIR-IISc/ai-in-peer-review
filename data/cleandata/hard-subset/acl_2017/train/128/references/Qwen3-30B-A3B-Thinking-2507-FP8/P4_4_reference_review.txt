

# Review of "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural network architecture for natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. K-SAN uses external knowledge (such as dependency relations or semantic graphs) as a "teacher" to guide the attention mechanism of the model, allowing it to focus on relevant substructures within input utterances for better semantic tagging. The model first generates knowledge-guided substructures from the input using an external parser, encodes these substructures into vector representations, computes attention weights based on the match between the input and substructures, and combines this knowledge-guided representation with the input for semantic tagging. The authors evaluate their approach on the ATIS dataset, showing improvements over baseline models, particularly on smaller training datasets.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
1. **Knowledge-guided attention mechanism**: The paper introduces a novel attention mechanism that uses external knowledge to guide the model's focus on relevant substructures, rather than simply incorporating knowledge as additional features.
2. **Generalization to multiple knowledge sources**: The approach works with various types of knowledge (dependency relations, semantic graphs, etc.) without requiring a specific schema.
3. **Improved performance with limited data**: The model shows particularly strong improvements on smaller training datasets, suggesting better generalization capabilities.

## Strengths

1. **Novel attention mechanism for knowledge integration**: The paper presents a clear conceptual innovation in how knowledge is integrated into neural models. Unlike previous approaches that simply add knowledge features to the input, K-SAN uses an attention mechanism to automatically determine which parts of the knowledge are most relevant for the task. The attention mechanism is computed as a weighted sum of knowledge-guided substructure embeddings, with weights determined by the match between the input and each substructure. This addresses the "poor generalization" limitation mentioned in the paper regarding previous feature enrichment approaches.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline models, particularly on the smaller training datasets (Small and Medium). The improvements are statistically significant (p<0.05) in most cases, with K-SAN (CNN) achieving the best results on the Small (74.60 F1) and Medium (87.99 F1) datasets, while K-SAN (RNN) achieved the best result on the Large dataset (94.97 F1). The attention visualization in Figure 5 provides compelling evidence that the model is learning to focus on relevant substructures (e.g., origin, destination, time), even when the dependency parsing is imperfect.

3. **Demonstrated generalization to multiple knowledge sources**: The authors show that K-SAN works effectively with different knowledge sources (dependency trees from Stanford and SyntaxNet parsers, AMR graphs from rule-based and JAMR parsers), with similar performance across all resources. This addresses the "generalization for different knowledge" claim and demonstrates the robustness of the approach to different types of knowledge representation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear distinction from related work**: While the paper claims K-SAN is the first neural network approach that utilizes knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work on neural attention mechanisms and memory networks. The approach bears strong similarities to Dynamic Memory Networks (Xiong et al., 2016) and memory-based approaches for NLU, but the paper doesn't adequately explain how K-SAN differs from these methods in terms of the specific attention mechanism or knowledge integration.

2. **Mischaracterization of "end-to-end" learning**: The paper claims "end-to-end learning" as a key contribution, but the model still relies on an external dependency parser to generate the knowledge-guided substructures. This is not truly end-to-end learning since the dependency parsing step is not trained jointly with the neural network. Previous approaches (e.g., Tree-RNN, DCNN) also used external parsers, so this isn't a novel aspect of the approach as claimed.

3. **Limited analysis of failure cases and error propagation**: The paper mentions that dependency parsing errors may occur (Figure 5), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how often these errors occur or how they affect the model's performance. Without understanding the failure modes, it's difficult to assess the robustness of the approach. The paper also doesn't analyze cases where the model fails to correctly identify relevant substructures despite having the knowledge available.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first neural network approach that utilizes knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion. However, the model still requires an external dependency parser to generate the knowledge-guided substructures. How does this differ from previous approaches that also used external parsers (e.g., Tree-RNN, DCNN) as part of their pipeline?

2. The paper states that "the number of substructures may be less than the number of words in the utterance, because non-leaf nodes do not have corresponding substructure in order to reduce the duplicated information." How were these substructures selected? Is there a formal way to determine which substructures are most informative, or is it based solely on the dependency parsing output?

3. The attention visualization in Figure 5 shows that the model can still work even when the dependency parsing is incorrect. However, the paper doesn't provide any analysis of how often the dependency parser makes errors and how the model handles them. Could you provide more details on this, including a breakdown of error cases where the dependency parsing was incorrect but the model still performed well?
