- Proposes K-SAN: a framework that uses external knowledge (dependency parses, AMR) to guide attention for improved NLU/slot filling.  
- Main contribution: end-to-end attention guided by external knowledge that improves performance, especially on small datasets.  
- Main contribution: flexible across different knowledge sources without requiring specific schemas.  
- Main contribution: shows robustness to data scarcity, improving generalization with limited labeled data.  

- Strength: Novel integration of knowledge as a teacher to guide attention rather than as extra features, with supportive attention visualizations.  
- Strength: Strong empirical evidence of data efficiency (notable F1 gains on small datasets).  
- Strength: Demonstrated flexibility across multiple knowledge sources and parsers with consistent improvements.  
- Strength: Clear, well-structured presentation and meaningful analysis (figures and experimental details).  

- Weakness: Missing comparisons to recent SOTA methods (e.g., BERT/transformers), making significance unclear.  
- Weakness: Core mechanism (attention over substructures) is not substantially novel relative to existing attention approaches.  
- Weakness: Lacks ablation studies to isolate contributions of components (knowledge encoding vs. attention).  
- Weakness: No empirical analysis of computational complexity, training time, or scalability despite efficiency claims.  

- Question: Why were comparisons to recent SOTA NLU methods (BERT-based) omitted, and how would K-SAN compare?  
- Question: How does the model handle parser errors in practice, and are there specific error patterns it is robust to?  
- Question: Can the authors provide ablation studies quantifying each component’s contribution?  

- Overall recommendation: Weak accept (3.5/5); interesting, practically useful contribution but needs SOTA comparisons and ablations to strengthen impact.
