

# Review of "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach to natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. The core idea is to use external knowledge (such as dependency parsing results) as a "teacher" to guide attention mechanisms in neural networks, rather than simply incorporating knowledge as additional features. The model encodes knowledge-guided substructures (e.g., paths from root to leaves in dependency trees), computes attention weights between the input utterance and these substructures, and uses this information to improve slot filling performance. The authors claim their approach is end-to-end, generalizable to different knowledge sources, and more efficient than previous methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Marginal performance improvement on ATIS**: The paper demonstrates small but consistent F1 score improvements (0.5-1.0 points) over previous models on the standard ATIS benchmark, particularly for smaller training datasets.

2. **Attention visualization**: The paper provides visualizations of attention weights for different knowledge-guided substructures, offering some insight into how the model processes information.

3. **Generalization to different knowledge sources**: The paper shows that K-SAN can work with different knowledge representations (dependency trees, AMR graphs) with similar performance.

## Strengths

1. **Thorough evaluation on standard benchmark**: The paper evaluates on the widely used ATIS dataset with multiple training data sizes (Small, Medium, Large), which is good practice for NLU research. The ablation study on different knowledge sources (dependency trees vs. AMR graphs) demonstrates the model's robustness to different knowledge representations.

2. **Attention visualization**: Figure 5 provides useful insights into how the model attends to different substructures and relations, which helps understand the model's behavior.

3. **Clear methodology**: The paper presents a well-structured description of the model architecture and the knowledge encoding process, making it relatively easy to understand the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty**: The paper's claim that this is "the first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion" is incorrect. Memory networks (Weston et al., 2015) and dynamic memory networks (Xiong et al., 2016) already use external knowledge with attention mechanisms in an end-to-end manner. The paper fails to properly distinguish its approach from these prior works, making the claimed novelty questionable.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares against outdated baselines (e.g., Tree-RNN from 2015) and does not include recent state-of-the-art models like BERT-based approaches for slot filling. The marginal improvements reported (e.g., 0.98 F1 points on Large dataset) are not significant enough to justify claiming "state-of-the-art performance" when compared to modern methods that routinely achieve 97-98 F1 on ATIS.

3. **Overstated claims about generalization**: The paper claims that K-SAN "carries better generalization and is less sensitive to unseen data" based on performance on ATIS with small training data. However, there's no evidence of generalization to other domains or tasks beyond this specific dataset. The small performance gains on the small dataset don't necessarily indicate robust generalization.

4. **Lack of ablation study on key components**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to determine which components of K-SAN are actually contributing to the performance gains. For example, it's unclear whether the attention mechanism or the knowledge-guided substructures are the main drivers of the improvements.

5. **Questionable efficiency claims**: The paper claims "efficiency and parallelizability" because substructures are modeled separately, but provides no empirical evidence to support this claim. The paper doesn't compare training or inference times with baseline models, making this claim unsubstantiated.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your K-SAN model differ from Dynamic Memory Networks (DMN) proposed by Xiong et al. (2016), which also uses external knowledge with an attention mechanism? Specifically, what makes your approach novel compared to DMN?

2. The paper compares against baselines that are several years old (e.g., Tree-RNN from 2015). Why didn't you compare against more recent state-of-the-art models like BERT-based approaches for slot filling? How would your model perform against these more recent methods?

3. The marginal improvements (less than 1 F1 point) reported in Table 1 seem to be of limited practical significance. How do you justify the claim that your approach "significantly outperforms" the baselines, especially given the small improvements?

4. The paper claims that K-SAN "learns the attention guided by external knowledge," but the attention mechanism is based on the simple inner product of vectors (Equation 1). Why didn't you use a more sophisticated attention mechanism that has become standard in recent literature?

5. The paper states that "knowledge plays the role of a teacher to guide networks where and how much to focus attention considering the whole linguistic structure simultaneously." However, the model architecture (Figure 2) appears to simply add the knowledge-guided representation as an additional input to the RNN tagger. How does this fundamentally differ from simply adding additional features to the RNN tagger?

## Final Recommendation

Reject

This paper makes incremental improvements to existing approaches without sufficient novelty or convincing evidence of significant benefits over established methods. The claimed "end-to-end" approach is not novel, the improvements are marginal and not statistically significant when compared to modern baselines, and the paper fails to properly contextualize its work within the broader literature on knowledge-based neural networks. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address the lack of clear novelty, provide meaningful comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, and provide stronger empirical evidence to support their claims.
