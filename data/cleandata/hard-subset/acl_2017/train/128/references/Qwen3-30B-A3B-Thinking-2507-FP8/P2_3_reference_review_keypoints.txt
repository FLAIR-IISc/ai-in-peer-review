- Core contribution: Introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) using external knowledge to guide attention over salient substructures in NLU.
- Contribution: Shows K-SAN generalizes to multiple knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR) without requiring specific schemas.
- Contribution: Demonstrates robustness to data scarcity, with notable gains on small training sets.

- Strength: Strong empirical validation on ATIS across Small/Medium/Large datasets, outperforming baselines especially on small data.
- Strength: Effective attention visualizations showing the model attends to relevant substructures even with parser errors.
- Strength: Empirical evidence of comparable performance across different parsers/knowledge representations, indicating flexibility.
- Strength: Clearly differentiates K-SAN from prior structural models by emphasizing controlled attention to substructures.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—using external knowledge to guide attention is not shown to substantially differ from prior attention/memory or structural NLP work.
- Weakness: Lacks theoretical justification for why the proposed attention mechanism outperforms alternatives.
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of how noisy or imperfect external knowledge (e.g., parser errors) affects performance.
- Weakness: Efficiency and parallelizability claims are unsubstantiated—no runtime or scalability experiments provided.

- Question: How does your attention mechanism concretely differ from Tree-RNNs in controlling attention over structural information?
- Question: Can you provide runtime/training-time comparisons to support efficiency and parallelizability claims?
- Question: Have you quantified K-SAN’s robustness when external knowledge (parses) is incorrect or missing?

- Overall: Well-motivated, empirically validated approach with useful generalization properties; recommends acceptance with minor revisions to clarify novelty, theoretical justification, robustness to noisy knowledge, and efficiency claims.
