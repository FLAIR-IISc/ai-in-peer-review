

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach for natural language understanding in spoken dialogue systems. The key idea is to use external knowledge (such as dependency parses or semantic graphs) as a "teacher" to guide attention mechanisms in a neural network, rather than using knowledge as additional features. The model learns to automatically determine which substructures of the input sentence are most relevant for semantic tagging, with the knowledge source guiding where and how much attention should be paid to different parts of the sentence.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Novel conceptual framework for knowledge integration**: The paper introduces a fundamentally different approach to incorporating external knowledge into neural models, where knowledge serves as a "teacher" to guide attention rather than being used as additional features. This addresses the key limitation of previous work (error propagation) that the authors correctly identify.
2. **Generalizable framework for multiple knowledge sources**: The model is designed to work with different types of knowledge (dependency relations, AMR graphs, etc.) without requiring a specific schema, demonstrating flexibility that previous work lacks.
3. **Strong empirical validation**: The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the ATIS benchmark, with significant improvements especially on smaller datasets, demonstrating better generalization.

## Strengths
1. **Truly novel conceptual approach**: This is the most compelling strength of the paper. The authors correctly identify a fundamental limitation of prior work (using knowledge as features, leading to error propagation and poor generalization) and propose a bold, novel solution that reframes how knowledge is integrated into neural models. The "knowledge as teacher" concept is elegant and represents a significant conceptual advance over previous feature-based approaches.

2. **Strong empirical results with clear practical value**: The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the ATIS benchmark, with significant improvements over strong baselines, especially on smaller datasets. This is particularly impressive given the authors' claim that their model is more robust to data scarcity, which is a common challenge in NLU. The results are clearly presented with appropriate statistical significance testing.

3. **Clear attention visualization**: The attention visualization in Figure 5 provides compelling evidence that the model is learning to focus on relevant substructures (e.g., origin, destination, time), supporting the authors' claims about how the knowledge-guided attention mechanism works.

4. **Practical implementation and efficiency**: The paper demonstrates that the model can be implemented efficiently, with the authors noting that "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words in the input sentence." This addresses a common concern about the scalability of structured neural models.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of clear distinction from prior work**: While the authors claim novelty, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish K-SAN from prior work that used linguistic structures for attention (e.g., Tai et al., 2015; Ma et al., 2015a). The authors should more clearly articulate how their attention mechanism differs from previous tree-based approaches. The paper states that "most previous work utilized such linguistic knowledge as additional features," but it's not clear how K-SAN's attention mechanism differs from the tree-based attention used in Tai et al. (2015).

2. **Limited scope of knowledge sources tested**: The paper only tests dependency parsing and AMR as knowledge sources. While the authors claim generalization to different knowledge types, they don't demonstrate this with a wide variety of knowledge sources. It would be much more convincing if they tested with completely different knowledge sources (e.g., knowledge graphs like Freebase) to show the framework's true generality.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper lacks critical ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the knowledge-guided attention mechanism. For example, it would be valuable to see how much the model improves by using attention versus simply concatenating knowledge-guided substructures as features. This would strengthen the claim that the attention mechanism is the key innovation.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does K-SAN specifically differ from the tree-structured attention approach in Tai et al. (2015), which also uses dependency trees to guide attention? The authors claim novelty, but the distinction isn't clear.

2. The paper only tests dependency parsing and AMR as knowledge sources. Could you provide an example of how K-SAN would work with a completely different type of knowledge source, such as a knowledge graph like Freebase? What would the knowledge-guided substructures look like in that case?

3. The paper claims that K-SAN avoids error propagation from knowledge extraction, but the dependency parser is still used to extract knowledge. How does K-SAN specifically avoid error propagation from parser errors? Could you provide an analysis showing how K-SAN performs when the parser is intentionally corrupted?

## Recommendation
Accept

## Justification
This paper presents a genuinely novel and bold approach to integrating external knowledge into neural models for NLU. The conceptual shift from using knowledge as features to using knowledge as a "teacher" that guides attention is significant and addresses a fundamental limitation of prior work. The approach is well-motivated, well-executed, and demonstrates strong empirical results.

While the paper has some weaknesses in clearly distinguishing itself from prior work and demonstrating the full scope of its generalization, these are relatively minor compared to the novelty and impact of the core idea. As an ACL reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological perfection, I find the conceptual contribution of this paper to be substantial and worthy of acceptance. The strong empirical results on the ATIS benchmark further support the significance of the work.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by introducing a new paradigm for knowledge integration that could be applied to many other NLP tasks beyond NLU. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel approach that ACL should encourage.
