

# Peer Review: Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach to natural language understanding (NLU) that uses external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention mechanisms rather than simply incorporating knowledge as additional features. The authors claim K-SAN learns to automatically focus on important substructures within input sentences by leveraging external knowledge sources (dependency relations, semantic graphs, etc.) in an end-to-end fashion. The main contributions I see as most significant are:

1. **Conceptual distinction between knowledge integration approaches**: The paper clearly differentiates between using knowledge as additional features (which can propagate errors) versus using knowledge as a teacher to guide attention (which avoids this issue).
2. **Demonstrated generalization across knowledge sources**: The model works effectively with various knowledge representations (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring specific schema.
3. **Robustness on small datasets**: The model shows significant improvements on smaller training datasets, suggesting better generalization capabilities.

## Strengths

1. **Clear conceptual contribution**: The paper makes a compelling argument for using knowledge as a "teacher" rather than as additional features. This distinction is important and addresses a real limitation in prior work where error propagation from knowledge extraction could degrade performance. The authors convincingly argue that their approach avoids this limitation while still leveraging knowledge effectively.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The experimental results demonstrate statistically significant improvements (p < 0.05) over strong baselines, particularly on smaller training datasets. The improvement on the Small dataset (1.5-2.5% F1) is especially noteworthy, suggesting K-SAN's value in data-scarce scenarios common in practical NLU applications.

3. **Good analysis of attention mechanisms**: The attention visualization (Figure 5) provides compelling evidence that the model is learning to focus on meaningful substructures rather than random patterns. The consistency of attention patterns across different training set sizes is particularly convincing for the model's robustness.

4. **Demonstrated generalization across knowledge sources**: The experiments with different knowledge resources (Stanford parser, SyntaxNet, rule-based AMR, JAMR) show consistent performance improvements across different knowledge representations, supporting the claim of generalization.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares against relatively dated baselines (e.g., CRF, RNN, Tree-RNN) but doesn't include comparisons with more recent approaches like BERT-based models or transformer architectures that have since become standard for NLU tasks. This limits the assessment of K-SAN's relative standing in the current NLU landscape.

2. **Insufficient analysis of model complexity**: While the authors claim "efficiency and parallelizability," they provide no concrete evidence about computational complexity, inference time, or scalability. The paper states that "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words," but doesn't quantify this advantage or compare it to baselines.

3. **Lack of failure case analysis**: The paper demonstrates strong performance but doesn't analyze when and why K-SAN fails. Understanding failure modes would be valuable for assessing the model's practical limitations and potential for improvement.

4. **Questionable novelty of attention mechanism**: The attention mechanism used (equation 1) is quite simple (inner product with softmax) and bears strong resemblance to existing attention mechanisms in neural networks. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this specific attention mechanism is novel or better than alternatives.

5. **Limited discussion of knowledge quality impact**: The paper notes that "the performance of these approaches highly depends on the quality of 'whole' sentence parsing," but doesn't analyze how K-SAN handles noisy or incomplete knowledge sources. This is particularly important since external knowledge (like dependency parses) often contains errors.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does K-SAN compare to recent transformer-based models (e.g., BERT) on the ATIS benchmark? Given that transformer architectures have become standard for NLU tasks, a comparison would better position your work in the current landscape.

2. Could you provide more details on the computational efficiency claims? Specifically, what is the exact speedup or complexity improvement compared to baselines like Tree-RNN or DCNN?

3. The paper notes that dependency parsing errors affect performance. Could you analyze how K-SAN handles cases where the external knowledge (e.g., dependency tree) contains errors? Does the model show robustness to knowledge quality variations?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with a clear conceptual contribution that addresses a real limitation in prior work. The empirical results are strong, particularly showing significant improvements on smaller datasets. The model demonstrates good generalization across different knowledge sources, which is valuable for practical applications. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing against more recent state-of-the-art models and providing more detailed analysis of computational efficiency and failure modes. While the attention mechanism itself isn't particularly novel, the conceptual approach of using knowledge as a "teacher" is well-motivated and supported by strong empirical results.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to address the comparison with recent approaches and provide more concrete evidence for the efficiency claims. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of knowledge-guided NLU, especially for data-scarce scenarios.
