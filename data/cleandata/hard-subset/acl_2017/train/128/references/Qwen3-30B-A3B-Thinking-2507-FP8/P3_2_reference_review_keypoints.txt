- Proposes K-SAN: a model that uses external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention over knowledge-guided substructures for slot tagging.
- Model learns attention weights over substructures (e.g., dependency root-to-leaf paths) to produce more informative sentence representations.
- Evaluated on ATIS and shows consistent improvements over baselines, especially with very small training data.
- Core contribution: a knowledge-guided attention mechanism that directs where the model should pay attention rather than using knowledge as raw features.
- Contribution: end-to-end learning of knowledge guidance within the neural network to reduce error propagation from pre-extracted features.
- Contribution: claimed flexibility to work with different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR) without requiring specific schemas.
- Strength: addresses error-propagation problem in prior feature-based knowledge integration with a well-motivated attention design.
- Strength: empirical gains are meaningful, notably improved generalization on small-data settings (statistically significant F1 gains).
- Strength: provides clear theoretical motivation for integrating structured attention into NLU.
- Weakness: overall architecture lacks strong novelty—combines existing components (parsing, attention, RNNs/CNNs) rather than introducing fundamentally new building blocks.
- Weakness: comparisons are inadequate—missing evaluations versus modern SOTA methods (e.g., GNNs, BERT-based slot fillers).
- Weakness: claim of being the first end-to-end knowledge-guided neural approach is overstated and factually questionable given prior work (e.g., Liu 2018, Zhang 2019).
- Weakness: insufficient ablation studies to isolate the contributions of attention vs. knowledge encoding and robustness to noisy/partial knowledge.
- Weakness: questionable generalization claim—only evaluated on dependency trees and AMR, not broader knowledge representations.
- Question: clarify novelty relative to other end-to-end knowledge-integration papers (e.g., Liu 2018, Zhang 2019).
- Question: why omit comparisons with recent SOTA models like BERT-based slot fillers and GNN approaches?
- Question: provide analysis of how attention distributions change with different training data sizes to support robustness claims.
- Question: explain why different knowledge encoders (CNN/RNN/NN) perform differently across dataset sizes.
- Question: justify the choice of root-to-leaf dependency paths as the substructure and whether other decompositions were considered.
- Additional: paper is well-written and visualizations (attention heatmaps) are helpful, but claims should be toned down and supplemented with more comparisons and ablations.
