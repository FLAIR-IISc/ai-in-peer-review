- Summary: Proposes K-SAN, which uses external knowledge to guide attention over sentence substructures for slot filling; shows SOTA on ATIS and works with dependency trees and AMR.

- Contribution 1: Novel attention mechanism where external knowledge teaches the model to focus on relevant substructures instead of being used only as features.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates improved generalization, especially with limited training data.

- Contribution 3: Flexible across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependencies, rule-based/JAMR AMR).

- Strength: Strong empirical results with statistically significant improvements and attention visualizations supporting the method.

- Strength: Addresses prior methods' limitations (error propagation from feature-based knowledge) via end-to-end knowledge-guided attention.

- Strength: Robust generalization across multiple knowledge representations and parsers.

- Strength: Clear, well-motivated model architecture grounded in attention/memory network theory.

- Weakness: Core idea lacks substantial novelty relative to prior attention and syntax-aware models.

- Weakness: Insufficient ablation study to isolate effects of attention vs. substructure representations.

- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope (only ATIS), reducing evidence of broader applicability.

- Weakness: Claims of efficiency/parallelizability lack quantitative runtime or memory comparisons.

- Question: Please provide ablation comparing attention vs. using substructures without attention.

- Question: How does the model handle utterances with no relevant substructures?

- Question: Provide training time and memory usage comparisons with baselines.

- Question: Discuss challenges and plans for scaling to larger, more diverse datasets.

- Overall recommendation: Weak Accept — valuable and well-executed but not transformative; needs stronger ablation, broader evaluation, and computational analysis.
