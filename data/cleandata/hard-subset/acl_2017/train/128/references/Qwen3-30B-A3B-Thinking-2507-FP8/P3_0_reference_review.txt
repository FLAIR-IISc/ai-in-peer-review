

# Peer Review: "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach for natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. The key innovation is using external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses, semantic graphs) to guide attention mechanisms during slot filling, rather than treating knowledge as static features. The authors claim their model automatically learns where and how much to focus attention on different substructures of the input sentence, which they argue avoids error propagation from external parsers and improves generalization.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **Demonstrated robustness to limited training data**: The model shows significant performance improvements over baselines, particularly on small datasets (Small and Medium), suggesting better generalization capabilities.
2. **Flexibility across knowledge sources**: The model effectively utilizes various types of knowledge representations (dependency trees, semantic graphs) without requiring specific schema, demonstrating strong generalization.
3. **Practical implementation of knowledge-guided attention**: The proposed attention mechanism provides a concrete way to incorporate structured knowledge into sequence tagging models.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful limitation in prior work**: The paper correctly identifies a key problem in the literature: most prior approaches treat knowledge as additional features that can introduce error propagation (e.g., if the parser makes mistakes, the features become unreliable). The authors' approach of using knowledge to guide attention rather than as features is a meaningful step toward solving this problem, and their experimental results on small datasets strongly support this claim. The 2-3 point F1 improvement on the Small dataset (74.60 vs. 73.57 for the best baseline) is substantial in this context.

2. **Strong empirical validation of generalization capability**: The experiments with different knowledge sources (Stanford parser vs. SyntaxNet, rule-based AMR vs. JAMR) provide compelling evidence that the approach isn't tied to a specific parser or knowledge representation. The consistent performance across these variations (all K-SAN models outperforming baselines by a significant margin) demonstrates the robustness of the approach, which is a major strength.

3. **Clear demonstration of attention mechanism utility**: The attention visualization in Figure 5 is particularly compelling, showing that the model learns to focus on relevant substructures even when the parser makes errors. This visualization provides concrete evidence that the model is learning meaningful attention patterns rather than simply memorizing training examples.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper claims "to our knowledge, this is the first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion," but this is inaccurate. Memory networks (Weston et al., 2015) and dynamic memory networks (Xiong et al., 2016) explicitly use external knowledge to guide attention in an end-to-end manner. The paper also fails to distinguish itself from prior work on structural attention, such as the tree-based RNNs (Tai et al., 2015) that use attention over tree structures. This misrepresentation of the related work weakens the paper's contribution claim.

2. **Lack of evidence for claimed efficiency benefits**: The paper claims "efficiency and parallelizability" as a contribution, stating that "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words in the input sentence." However, the paper provides no empirical evidence for this claim. There are no comparisons of training or inference times against baseline models, making this claim unsubstantiated and potentially misleading.

3. **Limited evaluation scope**: The paper only evaluates on the ATIS dataset, which is a relatively small, well-studied benchmark for NLU. The authors do not demonstrate the model's performance on more diverse datasets or in more challenging real-world scenarios. This limits the generalizability of their findings and makes it difficult to assess whether the approach truly generalizes beyond the specific characteristics of the ATIS corpus.

4. **Insufficient analysis of attention mechanisms**: While the paper provides attention visualizations, it lacks a detailed analysis of what the attention patterns actually represent. The paper doesn't address whether the attention patterns correspond to meaningful linguistic or semantic structures, or whether they differ meaningfully from attention patterns learned by standard sequence models. This weakens the claim that the model is truly learning to focus on relevant substructures.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that the model's performance depends on the quality of the external parser (Figure 5 shows an example where the parser made errors), but it doesn't thoroughly explore the implications of this dependency. The authors should discuss how the model would perform when using a more accurate parser, or whether the attention mechanism can compensate for parser errors to a greater extent than shown in the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first to use knowledge as a "teacher" in an end-to-end manner, but memory networks (Weston et al., 2015; Xiong et al., 2016) explicitly use external knowledge to guide attention. How does K-SAN fundamentally differ from these approaches, and why should they be considered distinct?

2. You claim "efficiency and parallelizability" as a contribution, but provide no evidence. Can you provide training and inference time comparisons with baseline models to substantiate this claim?

3. The paper only evaluates on the ATIS dataset. How would the model perform on more diverse datasets (e.g., MultiWOZ, SNIPS) that contain more complex dialogue structures and multiple domains?

4. The attention visualization in Figure 5 shows attention to both words and relations, but it's unclear how this attention differs from what a standard attention mechanism would learn. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the attention patterns for correct vs. incorrect predictions?

5. How does the model's performance change when using a more accurate dependency parser (e.g., a neural parser) instead of the Stanford parser? Does the model's robustness to parser errors depend on the quality of the parser?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in NLU and demonstrates a useful approach for incorporating structured knowledge into sequence tagging models. The empirical results are strong, particularly the improvements on small datasets, and the generalization across different knowledge sources is compelling.

However, the paper's novelty claim is overstated, and it lacks sufficient evidence for some of its claims (particularly regarding efficiency). The limited evaluation scope also weakens the impact of the findings.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the concerns about novelty, provide evidence for the efficiency claim, and expand their evaluation to more diverse datasets. The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution with these revisions, but as it stands, the claims require more careful justification.

## Additional Comments

The paper's writing is generally clear and well-organized, with good use of figures to illustrate the model and results. The technical details are mostly well-explained, though the related work section would benefit from a more nuanced comparison with prior attention-based approaches.

The authors should consider how their approach might be extended to handle more complex dialogue contexts, where understanding the conversation history is crucial. While the paper focuses on single-turn NLU, this limitation should be acknowledged in the conclusion.
