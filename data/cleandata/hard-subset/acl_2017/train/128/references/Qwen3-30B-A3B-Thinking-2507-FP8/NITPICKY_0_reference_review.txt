

# ACL Review: Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural network model for natural language understanding (NLU) that leverages external knowledge (e.g., dependency relations) to guide attention mechanisms during sequence tagging. The authors claim their model is the first to use general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion for NLU tasks, with the goal of better generalization, especially with limited training data. They evaluate their approach on the ATIS benchmark dataset, showing improvements over baselines, particularly on smaller datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Knowledge-guided attention mechanism**: The paper proposes a method to incorporate external knowledge (dependency relations) into the attention mechanism for sequence tagging, with the claim that it's the first end-to-end approach to do so.
2. **Generalization across knowledge sources**: The model is designed to work with various types of knowledge (dependency relations, knowledge graph-specific relations, etc.).
3. **Robustness to data scarcity**: The authors claim their model achieves better performance with limited training data, particularly on the small dataset.

## Strengths
1. **Relevant problem addressed**: The paper tackles an important challenge in NLU - leveraging external knowledge to improve performance, especially in low-resource scenarios. This is a valuable direction for research.
2. **Promising results on small datasets**: The model shows significant improvements over baselines on the small dataset (1/40 training data), which is a meaningful contribution for low-resource NLU applications.
3. **Attention visualization**: The attention visualization in Figure 5 provides useful insights into how the model utilizes knowledge-guided structures, making the approach more interpretable.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of clear novelty claim**: The paper claims to be the first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion, but prior work by Liu et al. (2013) and Chen et al. (2015) already used linguistic knowledge (parse trees) for language understanding. The paper fails to clearly articulate how K-SAN differs from these prior approaches. For example, Liu et al. (2013) used parse trees as input to their model, which is similar to K-SAN's approach but without the attention mechanism. The authors don't make this distinction clear, making their novelty claim unsubstantiated.

2. **Methodological inconsistency**: The paper states "we do not utilize the dependency relation labels in the experiments for better generalization" (Section 3.1), which is highly problematic. Dependency relation labels are precisely what make dependency parsing useful for capturing linguistic structure. If they're not using the labels, what's the point of using dependency parsing? This undermines the core of their approach. The paper should either explain how they're using dependency parsing without relation labels or justify why they're not using the labels that make dependency parsing meaningful.

3. **Inadequate statistical validation**: The paper claims statistical significance with "† indicates that the performance is significantly better than all baseline models with p < 0.05 in the t-test," but provides no details on how the t-test was conducted or how multiple comparisons were handled. In NLP research, this is a serious omission. They should have provided more details about the statistical testing procedure, including whether they corrected for multiple comparisons (e.g., Bonferroni correction), which is essential for claiming statistical significance across multiple experiments.

4. **Misleading claims about attention**: The paper states in Figure 5 that "the dependency relations are incorrectly parsed by the Stanford parser in this example, but our model is still able to benefit from the structural information." This is misleading. If the dependency relations are incorrectly parsed, the model isn't actually benefiting from "structural information" but rather might be learning to ignore incorrect structures. The authors should clarify how the model is able to benefit from incorrect structural information, or acknowledge that the model is robust to parser errors rather than benefiting from incorrect structure.

5. **Formatting and consistency issues**: The paper contains multiple inconsistent formatting issues that suggest a lack of attention to detail:
   - Inconsistent citations: "Tur and De Mori, 2011" in the text vs. "Tur and Mori, 2011" in references
   - Inconsistent terminology: "back-end" vs. "backend" (e.g., "back-end knowledge and action sources" vs. "backend results")
   - Inconsistent hyphenation: "linear chain" vs. "linear-chain"
   - Confusing notation: "3 RNN" in Table 1 (what does the "3" represent? The number of RNN layers? The number of encoders?)
   - Inconsistent reference formatting: "Banarescu et al.,? 2013" should be "Banarescu et al., 2013" without the question mark

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states "we do not utilize the dependency relation labels in the experiments for better generalization" (Section 3.1). Can you clarify why you're not using the dependency relation labels, which are typically the most informative part of dependency parsing? How does this approach still leverage linguistic structure?

2. The paper claims to be the first end-to-end approach to use general knowledge as guidance, but Liu et al. (2013) and Chen et al. (2015) used parse trees as input to their models. How is K-SAN fundamentally different from these prior approaches? Please clarify the key methodological differences.

3. The paper claims statistical significance with p < 0.05 but doesn't provide details on how the t-test was conducted or how multiple comparisons were handled. Could you provide more details on your statistical testing procedure, including whether you corrected for multiple comparisons?

4. In Figure 5, the dependency relations are incorrectly parsed, yet the paper claims the model "benefits from structural information." How is the model benefiting from incorrect structural information? Is it learning to ignore incorrect parses, and if so, how does this differ from simply being robust to parsing errors?

5. What does the "3" represent in "K-SAN (CNN) 3 RNN" in Table 1? This notation is confusing and not explained anywhere in the paper.

## Final Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper addresses a relevant problem in NLU and shows promising results on smaller datasets, the fundamental issues with the paper's novelty claim, methodological consistency, statistical validation, and numerous formatting inconsistencies are too severe to overlook. The lack of clear distinction from prior work, the questionable decision to not use dependency relation labels, and the misleading claims about attention all significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The formatting and consistency issues further suggest a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for an ACL submission. A paper with these issues would require substantial revision before it could be considered for publication at ACL.
