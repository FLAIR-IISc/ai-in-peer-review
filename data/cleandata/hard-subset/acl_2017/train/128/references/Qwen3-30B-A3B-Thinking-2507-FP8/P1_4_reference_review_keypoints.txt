- Proposes K-SAN: uses external knowledge as a teacher to guide attention to input substructures, learned end-to-end.  
- Main novelty: treating knowledge as attention guidance rather than additional features, reducing error propagation and improving generalization.  
- Shows significant performance gains on small-data settings, demonstrating practical value when labeled data is scarce.  
- Model is flexible across knowledge sources, working with dependency parses and AMR graphs.  
- Empirical results on ATIS show consistent improvements over baselines, notably a 1.03 F1-point gain on the smallest training split.  
- Attention visualizations indicate the model focuses on relevant substructures (origin, destination, time) robustly across dataset sizes.  
- Demonstrated robustness across different knowledge extractors (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet; Rule-Based AMR vs. JAMR).  
- Limited novelty: core mechanism builds on existing memory networks and attention work and is not sufficiently differentiated from prior literature.  
- Inadequate comparison with recent state-of-the-art models (e.g., transformer-based models like BERT) weakens claims of achieving state-of-the-art.  
- No analysis of how knowledge extraction errors affect model performance or degradation under noisy/incomplete knowledge.  
- Efficiency and parallelizability claims are unsupported by runtime or scalability evidence.  
- Asks authors to analyze model performance under erroneous external knowledge and provide robustness evidence.  
- Requests comparisons with transformer-based NLU models (e.g., BERT fine-tuned for slot filling).  
- Requests runtime comparisons or scalability analysis to substantiate efficiency claims.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept, contingent on addressing novelty positioning, comparisons with recent approaches, and efficiency/robustness analyses.
