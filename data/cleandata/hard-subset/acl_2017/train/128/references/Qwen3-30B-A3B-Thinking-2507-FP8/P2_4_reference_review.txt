

# Review for ACL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach for natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. The core idea is to use external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses, semantic graphs) to guide attention mechanisms in neural networks for better slot filling performance. Rather than using knowledge as additional features, K-SAN treats knowledge as a "teacher" that guides where and how much attention the model should pay to different substructures in the input sentence.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1. A novel end-to-end framework that automatically learns attention guided by external knowledge for NLU tasks, with demonstrated improvements especially on small datasets.
2. Demonstrated flexibility in handling different types of knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring specific schema.
3. Evidence of improved robustness to data scarcity, a significant practical advantage for real-world NLU applications.

## Strengths

1. **Novel integration of knowledge guidance in attention mechanisms**: The paper proposes a genuinely novel way to incorporate external knowledge into neural networks for NLU. Unlike previous work that used knowledge as additional features, K-SAN uses knowledge to guide attention directly within the model architecture. This is well-motivated by the limitations of feature enrichment approaches (poor generalization, error propagation) and is clearly demonstrated through the attention visualization in Figure 5. The model's ability to work with imperfect external knowledge (as shown in Figure 5) is particularly impressive.

2. **Strong empirical evidence of data efficiency**: The paper demonstrates that K-SAN provides significant improvements over baselines especially when training data is limited (Table 1 shows +5.1 F1 points on Small dataset vs. best baseline). This is a valuable practical contribution for NLU systems where labeled data is scarce. The model's ability to generalize better from limited data addresses a real-world constraint in dialogue systems development.

3. **Demonstrated flexibility across knowledge sources**: The paper shows K-SAN works well with multiple knowledge sources (dependency trees from different parsers, AMR graphs from different parsers) with consistent performance improvements (Table 2). This demonstrates the model's generalizability beyond a specific knowledge representation, making it more practical for real-world deployment where knowledge sources may vary.

4. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is well-written with clear explanations of the model architecture (Figure 2), thorough experimental setup (Section 5.1), and meaningful analysis (Figures 5-6). The attention visualization provides strong evidence for the model's intended behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent SOTA methods**: The paper compares against older baselines (CRF, RNN, DCNN, Tree-RNN) but does not include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art NLU approaches, such as BERT-based models or transformer architectures that have become dominant in NLP. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess the true significance of the claimed "state-of-the-art" performance. The paper would be significantly strengthened by showing how K-SAN compares to current SOTA methods.

2. **Limited novelty in the core mechanism**: While the application is interesting, the core mechanism (using attention to combine substructure representations) is not substantially different from existing attention-based approaches in NLP. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its attention mechanism from those used in other successful models (e.g., in machine translation or question answering). The novelty appears more in the application to NLU than in the underlying mechanism itself.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (knowledge encoding module, attention mechanism, etc.). Without these, it's difficult to determine which parts of the model are most important for the performance gains. For instance, how much of the improvement comes from the attention mechanism versus simply incorporating the knowledge representations?

4. **Lack of discussion on computational complexity**: The paper claims "efficiency and parallelizability" as a contribution but doesn't provide any empirical evidence or analysis of the computational cost. How does the training time compare to baselines? Does the model scale well with larger knowledge representations? These details are critical for assessing the practical utility of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did the authors choose not to compare K-SAN with more recent SOTA NLU methods (e.g., BERT-based approaches)? How would K-SAN perform relative to these models?

2. The paper mentions that the Stanford parser sometimes produces incorrect dependency parses (Figure 5), yet the model still performs well. Can you provide more details about how the model handles such errors, and whether there are specific error patterns the model is robust to?

3. Could you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component (knowledge encoding, attention mechanism) to the overall performance gain?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting approach to integrating knowledge into neural network models for NLU, with clear benefits in data efficiency and flexibility across knowledge sources. The core idea of using knowledge to guide attention is well-motivated and well-executed. However, the lack of comparison with recent SOTA methods and insufficient novelty in the core mechanism limit the paper's impact. With the suggested improvements (particularly adding comparisons to current SOTA and providing ablation studies), this could be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid but not groundbreaking paper that makes a useful incremental contribution to the NLU literature.

I recommend **weak acceptance** (3.5/5), with the understanding that the authors could significantly strengthen the paper by addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The paper has merit but needs to better position itself within the current landscape of NLP research.
