- Proposes K-SAN: an end-to-end model that uses external knowledge (e.g., dependency relations) to guide attention for sequence tagging/slot filling.
- Encodes knowledge-guided substructures, applies attention to weigh them, and integrates with input to improve slot filling.
- Claims: end-to-end knowledge-guided attention, generalization across knowledge types, and efficiency via parallelizable substructure modeling.

- Strength: Addresses error propagation from feature extraction by learning attention end-to-end using external knowledge as guidance.
- Strength: Shows compelling empirical gains in low-resource settings (notably on 1/40 training data), improving F1 by ~1.5–2 points.
- Strength: Demonstrates robustness to different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR) with consistent performance.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—similar to memory networks and prior structure-aware models; insufficiently distinguishes K-SAN from related work.
- Weakness: Superficial attention analysis; lacks deeper explanation of why certain substructures get high attention or how attention relates to error types.
- Weakness: Relies on external dependency parser but does not quantify robustness to parsing errors or their impact on performance.
- Weakness: Fails to compare against more recent knowledge-based methods (e.g., GNNs, transformer-based knowledge integration).
- Weakness: Efficiency and parallelizability claims are unsubstantiated by runtime or complexity analysis.

- Question: How does K-SAN perform quantitatively when the external knowledge source (parser) produces incorrect structures?
- Question: How does K-SAN compare to recent transformer-based approaches that integrate external knowledge, and why were such comparisons omitted?
- Question: In what way is using knowledge as a "teacher" different from simply providing knowledge as additional input features?

- Conclusion: Valuable incremental contribution addressing error propagation and showing strong low-resource results, but novelty and some claims are insufficiently supported; needs stronger analysis and comparisons.
