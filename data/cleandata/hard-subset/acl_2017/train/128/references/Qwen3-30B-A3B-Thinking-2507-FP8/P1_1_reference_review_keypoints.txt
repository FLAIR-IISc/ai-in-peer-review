- Proposes K-SAN: a neural architecture that uses external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention over knowledge-guided substructures for sequence tagging.

- Encodes substructures (e.g., dependency tree paths) and computes attention to create sentence representations for slot filling.

- Evaluated on ATIS, showing K-SAN outperforms CRF, RNN, CNN, DCNN, and Tree-RNN baselines, particularly with limited training data.

- Demonstrates generalization across knowledge types (dependency trees and AMR graphs) and across different parsers.

- Main contribution: reframing external knowledge as a teacher for attention rather than as additional features to avoid error propagation.

- Main contribution: end-to-end integration of knowledge allowing the model to learn important substructures without feature engineering.

- Main contribution: robustness to different knowledge representations without schema-specific adaptations.

- Strength: novel conceptual shift addressing error propagation from prior processing steps.

- Strength: strong empirical gains in data-scarce settings (notably a 2–3% absolute improvement on small data).

- Strength: consistent improvements across multiple knowledge sources and parsers, indicating versatility.

- Weakness: lacks comparison to recent state-of-the-art models (e.g., BERT-based or modern knowledge-enhanced transformers like K-BERT).

- Weakness: insufficient analysis/evidence of how the "knowledge as teacher" mechanism concretely improves attention compared to other methods.

- Weakness: no systematic ablation studying the effect of knowledge quality or parser errors on final performance.

- Question: need analysis showing how knowledge-extraction errors (e.g., parsing mistakes) impact K-SAN versus feature-based approaches.

- Question: unclear rationale for number of substructures; request ablation on how varying substructure count affects performance.

- Question: asks for insight into why different knowledge representations (dependency vs AMR) yield similar results despite structural differences.

- Additional comment: paper is well-written with clear experiments but misses comparison to modern baselines.

- Additional comment: Figure 5 visualization is helpful but should include comparisons of attention distributions with baselines.

- Additional comment: claims of computational efficiency and parallelizability need empirical training/inference time comparisons.
