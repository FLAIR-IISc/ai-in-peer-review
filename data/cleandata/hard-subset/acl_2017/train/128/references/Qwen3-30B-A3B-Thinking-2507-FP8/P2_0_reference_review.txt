

# Review of "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach for natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. The authors address limitations in traditional sequence tagging approaches that treat input as flat structures, potentially ignoring meaningful linguistic relationships. Instead, K-SAN uses external knowledge (e.g., dependency relations, semantic graphs) to guide attention to salient substructures in the input sentence. The model learns to automatically determine where and how much to focus attention based on this knowledge, rather than treating knowledge as additional features. The approach is evaluated on the ATIS benchmark dataset, showing consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly on smaller training datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Knowledge-guided attention mechanism**: The core contribution is the attention mechanism that uses external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide where the model should focus attention. This is fundamentally different from previous approaches that simply added knowledge as features.

2. **End-to-end learning of attention**: The model learns to use knowledge to guide attention without requiring manual feature engineering or external supervision for the attention weights.

3. **Robust generalization to data scarcity**: The model demonstrates significantly better performance on small training datasets, suggesting superior generalization capabilities.

## Strengths

1. **Novel attention mechanism with clear theoretical distinction**: The paper makes a strong theoretical case for why knowledge-guided attention is different from simply adding knowledge as features. The authors clearly articulate that previous approaches suffer from "poor generalization" and "error propagation" due to feature enrichment approaches, while K-SAN addresses these issues by having knowledge "play the role of a teacher to guide networks where and how much to focus attention considering the whole linguistic structure simultaneously." The empirical results (Table 1) provide strong evidence for this claim, with K-SAN outperforming all baselines, especially on the Small dataset (74.60 vs 73.57 for the best baseline).

2. **Demonstration of generalization across knowledge sources**: The paper effectively shows that K-SAN works well with different types of knowledge (dependency trees from Stanford vs. SyntaxNet, AMR graphs from rule-based parsers vs. JAMR), as shown in Table 2. This demonstrates the flexibility of the approach and supports the claim of "generalization for different knowledge" as a contribution. The consistent performance across different knowledge sources is compelling evidence for the robustness of the approach.

3. **Strong empirical validation of the core claim**: The attention visualization in Figure 5 provides clear evidence that the model learns to focus on relevant substructures (e.g., origin, destination, time) even with limited training data. The figure shows that the attention weights are meaningful and align with the semantic tags, supporting the claim that knowledge guides the model to focus on the right parts of the sentence.

4. **Clear demonstration of robustness to data scarcity**: The performance improvement on the Small dataset (1/40 training data) is particularly significant (74.60 F1 for K-SAN vs. 73.57 for the best baseline), which directly addresses the claim of "better generalization" and "less sensitive to unseen data." This is a critical advantage for real-world NLU systems where labeled data is often scarce.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with modern sequence tagging approaches**: While the paper compares with several baselines, it does not include comparisons with recent transformer-based approaches for slot filling, which have become state-of-the-art in NLP. For example, models like BERT fine-tuned for slot filling would provide a stronger benchmark. The absence of these comparisons weakens the claim that K-SAN achieves "state-of-the-art performance" on the ATIS benchmark.

2. **Limited analysis of the dependency parser's impact**: The paper acknowledges that the quality of the dependency parser affects performance (as seen in Figure 5), but it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how sensitive K-SAN is to parser errors. A more detailed analysis of this dependency would strengthen the paper's claims about the robustness of the approach.

3. **Lack of ablation study on knowledge utilization**: The paper doesn't include an ablation study to isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism versus simply using the knowledge-guided substructures as additional input. Without this, it's difficult to determine how much of the improvement comes from the attention mechanism itself versus the mere inclusion of the substructures.

4. **Insufficient theoretical justification for the approach**: While the paper describes what K-SAN does well, it doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why the attention-guided approach should outperform feature-based approaches. A more detailed analysis of the error propagation issue and how K-SAN specifically avoids it would strengthen the paper.

5. **Overstated claims about "end-to-end" learning**: The paper claims "end-to-end learning" as a key contribution, but it's not clear how this differs from simply using a knowledge-guided substructure as an additional input to a neural network. The authors don't sufficiently explain why the attention mechanism makes this truly end-to-end rather than a simple extension of previous feature-based approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims K-SAN uses knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention. Can you provide more specific evidence that the attention mechanism is actually learning to use the external knowledge as a teacher, rather than simply learning to use the substructures as additional features? How do you distinguish between these two scenarios?

2. The paper mentions that "the model automatically learns important substructures with an attention mechanism." How does this differ from simply using the knowledge-guided substructures as input features to the neural network, as done in previous work?

3. Given that transformer-based models have become dominant in NLP, how would K-SAN compare with these modern approaches for slot filling? Would the knowledge-guided attention mechanism complement or conflict with transformer architectures?

4. The paper notes that dependency parsing errors can affect performance (Figure 5), but doesn't analyze the sensitivity to parser quality. How much would K-SAN's performance degrade with a poor parser, and is there a threshold where the model would start to suffer from error propagation?

5. In Table 2, the performance with AMR graphs is similar to dependency trees despite the smaller number of substructures. Why does the model perform well with fewer substructures (e.g., 8 vs. 53), and what does this imply about the nature of the knowledge needed for effective attention?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to incorporating knowledge into sequence tagging for NLU. The core idea of using knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention is conceptually sound and the empirical results support the claims. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing with more recent approaches, providing a more thorough analysis of the dependency on external parsers, and including an ablation study to isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism.

I recommend **Accept** with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses in the author response, particularly regarding comparison with modern approaches and a more detailed analysis of the knowledge source dependency. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge-enhanced NLU, and with minor revisions, it would be a strong addition to ACL proceedings.
