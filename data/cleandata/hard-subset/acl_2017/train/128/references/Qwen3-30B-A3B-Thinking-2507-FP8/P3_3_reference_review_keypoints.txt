- Proposes K-SAN: a knowledge-guided structural attention network that uses external knowledge to guide attention over input substructures for slot filling.
- Encodes knowledge-guided substructures and computes attention weights (pi = softmax(u^T mi)) to combine with input representations.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements on ATIS, especially in low-data regimes.
- Shows practical gains over strong baselines (CRF, RNN, DCNN, Tree-RNN) with notable improvement on a small dataset.
- Generalizes across different knowledge sources (dependency parses and AMR graphs) and different parsers without schema changes.
- Conceptual contribution: framing external knowledge as a “teacher” to avoid error propagation from prior feature extraction steps.
- Strength: simple, well-motivated attention mechanism that is effective and clearly explained.
- Weakness: lacks theoretical justification for using the inner-product attention (u^T mi) versus other attention formulations.
- Weakness: does not compare against recent state-of-the-art baselines (e.g., transformer-based or graph neural network slot-filling models).
- Weakness: claims about efficiency and parallelizability are unsubstantiated by empirical runtime or resource comparisons.
- Weakness: limited and anecdotal error analysis; lacks systematic investigation of failure modes.
- Question: need justification and ablation for tying weights of Mkg and Min and its impact on performance.
- Question: why choose inner-product attention over alternatives like additive or scaled dot-product attention—request for analysis/comparison.
- Question: clarify “end-to-end” claim given reliance on an external dependency parser and discuss impact of a jointly trained parser.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address theoretical analysis of attention, stronger SOTA comparisons, and empirical support for efficiency claims.
