- Proposes K-SAN, which uses external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention rather than as additional features.  
- Claims K-SAN learns to focus on important sentence substructures using various knowledge sources in an end-to-end manner.  
- Key contributions: conceptual distinction between knowledge-as-features vs knowledge-as-teacher.  
- Key contributions: demonstrated generalization across different knowledge representations (dependency trees, AMR).  
- Key contributions: improved robustness and performance on small training datasets.  

- Strength: Clear conceptual argument that using knowledge as a teacher avoids error propagation from noisy knowledge extraction.  
- Strength: Strong empirical validation with statistically significant improvements, notably 1.5–2.5% F1 gains on small data.  
- Strength: Attention visualization shows the model focuses on meaningful substructures and is consistent across dataset sizes.  
- Strength: Consistent performance gains across different knowledge sources/parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet, JAMR, rule-based AMR).  

- Weakness: Lacks comparison to recent transformer/BERT-based state-of-the-art models, limiting contemporary relevance.  
- Weakness: Provides no concrete analysis or measurements of computational complexity, inference time, or scalability.  
- Weakness: No analysis of failure cases or when/why K-SAN fails.  
- Weakness: Attention mechanism is a simple inner-product + softmax and not clearly novel compared to existing attention methods.  
- Weakness: Insufficient exploration of how noisy or incomplete external knowledge affects performance.  

- Question: How does K-SAN compare to transformer-based models (e.g., BERT) on ATIS?  
- Question: Can you provide concrete speedups or complexity comparisons versus Tree-RNN or DCNN?  
- Question: How robust is K-SAN to errors in external knowledge (e.g., dependency parse errors)?  

- Overall: Well-written paper with a meaningful conceptual contribution and strong empirical results, especially for data-scarce settings.  
- Overall: Needs comparisons to modern baselines and more concrete analyses of efficiency and failure modes.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address SOTA comparisons and provide concrete efficiency/failure analyses.
