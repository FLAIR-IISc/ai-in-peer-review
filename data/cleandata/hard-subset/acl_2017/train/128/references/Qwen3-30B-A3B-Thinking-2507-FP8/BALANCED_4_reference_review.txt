

# Review for ACL Conference

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural network architecture for natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. The key idea is to use external knowledge (e.g., dependency parses, semantic graphs) not as additional features, but as a "teacher" to guide the attention mechanism of a neural network model. The model learns to focus attention on salient substructures within the input sentence that are relevant for slot filling.

Based on my assessment, the paper's main contributions (in order of strength) are:

1. **Demonstrated generalization capability**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence that the model generalizes better to unseen data, particularly when training data is limited (Small dataset), which is a significant practical contribution for NLU systems in real-world scenarios with data scarcity.

2. **Flexibility across different knowledge sources**: The paper effectively demonstrates that the model can work with various types of knowledge representations (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring architectural modifications, showing robustness to the specific knowledge source used.

3. **Attention-guided structural modeling**: The visualization of attention weights provides evidence that the model successfully focuses on relevant substructures, offering an interpretable mechanism for understanding how the model processes input sentences.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results on data-scarce scenarios**: The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baselines, particularly on the Small dataset (1/40 training data), where K-SAN (CNN) achieves 74.60% F1 compared to 73.57% for the best baseline. This is a compelling contribution as it addresses a critical real-world challenge in NLU systems where labeled data is often limited. The consistent improvement across all dataset sizes (Small, Medium, Large) demonstrates robustness.

2. **Effective demonstration of knowledge generalization**: The experiments with different knowledge sources (Stanford parser, SyntaxNet, Rule-Based AMR, JAMR) show that the model maintains performance across diverse knowledge representations. Table 2 clearly demonstrates that all knowledge sources lead to similar performance improvements, which strongly supports the claim that the model generalizes well to different types of knowledge.

3. **Interpretable attention visualization**: Figure 5 provides clear evidence that the model learns to focus on relevant substructures (e.g., origin, destination, time) rather than simply memorizing the input. The attention maps visually confirm the model's ability to leverage structural information, which is a significant contribution to understanding how the model works.

4. **Strong baseline comparisons**: The paper includes a thorough comparison with both traditional baselines (CRF, RNN, CNN) and structural approaches (DCNN, Tree-RNN), providing a comprehensive context for evaluating the proposed model's performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty distinction from prior work**: The paper claims novelty in "using knowledge as a teacher," but this is not sufficiently distinguished from prior work. Tree-RNN (Tai et al., 2015) and Dependency-based CNN (Ma et al., 2015a) also use structural information to guide the model. The paper doesn't clearly explain how K-SAN differs in mechanism from these approaches. For example, Tree-RNN uses tree structures to guide RNN operations, while K-SAN uses attention over substructures. The paper needs to better articulate this distinction.

2. **Overstated efficiency claims without evidence**: The paper claims "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words in the input sentence" but provides no analysis or evidence to support this claim. Without computational complexity analysis or training time comparisons with baselines, this claim appears unsubstantiated and weakens the paper's contribution.

3. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with older baselines (e.g., CRF, RNN, CNN) but misses key recent advances in NLU, such as BERT-based approaches for slot filling. For example, recent work like "BERT for Slot Filling" (Ruder et al., 2020) has become the new standard for slot filling tasks. Without comparison to these state-of-the-art models, the claimed "state-of-the-art" performance is questionable.

4. **Inadequate discussion of knowledge dependency**: The paper acknowledges that the dependency parser may make errors (as shown in Figure 5), but doesn't analyze how often the model succeeds or fails when the knowledge is incorrect. A more thorough analysis of failure cases when knowledge is inaccurate would strengthen the paper's argument about robustness.

5. **"End-to-end" claim is misleading**: The paper claims "end-to-end learning" as a contribution, but the knowledge (dependency parses) is obtained from an external parser. This means the system is not truly end-to-end, as the dependency parser is a separate component. The paper needs to clarify this limitation and explain how it would integrate a differentiable parser.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does K-SAN specifically differ from Tree-LSTM (Tai et al., 2015) and Dependency-based CNN (Ma et al., 2015a) in terms of both architecture and mechanism? The paper claims novelty but doesn't sufficiently distinguish from these related works.

2. The paper claims "end-to-end learning" but relies on an external dependency parser. Can you clarify how this is truly end-to-end, and what would be required to make the entire pipeline (including the parser) end-to-end?

3. The paper claims "modeling time may not increase linearly" with input length. Can you provide computational complexity analysis or training time comparisons with baselines to support this claim?

4. The paper compares with traditional models but not with recent BERT-based approaches for slot filling. How would K-SAN perform compared to these state-of-the-art models?

5. In Figure 5, the dependency relations are incorrectly parsed, yet the model still benefits from the structural information. How often does this happen in practice, and what's the model's performance when the knowledge is consistently inaccurate?

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper presents a well-executed study with strong empirical results on data-scarce scenarios, the claimed novelty is not sufficiently supported, and the efficiency claims lack evidence. The paper would benefit from a clearer distinction from prior work, a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art models, and additional analysis of the model's behavior when knowledge is inaccurate.

I recommend **weak rejection** with the following conditions for potential resubmission: (1) provide a more detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art models (especially BERT-based approaches), (2) clarify the "end-to-end" claim and address the dependency on external parsers, (3) provide evidence for the efficiency claims, and (4) include a more thorough analysis of failure cases when knowledge is inaccurate.

The core idea of using knowledge to guide attention is promising, but the paper needs to better position itself within the existing literature and provide stronger evidence for its claims to meet ACL's high standards.
