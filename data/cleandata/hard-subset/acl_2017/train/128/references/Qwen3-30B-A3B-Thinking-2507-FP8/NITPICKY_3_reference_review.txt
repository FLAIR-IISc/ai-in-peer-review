

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach for natural language understanding (NLU) that leverages external knowledge to guide attention mechanisms for sequence tagging. The authors claim their model addresses limitations of traditional RNNs by treating knowledge as a "teacher" that guides where and how much attention to pay to different substructures within the input sentence. The model is evaluated on the ATIS dataset, with results showing improvements over baselines, particularly with limited training data.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel neural network architecture that uses external knowledge to guide attention for sequence tagging tasks
2. Demonstration that different knowledge sources (dependency relations, AMR graphs) can be effectively incorporated into the model
3. Evidence that the model achieves better generalization with limited training data

## Strengths
1. **Genuine problem identification**: The paper correctly identifies a limitation in current sequence tagging models (treating sequences as flat structures without leveraging linguistic structures), which is a valid and important issue in NLU.

2. **Strong empirical results with limited data**: The results showing improved performance on small datasets (Small and Medium) are compelling, as this addresses a practical challenge in NLU where labeled data is often scarce. The improvement over baselines is significant (p < 0.05) for small datasets, which is meaningful for real-world applications.

3. **Good generalization to different knowledge sources**: The model's ability to work with different knowledge resources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) demonstrates robustness and practical utility, which is an important contribution for real-world deployment.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of clarity on core model mechanics**: The paper fails to clearly explain how the knowledge-guided attention mechanism actually works. For example, in section 4.2, the authors state "Mo can be added into gating mechanisms for modeling contextual knowledge similarly" but provide no mathematical details or implementation specifics. This is a major methodological flaw that prevents proper evaluation of the proposed approach.

2. **Critical omission of key results**: The paper claims that "CNN for encoding performs best on Small (75% on F1) and Medium (88% on F1), and RNN for encoding performs best on the Large set (95% on F1)," but these specific results are not presented in the tables. The only results shown are for K-SAN (CNN), with no comparison to the other encoding methods. This omission makes it impossible to assess the full scope of the model's performance and the authors' claims about which encoding method performs best.

3. **Misrepresentation of "end-to-end" learning**: The paper claims to have an "end-to-end learning" approach, but the model relies on an external dependency parser (Stanford parser) for generating the knowledge-guided substructures. This is not truly end-to-end, as the dependency parsing step is separate from the neural network training. The authors fail to address how this external dependency affects the model's robustness or error propagation.

4. **Insufficient ablation study**: The paper does not provide a thorough ablation study to show the contribution of each component (knowledge encoding, attention mechanism, etc.) to the overall performance. Without this, it's impossible to determine which aspects of the model are most important for the observed improvements.

5. **Inconsistent terminology and formatting**: The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies that undermine the paper's professionalism:
   - "applied" is cut off as "ap-" in Figure 1 description
   - "substructures" is cut off as "substruc- tures" in section 3.1
   - Inconsistent citation formatting in references (some have "et al." and some don't)
   - In Table 1, the "3" in "K-SAN (CNN) 3 RNN" appears to be a typo
   - In Figure 5 caption, "Relations and words with darker color indicate higher attention weights" but the visualization uses different color schemes for words and relations, which is confusing

## Questions to Authors
1. In section 3.2, you state "the weights of Mkg and Min are tied together based on their consistent ability of sequence encoding." Please clarify what "consistent ability of sequence encoding" means and provide the exact mechanism for tying these weights.

2. The paper claims that "CNN for encoding performs best on Small (75% on F1) and Medium (88% on F1), and RNN for encoding performs best on the Large set (95% on F1)" but these results are not shown in the tables. Please provide these specific results to support your claims.

3. You claim the model is "end-to-end" but rely on an external dependency parser. How do you address the potential error propagation from the dependency parser to your model? Can you provide results showing performance when using a different dependency parser (e.g., SyntaxNet)?

4. In section 5.5, you report that dependency trees have more substructures than AMR graphs (53 and 25 vs. 19 and 8). Why is this the case? Is this expected, or does it indicate a limitation in the AMR parsing?

5. The paper states "the proposed model presents the state-of-the-art performance on the large dataset," but doesn't compare to recent state-of-the-art models like BERT-based approaches. Please provide a comparison with the most recent NLU models.

## Recommendation
Reject

Despite the paper's interesting problem identification and promising results on small datasets, the numerous methodological ambiguities, critical omissions of key results, and misrepresentation of the model's "end-to-end" nature are significant flaws. The lack of clarity on the core model mechanics makes it impossible to properly evaluate the contribution, and the omission of key results undermines the paper's claims. These issues, combined with the formatting inconsistencies, make the paper unsuitable for publication in ACL.
