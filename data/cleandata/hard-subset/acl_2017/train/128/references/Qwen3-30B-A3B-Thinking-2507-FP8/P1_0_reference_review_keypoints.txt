- Proposes K-SAN, a model that uses external knowledge to guide attention for slot filling, aiming to reduce error propagation.  
- Key innovation: use knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention rather than as additional features.  
- Shows generalization across knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR) without schema adaptation.  
- Demonstrates strong performance on small datasets, improving generalization with limited data.  
- Addresses a meaningful NLU problem by targeting error propagation from knowledge extractors.  
- Attention visualizations (Figure 5) indicate the model focuses on salient input substructures.  
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and compares to relevant baselines.  
- Limited novelty: the attention mechanism itself is similar to existing attention-based models.  
- Lacks comparison with recent transformer-based/state-of-the-art models (e.g., BERT).  
- Claims about computational efficiency are unsubstantiated (no runtime or scalability analysis).  
- Evaluation is limited to ATIS, so domain and dataset generalizability are unclear.  
- Insufficient analysis of error cases where external knowledge is incorrect or noisy.  
- Question: Provide detailed analysis of how K-SAN handles incorrect knowledge versus other approaches.  
- Question: How would K-SAN compare to transformer-based architectures on ATIS?  
- Question: Provide runtime/scalability results to support the "modeling time may not increase linearly" claim.  
- Question: Have you evaluated K-SAN on other NLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS, MultiWOZ) for broader validity?  
- Question: Provide more examples and analysis of cases where the model overcomes parser errors.  
- Additional comment: Novelty claim is somewhat overstated; authors should better differentiate K-SAN from standard attention models and add more comparisons and evidence.
