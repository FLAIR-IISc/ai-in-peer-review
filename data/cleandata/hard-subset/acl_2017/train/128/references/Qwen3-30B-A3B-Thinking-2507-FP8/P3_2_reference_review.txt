

# Peer Review: "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel neural architecture for natural language understanding (NLU) in spoken dialogue systems. The authors argue that traditional NLU approaches treat input sequences as flat structures, ignoring valuable linguistic structures and semantic relationships. While previous work has attempted to incorporate external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees, knowledge graphs), these approaches typically add knowledge as features, which can lead to poor generalization and error propagation.

K-SAN addresses these limitations by using knowledge as a "teacher" that guides the model's attention to salient substructures in the input. The model automatically learns attention weights for different knowledge-guided substructures (e.g., paths from root to leaves in dependency trees), creating a sentence representation that's more informative for slot tagging. The paper evaluates K-SAN on the ATIS benchmark dataset, showing consistent improvements over baselines, particularly with smaller training datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Knowledge-guided attention mechanism**: The core contribution is a novel attention mechanism that leverages external knowledge to guide where the model should pay attention, rather than simply adding knowledge as features.

2. **End-to-end learning**: The model learns to incorporate knowledge guidance directly in the neural network, avoiding the error propagation issues of previous feature-based approaches.

3. **Generalization to different knowledge sources**: The model works with various types of knowledge (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring specific schema, demonstrating flexibility.

## Strengths

1. **Novel attention mechanism addressing a real problem**: The paper correctly identifies a significant limitation in current approaches to incorporating knowledge in NLU: error propagation from pre-extracted features. The knowledge-guided attention mechanism is a thoughtful solution to this problem, as it allows the model to learn which parts of the knowledge are most relevant for the task rather than being forced to use all information. The attention mechanism is properly motivated by the need to avoid error propagation, and the model architecture is well-designed to implement this.

2. **Strong empirical results with meaningful improvements**: The results show consistent improvements over baselines, particularly with smaller datasets. On the Small dataset (1/40 of training data), K-SAN (CNN) achieves 74.60 F1 vs. 73.57 for the best baseline (CNN Encoder-Tagger), which is a statistically significant improvement. This demonstrates better generalization to unseen data, which is crucial for real-world NLU systems that often face data scarcity. The improvement on the Small dataset is particularly notable, suggesting the model is more robust when training data is limited.

3. **Theoretical soundness**: The paper provides a clear theoretical motivation for why structured attention is important for NLU. The authors correctly identify that linguistic structures contain important information for semantic understanding, and that previous approaches have failed to properly integrate this information without introducing error propagation. The model architecture is well-motivated and logically follows from this analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in model architecture**: While the attention mechanism is novel, the overall model architecture is not particularly innovative. The paper essentially combines existing components (dependency parsing, attention mechanisms, RNNs) in a new way, but the core building blocks are not novel. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from previous work that also used dependency trees or semantic graphs for NLU. For instance, the Tree-RNN approach (Tai et al., 2015) also uses tree structures for NLU, and the main difference is that K-SAN uses attention over the substructures rather than processing them in tree order. This is a significant limitation given ACL's emphasis on novel architectures.

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with several baselines but doesn't compare with recent state-of-the-art approaches for NLU that use more sophisticated knowledge integration. For example, there's no comparison with models that use graph neural networks (GNNs) for knowledge integration, which have become increasingly popular for this task. The paper also doesn't compare with more recent sequence tagging models like BERT-based approaches, which would have been a stronger benchmark. The strongest baseline in the paper (CNN Encoder-Tagger) is actually an older approach from 2014 (Kim, 2014), which is not the state-of-the-art for slot filling.

3. **Overstated claim about end-to-end learning**: The paper claims to be "the first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion." This is inaccurate. Several recent papers (e.g., Liu et al. 2018, Zhang et al. 2019) have proposed end-to-end models that incorporate knowledge graphs for NLU. The authors should revise this claim to be more accurate. For instance, the paper by Liu et al. (2018) "Knowledge Graph Embedding for Spoken Language Understanding" uses a similar approach to incorporate knowledge graphs in an end-to-end manner.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component. For example, it would be valuable to see how much of the improvement comes from the attention mechanism versus the knowledge encoding. The paper also doesn't show how the model performs when using only partial knowledge or when the knowledge is incorrect. Without these ablation studies, it's difficult to determine which aspect of the model is truly responsible for the performance gains.

5. **Questionable claim about knowledge generalization**: The paper claims that K-SAN can work with different types of knowledge without requiring specific schema, but the evaluation only shows results for dependency trees and AMR graphs. There's no evidence that the model would work with other types of knowledge representations (e.g., semantic frames, frame semantics), which would be necessary to support the claim of generalization to different knowledge sources. The paper mentions "different types of parsing results, such as dependency relations, knowledge graph-specific relations, and parsing output of handcrafted grammars," but only evaluates two types.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion. However, there are several papers (e.g., Liu et al. 2018, Zhang et al. 2019) that have proposed similar approaches. Please clarify the novelty of your work in relation to these papers.

2. The paper compares with several baselines but doesn't compare with recent state-of-the-art models like BERT-based slot fillers. Why was this comparison omitted, and how would K-SAN perform against these models?

3. The paper states that the model is more robust to data scarcity, but doesn't provide analysis on how the attention weights change with different data sizes. Can you provide more detailed analysis on how the attention distribution changes as training data increases?

4. The paper mentions that different knowledge encoding models (NN, RNN, CNN) were used, but doesn't explain why CNN performed best on Small/Medium and RNN on Large. What is the underlying reason for this difference in performance?

5. The paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of why the authors chose to use dependency paths from the root to leaves as substructures, rather than other possible structural decompositions. What would happen if the authors used different structural decompositions?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and the evaluation is thorough for the chosen baselines. The visualization of attention weights (Figure 5) is particularly useful for understanding how the model leverages knowledge. The authors have clearly put effort into the technical details and the evaluation.

However, the claims about novelty and end-to-end learning need to be revised to accurately reflect the current state of the field. The paper would be stronger with a more thorough comparison to recent work in the area and with additional ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component. With these revisions, this could be a valuable contribution to the field, but as it stands, the novelty is limited and the claims need to be more carefully presented.
