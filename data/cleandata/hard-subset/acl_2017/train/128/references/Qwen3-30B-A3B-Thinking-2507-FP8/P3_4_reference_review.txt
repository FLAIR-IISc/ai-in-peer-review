

# Peer Review: "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach for natural language understanding (NLU) that leverages external knowledge (e.g., dependency relations) as a "teacher" to guide attention mechanisms in sequence tagging tasks. The model encodes knowledge-guided substructures from external resources, uses an attention mechanism to weigh these substructures, and integrates them with the input sentence to improve slot filling performance. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning of knowledge-guided attention, generalization across different knowledge types, and efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

## Main Contributions
1. **Knowledge as a teacher**: The end-to-end learning of attention guided by external knowledge, avoiding feature extraction errors from previous processing steps.
2. **Robustness to data scarcity**: Demonstrated improvements on small training datasets, suggesting better generalization capabilities.
3. **Knowledge flexibility**: Ability to work with different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring specific schema.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a critical limitation in NLU**: The paper effectively identifies a key limitation in prior work—feature extraction errors from external knowledge sources that propagate to the neural model. K-SAN's end-to-end attention mechanism elegantly avoids this problem, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Compelling empirical results on data-scarce scenarios**: The performance gains on the small dataset (1/40 training data) are particularly impressive, with K-SAN outperforming baselines by 1.5-2 F1 points. This addresses a real-world challenge in NLU where labeled data is often limited, making the contribution practically valuable.

3. **Robustness to different knowledge sources**: The evaluation across dependency trees and AMR graphs demonstrates the model's flexibility. The consistent performance across different knowledge formats (Table 2) shows the approach is not tied to a specific knowledge representation, which is a strong point for generalization.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty and insufficient differentiation from prior work**: While the paper claims novelty, the core idea of using knowledge to guide attention is quite similar to memory networks (Weston et al., 2015; Xiong et al., 2016) and previous work on incorporating linguistic structures (Ma et al., 2015a; Tai et al., 2015). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how K-SAN differs from these approaches beyond the specific NLU application. The "knowledge as teacher" concept is not substantially different from previous attention-based knowledge integration.

2. **Insufficient analysis of attention mechanisms**: The attention visualization (Figure 5) is helpful but superficial. The paper fails to provide deeper analysis of *why* certain substructures receive higher attention weights or how these attention patterns correlate with specific error types in slot filling. Without this, it's difficult to assess whether the attention mechanism is actually learning meaningful linguistic structures or simply memorizing patterns.

3. **Dependency parsing as a bottleneck**: The paper relies on an external dependency parser (Stanford Parser) for knowledge extraction, but doesn't analyze how parsing errors affect performance. Figure 5 shows an example where the parser made mistakes, yet K-SAN still performed well. However, the paper doesn't quantify how robust the model is to parsing errors or how it handles cases where the knowledge source is unreliable—critical for real-world deployment.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent knowledge-based approaches**: The paper doesn't compare with more recent approaches that leverage knowledge graphs for NLU, such as graph neural networks (GNNs) or transformer-based models with knowledge integration. This omission weakens the claim of being state-of-the-art.

5. **Overstated efficiency claims**: The paper claims "efficiency and parallelizability" as a contribution, but provides no empirical evidence to support that modeling time doesn't increase linearly with input length. Without time complexity analysis or runtime comparisons, this claim remains unsubstantiated.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does K-SAN specifically handle cases where the external knowledge source (e.g., dependency parser) produces incorrect structural information? Can you provide quantitative analysis of performance degradation when parsing errors occur?

2. How does K-SAN compare to recent transformer-based approaches that incorporate external knowledge (e.g., using knowledge graphs as input to BERT)? Given the rapid advancement in this area, why wasn't this comparison included?

3. The paper claims "knowledge as a teacher" but the attention mechanism is learned end-to-end. How is this different from simply using the knowledge as additional input features (as in prior work), rather than as a "teacher" guiding the attention process?

## Conclusion

This paper makes a valuable contribution to NLU by addressing the error propagation problem in knowledge integration. The empirical results, particularly on small datasets, are compelling and address a practical challenge. However, the novelty is limited compared to prior work, and several claims (efficiency, knowledge as "teacher") are not sufficiently supported. With stronger differentiation from prior work and more comprehensive analysis, this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, the paper makes a solid incremental contribution to the field but doesn't represent a significant leap forward.
