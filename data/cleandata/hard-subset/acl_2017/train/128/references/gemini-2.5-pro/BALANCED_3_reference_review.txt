**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper introduces the Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Network (K-SAN), a novel neural architecture for sequence tagging, demonstrated on the task of slot filling for natural language understanding. The core idea is to leverage external linguistic knowledge, such as dependency parses or AMR graphs, to guide the model's attention. The method first extracts a set of "substructures" (e.g., paths from the root to each leaf in a dependency tree) from the input utterance. These substructures, along with the full utterance, are encoded into vector representations. An attention mechanism then computes a weighted sum of the substructure representations, where the weights are determined by the relevance of each substructure to the full utterance. This final knowledge-guided representation is then used to augment a standard recurrent neural network (RNN) tagger at each time step, improving its ability to capture long-range dependencies and structural relationships relevant to the slot filling task.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and flexible method for integrating structured prior knowledge into an RNN sequence tagger. The proposed attention mechanism allows the model to softly select and weight salient structural components of an utterance, which is a more flexible approach than rigidly defined structural models (e.g., Tree-LSTMs) or simple feature engineering.
2.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation on the ATIS benchmark demonstrating that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results. The experiments convincingly show that the performance gains are particularly significant in low-data scenarios, highlighting the model's data efficiency and generalization capabilities.
3.  A clear demonstration of the framework's robustness to different sources and types of knowledge. The model maintains strong performance when using both syntactic (dependency trees) and semantic (AMR graphs) structures, and is not sensitive to the specific parser used to generate them.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed method for integrating structural knowledge is elegant and sound. By treating knowledge-derived substructures as a memory and using an attention mechanism to "query" this memory with the representation of the full sentence, the model can dynamically focus on the most relevant structural information. This is a powerful way to incorporate non-local dependencies (e.g., between a verb and its distant arguments) into a fundamentally sequential RNN model. The joint architecture that combines the standard RNN with the knowledge-guided one is a sensible way to retain the benefits of both local and structural context.
2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results on the ATIS slot filling task, outperforming a solid set of baselines, including other structure-aware models like Tree-RNN. The decision to evaluate on varying sizes of the training set is a major strength, as it provides strong evidence that the structural guidance is particularly beneficial for generalization in low-resource settings. The inclusion of statistical significance tests further validates the claims of improvement.
3.  **Demonstrated Flexibility and Generalization:** A significant strength of the paper is the exploration of different knowledge sources. By showing consistent improvements with dependency trees from two different parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and semantic graphs from two different AMR parsers (Rule-based, JAMR), the authors convincingly argue that their framework is not tied to a specific knowledge schema or parser. This makes the approach general and widely applicable.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The model is described in detail, and Figure 2 provides an excellent illustration of the overall architecture. The motivation and related work are well-contextualized, making the contributions clear.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The paper's claims are validated on a single, well-studied dataset (ATIS) and a single task (slot filling). While ATIS is a standard benchmark, its utterances are typically short and syntactically simple. The true test for a structure-aware model would be on tasks with longer, more complex sentences where syntactic relationships are more challenging to capture. It is unclear how the model's performance and computational cost would scale when the number of substructures becomes very large. Evaluating the model on an additional dataset from a different domain or with more complex linguistic phenomena would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.
2.  **Unsupported Claims of Efficiency:** The authors claim "Efficiency and parallelizability" as a contribution, suggesting that modeling time may not increase linearly with sentence length. However, this claim is not supported by any theoretical analysis or empirical evidence (e.g., runtime comparisons, complexity analysis). The model has several components that could introduce significant overhead: an external parser must be run, and then every root-to-leaf path must be encoded. For long sentences with bushy parse trees, this could be computationally expensive. A more rigorous discussion of the computational complexity is needed to substantiate this claim.
3.  **Ambiguity in Substructure Definition and Potential Underutilization of Knowledge:** The choice to define substructures as root-to-leaf paths in the parse tree feels somewhat arbitrary, and the decision to discard dependency relation labels is not well-justified. These labels (e.g., `nsubj`, `dobj`) contain rich syntactic information that could be highly beneficial for a "knowledge-guided" model. An ablation study exploring the impact of including these labels, or using alternative substructure definitions (e.g., individual dependency arcs), would be valuable. This is further confused by the attention visualization in Figure 5, which appears to show attention over individual dependency relations (the lines), even though the text states labels are not used. The link between the model's mechanism (attending over paths) and this visualization needs clarification.
4.  **Missing Simpler Structural Baseline:** While the comparisons to DCNN and Tree-RNN are valuable, the paper could benefit from a simpler baseline to better isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism. For instance, one could create features by pooling the embeddings of words in each dependency path and feeding these features into the baseline RNN tagger. This would help determine if the performance gain comes from the sophisticated attention mechanism itself or simply from making the model aware of syntactically related, non-adjacent words.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the efficiency claim? Can you provide a complexity analysis of the K-SAN model and/or empirical runtime comparisons against the baseline RNN tagger, showing how inference time scales with sentence length and parse tree complexity?
2.  Regarding the attention visualization in Figure 5: since the model computes attention over entire root-to-leaf paths, how are the attention weights for individual words and dependency arcs in the figure derived? Furthermore, could you provide more justification for the design choice of discarding dependency relation labels, and have you experimented with including them?
3.  The model's performance on the relatively simple ATIS dataset is impressive. Do you have any insights or preliminary results on how K-SAN might perform on datasets with longer sentences and more complex syntactic structures, where dependency parsers are also typically less accurate?
