### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Network (K-SAN), a novel architecture for sequence tagging tasks, demonstrated on slot filling for NLU. The core idea is to leverage external knowledge, such as dependency parses or AMR graphs, not as direct input features, but as a "teacher" to guide an attention mechanism. The model first extracts linguistic substructures from the knowledge source. It then learns to compute an attention distribution over these substructures to create a knowledge-guided sentence representation. This representation is subsequently fed into an RNN-based tagger at each timestep to improve slot prediction. The model is trained end-to-end and evaluated on the ATIS dataset, showing significant improvements over baseline and prior structural models, especially in low-data scenarios.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel and effective architecture for integrating structured knowledge into neural sequence taggers.** The "knowledge as a teacher" paradigm, where knowledge guides an attention mechanism over extracted substructures, is a compelling alternative to simply using structural information as input features. The strong empirical results validate the effectiveness of this approach.
2.  **A thorough empirical investigation demonstrating the model's robustness and generalizability.** The authors evaluate K-SAN across varying training data sizes and multiple types of knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs from different parsers). The consistent performance gains across these diverse settings provide strong evidence for the model's practical utility and flexibility.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Soundness of the Proposed Method:** The central idea of using linguistic structures to guide an attention mechanism is elegant and well-motivated. It provides a principled way to incorporate prior knowledge while mitigating the risk of error propagation from imperfect parsers. By learning to weigh different substructures, the model can focus on the most informative parts of the parse while potentially down-weighting noisy or irrelevant ones. The end-to-end nature of the framework is also a significant strength.

2.  **Strong and Significant Empirical Results:** K-SAN achieves state-of-the-art results on the ATIS slot filling benchmark. Crucially, the performance gains are most pronounced in the low-data settings (Small and Medium), highlighting the model's improved data efficiency and generalization capabilities. This is a highly valuable property for real-world applications where large annotated datasets are not always available. The reported improvements are statistically significant, lending confidence to the conclusions.

3.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is a key strength of this work. By evaluating on three different dataset sizes, the authors convincingly demonstrate the model's advantage in data-scarce scenarios. Furthermore, testing the model with four different knowledge sources (Stanford and SyntaxNet dependency parsers, Rule-based and JAMR AMR parsers) strongly supports the paper's claim of generalization. The fact that the model performs well irrespective of the specific parser or knowledge type underscores its robustness.

4.  **Good Model Analysis:** The attention visualization in Figure 5 provides useful qualitative insight into the model's behavior. It demonstrates that the model learns to assign higher attention to semantically critical words and their corresponding relations (e.g., locations and dates), which aligns with intuition and helps build confidence that the attention mechanism is functioning as intended.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for Superiority over "Knowledge as Features":** The paper motivates its approach as being superior to using knowledge as enriched features, but the experimental comparison is not direct enough. The "Structural CRF" baseline is a traditional model, while DCNN and Tree-RNN are primarily sentence embedding methods. A stronger and more direct baseline would be to augment the input of the same RNN tagger with features derived from the knowledge source (e.g., concatenating the word embedding with embeddings for its syntactic head and the dependency relation). This would allow for a true apples-to-apples comparison between the "knowledge as features" vs. "knowledge as a teacher" paradigms within an identical neural architecture.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Substructure Extraction:** The procedure for extracting substructures, a critical step in the pipeline, is not sufficiently detailed for replication. The paper states that for dependency trees, substructures are paths from the root to all leaves. However, it is unclear if function words or relation labels are included, and the examples are ambiguous. For AMR graphs, which are not necessarily trees, the process of extracting "paths" is even less clear. A precise, algorithmic description of this extraction process for both knowledge types is needed for the work to be reproducible.

3.  **Unsubstantiated Claims of Efficiency:** The third claimed contribution is "Efficiency and parallelizability," based on the idea that substructures are modeled separately. However, this claim is not supported by any theoretical or empirical evidence. The paper provides no complexity analysis, runtime comparisons, or discussion of how this parallelization would be implemented in practice. Given that the knowledge encoding module is followed by a sequential RNN, the overall efficiency benefit is not obvious and the claim feels overstated.

4.  **Unexplored Design Choices:** The paper makes several important design choices without justification. For instance, the weights of the sentence encoder (Min) and the substructure encoder (Mkg) are tied. While this reduces parameters, it's not clear if this is optimal, as sentences and extracted substructures have different properties (e.g., substructures can be non-contiguous). Similarly, the joint tagger's combination weight `α` is fixed at 0.5. Ablation studies exploring the impact of untying the weights or tuning `α` would strengthen the paper by providing a deeper understanding of the model's components.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a more precise, step-by-step description of how substructures are extracted from both dependency trees and AMR graphs? Specifically, how are non-leaf nodes handled, are dependency labels used, and how are paths defined and traversed in a graph structure like AMR?
2.  Your primary motivation is that guiding attention with knowledge is superior to using it as features. To strengthen this claim, could you compare your model against a strong baseline where structural information (e.g., embeddings of a word's syntactic head and dependency label) is directly concatenated with word embeddings as input to your RNN tagger?
3.  Regarding the claim of efficiency and parallelizability, could you provide either a computational complexity analysis or empirical runtime data to substantiate this? How does the computational cost of the K-SAN module compare to that of the final RNN tagger?
