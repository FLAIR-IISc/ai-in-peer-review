### Summary of the paper
This paper proposes the Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Network (K-SAN), a model for sequence tagging in natural language understanding, with a specific focus on slot filling. The core idea is to leverage external linguistic knowledge, such as dependency parses or AMR graphs, to guide an attention mechanism. The model first parses an input utterance to generate a set of "knowledge-guided substructures" (e.g., paths in a dependency tree). These substructures are then individually encoded into vector representations. An attention mechanism computes a weighted sum of these substructure representations, where the weights are determined by the similarity between each substructure and an encoding of the full input utterance. This final "knowledge-guided representation" is then fed as an additional input at each time step to a standard Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) tagger. The authors evaluate their model on the ATIS dataset, demonstrating improved performance over several baselines, particularly in low-data settings.

### Contributions
1.  **An Application of Attention to Syntactic Substructures for Slot Filling:** The main contribution is an architecture that uses an attention mechanism to explicitly model the importance of different syntactic or semantic substructures (derived from parsers) for the task of slot filling.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results on the ATIS Benchmark:** The paper presents state-of-the-art results on the ATIS slot filling task. The experiments are conducted across varying training data sizes, showing that the proposed method is particularly effective in low-resource scenarios.
3.  **Analysis of Robustness to Knowledge Source:** The authors demonstrate that their model's performance is robust to different sources of knowledge, achieving similar gains using dependency trees from two different parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) as well as AMR graphs from two different parsers (Rule-Based, JAMR).

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed K-SAN model achieves state-of-the-art results on the widely-used ATIS benchmark, outperforming established CRF and RNN baselines, as well as more recent structural models like Tree-RNN. The gains are statistically significant and are most pronounced when training data is scarce, which suggests the method provides a useful inductive bias that improves data efficiency.
2.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The intuition behind the model is sound. Slot filling often requires understanding long-distance relationships between words (e.g., relating a verb to its distant arguments), which are often captured more directly by syntactic dependencies than by the linear sequence of words. Using these dependencies to guide the model's focus is a logical and well-motivated strategy.
3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The authors provide a good set of experiments to validate their approach. They compare three different encoders (NN, RNN, CNN) for the substructures, evaluate on three different data scales, and test four different external knowledge sources. This comprehensive analysis strengthens the paper's conclusions about the model's effectiveness and robustness. The attention visualization in Figure 5 also provides some useful qualitative insight into the model's behavior.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The proposed K-SAN architecture is largely a novel combination of existing, well-established components. The core mechanism is a standard attention model applied over a set of inputs (the substructures), which is reminiscent of memory networks and other similar attention-based architectures. While the application to parsed substructures for slot filling is new, the underlying neural components are not. The claim of being the "first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion" seems overstated, as prior work (including some cited, like Tree-RNNs) also integrates structural knowledge into end-to-end neural models.
2.  **Evaluation is Confined to a Single, Simple Domain:** All experiments are conducted on the ATIS dataset. While ATIS is a standard benchmark, it is a single-domain, relatively clean, and now quite old dataset with limited linguistic variability. The claim of "better generalization" is not fully substantiated by showing robustness to data scarcity on just one dataset. For a method that leverages general linguistic structure, it is critical to demonstrate its effectiveness on more diverse and complex datasets (e.g., SNIPS, or datasets from other domains) to prove that the findings are not specific to the idiosyncrasies of ATIS.
3.  **Dependence on External Parsers and Unclear Handling of Errors:** The method is fundamentally dependent on the output of an external, pre-trained parser. This introduces a pipeline component, and the model's performance is thus coupled to the parser's accuracy. The authors claim their approach avoids the error propagation of feature-based methods, but it is not clear how this is achieved. While the attention mechanism might learn to down-weight irrelevant or incorrect substructures, a more systematic analysis of the model's sensitivity to parser quality is needed. The paper shows an example of a parsing error in Figure 5 but does not analyze this potential weakness rigorously.
4.  **Ambiguity in Substructure Generation:** The procedure for extracting "substructures" is not formally defined. For dependency trees, it is described as "paths from the root to all leaves," and for AMR, an example is shown in Figure 6, but a clear, reproducible algorithm is missing. For example, the order of words in a substructure (e.g., "show flights from seattle" in Figure 3 vs. "show flights seattle from" in Section 3.2) is critical for a sequence encoder like an RNN or CNN, but the principle governing this ordering is not explained. This lack of methodological clarity is a significant weakness.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the claim regarding novelty? How does your method of "knowledge as a teacher" to guide attention fundamentally differ from models like Tree-LSTMs (Tai et al., 2015) which also use tree structures to guide the composition process in an end-to-end model?
2.  Could you provide a more formal and precise algorithm for extracting and ordering the words within a "substructure" from both dependency trees and AMR graphs? This is crucial for the reproducibility of your work.
3.  Given that the model's effectiveness is demonstrated only on ATIS, how do you expect it to perform on more modern and linguistically diverse NLU benchmarks like SNIPS or multi-domain datasets? Have you performed any preliminary experiments to support the broader generalization claims?
