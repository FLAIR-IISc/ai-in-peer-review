**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper proposes the Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Network (K-SAN), a novel neural architecture for sequence tagging, demonstrated on the task of slot filling for spoken language understanding. The core idea is to leverage external linguistic knowledge, such as dependency trees or AMR graphs, not as direct input features, but as a "teacher" to guide an attention mechanism. The model first extracts a set of "substructures" (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in a dependency tree) from the linguistic parse of an utterance. It then learns an attention distribution over these substructures to compute a single, knowledge-aware sentence representation. This representation is then fed into every time step of a recurrent neural network (RNN) tagger to improve its predictions.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and conceptually interesting method for integrating structured linguistic knowledge into neural sequence models. Instead of using knowledge as static features or relying on fixed tree-structured architectures, K-SAN dynamically learns the importance of different structural components of an utterance via an attention mechanism, which is a more flexible approach to incorporating structural bias.
2.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating that this method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ATIS slot filling benchmark. The experiments convincingly show the model's robustness, particularly its strong performance in low-data settings and its ability to generalize across different types and sources of knowledge (e.g., dependency vs. AMR parsers), highlighting its practical utility.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of Knowledge Integration:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel approach to leveraging structured knowledge. The "knowledge as a teacher" paradigm, where syntactic or semantic structures define the items over which the model attends, is a clear departure from prior work. Methods like Tree-RNNs enforce a rigid compositional structure, while feature-based approaches can be brittle. K-SAN offers a compelling middle ground, using the structure to guide attention while retaining the flexibility to weigh different parts of that structure dynamically. This allows the model to potentially learn to ignore parser errors, as suggested by the example in Figure 5.

2.  **Excellent Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper presents strong results, outperforming a well-chosen set of baselines, including competitive structural models like DCNN and Tree-RNN. The improvements are particularly significant in the small and medium data settings, which provides strong evidence that the structural guidance helps the model generalize better from limited data. The ablation study on knowledge sources (Table 2) is particularly valuable, demonstrating that the framework is robust and not overly dependent on a single specific parser or knowledge representation, which is a crucial quality for real-world applications.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is clearly explained with helpful diagrams (Figure 2), and the motivation is well-articulated. The experiments are well-structured and answer key questions about the model's performance, generalization, and behavior (via the attention visualization).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Questionable Definition of "Substructures":** The method for extracting substructures—defined as paths from the root to each leaf node in the dependency tree—seems overly simplistic and potentially suboptimal. This approach has two main drawbacks: (a) It introduces significant redundancy, as the initial parts of paths from the root are repeated across many substructures. (b) It can break up meaningful syntactic constituents. For instance, in the example "show flights to san francisco" (Figure 3), the substructure is given as "show flights to francisco san", which scrambles the order of the multi-word entity "san francisco". A more principled approach, such as using individual dependency arcs (head-modifier pairs), n-grams along dependency paths, or complete subtrees as the units for attention, might capture more coherent linguistic information. The paper lacks a justification for this specific choice over other alternatives.

2.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Core Mechanism:** While the overall framework is novel, the constituent components are standard. The attention mechanism is a basic dot-product attention, and the tagger is a standard RNN/GRU. The claim of "end-to-end learning" is also slightly misleading, as the model relies on a frozen, pre-trained external parser. This means that errors from the parser are a fixed input to the system, which contradicts the spirit of a truly end-to-end model where all components are trainable. While the attention may learn to down-weight noisy substructures, this is a mitigation strategy, not a removal of the error propagation problem.

3.  **Unclear Motivation for the Joint Model:** The best-performing model is the "joint RNN tagger," which is a simple linear interpolation of a standard chain-based RNN and the proposed knowledge-guided RNN. This feels like an ad-hoc ensemble that, while effective, weakens the central claim that the knowledge-guided representation `o` is a superior way to model the sentence for tagging. The success of the joint model implies that the standard sequential modeling of the chain-based RNN captures essential information that is lost when relying solely on the dependency-based substructures. A deeper analysis of what each component contributes would strengthen the paper. For instance, is the chain model better at capturing local phrase-level coherence that is disrupted by the root-to-leaf substructure definition?

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing root-to-leaf paths as the fundamental substructures? Have you experimented with or considered alternatives like individual dependency relations (e.g., `(flights -> from, seattle)`) or complete subtrees as the items for the attention mechanism? It seems these alternatives might preserve local context and constituent structure more effectively.

2.  The "joint model" achieves the best performance by combining the knowledge-guided model with a standard sequential RNN. What does this suggest about the information captured by your proposed knowledge-guided representation `o`? Does it imply that the flat, linear sequence information remains crucial and is not fully captured by the set of dependency-based substructures you extract?

3.  Regarding the claim of "efficiency and parallelizability," could you provide a complexity analysis? While encoding substructures can be parallelized, the number of substructures scales with sentence length. For very long sentences, encoding dozens of potentially long substructures could be more computationally expensive than a single pass with a Tree-RNN. How does the practical inference time of K-SAN compare to the Tree-RNN baseline?
