### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel architecture for sequence tagging tasks in NLU, specifically slot filling. The core idea is to leverage external linguistic knowledge, such as dependency parses or AMR graphs, not as fixed input features or as a rigid structural constraint on the model, but as a form of "teacher" that guides an attention mechanism. The model first decomposes the linguistic parse of a sentence into a set of "substructures" (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in a dependency tree). It then learns to dynamically attend to these substructures to create a single, knowledge-aware sentence representation. This representation is then used to augment a standard RNN-based sequence tagger, providing it with structural context at each timestep.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and highly flexible paradigm for integrating symbolic knowledge into neural models.** The key innovation is treating linguistic structures as a dynamic, attendable memory rather than a static constraint. This "knowledge as teacher" approach, where the model learns *what* structural information is important for the task, is a significant conceptual advance over prior methods that either use structural information as simple features or force the network architecture to conform to a parse tree (e.g., Tree-RNNs).

2.  **An empirical demonstration of the value of structural priors, especially in low-data scenarios.** The paper shows that by guiding the model with explicit linguistic structure, K-SAN achieves substantial performance gains on smaller training sets, effectively demonstrating that this knowledge helps the model generalize better and more quickly.

### Strengths

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty:** The paper's primary strength is its bold and original approach. By decoupling the model's sequential processing from the hierarchical linguistic structure and connecting them with a soft attention mechanism, the authors propose a new way to bridge the gap between symbolic and connectionist methods. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing architecture; it is a genuinely new way of thinking about the problem. This kind of creative, high-risk, high-reward research is exactly what the field needs.

2.  **Robustness and Generalization of the Framework:** The authors provide compelling evidence that their framework is not tied to a specific type of knowledge. Experiments in Table 2 show that K-SAN performs consistently well when guided by different syntactic parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and even different formalisms (dependency trees vs. semantic AMR graphs). This strongly suggests that the architectural principle is sound and broadly applicable, which is a hallmark of a powerful idea.

3.  **Excellent Performance in Data-Scarce Regimes:** The results on the "Small" and "Medium" subsets of the ATIS dataset are particularly impressive. The significant F1 score improvements over strong baselines highlight the practical utility of the model. In many real-world NLU applications, large annotated datasets are not available, and methods that can effectively leverage prior knowledge to compensate for a lack of data are extremely valuable. The attention visualization (Figure 5) further supports this, suggesting the model can learn to focus on salient structures even with limited training examples.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are areas that could be improved in future work. My comments here are intended as constructive feedback rather than strong arguments for rejection.

1.  **Limited Evaluation on a Single, Simple Domain:** All experiments are conducted on the ATIS dataset. While a standard benchmark, ATIS is a relatively small, closed-domain dataset with limited linguistic variation. The true power and potential failure modes of this architecture would be better understood through evaluation on more complex and diverse datasets, such as multi-domain dialogue or open-domain question answering, where syntactic ambiguity and structural complexity are far greater. While the current evaluation is sufficient to prove the concept, its scope limits the claims of general applicability.

2.  **The Knowledge Integration Mechanism is Relatively Simple:** The final knowledge-guided representation `o` is incorporated into the RNN by simply adding it as a fixed bias vector to the hidden state update at each timestep (Equation 8). This seems like a missed opportunity for a more dynamic integration. The structural information relevant for tagging "seattle" as an origin city might be different from the information needed for "saturday" as a departure day. A more sophisticated mechanism, perhaps using gating or conditioning the attention query on the RNN's current state, could potentially yield even better results. However, for a foundational paper introducing a new framework, this simplicity is acceptable.

3.  **The "Efficiency" Contribution is Overstated:** The authors list "Efficiency and parallelizability" as a contribution, arguing that substructures are modeled separately. This seems misleading, as the main computational path is still a sequential RNN, which is inherently not parallelizable over the time dimension. Furthermore, the proposed method adds the overhead of parsing the input and encoding all substructures. This is a minor point, but the claim should be tempered.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The attention query is a static representation of the entire input sentence (`u`). Have you considered making this query dynamic? For instance, using the RNN's hidden state `h_t-1` as the query to compute attention for the current step `t`. This could allow the model to focus on different substructures as it moves through the sentence, which seems intuitively appealing.

2.  Figure 5 provides a compelling visualization of the learned attention. Could you provide more analysis on whether the model learns to attend to specific, predictable substructures when tagging certain slot types? For example, does it consistently place high attention on prepositional phrases headed by "from" when predicting `fromloc.*` slots? This would further strengthen the claim that the attention mechanism is learning interpretable linguistic patterns.
