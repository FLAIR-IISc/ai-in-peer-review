**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper introduces a Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Network (K-SAN) for natural language understanding, specifically focusing on the task of slot filling. The core idea is to leverage external linguistic knowledge, such as dependency parse trees or AMR graphs, to guide an attention mechanism. The model first parses an input utterance to extract a set of "knowledge-guided substructures" (e.g., paths from the root to leaves in a dependency tree). These substructures and the full utterance are encoded into vector representations. An attention mechanism then computes a weighted sum of the substructure representations, where the weights are determined by the similarity between each substructure and the full utterance. This final knowledge-guided representation is used as an additional input at each timestep of an RNN-based sequence tagger. The authors claim the model is end-to-end, generalizable to different knowledge sources, and efficient.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel attention-based method for incorporating structured linguistic information into a sequence tagging model. Unlike rigid tree-based RNNs, this approach offers a more flexible, soft-selection mechanism over linguistic fragments.
2.  An empirical evaluation on the ATIS dataset demonstrating that the proposed model achieves strong results, particularly in low-data settings, outperforming several baseline and structural models.
3.  A robustness analysis showing that the model's performance is consistent across different sources of knowledge (dependency vs. AMR) and different parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet, JAMR).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty of the Attention Mechanism:** The central mechanism—using a sentence embedding to attend over embeddings of its own linguistic substructures—is an elegant and interesting contribution. It provides a compelling alternative to prior work that either uses knowledge as simple input features or enforces a rigid compositional structure (e.g., Tree-LSTMs). This soft, data-driven approach to selecting salient structural components is a primary strength.
2.  **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The experiments convincingly show that K-SAN provides a substantial performance boost when training data is scarce (Small and Medium datasets in Table 1). The F1 score improvements of +1.03 on Small and +2.47 on Medium over the strongest baseline (CNN Encoder-Tagger) are significant. This suggests the model's structural bias provides a valuable regularizing effect, which is a highly desirable property.
3.  **Thorough Generalization Experiments:** The experiments in Section 5.5, which swap out different parsers and knowledge representations (syntactic vs. semantic), are a strong point of the paper. The model's ability to maintain high performance across these varied conditions provides solid evidence for its robustness and generalizability, supporting a key claim made by the authors.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity and Justification:** The description of the core method lacks precision, leaving key details ambiguous.
    *   **Substructure Extraction:** The process of creating substructures is poorly defined. For dependency trees, it is described as "paths from the root to all leaves." However, the example in Figure 3 ("show flights to francisco san") contains an unusual word order that is not explained. A formal algorithm or unambiguous description is necessary. The exclusion of non-leaf nodes "to reduce the duplicated information" feels ad-hoc and requires stronger justification. The process for AMR graphs is even more opaque.
    *   **Tied Encoders (Mkg and Min):** The paper states that the weights for the substructure encoder and the sentence encoder are tied, justified by their "consistent ability of sequence encoding." This is not a rigorous justification. A full sentence and a short phrasal substructure have very different properties, and forcing them to share an encoder is a strong, non-obvious design choice that warrants an ablation study or a more theoretical argument.
    *   **Fixed Joint Model Parameter (α):** In the joint model (Eq. 11), the crucial balancing parameter α is simply set to 0.5. Given that all other hyperparameters were tuned, fixing this one seems like an oversight. Its value could significantly impact the model, and it should have been tuned on the development set or even learned as part of the model.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:**
    *   **Unsubstantiated Efficiency Claim:** The paper lists "Efficiency and parallelizability" as a main contribution but provides zero empirical evidence to support it. There are no runtime comparisons, complexity analyses, or even anecdotal reports on training time. Without this, the claim is baseless.
    *   **Baseline Comparability:** The performance reported for the RNN Tagger baseline (92.97 F1 on the large set) is substantially lower than the 95.79 F1 reported in the cited work of Mesnil et al. (2015) for a similar model on the same dataset. This discrepancy raises concerns about whether the baselines were implemented and tuned with the same diligence as the proposed model, potentially inflating the relative gains of K-SAN.
    *   **Coarse Significance Testing:** The paper notes significance with a single symbol (†) indicating that K-SAN is better than "all baseline models". This is not sufficiently informative. For a rigorous comparison, significance should be tested against the strongest individual baseline to clearly establish the marginal contribution of the proposed method.

3.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of presentation and formatting errors, suggesting a lack of care in its preparation.
    *   **Figures:** Figure 2, illustrating the overall architecture, is cluttered and difficult to follow. The flow of information is not intuitive.
    *   **References:** The bibliography is riddled with severe formatting errors. Multiple entries contain BibTeX artifacts like "et al\.,?", question marks, and `\Q \E` tags (e.g., "Banarescu et al\.,? 2013, Banarescu et al\.,", "Davidson.,? \Q1967\E, Davidson.,"). Author names are inconsistently formatted. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier venue and detracts from the paper's credibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please provide a precise, algorithmic definition of how "knowledge-guided substructures" are extracted from both dependency trees and AMR graphs? Please clarify how word order within a substructure is determined.
2.  What is the empirical justification for tying the weights of the substructure encoder (Mkg) and sentence encoder (Min)? Have you conducted an ablation study with untied weights?
3.  Could you please clarify the implementation details of your baselines, particularly the RNN Tagger, and explain the performance discrepancy between your reported result (92.97 F1) and the one in Mesnil et al. (2015) (95.79 F1)?
4.  Given that "Efficiency and parallelizability" is listed as a main contribution, could you provide empirical evidence (e.g., training/inference times vs. baselines like Tree-RNN) to substantiate this claim?
