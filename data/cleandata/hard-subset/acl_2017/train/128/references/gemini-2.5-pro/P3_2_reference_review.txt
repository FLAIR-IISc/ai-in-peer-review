### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Network (K-SAN), a novel architecture for sequence tagging tasks in NLU, specifically slot filling. The core idea is to leverage external knowledge sources, such as dependency parses or AMR graphs, not as direct input features, but as a "teacher" to guide an attention mechanism. The model first extracts a set of "substructures" (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in a dependency tree) from the knowledge graph of an input utterance. These substructures are encoded into vector representations, forming a memory. An attention mechanism then learns to weight the importance of each substructure relative to the overall utterance representation. The resulting attention-weighted sum of substructure vectors creates a single, knowledge-guided sentence representation. This representation is then fed as a constant input into every timestep of a standard RNN tagger, providing it with global, structurally-informed context to improve tagging accuracy.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Architectural Paradigm for Integrating Knowledge:** The primary contribution is the method of using external structures to define a discrete set of substructures for an attention mechanism. This provides a global, contextual vector that informs a local sequence tagger. This is a departure from prior work that either uses structural information locally (e.g., as features) or encodes the entire structure into a single vector (e.g., Tree-RNN), and it offers a compelling way to manage noisy structural information.
2.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration of Robustness:** The paper provides compelling evidence that this architecture is robust to both data scarcity and noise in the provided knowledge. The model shows significant performance gains over baselines on smaller training sets, and maintains high performance even when using imperfect parses (as shown in Figure 5) or different parsing tools.
3.  **Flexibility Across Knowledge Sources:** The authors successfully demonstrate that the K-SAN framework is agnostic to the specific type of knowledge, achieving state-of-the-art results on the ATIS benchmark using both syntactic (dependency trees from two different parsers) and semantic (AMR graphs from two different parsers) information sources without architectural modification.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model:** The motivation for the model is very clear. Using knowledge as input features can lead to error propagation and poor generalization. The proposed "knowledge as a teacher" approach, which guides attention over salient parts of the linguistic structure, is an elegant solution. It allows the model to learn to focus on reliable parts of a potentially noisy parse while ignoring irrelevant or erroneous substructures. The architecture cleanly separates the encoding of the knowledge structure from the sequential tagging task, while still allowing for end-to-end training.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Experimental Results:** The paper reports state-of-the-art results on the ATIS benchmark. The experimental setup is thorough, comparing against a strong set of both standard and structure-aware baselines. The most impressive aspect is the analysis across different training data sizes (Table 1), which convincingly demonstrates the model's superior generalization in low-data regimes. This is a significant practical advantage.
3.  **Excellent Analysis and Visualization:** The authors go beyond simply reporting F1 scores. The attention visualization in Figure 5 is insightful, showing that the model learns to focus on semantically important words and relations even with limited training data. The analysis in Section 5.5, which swaps out different knowledge sources (Stanford parser, SyntaxNet, AMR parsers), strongly supports the paper's central claim of generalization and robustness. The ability to achieve similar SOTA performance with four different structural inputs is a powerful result.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Evaluation on a Simple, Closed-Domain Dataset:** While ATIS is a standard benchmark, it is a relatively simple, closed-domain dataset with short, formulaic sentences. The dependency parses are unlikely to be very complex or deep. It remains an open question how K-SAN would perform on more complex, open-domain text (e.g., from weblogs, news, or general QA) where sentences are longer and syntactic structures are far more varied and ambiguous. In such scenarios, the number of root-to-leaf substructures could become very large, potentially posing computational challenges and making the attention task more difficult.
2.  **Ambiguity in Substructure Extraction from Graphs:** The paper states that substructures are "built according to the paths from the root to all leaves." This is intuitive for a tree. However, for an AMR graph, which is a directed acyclic graph (DAG) and not necessarily a tree, this process is less clear. For example, how are re-entrancies (nodes with multiple parents) handled? Is a path traced through only one parent? A more formal definition of the substructure extraction algorithm, especially for general graphs, is needed for clarity and reproducibility.
3.  **The Joint Model Is Under-motivated:** The final model used for reporting results is a "joint" tagger that averages the hidden states of a standard chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN (Eq. 11) with a fixed alpha of 0.5. This feels somewhat ad-hoc. If the knowledge-guided representation `o` provides a superior global context, why is the standard RNN hidden state still necessary? Its inclusion suggests that the knowledge-guided model on its own may be missing some crucial local, sequential dependencies. An ablation study showing the performance of the pure knowledge-guided tagger (Eq. 10) versus the joint model would be very valuable to understand this design choice.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the performance of the knowledge-guided RNN tagger (using Eq. 10) on its own, without being combined with the chain-based RNN in the "joint" model? This would help clarify the contribution of each component and motivate the decision to use a fixed-weight average.
2.  How does the model's computational complexity (both in training and inference) scale with sentence length? Specifically, since the number of substructures is tied to the number of leaf nodes in the parse tree, is there a performance bottleneck for long sentences with many dependencies?
3.  Regarding substructure extraction from AMR graphs, could you please provide a more precise definition of how paths are generated, particularly in cases of nodes with multiple incoming edges (re-entrancies)? For instance, in the AMR for "the boy who was chasing the dog that barked", how would substructures involving "the dog" be created?
