**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper introduces the Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Network (K-SAN), a model for sequence tagging, which is evaluated on the task of slot filling for NLU. The central idea is to use external linguistic knowledge, such as dependency parses or AMR graphs, to derive "substructures" from an input sentence. The model then employs an attention mechanism to learn a weighted combination of these substructure representations. This resulting knowledge-guided vector is used to augment a standard RNN-based tagger at each timestep.

As I see them, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  An effective and flexible framework for integrating various forms of structured linguistic knowledge into an end-to-end neural sequence tagger. The model demonstrates impressive robustness to the specific choice of knowledge source (e.g., Stanford vs. SyntaxNet parser; dependency vs. AMR graphs) and to parsing errors.
2.  A strong empirical demonstration on the ATIS benchmark, showing that this method of knowledge integration significantly improves performance over non-structural and other structure-aware baselines, particularly in low-data scenarios.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Soundness of Knowledge Integration:** The approach of treating linguistic substructures as items in a memory to attend over is well-motivated. It provides a soft, learnable mechanism for incorporating structural biases, which is a clear advantage over methods that use syntactic information as hard features. The model's demonstrated ability to benefit from imperfect parses (as noted in Figure 5) is a significant strength, addressing a common failure point of syntax-aware models.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Validation of Generalization:** The authors convincingly support their claim that the model generalizes across different knowledge sources. The experiments in Section 5.5, comparing two different dependency parsers and two different AMR parsers, show remarkably stable and strong performance. This is a crucial result, suggesting the architecture is not over-tuned to one particular type of structural information.
3.  **Effectiveness in Low-Data Regimes:** The experiments on reduced training set sizes (Small and Medium) clearly show that K-SAN provides a larger relative improvement when data is scarce. This supports the hypothesis that the structural priors provided by the external knowledge are most valuable when the model cannot learn underlying linguistic patterns from data alone.
4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The inclusion of an attention visualization in Figure 5 is valuable. It provides qualitative evidence that the model is indeed learning to focus on semantically salient substructures, lending credibility to the proposed attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is promising and the results are strong, the paper is marred by a significant number of methodological omissions, unsubstantiated claims, and presentation flaws.

1.  **Critical Lack of Methodological Detail:** The paper omits several details that are essential for reproducibility and for trusting the reported results.
    *   **Substructure Generation:** The process of converting a path in a dependency tree or AMR graph into an ordered sequence of words (e.g., "show flights seattle from" in Section 3.2) is not defined. The traversal strategy (e.g., depth-first, bread-first) is a critical, unstated methodological detail.
    *   **Development Set:** The authors state that "hyperparameters are tuned in the dev set," but the standard ATIS benchmark does not have a canonical development set. The paper provides no information on how a development set was created from the training data, making it impossible to fairly reproduce the hyperparameter search.
    *   **Significance Testing:** The paper claims statistical significance using a t-test but provides no details on the testing procedure. Were experiments run with multiple random seeds to obtain variance estimates? Without this information, the significance claims are not verifiable.
2.  **Unsupported Claims and Ad-hoc Design:**
    *   **Joint Model:** The final reported results come from a "Joint RNN Tagger" which linearly interpolates between a standard RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. The interpolation weight `alpha` is fixed to 0.5 "for balancing two sides." This is an entirely ad-hoc choice. There is no ablation study or sensitivity analysis to justify this value, nor any attempt to learn it as a parameter. The performance of the final model hinges on this arbitrary decision.
    *   **Efficiency and Parallelizability:** One of the three main contributions listed in the introduction is "Efficiency and parallelizability." This claim is never substantiated anywhere in the paper. No runtime analysis, complexity comparison, or empirical timing results are presented to support it. This is a major unsupported claim.
3.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Flaws:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness in its preparation, which undermines its overall quality.
    *   The acronym IOB is incorrectly expanded as "in-out-begin" in the introduction; the standard expansion is "Inside-Outside-Beginning."
    *   The entire References section is severely malformed. Citations are inconsistent and contain parsing artifacts (e.g., `Banarescu et al\.,? 2013, Banarescu et al\.,`, `\Q1967\E`). This level of formatting error is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   In Figure 1's caption, the "(S)" appears to be a typo with no corresponding explanation.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a precise, algorithmic description of how you generate the ordered word sequences for the substructures from the input dependency trees and AMR graphs?
2.  Please clarify how the development set for hyperparameter tuning was constructed from the ATIS dataset.
3.  Can you provide a justification for fixing the joint model's interpolation weight `alpha` to 0.5? Please provide a sensitivity analysis or ablation study showing the impact of this hyperparameter on model performance.
4.  Regarding your third claimed contribution, could you provide empirical evidence (e.g., training/inference times) or a computational complexity analysis to support the claim of "Efficiency and parallelizability" relative to the baselines?
