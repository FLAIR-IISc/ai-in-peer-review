Summary
The paper proposes K-SAN, a sequence tagging model that encodes linguistic substructures (e.g., dependency paths or AMR paths) as a memory of vectors and computes a single knowledge-guided representation via inner-product attention with a sentence embedding. This representation is fed into an RNN tagger, optionally combined with a separate chain-based RNN in a joint tagger. Experiments on ATIS show gains over several baselines and provide attention visualizations.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical formulation that “flattens” structured linguistic information into an array of substructure vectors connected to a tagger, akin to syntax-based attention/memory.
2) An empirical demonstration on ATIS that structural attention can improve slot filling, especially in low-resource regimes and across multiple structure sources.
3) Diagnostic visualizations of which substructures the model attends to.

Strengths
- Simple, modular design: the substructure encoder (NN/CNN/RNN) and attention/memory interface are easy to implement and potentially reusable across structure sources.
- Empirical improvements on ATIS, notably in small-data settings, and consistency across dependency and AMR inputs.
- Clear exposition and figures; end-to-end training without hand-crafted feature engineering.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: the substructure encoder is close to DCNN-style sequence composition and the main step is a straightforward flattening/memory interface; overall similar in spirit to structured/syntax-based attention.
- Experimental clarity and fairness: insufficient details on how DCNN/TreeRNN baselines are used (sentence vs substructure embeddings; joint vs independent training); joint tagger uses two RNNs, likely increasing capacity, yet parameter counts and matched-capacity baselines are not reported.
- Missing controls/ablations: no baseline injecting simple knowledge features (e.g., heads, NER) directly into the RNN; no ablation of joint vs single tagger; no variance across seeds; no per-token dynamic attention comparison.
- Design concerns: a single static attention shared across all time steps can be suboptimal; inner-product with the global sentence vector may bias toward nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes). Alternatives (bilinear/additive, token-conditioned attention) are not explored.
- Robustness and scope: no analysis of sensitivity to parsing/AMR errors; claims of generalizing to “knowledge” are weak since inputs must be word sequences; terminology conflates syntactic structure with external/world knowledge.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- How exactly were TreeRNN/DCNN baselines trained and interfaced with the tagger? Were they jointly fine-tuned?
- What are parameter counts and training budgets for all systems? Do gains persist under capacity-matched baselines and a single-RNN variant?
- Why not per-token substructure attention or alternative scoring (bilinear/additive)? Any preliminary results?
- How sensitive is K-SAN to parser errors and the number/quality of substructures?

Post-rebuttal: Main concerns on baseline usage and extra parameters from two RNNs were not addressed; my score remains unchanged.
