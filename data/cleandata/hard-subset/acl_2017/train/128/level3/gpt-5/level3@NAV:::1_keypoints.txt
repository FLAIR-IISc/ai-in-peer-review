- Proposes K-SAN: encodes linguistic substructures as memory vectors and uses inner-product attention with a sentence embedding to produce a knowledge-guided representation for an RNN tagger.  
- Optionally combines the knowledge-guided RNN with a separate chain-based RNN in a joint tagger.  
- Evaluated on ATIS showing improvements over baselines and provides attention visualizations.  
- Contribution: flattens structured linguistic information into an array of substructure vectors connected to a tagger (syntax-based attention/memory).  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that structural attention improves slot filling, especially in low-resource settings and across dependency and AMR structures.  
- Contribution: diagnostic visualizations showing which substructures the model attends to.  
- Strength: simple, modular design—substructure encoder and attention/memory interface are easy to implement and reuse.  
- Strength: empirical gains on ATIS, notably in small-data regimes, consistent across different structure sources.  
- Strength: clear exposition and figures; end-to-end training without hand-crafted features.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—substructure encoder resembles DCNN-style composition and approach is similar to prior structured/syntax-based attention.  
- Weakness: experimental clarity/fairness concerns—unclear how DCNN/TreeRNN baselines were used and whether joint tagger benefits from increased capacity; parameter-matched baselines not reported.  
- Weakness: missing controls and ablations—no simple knowledge-feature baselines, no joint vs single tagger ablation, no seed variance reporting, no per-token dynamic attention comparison.  
- Weakness: design concerns—single static attention across time steps and inner-product with global sentence vector may bias toward whole-sentence-like nodes; alternatives (bilinear/additive, token-conditioned attention) not explored.  
- Weakness: robustness and scope issues—no sensitivity analysis to parsing/AMR errors; claims of generalizing to “knowledge” are weak since inputs remain word sequences and terminology conflates syntactic structure with external knowledge.  
- Question: how were TreeRNN/DCNN baselines trained and interfaced with the tagger, and were they fine-tuned jointly?  
- Question: what are parameter counts and training budgets for all systems, and do gains hold with capacity-matched baselines or a single-RNN variant?  
- Question: why not use per-token substructure attention or alternative scoring (bilinear/additive), and are there preliminary results?  
- Question: how sensitive is K-SAN to parser/AMR errors and to the number/quality of substructures?  
- Post-rebuttal: authors did not address baseline/capacity concerns; reviewer’s score remains unchanged.
