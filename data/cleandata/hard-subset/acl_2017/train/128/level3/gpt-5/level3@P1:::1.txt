Summary
The paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The approach encodes a set of “substructures” derived from an external analyzer (e.g., dependency paths or AMR fragments) into vectors, computes an attention distribution over these substructures given a sentence embedding, and injects the resulting knowledge-guided representation into a recurrent tagger. A joint model combines a standard chain RNN with the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS show modest but consistent improvements over neural baselines and structurally informed encoders, with larger gains in low-resource settings, and similar performance across different sources of structural annotations.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A practical structural-attention architecture for sequence tagging that flattens linguistic structure into a set of substructure embeddings and integrates them via sentence-level attention into an RNN tagger. This simple design unifies multiple structure encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) and multiple structure sources (dependency/AMR).
- An empirical study demonstrating that such substructure-based attention yields improvements on ATIS, particularly with smaller training sets, and that gains are relatively robust across parsers/formalisms.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and modularity: The pipeline is easy to understand and implement—(i) extract substructures, (ii) encode them, (iii) attend w.r.t. a sentence embedding, and (iv) feed into a tagger. The tying of Min/Mkg and the weighted sum attention over substructures are straightforward and likely to be stable in training.
- Consistent empirical gains: On ATIS, K-SAN modestly but consistently outperforms strong baselines. For example, with full training data, K-SAN (RNN encoder) reaches 94.97 F1 vs. 93.88 for the CNN Encoder-Tagger baseline (~+1.1 absolute); with 1/10 data, K-SAN (CNN encoder) obtains 87.99 vs. 85.52 (~+2.5), with statistical significance marked for most settings. The low-resource gains support the claim that structure-guided attention helps generalization.
- Generalization across “knowledge” sources: Using both dependency trees (Stanford/SyntaxNet) and AMR (rule-based/JAMR) yields similar performance, despite AMR yielding fewer substructures. This suggests the method is not overly tuned to a specific parser or formalism and can handle both syntactic and (lightweight) semantic graphs.
- Attention analysis: Visualizations indicate the model focuses on salient regions (e.g., origin/destination/time spans), and the attention patterns are relatively stable across data sizes, lending interpretability and some support for the “teacher” narrative.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: The core idea—encode structural fragments into vectors and attend to them—is close to existing structured/syntax-based attention and DCNN- or tree-based encoding approaches. The main difference is to flatten trees/graphs to substructures and use sentence-level attention; methodologically, this feels incremental rather than a substantial advance.
- Baseline fairness and missing controls: The experimental section lacks critical details about how DCNN/Tree-RNN baselines were integrated with the tagger and whether parameter budgets were matched. The joint model uses two RNNs; without parameter-matched baselines (e.g., a wider single RNN or a two-tower RNN without structure), it is hard to attribute gains to the structural attention rather than extra capacity. A strong control—simply concatenating simple knowledge features (e.g., head words, dependency head embeddings, NER tags) to the RNN input—would test whether the benefits come from attention over substructures vs. feature augmentation.
- Static, sentence-level attention for token tagging: The model computes one attention distribution per sentence and uses it for all token predictions. For slot filling, the relevant substructure often differs across positions; per-token or per-time-step attention would be a more natural fit. The paper does not justify the design choice nor compare to a token-level attention variant.
- Sensitivity to parser errors and attention bias: There is no quantitative analysis of robustness to noisy parses, despite claims of robustness. The inner-product attention between a global sentence embedding and substructure embeddings may bias attention toward substructures most similar to the whole sentence (e.g., frequent or content-heavy fragments), not necessarily the ones most informative for a given token’s tag.
- “Knowledge” terminology and scope: The paper frames syntactic/AMR structure as “knowledge,” but the method still depends on being able to linearize structures as word sequences; richer KB facts or non-linguistic knowledge (e.g., entities/relations not present in the sentence) are outside scope. This weakens the claimed generality of “using knowledge as a teacher.”

Questions to Authors
- Baseline parity: What are the exact parameter counts for the joint RNN vs. baselines (RNN, CNN Encoder-Tagger, DCNN, Tree-RNN)? Can you report results with a parameter-matched non-structural model (e.g., two RNN towers without structure, or a larger single RNN)?
- Feature controls: How does K-SAN compare to a baseline that feeds simple structural features directly (e.g., concatenating dependency head embeddings, path indicators, or NER) into the RNN? This isolates the effect of attention over substructures vs. feature enrichment.
- Token-level attention: Have you tried per-token attention over substructures (o_t computed with u_t or h_t), or gating o into the RNN at each step differently? If so, how do results compare?
- Parser robustness: Can you provide experiments that inject noise into parses or compare performance conditioned on parse quality buckets, to substantiate robustness claims?
- Substructure design: How sensitive are results to the substructure definition (root-to-leaf paths vs. other path schemes, inclusion of relation labels, maximum substructure length)? Ablations would clarify what drives the gains.
- Attribution of gains: In the joint tagger, how much does α matter? What is the performance of (i) chain-only, (ii) knowledge-only, and (iii) joint under parameter control?

Post-rebuttal note
The rebuttal did not resolve key concerns about baseline fairness (parameter counts, feature controls) and the necessity of two RNNs. My assessment and score remain unchanged.
