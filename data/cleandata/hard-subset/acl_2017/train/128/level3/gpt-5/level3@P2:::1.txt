Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The idea is to extract a set of substructures from an external analysis (e.g., dependency paths or AMR paths), encode each substructure as a vector, compute an attention distribution over substructures using a sentence embedding, and produce a knowledge-guided sentence representation that is fed into a sequence tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain-based RNN with a knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS (small/medium/full) show improvements over several neural and structure-aware baselines, and the authors explore multiple “knowledge” sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency trees and AMR).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A practical architecture that attends over linguistically motivated substructures to compute a sentence-level representation for sequence tagging, and a joint tagger combining chain-based and knowledge-guided branches.
2) An empirical study on ATIS showing gains, especially in low-resource settings, and a preliminary exploration of multiple upstream structure sources (dependency and AMR).
3) An attention visualization suggesting the model can focus on salient structures.

Strengths
- Practical and simple architecture: The substructure encoder + softmax attention + sentence-level fusion into a tagger is easy to implement, differentiable end-to-end, and compatible with different encoders (NN/RNN/CNN). This simplicity increases potential reproducibility and adoption.
- Consistent improvements on ATIS: K-SAN improves over a strong CNN encoder–tagger baseline across data regimes (e.g., ~+1.0 F1 on full ATIS and larger gains on small/medium), with reported significance. This supports the intuition that structure-aware signals help generalization, particularly with scarce data.
- Multiple structure sources: The paper shows similar gains using two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers, suggesting some robustness to the choice of upstream analyzer and supporting the claim that the framework can accommodate different “knowledge” encodings.
- Attention analysis: Visualizations (even with imperfect parses) indicate the model attends to intuitively relevant substructures (origins, destinations, times), offering qualitative support that the mechanism is doing something meaningful.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior structured/syntax-aware attention: Flattening substructures into a memory and attending to them with an inner product against a sentence vector is a straightforward variant of existing attention/memory ideas. Unlike tree-structured encoders that propagate composition over full structures, K-SAN uses a single global attention over pre-extracted paths and injects one sentence-level vector into a standard RNN. The novelty is thus modest.
- Baseline and parameter fairness unclear: It is unclear whether baselines (Tree-RNN, DCNN, CNN, plain RNN) were matched for parameter count, optimization budgets, and preprocessing. The joint tagger uses two RNN branches, plausibly increasing capacity; there is no parameter-controlled baseline (e.g., a larger single-branch RNN with the same number of parameters) to rule out capacity as the source of gains. This undermines the empirical claims.
- Missing feature-control baseline: There is no baseline that directly feeds simple structural features (e.g., head word embeddings, dependency labels, NER/gazetteers) into the RNN at each timestep. Such a baseline would test whether the gains derive from structured attention per se or just from extra structural signals.
- Static, sentence-level attention for sequence tagging: The model computes one attention distribution for the entire sentence and injects a single global vector o at all timesteps. For slot filling, per-token (time-varying) attention over substructures seems more appropriate; the paper does not justify this design choice nor compare against token-level attention.
- Sensitivity to parser errors not analyzed: Although the authors show a qualitative example with misparses, there is no quantitative stress test (e.g., controlled noise injection or correlation with parse quality) to substantiate robustness claims.
- “Knowledge” generalization and terminology: The framework requires substructures to be representable as token sequences; many knowledge sources (e.g., KB facts, entity types, cross-sentence/coreference links) are not naturally path-like word sequences. Thus the “generalization to different kinds of knowledge” claim is limited. Also, calling syntactic/AMR structure “knowledge” is somewhat misleading in the SLU literature, where “knowledge” typically denotes external world/entity knowledge.
- Potential attention bias: The inner-product attention between the sentence embedding and substructure vectors may favor substructures lexically similar to the whole sentence (e.g., content-heavy leaf paths), not necessarily those most predictive for a particular token’s tag. This risk is neither discussed nor ablated (e.g., alternative similarity functions or normalization).

Questions to authors
- Baselines and capacity control:
  - What are the exact parameter counts for all models (including the joint tagger’s two RNN branches)? Please provide a parameter-matched single-branch RNN baseline.
  - Did Tree-RNN/DCNN baselines produce both sentence and substructure embeddings, or only sentence embeddings? How were they plugged into the tagger?
  - Why no CRF decoding layer on top of RNNs for fair comparison with sequence models that capture tag dependencies?
- Design choices:
  - Why a single sentence-level attention vector rather than per-token attention over substructures? Did you try conditioning attention on ht or wt at each timestep?
  - Why inner-product similarity? Did you try bilinear or MLP attention to reduce bias toward lexically similar substructures?
  - What is the effect of tying Mkg and Min weights vs. untied?
- Feature-control baseline:
  - Please include baselines that concatenate head word embeddings, dependency labels (if available), and simple gazetteer/NER features to the input at each timestep.
- Robustness:
  - Can you report performance stratified by parse quality (e.g., by dependency LAS bins) or with synthetic noise added to parses?
  - How do results change if you use gold AMR (where available) vs. automatic?
- Efficiency:
  - You claim efficiency/parallelizability. Please provide wall-clock training/inference times vs. baselines as sentence length grows, and show how the number of substructures affects time/memory.

Additional comments
- The visualization section is useful; adding token-level attention maps (if implemented) would strengthen the narrative.
- Please clarify precisely how AMR substructures are formed (node/edge selection, linearization order) and whether relation labels were used or discarded.
- Consider evaluating on a second SLU dataset (e.g., MIT Restaurant/Movie) to mitigate ATIS saturation concerns.

Post-rebuttal note
The response did not address core concerns about baseline comparability (parameter counts) and the necessity of two RNN branches. Absent these clarifications and stronger ablations, my assessment remains unchanged.
