- Paper proposes K-SAN: flatten linguistic substructures into memory vectors, attend with a sentence embedding u, produce knowledge vector o that augments a sequence tagger mixing chain and knowledge-guided RNNs.
- Contribution: practical architecture that injects flattened substructure memories via simple attention into a tagger.
- Contribution: empirical claim that flattened substructure memories give small gains on ATIS under low-data settings and with dependency/AMR sources.
- Strength: simple, practical, end-to-end and easy-to-implement integration of structural info with standard taggers.
- Strength: sensible engineering choices (encoder variants, weight tying, single attention pass) that keep the model lightweight.
- Strength: shows some robustness in low-resource regimes (ATIS Small/Medium improvements).
- Strength: useful qualitative attention visualizations showing focus on intuitively relevant regions despite parser noise.
- Weakness: limited novelty — idea closely resembles prior structure-aware attention/memory methods and DCNN-style encodings.
- Weakness: uses a single sentence-level attention for a token-level task; no justification or experiments with per-token queries.
- Weakness: unclear/unfair baselines — unclear if Tree-RNN/DCNN were jointly trained, and parameter counts/comparisons are not matched.
- Weakness: missing simple feature-control baseline (e.g., concatenating heads/POS/NER to inputs) to isolate knowledge vs. extra features/parameters.
- Weakness: no systematic analysis of sensitivity to parser errors or different parser qualities.
- Weakness: attention scoring via inner product with u may bias toward frequent/global substructures; no ablation of alternative scorers.
- Weakness: overstated “knowledge” generality — method requires substructures as token sequences and calling syntax/AMR “knowledge” is misleading.
- Weakness: narrow empirical scope — only ATIS with modest, setup-dependent gains and insufficient implementation parity details.
- Question: Were DCNN and Tree-RNN baselines trained end-to-end with the tagger or used as frozen encoders, and did they encode substructures comparably?
- Question: Provide parameter counts for each model and parameter-matched baselines (e.g., wider single-RNN) to control for extra capacity.
- Question: Why use sentence-level attention instead of token-level (h_t) or multi-hop attention; were these tried and what were results?
- Question: Were alternative attention functions (additive/bilinear) evaluated, and did they change bias toward substructures?
- Question: Can you report a baseline that concatenates dependency heads/POS/NER to embeddings and compare?
- Question: How sensitive is performance to parser quality — results across parsers or with synthetic tree/AMR noise?
- Question: In AMR experiments, how are non-lexicalized nodes handled and what vocabulary overlap exists with the tagger?
- Suggestion: Try a single RNN with per-token attention over substructures and residual fusion, plus parameter-matched baselines.
- Suggestion: Add ablations (no-attention, no-knowledge, uniform mi, attention-only) and alternative scoring functions.
- Suggestion: Clarify and report tied vs. untied Min/Mkg weight settings and rationale.
- Suggestion: Analyze attention distributions per token and measure variance across tokens/utterances.
- Overall: Clean, practical idea but limited novelty and insufficient empirical validation due to baseline/parameter confounds, static attention, and lack of robustness analysis.
- Post-rebuttal: Authors’ response did not resolve core concerns about baselines and extra RNN parameters; score unchanged.
