Summary of the paper:
The paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. An utterance is parsed into substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf dependency paths). Each substructure xi is embedded into a memory vector mi; the sentence is embedded to u. A global attention over substructures is computed via pi = softmax(u^T mi), producing h = Σi pi mi and o = Mout(h + u). The knowledge-guided vector o is fed to a tagger. The final model “jointly” combines two RNN taggers: a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN, linearly mixing their hidden states. Experiments on ATIS report modest improvements over baselines, including DCNN and TreeRNN variants; a brief “knowledge generalization” experiment swaps in AMR-based substructures.

Contributions (as I see them):
- A practical neural architecture that flattens linguistic substructures into an array of memory vectors and uses a simple attention mechanism to inject a sentence-level knowledge-guided summary into a sequence tagger.
- An empirical demonstration that such flattened substructure memories can provide small gains on ATIS under low-data settings and across two structure sources (dependency, AMR).

Strengths:
- Simplicity and practicality: The “flattening” of structural information into a set of vectors aligned with a standard tagger is easy to implement, end-to-end trainable, and likely portable to other sequence labeling tasks.
- Reasonable engineering choices: The substructure encoder options (NN/RNN/CNN), weight tying between Min and Mkg, and a single attention pass keep the method lightweight compared to more complex memory architectures.
- Some robustness in low-resource regime: On ATIS Small/Medium, K-SAN-CNN shows improvements over plain RNN/CNN encoder baselines, suggesting the substructure store can help when data are scarce.
- Qualitative analysis: The attention visualizations are helpful and show the model can focus on intuitively relevant regions (origin/destination/time), even with parsing noise.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: The core idea (store substructures as vectors and attend to them) closely resembles prior syntax/structure-aware attention/memory ideas and DCNN-style encoding of dependency contexts. The main technical step—flattening substructures and a single inner-product attention—is incremental.
- Static, sentence-level attention for a token-level task: The model computes a single attention distribution (query = u) shared by all time steps. For slot filling, per-token queries (e.g., using h_t) are standard and would likely be more effective. The paper gives no justification for using a global “filter” instead of dynamic per-word attention.
- Unclear and potentially unfair baselines and parameter comparisons: It is not clear whether Tree-RNN and DCNN baselines were trained end-to-end with the tagger or used only to produce sentence embeddings; whether they encode substructures and sentences in a comparable joint objective; and whether parameter counts were matched. The proposed “joint” tagger uses two RNNs; comparable baselines with the same parameter budget are not reported.
- Missing feature-control baseline: The paper does not include a straightforward baseline that feeds simple knowledge-derived features (e.g., head words, dependency heads, NER tags) directly to the RNN inputs. Without this, it is hard to separate benefits of “knowledge” from benefits of extra features/parameters.
- No sensitivity to parser errors: While a visualization notes an example with parser errors, there is no systematic analysis (e.g., perturb parsing quality, compare parsers, correlate attention with error regions) to support robustness claims.
- Attention scoring may be biased: Inner products with u may overweight substructures similar to the overall sentence embedding, potentially preferring frequent/leaf-heavy paths and missing rare but crucial functional nodes; no ablation compares alternative scorers (bilinear/additive MLP) or normalization.
- Overstated generality of “knowledge”: The approach requires substructures to be representable as sequences of tokens/concepts. This limits applicability to many knowledge sources (e.g., richly typed KBs, cross-utterance dialogue state). Also, calling syntax/AMR “knowledge” is misleading; in NLP, “knowledge” often denotes external/world knowledge.
- Empirical scope is narrow: Results are only on ATIS; improvements are modest and depend on setup. Lacking detailed training/implementation parity, the empirical evidence is not compelling.

Questions to authors:
- How exactly were the DCNN and Tree-RNN baselines integrated with the tagger? Were their encoders trained jointly with slot loss, or used as frozen sentence encoders? Did they also encode substructures independently of the tagger?
- What are the parameter counts for each model, including the joint RNN (two encoders, two RNNs)? Can you provide parameter-matched baselines (e.g., wider single-RNN) and report results?
- Why was a single sentence-level attention used instead of per-token attention (query = h_t)? Did you try token-level attention or multi-hop attention, and with what effect?
- Did you evaluate alternative attention scoring functions (additive/Bahdanau, bilinear)? Any differences in bias toward certain substructures?
- Can you report a baseline where dependency heads, head POS, or NER tags are concatenated to word embeddings and fed to a single RNN? How does it compare?
- How sensitive is performance to parser quality? Please provide results across parsers with measured LAS/UAS, or with synthetic noise on trees/AMRs.
- In the “generalization to knowledge” experiment, AMR substructures are sequences of concepts. How are non-lexicalized AMR nodes handled, and how much overlap is there with the tagger’s vocabulary?

Additional comments and suggestions:
- Consider a single RNN tagger with per-token attention over substructures and residual fusion (o_t added to h_t), plus a parameter-matched baseline.
- Add ablations: no-attention (uniform over mi), no-knowledge (remove mi), attention-only (use h without u), and alternative scoring functions.
- Clarify whether Min and Mkg weights are always tied and why; report untied results.
- Include an analysis of which substructures receive attention for different token positions; measure variance of attention across tokens/utterances.

Overall assessment:
A clean, practical architecture with intuitive motivation, but the methodological novelty is limited, and the empirical validation is not sufficiently convincing due to baseline ambiguity, parameter confounds, static attention for a token-level task, and lack of robustness analysis. Works on ATIS, but takeaways are weak.
