- Proposal: K-SAN attends over linguistically motivated substructures (dependency/AMR paths), encodes them, computes attention with a sentence embedding, and feeds a knowledge-guided sentence vector into a sequence tagger.
- Contribution 1: Practical architecture combining substructure attention and a joint tagger with chain-based and knowledge-guided RNN branches.
- Contribution 2: Empirical gains on ATIS, especially in low-resource settings, and exploration of multiple upstream analyzers.
- Contribution 3: Attention visualizations suggesting focus on salient structures.

- Strength: Simple, practical, end-to-end differentiable design compatible with various encoders, aiding reproducibility.
- Strength: Consistent F1 improvements over strong baselines on ATIS, with larger gains in small/medium data regimes.
- Strength: Robustness to choice of upstream parser shown by similar gains across dependency and AMR variants.
- Strength: Qualitative attention analysis indicates model attends to intuitively relevant substructures.

- Weakness: Modest novelty—approach is a straightforward application of memory/attention over flattened pre-extracted paths versus full tree encoders.
- Weakness: Unclear whether baseline models were matched for parameter count and optimization; joint tagger may gain from increased capacity.
- Weakness: Missing simple feature-control baseline that injects structural features per timestep to test whether attention is necessary.
- Weakness: Uses a single static sentence-level attention vector for all timesteps, which may be unsuitable for token-level slot prediction.
- Weakness: No quantitative analysis of sensitivity to parser errors or controlled noise experiments to substantiate robustness.
- Weakness: Claim of generalization to different “knowledge” is limited because many knowledge types are not path-like token sequences; terminology of “knowledge” is misleading.
- Weakness: Inner-product attention may bias toward lexically similar substructures rather than those predictive for specific tokens, with no ablation of alternative similarity functions.

- Question (baselines/capacity): Report exact parameter counts for all models and include a parameter-matched single-branch RNN baseline.
- Question (baselines): Clarify how Tree-RNN/DCNN baselines produced embeddings and how they were integrated into the tagger.
- Question (decoding): Why was no CRF decoding layer used to capture tag dependencies in comparisons?
- Question (design): Why a single sentence-level attention vector instead of per-token (time-varying) attention conditioned on ht or wt?
- Question (design): Why use inner-product attention—were bilinear or MLP attention variants tried to reduce lexical bias?
- Question (design): What is the effect of tying vs. untied Mkg and Min weights?
- Question (baseline request): Please include baselines that concatenate head-word embeddings, dependency labels, and gazetteer/NER features per timestep.
- Question (robustness): Can performance be reported stratified by parse quality or with synthetic noise added to parses?
- Question (robustness): How do results change using gold AMR vs. automatic AMR where available?
- Question (efficiency): Provide wall-clock training/inference times vs. baselines as sentence length and number of substructures vary.

- Additional: Add token-level attention visualizations to strengthen the narrative.
- Additional: Clarify how AMR substructures are formed, linearized, and whether relation labels are used.
- Additional: Evaluate on a second SLU dataset (e.g., MIT Restaurant/Movie) to reduce reliance on ATIS.

- Post-rebuttal: Authors did not address core concerns about baseline comparability and the necessity of two RNN branches; assessment remains unchanged.
