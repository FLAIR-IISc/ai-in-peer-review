- Proposes flattening structured linguistic information into an array of vectors and attaching it to a sequence tagger as extra knowledge.  
- Presents a neural architecture that integrates this flattened structural knowledge into a memory network for sequence tagging.  
- Claim of novelty is weak because the substructure encoder closely resembles existing methods like DCNN.  
- Overall architecture does not appear sufficiently novel.  
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about baselines and how sentence/substructure embeddings were computed.  
- Using two RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessary, inflates model capacity, and may be avoidable.  
- Hyperparameters and baseline network sizes are not comparable, undermining experimental fairness.  
- The claim that the model generalizes to different kinds of knowledge is questionable because substructures must be represented as word sequences.  
- The term “knowledge” is misleadingly used to mean syntax/semantics rather than external or world knowledge.  
- Experiments lack convincing takeaways and require clearer presentation and additional comparisons.  
- Authors did not address the reviewer’s main concerns in revisions, so scores remain unchanged.  
- Reviewer suggests adding a baseline that inputs additional knowledge as features to the RNN.  
- Reviewer notes potential sensitivity to parser errors and requests clarification on this issue.  
- Key questions for authors: provide baseline details, address encoder/architecture novelty, and clarify the meaning of “knowledge.”
