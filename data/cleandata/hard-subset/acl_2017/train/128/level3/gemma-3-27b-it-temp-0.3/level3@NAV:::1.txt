## Review of "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture designed to incorporate structural linguistic information into sequence tagging, specifically for slot filling in natural language understanding. The core idea is to encode substructures (derived from dependency parsing) as memory vectors and use an attention mechanism to integrate them with a recurrent tagger. While the motivation for leveraging structural knowledge is sound, and the approach is practically oriented, the paper suffers from several shortcomings in terms of novelty, experimental rigor, and clarity.

The primary contribution, as presented by the authors, is an end-to-end neural network that utilizes external knowledge to guide attention. However, the method largely resembles structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms already present in the literature, making the novelty incremental. A more accurate contribution lies in the practical demonstration of applying a relatively simple "flattening" of structured information into an array of vectors for integration with a tagger. The exploration of different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) is a secondary, though potentially valuable, contribution.

Several weaknesses significantly detract from the paper’s impact. The empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient detail regarding baseline implementation. It’s unclear whether TreeRNN and DCNN baselines computed embeddings independently of the tagger, potentially inflating their performance. The use of two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessarily complex and introduces a substantial parameter count without clear justification; a comparison to a baseline with a comparable number of parameters is missing. Crucially, the paper lacks a baseline that directly feeds additional knowledge features (e.g., head words, NER tags) into the RNN, failing to control for the simple effect of providing additional information. 

Furthermore, the paper doesn't analyze the model's sensitivity to parser errors, a critical consideration when relying on external parsing tools. The static attention mechanism over substructures is questionable; a per-word dynamic attention scheme would likely be more effective. The claim of generalizing to different “knowledge” types is weak, as the method fundamentally relies on representing structures as word sequences. Finally, framing syntactic/substructure information as “knowledge” is misleading, as it typically refers to external, world knowledge.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Approach:** The method is relatively straightforward to implement and apply to existing datasets.
*   **Exploration of Knowledge Sources:** Investigating both dependency trees and AMR graphs demonstrates a degree of flexibility.
*   **Potential for Improvement:** The core idea of integrating structural information has merit and could be further refined.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The approach largely builds upon existing structured attention mechanisms.
*   **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** Lack of detail and crucial baselines (feature-based) hinders evaluation.
*   **Unnecessary Complexity:** The dual-RNN architecture adds parameters without clear benefit.
*   **Lack of Error Analysis:** No assessment of sensitivity to parser errors.
*   **Questionable Design Choices:** Static attention and inner-product weighting may be suboptimal.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide detailed implementation specifics for all baselines, including hyperparameter settings and training procedures. Specifically, how were TreeRNN and DCNN baselines integrated with the tagger?
2.  What is the parameter count of the proposed K-SAN models compared to the baseline models?
3.  Why was a baseline that directly incorporates knowledge features (e.g., head words, NER tags) into the RNN not included?
4.  Did you observe any sensitivity to parser errors? If so, how did the model handle them?
5.  What is the rationale for using a static attention mechanism over substructures? Why not a per-word dynamic attention scheme?
