- Summary: Proposes K-SAN, which encodes root-to-leaf parse paths into memory vectors, applies sentence-level attention to form a knowledge representation, and combines a knowledge-guided RNN with a chain RNN for slot filling, showing gains on ATIS, especially in low-data settings.

- Contribution: Practical method to inject structural linguistic bias by flattening parses into path sequences, encoding them, and aggregating via attention to condition a sequence tagger.

- Strength: Simple, implementable architecture that reuses standard sequence encoders by converting trees/graphs into linear paths.

- Strength: Demonstrates notable performance improvements in low-data regimes, suggesting structural bias aids generalization.

- Strength: Validated across multiple structure sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers), showing consistent gains.

- Weakness: Experimental setup is unconvincing and possibly unfair, lacking detail and control over baseline implementations and parameter counts.

- Weakness: Unclear baseline implementation—Tree/DCNN baselines may not match K-SAN’s two-RNN architecture or parameter budget, confounding results.

- Weakness: Missing simple but important baseline of concatenating structural features to per-token embeddings to isolate benefits of the attention mechanism.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—approach resembles prior memory/structure-encoding methods and ancestor-path encodings like DCNNs.

- Weakness: Use of static, sentence-level attention is poorly motivated; dynamic per-token attention would likely be more appropriate.

- Weakness: Inner-product attention using the sentence vector may bias weights toward substructures similar to the whole sentence, down-weighting locally important elements.

- Weakness: Overstated “knowledge” terminology—linguistic parses are not external knowledge in the KB sense.

- Weakness: Model depends on upstream parser quality but lacks analysis of sensitivity to parsing errors or robustness to noisy parses.

- Post-rebuttal: Authors did not satisfactorily address main concerns about unfair baseline comparisons and parameter-count differences.

- Question: Clarify DCNN/Tree-RNN baseline architectures and report trainable parameter counts for K-SAN and baselines.

- Question: Justify the two-RNN joint design versus a single RNN that incorporates the knowledge vector at each timestep.

- Question: Provide results for a strong baseline that concatenates dependency-based features to per-token embeddings in a standard RNN tagger.

- Question: Explain the choice of static sentence-level attention instead of dynamic per-word attention and compare alternatives.

- Question: Analyze K-SAN’s robustness to parsing errors and how performance varies with parser quality.
