- Proposes K-SAN: encodes dependency substructures into memory vectors and uses attention to produce a knowledge-guided sentence vector fed to an RNN slot tagger.  
- Claims: practical architecture for integrating structural information and state-of-the-art performance on ATIS, especially in low-data settings.  
- Strong empirical performance: outperforms RNN and structure-aware baselines (DCNN, Tree-RNN), with notable gains on Small/Medium data.  
- Intuitive, well-motivated idea: attending to linguistic substructures addresses long-distance/hierarchical dependencies; attention visualization supports this.  
- Demonstrated robustness to knowledge source: similar gains using different dependency parsers and AMR parsers.  
- Unconvincing experimental comparisons: missing implementation details for DCNN/Tree-RNN baselines.  
- Possible parameter-count confound: joint tagger uses two RNNs, potentially inflating capacity versus baselines.  
- Missing simple feature-based baseline: no comparison to concatenating structural info with word embeddings into a single RNN.  
- Limited novelty: combines existing methods (encoding paths + attention) and reads as an engineering contribution rather than a major conceptual advance.  
- Questionable design: uses a single static sentence-level attention vector fed at every time step instead of dynamic per-token attention.  
- Inner-product attention may bias toward substructures similar to the overall sentence rather than token-relevant substructures.  
- Overstated generalization claims: framework requires knowledge decomposable into word sequences, limiting applicability to many knowledge formalisms.  
- Terminology concern: calling syntactic parse info “knowledge” is misleading; “linguistic structure” is more precise.  
- No analysis of sensitivity to parser errors despite reliance on external parsers.  
- Question to authors: clarify DCNN/Tree-RNN baseline implementations and tagger architecture/parameter parity.  
- Question to authors: compare joint two-RNN tagger against a single larger RNN with similar parameter count.  
- Question to authors: justify static sentence-level attention rather than dynamic token-level attention.  
- Question to authors: have you tried the simpler baseline of concatenating structural embeddings (e.g., syntactic head) to word embeddings for a single RNN?
