- Summary: Proposes K-SAN, which extracts parser substructures, encodes them as memory vectors, applies static sentence-level attention to form a knowledge-guided representation, and augments an RNN tagger jointly trained with a chain RNN.

- Main contribution: A practical approach for integrating syntactic substructures by flattening them into memory vectors and applying simple attention, akin to existing structured/syntax-based attention methods.

- Strength: Straightforward, intuitive method to leverage pre-existing syntactic parses within a standard RNN tagging pipeline.

- Strength: Empirical gains on ATIS over several baselines, suggesting attending over structural information helps slot-filling.

- Weakness: Experimental validation is unconvincing — unclear how structural baselines were integrated and missing a simple baseline that feeds syntactic features directly into the RNN.

- Weakness: Questionable architecture — joint two-RNN setup increases parameters without justification, and the attention is static for the whole sentence rather than dynamic per token.

- Weakness: Overstated claims and lack of analysis — limited to word-sequence-representable information, calling syntax “knowledge” is misleading, and no evaluation of sensitivity to parser errors.
