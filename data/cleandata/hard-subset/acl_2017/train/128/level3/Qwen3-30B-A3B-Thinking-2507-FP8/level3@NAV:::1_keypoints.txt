- Summary: Proposes K-SAN that flattens linguistic substructures into memory vectors for slot-filling; approach resembles syntax-based attention/DCNNs and has limited novelty and generalization only to sequence-representable structures.

- Strength: Shows reasonable performance on ATIS, especially with small training sets, indicating utility in low-data regimes.

- Strength: Conceptually simple architecture useful for integrating linguistic structure into sequence tagging.

- Strength: Attention weight visualizations offer insight into model focus on salient substructures.

- Weakness: Empirical validation is unconvincing due to poorly described baselines and unclear whether baselines were fairly integrated with the tagger.

- Weakness: Redundant architecture uses two RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) increasing parameters without justification or size-matched baselines.

- Weakness: Terminology is misleading—calling syntactic substructures "knowledge" is inappropriate compared to external knowledge sources.

- Weakness: No robustness analysis to parser errors, undermining real-world applicability.

- Weakness: Methodological flaws include static attention (not per-word dynamic) and inner-product weighting bias favoring leaf nodes.

- Question: Why use static attention instead of per-word dynamic attention, and how would that affect performance?

- Question: How were the TreeRNN and DCNN baselines implemented to ensure fair comparison with the joint RNN tagger?

- Question: Could simpler baselines (e.g., feeding head words/NER features into the RNN) control for feature-based effects?

- Overall: K-SAN is practical but lacks novelty and has weak experimental/methodological support; authors failed to address key concerns in rebuttal, leading to rejection.
