- Proposal: K-SAN — a neural model that encodes dependency trees/AMR graphs as substructure vectors and uses attention over them for sequence tagging.  
- Claimed contributions: end-to-end structural attention learning, generalization across knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability.  
- Main novelty concern: practical use of linguistic structures for slot-filling is useful but not highly novel relative to prior structured-attention/Tree-RNN work.  
- Model detail: encodes substructures from parses/AMR into vectors, then attention-weights those for semantic tag prediction.  
- Empirical strength: shows robustness to data scarcity with meaningful gains on a 1/40 training-data setting.  
- Flexibility strength: performs consistently across multiple knowledge sources/parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet, rule-based AMR, JAMR).  
- Interpretability strength: attention visualizations indicate the model focuses on relevant substructures (e.g., cities, times).  
- Weakness: insufficient novelty — approach resembles existing structured attention and Tree-RNN/DCNN methods.  
- Weakness: insufficient baseline details — unclear if baselines used comparable sentence/substructure embeddings, undermining claimed improvements.  
- Weakness: unjustified model complexity — uses two RNNs without comparison to simpler single-RNN alternatives or parameter-count controls.  
- Weakness: misleading terminology — calling syntactic/semantic sentence structures “knowledge” conflates with external KB-style knowledge.  
- Weakness: no analysis of sensitivity to parser errors despite relying on parsed substructures.  
- Question: did DCNN and Tree-RNN baselines use the same sentence/substructure embedding methods as K-SAN or were implementations different?  
- Question: why two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) instead of a single RNN integrating structure — theoretical/empirical justification?  
- Question: how does model performance degrade with parser errors (incorrect heads/relations)? provide sensitivity analysis.  
- Question: how does the requirement that knowledge be representable as word sequences limit applicability to other knowledge representations?
