

**Summary and Contributions**  
The paper proposes K-SAN, a neural architecture that encodes linguistic substructures (e.g., dependency paths) as memory vectors for slot-filling. The main contribution is a "flattening" of structured information into a vector array connected to a tagger, similar to syntax-based attention mechanisms. However, the substructure encoder closely resembles DCNNs, and the approach is practical but lacks novelty. The authors claim generalization across knowledge types, but this is limited to word-sequence representable structures.  

**Strengths**  
1. The model demonstrates reasonable performance on the ATIS dataset, particularly for small training sets, suggesting utility in data-scarce scenarios.  
2. The architecture is conceptually straightforward and could be useful for integrating linguistic structure into sequence tagging.  
3. The visualization of attention weights provides some insight into how the model focuses on salient substructures.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Unconvincing empirical validation**: Baseline implementations are inadequately described, making it unclear whether TreeRNN/DCNN baselines computed sentence and substructure embeddings independently of the tagger. The authors failed to address this in rebuttal.  
2. **Redundant architecture**: Using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) increases parameters without justification; comparable baseline sizes were not reported.  
3. **Misleading terminology**: Labeling syntactic substructures as "knowledge" is inaccurate, as "knowledge" typically denotes external world knowledge (e.g., knowledge graphs).  
4. **Lack of robustness analysis**: No evaluation of sensitivity to parser errors, which is critical for real-world deployment.  
5. **Methodological flaws**: The static attention over substructures (vs. per-word dynamic attention) and inner-product weighting bias toward leaf nodes weaken the approach.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Why was a static attention mechanism used instead of per-word dynamic attention, and how would this impact performance?  
2. How were the TreeRNN and DCNN baselines implemented to ensure fair comparison with the joint RNN tagger?  
3. Could a simpler baseline (e.g., directly feeding head words/NER features into the RNN) control for feature-based effects?  

Overall, while K-SAN offers a practical framework, its lack of novelty, weak experimental validation, and unresolved methodological issues prevent it from making a significant contribution to the field. The post-rebuttal failure to address key concerns solidifies the decision against acceptance.
