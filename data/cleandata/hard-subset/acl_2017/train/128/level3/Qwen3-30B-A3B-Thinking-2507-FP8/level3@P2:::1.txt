

# Review for "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture that incorporates linguistic structures (specifically dependency trees and AMR graphs) as "knowledge" to guide attention for sequence tagging in natural language understanding. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) end-to-end learning of structural attention, (2) generalization across different knowledge types, and (3) efficiency and parallelizability.

The most significant contribution I see is the practical implementation of using linguistic structures for slot-filling, though this is not highly novel. The model encodes substructures from dependency trees or AMR graphs into vector representations, then uses attention to weigh these substructures when predicting semantic tags. The authors claim this approach generalizes better with limited training data.

## Strengths

1. **Demonstrated robustness to data scarcity**: The model shows meaningful improvements on the Small dataset (1/40 training data) compared to baselines, suggesting better generalization capabilities when training data is limited. This is particularly valuable for real-world NLU applications where labeled data is scarce.

2. **Generalization across knowledge sources**: The model performs consistently well across different knowledge representations (dependency trees from Stanford and SyntaxNet, AMR graphs from rule-based and JAMR parsers), demonstrating the flexibility of the approach to incorporate various structured linguistic information.

3. **Clear visualization of attention mechanisms**: The attention visualization (Figure 5) effectively demonstrates that the model learns to focus on relevant substructures (e.g., origin/destination cities, time-related phrases), providing interpretable evidence of the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty**: The core approach is essentially a structured attention mechanism similar to existing approaches like DCNN (Ma et al., 2015a) and Tree-RNN (Tai et al., 2015). The authors' "knowledge-guided" attention appears to be a rebranding of standard structured attention, with no clear distinction from prior work that uses linguistic structures.

2. **Insufficient baseline comparison**: The paper does not provide enough detail about how baselines were implemented. Crucially, it's unclear whether Tree-RNN and DCNN baselines used the same sentence and substructure embedding approaches as K-SAN, making the reported improvements difficult to verify. The claim that K-SAN "outperforms state-of-the-art neural network based frameworks" is questionable without this information.

3. **Unjustified model complexity**: The joint RNN tagger uses two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) without justification or comparison to a simpler alternative that directly incorporates structural information. The paper fails to report whether the model size was comparable to baselines, making it unclear if the performance gains are due to better architecture or simply more parameters.

4. **Misleading terminology**: Using "knowledge" to refer to linguistic structures (dependency trees, AMR graphs) is misleading, as "knowledge" in NLP typically refers to external knowledge bases (e.g., Freebase, Wikipedia), not syntactic/semantic structures of the input sentence. This misnomer undermines the paper's main claim.

5. **No analysis of parser error sensitivity**: The paper uses a dependency parser to extract substructures but provides no analysis of how the model performs when the parser makes errors (as shown in Figure 5). Given that parsing errors are common in real-world applications, this is a significant omission.

## Questions to Authors

1. How exactly do the DCNN and Tree-RNN baselines differ from K-SAN in terms of how they incorporate linguistic structures? Specifically, did they use the same sentence and substructure embedding approaches, or were they implemented differently?

2. Why does K-SAN use two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) instead of a single RNN that directly incorporates structural information? What is the theoretical justification for this design choice, and how does it compare to simpler alternatives?

3. Could you provide an analysis of how the model performs when the dependency parser makes errors (e.g., incorrect head words, misparsed relations)? How sensitive is the model to parser quality?

4. The paper claims "knowledge" from different sources, but the approach requires knowledge to be representable as word sequences (e.g., paths in dependency trees). How does this limit the model's applicability to knowledge representations that cannot be easily converted to sequences?

The paper presents a practical approach that works on ATIS, but the lack of novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and misleading terminology significantly undermine its contribution. The authors should clarify these points before the work could be considered for acceptance.
