

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a model for natural language understanding that incorporates linguistic structure into sequence tagging through attention over knowledge-guided substructures. The core contribution is a method for encoding structural information (e.g., dependency paths) as memory vectors that guide attention during slot filling. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning of substructure importance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

The strongest contribution is the demonstration that incorporating structural information through attention improves performance on small datasets (Small: 1/40 training data), with K-SAN (CNN) achieving 74.60 F1 compared to the best baseline (CNN) at 73.57. This suggests better generalization to unseen data, which is valuable for NLU systems with limited training data. The model shows consistent improvement across dataset sizes, with significant gains on Small (1.03 F1) and Medium (2.47 F1) datasets.

A notable strength is the model's ability to work with different knowledge resources. K-SAN achieves comparable performance using dependency trees (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and AMR graphs (Rule-Based, JAMR), demonstrating generalization to different structural representations. This is important for practical deployment where different knowledge sources may be available.

Another strength is the model's efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling. The authors claim the model doesn't scale linearly with sentence length, though this isn't thoroughly demonstrated with runtime measurements.

The main weakness is the lack of novelty. The approach closely resembles existing structured attention methods like Tree-RNN and DCNN, with the key difference being that K-SAN uses attention over pre-extracted substructures rather than learning tree structures directly. The "knowledge-guided" aspect is misleading since linguistic structures aren't external knowledge but inherent to the sentence, contradicting the paper's framing.

The experimental validation is weak. The paper doesn't clarify how the baselines (Tree-RNN, DCNN) were implemented. Crucially, it's unclear whether these baselines used the same sentence and substructure embeddings as K-SAN, making the comparison invalid. The authors should have provided a direct comparison where all models use the same sentence embeddings and only differ in how they incorporate structural information.

The model's architecture is unnecessarily complex. Using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) increases parameters without clear justification. A more parsimonious baseline would feed knowledge features (e.g., head words from dependency trees) directly into a single RNN, which would control for the simple effect of adding features. The authors don't provide parameter count comparisons or ablation studies to justify the dual-RNN design.

The attention mechanism is static, applying the same attention weights to all words in the sentence. This is problematic because different words might require different attention to substructures. The authors don't justify why per-word dynamic attention wasn't used, which would be more natural for sequence tagging.

The claim about "generalization to different knowledge" is overstated. The model only works with structured information representable as word sequences (dependency paths, AMR graphs), excluding many knowledge bases that don't produce such sequences. This severely limits the model's applicability.

The paper also fails to analyze sensitivity to parser errors. Since the approach relies on dependency parsing, it's critical to understand how errors in the parser affect performance, but this analysis is absent.

The paper's terminology is misleading. Calling linguistic structures "knowledge" confuses the field, as knowledge typically refers to external knowledge bases like Freebase or WordNet, not syntactic structures. This misnomer undermines the paper's core contribution.

In summary, K-SAN presents a practical approach that improves slot filling performance on small datasets, but the contribution lacks novelty, the experimental validation is insufficient, and the claims are overstated. The model's architecture could be simplified, and the experimental design should have included more appropriate baselines. The authors' post-rebuttal response failed to address the critical concerns about baseline implementation and unnecessary complexity.

Questions to Authors:
1. How exactly were Tree-RNN and DCNN baselines implemented? Did they use the same sentence and substructure embeddings as K-SAN, or did they compute embeddings differently?
2. Why use two separate RNNs instead of a single RNN with knowledge features (e.g., head words) added as input?
3. What are the parameter counts for K-SAN versus baselines, and how does the dual-RNN design affect model size?
4. How sensitive is the model to errors in dependency parsing, and what is the impact of incorrect substructures on performance?
5. Why use static attention over substructures rather than per-word dynamic attention?
