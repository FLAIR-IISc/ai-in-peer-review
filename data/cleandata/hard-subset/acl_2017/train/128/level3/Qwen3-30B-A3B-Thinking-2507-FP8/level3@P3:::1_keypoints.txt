- Paper proposes K-SAN: a sequence tagger that embeds linguistic substructures as memory vectors and uses knowledge-guided attention.
- Claimed contributions: end-to-end learning of knowledge-guided attention, generalization across knowledge representations, and efficiency via parallelizable substructure modeling.
- Core idea is a simple flattening of structured information into vectors connected to a tagger, resembling prior structured/syntax-based attention.
- Substructure encoder is similar to DCNN (embedding ancestor word sequences), reducing novelty.
- Main empirical contribution is showing structured information can improve slot filling on ATIS, especially with limited data.
- Strength: approach is practical and straightforward to implement, useful in data-scarce NLU settings.
- Strength: provides attention visualizations that illustrate focus on relevant substructures.
- Strength: shows consistent performance across different linguistic formalisms (dependency trees, AMR).
- Weakness: experimental baselines lack detail, making comparisons and claims unconvincing.
- Weakness: dual-RNN architecture increases parameters without justification or comparable baseline sizes.
- Weakness: calling syntactic structures “knowledge” is misleading and overstates generalization claims.
- Weakness: uses a static attention/filter over substructures rather than per-word dynamic attention, with no justification.
- Weakness: no analysis of sensitivity to parser errors despite reliance on external parses.
- Questions: clarify baseline implementations (Tree-RNN/DCNN), justify dual-RNN vs single-RNN alternative, and explain applicability beyond sequence-representable knowledge.
- Overall: practical but limited-novelty work with weak experimental validation and marginal interest to the community.
