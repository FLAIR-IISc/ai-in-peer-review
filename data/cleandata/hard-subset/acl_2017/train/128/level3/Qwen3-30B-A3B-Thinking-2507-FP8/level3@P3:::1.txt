

# Review of "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture for sequence tagging that incorporates linguistic substructures (e.g., dependency tree paths) as memory vectors. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) end-to-end learning of knowledge-guided attention, (2) generalization across different knowledge representations, and (3) efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

However, the actual contributions are more limited than claimed. The core idea is a simple "flattening" of structured information into an array of vectors connected to a tagger, similar to structured/syntax-based attention mechanisms. The substructure encoder resembles DCNN (embedding nodes from ancestor word sequences), making the approach practical but not highly novel. The key contribution appears to be the demonstration that structured information can improve slot filling performance on ATIS, particularly with limited training data.

## Strengths
1. **Practical implementation**: The approach is straightforward to implement and demonstrates clear improvements on the small training data setting, which is valuable for data-scarce NLU applications.
2. **Attention visualization**: The paper provides useful visualizations of the attention weights learned by the model, showing how it focuses on relevant substructures (e.g., origin/destination in flight booking examples).
3. **Robustness to different knowledge sources**: The model shows consistent performance across different knowledge representations (dependency trees, AMR graphs), suggesting some generalization capability.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient baseline details**: The experimental results are unconvincing due to lack of details about baseline implementations. It's unclear whether Tree-RNN and DCNN baselines computed both sentence and substructure embeddings independently of the joint tagger, making direct comparison impossible.
2. **Unnecessary model complexity**: The use of two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) appears to increase parameter count without clear justification. Comparable baseline sizes were not reported, and a simpler baseline feeding knowledge features directly into a single RNN should have been included to control for feature effects.
3. **Misleading terminology**: Calling syntactic/substructure information "knowledge" is misleading, as "knowledge" typically denotes external world knowledge (e.g., knowledge bases), not linguistic structures. This misnomer undermines the claimed contribution of generalizing to different knowledge types.
4. **Static attention mechanism**: The model uses a static attention/filter over substructures for all words, rather than per-word dynamic attention, which would likely be more effective. The authors provide no justification for this design choice.
5. **Lack of robustness analysis**: No analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors was provided, which is critical since the approach depends on external dependency parsing.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you clarify how the Tree-RNN and DCNN baselines were implemented? Specifically, did they compute sentence and substructure embeddings independently of the tagger, as the K-SAN model does?
2. Why was the joint RNN tagger with two separate RNNs chosen over a simpler baseline that feeds knowledge features directly into a single RNN? What evidence supports the claim that this dual-RNN architecture is necessary?
3. The paper claims generalization to different "knowledge" sources, but the approach only works with knowledge that can be represented as word sequences. How does this limitation affect the practical applicability of the model beyond the specific formalisms tested (dependency trees, AMR)?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a practical approach that works on the ATIS dataset but lacks novelty, strong theoretical contributions, and convincing experimental validation. The core idea is a straightforward extension of existing structured attention mechanisms, with minimal innovation. The empirical results are questionable due to insufficient baseline details, and the claimed generalization to different knowledge types is weak since the model's applicability is limited to knowledge formalisms that can be represented as word sequences.

While the model shows modest improvements (particularly on small datasets), the paper fails to demonstrate why this approach is superior to simpler alternatives that would be easier to implement and train. The post-rebuttal response did not adequately address the main concerns about baseline implementation and unnecessary parameter increase from the dual-RNN architecture, so the reviewer's score remains unchanged. The work is of marginal interest to the ACL community due to its limited novelty and weak validation.
