- Proposal: K-SAN incorporates linguistic structure into sequence tagging via attention over knowledge-guided substructures encoded as memory vectors.  
- Claimed contributions: end-to-end learning of substructure importance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency via parallelizable substructure modeling.  
- Empirical strength: improves slot-filling on small data (Small: K-SAN(CNN) 74.60 F1 vs baseline 73.57), with consistent gains across dataset sizes.  
- Knowledge-generalization: performs comparably using different structural resources (dependency trees and AMR), showing applicability to multiple representations.  
- Efficiency claim: model is parallelizable and purportedly does not scale linearly with sentence length, but runtime evidence is lacking.  
- Novelty concern: approach resembles existing structured-attention/Tree-RNN/DCNN methods; “knowledge-guided” framing is misleading since structures are inherent to sentences.  
- Weak experimental validation: baseline implementations (Tree-RNN, DCNN) are not clearly described, raising questions about fair comparison and embedding parity.  
- Architectural complexity: using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) increases parameters without justification or ablation studies; simpler single-RNN baselines not tested.  
- Static attention issue: attention weights are applied uniformly to all words rather than per-word dynamic attention, which is more natural for sequence tagging.  
- Overstated generalization: only works with structures representable as word sequences, excluding many external knowledge bases.  
- No parser-error analysis: sensitivity to dependency/AMR parser errors is not evaluated despite reliance on parsed substructures.  
- Terminology problem: calling syntactic/semantic structures “knowledge” misrepresents standard use of the term (external KBs).  
- Overall summary: practical small-data gains but limited novelty, insufficient/possibly invalid experiments, overstated claims, and unnecessary model complexity.  
- Questions for authors: clarify baseline implementations and embeddings, justify dual-RNN design vs single-RNN with knowledge features, provide parameter counts and ablations, analyze parser-error sensitivity, and explain choice of static vs per-word attention.
