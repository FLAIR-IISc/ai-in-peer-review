- Paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) to incorporate structural linguistic info into slot filling via memory vectors from dependency substructures.  
- Core idea: encode parse-derived substructures as vectors and attend to them during sequence tagging.  
- Demonstrates practical improvements on ATIS but overall novelty is limited.  
- Main contribution: practical demonstration of integrating readily available linguistic structures into an RNN tagger.  
- Method “flattens” structure into vectors and uses standard RNN+attention; does not fundamentally change sequence tagging paradigm.  
- Uses standard building blocks (RNNs, attention); novelty mainly in architectural assembly rather than new primitives.  
- Secondary contribution: experiments with different substructure encoders (NN, RNN, CNN).  
- Empirical finding: CNN encoders work better with smaller datasets, RNN encoders prefer larger datasets.  
- Strength: clear presentation of K-SAN architecture and reasonably thorough evaluation on ATIS.  
- Strength: inclusion of multiple knowledge-encoding schemes and observed gains over several baselines.  
- Major weakness: experimental validation not fully convincing; baseline implementation details are insufficient.  
- Unclear whether TreeRNN and DCNN baselines computed sentence and substructure embeddings independently of the tagger.  
- Missing baseline that simply feeds extra knowledge features (e.g., head words, NER tags) into the RNN to isolate architecture benefit.  
- Use of two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessarily complex; parameter-cost comparison with baselines not reported.  
- Attention is static/ global over substructures for all words rather than per-word dynamic attention, which seems limiting.  
- Inner-product weighting between sentence and substructures may bias toward nodes similar to whole sentence (favoring leaf nodes).  
- Claim of generalizing to different “knowledge” is weak because method relies on linearizing substructures as word sequences.  
- Framing syntactic/substructure info as “knowledge” is misleading compared to external/world knowledge sources.  
- No analysis of sensitivity to dependency parser errors; potential error propagation untested.  
- Questions for authors requested: details of baseline implementations and embeddings.  
- Question: whether a baseline feeding additional knowledge features into RNN was tested and its results.  
- Question: rationale for static attention and whether per-word dynamic attention was considered.  
- Question: experiments assessing sensitivity to parser errors.  
- Question: quantification of parameter increase due to the two RNNs.  
- Additional comments: writing is generally good but could be more concise.  
- Additional comments: attention visualizations are helpful but should highlight most influential substructures per word.  
- Additional comments: related work discussion is comprehensive but could be more critical.
