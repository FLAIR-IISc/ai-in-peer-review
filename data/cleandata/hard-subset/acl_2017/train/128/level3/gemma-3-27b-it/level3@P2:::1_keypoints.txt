- Paper proposes K-SAN: a neural tagger that attends to dependency-derived substructures represented as memory vectors for slot filling.
- Primary contribution is a practical method to integrate structured linguistic substructures into a neural NLU model.
- Approach flattens structured information into vector arrays similar to structured attention and connects them to a recurrent tagger.
- Authors claim applicability to different knowledge forms (dependency trees, AMR) but require substructures to be word sequences.
- Experiments on ATIS show performance gains, especially in low-data (Small and Medium) settings.
- Strength: conceptually simple, implementable design that easily integrates different substructure information.
- Strength: consistent performance improvements over strong baselines when data is scarce.
- Strength: demonstrated flexibility across different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR).
- Weakness: limited novelty relative to prior structured models (DCNNs, Tree-RNNs); distinction from prior work is not clearly emphasized.
- Weakness: missing a strong baseline that adds simple knowledge features to an RNN tagger, so gains may not be clearly attributable to structural attention.
- Weakness: architecture uses two RNNs (chain tagger + knowledge-guided tagger) which may be unnecessarily complex and prone to overfitting; parameter counts not compared.
- Weakness: no analysis of sensitivity to parser errors, so robustness to noisy parses is unclear.
- Weakness: attention is static per word (same weights to all substructures), lacking per-word dynamic attention to pick relevant substructures.
- Weakness: calling syntactic/substructure information “knowledge” is misleading compared to external/world knowledge.
- Question: provide detailed parameter counts for K-SAN variants and baselines.
- Question: analyze model sensitivity to dependency parser errors.
- Question: why not explore per-word dynamic attention and what are its potential benefits/challenges?
- Question: clarify baseline implementations (hyperparameters, training, DCNN/Tree-RNN feature extraction and tagger setup).
- Additional comment: attention visualizations are useful but need quantitative analysis of attention distributions.
- Additional comment: claim of generalization would be stronger with experiments on more diverse knowledge sources.
- Overall assessment: practical and promising but limited novelty and insufficient evaluation; currently a weak accept pending rebuttal/improvements.
