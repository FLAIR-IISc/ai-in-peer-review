## Review for ACL 2017 – Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural network architecture designed to incorporate prior linguistic knowledge – specifically, substructures derived from dependency parses – into a sequence tagging model for Natural Language Understanding (NLU). The core idea is to represent these substructures as memory vectors and attend to them during the tagging process.

**Summary and Contributions:**

The paper’s primary contribution lies in its practical approach to integrating structured linguistic information into a neural NLU model. While not fundamentally novel, K-SAN offers a relatively straightforward method of leveraging substructure information for improved slot filling performance. The model effectively "flattens" structured information into an array of vector representations, similar to existing structured attention mechanisms, and connects it to a recurrent tagger. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of the model's applicability to different forms of knowledge representation, although this claim is qualified by the need for substructures to be representable as word sequences. Finally, the experiments on the ATIS dataset demonstrate performance improvements, particularly in low-data scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Approach:** K-SAN provides a practical and implementable method for incorporating structured linguistic information into a neural NLU system. The model’s design is conceptually simple and allows for the integration of different types of substructure information with minimal architectural changes.
2. **Performance Improvements:** The experiments show consistent performance improvements over several strong baselines, particularly when training data is limited. This suggests that the model effectively leverages prior knowledge to mitigate the challenges of data scarcity. The results on the smaller datasets (Small and Medium) are the most compelling.
3. **Generalizability to Knowledge Sources:** The authors demonstrate the ability to utilize different types of knowledge resources – dependency trees and AMR graphs – with the same model architecture. While the applicability is limited by the requirement of representing substructures as word sequences, this flexibility is a positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core contribution – incorporating structured information into a neural network – is not entirely novel. Existing work, such as DCNNs and Tree-RNNs, already explores similar concepts. The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight what distinguishes K-SAN from these prior approaches beyond its specific implementation details.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The experimental setup lacks a strong baseline that directly isolates the impact of the knowledge-guided attention mechanism. A crucial comparison missing is a standard RNN tagger augmented with simple knowledge features (e.g., head words, POS tags, NER tags) as input. Without this, it is difficult to determine whether the performance gains are due to the structural attention mechanism itself, or simply the incorporation of additional lexical or syntactic features.
3. **Unnecessary Model Complexity:** The use of two separate RNNs – a chain-based tagger and a knowledge-guided tagger – seems unnecessarily complex. The justification for this design is not fully convincing, and the additional parameters likely contribute to overfitting. The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of parameter counts for K-SAN and the baselines.
4. **Lack of Robustness Analysis:** The paper does not address the potential impact of parser errors on model performance. Dependency parsing is not perfect, and inaccuracies in the parse tree can propagate errors to the substructure embeddings and ultimately affect the tagging accuracy. An analysis of model sensitivity to parsing errors would strengthen the evaluation.
5. **Static Attention Mechanism:** The model utilizes a static attention mechanism, applying the same weights to all substructures for each word. A more sophisticated approach would employ per-word dynamic attention, allowing the model to selectively attend to the most relevant substructures for each word in the input sentence.
6. **Misleading Terminology:** Referring to syntactic/substructure information as "knowledge" is potentially misleading. “Knowledge” typically implies external or world knowledge derived from knowledge bases or other sources, rather than internal linguistic structure.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a detailed comparison of the number of parameters for K-SAN (with each of the encoder types) and the baseline models?
2.  How sensitive is the model's performance to parser errors? Have you conducted any analysis to assess the impact of inaccurate dependency parses?
3.  Why was a per-word dynamic attention mechanism not explored? Could you comment on the potential benefits and challenges of such an approach?
4.  Can you clarify the specific implementation details of the baselines (e.g., hyperparameters, training procedure) to ensure a fair comparison? Specifically, how were DCNN and Tree-RNN implemented in terms of feature extraction and tagger architecture?

**Additional Comments:**

The visualizations of the attention weights (Figure 5) are helpful, but a quantitative analysis of the attention distributions would provide a more rigorous assessment of the model's behavior. The claim regarding generalization to various knowledge types would be stronger if the authors demonstrated the model's performance with more diverse knowledge sources beyond dependency trees and AMR graphs.



**Overall Assessment:**

K-SAN is a practical and reasonably effective approach to integrating structured linguistic information into an NLU model. However, the lack of significant novelty, the insufficient baseline comparisons, and the absence of robustness analysis weaken the overall impact of the paper. While the results are promising, further experimentation and a more thorough evaluation are needed to fully demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.



**Score:** Weak Accept (currently, based on the potential for improvement with rebuttal).
