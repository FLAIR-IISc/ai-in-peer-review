## Review of Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture designed to incorporate structural linguistic information into sequence tagging, specifically for slot filling in natural language understanding. The core idea is to encode substructures (derived from dependency parsing) as memory vectors and then attend to these vectors during the tagging process. While the motivation for leveraging structural knowledge is sound, and the proposed approach is practically implementable, the work suffers from a lack of novelty and convincing empirical validation.

The primary contribution, as framed by the authors, is the end-to-end learning of attention over substructures. However, this is largely a "flattening" of structured information into a vector space coupled with attention – a concept similar to structured or syntax-based attention mechanisms already present in the literature. A more significant contribution lies in the practical demonstration of how to integrate dependency parse information into an RNN-based slot filling system, even if the underlying method isn’t groundbreaking. Finally, the exploration of knowledge generalization across different knowledge sources (dependency trees vs. AMR graphs) is a minor positive aspect. 

Despite the reasonable approach, several weaknesses undermine the paper’s impact. The empirical results are unconvincing. The experimental setup lacks crucial details regarding baseline implementation. It’s unclear if TreeRNN and DCNN baselines computed embeddings independently of the tagger, potentially inflating their performance. The use of two separate RNNs – one chain-based and one knowledge-guided – seems unnecessary, increasing parameter count without clear justification, and a comparison of parameter sizes is missing. Crucially, no baseline incorporating simple knowledge features (e.g., head words, NER tags) into the RNN was included, failing to control for the effect of simply *providing* knowledge to the model. Furthermore, the analysis is limited – there’s no discussion of how sensitive the model is to parser errors. The static attention mechanism over substructures is also questionable; a per-word dynamic attention approach could potentially yield better results. The claim of generalizing to "different kinds of knowledge" is weak, as the method relies on representing structures as word sequences.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Approach:** The model provides a concrete method for incorporating structural knowledge into a standard slot filling pipeline.
*   **Knowledge Generalization Exploration:** The attempt to evaluate the model with different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) is a positive, though not decisive, contribution.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the architecture clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of flattening structural information and using attention is not novel.
*   **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:**  Missing details and controls in the baseline implementations cast doubt on the validity of the results.
*   **Unjustified Complexity:** The dual-RNN architecture adds complexity without clear benefits.
*   **Limited Analysis:** The lack of error analysis and sensitivity testing hinders understanding of the model's behavior.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Were the TreeRNN and DCNN baselines trained to compute embeddings independently of the tagger, or were they jointly trained as part of a single end-to-end system?
2.  Please provide a detailed comparison of the number of parameters in your K-SAN models and the baseline models.
3.  How did the performance change when incorporating simple knowledge features (e.g. head words, POS tags) directly into the standard RNN baseline?
