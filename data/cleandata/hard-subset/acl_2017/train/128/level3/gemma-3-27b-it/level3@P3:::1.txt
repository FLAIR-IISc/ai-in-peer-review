## Review of Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural architecture aiming to incorporate structural linguistic information into sequence tagging, specifically for slot filling in natural language understanding. The core idea is to encode substructures (derived from dependency parsing) as memory vectors and attend to them during sequence tagging, effectively guiding the RNN with prior knowledge. While the approach is practical and addresses a relevant problem – leveraging linguistic structure for NLU – its contributions are incremental, and the experimental validation is unconvincing.

**Summary:** The paper introduces K-SAN, a model that augments a standard RNN-based sequence tagger with a “knowledge encoding module” and an attention mechanism. This module encodes structural substructures (e.g., dependency paths) into vector representations, which are then used to inform the RNN’s hidden state. The authors claim that this allows the model to effectively extract salient knowledge, generalize to unseen data, and outperform state-of-the-art approaches.

**Contributions:** I identify three primary contributions, framed based on my assessment rather than the authors' claims:

1. **Practical Application of Structured Attention:** The paper demonstrates a workable method for integrating structural information from dependency parses into a neural sequence tagging framework using a relatively simple attention mechanism. This provides a concrete instantiation of the broader idea of structured attention.
2. **Exploration of Knowledge Encoding Strategies:** The work explores different methods (NN, RNN, CNN) for encoding the substructure information, offering insights into the impact of the encoding method on performance.
3. **Demonstration on a Standard NLU Task:** The paper applies the proposed method to the widely used ATIS dataset, providing a tangible test case for evaluating the effectiveness of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly identifies the limitations of traditional flat-sequence tagging approaches and articulates the motivation for incorporating structural knowledge.
2. **Relatively Simple and Understandable Architecture:** K-SAN’s architecture is straightforward and reasonably easy to understand. The use of attention over substructures is a logical and intuitive approach.
3. **Demonstrated Performance Gains (with caveats):** The results show improvements over some baselines, particularly on the smaller datasets, suggesting a potential benefit from incorporating structural knowledge.
4. **Exploration of Knowledge Generalization:** The attempt to show generalization to different knowledge sources (AMR graphs alongside dependency trees) is a valuable exploration, even if the results aren’t dramatically different.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Baseline Details & Comparison:** The most significant weakness is the lack of detail regarding the implementation and tuning of the baselines. It is unclear whether TreeRNN and DCNN were used to generate embeddings *independently* of the tagger, or whether they were integrated in the same way as K-SAN. This makes it difficult to fairly compare the performance.
2. **Redundant RNNs and Parameter Count:** The use of two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessary and potentially increases the parameter count without a clear justification. The paper does not report baseline model sizes to assess this impact.
3. **Missing Feature-Based Baseline:** A critical missing baseline is one that directly feeds additional knowledge features (e.g., head words, NER tags) into the RNN, *without* the complex substructure encoding. This would help disentangle whether the improvements are due to the sophisticated substructure representation or simply the inclusion of relevant features.
4. **Lack of Robustness Analysis:** There is no analysis of the model's sensitivity to parser errors. Dependency parsing is not perfect, and the model's performance could be significantly affected by inaccuracies in the parse trees.
5. **Static Attention Mechanism:** The use of a static attention/filter over substructures for all words is a limitation. A per-word, dynamic attention mechanism would likely be more effective, allowing the model to focus on the most relevant substructures for each individual word.
6. **Inner Product Weighting Bias:** The inner product weighting of substructures with the sentence embedding may introduce a bias towards nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes). This could limit the model’s ability to effectively utilize information from deeper, more abstract substructures.
7. **Weak Generalization Claim:** The claim that the model generalizes to different kinds of "knowledge" is weak. The substructures *must* be representable as word sequences, limiting its applicability to knowledge formalisms that can be linearized in this way.
8. **Misleading Terminology:** Referring to syntactic/substructure information as "knowledge" is misleading, as "knowledge" typically denotes external/world knowledge (e.g., facts from a knowledge base).

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a detailed description of how the baseline models (TreeRNN, DCNN) were implemented and tuned? Specifically, were embeddings computed independently of the tagger?
2. What is the parameter count for K-SAN compared to the baseline models?
3. Have you experimented with a baseline that directly feeds knowledge features (e.g., head words, NER tags) into the RNN, without the substructure encoding? If so, what were the results?
4. Have you analyzed the impact of parser errors on the model's performance?
5. Why was a static attention mechanism used instead of a per-word, dynamic attention mechanism?
6. Did you consider alternative weighting schemes to mitigate the potential bias introduced by the inner product weighting?



**Overall:**

This paper presents a reasonably practical approach to incorporating structural information into neural sequence tagging. However, the lack of strong experimental validation, particularly regarding baseline comparisons and robustness analysis, limits the significance of the findings. The incremental nature of the contribution, combined with the weaknesses in the experimental setup, leads me to believe that this paper does not currently meet the bar for acceptance. The post-rebuttal information confirms this assessment, as the authors did not address the core concerns raised about baseline usage and the added complexity of the dual-RNN architecture.
