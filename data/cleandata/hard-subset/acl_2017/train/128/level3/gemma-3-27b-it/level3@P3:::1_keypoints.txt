- Summary: Proposes K-SAN, which encodes dependency-derived substructures as memory vectors and attends to them to guide an RNN for slot filling.  
- Contribution 1: Practical method integrating dependency-structure via attention into sequence tagging (structured attention instantiation).  
- Contribution 2: Compares different substructure encoding methods (NN, RNN, CNN) to assess their impact.  
- Contribution 3: Evaluates the approach on the ATIS slot-filling task as a concrete test case.  
- Strength 1: Clearly motivates the need for structural knowledge beyond flat sequence tagging.  
- Strength 2: Architecture is simple and interpretable, using attention over substructures.  
- Strength 3: Shows some performance gains versus baselines, especially on smaller datasets.  
- Strength 4: Attempts to generalize to other knowledge sources (e.g., AMR graphs) as an exploratory analysis.  
- Weakness 1: Baseline implementation and tuning details are insufficient, making comparisons unclear.  
- Weakness 2: Dual-RNN design may be redundant and increases parameter count without justification or reported model sizes.  
- Weakness 3: Missing simple feature-based baseline (e.g., feeding head words/NER tags) to isolate the benefit of complex encoding.  
- Weakness 4: No analysis of robustness to parser errors, despite reliance on dependency parses.  
- Weakness 5: Uses a static, global attention over substructures instead of per-word dynamic attention, which is limiting.  
- Weakness 6: Inner-product weighting may bias attention toward substructures similar to the sentence (e.g., leaf nodes), reducing deeper substructure utility.  
- Weakness 7: Generalization claim is weak because substructures must be linearizable as word sequences, limiting applicability.  
- Weakness 8: Calling syntactic/substructure information “knowledge” is misleading relative to external/world knowledge.  
- Question 1: How were TreeRNN and DCNN baselines implemented and tuned—were embeddings computed independently of the tagger?  
- Question 2: What are the parameter counts for K-SAN versus baseline models?  
- Question 3: Did you try a baseline that directly feeds knowledge features (head words, NER) into the RNN, and what were the results?  
- Question 4: Have you measured the impact of parser errors on model performance?  
- Question 5: Why choose a static attention mechanism rather than per-word dynamic attention?  
- Question 6: Were alternative weighting schemes considered to avoid the inner-product bias?  
- Overall: K-SAN is a practical, incrementally novel approach, but weak experimental validation, missing baselines, and robustness concerns lead the reviewer to recommend rejection.
