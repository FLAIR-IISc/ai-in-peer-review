- Summary: Proposes K-SAN, a neural architecture that uses linguistic substructures (e.g., dependency relations) via a knowledge encoding module, sentence encoder, and tagger for slot filling.

- Contribution: Introduces a novel architecture that injects prior linguistic substructures to improve sequence tagging.

- Contribution: Frames K-SAN as a generalization of RNNs capable of handling non-flat, knowledge-guided topologies.

- Contribution: Claims the model composes salient substructures to learn robust sentence representations for NLU tasks.

- Strength: Novel approach for incorporating prior knowledge into neural NLU models to potentially boost sequence tagging.

- Strength: Can model complex, non-flat linguistic structures through knowledge-guided topology.

- Strength: Learns whole-sentence representations by composing informative substructures.

- Weakness: Lacks clear novelty because similar architectures exist in prior work.

- Weakness: Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient detail on baseline implementations and comparisons.

- Weakness: Uses a static attention/filter over substructures for all words, which may be suboptimal.

- Weakness: Inner-product weighting with the sentence embedding may bias selection toward substructures similar to the whole sentence.

- Weakness: Claim of generalizing to different kinds of "knowledge" is weak since substructures must be word-sequence representable.

- Question: Please provide more details on baseline implementations and comparison methodology.

- Question: Why use two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) instead of a single RNN with added knowledge features?

- Question: Can you analyze the model's sensitivity to parser errors?

- Question: Why choose static substructure attention instead of per-word dynamic attention?

- Question: How does the inner-product weighting with the sentence embedding affect performance?

- Additional comment: Practical approach but lacks strong novelty and convincing empirical takeaways; consider per-word dynamic attention and parser-error analysis to strengthen results.
