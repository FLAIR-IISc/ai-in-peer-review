**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel neural architecture called Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for natural language understanding (NLU) tasks, specifically slot filling. The model encodes structural linguistic substructures as memory vectors and uses them to guide the attention mechanism for sequence tagging. The architecture consists of a knowledge encoding module, a sentence encoder, and a tagger. The knowledge encoding module converts substructures into vector representations, which are then used to compute attention weights for the sentence encoder. The tagger uses the encoded knowledge to predict semantic tags for each word in the input sentence.

**Main Contributions**

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **Practical approach to incorporating structural information**: The paper proposes a simple and practical way to encode structural linguistic substructures as memory vectors for sequence tagging tasks.
2. **End-to-end learning with knowledge guidance**: The model learns to encode knowledge-guided substructures and use them to guide the attention mechanism in an end-to-end manner.
3. **Generalization to different knowledge resources**: The paper demonstrates that the model can generalize to different knowledge resources, including dependency trees and Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR) graphs.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of structural information**: The model effectively uses structural information to improve the performance of slot filling tasks.
2. **End-to-end learning**: The model learns to encode knowledge-guided substructures and use them to guide the attention mechanism in an end-to-end manner, which simplifies the training process.
3. **Generalization to different knowledge resources**: The model demonstrates good generalization to different knowledge resources, which makes it a flexible approach for various NLU tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty**: The paper's approach to encoding structural linguistic substructures as memory vectors is not highly novel and is similar to existing approaches such as DCNN.
2. **Insufficient details about baselines**: The paper does not provide sufficient details about how the baselines were implemented and compared, which makes it difficult to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed model.
3. **Unclear parameter count**: The paper does not report the parameter count of the proposed model and the baselines, which makes it difficult to evaluate the efficiency of the model.
4. **No analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors**: The paper does not provide an analysis of the model's sensitivity to parser errors, which is an important aspect of evaluating the robustness of the model.
5. **Limited applicability**: The paper's approach to encoding structural linguistic substructures as memory vectors may not be applicable to all types of knowledge resources, such as knowledge bases.
