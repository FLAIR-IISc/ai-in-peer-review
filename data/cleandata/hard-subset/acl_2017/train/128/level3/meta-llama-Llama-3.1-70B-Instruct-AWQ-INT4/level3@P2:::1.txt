**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel neural architecture called Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for natural language understanding (NLU) tasks, specifically slot filling. The model incorporates prior knowledge in the form of linguistic substructures, such as dependency relations, to improve the performance of sequence tagging. The K-SAN architecture consists of a knowledge encoding module, a sentence encoder, and a tagger. The knowledge encoding module converts substructures into vector representations, which are then used to compute attention weights for the sentence encoder. The tagger uses the encoded knowledge to predict semantic tags for each word in the input sentence.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. The paper proposes a novel neural architecture that incorporates prior knowledge in the form of linguistic substructures to improve the performance of sequence tagging.
2. The K-SAN architecture is a generalization of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) that can handle non-flat network topologies guided by prior knowledge.
3. The model is able to learn robust representations of whole sentences by composing salient substructures, which can improve the performance of NLU tasks.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to incorporating prior knowledge into neural architectures for NLU tasks, which can improve the performance of sequence tagging.
2. The K-SAN architecture is able to handle non-flat network topologies guided by prior knowledge, which can be useful for modeling complex linguistic structures.
3. The model is able to learn robust representations of whole sentences by composing salient substructures, which can improve the performance of NLU tasks.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The paper lacks novelty in its approach, as similar architectures have been proposed in the past.
2. The empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about how baselines were implemented and compared.
3. The model uses a static attention/filter over substructures for all words, which may not be optimal.
4. The inner-product weighting of substructures with the sentence embedding may bias weights toward nodes similar to the whole sentence.
5. The claim that the model generalizes to different kinds of "knowledge" is weak since substructures must be representable as word sequences, limiting applicability to some formalisms.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details about how the baselines were implemented and compared?
2. Why did you choose to use two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) instead of a single RNN with additional knowledge features?
3. Can you provide an analysis of the model's sensitivity to parser errors?
4. Why did you choose to use a static attention/filter over substructures for all words instead of per-word dynamic attention?
5. Can you provide more details about how the inner-product weighting of substructures with the sentence embedding affects the model's performance?

**Additional Comments**

The paper proposes a practical approach to incorporating prior knowledge into neural architectures for NLU tasks, but it lacks novelty and strong takeaways. The empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about how baselines were implemented and compared. The model's performance may be improved by using per-word dynamic attention and analyzing its sensitivity to parser errors.
