This paper proposes a neural architecture that encodes structural linguistic substructures as memory vectors for sequence tagging/slot-filling, which is a practical approach that works on one dataset. However, it lacks novelty, strong takeaways, and convincing experimental validation.

The main contribution of this paper is a simple "flattening" of structured information into an array of vectors connected to a tagger, similar to structured/syntax-based attention. The substructure encoder is similar to DCNN (embedding nodes from ancestor word sequences), which is not highly novel. The empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about how baselines were implemented and compared.

One of the primary concerns is that it is unclear whether TreeRNN and DCNN baselines were used to compute both sentence and substructure embeddings independently of the joint tagger. Additionally, using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) seems unnecessary and may only increase the parameter count; comparable baseline sizes are not reported. The authors should include a baseline that feeds additional knowledge features directly into the RNN (e.g., head words, NER) to control for simple feature effects.

Furthermore, there is no analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors, which is a crucial aspect of evaluating the robustness of the proposed model. The model uses a static attention/filter over substructures for all words; it is unclear why per-word dynamic attention wasn’t used. Inner-product weighting of substructures with the sentence embedding may bias weights toward nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).

The claim that the model generalizes to different kinds of "knowledge" is weak since substructures must be representable as word sequences, limiting applicability to some formalisms. Calling syntactic/substructure information "knowledge" is misleading because it typically denotes external/world knowledge (e.g., KBs).

The authors' rebuttal did not address the main concerns about baseline usage and extra parameters from two RNNs, so the reviewer’s score remains unchanged.

**Summary of the paper:**
The paper proposes a novel model, knowledge-guided structural attention networks (K-SAN), that leverages prior knowledge as guidance to incorporate non-flat topologies and learn suitable attention for different substructures that are salient for specific tasks. The structured information can be captured from small training data, so the model has better generalization and robustness.

**Main contributions:**

1. The paper proposes a neural architecture that encodes structural linguistic substructures as memory vectors for sequence tagging/slot-filling.
2. The model uses a simple "flattening" of structured information into an array of vectors connected to a tagger, similar to structured/syntax-based attention.
3. The substructure encoder is similar to DCNN (embedding nodes from ancestor word sequences).

**Strengths:**

1. The paper proposes a practical approach that works on one dataset.
2. The model has better generalization and robustness due to its ability to capture structured information from small training data.
3. The paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the proposed model and its components.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper lacks novelty, as the substructure encoder is similar to DCNN.
2. The empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about how baselines were implemented and compared.
3. The paper does not provide a clear analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors.
4. The model uses a static attention/filter over substructures for all words, which may not be optimal.
5. The claim that the model generalizes to different kinds of "knowledge" is weak.

**Questions to authors:**

1. Can you provide more details about how the baselines were implemented and compared?
2. Why did you choose to use two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided), and how did you determine the optimal parameter count?
3. Can you provide an analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors?
4. Why did you choose to use a static attention/filter over substructures for all words, and did you consider using per-word dynamic attention?
5. Can you provide more evidence to support the claim that the model generalizes to different kinds of "knowledge"?
