**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel neural architecture called Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for natural language understanding (NLU). K-SAN incorporates prior knowledge in the form of linguistic structures to guide the attention mechanism and improve the performance of sequence tagging tasks. The model uses a knowledge encoding module to convert linguistic structures into vector representations, which are then used to compute attention weights for different substructures. The attention weights are used to generate a knowledge-guided representation of the input sentence, which is then fed into a recurrent neural network (RNN) tagger to predict semantic tags.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Knowledge-Guided Attention Mechanism**: The paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that uses prior knowledge in the form of linguistic structures to guide the attention weights. This allows the model to focus on salient substructures that are relevant to the task at hand.
2. **End-to-End Learning**: The paper proposes an end-to-end learning framework that integrates the knowledge encoding module, attention mechanism, and RNN tagger into a single neural network. This allows the model to learn the attention weights and the tagger jointly.
3. **Generalization to Different Knowledge Resources**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed model can generalize to different knowledge resources, including dependency trees and Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR) graphs.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved Performance**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed model outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on the ATIS benchmark dataset.
2. **Robustness to Data Scarcity**: The paper shows that the proposed model is robust to data scarcity and can perform well even with small amounts of training data.
3. **Generalization to Different Knowledge Resources**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed model can generalize to different knowledge resources, including dependency trees and AMR graphs.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Novelty**: The paper's approach is not highly novel, as it builds upon existing work on attention mechanisms and knowledge-guided neural networks.
2. **Insufficient Details on Baselines**: The paper does not provide sufficient details on how the baselines were implemented and compared.
3. **Unclear Evaluation Metrics**: The paper uses F1 score as the evaluation metric, but it is unclear how this metric was calculated and whether it is the most suitable metric for the task at hand.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the baselines were implemented and compared?
2. How did you calculate the F1 score, and is it the most suitable metric for the task at hand?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the attention weights and how they relate to the linguistic structures?
4. How does the proposed model handle errors in the linguistic structures, such as parsing errors?
5. Can you provide more details on the knowledge encoding module and how it converts linguistic structures into vector representations?
