- Proposes encoding structural linguistic substructures as memory vectors for sequence tagging/slot-filling.  
- Uses a "flattening" of structured information into an array of vectors connected to a tagger, akin to structured/syntax-based attention.  
- Substructure encoder resembles DCNN (embedding nodes from ancestor word sequences), reducing novelty.  
- Practical approach that works on one dataset and is clearly explained.  
- Claims better generalization and robustness from capturing structured information from small training data.  
- Empirical results are unconvincing due to insufficient details about baseline implementation and comparison.  
- Unclear whether TreeRNN/DCNN baselines computed sentence and substructure embeddings independently of the joint tagger.  
- Using two separate RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided) may be unnecessary and inflates parameter count; comparable baseline sizes not reported.  
- Authors should include a baseline that inputs additional knowledge features directly into the RNN (e.g., head words, NER) to control for simple feature effects.  
- No analysis of model sensitivity to parser errors, undermining robustness claims.  
- Model uses a static attention/filter over substructures for all words; unclear why per-word dynamic attention wasn’t used.  
- Inner-product weighting with the sentence embedding may bias weights toward nodes similar to the whole sentence (e.g., leaf nodes).  
- Claim that the model generalizes to different kinds of "knowledge" is weak because substructures must be representable as word sequences.  
- Calling syntactic/substructure information "knowledge" is misleading compared to external/world knowledge (e.g., KBs).  
- Authors’ rebuttal did not address main concerns about baselines and extra parameters, so reviewer score unchanged.  
- Questions for authors: provide detailed baseline implementation/comparison, justify two RNNs and parameter counts, analyze parser-error sensitivity, explain choice of static attention vs. per-word dynamic attention, and provide evidence for generalization to other "knowledge" types.
