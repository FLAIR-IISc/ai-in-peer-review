- Introduces K-SAN, an end-to-end neural architecture that integrates structured linguistic knowledge into attention for sequence tagging (slot filling).
- Claims K-SAN generalizes across different knowledge types (e.g., dependency trees, AMR) without a fixed schema.
- Reports computational efficiency, robustness on small data, and state-of-the-art results on ATIS.

- Simplifies structured knowledge as an array of vectors in memory, avoiding complex feature engineering.
- Shows robustness to data scarcity, performing well with limited labeled data.
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance on ATIS and effective attention highlighting salient substructures.
- Adapts consistently across different knowledge representations (dependency, AMR).

- Lacks detailed baseline configurations, making improvements hard to assess; misses a feature-based external-knowledge baseline (e.g., word heads, NER).
- Uses two RNNs (chain-based and knowledge-guided), which may be redundant and increase computational cost without clear justification.
- Employs static attention over substructure embeddings instead of exploring potentially superior dynamic per-word attention.
- Does not evaluate sensitivity to parser/AMR generator errors, leaving real-world robustness unclear.
- Uses the term "knowledge" ambiguously to mean syntactic/semantic structures rather than external/world knowledge.

- Why was a feature-based baseline incorporating external knowledge omitted, and how would it compare to K-SAN?
- Please provide baseline model configurations (hyperparameters, training setup) to clarify reported gains.
- Have you evaluated sensitivity to parser/AMR errors, and how would such errors impact real-world performance?
- Why choose static attention over dynamic attention for substructure embeddings; would dynamic attention help?

- Recommendation: Reject due to limited novelty, experimental rigor, architectural redundancy, missing baselines, and lack of parser-error analysis, but encourage revisions addressing these issues.
