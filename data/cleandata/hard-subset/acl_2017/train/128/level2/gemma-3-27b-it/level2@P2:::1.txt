This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach to natural language understanding (NLU) for dialogue systems. The core idea is to augment recurrent neural networks with non-flat network topologies guided by prior knowledge, enabling the model to capture richer linguistic structure and improve performance, especially with limited training data. The authors claim three primary contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a critical challenge in NLU – effectively leveraging linguistic structure and prior knowledge. Traditional RNN-based approaches often treat utterances as flat sequences, neglecting valuable structural information. K-SAN directly addresses this limitation by incorporating external knowledge to guide the attention mechanism.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** The integration of knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention within a neural network is a compelling concept. The model doesn't simply *use* knowledge as additional features; it *learns* where and how much to attend to based on the knowledge provided. This is a significant departure from previous approaches that largely relied on feature engineering.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the ATIS dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of K-SAN, consistently outperforming baseline models, including strong structural models like Tree-RNN and DCNN. The performance gains, particularly with smaller training sets, support the claim that K-SAN benefits from knowledge-guided attention and generalizes well.
4. **Generalizability of Knowledge Source:** The demonstration that K-SAN can leverage both dependency trees and AMR graphs as knowledge sources strengthens the argument for the model's flexibility and broader applicability. This is a valuable aspect, as the availability and quality of different knowledge resources vary.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper demonstrates improvements over baselines, the ablation study is limited. It would be beneficial to understand the contribution of each component of K-SAN more thoroughly. Specifically, how much does the knowledge-guided attention contribute versus simply incorporating structural information? Is the specific form of the knowledge encoding (NN, RNN, CNN) critical, or are the results relatively insensitive to this choice?
2. **Dependence on Parser Accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN is inherently tied to the accuracy of the underlying parser (Stanford or SyntaxNet for dependency trees, or a rule-based or JAMR parser for AMR graphs). Although the paper demonstrates robustness, it doesn’t explicitly address the impact of parsing errors.  A brief analysis of how parsing errors affect performance would be valuable. The example in Figure 5 illustrates a case where the dependency parse is incorrect, but the model still benefits; further analysis of these scenarios would be informative.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but lacks detailed discussion about the sensitivity of the model to these parameters. Reporting a more comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter search and their influence on performance would strengthen the findings.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Recent advances in pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) have significantly improved NLU performance.  It would be beneficial to compare K-SAN against these stronger baselines to establish a clearer understanding of its competitive advantage.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study analyzing the contribution of different components of K-SAN (e.g., knowledge encoding method, attention mechanism)?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to errors in the underlying parser? Have you conducted any analysis to quantify the impact of parsing accuracy on the model's performance?
3.  What is the computational overhead introduced by the knowledge-guided attention mechanism compared to a standard RNN or CNN-based model?
4.  Have you experimented with incorporating pre-trained language model embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) into the K-SAN framework?  If so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach.
*   The visualization in Figure 5 is helpful for understanding the attention mechanism.
*   The exploration of different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) is a strength of the work.
*   The conclusion effectively summarizes the contributions and potential future directions.
