- Novel approach: guides attention using external knowledge within neural networks, enabling learning of salient sentence substructures end-to-end.  
- Generalizable: can incorporate different knowledge sources (dependency relations, AMR) without schema changes, making it adaptable across NLU tasks.  
- Data efficiency: shows improved semantic tagging performance on ATIS, especially with smaller training sets.  
- Clear presentation: well-written motivation, logical exposition, and useful visualizations of architecture and attention.  

- Limited evaluation: experiments are restricted to a single dataset/task (ATIS), limiting claims of broad effectiveness.  
- Parser dependency: model performance relies on the quality of the dependency parser/knowledge source; robustness to noisy parses is not fully explored.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: optimal encoding networks (NN/RNN/CNN) vary with dataset size, and tuning guidance is limited.  
- Subjective attention interpretation: attention visualizations lack quantitative analysis to validate that attended substructures are truly correct.  

- How sensitive is K-SAN to dependency parsing errors, and have mitigation strategies (e.g., ensemble parsing, uncertainty) been tested?  
- What was the hyperparameter tuning process and how were optimal settings chosen for different dataset sizes?  
- What is the computational cost of K-SAN versus baselines, including parsing and knowledge encoding overhead?  
- Do attention visualizations provide insights into why certain substructures are salient for specific semantic tags?  

- Suggest adding discussion of related graph neural network work in NLU.  
- Recommend an ablation study to quantify contributions of components (attention, knowledge encoding).  
- Noted good practice: use of statistical significance marker (†) for reported improvements.
