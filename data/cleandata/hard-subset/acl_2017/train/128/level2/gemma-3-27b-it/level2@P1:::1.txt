This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for natural language understanding, specifically slot filling in dialogue systems. The authors argue that existing RNN-based approaches treat utterances as flat structures, ignoring valuable linguistic information. K-SAN aims to address this by incorporating prior knowledge to guide attention mechanisms and extract salient substructures. The primary contribution is a novel neural network architecture that leverages external knowledge in an end-to-end fashion to improve semantic tagging performance. Secondary contributions include the demonstration of the model's generalization ability to different knowledge types and the inherent efficiency stemming from modeling substructures separately.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of directly incorporating knowledge as a guiding signal *within* the network, rather than as additional features, is a significant contribution. This moves beyond typical feature enrichment strategies and potentially avoids issues of mismatch and error propagation. The architecture's attempt to learn attention based on prior knowledge is a strong point.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the ATIS dataset demonstrate that K-SAN outperforms various baseline models, including strong structural approaches like Tree-RNN and DCNN. The reported performance gains, particularly on smaller datasets, suggest the model effectively leverages knowledge to improve generalization.
3. **Demonstrated Generalizability of Knowledge Source:** Showing that the model works with different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) is a significant strength. This highlights the flexibility of the architecture and suggests it’s not overly reliant on a specific knowledge representation. The results in Table 2 confirm this point effectively.
4. **Attention Visualization:** The visualization of attention weights (Figure 5) provides valuable insight into how the model leverages knowledge. It demonstrates that the model correctly focuses on relevant substructures for accurate slot filling, which strengthens the claim of improved interpretability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset & Task:** The evaluation is limited to the ATIS dataset, which is a relatively well-studied benchmark. The results may not generalize to more complex or diverse NLU tasks.  Exploring other datasets and tasks would strengthen the claims of broader applicability.
2. **Dependency on Parser Quality:** While the authors demonstrate generalization across knowledge *sources*, the performance still fundamentally relies on the quality of the initial parsing (dependency or AMR). Errors in the parsing will inevitably propagate into the K-SAN model. While the authors acknowledge this, a more robust analysis of the impact of parsing errors on the model's performance is needed.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but lacks detail on the process. The optimal hyperparameters may be highly specific to the ATIS dataset and may not transfer well to other domains. More discussion around hyperparameter sensitivity and potential strategies for automated tuning would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** A thorough ablation study would clarify the contribution of each component of K-SAN. For example, it would be helpful to know the performance improvement gained solely from the attention mechanism vs. the structural encoding. This would help pinpoint the most important aspects of the architecture.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the accuracy of the dependency parser (or AMR parser)? Can you provide a quantitative analysis of the impact of parsing errors on slot filling accuracy?
2.  What strategies did you employ for hyperparameter tuning, and how sensitive are the results to different hyperparameter settings?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of K-SAN compared to the baseline models? The claim of "efficiency and parallelizability" needs further justification, especially given the added complexity of knowledge encoding and attention mechanisms.
4.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the knowledge encoding networks (NN, RNN, CNN) are initialized and trained?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of relevant research.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations and future research directions. For example, exploring different types of knowledge sources beyond dependency trees and AMR graphs.
