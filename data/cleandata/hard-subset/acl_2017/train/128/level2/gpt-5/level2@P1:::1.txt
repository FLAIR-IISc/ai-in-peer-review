Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The key idea is to use external linguistic structures (e.g., dependency parses or AMR graphs) to generate substructures for an input utterance, embed those substructures, and learn an attention distribution over them conditioned on the utterance. The resulting knowledge-guided representation is combined with a standard RNN tagger (including a joint architecture that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided pathways). Experiments on ATIS show consistent improvements over CRF, RNN/CNN baselines and tree/structure-aware encoders, with larger gains in the small-data regime. The method is evaluated with different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet parses and rule-based/JAMR AMR), suggesting generality.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A task-oriented, end-to-end architecture that integrates external knowledge as attention over substructures rather than as fixed input features, and combines it with a chain RNN tagger for slot filling.
2) Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided structural attention improves performance (especially with limited data) and is robust to different knowledge sources (syntactic and semantic graphs).
3) Analysis via attention visualizations and encoder variants (NN/RNN/CNN) showing the flexibility of the framework.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and plausibility: Treating knowledge as a teacher that guides attention over substructures is a clear departure from feature-augmentation approaches. The architecture remains end-to-end differentiable and avoids hard reliance on entire (potentially noisy) parses.
- Solid empirical improvements with significance: On ATIS, K-SAN outperforms strong neural baselines and structure-aware encoders, with statistically significant gains. Improvements are most pronounced with small/medium training sets, supporting the generalization claim.
- Generalization across knowledge sources: The model works similarly with dependency trees from two parsers and AMR graphs from two parsers, despite different substructure counts. This strengthens the claim that K-SAN is knowledge-agnostic and robust to parser variation.
- Sensible engineering and ablations: The paper explores several encoders for Mkg/Min (NN, RNN, CNN), shows that different encoders are preferable at different data scales, and includes a joint tagger that balances chain and knowledge-guided signals.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The model is well described with equations and figures; training details (optimizer, learning rates, dropout, hidden sizes) are provided. The attention visualizations are informative and help interpret model behavior.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: Results are only on ATIS, a small, relatively clean benchmark. The paper’s claims about generalization and robustness would be substantially stronger with additional datasets (e.g., MIT Restaurant/Movie, SNIPS, or other domains) and/or ASR outputs to show robustness to noise typical in SDS.
- Missing modern baselines: The comparison omits strong sequence labeling baselines popular by 2016–2017, such as BiLSTM-CRF (with/without character embeddings). The gains over the current CNN/RNN baselines are modest (~1–2 F1) on the large set; showing improvements over BiLSTM-CRF is important to establish state of the art.
- Incomplete analysis/ablations: Key controls are missing: (a) using uniform attention over substructures; (b) removing the joint pathway (report separate results for chain-only, knowledge-only); (c) adding dependency/AMR relation labels to substructure representations; (d) analyzing sensitivity to parse noise (e.g., by perturbing parses or using lower-quality parsers) beyond a single anecdotal example. These would help isolate where the gains come from and how attention contributes.
- Efficiency claims unsubstantiated: The paper asserts efficiency/parallelizability and suggests time may not grow linearly with sentence length, but provides no runtime, memory, or complexity analysis. Since the number of substructures can be large (up to 53 for dependency), understanding the computational trade-offs matters, especially for deployment in SDS.
- Design choices for substructures: Constructing substructures as root-to-leaf paths while ignoring relation labels may yield unnatural token orders and potentially discard useful information. It would be helpful to justify this choice versus alternatives (e.g., including labeled edges, shortest dependency paths between salient tokens, or bag-of-edges representations), and to report the empirical effect of including labels.

Questions to the authors
- Can you report results against a BiLSTM-CRF baseline (with and without character embeddings)? This would contextualize the improvements relative to widely-used slot-filling models.
- How sensitive is performance to the balancing parameter α in the joint tagger? Did you tune α per data size, and can you provide a sensitivity plot?
- What is the average number of substructures per sentence for each knowledge source, and how does F1 vary with that number? Relatedly, what is the average attention entropy—does the model concentrate on a few substructures?
- Have you tried incorporating dependency/AMR relation labels into the substructure encoder? If so, what was the impact? If not, can you comment on feasibility and expected effects?
- Can you provide training/inference time per sentence for the different encoders and knowledge sources, and compare to chain-only models?
- How are root-to-leaf sequences linearized (original order vs. path order), and do you de-duplicate tokens across substructures? Any observed impact on performance?
- Do gains persist on noisy ASR transcripts? If not available, could you simulate noise to approximate real SDS conditions?

Additional comments
- The idea is timely and well-motivated; strengthening the empirical section with stronger baselines, more datasets, and ablations would make the contribution compelling.
- Minor: some references appear incomplete or inconsistently formatted; a pass to standardize citations would improve readability.
