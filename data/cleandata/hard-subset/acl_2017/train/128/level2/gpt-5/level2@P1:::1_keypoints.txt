- Proposes K-SAN: attention over linguistic substructures (dependency/AMR) combined with an RNN tagger for slot filling.  
- Substructures are embedded and attended to conditioned on the utterance; knowledge-guided representation fused with chain RNN pathway.  
- Evaluated on ATIS, showing consistent improvements over CRF/RNN/CNN and structure-aware encoders, with larger gains in low-data settings.  
- Demonstrates robustness across different knowledge sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers).  

Contributions:
- End-to-end architecture that integrates external knowledge as attention over substructures rather than fixed input features.  
- Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided structural attention improves slot-filling performance, especially with limited data, and is knowledge-source agnostic.  
- Analysis through attention visualizations and encoder variants (NN/RNN/CNN) showing framework flexibility.  

Strengths:
- Conceptually clear approach treating knowledge as a teacher that guides attention and avoids hard reliance on noisy parses.  
- Solid, statistically significant empirical improvements on ATIS, especially for small/medium training sets.  
- Generalization across knowledge sources strengthens claim of robustness to parser variation.  
- Thorough engineering and sensible ablations exploring multiple encoders and a joint tagger.  
- Clear presentation with equations, figures, training details, and informative attention visualizations.  

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation limited to ATIS; lacks additional datasets (e.g., MIT, SNIPS) or ASR/noisy inputs to demonstrate broader robustness.  
- Missing modern strong baselines such as BiLSTM-CRF (with/without char embeddings); reported gains over existing baselines are modest on large data.  
- Incomplete ablations/controls: uniform attention baseline, separate chain-only/knowledge-only results, inclusion of relation labels, and systematic parse-noise sensitivity are missing.  
- Efficiency/parallelizability claims lack runtime, memory, or complexity analysis despite potentially large numbers of substructures.  
- Substructure design (root-to-leaf paths without relation labels) may reorder tokens and drop information; justification and alternatives are not empirically explored.  

Questions for authors:
- Please report results against a BiLSTM-CRF baseline (with and without character embeddings).  
- How sensitive is performance to the balancing parameter α in the joint tagger; was α tuned per data size?  
- What is the average number of substructures per sentence per knowledge source, and how does F1 vary with that number; what is the average attention entropy?  
- Have you tried including dependency/AMR relation labels in substructure encoders; if so, what was the impact?  
- Provide training/inference time per sentence for different encoders and knowledge sources, compared to chain-only models.  
- How are root-to-leaf sequences linearized and are tokens de-duplicated across substructures; any observed impact on performance?  
- Do gains persist on noisy ASR transcripts or simulated noise for SDS conditions?  

Additional comments:
- Idea is timely and well-motivated; stronger empirical validation (more datasets, baselines, ablations) would make the contribution more compelling.  
- Minor: references need formatting consistency.
