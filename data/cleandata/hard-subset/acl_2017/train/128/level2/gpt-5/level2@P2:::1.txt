Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The key idea is to extract substructures from an external knowledge source (e.g., dependency or AMR parses), embed those substructures, and attend over them to build a knowledge-guided sentence representation that conditions an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a standard chain RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS show consistent gains over several baselines and prior structural encoders, with particularly strong improvements in low-data regimes, and robustness across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency, rule-based/JAMR AMR).

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A task-specific architectural instantiation of memory/attention over knowledge-guided substructures for sequence tagging, trained end-to-end and jointly with a standard tagger.
2) An empirical demonstration that knowledge-guided attention improves slot filling, especially with limited training data, and that the approach is robust to different knowledge sources and even noisy parses.
3) A compact analysis (visualizations) suggesting the attention mechanism focuses on semantically salient parts (e.g., origin/destination/time) relevant to tagging.

Strengths
- Clear, principled integration of external structure via attention. Casting parse-derived substructures as an addressable memory and deriving a sentence representation by attending to them is elegant and well-justified. The design (shared encoder for utterance/substructures, simple dot-product attention, residual combination with u) is easy to train end-to-end and avoids hand-crafted feature engineering.

- Consistent empirical gains, especially in low-resource settings. On ATIS, K-SAN improves over CRF, RNN, and CNN encoder baselines and over structural encoders (DCNN, Tree-RNN). The improvements are largest with 1/40 and 1/10 data, supporting the claim that knowledge guidance aids generalization. Statistical tests are reported.

- Robustness across knowledge sources/formats. Using two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers yields similar improvements, supporting the “no schema required” claim and suggesting the method tolerates parser noise and differences in structure density. Reporting the maximum number of substructures helps contextualize capacity.

- Qualitative interpretability via attention visualizations. The figures show the model attending to intuitively relevant words/relations (origins, destinations, temporal expressions), even when the dependency parse is partly wrong, lending credibility to the mechanism.

- A simple joint architecture that mitigates noisy knowledge. The joint tagger balances chain-based and knowledge-guided hidden states, which is sensible and may explain robustness to knowledge imperfections.

Weaknesses
- Limited and somewhat dated baselines for slot filling. The strongest baselines reported are single-direction RNN and CNN encoder-taggers; more competitive baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/GRU with CRF decoding, character-level features, and/or joint intent-slot modeling) were already common and typically achieve ≥95 F1 on ATIS. The best K-SAN result (≈95 F1) may not exceed such strong baselines; thus the “state-of-the-art” claim is not fully substantiated. Including those baselines or clarifying the scope (lexical-only) would strengthen the claims.

- Missing ablations to isolate what helps. It is unclear whether gains come from attention, from representing the sentence via any set of subspans, or specifically from knowledge-guided substructures. Useful controls: attention over random substructures, over surface n-grams/spans, uniform (non-attentive) pooling of substructures, and using dependency/AMR edge labels vs. ignoring them. Similarly, an ablation of the joint tagger vs. knowledge-guided alone would quantify each component.

- Efficiency/parallelizability claims are not evaluated. The paper claims that modeling substructures separately improves efficiency and parallelism, but there are no runtime, memory, or throughput measurements, nor complexity analysis vs. sentence length and substructure count. An empirical evaluation would be needed to support this contribution.

- Limited dataset scope. Results are only on ATIS. Demonstrating gains on additional slot-filling datasets (e.g., MIT Movies/Restaurant or other domains) would better support generalization claims beyond the robustness to knowledge sources.

- Some modeling and training details lack clarity. It is not fully specified whether word embeddings are fixed or fine-tuned; how many random seeds/runs are averaged for the reported numbers (important for t-tests); dimensionalities/projections for injecting o into the RNN (Mo vs. o notation); and how α was selected or whether the joint model is sensitive to α. Also, the decision to ignore dependency/AMR labels “for better generalization” is plausible but untested.

Questions to authors
- How does K-SAN compare to a strong BiLSTM-CRF baseline with the same lexical-only feature set and comparable parameter count? Does K-SAN still improve when added on top of BiLSTM-CRF?

- Can you provide ablations: (a) attention vs. uniform weighting of substructures, (b) knowledge-guided substructures vs. random spans vs. surface n-grams, and (c) with vs. without the joint combination (α=1 vs. α=0)? This would clarify the source of gains.

- Did you experiment with using dependency/AMR edge labels in substructure encoding? If so, what was the impact? If not, could you comment on expected trade-offs?

- Are the improvements stable across random seeds? How many runs underpin the t-tests? Please report mean±std.

- Can you quantify efficiency: average number of substructures per sentence for each knowledge source, training/inference time per epoch, and sensitivity to sentence length?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider evaluating on an additional domain to support the generalization narrative beyond ATIS.

- Clarify the exact mechanism for integrating o into the RNN (is Mo a learned projection of o? is o concatenated or added? dimensionality alignment).

- Release code and parsing/AMR extraction recipes if possible; the approach is practical and reproducible.

- Minor: clean up references formatting and ensure figures are legible (some labels and punctuation appear garbled).
