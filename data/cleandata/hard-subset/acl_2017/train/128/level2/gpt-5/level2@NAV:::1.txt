Summary:
The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The key idea is to encode substructures derived from external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees or AMR graphs) into a memory, attend over these substructures to form a knowledge-guided sentence representation, and integrate this representation into an RNN-GRU tagger. A joint tagger blends chain-based and knowledge-guided hidden states to mitigate parser noise. Experiments on ATIS (small/medium/full) show consistent F1 improvements over CRF, CNN, and tree-structured baselines, with the largest gains in low-data settings. The model is trained end-to-end, and attention visualizations demonstrate plausible focus on salient relations.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A substructure-level attention mechanism over knowledge-derived paths that yields a task-specific sentence representation for sequence tagging, trained end-to-end.
2) A joint RNN that balances chain and knowledge-guided signals to improve robustness to noisy structures.
3) Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided attention improves low-resource slot filling and generalizes across heterogeneous knowledge sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers).

Strengths:
- Methodological clarity and simplicity: the architecture is well-motivated, integrates knowledge via attention in an end-to-end fashion, and evaluates multiple encoders (NN/RNN/CNN).
- Strong empirical gains on ATIS, especially in low-resource regimes, with statistical significance reported; the joint model consistently outperforms strong baselines, including structural ones (DCNN, Tree-RNN).
- Robustness to knowledge source variation: comparable gains with different parsers and with syntactic vs. semantic graphs supports the claim of generalization.
- Useful qualitative analysis: attention visualizations align with intuitively salient slots and relations, supporting interpretability.

Weaknesses:
- Limited evaluation scope: only ATIS is used. Domain generalization beyond flight-booking remains untested.
- Baseline coverage: missing comparisons to BiLSTM(-CRF) sequence taggers and CRF decoding on top of RNNs, which are strong slot-filling baselines; current CRF baseline is relatively weak.
- Incomplete ablations: no study of attention vs. uniform pooling, effect of dropping the joint path (α), impact of using relation labels, or untied encoders; hard to isolate which components drive gains.
- Efficiency claim unsubstantiated: no runtime, memory, or parallelization results despite efficiency being a stated contribution.
- Sensitivity to parser noise is only qualitatively discussed; no controlled noise analysis or calibration to parsing accuracy.

Questions to authors:
- Can you add ablations for attention (uniform/soft gates), relation labels, α-sweep, and with/without joint tagger?
- How does K-SAN compare to BiLSTM-CRF and RNN-CRF baselines on ATIS?
- Please provide runtime and throughput vs. sentence length and number of substructures.
- How sensitive is performance to parsing errors? Can you report performance vs. parser accuracy or under synthetic noise?
- Will code and preprocessing (substructure extraction for AMR/dependency) be released?
