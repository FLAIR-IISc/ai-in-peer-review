Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. The core idea is to encode substructures derived from external linguistic or semantic resources (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs), attend over these substructures conditioned on the utterance representation, and inject the resulting knowledge-guided vector into a recurrent tagger. A joint tagger combines a chain-based RNN and a knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS demonstrate consistent gains over strong baselines across small, medium, and full training regimes, and show robustness to different knowledge sources.

As seen in this submission, the main contributions are:
1) A general attention-over-substructures framework that integrates prior linguistic/semantic structures into sequence tagging end-to-end, with knowledge treated as a guidance signal rather than as fixed features.
2) Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided attention improves slot filling, particularly in low-resource settings, and generalizes across heterogeneous knowledge sources (dependency parses from Stanford and SyntaxNet; AMR from rule-based and JAMR parsers).
3) A practical joint architecture that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided information, yielding state-of-the-art ATIS performance among compared systems.

Strengths
- Clear and general mechanism for using structure as “teacher”: The attention over substructure embeddings (pi = softmax(u^T mi); h = Σ pi mi; o = Mout(h + u)) offers a clean, differentiable way to prioritize salient parts of external structures. Unlike prior feature-augmentation approaches, the method is end-to-end and learns to align sentence representations with substructures dynamically. This design is simple yet general enough to accommodate both syntax and semantics.

- Strong empirical results, especially with scarce data: On ATIS, K-SAN variants significantly outperform non-knowledge neural baselines (Table 1, † marks, p < 0.05) and are competitive or better than structural encoders. Gains are largest in the Small and Medium regimes (e.g., K-SAN(CNN): 74.60 vs CNN 73.57 on Small; 87.99 vs 85.52 on Medium), supporting the claim that knowledge improves generalization when data are limited.

- Robustness across knowledge sources: Table 2 shows consistent performance across four distinct sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency parsers; rule-based/JAMR AMR), with similar F1 on all data sizes despite different maximum numbers of substructures. This suggests the approach is not brittle to parser choice or representation schema.

- Interpretable attention analyses: Figure 5 qualitatively demonstrates that the attention mechanism highlights origin/destination/time tokens and relations relevant for slot decisions, even when parser outputs contain errors. Similar attention patterns across training sizes indicate the model can learn salient focus with limited supervision.

- Practical joint design and training details: The joint tagger balancing chain-based and knowledge-guided paths (α = 0.5) is reasonable and likely contributes to robustness against noisy structures. Training specifics (optimizer, dropout ranges, embedding sizes) and hyperparameter tuning on dev are reported, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines relative to the 2017 tagging landscape: The evaluation does not include stronger contemporary sequence-tagging baselines such as BiLSTM or BiLSTM-CRF (e.g., Huang et al., 2015), which are widely used for NLU slot filling. The closest neural baseline is a unidirectional RNN and a CNN sentence encoder; the absence of BiLSTM(-CRF) makes it hard to assess absolute competitiveness.

- Insufficient ablations to isolate where gains come from: The paper ties Mkg and Min weights; injects o at each timestep via Mo; and uses a joint combination with a fixed α. Ablations that (a) untie Mkg/Min, (b) remove attention (uniform averaging), (c) vary or learn α, and (d) compare “o once per sequence” vs “o at every timestep” would clarify which elements are essential.

- Scope restricted to a single small dataset: All experiments are on ATIS. While ATIS is standard, it is narrow and relatively clean. Demonstrating effectiveness on an additional NLU dataset (e.g., SNIPS, or another SLU domain) or at least intent classification within ATIS would strengthen claims of generality.

- Claims on efficiency and parallelizability lack evidence: The text asserts potential efficiency benefits (“may not increase linearly”), but no runtime or memory measurements are provided. A brief complexity analysis or empirical timing would substantiate this point.

- Statistical testing and comparison scope: Significance is reported only relative to “all baseline models” (non-knowledge). It is not stated whether improvements over structural baselines (DCNN, Tree-RNN) are significant, nor are corrections for multiple comparisons discussed.

Questions to the authors
- How are AMR substructures constructed given reentrancies and DAG structure? Are only root-to-leaf simple paths used, and how are duplicates handled? Are AMR relation labels used or discarded (as with dependency labels)?

- Why are Mkg and Min weights tied? Was an untied variant evaluated, and if so, how did performance change?

- Is α fixed at 0.5 for all settings, or tuned on dev? Would learning α (or gating between paths) yield further gains?

- How sensitive is performance to the number and length of substructures? Is there any pruning or cap, and how does that interact with attention entropy?

- Could the approach be combined with a BiLSTM-CRF tagger, and if attempted, how does it compare to a strong BiLSTM-CRF baseline?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include a BiLSTM(-CRF) baseline and a K-SAN+BiLSTM(-CRF) variant to situate performance among widely adopted architectures.

- Add ablations for attention vs uniform averaging over substructures, untied encoders, and alternative injection points for o.

- Provide runtime or complexity analysis, and report average number of substructures and attention sparsity/entropy to illuminate efficiency and interpretability.

- Clarify whether dependency/AMR labels are ever used; if not, consider an experiment incorporating labels to quantify the value of labeled vs unlabeled structures.

- Expand qualitative analysis with error categories (e.g., time vs location slots) and examples where knowledge corrects a chain-based error.
