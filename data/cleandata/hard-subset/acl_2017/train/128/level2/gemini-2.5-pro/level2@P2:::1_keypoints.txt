- Proposes K-SAN: attention over knowledge-derived substructures (root-to-leaf paths) to produce a context vector fed to an RNN for slot filling, achieving SOTA on ATIS.
- Contribution: a novel, soft attention mechanism to integrate structured linguistic knowledge into RNN sequence tagging.
- Contribution: empirical evidence of robustness and generalization, especially in low-data settings and across different knowledge types (dependency vs. AMR).
- Strength: model design is well-motivated and sound, combining knowledge-guided attention with a joint sequential RNN to mitigate parsing errors.
- Strength: strong empirical results outperforming strong baselines (RNN, CNN, DCNN, Tree-RNN), with significant gains, notably in small/medium data regimes.
- Strength: thorough generalization analysis using four different parsers/knowledge sources, showing stable performance across them.
- Strength: clear, well-written presentation with helpful diagrams and transparent experimental setup.
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single, narrow dataset (ATIS); claims of generalizability would be stronger with additional, noisier or cross-domain datasets.
- Weakness: unclear motivation for defining substructures as root-to-leaf paths and lack of comparison/ablation with alternatives (edges, subtrees).
- Weakness: ambiguous design choice of tying encoder weights for substructures and full sentence (Mkg vs. Min) without justification or ablation.
- Question: why are the substructure encoder and full-sentence encoder weights tied, and what are results with untied weights?
- Question: why choose root-to-leaf paths as substructures instead of individual edges or constituent subtrees, and can you provide comparative/evidence?
