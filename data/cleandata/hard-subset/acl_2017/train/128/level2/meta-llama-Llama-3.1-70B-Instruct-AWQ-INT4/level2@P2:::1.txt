**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel model, Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), for natural language understanding (NLU) tasks. K-SAN leverages prior knowledge to incorporate non-flat topologies and learn suitable attention for different substructures that are salient for specific tasks. The model is evaluated on the benchmark ATIS dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **End-to-End Learning**: K-SAN is the first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion, where the model automatically learns important substructures with an attention mechanism.
2. **Generalization for Different Knowledge**: K-SAN can generalize to different types of knowledge, including dependency relations, knowledge graph-specific relations, and parsing output of handcrafted grammars.
3. **Efficiency and Parallelizability**: K-SAN can model substructures from the input utterance separately, allowing for efficient computation and parallelization.

**Strengths**

1. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: K-SAN achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ATIS dataset, outperforming existing models that leverage external knowledge.
2. **Robustness to Data Scarcity**: K-SAN demonstrates robustness to data scarcity, achieving significant improvements on small and medium-sized training datasets.
3. **Generalization to Different Knowledge**: K-SAN can generalize to different types of knowledge, including dependency relations and abstract meaning representation (AMR) graphs.
4. **Efficient Computation**: K-SAN can model substructures from the input utterance separately, allowing for efficient computation and parallelization.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates K-SAN on the ATIS dataset, which may not be representative of other NLU tasks or datasets.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study**: The paper does not provide an ablation study to evaluate the contribution of each component of the K-SAN model.
3. **Dependence on Pre-Trained Parser**: K-SAN relies on a pre-trained parser to generate dependency relations, which may not always be available or accurate.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does K-SAN handle out-of-vocabulary words or unseen substructures in the test data?
2. Can K-SAN be applied to other NLU tasks, such as intent detection or dialogue state tracking?
3. How does the choice of knowledge encoding model (e.g., NN, RNN, CNN) affect the performance of K-SAN?
