**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel model, Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), for natural language understanding (NLU) tasks. K-SAN leverages prior knowledge as guidance to incorporate non-flat topologies and learn suitable attention for different substructures that are salient for specific tasks. The model uses a knowledge encoding module to generate a linguistic structure for the input utterance, which is then used to guide the attention mechanism. The attention mechanism is used to select the most relevant substructures for predicting the semantic tags of the input sentence. The model is evaluated on the ATIS benchmark dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **End-to-End Learning**: The paper proposes an end-to-end learning approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion, where the model automatically learns important substructures with an attention mechanism.
2. **Generalization for Different Knowledge**: The paper shows that the proposed model can generalize to different types of knowledge, including dependency relations, knowledge graph-specific relations, and parsing output of handcrafted grammars.
3. **Efficiency and Parallelizability**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed model can be parallelized, making it more efficient and scalable.

**Strengths**

1. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ATIS benchmark dataset, outperforming existing models.
2. **Robustness to Data Scarcity**: The paper shows that the proposed model is robust to data scarcity and can perform well even with small amounts of training data.
3. **Generalization to Different Knowledge**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed model can generalize to different types of knowledge, making it a versatile approach for NLU tasks.
4. **Efficient and Parallelizable**: The paper shows that the proposed model can be parallelized, making it more efficient and scalable.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Complexity of the Model**: The proposed model is complex and requires a large number of parameters, which can make it difficult to train and optimize.
2. **Dependence on Prior Knowledge**: The paper relies heavily on prior knowledge, which may not always be available or accurate.
3. **Limited Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the proposed model on a single dataset (ATIS), which may not be representative of all NLU tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the proposed model handle out-of-vocabulary words or unseen substructures in the test data?
2. Can the proposed model be applied to other NLU tasks, such as intent detection or dialogue management?
3. How does the proposed model compare to other attention-based models, such as transformer-based models?
