**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel model, Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), for Natural Language Understanding (NLU) tasks. K-SAN leverages prior knowledge to incorporate non-flat topologies and learn suitable attention for different substructures that are salient for specific tasks. The model consists of a knowledge encoding module, a knowledge attention distribution module, and a sequence tagging module. The knowledge encoding module generates a set of vector representations for knowledge-guided substructures, which are then used to compute the attention distribution. The sequence tagging module uses the attention-weighted sum of the encoded knowledge embeddings to generate the output knowledge-guided representation.

**Main Contributions**

1. **End-to-end learning**: The paper proposes the first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion, where the model automatically learns important substructures with an attention mechanism.
2. **Generalization for different knowledge**: The model can incorporate different types of parsing results, such as dependency relations, knowledge graph-specific relations, and parsing output of handcrafted grammars, as knowledge guidance.
3. **Efficiency and parallelizability**: The model can be parallelized, and the substructures from the input utterance are modeled separately, which may not increase the modeling time linearly with respect to the number of words in the input sentence.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved performance**: The proposed model outperforms state-of-the-art models on the ATIS benchmark dataset, especially on small and medium-sized training datasets.
2. **Robustness to data scarcity**: The model is less sensitive to unseen data and can generalize well even with small training datasets.
3. **Flexibility in knowledge representation**: The model can incorporate different types of knowledge representations, such as dependency relations and Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR) graphs.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Complexity of the model**: The model has multiple components, which may make it difficult to train and optimize.
2. **Dependence on parsing quality**: The model relies on the quality of the parsing output, which may affect its performance.
3. **Limited interpretability**: The attention mechanism may not provide clear insights into the decision-making process of the model.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the model handle cases where the parsing output is incorrect or incomplete?
2. Can the model be extended to other NLU tasks, such as intent determination and dialogue management?
3. How does the model compare to other attention-based models in terms of performance and interpretability?
