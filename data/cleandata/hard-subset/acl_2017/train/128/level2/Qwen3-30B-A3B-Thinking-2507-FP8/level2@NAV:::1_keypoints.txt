- Proposal: K-SAN integrates external knowledge to guide attention toward salient sentence substructures for NLU, aiming to better capture linguistic structure, especially with limited data.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a flexible framework that uses external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention mechanisms.

- Contribution 2: Empirically shows improved generalization when training data is scarce.

- Contribution 3: Validates the approach across multiple knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs).

- Strength: Demonstrates meaningful improvement on the Small dataset (K-SAN (CNN) 74.60 F1 vs. 73.57 baseline).

- Strength: Attention visualizations show focus on relevant substructures (e.g., "origin", "destination") even with minimal training.

- Strength: Ablation study across knowledge sources (Table 2) indicates flexibility and robustness.

- Weakness: Novelty is weak—insufficiently distinguished from prior structural models like Tree-RNN and DCNN.

- Weakness: Modest gains on the full dataset (≈0.9% F1) with unclear practical significance.

- Weakness: Dependency parsing is used as a separate preprocessing step, contradicting the "end-to-end" claim.

- Weakness: Paper does not address potential error propagation from noisy dependency parses to the NLU model.

- Question: How does K-SAN specifically differ from Tree-RNN in architecture and learning?

- Question: What evidence shows the attention mechanism learns truly salient substructures rather than memorizing patterns?

- Question: How robust is the model to noisy or inaccurate dependency parsing results?
