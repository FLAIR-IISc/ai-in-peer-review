- Summary: Proposes K-SAN, an end-to-end NLU model that uses external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention to salient sentence substructures, generalizes across knowledge types, and models substructures separately for efficiency; evaluated on ATIS with gains, especially on small datasets.

- Strength: Novel end-to-end use of knowledge to guide attention rather than as extra features, reducing error-propagation from feature extraction.

- Strength: Demonstrates empirical improvements over baselines (notably on small data; e.g., Small: 74.60 F1 vs. 73.57 baseline), supporting better generalization with limited labels.

- Strength: Robustness and flexibility shown by consistent performance across different knowledge sources and parsers (dependency trees vs. AMR; Stanford vs. SyntaxNet).

- Strength: Attention visualizations provide intuitive evidence that the model can identify salient substructures from small training data.

- Weakness: No analysis of the model’s robustness to errors in external knowledge (e.g., dependency parsing mistakes) or frequency/impact of such errors.

- Weakness: Claims about efficiency and parallelizability lack empirical runtime measurements or comparisons to baselines.

- Weakness: Limited comparison with Tree-RNN; the paper lacks detailed analysis explaining why K-SAN outperforms structural baselines.

- Weakness: Unclear criteria for what makes a substructure "salient" beyond showing attention weights; lacks evidence linking attention to task-relevant semantics.

- Question: How does the model handle parsing errors shown in Figure 5, how often do they occur, and how does performance change with a more accurate parser (e.g., SyntaxNet)?

- Question: Can you provide specific attention-visualization examples demonstrating that identified substructures are semantically meaningful for slot filling?

- Question: Please provide runtime measurements comparing K-SAN to baseline models (particularly Tree-RNN) on the same hardware to substantiate efficiency claims.

- Question: How would K-SAN handle diverse knowledge sources with different structural properties (e.g., knowledge graphs with variable relation counts) in practice?
