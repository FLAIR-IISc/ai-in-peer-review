

Summary: The paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a model that extends RNNs to incorporate non-flat network topologies guided by external knowledge. The main contributions are: (1) an end-to-end approach that automatically learns salient substructures with attention mechanisms rather than using fixed linguistic features; (2) flexibility to work with various knowledge sources without requiring specific schemas; (3) improved generalization particularly on small datasets.

Strengths: The paper addresses a meaningful limitation in current NLU approaches by recognizing that natural language has structured information beyond flat sequences. The model's performance improvements on small datasets (with 1/40 training data) are significant (74.60 F1 vs. 73.57 for CNN baseline), demonstrating better generalization capabilities than existing approaches. The attention visualization (Figure 5) provides evidence that the model learns meaningful attention patterns to salient substructures even with limited training data. The model's ability to work with different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) shows robustness and generalization, with consistent performance across different knowledge resources.

Weaknesses: The paper's claim of "end-to-end learning" as a key contribution is not sufficiently differentiated from existing approaches that also use structural information end-to-end (e.g., Tree-RNN). The comparison with state-of-the-art models is limited to those published before 2015, missing more recent advances in NLU that incorporate structural information (e.g., recent Transformer-based models or more sophisticated dependency-based approaches). The paper does not adequately address how the model handles errors in the external knowledge source (e.g., when the dependency parser makes mistakes), which could be critical for real-world deployment. The experiments do not include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism versus the structural knowledge itself.

Questions to Authors: How does the model perform when the external knowledge source (dependency parser) makes errors, and what mechanisms does the model employ to mitigate such errors? What is the computational complexity of the model compared to standard RNNs, and how does it scale with input length? How does the model's performance compare to recent transformer-based architectures that incorporate structural information?
