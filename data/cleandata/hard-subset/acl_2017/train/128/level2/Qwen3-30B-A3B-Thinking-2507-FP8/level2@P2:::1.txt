

Summary: The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a model for natural language understanding that incorporates external knowledge structures (e.g., dependency trees, AMR graphs) through an attention mechanism. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization across different knowledge sources, and efficiency gains. However, the primary contribution appears to be the attention mechanism for integrating knowledge structures, with the "end-to-end" claim being misleading.

Strengths:
1. The model demonstrates significant improvements over baselines, especially on the Small dataset (74.60 F1 vs. 73.57 for CNN baseline), suggesting better generalization to limited training data. This is particularly valuable for real-world NLU applications where labeled data is scarce.
2. The attention visualization (Figure 5) compellingly shows the model learning to focus on relevant substructures (e.g., origin, destination, time), providing evidence for the claimed ability to identify salient linguistic structures.
3. The model's ability to work with different knowledge sources (dependency trees from Stanford and SyntaxNet, AMR graphs from rule-based and JAMR parsers) demonstrates practical flexibility, supporting the generalization claim.

Weaknesses:
1. The "end-to-end" claim is misleading. The dependency parsing step (using Stanford parser) is a separate, pre-trained component that's not jointly trained with the NLU model. This contradicts the authors' assertion that "knowledge plays the role of a teacher to guide networks where and how much to focus attention." The model is not truly end-to-end as claimed.

2. The efficiency advantage ("modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words") is unsupported. The paper provides no runtime analysis or comparison to baseline models, making it impossible to verify this claim. The architecture actually requires additional processing for knowledge extraction, which could increase computational cost.

3. The paper fails to adequately address error propagation from the dependency parser. Figure 5 shows incorrect parsing, yet the model still performs well. However, the authors don't demonstrate how robust the model is to parsing errors, which is a critical concern for real-world deployment where parsers aren't perfect.

4. The comparison with prior work that uses dependency structures (e.g., Tree-RNN, DCNN) is superficial. The authors claim previous approaches "highly depend on the quality of 'whole' sentence parsing," but don't clearly explain how K-SAN avoids this limitation. The paper doesn't provide evidence that K-SAN is more robust to parsing errors than previous methods.

Additional Comments: The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the relationship between knowledge structure quality and model performance. The results in Table 2 show similar performance across different knowledge sources, but it's unclear whether this is due to the model's robustness or the quality of the knowledge sources being comparable.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to incorporating linguistic structure in NLU, but the claims need to be more carefully qualified. The attention mechanism is interesting and well-validated through experiments, but the "end-to-end" and efficiency claims require substantial revision. The authors should clarify that the knowledge extraction step (dependency parsing) is not part of the end-to-end training process, and provide evidence for their efficiency claim through runtime analysis.
