

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach for natural language understanding that incorporates external knowledge to guide attention mechanisms toward salient substructures in sentences. The authors claim their model can better capture linguistic structures than traditional flat RNN approaches, especially with limited training data.

The main contributions I identify are: 1) a flexible framework that integrates external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention mechanisms for NLU tasks, 2) empirical demonstration of improved generalization with scarce training data, and 3) validation across multiple knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs).

Strengths: The paper makes a strong case for improved performance on the Small dataset (1/40 training data), where K-SAN (CNN) achieves 74.60 F1 compared to 73.57 for the best baseline. This demonstrates meaningful generalization capabilities for data-scarce scenarios. The attention visualization in Figure 5 compellingly shows the model focusing on relevant substructures like "origin" and "destination" even with minimal training. The ablation study across different knowledge sources (Table 2) effectively demonstrates the framework's flexibility and robustness.

Weaknesses: The novelty claim is weak—the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish K-SAN from prior work like Tree-RNN (Tai et al., 2015) and DCNN (Ma et al., 2015a), which also use structural information. The performance gains on the full dataset are modest (0.9% F1 improvement), and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this incremental improvement is significant for practical applications. The dependency parsing step is treated as a separate pre-processing step rather than being integrated into end-to-end learning as claimed, creating a disconnect between the "end-to-end" claim and actual implementation. The paper also fails to address potential error propagation from dependency parsing errors to the NLU model.

Questions to Authors: How does K-SAN specifically differ from Tree-RNN in terms of architectural design and learning process? What evidence supports the claim that K-SAN's attention mechanism learns "salient" substructures rather than simply memorizing training patterns? How would the model perform with noisy or inaccurate dependency parsing results?
