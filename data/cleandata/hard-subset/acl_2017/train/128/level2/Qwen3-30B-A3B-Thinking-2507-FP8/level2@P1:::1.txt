

Summary: This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel neural architecture for natural language understanding (NLU) that incorporates external knowledge to guide attention to salient substructures in sentences. The main contributions are: 1) an end-to-end learning framework where knowledge serves as a "teacher" to guide attention rather than as additional features, 2) the ability to generalize across different types of knowledge sources (dependency relations, knowledge graphs, etc.), and 3) improved efficiency through modeling substructures separately. The authors demonstrate K-SAN's effectiveness on the ATIS slot filling task, showing improvements especially on small training datasets.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful limitation in current NLU approaches: most prior work uses external knowledge as additional features rather than integrating it into the model's attention mechanism. K-SAN's end-to-end approach of using knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention is a novel and well-motivated contribution that avoids error propagation from feature extraction.

2. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines, particularly on small datasets (Small: 74.60 F1 vs. 73.57 for best baseline), which strongly supports the claim that the model generalizes better from limited training data. This is a crucial advantage for practical NLU systems where labeled data is scarce.

3. The thorough analysis of different knowledge sources (dependency trees vs. AMR graphs) and parsers (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet) shows the model's flexibility and robustness. The consistent performance across different knowledge representations is a strong point for the model's generalizability claim.

4. The attention visualization (Figure 5) effectively demonstrates how the model identifies salient substructures, providing intuitive evidence for the claimed mechanism of "learning correct attention from small training data."

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't adequately address how the model handles errors in the external knowledge source (dependency parsing). In Figure 5, the Stanford parser incorrectly parses the dependency relations, yet the model still performs well. It would be valuable to analyze how often the model's performance is negatively affected by parsing errors, and whether the model can recover from such errors.

2. The claim about "efficiency and parallelizability" is not substantiated with empirical evidence. The paper states that "modeling time may not increase linearly with respect to the number of words," but provides no runtime measurements or comparisons to baseline models to support this claim.

3. The comparison with Tree-RNN (a structural model) is limited. While K-SAN outperforms Tree-RNN on Small and Medium datasets, the paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why K-SAN performs better. The authors claim "the proposed model is able to pay correct attention to important substructures," but don't explain how K-SAN's attention mechanism differs from Tree-RNN's structure-based approach.

4. The paper doesn't clearly explain how the model identifies "salient" substructures beyond the attention mechanism. The paper states that "the model automatically figures out the salient substructures," but doesn't provide evidence or analysis of what makes certain substructures salient for specific tasks.

Questions to Authors:
1. In Figure 5, the dependency parsing is incorrect. Can you elaborate on how the model handles such parsing errors, and how often these errors occur in the dataset? How does the model's performance change when using a more accurate parser (e.g., SyntaxNet)?

2. The paper claims that K-SAN "automatically figures out the salient substructures." Could you provide specific examples from the attention visualization (Figure 5) that demonstrate how the model identifies substructures that are semantically meaningful for slot filling?

3. The computational efficiency claim is not supported by empirical evidence. Could you provide runtime measurements comparing K-SAN with baseline models (particularly Tree-RNN) on the same hardware?

4. The paper states that "different types of parsing results, such as dependency relations, knowledge graph-specific relations, and parsing output of handcrafted grammars, can serve as the knowledge guidance." Could you elaborate on how the model would handle knowledge sources with different structural properties (e.g., knowledge graphs with varying numbers of relations)?
