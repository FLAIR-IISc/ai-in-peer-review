- Summary: Proposes K-SAN, using attention over external knowledge structures for NLU; main novelty is the attention-based integration, and the "end-to-end" claim is misleading.
- Contribution claims: end-to-end knowledge guidance, generalization across knowledge sources, and efficiency gains — but only the attention mechanism is convincingly new.
- Strength 1: Significant F1 improvements over baselines, especially on small-data settings, indicating better generalization with limited labeled data.
- Strength 2: Attention visualizations show the model focuses on relevant substructures (e.g., origin, destination, time), supporting the interpretability claim.
- Strength 3: Works with multiple knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet parses, rule-based/JAMR AMR), demonstrating practical flexibility.
- Weakness 1: "End-to-end" claim is misleading because dependency parsing is a separate pre-trained step, not jointly learned with the model.
- Weakness 2: Efficiency claim is unsupported—no runtime analysis or comparisons, and knowledge extraction likely increases computational cost.
- Weakness 3: Paper does not address error propagation from parsers or demonstrate robustness to parsing errors despite showing incorrect parses.
- Weakness 4: Comparison to prior dependency-structure methods is superficial; no evidence K-SAN is more robust to parsing quality than earlier approaches.
- Additional: Needs nuanced discussion of how knowledge quality affects performance; similar results across sources leave unclear whether robustness or comparable source quality explains findings.
