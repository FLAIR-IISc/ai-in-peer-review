## Review of "Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks"

This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel RNN generalization designed to incorporate prior knowledge into natural language understanding (NLU) for dialogue systems. The core idea is to leverage external knowledge to guide attention mechanisms, allowing the model to capture salient substructures within sentences, particularly beneficial when training data is limited. The authors claim contributions in end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

The primary contribution of this work, in my assessment, lies in the exploration of integrating structural knowledge directly into the attention mechanism of an RNN, rather than simply using it as additional features. The experimental results on the ATIS dataset demonstrate a consistent performance improvement over strong baselines, particularly with smaller training sets, suggesting the model effectively leverages knowledge to overcome data scarcity. The ablation study with different knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) further strengthens this claim, showing robustness to the specific knowledge representation used.

The strengths of the paper include a clear motivation, a well-defined model architecture, and a thorough experimental evaluation. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison against relevant baselines, including both traditional methods and recent deep learning approaches. The visualization of attention weights (Figure 5) offers valuable insight into how the model utilizes the knowledge-guided structures.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The presentation of the model is somewhat dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the knowledge encoding module. While the authors mention the limitations of feature enrichment approaches, they don't fully explore *why* K-SAN avoids these issues beyond stating it learns attention automatically. A deeper analysis of the learned attention patterns and their correlation with linguistic properties would be valuable. Furthermore, the impact of different parsing qualities on the model's performance isn't adequately addressed. The reliance on the Stanford dependency parser, which can be prone to errors, is a potential concern.

Finally, the paper lacks a discussion of computational complexity. While the authors claim efficiency, a quantitative analysis of training and inference time compared to the baselines would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the choice of knowledge encoding network (NN, RNN, CNN) impacts performance and generalization?
2.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the dependency parsing or AMR graph? Have you experimented with noisy or imperfect knowledge sources?
3.  What is the computational overhead of K-SAN compared to the baseline RNN models, in terms of training and inference time?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written overall, but the formatting of some equations could be improved for readability. The references are comprehensive. Despite the weaknesses, the work presents a promising approach to incorporating knowledge into NLU systems and warrants further investigation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
