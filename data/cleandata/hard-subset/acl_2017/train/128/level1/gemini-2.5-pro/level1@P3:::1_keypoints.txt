- K-SAN augments RNN taggers with explicit parsed substructures and an attention mechanism to produce a knowledge-guided vector fed at each time step.
- Substructures are defined as root-to-leaf dependency paths; attention weights are computed by matching substructure embeddings to a global sentence embedding.
- Evaluated on ATIS, K-SAN outperforms baseline RNNs and prior structure-aware models, with larger gains in low-data settings.
- Main contribution: a novel, end-to-end method to integrate external symbolic structure into sequence tagging via attention over discrete substructures.
- Main contribution: empirical evidence of robustness to data scarcity and to choice of knowledge source (different parsers and formalisms).
- Strength: elegant formulation treating parsed substructures as an external memory queried by attention, reducing reliance on full tree encodings.
- Strength: strong empirical performance, especially on small and medium training sets, suggesting structural guidance acts as a regularizer.
- Strength: demonstrated flexibility across multiple parsers/formalisms and plausible learned attention patterns even with limited data.
- Weakness: choice of substructures (root-to-leaf paths) is arbitrary, potentially redundant, and not compared to alternatives like edges or subtrees.
- Weakness: experiments are limited to a single, simple, closed-domain dataset (ATIS), so generalization to other tasks/domains is uncertain.
- Weakness: attention is static/global (single vector o used at all timesteps), which may lack token-level, context-sensitive guidance.
- Question: why choose root-to-leaf paths over other decompositions, and were alternatives empirically tested?
- Question: did you consider dynamic per-token attention conditioned on RNN states, and what trade-offs do you foresee?
- Question: why do syntactic dependency trees and semantic AMR graphs yield similar gains—an ATIS artifact or limitation in how the model uses structure?
