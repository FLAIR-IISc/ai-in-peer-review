- Proposes K-SAN: a model that decomposes linguistic structures (e.g., dependency trees) into substructures and uses attention over them to guide an RNN tagger for slot filling.  
- Evaluated on ATIS, showing superior performance, especially in low-data settings.

- Contribution: a novel, flexible architecture for integrating structured knowledge via attention over decomposed substructures.  
- Contribution: thorough empirical validation demonstrating SOTA on ATIS and robustness across knowledge types and parsers (dependency vs AMR, Stanford vs SyntaxNet).

- Strength: Well-motivated and novel approach that bridges flat RNN processing and hierarchical linguistic structure using selective attention.  
- Strength: Impressive performance gains, notably on Small and Medium data subsets, indicating better generalization with limited data.  
- Strength: Demonstrated generalization across different knowledge sources and parsers, suggesting the method is not overfitted to a specific structure.  
- Strength: Clear presentation and helpful figures explaining the architecture and results.

- Weakness: Increased model complexity and dependence on external parsers, which may be costly or unavailable/less accurate in other languages/domains.  
- Weakness: Limited, mostly qualitative analysis of the attention mechanism (single example), lacking quantitative/error analyses on parsing mistakes.  
- Weakness: Experiments confined to ATIS, a simple closed-domain dataset, so generalization to longer, open-domain text is unclear.

- Question: Why fix the mixing weight α=0.5 for combining chain-based and knowledge-guided hidden states; was a learnable gate or dynamic weighting considered?  
- Question: Can authors provide systematic analysis of robustness to parsing errors and details of the incorrect parse shown in Figure 5?  
- Question: Why choose root-to-leaf paths as substructures; were alternatives (e.g., dependency edges, arbitrary node-to-node paths, entity-to-entity paths) considered?
