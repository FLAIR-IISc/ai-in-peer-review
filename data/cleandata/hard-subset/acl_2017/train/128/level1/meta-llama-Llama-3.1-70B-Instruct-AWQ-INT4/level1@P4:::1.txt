**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel model, Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), for natural language understanding (NLU) tasks. K-SAN is a generalization of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) that incorporates non-flat network topologies guided by prior knowledge. The model uses a knowledge encoding module to generate a linguistic structure for the input utterance, which is then used to guide the attention mechanism in the RNN. The authors claim that K-SAN can capture important substructures from small training data and generalize to previously unseen test data.

**Main Contributions**

1. **End-to-end learning**: The authors propose an end-to-end learning approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an RNN architecture.
2. **Generalization for different knowledge**: The model can accommodate different types of knowledge, including dependency relations, knowledge graph-specific relations, and parsing output of handcrafted grammars.
3. **Efficiency and parallelizability**: The model can be parallelized, and the substructures from the input utterance are modeled separately, which may not increase the modeling time linearly with respect to the number of words in the input sentence.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved performance**: The authors demonstrate that K-SAN outperforms state-of-the-art neural network-based frameworks on the ATIS benchmark dataset.
2. **Robustness to data scarcity**: The model shows better generalization and is less sensitive to unseen data, especially when the training data is scarce.
3. **Flexibility in knowledge representation**: The model can accommodate different types of knowledge, which makes it a versatile approach for NLU tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on knowledge quality**: The performance of K-SAN may depend on the quality of the knowledge used to guide the attention mechanism.
2. **Increased complexity**: The model introduces additional complexity compared to traditional RNN architectures, which may make it more challenging to train and optimize.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate K-SAN on the ATIS benchmark dataset, which may not be representative of all NLU tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of knowledge quality and its impact on the performance of K-SAN?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the training process and the optimization techniques used to train K-SAN?
3. How do the authors plan to extend K-SAN to other NLU tasks and datasets?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed model and its components. The experimental results are promising, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of K-SAN on the ATIS benchmark dataset. However, more evaluation and analysis are needed to fully understand the strengths and limitations of the proposed model.
