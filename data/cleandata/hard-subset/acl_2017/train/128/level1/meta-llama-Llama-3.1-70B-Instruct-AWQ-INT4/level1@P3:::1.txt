**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel model, Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), that leverages prior knowledge to incorporate non-flat topologies and learn suitable attention for different substructures that are salient for specific tasks. The model is designed for natural language understanding (NLU) tasks, specifically slot filling, and is evaluated on the ATIS benchmark dataset.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention**: The paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that leverages prior knowledge to guide the model's attention to salient substructures in the input sentence.
2. **End-to-End Learning**: The model is trained end-to-end, allowing it to learn the attention mechanism and the slot filling task simultaneously.
3. **Generalization to Different Knowledge Resources**: The paper demonstrates the model's ability to generalize to different knowledge resources, including dependency trees and Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR) graphs.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved Performance**: The proposed model outperforms state-of-the-art models on the ATIS benchmark dataset, especially on smaller training sets.
2. **Robustness to Data Scarcity**: The model's ability to leverage prior knowledge allows it to perform well even with limited training data.
3. **Flexibility**: The model can be applied to different knowledge resources, including dependency trees and AMR graphs.
4. **Interpretability**: The attention mechanism provides insights into which substructures are most relevant for the slot filling task.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Assumes Availability of Prior Knowledge**: The model relies on the availability of prior knowledge, which may not always be available or accurate.
2. **Requires Additional Preprocessing**: The model requires additional preprocessing steps, such as parsing the input sentence and generating knowledge-guided substructures.
3. **May Not Generalize to Other Tasks**: The model is specifically designed for slot filling tasks and may not generalize to other NLU tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the model handle cases where the prior knowledge is incomplete or inaccurate?
2. Can the model be applied to other NLU tasks, such as intent determination or dialogue management?
3. How does the model's performance compare to other state-of-the-art models on larger datasets?
4. Can the attention mechanism be used to provide insights into the model's decision-making process?
5. How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary words or unseen substructures?
