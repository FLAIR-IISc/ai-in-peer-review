Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance and a knowledge-derived structure (dependency tree or AMR graph), the method extracts substructures (root-to-leaf paths), embeds them, computes attention over substructures w.r.t. a sentence embedding, and forms a knowledge-guided sentence representation that conditions an RNN (GRU) tagger. A joint tagger interpolates a plain chain RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN. Experiments on ATIS show consistent F1 gains over several baselines, with stronger improvements in low-resource settings, and robustness to different parsers/knowledge sources. Visualizations suggest interpretable attention to salient substructures.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, general architecture that attends over knowledge-derived substructures and integrates the resulting sentence-level representation into a sequence tagger, plus a joint model that balances chain-based and knowledge-guided information.
2) Empirical evidence that knowledge-guided substructure attention improves slot filling, especially under data scarcity, and that it generalizes across syntactic (dependency) and semantic (AMR) knowledge and parser variants.
3) Analysis illustrating that attention focuses on semantically critical parts and remains helpful even with imperfect parses.

Assessment against ACL reviewing criteria
- Novelty: Moderate. While leveraging external structure and attention/memory mechanisms is known, attending over substructures extracted from knowledge to inform sequence tagging, and the joint tagger formulation, provide a reasonably new combination for NLU.
- Soundness/correctness: The model is well specified, differentiable, and trained end-to-end (w.r.t. neural components). The use of external parsers is a fixed pre-processing step. Claims about efficiency are not empirically supported.
- Significance: Improvements on ATIS (notably in low-resource regimes) are encouraging, but the scope is limited to a single, relatively small dataset and does not include the strongest contemporary baselines.
- Clarity: Generally clear; figures and equations are helpful. Some design choices (e.g., tied encoders, fixed α) need justification, and substructure construction details could be clearer.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: The architecture is easy to implement and agnostic to the knowledge format (dependency or AMR). It requires minimal changes to standard RNN taggers and should be portable to other sequence labeling tasks.
- Consistent empirical gains, especially in low-resource settings: K-SAN (CNN/RNN encoders) outperforms CRF/RNN/CNN baselines across Small/Medium/Large ATIS splits, with statistically significant improvements and the largest gains when training data is scarce. This supports the claim that knowledge-guided attention helps generalization.
- Robustness to noisy or alternative knowledge sources: Comparable performance is obtained with different dependency parsers (Stanford, SyntaxNet) and AMR parsers (rule-based, JAMR) despite large variation in the number of extracted substructures. This indicates the method is not brittle to parser noise or knowledge type.
- Interpretability: Attention visualizations align with intuitively relevant regions (origin/destination/time), including cases where the parse is imperfect, providing qualitative evidence that the model learns meaningful, task-relevant substructures.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation and baselines: Results are only on ATIS slot filling. Stronger and standard baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF sequence tagging, possibly with character features) are missing. Without these, it is hard to assess how competitive K-SAN is relative to the state of the art at the time.
- Global (sentence-level) knowledge vector for all positions: The knowledge-guided representation o is broadcast to every time step. This limits the ability to condition on token-specific context/substructures. A position-wise attention (token-conditioned) could be more appropriate for slot filling; the current design may underutilize the knowledge signal.
- “End-to-end” claim is overstated: The approach depends on external parsers (dependency/AMR) that are not learned jointly and can introduce pipeline errors. While the neural parts are trained end-to-end, the overall system is not trained end-to-end from text to tags with respect to the knowledge extraction.
- Insufficient ablations and sensitivity analyses: Key choices—tied weights for Mkg/Min, fixed α=0.5 in the joint tagger, excluding dependency labels—are not justified via ablations. It is unclear whether including edge labels or untied encoders would help, or how sensitive results are to α and the number/quality of substructures.
- Efficiency claim unsubstantiated: The paper claims parallelizability and non-linear scaling with sentence length, but provides no runtime/memory analysis, training speed, or complexity comparisons versus baselines or structural alternatives (e.g., Tree-LSTM/DCNN).

Questions to authors
- How does K-SAN compare to a strong BiLSTM-CRF baseline (with and without character embeddings) on ATIS under the same data splits? This is important for situating the contribution.
- Have you tried token-conditioned knowledge attention, i.e., computing attention over substructures per time step t (conditioning on wt or ht−1) rather than a single global o? If not, can you comment on feasibility and expected impact?
- Why tie Mkg and Min weights? Given that substructures and full sentences have different distributions, untied encoders could be beneficial. Please provide ablations.
- You drop dependency/AMR relation labels for “better generalization.” What is the empirical effect of including relation labels (e.g., via learned relation embeddings or edge-labeled compositional encoders)?
- The joint interpolation weight α is fixed to 0.5. Did you tune α or learn it (globally or per-sentence)? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- How many random seeds were run for each setting, and are the reported gains robust across seeds? Are the significance tests computed over multiple runs?

Overall, the paper presents a neat and practical idea with solid indications of benefit in low-resource slot filling and robustness across knowledge sources. Expanding the empirical scope and strengthening baselines/ablations would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
