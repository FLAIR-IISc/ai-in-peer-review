- Summary: K-SAN attends over knowledge-induced substructures (dependency/AMR paths) to produce a knowledge-guided representation injected into an RNN tagger; a joint tagger combines chain and knowledge signals and yields F1 gains on ATIS, especially in low-resource settings, and is robust across parsers and knowledge sources.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a simple end-to-end structural attention mechanism that attends over substructure embeddings and injects the aggregated representation into sequence tagging.

- Contribution 2: Empirically shows attention over knowledge-guided substructures improves slot filling and generalizes across heterogeneous external knowledge sources, with larger gains on scarce data.

- Contribution 3: Introduces a joint modeling scheme that combines chain-based and knowledge-guided signals to mitigate parser noise.

- Strength: Clear, well-specified architecture with end-to-end differentiability and straightforward implementation (various substructure encoders, inner-product attention, weighted aggregation).

- Strength: Consistent, statistically significant empirical gains over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines on ATIS, with largest relative improvements in Small/Medium training subsets.

- Strength: Robust results across different dependency parsers and AMR parsers; both syntactic and semantic structures provide benefits.

- Strength: Qualitative attention visualizations show the model attends to relevant substructures (origin/destination/time) and offers interpretability and some robustness to parse errors.

- Strength: Practical design choices (ignoring dependency labels, tying encoder weights) reduce parameter count and improve applicability across knowledge sources; easy to integrate with RNN-GRU taggers.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—approach is incremental relative to existing memory/attention models and syntax-aware encoders (dependency-CNN, Tree-LSTM).

- Weakness: Narrow evaluation—only ATIS used, omission of strong baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, BiLSTM+char embeddings), limiting claims about state-of-the-art and generality.

- Weakness: Incomplete ablations and sensitivity analyses—no controlled chain-only vs knowledge-only vs joint comparisons, α fixed at 0.5, tied vs untied encoders not explored, and label-ignoring decision unquantified.

- Weakness: Efficiency claims (parallelizability, modeling substructures separately) are unsubstantiated—no runtime, memory, or complexity analysis provided.

- Weakness: Limited analysis of parser quality impact—no quantitative correlation between parse accuracy and slot-filling performance and no exploration of trade-offs when ignoring relation labels.

- Question: Why are strong baselines like BiLSTM-CRF and BiLSTM with character embeddings not included, and can the authors compare to them on ATIS?

- Question: Please provide ablations: chain-only tagger with same hyperparameters, knowledge-only tagger (α=0), α sensitivity sweep (e.g., 0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0), and untied Min/Mkg results.

- Question: Did you try using dependency relation labels or AMR edge labels in substructure encoders, and how did that affect performance and generalization?

- Question: How are substructures extracted from AMR graphs (paths, shortest paths, fragments), how is redundancy avoided, and how is coverage ensured given AMR is not a tree?

- Question: What is the average number of substructures per sentence for each knowledge source on ATIS, and what is the additional runtime/memory overhead versus a chain-only tagger (batching/parallelization details)?

- Question: Did you explore alternative attention scoring functions (bilinear, MLP) or multi-hop attention, and what were the effects?

- Question: Would pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe) or character-level embeddings change the relative gains of K-SAN?
