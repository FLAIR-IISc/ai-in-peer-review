Summary and Claimed Contributions
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling. Given an utterance, the model extracts knowledge-guided substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf paths in a dependency tree or paths in an AMR graph), encodes each substructure and the whole sentence into vectors, computes attention over substructures via an inner product with the sentence vector, aggregates them, and feeds a knowledge-guided sentence representation o into an RNN tagger. A joint tagger combines a plain chain RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN (fixed α=0.5). Experiments on ATIS with varying training sizes show K-SAN improves over several baselines, with robustness across different knowledge sources (two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers) and larger gains in low-resource settings.

Contributions (as I see them), strongest first:
1) A simple, end-to-end architecture that uses external structure as “teacher” to drive attention over parse/graph-derived substructures for sequence tagging.
2) An empirical demonstration that the approach is relatively robust to data scarcity and to the choice/quality of structured knowledge (syntax vs. AMR; different parsers).
3) A joint tagging architecture that blends chain-based and knowledge-guided signals.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and flexibility: The model cleanly factors knowledge acquisition (substructure extraction), representation (Mkg/Min encoders: NN/RNN/CNN), and usage (attention + aggregation) in an end-to-end differentiable pipeline. Tying Min and Mkg is sensible, and the inner-product attention is computationally light.

- Robust improvements in low-resource regimes: On ATIS Small/Medium, K-SAN (CNN) yields sizable F1 gains over non-knowledge baselines and topology-aware encoders (DCNN/Tree-RNN). This supports the claim that externally guided substructure attention improves generalization when training data is scarce.

- Knowledge source generalization: Results are consistent across four knowledge providers (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependencies; rule-based/JAMR AMR), despite different substructure counts. This suggests the method is not brittle to a particular parser or formalism and can use syntax or semantics.

- Interpretability: Visualizations indicate attention aligns with semantically salient spans (origin, destination, temporal expressions) even when parses contain errors. This is valuable for debugging and for explaining decisions.

- Practicality: Because substructures are encoded independently and combined via a softmax-weighted sum, the approach is easily parallelized and should scale to longer sentences and alternative knowledge sources.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and dated baselines: The paper compares to CRF, RNN, CNN, DCNN, and Tree-RNN, but omits stronger contemporaneous baselines for ATIS slot filling (e.g., BiLSTM, BiLSTM-CRF, char/word-composed models). Reported large-set F1 (≈95) is unlikely to be state of the art by 2016–2017 standards; thus, the SOTA claim is not well supported.

- Limited evaluation scope: Only ATIS is used. The gains could be dataset-specific (short, template-like utterances). No evaluation on other NLU datasets (e.g., SNIPS or broader SLU corpora) or on related tagging tasks (NER, SRL) limits the generality of conclusions.

- Architectural choices underexplored: o is a single sentence-level vector injected identically at every timestep. This ignores position-sensitive cues that often matter for slot labeling. No experiment conditions attention on h_t (token-level context), and no CRF layer is used to model tag dependencies. The fixed α=0.5 is arbitrary; a learnable mixture or gating could help.

- Missing ablations and controls: It’s unclear how much of the gain comes from attention vs. simply adding parse-derived features. Needed: (i) uniform averaging over substructures; (ii) using full-tree encoding without attention; (iii) using labeled dependencies/AMR relations vs. unlabeled; (iv) untied Min/Mkg; (v) removing Mout; (vi) chain-only vs. knowledge-only vs. learned α. These would test the specific contribution of the attention and design choices.

- Efficiency claims unsubstantiated: While the design is amenable to parallelism, the paper provides no runtime/memory benchmarks, and substructure counts can be large (up to 53). Discussion of asymptotic complexity vs. sentence length and empirical throughput would strengthen the efficiency claim.

- AMR encoding details unclear: The mapping from AMR concepts/relations to token sequences is nontrivial; Figure 6 suggests a token-level projection, but the procedure, handling of concept-token mismatches, and use (or non-use) of relation labels are not specified. This weakens the semantic knowledge generalization story.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines: Can you add BiLSTM and BiLSTM-CRF baselines (with/without char features) on ATIS, and report whether K-SAN still improves when added on top of such stronger models?

- Token-level attention: Did you try conditioning the attention on the current hidden state h_t (i.e., per-timestep attention) rather than a single sentence-level u? If so, what are the results?

- Mixture weight α: What happens if α is learned (e.g., via a gate conditioned on u or h_t)? Any benefit over a fixed 0.5?

- Ablations: Please report ablations for (i) uniform substructure averaging; (ii) no Mout; (iii) untied Min/Mkg; (iv) using labeled dependencies/AMR relations. How sensitive is performance to these choices?

- AMR specifics: How are AMR concepts aligned to tokens? Do you collapse or anonymize concepts not directly tied to surface tokens? Did you explore using AMR relation labels?

- Model details: Is the tagger uni- or bi-directional GRU? What batch size, clipping, early stopping, and number of random seeds were used? Are results averaged with confidence intervals?

- Efficiency: Can you provide training/inference time and memory vs. sentence length and substructure count, and compare to CNN/Tree-RNN/DCNN?

Overall, the paper presents a neat, simple mechanism to inject external structure into sequence tagging via attention over substructures and shows promising gains in low-resource settings. However, stronger baselines, broader evaluation, and deeper ablations are needed to establish significance and clarify where the gains come from.
