Summary
The paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling in NLU. K-SAN encodes knowledge-induced substructures (e.g., root-to-leaf dependency paths or AMR path fragments) into a memory, computes an attention distribution over them conditioned on an utterance embedding, aggregates a knowledge-guided representation, and injects it into an RNN-based sequence tagger. A joint tagger combines a chain-based RNN and the knowledge-guided RNN to balance raw sequence and structural cues. Experiments on ATIS show consistent F1 gains over CRF/RNN/CNN baselines and over structural baselines (dependency-CNN and Tree-RNN), with larger improvements in low-resource settings. The approach appears robust across different knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet dependency parses; rule-based/JAMR AMR).

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, end-to-end structural attention mechanism that attends over knowledge-guided substructures and integrates the resulting representation into sequence tagging. The formulation is clear and practically implementable (inner-product attention, weighted aggregation, joint tagger).
2) Empirical demonstration that attention over knowledge-guided substructures improves slot filling, particularly with scarce training data, and generalizes across heterogeneous external knowledge sources (dependency, AMR, different parsers).
3) A joint modeling scheme that mitigates parser noise by combining chain-based and knowledge-guided signals.

Strengths
- Clear architectural formulation and end-to-end training: The model is well-specified, including how substructures are encoded (NN/RNN/CNN), attention is computed (softmax over u^T m_i), and the knowledge-guided representation is injected into the RNN tagger. The joint tagger is a straightforward extension that preserves differentiability throughout, facilitating end-to-end learning without feature engineering.

- Consistent empirical gains with statistical significance, especially in low-resource setting: On ATIS, K-SAN variants improve over CRF, simple RNN, and CNN encoder-taggers, with statistically significant gains (p<0.05) and the largest relative improvements on the Small and Medium training subsets. This supports the claim that knowledge-guided substructures help generalize from limited data.

- Robustness across knowledge sources and parsers: Results are similar across different dependency parsers and AMR parsers, and both syntactic (dependency) and semantic (AMR) structures confer benefits. This supports the generality claim and reduces concerns that improvements hinge on a single parser or schema.

- Qualitative analysis of attention: Visualizations indicate the model learns to attend to substructures relevant to slots (e.g., origin/destination/time phrases), and appears somewhat robust to parsing errors. This is aligned with the intended mechanism and provides interpretability.

- Practicality: The approach does not rely on dependency labels (improving applicability across knowledge sources) and ties encoder weights for substructures and utterance, reducing parameters. The method is simple to integrate with standard RNN-GRU taggers.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing attention/memory models and syntax-aware encoders: The core idea—attention over stored substructure embeddings conditioned on an utterance—is close to memory networks and attention mechanisms, and prior work integrates syntactic structures into neural encoders (dependency-CNN, Tree-LSTM). The main novelty is applying substructure-level attention to slot tagging with joint chaining, which, while useful, is incremental.

- Narrow evaluation on a single, saturated dataset: ATIS is small and well-studied; many recent strong baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, BiLSTM with character embeddings, attention-enhanced BiLSTMs) are missing. Claims of state-of-the-art are constrained by the baseline set and dataset choice. No cross-domain or larger benchmarks limit conclusions about generality and scalability.

- Incomplete ablations and sensitivity analyses: Results are reported primarily for the joint tagger; there is no direct ablation comparing (i) chain-only vs (ii) knowledge-only vs (iii) joint within the same architecture and hyperparameters. The effect of α is fixed (0.5) without sensitivity study. The decision to tie Min and Mkg is not contrasted with untied encoders. The impact of ignoring dependency labels is not quantified.

- Efficiency claims not substantiated: The paper claims potential efficiency and parallelizability because substructures are modeled separately, but provides no runtime/memory analysis, complexity discussion, or wall-clock comparisons. In practice, attending over many substructures could add overhead.

- Limited analysis of parser quality and error robustness: While a visualization shows robustness to a parsing error, there is no quantitative correlation between parse accuracy/quality and slot-filling performance. The choice to ignore relation labels for generalization is reasonable, but may discard informative cues; this trade-off is not empirically explored.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines: Why are BiLSTM-CRF and BiLSTM with character embeddings not included? Can you compare to a strong BiLSTM-CRF baseline on ATIS to contextualize gains?

- Ablations: Please report (i) chain-only tagger with the same architecture/hyperparameters as h1, (ii) knowledge-only tagger (h2 with α=0), and (iii) varying α (e.g., 0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0). Also, do untied Min/Mkg change performance?

- Knowledge labels: Did you try using dependency relation labels or AMR edge labels in the substructure encoder? If so, how does it affect performance and generalization?

- Substructure construction for AMR: How exactly are substructures extracted from AMR graphs (which are not trees)? Are they root-to-leaf simple paths, shortest paths, or another scheme? How do you avoid redundancy and ensure coverage?

- Efficiency: What is the average number of substructures per sentence for each knowledge source on ATIS, and what is the additional runtime/memory overhead relative to the chain-only tagger? Any batching or parallelization details?

- Attention mechanism variants: Did you explore alternative attention scoring functions (e.g., bilinear, MLP attention) or multi-hop attention? If tried, what were the effects?

- External embeddings: Would pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe) or character-level embeddings change the relative gains of K-SAN?

Overall, the paper presents a clear and useful application of structural attention to slot filling with encouraging results, but the novelty is moderate and the evaluation scope is limited. Stronger baselines, broader datasets, and deeper ablations would substantially strengthen the empirical case.
