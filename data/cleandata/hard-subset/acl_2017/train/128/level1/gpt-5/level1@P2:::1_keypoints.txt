- Proposes K-SAN: attend over knowledge-derived substructures (dependency/AMR paths) to form a sentence vector that conditions an RNN slot tagger, with a joint interpolation of chain and knowledge-guided RNNs.  
- Extracts root-to-leaf substructures, embeds them, computes attention versus sentence embedding, and uses the result to improve tagging.  
- Contribution: a simple, general architecture for attending over knowledge substructures and integrating them into sequence tagging, plus a joint model balancing chain and knowledge signals.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence of consistent F1 gains on ATIS, especially in low-resource regimes, and robustness across different parsers and knowledge types.  
- Contribution: analysis showing attention focuses on semantically relevant substructures and remains meaningful with imperfect parses.  
- Novelty: moderate — combines known attention/memory ideas with substructure attention and a joint tagger in a reasonably new way for NLU.  
- Soundness: model is well specified and end-to-end for neural parts, but reliance on external parsers is a preprocessing pipeline step.  
- Significance: limited by evaluation on a single, small dataset (ATIS) and absence of the strongest contemporary baselines.  
- Clarity: generally clear with helpful figures/equations, though some design choices and substructure details need more explanation.  
- Strength: conceptually simple, easy to implement, and agnostic to knowledge format, requiring minimal changes to standard RNN taggers.  
- Strength: consistent empirical improvements across dataset sizes, with largest gains under data scarcity, supporting better generalization.  
- Strength: robust to parser/noise variation — similar performance with different dependency and AMR parsers despite varying substructure counts.  
- Strength: interpretable attention visualizations that align with task-relevant regions (e.g., origin/destination/time).  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to ATIS and missing strong baselines like BiLSTM-CRF (with character features), making competitiveness unclear.  
- Weakness: uses a single global knowledge vector broadcast to all time steps, potentially underutilizing token-specific context; token-conditioned attention might be better.  
- Weakness: “end-to-end” claim overstated because external parsers are not learned jointly and can introduce pipeline errors.  
- Weakness: insufficient ablations/sensitivity analyses for tied encoders, fixed α, omission of relation labels, and number/quality of substructures.  
- Weakness: efficiency/parallelizability claims lack empirical runtime or complexity comparisons to baselines or structural alternatives.  
- Question: How does K-SAN compare to a strong BiLSTM-CRF baseline (with/without char embeddings) on the same ATIS splits?  
- Question: Have you tried token-conditioned attention over substructures per time step, and what is its feasibility/impact?  
- Question: Why tie Mkg and Min weights — any ablations comparing tied vs. untied encoders?  
- Question: What is the effect of including dependency/AMR relation labels (e.g., via relation embeddings)?  
- Question: Was the joint interpolation weight α tuned or learned, and how sensitive are results to its value?  
- Question: How many random seeds were used, are gains robust across seeds, and are significance tests computed over multiple runs?  
- Overall: neat, practical idea with promising low-resource and robustness results, but acceptance case would be strengthened by broader evaluation, stronger baselines, and more ablations.
