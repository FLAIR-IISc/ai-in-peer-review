Summary and contributions:
This paper proposes Knowledge-guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for slot filling, which augment an RNN tagger with an attention-weighted summary over substructures extracted from external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees or AMR). On ATIS, K-SAN improves F1, especially in low-resource regimes, and appears robust to noisy parses.

Main contributions as I see them (ordered by strength):
1) Empirical finding that attending over knowledge-guided substructures yields consistent, statistically significant gains on slot filling, with strong improvements in low-data settings and state-of-the-art results on full ATIS.
2) A general, end-to-end architecture that integrates external structures via a memory/attention mechanism and supports multiple encoders (NN/RNN/CNN) and knowledge sources.
3) Demonstration of knowledge-source agnosticism: similar gains using different parsers and both syntactic (dependency) and semantic (AMR) structures.

Strengths:
- Solid empirical improvements with significance testing. K-SAN(CNN) improves over the best non-knowledge baseline (CNN encoder-tagger) across Small/Medium/Large splits (e.g., 74.60 vs 73.57; 87.99 vs 85.52; 94.86–95.00 vs 93.88), and most K-SAN variants outperform structural baselines (DCNN, Tree-RNN).
- Robustness and generalization of the approach. Table 2 shows comparable gains across two dependency parsers and two AMR parsers despite varying numbers of substructures; Figure 5 suggests attention focuses on salient spans even with incorrect parses.
- Clear, interpretable mechanism. The attention over substructures yields intuitive visualizations linking salient syntactic/semantic spans to slot decisions, aiding interpretability.
- Thoughtful comparisons and variants. The paper explores multiple encoders for the memory, a joint tagger combining chain-based and knowledge-guided signals, and includes t-tests.

Weaknesses:
- Limited evaluation scope. Results are only on ATIS slot filling; no cross-domain datasets, intent classification, or ASR transcripts. Generalization claims remain tentative.
- Missing strong modern baselines. No BiLSTM-CRF (with/without character embeddings) or CRF decoding on top of the RNN; comparisons to stronger sequence taggers are needed to isolate the benefit of knowledge vs capacity.
- Insufficient ablations and analysis. The paper lacks “no-attention” (uniform averaging) and “no-knowledge” ablations within the same architecture, and does not quantify sensitivity to substructure design (root-to-leaf choice) or to using relation labels.
- Unsubstantiated efficiency claim. Parallelizability and training/runtime benefits are asserted but not measured; no complexity or wall-clock analysis is provided. α is fixed (0.5) without tuning evidence.

Questions to authors (optional):
- Can you report BiLSTM-CRF and/or CRF-decoder baselines and K-SAN+CRF?
- What is the effect of removing attention (uniform weights) and of removing knowledge entirely?
- Do relation labels help when available?
- Please provide runtime/memory comparisons and sensitivity to parser quality (e.g., correlation of F1 with parse accuracy).
