

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel model for natural language understanding (NLU) that incorporates external knowledge (e.g., dependency trees or semantic graphs) to guide attention to salient substructures within sentences. The authors claim K-SAN automatically learns important substructures with attention mechanisms, generalizes better to unseen data with small training sets, and works with various knowledge representations.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1. **A novel structural attention mechanism** that uses external knowledge to guide attention to salient substructures for sequence tagging tasks, representing a significant technical advancement over previous approaches that simply treated knowledge as additional features.
2. **Demonstrated generalization capability** with small training data, showing significant improvements on the smallest dataset (74.60 F1 vs. 73.57 for the best baseline), which is particularly valuable for real-world NLU applications where labeled data is scarce.
3. **Robustness to different knowledge representations**, showing consistent performance across dependency trees and semantic graphs (AMR), demonstrating the model's flexibility and generalization capability.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling empirical evidence for data-scarce scenarios**: The most significant strength is the clear demonstration that K-SAN excels when training data is limited. On the smallest dataset (1/40 of training data), K-SAN (CNN) achieves 74.60 F1 compared to 73.57 for the best baseline. This is crucial for real-world NLU applications where labeled data is expensive to obtain. The paper effectively shows that the model learns meaningful patterns from minimal data, as evidenced by the attention visualization (Figure 5) showing consistent attention patterns across dataset sizes.

2. **Strong visualization of attention mechanisms**: The attention visualization (Figure 5) is exceptionally well-executed and provides compelling evidence that the model is learning to focus on meaningful substructures. The model correctly identifies salient elements like "origin," "destination," and "time" even with limited training data, directly supporting the claim that knowledge guides attention to semantically relevant parts.

3. **Robustness to different knowledge sources**: The paper's demonstration that K-SAN works equally well with dependency trees (Stanford parser) and semantic graphs (AMR) is impressive. Table 2 shows consistent performance across different knowledge representations, suggesting the core mechanism is not dependent on a specific type of knowledge. This generalization capability is a significant contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of analysis on knowledge quality dependence**: The paper claims robustness to parsing errors (Figure 5 shows incorrect dependency parsing), but doesn't provide evidence of how the model performs with different quality knowledge sources. The authors should compare performance with high-quality parsers versus lower-quality ones to determine how much the model relies on the external knowledge's quality. This is critical for understanding real-world applicability.

2. **Unsubstantiated efficiency claim**: The paper claims K-SAN is "efficient and parallelizable" with "modeling time not increasing linearly with sentence length," but provides no empirical evidence or analysis of computational complexity. Without this, the claim remains unsupported and weakens the paper's contribution. The authors should provide runtime comparisons with baseline models.

3. **Insufficient differentiation from prior work**: The paper states that previous approaches "utilize linguistic knowledge as additional features," but doesn't clearly explain how K-SAN's end-to-end learning differs in practice from simply using knowledge features. The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why the structural attention approach is superior to feature-based approaches beyond the numerical results. A more detailed comparison of the model architectures would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper shows robustness to parsing errors, but how does K-SAN perform when using a high-quality dependency parser (e.g., a neural parser with higher accuracy)? Would performance improve significantly, or does the model's robustness to errors make this less important?

2. Could you provide more details about the computational complexity analysis? Specifically, how does the training and inference time scale with sentence length compared to standard RNNs, and what is the actual memory usage difference?

3. The paper claims K-SAN avoids error propagation issues, but doesn't provide evidence. Could you compare the error rates in the dependency parsing step versus the final slot tagging performance between K-SAN and a baseline that uses dependency features as input? This would help validate the claim about avoiding error propagation.
