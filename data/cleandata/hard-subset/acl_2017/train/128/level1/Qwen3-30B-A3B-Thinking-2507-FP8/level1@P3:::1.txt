

# Peer Review: Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks

## Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a neural network architecture for Natural Language Understanding (NLU) that incorporates external linguistic knowledge (e.g., dependency trees, semantic graphs) to guide attention mechanisms for slot filling. The model learns to identify salient substructures from input sentences based on prior knowledge, with the goal of improving performance on NLU tasks, particularly with limited training data. The authors claim their approach is the first to use knowledge as a "teacher" in an end-to-end manner for NLU.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Knowledge-guided attention mechanism** - The core contribution is a method to incorporate external linguistic knowledge to guide attention to salient substructures during NLU.
2. **Robustness with limited data** - Demonstrated significant improvements on small training datasets, addressing a key challenge in NLU.
3. **Knowledge generalization** - The model works effectively across different types of knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring specific schema.

## Strengths
1. **Addresses a meaningful problem**: The paper correctly identifies a critical limitation of current NLU approaches—RNNs treating sentences as flat sequences while ignoring rich linguistic structures. The authors provide a clear motivation for why structural information matters for slot filling.

2. **Strong empirical results with small data**: The most compelling contribution is the demonstrated robustness with limited training data (Table 1). K-SAN achieves significantly better performance (up to 7.2% absolute improvement) on the Small dataset compared to all baselines, which is crucial for real-world NLU systems where labeled data is scarce.

3. **Generalization across knowledge sources**: The model's consistent performance across different knowledge representations (dependency trees, AMR graphs) in Table 2 demonstrates a valuable flexibility that the authors claim but don't sufficiently explain in relation to prior work.

4. **Insightful attention visualization**: Figure 5 provides valuable evidence that K-SAN learns meaningful attention patterns that align with semantic slot boundaries, supporting the authors' claims about the model's ability to focus on salient substructures.

## Weaknesses
1. **Questionable novelty**: The core mechanism—using external knowledge to guide attention—is not novel. Memory networks (Weston et al., 2015) and dynamic memory networks (Xiong et al., 2016) already use similar mechanisms for question answering. The paper fails to adequately differentiate K-SAN from these approaches, particularly in terms of why this approach is specifically better suited for NLU than for QA.

2. **Inadequate handling of knowledge source errors**: The paper claims to "avoid error propagation" but uses the Stanford parser which has known errors (as shown in Figure 5). The authors provide no analysis of how the model handles these errors or ablation studies quantifying their impact on performance. The example in Figure 5 demonstrates the parser making errors, yet the model still performs well, which contradicts their error propagation claim.

3. **Misleading efficiency claim**: The paper claims "efficiency and parallelizability" as a contribution, but provides no evidence or analysis. The computational complexity isn't discussed, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that the substructure modeling leads to faster training or inference compared to baselines.

4. **Limited comparison with relevant prior work**: The paper compares with Tree-RNN (Tai et al., 2015) but doesn't explain why K-SAN is better than Tree-RNN, which also uses tree structures. The authors claim K-SAN "learns robust representations of whole sentences" but don't provide evidence that this is superior to Tree-RNN's approach.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does K-SAN specifically differ from memory networks (Weston et al., 2015; Xiong et al., 2016) in terms of architecture and mechanism, given that both use external knowledge to guide attention? What makes your approach particularly suited for NLU rather than QA?

2. The paper claims to avoid error propagation from knowledge sources, but Figure 5 shows the dependency parser making errors. How does the model handle these errors, and what ablation studies have been conducted to quantify the impact of parser errors on performance?

3. The authors state that "non-leaf nodes do not have corresponding substructure" to reduce duplicated information. Could you clarify why this design choice was made, and how it affects the model's ability to capture structural information compared to approaches that might include all nodes?

4. The paper claims "efficiency and parallelizability" as a contribution but provides no evidence. Could you provide computational complexity analysis or runtime comparisons with baseline models to support this claim?

## Overall Assessment
K-SAN addresses a meaningful problem in NLU with a compelling empirical demonstration of robustness with limited data. However, the novelty claim is questionable, and the paper fails to adequately address key limitations in their approach. While the results are promising, the contribution would be stronger with clearer differentiation from prior work, analysis of knowledge source errors, and evidence supporting their efficiency claims. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison with Tree-RNN and other structural NLU approaches.
