- Proposal: Introduces K-SAN, a neural NLU architecture that uses prior structured knowledge to guide attention over salient sentence substructures.  
- Contribution 1: Practical framework for incorporating structured knowledge into sequence tagging via attention to substructures.  
- Contribution 2: Empirical evidence that K-SAN improves generalization, especially with limited training data.  
- Contribution 3: Framework is robust to different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR) without requiring specific schemas.  
- Strength 1: Advances beyond feature-enrichment by learning attention from knowledge, avoiding some error-propagation issues and outperforming structural baselines.  
- Strength 2: Strong validation that the model focuses on critical substructures and benefits most on small datasets.  
- Strength 3: Demonstrated flexibility across multiple parsers and knowledge representations.  
- Weakness 1: Core mechanism (attention over substructures) lacks clear novelty relative to prior memory/attention models; “end-to-end” claim is misleading because parsers are external.  
- Weakness 2: Insufficient analysis of how parser/knowledge-source errors affect model performance; error frequency and impact are not quantified.  
- Weakness 3: Efficiency and parallelizability claims are unsubstantiated by runtime or scaling experiments.  
- Question 1: How can the model be made fully end-to-end by integrating knowledge extraction into training?  
- Question 2: Can the authors provide systematic analysis of model robustness to error-prone knowledge sources?  
- Question 3: What specific computational savings (training/inference time, scaling behavior) support the efficiency claim?  
- Overall: Promising, flexible approach with strong empirical gains (esp. for small data), but novelty, robustness to parser errors, and efficiency claims need clearer evidence; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
