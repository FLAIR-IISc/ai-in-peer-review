- Proposes K-SAN, a neural NLU model that uses external linguistic knowledge to guide attention for slot filling, aiming to learn salient sentence substructures end-to-end.

- Core contribution: a knowledge-guided attention mechanism that incorporates dependency trees/semantic graphs into NLU.

- Demonstrates robustness with limited training data, showing up to ~7.2% absolute improvement on small datasets.

- Shows generalization across different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR) without requiring specific schemas.

- Provides attention visualizations indicating the model learns meaningful substructure-focused attention aligned with semantic slots.

- Novelty is questionable: similar ideas exist in memory networks and dynamic memory networks used for QA.

- Does not analyze or quantify impact of errors from knowledge sources (e.g., parser mistakes), despite claiming to avoid error propagation.

- Claims efficiency and parallelizability but provides no computational complexity analysis or runtime evidence.

- Limited comparison to relevant prior work (e.g., Tree-RNN); lacks clear evidence that K-SAN outperforms or differs substantially.

- Asks authors to clarify architectural differences from memory networks and why K-SAN is particularly suited for NLU vs QA.

- Requests analysis/ablations on handling of parser/knowledge-source errors and their effect on performance.

- Asks justification for excluding non-leaf nodes/substructures and how that design choice impacts structural representation.

- Requests computational complexity or runtime comparisons to support the efficiency and parallelizability claim.

- Overall: promising empirical gains on low-data NLU but weakened by unclear novelty, insufficient error analysis, and missing efficiency/prior-work comparisons.
