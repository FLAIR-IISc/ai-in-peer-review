

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel neural network architecture for natural language understanding (NLU) that incorporates external knowledge to guide attention over non-flat sentence structures. The model uses prior knowledge (e.g., dependency relations or semantic graphs) to identify salient substructures in input sentences, then learns to focus attention on these substructures to improve semantic slot tagging. The authors claim that K-SAN generalizes better to unseen data, especially with limited training data, and outperforms state-of-the-art neural network-based frameworks on the ATIS benchmark dataset.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel neural network architecture that automatically learns important substructures from external knowledge using an attention mechanism, rather than treating input sequences as flat structures.
2. Demonstrated generalization capability across different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) without requiring major architectural changes.
3. Improved performance with limited training data, showing significant gains on the Small dataset (1/40 training data) compared to baselines.

## Strengths
1. **Addressing a meaningful problem**: The paper correctly identifies the limitation of traditional RNN-based NLU approaches that treat input as flat structures, ignoring natural language's rich linguistic properties. The authors properly critique prior work that uses linguistic knowledge as additional features (which suffers from error propagation) or relies on parsing quality (which can be brittle).

2. **Strong empirical validation of generalization**: The results in Table 1 demonstrate significant improvements with limited training data, where K-SAN (CNN) achieves 74.60 F1 on the Small dataset (1/40 training data) compared to 73.57 for the best baseline (CNN Encoder-Tagger), with statistically significant gains. This addresses a practical challenge in NLU where data scarcity is common.

3. **Flexibility across knowledge sources**: The model's performance across different knowledge resources (dependency trees from Stanford/SyntaxNet and AMR graphs from rule-based/JAMR parsers) in Table 2 shows consistent improvements over baselines, with similar performance across all knowledge sources. This demonstrates the model's generalization capability to different types of linguistic structure.

4. **Insightful attention visualization**: Figure 5 provides valuable visualization of attention weights on words and relations, showing how the model identifies salient substructures (e.g., origin, destination, time) for correct slot tagging. This validates the authors' claim about the model learning meaningful attention.

## Weaknesses
1. **Overstated novelty**: The paper builds on existing attention mechanisms (e.g., memory networks) and structural neural networks (e.g., Tree-RNN, DCNN). While the specific combination of knowledge-guided attention is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from prior work. The claim that "this is the first neural network approach that utilizes general knowledge as guidance in an end-to-end fashion" is questionable since prior work (e.g., Liu et al. 2013) used linguistic knowledge in similar ways.

2. **Lack of robustness analysis to external knowledge quality**: The paper acknowledges that dependency relations may be incorrectly parsed (Figure 5), but doesn't quantify how the model's performance degrades with parser errors. This is a significant limitation since the model's effectiveness depends on the quality of the external knowledge source.

3. **Misleading "end-to-end" claim**: The paper claims K-SAN is end-to-end, but the dependency parsing step is external and not learned. This is inconsistent with the standard definition of end-to-end learning, where all components are trained jointly. The model requires a pre-trained dependency parser, which is a significant limitation for real-world deployment.

4. **Insufficient explanation for encoding approach performance differences**: The paper observes that CNN encoding works better for Small/Medium data while RNN encoding works better for Large data, but provides no explanation for this phenomenon. A deeper analysis of why different encoding strategies work better with different data sizes would strengthen the paper.

5. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with several baselines but doesn't include more recent models that might have incorporated similar ideas (e.g., subsequent work on attention mechanisms for NLU). A more comprehensive comparison would better establish the model's relative contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the model's performance change when the dependency parser is intentionally degraded (e.g., with varying levels of error rates)? This would help assess the model's robustness to external knowledge quality.

2. The paper claims K-SAN is "end-to-end," but the dependency parsing is external. Could you clarify what you mean by "end-to-end" in this context, and why the dependency parsing step doesn't disqualify this claim?

3. The paper observes that CNN encoding works better for Small/Medium data while RNN encoding works better for Large data. Could you provide an analysis of why this difference exists?
