- Proposes K-SAN: uses external structured knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention to salient sentence substructures for NLU.  
- Claims three contributions: end-to-end learning of knowledge-guided attention, generalization across knowledge sources, and efficiency via parallelizable substructure modeling.  
- Main empirical claim: structured knowledge improves NLU, especially with limited training data, outperforming baselines on ATIS (notably 74.60% vs. 73.57% F1 on the smallest set).  
- Attention visualization (Fig. 5) convincingly shows the model focuses on semantically relevant substructures even with scarce data.  
- Generalization across knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) is a practically valuable contribution.  
- Strength: meaningfully incorporates structured linguistic knowledge into neural NLU models.  
- Strength: demonstrates data efficiency with significant gains on small datasets.  
- Strength: attention visualization supports the proposed mechanism.  
- Weakness: The "end-to-end" claim is misleading because dependency parsing is separate pre-processing that can introduce errors.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to modern transformer-based architectures, limiting assessment against current SOTA.  
- Weakness: No analysis of how parser errors affect downstream performance, which is critical for real-world use.  
- Weakness: Claimed efficiency (non-linear time complexity) is not validated with runtime experiments.  
- Recommendation: Authors should clarify handling of parser errors and provide more comprehensive comparisons to contemporary state-of-the-art NLU models.
