- Proposes K-SAN: a neural NLU model that uses external knowledge to guide attention over sentence substructures, improving slot tagging and generalization on ATIS.
- Contribution: introduces a knowledge-guided attention architecture that learns salient substructures instead of treating inputs as flat sequences.
- Contribution: shows the model generalizes across different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR) without major architectural changes.
- Contribution: demonstrates improved performance with limited training data, with significant gains on a Small (1/40) dataset.
- Strength: addresses the limitation of flat-sequence RNNs by leveraging linguistic structure while avoiding simple feature-based pipelines.
- Strength: strong empirical validation—statistically significant improvement on scarce-data settings (Small dataset).
- Strength: consistent improvements across multiple knowledge sources (Stanford/SyntaxNet deps, rule-based/JAMR AMR), showing flexibility.
- Strength: attention visualizations illustrate the model identifies meaningful substructures for correct slot tagging.
- Weakness: novelty is overstated—builds on existing attention and structural NN work and doesn't clearly separate its contribution from prior art.
- Weakness: no quantified robustness analysis showing how parser errors in external knowledge affect model performance.
- Weakness: the "end-to-end" claim is misleading because dependency parsing is external and not jointly learned.
- Weakness: lacks explanation for why CNN encoders work better on Small/Medium data while RNN encoders work better on Large data.
- Weakness: limited comparison set—doesn't include some more recent or closely related state-of-the-art baselines.
- Question: How does performance change when the dependency parser quality is systematically degraded?
- Question: Please clarify the meaning of "end-to-end" given the external dependency parser.
- Question: Can the authors analyze why CNN encoding outperforms RNNs on small/medium data but not on large data?
