

This paper proposes Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), which aims to improve NLU by incorporating external knowledge as a "teacher" to guide attention to salient substructures in sentences. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) end-to-end learning of knowledge-guided attention, (2) generalization across different knowledge sources, and (3) efficiency through parallelizable substructure modeling.

From my perspective, the most significant contribution is the demonstration that structured knowledge incorporation improves NLU performance, particularly with limited training data. The paper effectively shows that K-SAN outperforms strong baselines on the ATIS dataset, with the most notable gains on the smallest training set (74.60% vs. 73.57% F1 for CNN baseline). The attention visualization in Figure 5 compellingly demonstrates the model's ability to focus on relevant substructures even with scarce data. The generalization across knowledge sources (dependency trees and AMR graphs) is also a valuable practical contribution.

Strengths: 
1) The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of incorporating structured linguistic knowledge into neural NLU models without error propagation from external parsers. 
2) The data efficiency demonstrated (significant gains on small datasets) addresses a critical challenge in NLU where large training sets are often required. 
3) The attention visualization provides clear evidence that the model learns to focus on semantically relevant substructures, supporting the claimed mechanism.

Weaknesses:
1) The "end-to-end" claim is misleading, as the dependency parsing step is a separate pre-processing component that introduces potential error sources not addressed in the paper. 
2) The comparison to recent transformer-based architectures (which have become dominant in NLU) is insufficient, making it difficult to assess the model's standing in current NLU research.
3) The paper doesn't analyze how errors in the dependency parser affect model performance, which is crucial for real-world deployment.
4) The claimed efficiency benefit (non-linear time complexity) isn't adequately demonstrated with runtime experiments.

The authors should clarify how they handle parser errors and provide more comprehensive comparisons to contemporary state-of-the-art NLU models.
