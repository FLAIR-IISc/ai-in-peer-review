This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for natural language understanding, specifically slot filling in dialogue systems. The core idea is to augment RNNs with a mechanism to incorporate prior knowledge in the form of structural relationships, guiding attention and improving generalization, particularly in low-data scenarios. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization across different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability due to separate substructure modeling.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial challenge in dialogue systems – leveraging external knowledge to improve NLU performance, especially when training data is limited. Traditional approaches often treat knowledge as additional features, potentially leading to error propagation. K-SAN attempts to directly integrate knowledge into the model's architecture, which is a promising direction.
2. **Novelty of Knowledge Integration:**  The approach of using knowledge to *guide* attention within an RNN is novel.  Instead of simply adding knowledge as features, K-SAN learns *where* to focus attention based on the provided structural information. This is a significant departure from previous feature-enrichment methods. The demonstrated ability to handle diverse knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) further strengthens this point.
3. **Empirical Results & Ablation:** The experiments on the ATIS dataset demonstrate consistent performance improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art structural models.  The ablation study, showing performance with different knowledge sources (Stanford vs. SyntaxNet parsers, rule-based vs. JAMR AMR parsers), convincingly demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of the approach. The results showing better performance in the small data setting are particularly noteworthy, supporting the claim of improved generalization.
4. **Clear Explanation and Architecture:** The paper clearly explains the K-SAN architecture with a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The breakdown of the knowledge encoding module and attention mechanism is well-defined and easy to follow. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is solely focused on the ATIS dataset, which, while a standard benchmark, is relatively narrow in scope.  It’s unclear how well K-SAN would generalize to other dialogue domains or more complex NLU tasks. The lack of results on more challenging datasets limits the overall impact of the findings.
2. **Dependency on Parser Quality:** While the paper demonstrates robustness to different parsers, the performance is still inherently tied to the accuracy of the underlying knowledge source (dependency parser or AMR parser). Errors in parsing can propagate and negatively impact the model’s performance. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of K-SAN to parsing errors.  The example in Figure 5 hints at this issue.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but doesn’t provide sufficient detail about the process. The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the weighting factor α in the joint RNN tagger) could significantly affect performance, and it’s unclear how sensitive the model is to these settings. A more comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Identifying specific types of errors that K-SAN still struggles with would provide valuable insights for future research and potential improvements. What kind of slot filling errors are *not* reduced by K-SAN, and why?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details regarding the hyperparameter tuning process, specifically for α in the joint RNN tagger? How was the optimal value determined, and how sensitive is performance to changes in this parameter?
2.  Given the dependence on parser accuracy, have you explored any techniques to mitigate the impact of parsing errors? For example, could the attention mechanism be designed to be more robust to noisy knowledge signals?
3.  What is the computational overhead of K-SAN compared to the baseline RNN tagger, particularly with respect to training time and memory usage? The claim of efficiency/parallelizability is not quantitatively supported.
4.  Could you elaborate on the types of linguistic structures *beyond* dependency trees and AMR graphs that could be used as knowledge guidance? Are there any theoretical limitations to the types of knowledge that can be effectively incorporated into K-SAN?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.
*  The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
*  The visualization of the attention weights (Figure 5) is helpful and illustrative.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to knowledge-guided NLU. While the evaluation is limited in scope, the empirical results are encouraging, and the theoretical contributions are significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
