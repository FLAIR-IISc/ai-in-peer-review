## Review of “Knowledge as a Teacher: Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks”

This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel approach to natural language understanding (NLU) aiming to improve semantic tagging by incorporating prior knowledge through non-flat network topologies. The core idea is to leverage external knowledge, such as dependency parse trees, to guide the attention mechanism within an RNN framework, allowing the model to focus on salient substructures. The authors claim contributions in end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization across knowledge types, and efficiency through parallel substructure modeling.

I believe the most significant contribution of this work lies in the *systematic exploration of integrating structured knowledge into an attention-based RNN for sequence tagging*. While the idea of leveraging external knowledge for NLU isn’t novel, the K-SAN architecture provides a concrete mechanism for directly guiding attention based on substructures derived from this knowledge. The experimental validation on the ATIS dataset, showcasing performance gains particularly with limited data, strengthens this contribution. The attempt to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach by evaluating different knowledge sources (dependency trees, AMR graphs) is also commendable. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Formulation & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional flat RNN structures in capturing linguistic relationships and motivates the need for knowledge-guided attention.
2. **Novel Architecture:** The K-SAN architecture offers a principled way to incorporate external knowledge, moving beyond simply adding knowledge-derived features. The attention mechanism, guided by substructures, allows the model to dynamically focus on relevant information.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The results on the ATIS dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of K-SAN, especially in low-data scenarios, and outperform several strong baselines. The gains suggest a tangible benefit from incorporating the knowledge-guided attention.
4. **Generalization Across Knowledge Sources:** Exploring different knowledge resources like dependency trees and AMR graphs, even if the performance differences aren't massive, indicates the flexibility of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** The core components—RNNs, attention mechanisms, and dependency parsing—are all well-established techniques. The novelty primarily lies in their combination and specific application to knowledge-guided attention, which requires stronger justification compared to related work.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:** While results are shown with K-SAN, a more detailed ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of each component (e.g., attention mechanism alone, different knowledge encodings). How much of the performance gain is *specifically* due to the knowledge guidance versus simply using a more complex architecture?
3. **Dependency on Parser Quality:** The model’s performance is inherently tied to the accuracy of the external knowledge source (e.g., the dependency parser). The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t sufficiently discuss the impact of parsing errors on the results or potential robustness strategies.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to isolate the impact of the knowledge-guided attention mechanism on performance? Specifically, what is the performance of the model *without* the knowledge guidance?
2. How sensitive is the model's performance to errors in the dependency parsing (or other knowledge source)? Did you experiment with noisy or imperfect knowledge representations?
3. The paper mentions “generalization for different knowledge.” Can you discuss any challenges or limitations encountered when applying K-SAN with different knowledge bases beyond dependency trees and AMR graphs?
