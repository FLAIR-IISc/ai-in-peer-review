This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN) for natural language understanding, specifically slot filling in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core idea is to move beyond treating utterances as flat sequences and incorporate prior knowledge (dependency structures in this work) to guide attention and improve understanding, especially in low-data scenarios. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability due to substructure modeling.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core Limitation of RNNs:** The fundamental motivation – that standard RNNs fail to adequately capture the structured information inherent in language – is highly relevant and well-articulated. Recognizing that flat sequence modeling is suboptimal for tasks demanding relational understanding is a significant insight.
2. **Novel Integration of Knowledge & Attention:** The approach of using prior knowledge to *guide* attention within an RNN framework is a strong contribution. This is distinct from simply concatenating knowledge-based features. The attention mechanism allows the model to dynamically weight the importance of different substructures, which is more flexible and potentially more effective than hard-coding structural relationships.
3. **Empirical Validation on a Standard Benchmark:** The experiments on the ATIS dataset provide a solid empirical foundation. The results consistently demonstrate improvements over strong baselines, including both traditional CRF methods and other neural network architectures. The improvement on the "Small" dataset is particularly compelling, suggesting better generalization with limited data.
4. **Demonstrated Generalizability of Knowledge Sources:** Showing positive results with different parsing tools (Stanford and SyntaxNet) and exploring AMR graphs for knowledge representation is a strong validation of the method’s flexibility and underlying principles. This strengthens the claim of generalization beyond a specific knowledge schema.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Architecture:** While the *combination* of knowledge guidance and attention is novel, the individual components – RNNs, attention mechanisms, and dependency parsing – are all well-established. The architectural innovation is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The core idea feels like a logical extension of existing work rather than a radical departure.
2. **Overstated Claims of Efficiency:** The claim of parallelizability needs more justification. While modeling substructures *allows* for potential parallel processing, the paper doesn't provide any evidence (e.g., runtime comparisons) to support this claim. The computational cost of parsing and processing substructures could negate any benefits.
3. **Insufficient Analysis of Attention Weights:** The attention visualization (Figure 5) is helpful, but lacks in-depth analysis. It would be significantly stronger if the authors investigated *why* the model is attending to specific substructures. Are the attended structures semantically meaningful? Are they consistent across different utterances? A more rigorous analysis would demonstrate the model’s learning of linguistic principles.
4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Structural Parsers as Features:** The comparison to baseline models employing dependency parsers (CRF and DCNN) is somewhat weak. These models *use* the parser output as features. A more direct comparison would involve feeding the dependency parse trees directly into a comparable RNN architecture (without the attention mechanism) to isolate the benefit of the proposed attention guidance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide runtime comparisons (training and inference) between K-SAN and a comparable RNN model *without* the knowledge-guided attention mechanism, to substantiate the claim of efficiency/parallelizability?
2.  The paper highlights the importance of capturing salient substructures. Can you provide examples of instances where K-SAN correctly identifies and attends to substructures that simpler models miss, and explain why this leads to improved performance?
3.  You mention potential error propagation from parsing. How robust is K-SAN to noisy or incorrect dependency parses? Have you explored methods for mitigating the effects of parsing errors?
4.  How sensitive is K-SAN to the choice of knowledge representation (dependency parsing vs. AMR, rule-based vs. learned parsers)? What factors should guide the selection of an appropriate knowledge source for a given task?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and concise, though the introduction could be more focused on the specific problem being addressed.
* The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but could benefit from a discussion of recent advances in graph neural networks (GNNs) for NLP, which are relevant to the idea of structural modeling.
* The experimental setup is well-described, but the hyperparameter tuning process could be elaborated upon.
