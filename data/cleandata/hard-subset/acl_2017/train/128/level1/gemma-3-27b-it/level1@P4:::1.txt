This paper introduces Knowledge-Guided Structural Attention Networks (K-SAN), a novel model for natural language understanding (NLU) in dialogue systems. The core idea is to augment recurrent neural networks with non-flat network topologies guided by prior knowledge, aiming to capture salient substructures and improve generalization, particularly with limited training data. The authors claim three main contributions: end-to-end learning with knowledge guidance, generalization to different knowledge types, and efficiency/parallelizability due to substructure modeling.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Key Limitation of Standard RNNs:** The paper directly addresses a crucial limitation of standard RNNs – their inability to effectively model the structured information inherent in natural language. By incorporating prior knowledge through structural attention, K-SAN attempts to move beyond treating utterances as flat sequences. This is a strong conceptual contribution.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation on the widely-used ATIS dataset. The comparison against several strong baselines, including both traditional and recent deep learning approaches, provides convincing evidence of K-SAN’s effectiveness. The ablation study across different dataset sizes (Small, Medium, Large) is particularly valuable, showcasing the model’s robustness and ability to generalize.
3. **Demonstrated Generalization to Different Knowledge Sources:** The experiments showing comparable performance with both dependency tree and AMR graph knowledge is a significant strength. This validates the claim of generalization to different knowledge types and demonstrates the flexibility of the K-SAN framework. It showcases that the method isn't tied to a specific type of linguistic annotation.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The figures and tables are informative and effectively illustrate the model architecture and experimental results. The introduction provides sufficient motivation and context for the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty of the Architecture:** While the idea of integrating knowledge into RNNs is valuable, the architectural novelty of K-SAN is relatively incremental. The core mechanism – using attention to weight different substructures – is a common technique in neural networks. The primary contribution resides more in the specific application to NLU with prior knowledge and the demonstrated performance gains.
2. **Dependence on Parser Accuracy:** The performance of K-SAN heavily relies on the accuracy of the underlying dependency parser or AMR parser. While the paper shows that the model is somewhat robust, errors in the parsed structure can propagate and negatively impact the performance. The paper doesn’t thoroughly investigate the impact of parser errors or explore methods for mitigating them (e.g., using noisy or probabilistic parsing).
3. **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity:** The claim of efficiency and parallelizability isn’t fully substantiated. While modeling substructures separately *could* lead to efficiency gains, the paper doesn’t provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of K-SAN compared to standard RNNs, particularly for long utterances with complex structures. A more in-depth analysis of runtime and memory usage would strengthen this claim.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper presents quantitative results but lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Understanding the types of errors K-SAN still makes and the reasons behind them would provide valuable insights for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of K-SAN to the accuracy of the dependency parser (or AMR parser)? Can you provide a quantitative analysis of the impact of parsing errors on the final results?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of K-SAN, including runtime and memory usage, compared to a standard RNN model?
3. Could you provide examples of specific utterances where K-SAN significantly outperforms the baseline models, and explain *why* K-SAN is able to achieve better performance in those cases?
4. While you mention using different knowledge encoding networks (NN, RNN, CNN), do different encodings lead to significantly different performance? If so, under what conditions?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is reasonably comprehensive.
*   The choice of the ATIS dataset is appropriate for evaluating the proposed model.
*   The hyperparameter tuning process could be described in more detail.
