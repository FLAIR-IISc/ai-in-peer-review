

# Peer Review

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for KB relation detection, addressing key challenges in KBQA that differentiate it from general relation extraction: large relation sets (thousands vs. hundreds), zero-shot learning requirements, and multi-relation chains. The authors claim two main contributions: (1) a novel hierarchical matching approach between questions and relations at both word-level and relation-level representations, enhanced with residual learning for deeper architectures; and (2) demonstration that their improved relation detector enables a simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP benchmarks.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of KBQA-specific challenges**: The paper effectively articulates the critical differences between general relation extraction and KBQA relation detection (large relation sets, zero-shot learning, multi-relation chains), which is a significant contribution to the field. This problem formulation is well-motivated and clearly explains why existing relation extraction methods are insufficient for KBQA.

2. **Well-justified hierarchical matching approach**: The authors correctly identify that different levels of abstraction are needed for matching questions to relations (word-level for short phrases, relation-level for global context), and their hierarchical matching strategy is theoretically sound. The ablation studies (Table 2) convincingly demonstrate that both levels of representation are necessary, especially for SimpleQuestions where unseen relations are more common.

3. **Effective integration of residual learning**: The paper makes a compelling case for using residual connections in sequence matching, addressing the training difficulties of deeper architectures. The ablation studies show that residual learning provides significant gains (particularly on WebQSP), and the analysis that residual learning enables better hierarchical matching (rather than just combining two BiLSTMs) is particularly strong.

4. **Practical KBQA system with entity re-ranking**: The paper's KBQA pipeline is simple yet effective, particularly the entity re-ranking step that leverages relation detection to resolve entity ambiguities. This demonstrates how relation detection can be productively integrated into the KBQA pipeline, which is a valuable contribution beyond just relation detection.

5. **Strong empirical results**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on both relation detection (93.3% on SimpleQuestions, 82.53% on WebQSP) and KBQA (improving by 2-3% over baselines), with clear ablation studies showing the importance of each component. The results are statistically significant (p < 0.01 for WebQSP).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core approach**: While the application to KBQA is novel, the hierarchical matching and residual learning components are not themselves novel to NLP. The paper would benefit from more explicit comparison to how these techniques have been used in other sequence matching tasks (e.g., natural language inference) and why they're particularly effective for this specific KBQA problem.

2. **Insufficient analysis of why residual learning works**: The paper shows residual learning improves performance but doesn't deeply analyze *why* it works better than attention-based approaches for this specific task. The comparison to Parikh et al. (2016) is superficial, and the authors don't provide evidence that residual learning enables better hierarchical matching (beyond the claim).

3. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper lacks an error analysis showing what types of questions the model struggles with (e.g., questions with complex syntax, negation, or ambiguous relations). This would help understand the model's limitations and guide future work.

4. **Overstated "zero-shot" capability**: The paper claims their model handles zero-shot learning better, but the SimpleQuestions test set has only 14% unseen relations. The paper doesn't provide evidence of how the model performs when faced with higher proportions of unseen relations (e.g., 50% or more).

5. **Underdeveloped discussion of entity re-ranking**: The entity re-ranking step is crucial to the KBQA performance, but the paper doesn't explore how sensitive the system is to the quality of the initial entity linker. If the initial entity linker is poor, does the re-ranking step still help, and by how much?

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the HR-BiLSTM handles zero-shot learning better than baselines, but SimpleQuestions has only 14% unseen relations. Could you provide results on a more challenging zero-shot setting (e.g., randomly removing 50% of relations from training) to better demonstrate this capability?

2. Why does residual learning help more on WebQSP than SimpleQuestions? Is it because WebQSP has more complex relation chains that benefit from deeper representations, or is there another factor?

3. The paper states that "entity re-ranking" is crucial for performance, but Appendix C (not provided in the submission) contains the detailed performance of this step. Could you provide a brief summary of the key findings from this analysis in the response?

4. In Table 2, the HR-BiLSTM with residual learning outperforms the attention-based approach (92.6% vs. 92.5%), but the paper doesn't explain why attention doesn't work as well. Could you provide a qualitative analysis of examples where attention fails compared to residual learning?

5. The paper mentions that "in contrast to previous KBQA systems, our system does not use joint-inference or feature-based re-ranking step." How much of the performance gain is due to the improved relation detection versus the simplified pipeline?
