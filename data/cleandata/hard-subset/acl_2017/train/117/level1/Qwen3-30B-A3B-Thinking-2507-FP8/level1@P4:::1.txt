

Summary and Main Contributions:
This paper proposes a hierarchical residual bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for knowledge base relation detection in question answering systems. The main contributions as assessed objectively are: (1) A novel hierarchical matching approach that combines relation-level and word-level relation representations to address the challenges of KB relation detection (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation chains), enhanced with residual learning for better training and representation; (2) A demonstration that this improved relation detection directly leads to state-of-the-art performance on both relation detection tasks and KBQA end tasks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP benchmarks) through a simple KBQA system that integrates entity re-ranking and relation detection.

Strengths:
1. Strong empirical validation: The paper demonstrates significant improvements over strong baselines (2-3% absolute gain on relation detection, p<0.001 for SimpleQuestions) and achieves state-of-the-art KBQA results (4th row in Table 3). The ablation studies thoroughly validate the contributions of each component (hierarchical matching, residual learning, relation-level vs. word-level representations).

2. Clear technical motivation: The paper effectively articulates the specific challenges of KB relation detection compared to general relation extraction (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, multi-relation chains), providing a strong rationale for their approach. The analysis of relation representation granularity (Table 1) is particularly compelling.

3. Effective integration with KBQA: Unlike many relation detection papers that focus solely on the relation detection task, this work demonstrates how improved relation detection directly benefits the KBQA end task through their simple but effective two-step relation detection pipeline with entity re-ranking. The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is a novel and practical way to address entity linking ambiguities.

4. Comprehensive ablation analysis: The paper provides detailed ablation studies (Table 2) that isolate the contribution of each architectural component, showing that hierarchical matching (93.3% vs 91.2/88.8%) and residual learning (82.53% vs 80.65%) are both essential for the performance gains.

5. Practical implementation: The KBQA system they propose is relatively simple (no joint inference or complex re-ranking), yet achieves competitive results. This demonstrates the practical utility of their relation detection improvements.

Weaknesses:
1. Limited novelty of core architecture: While the application to KB relation detection is valuable, the core components (BiLSTMs, residual learning, hierarchical matching) are combinations of existing techniques rather than fundamentally new ideas. The novelty appears to be in the specific application rather than in the model architecture itself.

2. Insufficient comparison with recent approaches: The paper compares with baselines from 2014-2016 but doesn't include comparisons with more recent relation detection methods published in 2016-2017 that might have been available at ACL 2017 submission time (e.g., Zhou et al. 2016, Wang et al. 2016).

3. Limited analysis of failure cases: The paper presents strong results but lacks analysis of where the model fails or what types of questions are particularly challenging. This limits understanding of the model's capabilities and limitations.

4. Incomplete justification of entity re-ranking: While the entity re-ranking step is shown to improve performance, the paper doesn't fully explain why this step is necessary or how it specifically interacts with the relation detection process beyond the examples provided.

5. Underexplored residual learning rationale: The paper shows residual learning improves performance but doesn't deeply analyze why it's particularly effective for hierarchical matching in this context. The connection between residual learning and the hierarchical matching problem could be more thoroughly explored.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper claims SimpleQuestions suffers more from unseen relations than WebQSP. Can you provide quantitative evidence for this claim, such as the proportion of unseen relations in the test sets for each dataset?

2. The paper shows residual learning helps more on WebQSP than SimpleQuestions. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why this difference exists, particularly given that SimpleQuestions is a single-relation task while WebQSP is multi-relation?

3. The paper states that constraint detection is "crucial" for their system. How does this differ from previous KBQA systems, and what specific aspects of their relation detection model make constraint detection more important?
