

This paper proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), addressing key challenges in KB-specific relation detection: large relation vocabulary (thousands of types), zero-shot learning requirements, and multi-relation chains. The authors claim two main contributions: (1) an improved relation detection model using hierarchical matching between questions and both word-level and relation-level representations with residual learning, and (2) demonstration that this improved detector enables their simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP benchmarks.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for the unique challenges of KB relation detection versus general relation extraction, identifying three critical differences that justify their approach. The HR-BiLSTM design is well-motivated, with thorough ablation studies showing that hierarchical matching (combining word-level and relation-level representations) and residual learning significantly improve performance (93.3% vs. 91.2% on SimpleQuestions). Crucially, the paper demonstrates that improved relation detection directly benefits the end KBQA task, with their simple system (without complex re-ranking) achieving state-of-the-art results. The entity re-ranking step, which leverages relation detection to resolve entity linking ambiguities, is particularly insightful and well-justified.

Weaknesses: The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art" results is questionable given the age of the cited baselines (AMPCNN, BiCNN from 2016) and lack of comparison with more recent approaches. The KBQA system remains dependent on a pre-existing entity linker (S-MART), limiting claims about end-to-end novelty. The paper doesn't adequately address why residual learning outperforms attention mechanisms beyond showing slightly better numbers, and the zero-shot learning claim isn't specifically validated (only mentioning 14% unseen relations in SimpleQuestions without performance metrics on these cases).

Questions: 1) Could the authors provide specific performance metrics on unseen relations in SimpleQuestions? 2) How would the model scale to knowledge bases with 10,000+ relation types? 3) What are the limitations of treating relation names as word sequences (e.g., for relations with non-sequential or non-English names)?
