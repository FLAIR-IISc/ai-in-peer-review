

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a hierarchical residual bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for KB relation detection, which is a critical component in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems. The authors identify three key challenges in KB relation detection that distinguish it from general relation extraction: (1) large relation vocabulary (thousands of relations), (2) zero-shot learning requirements due to unseen relations in test data, and (3) the need to handle relation chains for complex questions. 

The paper's main contributions, as I see them (ordered by strength):

1. A novel hierarchical matching approach that combines word-level and relation-level representations for relation detection, using residual learning to enable deeper hierarchical matching and improve training stability.
2. A simple but effective KBQA system that integrates relation detection with entity re-ranking, demonstrating how improved relation detection directly benefits the KBQA end task.
3. Comprehensive empirical validation showing state-of-the-art results on both relation detection (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) and KBQA end tasks, with strong ablation studies.

## Strengths

1. **Clear articulation of the KBQA-specific challenges**: The paper effectively identifies and explains why KB relation detection differs significantly from general relation extraction tasks. The authors correctly note that most prior work assumes a small, closed set of relations, whereas KBQA requires handling thousands of relations and zero-shot learning. This problem statement is well-motivated and provides a strong foundation for the proposed solution.

2. **Novel hierarchical residual matching approach**: The HR-BiLSTM model is well-designed to address the specific challenges of KB relation detection. The key insight is recognizing that relation names contain global information (better captured by relation-level representations) while word sequences contain local information (better captured by word-level representations). The residual learning component is particularly valuable as it enables effective training of deeper hierarchical matching, which is not possible with standard approaches. The ablation studies (Table 2) convincingly demonstrate that both the hierarchical matching and residual learning components are necessary for optimal performance.

3. **Strong empirical validation with meaningful ablation studies**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple dimensions: (1) relation detection performance on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, (2) ablation studies of the proposed components, and (3) end-to-end KBQA results. The improvements over baselines (2-3% on relation detection, 2-3% on KBQA) are statistically significant (p < 0.01) and consistent across datasets. The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is particularly insightful as it addresses a key bottleneck in KBQA systems (entity linking ambiguities) and demonstrates how relation detection can improve the overall pipeline.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited discussion of why residual learning is particularly effective for this task**: While the paper demonstrates that residual learning improves performance, it doesn't thoroughly explain why it's more beneficial for KB relation detection than for other NLP tasks. The authors note that residual learning helps more on WebQSP (which involves longer relation chains) than on SimpleQuestions, but don't provide deeper insights into the structural properties of KB relation detection that make residual learning so effective.

2. **Insufficient analysis of the entity re-ranking component**: The paper states that removing the entity re-ranking step significantly decreases performance (Table 3), but doesn't provide a detailed breakdown of how much this specific component contributes to the overall improvement. The authors claim it "helps entity disambiguation in the initial entity linking," but don't quantify the improvement in entity linking accuracy or show examples of cases where this re-ranking step was particularly beneficial.

3. **Lack of computational complexity analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the proposed HR-BiLSTM model compared to baselines. Given that KBQA systems need to process many questions in real-time, understanding the inference time and memory requirements would be valuable for practical applications. The paper mentions using a 400-dimensional hidden state, but doesn't compare the computational requirements with other approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Table 2, the residual learning approach helps WebQSP more than SimpleQuestions. Could you elaborate on why the hierarchical matching is more beneficial for WebQSP, given that it involves relation chains? Is it because WebQSP questions require longer context matching?

2. The paper claims the entity re-ranking step "helps entity disambiguation in the initial entity linking." Could you provide specific examples of questions where the entity re-ranking step corrected incorrect entity linking results that the baseline entity linker could not resolve?

3. The paper states that replacing BiLSTM with CNN in the hierarchical matching framework results in a large performance drop on WebQSP. Could you explain why LSTMs work better for handling relation chains in WebQSP compared to CNNs? Is it solely due to LSTMs' ability to handle longer dependencies, or are there other factors?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to KBQA research by addressing a key challenge (relation detection) in a novel and effective way. The proposed HR-BiLSTM model is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear improvements over previous approaches. The integration of relation detection into a full KBQA pipeline with the entity re-ranking step is particularly insightful and shows how improvements in subtasks can directly benefit the end task. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a compelling case for its approach.

The weaknesses identified are not severe enough to prevent acceptance, but addressing them in the camera-ready version would strengthen the paper. The paper's contributions are significant, well-validated, and relevant to the ACL community, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ACL 2017.
