

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), which differs from general relation extraction due to the large number of relations, unseen relations in test data, and the need for chain relations in complex questions. The authors propose a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that matches questions to both word-level and relation-level representations of relations, enhanced with residual learning to facilitate training. They also demonstrate how this improved relation detector integrates into a simple KBQA system that uses relation detection to re-rank entities, resolving a common bottleneck in KBQA systems.

## Main Contributions
1. The HR-BiLSTM model for hierarchical relation matching with residual learning, which effectively handles both word-level and relation-level representations while overcoming training difficulties in deep architectures.
2. A practical KBQA system that integrates relation detection with entity linking through an entity re-ranking step, significantly improving overall KBQA accuracy.
3. Comprehensive empirical validation showing state-of-the-art results on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) benchmarks.

## Strengths

1. The HR-BiLSTM model is a well-motivated and technically sound contribution. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches (using only word-level or relation-level representations) and propose a hierarchical matching approach that addresses these limitations. The residual learning component is particularly clever, adapting a successful computer vision technique to NLP to overcome the training difficulties of deep hierarchical architectures. The ablation studies in Table 2 provide compelling evidence for the necessity of both hierarchical matching and residual learning.

2. The integration of the relation detector into the KBQA system is insightful and practical. The entity re-ranking step is a novel application of relation detection that directly addresses a well-known bottleneck in KBQA systems (entity ambiguity). The authors demonstrate that this simple integration leads to significant improvements in overall KBQA accuracy, which is a strong practical contribution beyond just relation detection.

3. The results on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP are impressive and well-supported. The HR-BiLSTM achieves 93.3% accuracy on SimpleQuestions (2-3% improvement over previous state-of-the-art) and 82.53% on WebQSP (3.1% improvement). Crucially, these improvements translate directly to the KBQA end task, with a 2-3% improvement over baselines on both datasets without complex joint-inference or feature-based re-ranking steps.

4. The paper provides a clear and thorough analysis of the differences between general relation extraction and KBQA relation detection, which is crucial for contextualizing the problem and justifying the proposed approach. The discussion of why word-level vs. relation-level representations are needed is particularly strong and well-supported by examples.

## Weaknesses

1. The comparison with previous work is not entirely fair or comprehensive. For SimpleQuestions, the authors compare with AMPCNN (Yin et al., 2016) but omit the more recent state-of-the-art work by Bao et al. (2016) which they cite in their references. Similarly, for WebQSP, they compare with STAGG (Yih et al., 2015) but don't include the most recent results on this dataset. This makes it difficult to fully assess the significance of their contribution.

2. The paper claims that the KBQA system achieves state-of-the-art results without using joint-inference or feature-based re-ranking, but the constraint detection step involves matching the question to candidate entities, which is functionally similar to feature-based re-ranking. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how this differs from previous approaches and why it's considered less complex.

3. The ablation study in Table 3 shows that removing the entity re-ranking step significantly decreases performance, but it's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the relation detection model itself versus the entity re-ranking step. A more controlled experiment that isolates the contribution of the relation detection model would strengthen the claims.

4. The paper briefly mentions that attention mechanisms perform worse than the proposed approach (Table 2), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why. Understanding why attention-based approaches don't work as well for this specific hierarchical matching task would add depth to the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your HR-BiLSTM model compare with the more recent state-of-the-art on SimpleQuestions (e.g., Bao et al., 2016), which you cite but don't include in your comparison?

2. Can you provide more details on how much of the KBQA performance improvement comes from the relation detection model itself versus the entity re-ranking step? A controlled experiment would help clarify this.

3. The constraint detection step involves matching the question to candidate entities - how is this different from previous feature-based re-ranking approaches, and why is it considered less complex?

4. The paper mentions that residual learning helps more on WebQSP than SimpleQuestions. Could you provide more insight into why this difference in impact occurs, given the different characteristics of these datasets?
