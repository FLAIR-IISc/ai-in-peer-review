Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents a neural relation detection model for knowledge base question answering (KBQA). The model, HR-BiLSTM, compares questions with relations at two granularities—word-level (tokenized relation names) and relation-level (relation IDs)—and combines two levels of question abstraction using residual connections between stacked BiLSTMs. The authors also build a straightforward KBQA pipeline in which relation detection is used twice: first to rerank initial entity linking candidates, then to select the core relation or relation chain. The approach achieves strong results on SimpleQuestions (single-relation) and WebQSP (multi-relation).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A multi-granularity relation detection architecture (word-level + relation-level) with hierarchical residual matching that improves generalization and training stability, especially for multi-hop relations.
2) A simple, effective KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection to rerank entity candidates and select relation chains, yielding state-of-the-art or competitive end-to-end QA performance.

Strengths
- Clear and effective modeling of multi-granularity relation representations. Treating relation IDs and their tokenized forms jointly addresses the closed-set vs zero-shot tension in KBQA relation detection. The ablations (Table 2) convincingly show that using both levels improves over either alone on both datasets, with larger gains on SimpleQuestions where unseen relations are more prevalent.
- Hierarchical residual matching yields consistent gains and seems particularly well-motivated for multi-hop relation chains. The residual connections between stacked BiLSTM layers make the two abstraction levels comparable and complementary. The performance jump on WebQSP (80.65 to 82.53 with residuals) suggests the training and representational benefits are substantive beyond a simple ensembling of layers.
- Practical and impactful integration into the KBQA pipeline via entity reranking. Using the relation detector to prefer entities whose neighboring relations are consistent with the question is a simple idea with real payoff. The ablation shows this step materially improves end-to-end accuracy, addressing a known bottleneck in KBQA where entity names are ambiguous.
- Strong empirical results with careful baselines and ablations. The paper reimplements strong baselines (AMPCNN, BiCNN) and tests a range of alternatives (single-layer BiLSTM, attention-based matching, CNN replacements), which increases confidence in the reported gains. Reported p-values for relation detection improvements further support significance.
- Simplicity and portability. The approach does not rely on heavy joint inference or complex hand-engineered features; it builds on widely used components (pretrained word embeddings, BiLSTMs, cosine similarity, ranking loss), which facilitates adoption.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior sequence matching and multi-granularity designs. Splitting relation names into words and matching with question text is established in KBQA (e.g., Yin et al., Yih et al., Liang et al.). Residual connections for deep sequential encoders were known by 2016. The paper’s novelty lies in combining these ideas for hierarchical question–relation matching, which is useful but not a fundamental modeling shift.
- Limited analysis of the zero-shot/unseen relation claim. While the motivation emphasizes unseen relations (e.g., 14% in SimpleQuestions), the paper does not break down relation detection accuracy by seen vs unseen relations or by chain length. Such analysis is important to substantiate the generalization advantage of word-level and hierarchical matching.
- Attention baseline may be under-specified and possibly suboptimal. The decomposable attention (Parikh et al.) is designed for sentence-pair inference on relatively long sequences; applying it naively may not be ideal for short relation tokens. Without a stronger attentive matching baseline (e.g., BiDAF-like token alignment or soft interaction over both layers) it is hard to conclude that residuals are definitively better than attention for hierarchical matching.
- Reproducibility and dataset transparency concerns. The WebQSP relation detection dataset is “to be released.” Precise details for candidate generation (e.g., relation chain enumeration, maximum chain length), and hyperparameters (K, K0, l, alpha, lambda mixing in Eq. for query scoring) are lightly specified. These choices can impact both difficulty and performance; more detail is needed for fair comparison and reproduction.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and brittle. The character-overlap matching for constraints (dates, entities) and special rules seem ad hoc compared to the stronger learning-based components. The paper shows that constraint detection is crucial to the final score on WebQSP; the reliance on simple heuristics raises questions about robustness and portability to other datasets/KBs.

Questions to authors
- Unseen relations: Can you report relation detection accuracy separately for seen vs unseen relations on SimpleQuestions, and for differing chain lengths on WebQSP? Do hierarchical residuals help more in zero-shot settings?
- Residual variants: Which residual connection performed best—on hidden states before pooling or on pooled vectors? Did you try layer normalization or highway connections? How sensitive are results to the residual choice?
- Candidate generation: What is the maximum chain length considered on WebQSP? How large are the candidate relation/chain pools per entity on average? What are l, K, K0 in practice?
- Mixing weights: How are alpha (entity reranking) and lambda (final query score) tuned? Are results stable across a range of values?
- Attention baseline: What exact attention architecture was used (one-way or two-way), and did you allow attention over both layers? Could you provide dev-set comparisons to confirm the implementation is competitive?
- Significance testing: What test (e.g., paired bootstrap, approximate randomization) was used to compute p-values in Table 2?
- Runtime and scalability: What is the computational cost of scoring all relations connected to candidate entities during entity reranking? How does this scale with larger KBs?
- Resource release: Will you release the WebQSP relation detection dataset, code, and all hyperparameters to enable reproducibility?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid, well-executed paper with clear empirical gains and a practical design that addresses a real pain point in KBQA (entity ambiguity) via improved relation detection. The technical novelty is moderate but focused; the hierarchical residual matching is well-motivated and supported by ablations, particularly on multi-relation WebQSP. I recommend acceptance after the authors address the analysis and reproducibility gaps, especially unseen-relation breakdowns, candidate generation details, and stronger attention baselines.
