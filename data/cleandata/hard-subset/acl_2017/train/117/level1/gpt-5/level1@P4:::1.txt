Summary and contributions
This paper addresses relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA), proposing a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches a question to both word-level and relation-level representations of candidate KB relations. The model uses two BiLSTM layers for the question to capture different abstraction levels; residual connections are introduced to ease training and encourage complementary matching across layers. The paper further integrates the detector into a simple KBQA pipeline with two-step relation detection: (i) entity re-ranking using relation signals scored directly from the raw question; (ii) relation (or relation-chain) detection conditioned on the chosen topic entity via entity masking; followed by an optional constraint detection module.

Contributions as assessed:
1) A hierarchical residual matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection that jointly leverages word-level and relation-level (relation-name) representations. The residual combination across question encoder layers improves training stability and performance over naïve combination or attention baselines.
2) A pragmatic two-step integration of relation detection into a KBQA pipeline, where relation scores help disambiguate entities and, in turn, better constrain relation search, yielding state-of-the-art or competitive results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP with minimal additional components.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements on relation detection across two benchmarks with challenging characteristics (zero-shot relations in SimpleQuestions; relation chains in WebQSP). The proposed model yields 2–3% absolute gains over strong BiLSTM/CNN baselines on both datasets. Ablations indicate that using both relation word tokens and relation-name tokens accounts for a substantial portion of the gains, with residual matching further improving WebQSP (longer contexts/relations) by ~2% over weighted sum or attention.
- Effective and simple KBQA pipeline design that demonstrates end-task impact. The two-step relation detection improves entity disambiguation (first step) and relation selection (second step), leading to 2–3% end-to-end gains over baselines using the same pipeline but different relation detectors. Despite omitting heavier joint inference or answer re-ranking, the system achieves state-of-the-art (or close) performance.
- Sound motivation and design choices tailored to KBQA constraints. The paper articulates why treating relation names at two granularities addresses both data sparsity (relation-level tokens) and generalization to unseen relations (word-level tokens), and why deeper question encoders help match phrases of varying length/abstraction. Entity masking (<e>) is a reasonable and well-supported choice to inject positional information.
- Clear ablation and alternative comparisons. The paper contrasts residual vs weighted sum vs attention-based hierarchical matching, and BiLSTM vs CNN encoders, helping isolate where the improvements come from. It also studies the effect of removing relation words or relation names.
- Practicality. The method leverages existing entity linkers and standard pre-trained word embeddings, and relies on cosine similarity and max-pooling, making it relatively easy to implement and efficient enough for candidate reranking in KBQA.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental. Residual connections over stacked recurrent encoders and multi-granularity matching are established ideas; the novelty mainly lies in combining relation-level and word-level representations with residual matching for this task. While the empirical benefits are clear, the architectural contribution is modest relative to prior work on residual RNNs and hierarchical matching/attention.
- Some technical details are underspecified or potentially inconsistent. The ranking loss is written as l = max{0, srel(r+; q) + srel(r−; q)}, which seems unusual (typically a margin-based max{0, γ − s+ + s−}). Clarification is needed on the margin and sign. The description of initializing the “relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence” on the relation side is unclear (which LSTM is initialized by which, and how parameters/embeddings are shared).
- Limited analysis of zero-shot generalization and OOV handling. The central motivation includes unseen relations; however, the results do not report performance broken down by seen vs unseen relation names (in training) for SimpleQuestions, nor by chain length for WebQSP. This would more directly substantiate the claimed benefits of word-level relation encoding and hierarchical matching.
- Evaluation comparability and reproducibility gaps. The WebQSP relation detection dataset is constructed by the authors and “will be released.” Without public availability at review time, reproducibility is hampered. End-to-end comparisons rely on different entity linkers across works; while this is common, more controlled comparisons (same linker + same candidate budgets) and reporting of entity linking improvements due to the re-ranking step would strengthen claims. Statistical significance is only partially reported and the test setup is not fully described.
- Heuristic constraint detection and search limitations. The constraint module uses character overlap heuristics (with special handling for dates/types), which can be brittle and language-dependent. The pipeline uses top-1 beam for query generation, increasing error propagation. Although results are strong, it’s unclear how much headroom remains with a learning-based constraint model or beam search; an ablation quantifying constraint detection contribution per dataset is only briefly summarized.

Questions to authors
- Loss function: Please provide the exact form of the ranking loss, including the margin γ and signs (is it max{0, γ − srel(r+; q) + srel(r−; q)}?). How are negative samples selected per question?
- Relation encoders: Do the word-level and relation-level encoders share parameters and embeddings? What exactly is initialized by “the final state representations of the word sequence”—is the relation-name LSTM initialized by the word-level relation LSTM for the same relation? How are unseen relation-name tokens handled (random init or composed from words)?
- Zero-shot and chain breakdown: Can you report relation detection accuracy on SimpleQuestions split by seen vs unseen relations (w.r.t. training) and on WebQSP by chain length (1 vs ≥2)? Also report end-to-end QA accuracy under these splits.
- Entity re-ranking: What is the average number of relations scored in Step 4.1 per question, and the computational cost? How sensitive are results to K, K′, l, and α? Please provide the improvement in top-1 entity linking accuracy due to re-ranking (overall and for ambiguous mentions).
- Query search and constraints: How many constraints are typically added, and how sensitive is performance to the threshold θ? Have you tried a beam over (entity, relation) pairs before constraint detection; if so, what is the trade-off?
- Reproducibility: Will you release the WebQSP relation detection dataset, code, and hyperparameters to reproduce the ablations and end-to-end results?
