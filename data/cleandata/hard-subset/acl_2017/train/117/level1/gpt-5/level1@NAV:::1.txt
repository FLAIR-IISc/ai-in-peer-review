Summary
The paper proposes a neural relation detection model for KBQA that performs hierarchical matching between a question and a relation represented at two granularities (word-level tokens from the relation name and relation-level IDs). The question is encoded by stacked BiLSTMs with residual connections to enable multi-level abstraction and easier optimization. The model, HR-BiLSTM, is integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline that uses relation scores to re-rank entity linker outputs and then to select relations (or relation chains), optionally followed by constraint detection. Experiments on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP show 2–3% gains in relation detection and corresponding 2–3% improvements in end-to-end QA, with extensive ablations.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical residual BiLSTM for question–relation matching that combines relation word-level and relation-level representations and leverages residual connections for stable multi-layer training.
2) A practical two-step KBQA pipeline where relation detection informs entity re-ranking, demonstrably improving downstream QA accuracy on both single- and multi-relation benchmarks.
3) An evaluation setup for relation detection on WebQSP (including relation-chain candidates) with thorough ablations isolating design choices.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains with strong baselines: HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection by 2–3% on both datasets and translates to similar end-task gains without complex joint inference, indicating practical value.
- Well-motivated multi-granularity design: Combining relation words and relation IDs addresses both zero-shot generalization and global semantics; ablations validate each component’s contribution.
- Residual training benefits demonstrated: Compared to weighted sums and attention, residual connections lead to better optimization and higher accuracy, especially on WebQSP where longer dependencies matter.
- Sensible pipeline impact: Relation-aware entity re-ranking directly mitigates entity ambiguity, a known KBQA bottleneck; removing it significantly hurts performance, underscoring integration benefits.
- Comprehensive ablations: Variants (no residuals, attention, CNN encoders, single-layer encoders, removal of components) provide convincing evidence for design choices.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: Using residual connections and multi-granularity representations adapts known ideas; the methodological contribution may be viewed as engineering rather than conceptual.
- Limited zero-shot analysis: While the approach targets unseen relations, there is no targeted evaluation split or metric isolating zero-shot performance beyond aggregate results.
- Dependency on external entity linker: Gains partly hinge on upstream linker quality; robustness to poorer linkers or joint training is not explored.
- Reproducibility concerns: The WebQSP relation detection construction is “to be released”; details on candidate pool sizes and preprocessing may affect comparability.
- Efficiency not reported: No runtime/memory analysis, despite deeper models and two-step scoring over large candidate sets.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide a dedicated zero-shot evaluation (e.g., held-out relation IDs) to quantify generalization?
- What are K, K′, and l sensitivities, and what is the computational cost per question?
- How are relation chains tokenized and limited in length at training/test time?
- Will you release code, data splits, and candidate generation scripts to ensure reproducibility?
