Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper tackles relation detection for KBQA, proposing a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: relation-name tokens (global) and word tokens (local). Residual connections across BiLSTM layers aim to ease training and enable hierarchical matching.
- The model is embedded in a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step use of relation detection: (i) relation-aware entity re-ranking of candidates from an off-the-shelf linker, and (ii) final relation (or core-chain) selection; an optional constraint detection step performs lightweight subgraph entity matching.
- Empirically, the approach yields state-of-the-art or competitive results on SimpleQuestions (single relation) and WebQSP (multi-relation), with 2–3% absolute gains in relation detection and corresponding end-to-end improvements.

Main contributions (ordered by strength)
1) A practical, strong relation detection architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines multi-granularity relation representations with residual hierarchical matching, improving accuracy on both SQ and WQ relation detection tasks.
2) A simple yet effective KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection to mitigate entity linking errors via relation-aware re-ranking, leading to state-of-the-art or comparable end-to-end accuracy without heavy joint inference or complex re-rankers.
3) Clear ablations showing the value of multi-granularity and residual connections, especially on multi-relation WebQSP.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results with careful ablations: HR-BiLSTM outperforms baselines by 2–3% absolute on relation detection (Table 2) and yields consistent end-to-end KBQA gains. The ablation suite is informative: removing relation-name or relation-word signals hurts; replacing residual combination with weighted sum or attention degrades; using CNN instead of BiLSTM for questions harms WebQSP, aligning with the intuition that LSTMs handle relation chains better.
- Addresses KBQA-specific challenges rather than generic RE: The paper motivates why KB relation detection differs (large label space, zero-shot relations, chains). Breaking relation names into words and retaining relation-name tokens balances generalization to unseen relations with the global signal of relation identities.
- Simple, general pipeline that reduces an important bottleneck: Entity re-ranking via relation detection is well-motivated and effective, especially given ambiguity in entity linking. Reported improvements when removing re-ranking corroborate its impact.
- Reasonable methodological choices and clarity: The hierarchical residual matching is conceptually simple and easy to implement. Figure 2 and Algorithm 1 help readability; the training objective and evaluation protocols are clear for the core tasks.
- Significance and potential for reuse: The model is modular, data- and task-agnostic, and should transfer to other KBQA settings that require robust relation matching, particularly with unseen relations and chains.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: Combining word-level and relation-level representations for relation detection has precedents (e.g., word/char models in KBQA). Residual connections between BiLSTM layers are a standard deep learning tool. The novelty lies mainly in a specific instantiation (residual hierarchical matching for question–relation alignment), which, while effective, feels like a measured engineering advance rather than a conceptual breakthrough.
- Limited analysis of key claims: Zero-shot motivation is strong, but there is no breakdown of performance on seen vs unseen relations in SQ or analysis by chain length in WQ. Similarly, the claim that residuals improve “training ease” is not substantiated beyond loss observations; learning curves, convergence speed, or gradient statistics would help.
- Reproducibility and dataset availability: The WebQSP relation detection dataset is “to be made available,” which blocks reproduction at review time. Several crucial implementation details are deferred to appendices (constraint detection specifics, thresholds), and code availability is not mentioned. Hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., K, K′, l, α, λ) and negative sampling/candidate set formation are under-specified.
- Comparisons and fairness: While the baselines are sensible (AMPCNN, BiCNN), it would strengthen the case to compare against stronger recent attention-based or sequence-to-set models for relation detection on WQ, and to ensure uniform entity linking across methods in end-to-end comparisons. The constraint detection module is rather simplistic (character overlap); since it is reported as crucial on WQ, a comparison to stronger constraint modules (e.g., STAGG’s) or an ablation quantifying its standalone contribution would clarify where the gains come from.
- Efficiency and scalability: The paper doesn’t report runtime, memory, or throughput. The re-ranking step requires scoring relations for all entities in the top-K from the linker, which could be large. Candidate chain enumeration strategy and practical limits (max length, CVT handling) need clarification.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot analysis: What is the accuracy on unseen relations vs seen relations in SQ? How much do relation-name tokens vs word tokens contribute in the zero-shot subset?
- Candidate generation: For WQ, what is the maximum relation chain length considered? How are chains enumerated (e.g., branching factor limits, CVT nodes)? Average number of candidate relations/chains per entity?
- Training details: Do you use dropout, layer norm, or other regularization? Are word embeddings fine-tuned? How are negatives sampled in the ranking loss—full candidate set or sampled batch negatives?
- Sensitivity: Please report sensitivity to K, K′, l, and α in entity re-ranking, and λ in query scoring. How robust are results to these choices?
- Initialization: You state the relation sequence LSTM is initialized from the word sequence final state as a back-off. What is the effect of removing this initialization?
- Constraint detection: Beyond character overlap, did you try token-level or embedding-based matching? How many questions in WQ benefit from constraints, and what is the precision/recall of detected constraints?
- Efficiency: Provide training/inference time and memory footprint, and average time per question for the end-to-end system.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written, thorough empirical paper with a strong practical contribution to KBQA relation detection and a simple pipeline that yields state-of-the-art results. The methodological novelty is moderate, but the problem framing, ablations, and demonstrated impact justify publication. With additional analyses on zero-shot performance, chain-length effects, and reproducibility details (dataset/code), the paper would be even stronger.
