Summary and Contributions
This paper targets relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA), a setting with thousands of relations, zero-shot relations, and multi-relation chains. The core technical proposal is a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions against relation representations at two granularities: (i) word-level (tokenized relation name words) for generalization to unseen relations, and (ii) relation-level (entire relation names as tokens) for capturing global cues. Residual connections over a deep BiLSTM question encoder are used to make hierarchical matching trainable and complementary across layers. The model is embedded in a simple KBQA pipeline that uses relation detection both to re-rank entity linker outputs and to select the best relation/chain, plus a lightweight constraint-detection heuristic over a subgraph.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, effective hierarchical matching architecture for relation detection that combines relation word-level and relation-level views with residual connections over deep BiLSTMs, yielding consistent gains on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP relation detection.
2) A two-step KBQA pipeline where relation detection improves entity disambiguation and core-relation selection, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on SimpleQuestions and competitive results on WebQSP without joint inference or heavy re-ranking.
3) An evaluation setup for relation detection on WebQSP (constructing candidate relations/chains per topic entity), with ablations (granularity, residual vs attention, CNN vs LSTM) that illuminate design choices.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains with clear ablations. On relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves 2–3% over strong baselines (e.g., 93.3 vs 91.3 on SimpleQuestions; 82.53 vs 79.32 on WebQSP). Ablations show both representation granularities matter (drops when removing relation words or relation names), and residual connections outperform weighted sum and attention alternatives (e.g., 82.53 vs 80.65/81.38 on WebQSP). These results convincingly support the architectural choices.
- Addresses KBQA-specific challenges. Representing relations at the word level directly targets zero-shot generalization (unseen relation IDs), while preserving relation-level global cues combats spurious local matches (e.g., “play” vs “starring roles”). Deep LSTMs and residual hierarchical matching are well motivated for capturing multi-length phrasal cues and relation chains (WebQSP).
- Practical impact on end-to-end KBQA. The relation detector is used twice: first to re-rank entity candidates by collocational consistency with high-scoring relations, and then to select relations conditioned on the entity (with <e> marker). This pragmatic design improves the bottleneck of entity linking, and the end-task gains (reported as 2–3% over a strong AMPCNN/BiCNN-based pipeline, and SOTA on SimpleQuestions) demonstrate utility beyond standalone relation detection.
- Simplicity and portability. The method avoids highly specialized parsing or heavy feature engineering. The constraint detection module is lightweight. The overall pipeline should be easy to reproduce and to integrate with different linkers and KBs, given only pretrained word embeddings and standard BiLSTMs.
- Insightful comparisons. The paper contrasts CNN vs LSTM encoders for relation chains and finds LSTMs better on WebQSP, consistent with longer dependencies. It also compares hierarchical residual matching against decomposable attention, which underperforms here, suggesting the hierarchical residual design is a better fit for short sequences and pooled similarity.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty. Combining relation word-level and relation-level representations is intuitive and has antecedents (e.g., word-level relations in Yin et al., Yih et al., Liang et al.). Residual connections between BiLSTM layers are a direct adaptation of known ideas. The technical novelty is thus modest; the strength of the paper is more in careful engineering and ablations than in a fundamentally new model class.
- Zero-shot claim not directly evaluated. While SimpleQuestions contains unseen relations, the paper does not isolate a zero-shot split or report performance specifically on unseen-relation test cases. This weakens the claim that word-level relation encoding solves zero-shot generalization; a breakdown on seen vs unseen would strengthen the argument.
- Limited comparative breadth and detail. Some contemporaneous baselines are cited but not compared directly (e.g., character-level seq2seq relation models on SimpleQuestions). For WebQSP, relation detection is evaluated on a constructed task; more detail is needed to ensure fairness and comparability (candidate pool sizes, chain lengths). For end-to-end QA, it’s unclear whether all systems use the same entity linker outputs and KB snapshots.
- Heuristic constraint detection and lack of joint modeling. The constraint module relies on character-overlap and special rules for dates/types. While effective here, it may be brittle and domain-specific. The pipeline processes top-1 queries for constraint attachment, exacerbating error propagation; no beam or global re-ranking is used. This limits generality and may leave performance on multi-constraint questions on the table.
- Missing training and significance details. Some formulas are underspecified (e.g., the margin in the ranking loss), and hyperparameters (K, K’, l, α, λ, threshold θ) and their tuned values are not reported. Reported p-values lack details on test methodology and variance across runs. Runtime and model size are not discussed.

Questions to Authors
- Zero-shot analysis: What is the accuracy on test relations unseen during training on SimpleQuestions (and, if applicable, WebQSP)? How do the two granularities contribute on seen vs unseen splits?
- Residual variant: Which residual connection variant (hidden-state vs pooled) is used in the main results? Are the gains consistent across random seeds?
- Candidate pools: For WebQSP relation detection, what are the average sizes of candidate relations/chains per question, and the max chain length considered? How are negatives sampled?
- Pipeline hyperparameters: What values were selected for K, K′, l, α (entity re-ranking), λ (query fusion), and θ (constraint threshold)? How sensitive are results to these?
- Comparisons: Can you add a direct comparison to character-level seq2seq relation prediction on SimpleQuestions and to the latest WebQSP systems with joint inference?
- Resources: Will you release code and the WebQSP relation detection dataset to ensure reproducibility?

Additional Comments
- The loss function in Section 3.2 seems to omit the margin term; please clarify. 
- Reporting per-question-type breakdowns (single vs multi-hop; presence of dates/numbers) and an error analysis would add insight into where hierarchical residual matching helps most.
- A brief note on computational cost (training time, inference latency) would aid adoption.
