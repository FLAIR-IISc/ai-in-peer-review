**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a novel neural architecture for relation detection in the context of Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The core of the contribution is the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), a model designed to match questions with KB relations by considering relations at two granularities: as a sequence of words and as a single relation-name token. The question is encoded using a deep, two-layer BiLSTM, and the representations from both layers are combined via a residual connection to capture different levels of abstraction for matching. The authors embed this powerful relation detector into a simple but effective KBQA pipeline. This pipeline uniquely uses the relation detector in two stages: first, to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates (improving entity linking), and second, to identify the correct relation for the refined entity candidates. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach with state-of-the-art results on both the relation detection sub-task and the end-to-end KBQA task for two standard benchmarks: SimpleQuestions and WebQSP.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A novel and effective neural model (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection.** The key innovation lies in the hierarchical matching framework, which represents relations at both word and name levels, and uses a deep BiLSTM with residual connections to create multi-level question representations. The ablation studies provide strong evidence that this specific combination of ideas is superior to simpler alternatives.
2.  **A practical method for improving entity linking via relation detection.** The paper introduces a simple yet powerful two-step KBQA pipeline where the relation detector is first used to re-rank entity candidates before being used for its primary purpose. This demonstrates how a strong downstream component can effectively resolve ambiguity in an upstream task, a valuable insight for pipeline-based systems.
3.  **State-of-the-art empirical results on two distinct KBQA benchmarks.** The proposed system achieves excellent performance on both a single-relation task (SimpleQuestions) and a more complex multi-relation task (WebQSP), demonstrating the model's robustness and generalizability.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Novel Model Architecture:** The HR-BiLSTM architecture is thoughtfully designed. The motivation to use both word-level and relation-level representations is clear: one provides generalization to unseen relations, while the other captures global context. The application of residual connections from computer vision to this NLP sequence matching problem is a novel and impactful contribution. The authors' hypothesis that it simplifies training and encourages complementary representations in the deep BiLSTM is well-supported by the strong empirical gains, especially on the more complex WebQSP dataset.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimental Validation:** The paper's claims are backed by a comprehensive set of experiments. The evaluation is performed on two standard and diverse benchmarks, which shows the method is not over-tuned to a single task type. The ablation studies in Table 2 are particularly compelling, as they systematically dissect the proposed model and justify each major design choice (the necessity of both relation views, the superiority of residual learning over weighted sums or attention, and the choice of BiLSTMs over CNNs). This level of rigor significantly strengthens the paper's conclusions.
3.  **High-Impact System Design:** The KBQA pipeline, while simple, is very effective. The entity re-ranking step is a clever technique that directly addresses a well-known bottleneck in KBQA systems: entity ambiguity. By showing that a strong relation detector can improve entity linking, the paper provides a practical blueprint for building better KBQA systems. The fact that this relatively straightforward pipeline (without complex feature engineering or joint decoding) achieves state-of-the-art results underscores the power of the core relation detection model.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Analysis of the Constraint Detection Module:** The paper reports that the constraint detection step is "crucial" for performance on WebQSP, boosting accuracy from 58.0% to 66.8%. However, this module is described very briefly as a simple text matching method based on "maximum overlapping sequence of characters". Given its massive impact on the final score, this component feels underdeveloped and under-analyzed compared to the core relation detection model. The simplicity of this final step may also be a weak link in the overall system, and its performance characteristics (e.g., sensitivity to the matching threshold) are not discussed.
2.  **Insufficient Explanation for the Effectiveness of Residual Learning:** The paper claims that residual learning helps because the second layer fits the "residues from the first layer of matching" and makes the vector spaces comparable. While this intuition is borrowed from the original ResNet paper, there is no specific analysis in this paper to confirm this is happening for this NLP task. A deeper analysis, for example through visualization of the representations from each layer or an analysis of the training dynamics, would have provided more concrete evidence for *why* simple addition outperforms more complex, learned combinations like attention in this context.
3.  **The KBQA Pipeline Structure Lacks Novelty:** While the *use* of the relation detector within the pipeline is innovative, the overall staged architecture (Entity Linking -> Relation Detection -> Constraint Handling) is standard for KBQA systems (e.g., Yih et al., 2015). The paper's main contribution lies squarely in the relation detection component and its dual application, not in a new paradigm for KBQA system design. This is a minor weakness but should be acknowledged.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  In Section 3.2, you mention two ways of implementing the residual connection: summing the hidden states at each position `i` before max-pooling, or summing the max-pooled representations from each layer. Which of these two methods was used in the final experiments reported in the paper, and did you observe any significant performance difference between them?
2.  The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) uses the raw question text as input to the relation detector, while the main relation detection step (Section 4.2) uses a modified question where the entity mention is replaced with a special `<e>` token. What was the rationale for not using the `<e>` replacement during the entity re-ranking step as well, given that entity mention spans are available from the initial linker? Did you experiment with this and find it performed worse?
