### Summary of the paper
This paper introduces a novel neural network model, the Hierarchical Residual Bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for the task of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The model's key insight is to perform a hierarchical match between a question and a candidate KB relation. It represents relations at two granularities (word-level and full relation name) and matches them against question representations learned at different levels of abstraction by a deep, two-layer BiLSTM. To facilitate the training of this deep architecture and ensure the two layers are complementary, the model incorporates residual connections inspired by recent work in computer vision. The authors then integrate this powerful relation detector into a KBQA pipeline. A notable feature of this pipeline is a two-step application of the relation detector: first, it is used on the raw question to score relations associated with an initial set of entity candidates, thereby re-ranking the entities to improve disambiguation. Second, it is used to find the final relation for the top-ranked entities. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that their relation detection model and the full KBQA system achieve state-of-the-art performance on both the single-relation SimpleQuestions dataset and the multi-relation WebQSP dataset.

### Main contributions
1.  **A novel neural architecture for relation detection (HR-BiLSTM).** The primary contribution is a well-motivated model that performs hierarchical matching between questions and relations. The application of residual learning to facilitate the training of a deep BiLSTM for this matching task is a timely and effective methodological innovation for this problem.
2.  **A simple yet powerful KBQA system design that leverages an improved relation detector for entity disambiguation.** The paper demonstrates how a strong relation detection component can be used to address other bottlenecks in the KBQA pipeline, specifically entity linking ambiguity. The proposed entity re-ranking step, which uses relational context from the question to score entity candidates, is an effective and insightful addition to the standard pipeline.
3.  **State-of-the-art empirical results on two standard KBQA benchmarks.** The work provides strong evidence of its effectiveness by outperforming previous systems on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. The evaluation is thorough, assessing both the relation detection sub-task in isolation and the end-to-end KBQA performance, and is supported by a comprehensive set of ablation studies that validate the key components of the proposed model.

### Strengths
1.  **Novel and Effective Model Architecture.** The motivation for hierarchical matching is very clear: different parts of a relation name (individual words vs. the full phrase) may correspond to question phrases of varying lengths and abstraction levels. The proposed HR-BiLSTM architecture is a clever way to implement this. Using a deep BiLSTM to generate multi-level question representations is intuitive, but the authors correctly identify the potential training difficulty. The use of residual connections to overcome this is an elegant solution that is well-supported by the strong ablation results (e.g., HR-BiLSTM's 93.3% vs. 92.5% for the weighted sum model on SimpleQuestions, and a more significant 82.5% vs. 80.7% on WebQSP). This demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of deep neural network design.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation.** The paper sets a high standard for evaluation. By testing on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, it shows the model's robustness on tasks with different characteristics (single vs. multi-relation, more vs. less zero-shot relations). The decision to evaluate the relation detection module independently (Table 2) is excellent, as it isolates the contribution of the core model. The subsequent end-to-end KBQA evaluation (Table 3) then clearly demonstrates the downstream impact of this improved module. The ablation studies are particularly convincing, systematically dissecting the model to prove the value of each component (hierarchical inputs, residual learning, deep architecture, and LSTM vs. CNN choice).

3.  **Insightful Integration of Relation Detection and Entity Linking.** The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is a significant strength. Entity linking is a well-known bottleneck for KBQA systems. By using the relation detector to provide a contextual signal for entity disambiguation *before* committing to a single topic entity, the system can recover from initial linker errors. This demonstrates a synergistic system design where components mutually reinforce each other. The ablation results confirm the importance of this step, with performance on SimpleQuestions dropping from 79.1% to 77.2% without it.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Technical Detail on Constraint Detection.** The constraint detection module is crucial for performance on the WebQSP dataset, yet it is described quite sparsely in Section 4.4. The method is presented as a simple text matching approach based on character overlap, which seems underdeveloped compared to the sophisticated neural relation detector. The paper notes that their system's performance on topic entity and core-chain detection is high, leaving potential for the constraint module to improve results. However, this also makes it difficult to assess how much of the final SOTA performance on WebQSP is attributable to this heuristic-based final step versus the core relation detection model. More detail and analysis of this component's behavior and error patterns would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Lack of Discussion on Efficiency.** The proposed KBQA pipeline involves running the HR-BiLSTM model twice. The first step, entity re-ranking, requires scoring all relations associated with the top-K initial entity candidates. For entities with many relations, this could be computationally expensive. The paper does not provide any analysis of the inference time or computational complexity of the system compared to prior work like STAGG. While accuracy is paramount, practical applicability also depends on efficiency, making this an important omission.

3.  **Potential Overstatement of Simplicity.** The paper frames its KBQA system as "simple" and involving "minimal efforts beyond the training of the relation detection model." While the pipeline is conceptually straightforward, it still relies on several external components and carefully tuned steps: an initial entity linker, a two-step application of the core model, a separate constraint detection module, and several hyperparameters (`alpha`, `beta`, `l`, `K`, `K'`) that govern the combination of different scores. This framing slightly undersells the engineering effort required and the number of moving parts in the final system.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The ablation study shows that residual learning and the deep architecture provide a much larger benefit on WebQSP than on SimpleQuestions. Your hypothesis is that WebQSP requires matching larger context scopes. Could you provide a more detailed analysis to support this? For example, can you visualize or provide examples showing that the two layers of the question encoder are learning qualitatively different types of representations that are useful for the more complex questions in WebQSP?

2.  The entity re-ranking step seems critical. How sensitive is its performance to the choice of the initial entity linker and the initial beam size `K`? For instance, if a less accurate entity linker is used, can the re-ranking step still recover, or is its effectiveness highly dependent on the topic entity being present in the initial top-K candidates?
