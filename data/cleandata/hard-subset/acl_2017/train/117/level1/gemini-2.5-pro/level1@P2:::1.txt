**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), a challenging problem due to the large number of relations and the need to generalize to unseen relations. The authors propose a novel neural architecture, the Hierarchical Residual Bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for this task. The model's key idea is to perform hierarchical matching between a question and a candidate relation. It represents relations at both the word-level (for generalization) and the full relation name-level (for global context). Correspondingly, it uses a deep, two-layer BiLSTM to generate hierarchical representations of the question. Crucially, it employs residual connections to combine the representations from the two BiLSTM layers, which is shown to facilitate training and improve performance. The authors then integrate this improved relation detector into a simple yet effective KBQA pipeline. A notable feature of this pipeline is an entity re-ranking step, where the relation detector is first used on the raw question to disambiguate and re-rank initial entity candidates before final relation detection. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on both the standalone relation detection task and the end-to-end KBQA task for two standard benchmarks: SimpleQuestions (single-relation) and WebQSP (multi-relation).

**Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Matching Model with Residual Learning:** The primary contribution is the HR-BiLSTM architecture. The model is well-motivated, addressing the trade-off between generalization (word-level) and context (relation-level) in representing KB relations. The application of deep LSTMs to create hierarchical question representations and, most importantly, the use of a residual connection to effectively combine these layers for matching is a novel and empirically successful architectural contribution to sequence matching tasks.

2.  **A Simple and Effective KBQA System Showcasing Model Utility:** The paper presents a KBQA pipeline that demonstrates how a superior relation detection model can significantly improve the overall system. The proposed entity re-ranking step, which leverages the relation detector to disambiguate entity candidates, is a simple but powerful technique that effectively addresses the common entity linking bottleneck. Achieving state-of-the-art results with this relatively straightforward pipeline (lacking complex joint inference or feature-based re-rankers) underscores the strength and practical value of the core relation detection model.

**Strengths**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The paper reports excellent results that are a clear strength. The proposed HR-BiLSTM model significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on the isolated relation detection task for both SimpleQuestions (+2.0% accuracy) and WebQSP (+3.2% accuracy). This strong performance translates to the end-to-end KBQA task, where the system achieves new state-of-the-art results on SimpleQuestions and is highly competitive on the more complex WebQSP dataset.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Design:** The core intuition behind the HR-BiLSTM is compelling. The idea of matching different granularities of the relation (words vs. full name) with different levels of abstraction from the question (lower vs. upper LSTM layers) is elegant. Using residual connections to enable this hierarchical matching is a clever adaptation of an idea from computer vision that appears to solve a real training/optimization problem, as evidenced by its superiority over naive summation or attention mechanisms.

3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of ablation experiments in Table 2. These experiments systematically validate the design choices of the HR-BiLSTM model. They convincingly demonstrate that each key component—the hierarchical view of relations (both words and names), the deep architecture, and especially the residual learning mechanism—contributes positively to the final performance. This rigorous analysis greatly strengthens the paper's conclusions.

4.  **Practical System-Level Insight:** The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is a valuable contribution in its own right. It shows a practical way to create a feedback loop where a downstream module (relation detection) helps correct errors from an upstream one (entity linking). The significant performance drop when this step is removed (Table 3) highlights its importance and demonstrates a clear path for improving pipelined KBQA systems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity on Constraint Detection:** The constraint detection component (Section 4.4), which is critical for the strong results on the multi-relation WebQSP dataset, is described very briefly with technical details deferred to an appendix. The description of "text matching" and "entity-linking on a KB sub-graph" is vague. For a component responsible for lifting performance from 58.0% to 60.7% (a substantial portion of the overall gain over baselines), its implementation, heuristics, and parameter tuning should be more clearly explained in the main body of the paper to allow for proper assessment and reproducibility.

2.  **Potentially Misleading Terminology ("Residual Learning"):** The use of the term "residual learning" might be slightly imprecise. In the original ResNet paper (He et al., 2016), a layer learns a function F(x) to be added to its input x, effectively learning a residual to an identity mapping. Here, the output of the second BiLSTM, which takes the first layer's output as input (h_q^(2) = LSTM(h_q^(1))), is added to the first layer's output (h_q = h_q^(1) + h_q^(2)). While this is a shortcut connection that clearly helps, the analogy to learning a residual is not direct. A more precise description of the mechanism or a clearer justification for the terminology would improve the paper's technical clarity.

3.  **Reproducibility Concerns:** The experiments rely on specific entity linking outputs from previous work and a newly created relation detection dataset from WebQSP. The authors state that the WebQSP dataset "will be made publicly available soon," which is a positive sign. However, for a conference submission, the unavailability of key resources at the time of review makes it difficult to verify the results and hinders immediate reproducibility by the research community.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide more detail on the constraint detection mechanism? Specifically, what is the exact function used for "maximum overlapping sequence of characters," and how is the threshold θ tuned? Given its crucial role in the WebQSP results, a clearer explanation would be very helpful.

2.  Regarding the additive combination of representations from the two BiLSTM layers (h_q = h_q^(1) + h_q^(2)): a) Did you experiment with other parameter-free combinations? b) Why do you think this simple addition is so much more effective than the attention mechanism you tested, especially on the more complex WebQSP dataset?

3.  The best end-to-end results reported in the final row of Table 3 are from an ensemble of the "top-3 relation detectors". Could you clarify which three models from Table 2 were included in this ensemble and how their scores were combined to produce the final prediction?
