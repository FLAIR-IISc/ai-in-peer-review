### Summary and Contributions
This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The authors propose a novel neural network model, the Hierarchical Residual Bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM), designed to match natural language questions with KB relations. The model's key idea is to perform a hierarchical match: it creates multi-granularity representations for relations (both at the word-level and the full relation-name level) and matches them against multi-level abstractions of the question generated by a deep residual BiLSTM. The paper also presents a simple yet effective KBQA system that uses this relation detector in a two-step process: first, to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates to improve entity linking, and second, to detect the final relation for the top-ranked entities. The system is evaluated on two standard benchmarks, SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, achieving state-of-the-art results.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective relation detection model (HR-BiLSTM) that leverages hierarchical representations of both questions and relations, cleverly combined using residual connections to facilitate training and improve performance.
2.  A well-designed KBQA pipeline that demonstrates how a strong relation detector can be used not only for its primary task but also to significantly improve the upstream entity linking sub-task through an entity re-ranking mechanism.
3.  Strong empirical validation on both single-relation and multi-relation KBQA datasets, with thorough ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model components and system design.

### Strengths
1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Model Architecture.** The motivation for the HR-BiLSTM is very clear. The paper correctly identifies that representing KB relations solely by their full names suffers from data sparsity (especially for unseen relations), while using only constituent words loses global context. The proposed solution of using both representations hierarchically is elegant and intuitive. Furthermore, the application of residual connections to combine the different layers of the question encoder is a clever adaptation from computer vision that addresses the training difficulty of deep, multi-level matching models. The ablation study in Table 2 convincingly shows that both the hierarchical representation and the residual learning contribute significantly to the model's success.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation.** The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on two widely used and distinct KBQA benchmarks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), demonstrating the model's robustness for both single-relation and more complex multi-relation questions. The experiments are well-designed, comparing against strong and relevant baselines. The extensive ablation studies are a major strength, providing clear evidence for each of the core claims: the benefit of hierarchical representations (word + relation name), the advantage of residual learning over simpler aggregation or attention, and the superiority of LSTMs over CNNs for this task, particularly on the more complex WebQSP dataset.

3.  **Effective System-Level Integration.** Beyond proposing a strong standalone model, the paper demonstrates a powerful way to integrate it into a full KBQA system. The idea of using the relation detector for entity re-ranking is a significant contribution. It provides a practical solution to the common problem of entity linking ambiguity by using the relational context of the question to disambiguate entity candidates. The ablation result in Table 3 ("Our System w/o entity re-ranking") confirms the substantial impact of this step, highlighting a strong synergy between the entity linking and relation detection components. This makes the overall system more than just the sum of its parts.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Clarity on Implementation Details.** Some key implementation details are underspecified, which could hinder reproducibility.
    *   In Section 3.2, two methods for implementing residual connections are proposed: adding hidden states before max-pooling, or adding the max-pooled vectors. The paper mentions this was tuned as a hyperparameter, but it never states which method was used in the final model or if one consistently outperformed the other.
    *   The constraint detection module (Section 4.4) is described very briefly as a text matching approach based on "maximum overlapping sequence of characters". Given its crucial importance for the final performance on WebQSP (as shown in Table 3), this description is insufficient. More detail, perhaps an algorithm or a concrete example, would be necessary for a clear understanding.

2.  **Scalability of the Entity Re-ranking Step.** The proposed entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) scores all relations associated with the initial top-K entity candidates. For entities with a high degree in the knowledge base and a large K, this step could become a computational bottleneck. While this is likely not an issue for the datasets used, a brief discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability concerns of this approach would strengthen the paper. For instance, what were the typical values for K and the number of relations scored per question?

3.  **Limited Discussion on Error Analysis.** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper would benefit from a qualitative error analysis. What are the most common failure cases for the HR-BiLSTM model or the full KBQA system? For example, does it struggle with questions requiring very deep lexical or syntactic reasoning? Does the entity re-ranking step ever demote the correct entity? Such an analysis would provide deeper insight into the model's limitations and offer valuable directions for future work.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify which of the two residual connection methods described in Section 3.2 (summing hidden states vs. summing max-pooled outputs) was used for the final experiments? Was there a notable performance difference between the two?
2.  The entity re-ranking step seems potentially expensive. Could you comment on the runtime impact of this step and the trade-off between the initial number of candidates (K) and the final performance? For instance, how much does accuracy drop if K is reduced?
