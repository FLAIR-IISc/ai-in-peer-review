**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual Bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for matching questions to KB relations. The key ideas behind the model are (1) representing relations at two levels of granularity (word-level and full relation name) to handle both unseen relations and global context, and (2) using a deep BiLSTM with residual connections to create hierarchical question representations that can effectively match the different relation granularities. The paper also introduces a simple but effective KBQA system that uses this relation detector in a two-step process: first, to re-rank entity candidates from an initial linker, and second, to identify the correct relation for the top-ranked entities. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task for two standard benchmarks: SimpleQuestions (single-relation) and WebQSP (multi-relation).

**Contributions**

1.  A novel and well-motivated neural architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection that effectively combines multi-granularity relation representations with a hierarchical question encoder enhanced by residual learning. This model demonstrates significant improvements over strong baselines on its own.
2.  A simple and effective KBQA pipeline that integrates the proposed relation detector to improve a key bottleneck: entity ambiguity. The proposed entity re-ranking step, which uses relation detection scores to disambiguate entities, is a valuable contribution to pipeline-based KBQA systems.
3.  State-of-the-art empirical results on two widely-used and distinct KBQA benchmarks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), backed by a thorough set of ablation studies that validate the contributions of the different components of the proposed model and system.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated model:** The core HR-BiLSTM model is well-designed. The motivation for using both word-level and relation-level views is clearly explained and addresses a key trade-off between generalization (for unseen relations) and specificity (for ambiguous cases). The adaptation of residual connections from computer vision to facilitate this hierarchical matching is clever and the ablation studies show it is effective, particularly on the more complex WebQSP dataset.
2.  **Strong empirical results and analysis:** The paper presents compelling results, achieving state-of-the-art on two important benchmarks. The improvements are not marginal and are demonstrated both for the relation detection sub-task and the full KBQA system. The ablation studies in Table 2 are particularly strong, systematically dissecting the model to show the impact of each component (hierarchical views, residual learning, LSTM vs. CNN). This provides convincing evidence for the authors' claims.
3.  **Effective system design:** The proposed KBQA system demonstrates how a powerful component can elevate a simple pipeline. The entity re-ranking step is an elegant idea that uses the relation detector to address the known problem of entity linking ambiguity. This two-step application of the relation detector is a key factor in the system's success, as shown by the ablation study in Table 3.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and the proposed methods are described clearly. The authors' plan to release the newly created WebQSP relation detection dataset is commendable and will benefit future research.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of detail on computational feasibility:** The paper is unclear about how the candidate relations are generated and scored, particularly in the entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1). For the top-K entity candidates, the set of all associated relations in a large KB like Freebase could be enormous. Scoring all of them would be computationally very expensive. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how this is handled in practice (e.g., pruning strategies, average number of candidates). This detail is important for understanding the practicality of the approach.
2.  **Simplicity of the KBQA pipeline:** While the simplicity of the KBQA system is a strength, it also means that some comparisons to prior work may not be perfectly direct. For instance, the state-of-the-art STAGG system for WebQSP uses beam search and a feature-based re-ranker, which this system omits. While the proposed system's performance is excellent, it is heavily reliant on the quality of the relation detector. The constraint detection module, in particular, is described as a simple text matching approach, which may not be robust for more complex constraints.
3.  **Details of the ensemble model are missing:** The final, best-performing model mentioned in the last row of Table 3 is an ensemble of the top-3 relation detectors. However, the paper does not specify which three models were used or how their outputs were combined. This information is crucial for reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the practical implementation of the relation detection steps, especially regarding the size of the candidate relation set? Specifically, in the entity re-ranking step (Sec 4.1), what is the typical number of relations scored per question? Are there any filtering mechanisms applied before scoring?
2.  The paper achieves state-of-the-art on WebQSP with a simpler pipeline than prior work like STAGG (e.g., no beam search). Does this suggest that relation detection accuracy was the primary performance bottleneck in previous systems, and that a sufficiently powerful relation detector can compensate for less complex downstream components?
3.  For the ensemble model that achieves the best results in Table 3, could you please specify which three relation detector models from Section 5.2 were used and how their scores were combined to produce the final prediction?
