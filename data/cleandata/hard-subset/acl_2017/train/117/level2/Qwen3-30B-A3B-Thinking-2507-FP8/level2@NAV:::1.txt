

This paper proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for knowledge base relation detection, addressing key challenges in KBQA such as large relation sets, unseen relations, and multi-relation chains. The main contributions are: (1) a hierarchical matching approach that combines relation-level and word-level representations for improved relation detection; and (2) demonstration that this improved relation detector enables a simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP benchmarks.

Strengths: The paper makes a strong case for the necessity of specialized relation detection for KBQA, correctly identifying key differences from general relation extraction tasks. The HR-BiLSTM model achieves statistically significant improvements (2-3%) over strong baselines on both relation detection and KBQA tasks, with comprehensive ablation studies demonstrating the importance of hierarchical matching and residual learning. The integration of relation detection with KBQA through entity re-ranking and relation detection steps is practical and well-motivated.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for why residual learning specifically benefits this task beyond claiming it "makes training easier." The KBQA system described is quite simple, and the paper doesn't thoroughly analyze when entity re-ranking is most beneficial or how the approach scales to more complex KBQA scenarios. The comparison is limited to older neural baselines, omitting recent transformer-based relation detection methods that might set a stronger benchmark. Additionally, the paper doesn't adequately address how the model handles extremely rare relations beyond the 14% unseen relation rate in SimpleQuestions.

The work is well-executed and makes a meaningful contribution to KBQA, but would be strengthened by deeper analysis of the residual learning mechanism, more comprehensive comparison with recent approaches, and extended discussion of scalability to larger relation sets. The authors should also clarify how their approach might handle relations with significantly different semantic structures.
