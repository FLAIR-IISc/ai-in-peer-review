

**Summary of Contributions**  
The paper proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), addressing key challenges in KB-specific relation detection (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, multi-relation chains). The main contributions are: (1) a hierarchical matching approach that combines word-level and relation-level representations for better generalization to unseen relations; (2) a residual learning mechanism that improves training stability and enables deeper hierarchical matching; and (3) a simple KBQA system that integrates relation detection with entity re-ranking, achieving state-of-the-art results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP.

**Strengths**  
1. **Well-motivated approach to KBQA-specific challenges**: The paper clearly identifies critical differences between general relation extraction and KBQA relation detection (large relation sets, zero-shot learning, multi-relation chains), which justifies the need for their proposed model. The hierarchical matching approach directly addresses the trade-off between word-level granularity (better generalization) and relation-level global information (better semantic matching), a problem not adequately handled by prior work.

2. **Strong empirical results with thorough ablation studies**: The HR-BiLSTM model achieves significant improvements (2-3%) over strong baselines on both relation detection tasks (SimpleQuestions: 93.3% vs. 91.2% for best baseline; WebQSP: 82.53% vs. 79.32%). The ablation studies (Table 2) convincingly demonstrate that both hierarchical matching and residual learning are essential components—removing either substantially degrades performance, particularly on WebQSP.

3. **Effective KBQA system design**: The two-step relation detection framework (entity re-ranking followed by relation detection) is simple yet powerful. The entity re-ranking step is particularly novel—it leverages relation detection to resolve entity ambiguity, a critical bottleneck in KBQA. The system achieves state-of-the-art results without complex joint inference or feature-based re-ranking, which is impressive given the simplicity of the approach.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient evidence for zero-shot capability**: The paper claims to handle zero-shot learning (noting 14% of test relations in SimpleQuestions are unseen during training), but the evaluation does not specifically isolate and quantify zero-shot performance. The reported results (Table 2) are for the standard test set, which includes both seen and unseen relations. Without zero-shot-specific metrics, the claim about handling unseen relations lacks empirical substantiation.

2. **Limited comparison to the most recent work**: The paper compares to AMPCNN (Yin et al., 2016) as the best baseline on SimpleQuestions, but fails to discuss how their model compares to the 2016 WebQSP state-of-the-art (Yih et al., 2016). The WebQSP results in Table 3 are compared to STAGG (Yih et al., 2015), which is not the most recent work. This omission weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" results for WebQSP.

3. **Lack of analysis on model limitations**: The paper does not discuss potential failure cases or scalability limits. For example, how does the model perform on questions with complex syntactic structures (e.g., negation, multiple clauses)? How does it scale with extremely large KBs (e.g., Freebase with >10k relations)? The absence of such analysis makes it difficult to assess the real-world applicability of the approach.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Could the authors provide zero-shot evaluation metrics (e.g., accuracy on only unseen relations) to better substantiate the claim about handling unseen relations in KBQA?  
2. The WebQSP relation detection task is created from the original dataset by selecting "all relations and relation chains connecting to the topic entity." Could the authors clarify why this setup is appropriate for evaluating relation detection in KBQA, and how it differs from prior relation detection tasks on WebQSP?  
3. The paper states that constraint detection is "crucial" for their system, yet STAGG (Yih et al., 2015) reports it as optional. What specific aspects of their relation detection model make constraint detection more critical for their approach?
