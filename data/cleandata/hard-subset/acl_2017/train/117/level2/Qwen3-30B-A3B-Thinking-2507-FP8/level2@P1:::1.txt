

Summary of the paper: The paper proposes a hierarchical residual bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for knowledge base relation detection in KBQA systems. The authors identify key challenges specific to KBQA relation detection (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, relation chains) and propose a model that uses both relation-level and word-level representations with residual learning for hierarchical matching. They demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art relation detection performance on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, which in turn leads to improved KBQA results.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. A novel hierarchical residual BiLSTM model that effectively combines relation-level and word-level representations for KB relation detection
2. Comprehensive empirical evidence showing that improved relation detection directly enhances KBQA performance on both single-relation and multi-relation benchmarks
3. Clear articulation of the differences between general relation extraction and KB-specific relation detection, which guides their technical approach

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a well-motivated contribution to a critical subtask in KBQA. The authors correctly identify that existing relation extraction methods don't address the specific challenges of KBQA (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, relation chains), which is a significant gap in the literature.

2. The hierarchical matching approach with residual learning is well-justified and empirically validated through extensive ablation studies. The results in Table 2 clearly show that each component (hierarchical matching, residual learning) contributes to the overall improvement, with the full model outperforming strong baselines by 2-3% on both datasets.

3. The paper effectively demonstrates the end-to-end value of their relation detection model by showing how it improves the KBQA system. The entity re-ranking step is particularly clever and well-motivated, addressing a real bottleneck in KBQA systems (entity linking ambiguities).

4. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors carefully compare against relevant baselines, provide ablation studies, and validate their approach on two distinct benchmarks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) with different characteristics.

5. The paper is clearly written with good organization, making it easy to follow the technical contributions and experimental results.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how much of the KBQA performance improvement is directly attributable to the relation detection model versus the entity re-ranking step. While the authors state that the entity re-ranking relies on the relation detection model, it's not entirely clear whether the re-ranking mechanism itself is the primary driver of the improvement. More detailed ablation on this specific aspect would strengthen the claims.

2. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the computational complexity of the proposed approach. The hierarchical residual BiLSTM with multiple layers and residual connections likely increases training and inference time compared to simpler models. It would be valuable to include a brief discussion of computational costs.

3. The paper claims "state-of-the-art" results but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with all relevant recent work. For example, the paper doesn't compare against the neural symbolic machines approach (Liang et al., 2016) which also achieves strong results on KBQA. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's claims.

4. The explanation for why residual learning specifically helps with hierarchical matching is somewhat superficial. While the paper shows that residual learning improves performance, it doesn't provide deeper analysis of how the residual connections enable better learning of hierarchical representations. More theoretical or empirical analysis would strengthen this aspect.

5. The paper doesn't discuss potential failure cases or limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the model handle questions with complex negation, sarcasm, or ambiguous language that might affect relation detection? Including a brief analysis of these cases would make the paper more comprehensive.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more details on the entity re-ranking step and its relationship to the relation detection model? Specifically, how much of the KBQA performance improvement is due to the relation detection model versus the re-ranking mechanism itself?

2. The paper mentions that SimpleQuestions suffers more from unseen relations than WebQSP. Could you elaborate on how the hierarchical matching approach specifically addresses this challenge, particularly for relations that don't appear in the training data?

3. The paper claims state-of-the-art results on both benchmarks, but the comparison with STAGG (Yih et al., 2015) on WebQSP isn't entirely fair since STAGG uses a more complex system with multiple relation detectors. How does your method compare to a fairer baseline that uses a single relation detector?
