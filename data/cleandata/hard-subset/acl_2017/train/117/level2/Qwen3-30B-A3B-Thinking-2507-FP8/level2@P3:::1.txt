

Summary: The paper proposes a hierarchical residual bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for knowledge base relation detection that addresses key challenges specific to KBQA, including large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation chains. The main contributions are: (i) a hierarchical matching approach that combines relation-level and word-level representations with residual learning for improved relation detection; (ii) a simple KBQA system that integrates the relation detector with entity linking to demonstrate practical benefits; and (iii) state-of-the-art results on both relation detection and KBQA end tasks.

Strengths:
1. The paper effectively identifies and addresses the specific challenges of KB relation detection compared to general relation extraction, providing a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed approach. The analysis of differences (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, multi-relation chains) is well-motivated and clearly justifies the need for a specialized model.

2. The hierarchical residual learning approach is thoroughly validated through comprehensive ablation studies. The results demonstrate that both the hierarchical matching (combining relation-level and word-level representations) and residual learning components are essential for the improved performance, with significant gains over baselines on both SimpleQuestions (93.3% vs. 91.2%) and WebQSP (82.53% vs. 79.32%).

3. The integration of the relation detector into the KBQA system is well-executed and demonstrates practical utility. The entity re-ranking step provides a clear mechanism for how relation detection improves entity linking, which is a key bottleneck in KBQA systems. The results show that this simple integration improves KBQA accuracy by 2-3% over baselines without requiring complex additional components.

4. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing, with results on both relation detection tasks and the KBQA end task. The paper provides detailed analysis of the model's behavior across different datasets, showing that SimpleQuestions benefits from relation-level representations while WebQSP benefits from hierarchical matching.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison with recent methods for relation detection is somewhat limited. While the paper references several recent works, it does not adequately address how the proposed approach compares to more sophisticated methods that might address the same challenges, such as those using pre-trained relation embeddings or more advanced attention mechanisms. This weakens the novelty claim.

2. The paper lacks sufficient analysis of why residual learning specifically outperforms other hierarchical approaches. While the ablation study shows that residual learning improves performance, the paper does not provide deeper insights into the learned representations or why the residual connections make training more effective, beyond stating that it "makes the model training easier."

3. The KBQA system described is relatively simple, and the paper does not demonstrate how the relation detection improvements would translate to more complex, state-of-the-art KBQA systems. The results show improvements over specific baselines, but it's unclear whether the approach would maintain its advantage in more sophisticated architectures.

4. The paper lacks analysis of failure cases or error patterns. Understanding the specific scenarios where the model fails would provide valuable insights into its limitations and guide future work, but this analysis is absent.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the proposed approach compare to recent methods that use pre-trained relation embeddings (e.g., from TransE) for relation detection in KBQA? Would combining these approaches yield further improvements?

2. Can the authors provide more details on the specific scenarios where the entity re-ranking step is most beneficial, particularly for questions with ambiguous entity mentions?

3. What is the computational cost of the proposed HR-BiLSTM model compared to simpler baselines, and how does this scale with larger relation vocabularies?
