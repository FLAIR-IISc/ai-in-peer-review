This paper addresses the challenging problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), particularly focusing on the issues of unseen relations and the large number of relations in typical KBs. The authors propose a hierarchical recurrent neural network with residual learning (HR-BiLSTM) to compare questions and relation names at different levels of abstraction. They also integrate this relation detector into a simple KBQA system and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) QA benchmarks.

The primary contribution of this work is the HR-BiLSTM model for relation detection. The idea of combining word-level and relation-level representations with residual connections to handle the challenges of KB relation detection (large vocabulary, unseen relations) is a solid and well-motivated approach. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of both the hierarchical matching and the residual learning components. 

A second important contribution is the demonstration that improved relation detection can significantly benefit a KBQA system, even with a relatively simple KBQA pipeline. The end-to-end evaluation on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP showcases the practical impact of their relation detection model.

Strengths:

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper reports state-of-the-art or competitive results on two widely used KBQA datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP). This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparisons with several baselines are thorough and highlight the advantages of HR-BiLSTM.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges specific to KB relation detection (large vocabulary, unseen relations) and provides a rationale for the design choices in their model. The hierarchical matching and residual learning mechanisms are well-justified as solutions to these challenges.
3. **Comprehensive Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies in Section 5.2 are particularly strong. They systematically evaluate the contribution of each component of the HR-BiLSTM model (hierarchical matching, residual learning, word-level vs. relation-level representations) and provide clear insights into their effectiveness.
4. **Practical KBQA Integration:** Integrating the improved relation detector into a complete KBQA system and demonstrating its benefits is a valuable contribution. This shows that the approach is not just effective in isolation but also translates to improved performance in a real-world application.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of hierarchical matching and residual learning is effective, the individual components themselves (BiLSTMs, residual connections) are not novel. The paper doesn't sufficiently highlight what is genuinely new about *how* these components are applied to the specific task of KB relation detection.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning Details:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but lacks detail about the search space and process. Understanding how sensitive the performance is to different hyperparameter settings would be helpful, especially given the complexity of the model.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on overall accuracy scores. A more detailed error analysis, identifying common failure cases and potential areas for improvement, would strengthen the paper.  For example, are there specific types of relations or questions where the model struggles?
4. **KBQA System Simplicity:** While emphasizing the simplicity of their KBQA system is understandable, it also limits the scope of the evaluation. It's unclear how well the HR-BiLSTM would perform within a more sophisticated KBQA pipeline incorporating more advanced entity linking or query generation techniques.

Questions to Authors:

1.  Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process? What was the search space, and how were the best hyperparameters selected?
2.  Can you provide some examples of failure cases for the HR-BiLSTM model? What types of relations or questions does it struggle with?
3.  Do you foresee any challenges in applying this model to KBs with different characteristics (e.g., different size, schema, or data quality)?
4.  How does the performance compare when using pre-trained relation embeddings in conjunction with (or instead of) the HR-BiLSTM approach?



Additional Comments:

*   The writing quality is generally good, but the paper could benefit from more concise explanations in some sections.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential directions for future research.
*   The references are comprehensive.
