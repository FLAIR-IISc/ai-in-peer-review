Summary and contributions:
- The paper proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM for KB relation detection that jointly matches questions to relations at two granularities: relation-level tokens and word-level tokens. Residual connections across BiLSTM layers enable effective hierarchical matching.
- It integrates this detector into a simple two-step KBQA pipeline where relation scores re-rank entity linker outputs and then select core relations/chains, plus a lightweight constraint detection module. This yields strong end-to-end accuracy on SimpleQuestions (SOTA) and competitive SOTA-level results on WebQSP.
- Comprehensive ablations show benefits of multi-granularity relation representations and residual learning over weighted sums and attention baselines.

Strengths:
- Empirical gains with significance: On relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves 2–3% over strong BiLSTM/CNN baselines on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP (p<0.001/0.01), and boosts KBQA accuracy by 2–3% when swapped into the same pipeline, indicating the improvements are robust and practically useful.
- Careful ablations: Removing relation names or words, residual connections, depth, or replacing BiLSTM with CNN quantifies where the gains come from; results convincingly support the hierarchical residual design.
- Practical KBQA contribution: The entity re-ranking step using relation scores addresses a known bottleneck (entity ambiguity) and clearly improves end-to-end performance without heavy feature engineering or joint inference.
- Sensible treatment of zero-shot: Factorizing relation names into words, initializing relation encoders with word-level states, and reporting SimpleQuestions gains where unseen relations are common demonstrate attention to open-set issues.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is incremental: Residual connections and multi-granularity matching are adaptations of known ideas; the conceptual leap beyond prior sequence-matching approaches is moderate.
- Limited analysis and completeness: Key pipeline hyperparameters (K, K′, l, α, λ), candidate set sizes, and runtime are not fully specified; zero-shot performance is not isolated (e.g., per-seen/unseen breakdown); error analysis is minimal.
- Dependency on external components: Performance hinges on entity linker quality; the constraint detection relies on heuristic string overlap and may limit generalization to more complex constraints or KBs.
- Comparisons: While many baselines are cited, direct apples-to-apples comparisons to recent end-to-end or neural programmer models on identical setups are limited.

Questions to authors:
- Please provide exact values/sensitivity for K, K′, l, α, λ and the candidate pool sizes per dataset. What is the runtime and memory footprint?
- Can you report separate accuracy on seen vs unseen relations, and sensitivity to entity linker recall?
- How are relation names tokenized (e.g., handling camelCase/underscores) and are chains limited in length?
- Will code and the WebQSP relation detection set be released to ensure reproducibility?
