Summary and contributions
This paper addresses relation detection for KBQA, proposing a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: word-level (tokenized relation names) and relation-level (whole relation tokens or relation-chain tokens). Residual connections across stacked BiLSTMs are used to stabilize training and encourage complementary abstractions for matching. The model is integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline with a new “entity re-ranking via relation evidence” step, plus an optional constraint detection stage. On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, the method achieves strong relation detection performance and improves end-to-end QA, attaining state-of-the-art or competitive results.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A practical and effective relation detection approach that combines word-level and relation-level representations with residual hierarchical matching, yielding consistent gains and strong ablations.
2) A simple yet impactful KBQA pipeline that uses relation detection to re-rank entity linking candidates, improving robustness to entity ambiguity and yielding end-to-end gains on both single- and multi-relation benchmarks.

Strengths
- Well-motivated hierarchical granularity: The paper convincingly argues (with examples and ablations) that relation words capture local lexical match (helpful for unseen relations), while relation tokens capture global cues; combining them improves robustness, especially on SimpleQuestions. The ablations “w/o rel name” and “w/o rel words” clearly support this point.
- Effective and simple residual matching: The residual connections across question-encoding layers address optimization difficulty in hierarchical matching. The comparisons against weighted-sum and attention baselines show consistent gains, particularly on WebQSP where longer dependencies and relation chains matter.
- Strong empirical results with thorough ablations: The study compares against strong baselines (AMPCNN, BiCNN) and provides multiple ablations (residual vs attention, CNN vs BiLSTM, single vs stacked layers), with statistical significance reported. Gains are consistent across relation detection and end-to-end QA.
- Practical KBQA impact via entity re-ranking: Using relation evidence from the question to re-rank entity linking candidates is intuitive and useful. The ablation removing this step shows notable drops, indicating this is a key source of the end-to-end improvements and a contribution of independent interest.
- Clarity of pipeline and scope: The KBQA system remains intentionally simple (no heavy joint inference), making the contribution easy to isolate and likely easy to reproduce and extend.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing sequence matching and residual networks: While the integration is well-executed, the core architectural ideas (stacked BiLSTMs, residual connections, multi-granularity inputs) are incremental. The paper’s novelty lies mainly in their combination and application to KBQA relation detection, rather than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm.
- Zero-shot and generalization analysis is underdeveloped: The motivation emphasizes unseen relations, but the paper lacks a targeted breakdown (seen vs unseen relation performance on SimpleQuestions; effect of relation-name vocabulary coverage). Such analysis would substantiate the claimed advantages of word-level factors.
- Reproducibility and detail gaps: Several important details are missing or unclear:
  • The ranking loss is written as l = max{0, s(r+)+s(r−)} with an unspecified margin; this appears inconsistent with standard hinge losses (typically margin − s(r+)+s(r−)). Please clarify the exact loss.
  • Negative sampling strategy, batch size, dropout/regularization, optimizer, and early stopping criteria are not specified.
  • Hyperparameters for the KBQA pipeline (K, K’, l, α, λ, threshold θ) and how they are tuned are not fully reported; please include selected values and sensitivity analyses.
  • For WebQSP relation detection, the constructed dataset is “to be released.” Availability is crucial; without it, reproducing the relation detection results is difficult.
- Baseline coverage and fairness: CFO (Dai et al., 2016) and methods using pre-trained relation embeddings (e.g., TransE-based) are discussed but not compared in either relation detection or end-to-end QA settings. Given their relevance to large relation sets and zero-shot behavior, stronger comparisons or a rationale for omission would strengthen the empirical case.
- Constraint detection method and fairness: The constraint detection relies on character-overlap heuristics and some special rules (appendices). It likely contributes appreciably on WebQSP; a more detailed ablation of which constraints contribute, and whether competitors used comparable constraint modules, would clarify fairness and portability.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot evidence: Can you report relation detection accuracy separately for test relations seen vs unseen in training (on SimpleQuestions), and for chains with unseen components (on WebQSP)? How much does relation-level token use help/hurt in unseen settings?
- Loss and training specifics: Please clarify the exact ranking loss (including margin and signs), negative sampling (how many negatives per positive), optimizer, batch size, dropout/regularization, and training epochs/early stopping.
- Residual design: Which residual variant performed best (hidden-state vs pooled residual)? Do you have an ablation for initializing the relation-name BiLSTM with the word-sequence final state?
- Step-1 vs Step-2 models: Are the same HR-BiLSTM parameters used for both entity re-ranking (raw question) and per-entity relation detection (with <e> replacement), or do you fine-tune/train separate models?
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: What values did you use for K, K’, l, α, λ, θ on each dataset? How sensitive is performance to these?
- Runtime and scalability: What is the average number of candidate entities and relations considered per question in step 1 and step 2, and the average inference time per question?

Additional comments
- Please ensure the WebQSP relation detection dataset and code are released to support reproducibility.
- Some references have missing years or incomplete metadata; please standardize citations.
- A breakdown by relation-chain length and error analysis (entity vs relation vs constraint) would add insight.
- It would be valuable to compare or combine with a TransE-initialized relation encoder, or to include a character-level relation representation variant.
