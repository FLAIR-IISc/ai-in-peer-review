Summary and Contributions
- The paper addresses relation detection for KBQA, proposing a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: word-level tokens derived from relation names and relation-level tokens (relation IDs/names). Residual connections across stacked BiLSTMs aim to ease training and enable hierarchical matching.
- The method is integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection: (1) entity re-ranking using relation detection over relations connected to candidate entities, and (2) relation/core-chain detection given the (re-ranked) topic entity. An optional constraint detection step handles multi-entity constraints.
- Experiments on SimpleQuestions (single-relation) and WebQSP (multi-relation) show improved relation detection accuracy (SQ: 93.3%; WQSP: 82.53%) and competitive or state-of-the-art end-to-end QA accuracy. Ablations demonstrate benefits from hierarchical matching and residual learning.

Strengths
- Clear, effective modeling of granularity in relation representations: Combining relation words and relation IDs is well-motivated. Ablations show substantial gains over either alone (SQ: 93.3 vs 91.2/88.8; WQSP: 82.53 vs 81.69/79.68).
- Hierarchical residual matching improves over strong baselines: Residual connections outperform weighted sum and attention variants (SQ: 93.3 vs 92.5/92.6; WQSP: 82.53 vs 80.65/81.38), suggesting the residual architecture helps learn complementary abstractions.
- End-task impact is substantial despite a simple pipeline: Swapping in HR-BiLSTM yields 2–3% absolute improvements in KBQA over alternative relation detectors, while the system avoids heavy joint inference or complex global rerankers.
- Comprehensive ablations and comparisons: The paper compares against AMPCNN and character trigram BiCNN baselines; evaluates variants (no residuals, attention, single-layer, CNN encoders); and shows deeper LSTMs matter more on WebQSP (chains), consistent with longer-context modeling needs.
- Practical insight: The entity re-ranking step demonstrably mitigates entity linker ambiguity by leveraging question-relation signals, a frequent bottleneck in KBQA.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Residual connections in deep sequence models are known; the contribution is an incremental yet solid application to hierarchical question–relation matching. The idea of combining relation word-level and ID-level signals is also present in prior work (e.g., word-level relation representations, character n-grams). The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning versus CFO (Dai et al., 2016), character-level generative models (Golub & He, 2016), and semantic parser/neural programmer approaches (Liang et al., 2016) in both relation detection and end-to-end QA.
- Incomplete experimental detail and reproducibility gaps: Several critical hyperparameters are introduced but not fully specified (e.g., margin value, α and λ trade-off weights, l, K, K’), and the “Appendix” with constraint matching details is referenced but not available. For WebQSP relation detection, the new dataset is promised but not included, making verification difficult.
- Evaluation scope and fairness: Relation detection on WebQSP is evaluated by restricting candidates to relations/chains connected to the (gold) topic entity in the constructed task, which is reasonable but may inflate scores relative to full open-set conditions. The end-to-end results still rely on a single top-1 core-chain before constraint detection, which can propagate errors; comparisons with systems using beam search and final answer rerankers (e.g., STAGG variants) are only partially addressed.
- Missing analyses: There is little breakdown by (a) seen vs unseen relations in SQ (a key motivation), (b) chain length on WQSP, (c) error types (e.g., entity vs relation vs constraint), or (d) effect of initializing relation-level LSTMs from word-level states. Statistical significance is mentioned but without details about the test.
- Baseline coverage: The end-to-end comparisons are limited. Including CFO, neural symbolic machines (Liang et al., 2016), or text-augmented methods (Xu et al., 2016) would better contextualize significance. For relation detection, stronger attention-based sequence matching baselines (e.g., ESIM-like models) might be relevant.

Questions to Authors
- Zero-shot analysis: Can you report performance on SQ split by seen vs unseen relations, and ablate the role of relation-level tokens in the zero-shot subset?
- WebQSP chains: How does HR-BiLSTM perform across different chain lengths? Does residual learning’s gain correlate with longer chains?
- Hyperparameters: What are the exact values of the margin, α (entity re-ranking), λ (query scoring), l (number of top relations used for re-ranking), K and K’? How sensitive are results to these?
- Residual design: Which residual variant performed best (hidden-state vs max-pooling shortcut), and why? Did you try highway connections or layer normalization to isolate training-ease vs representational benefits?
- Efficiency: What are training/inference times and memory footprints compared to AMPCNN/BiCNN? How large is the per-question relation candidate set in each step?
- Constraint detection: Could you quantify the standalone benefit of the constraint step on WQSP (precision/recall on adding correct constraints)? Any failure modes (e.g., date normalization)?

Additional Comments and Suggestions
- Provide the WebQSP relation detection dataset and all code/scripts to ensure reproducibility.
- Add comparisons to CFO (and/or incorporate TransE-initialized relation embeddings) to assess complementary gains relative to KB-structural priors.
- Include ablations on the initialization of relation-level encoders from word-level states, and a variant without this back-off to clarify its contribution.
- Report calibration/score distributions for the two-step detection and entity re-ranking, and sensitivity to entity linker quality.
- Extend the attention baseline to stronger architectures (e.g., multi-head or cross-attention) to more definitively support the residual-over-attention claim.
- Add qualitative examples illustrating cases where relation-level tokens disambiguate misleading word-level matches and vice versa, including multi-constraint questions.
