Summary
The paper presents a neural approach to relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA). The core model, HR-BiLSTM, performs hierarchical sequence matching between questions and relations using two granularities of relation representations: word-level tokens from relation names and relation-level tokens (relation IDs and relation-chain IDs). Deep bidirectional LSTMs encode questions at two abstraction levels, combined via residual connections to ease training and encourage complementary representations. The work further integrates relation detection into a simple KBQA pipeline via a two-step use of the detector: (1) re-ranking entity linker outputs by relation evidence from the raw question, and (2) detecting relations given a candidate topic entity (with entity mention replaced by <e>). On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, the model improves relation detection accuracy over strong baselines and yields 2–3 point gains in end-to-end KBQA, achieving state-of-the-art on SimpleQuestions and competitive results on WebQSP.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A hierarchical residual BiLSTM architecture for relation detection that jointly leverages word-level and relation-level representations and residual connections for robust training, leading to consistent gains (up to 2–3% absolute) over strong neural baselines on two datasets.

2) A practical two-step integration of relation detection into KBQA for entity re-ranking and relation scoring that materially improves end-task performance without heavy joint inference or complex re-rankers.

Strengths
- Clear modeling insight and effective architecture: Combining word-level and relation-level signals addresses both zero-shot generalization (via words) and global semantics (via relation IDs). Ablations show each level matters and the combination outperforms either alone (e.g., 93.3 vs 91.2/88.8 on SimpleQuestions; 82.53 vs 81.69/79.68 on WebQSP).

- Residual learning for hierarchical sequence matching: The residual connections between layers yield better optimization and complementary abstractions compared to a naïve weighted sum or attention alternative, especially on WebQSP (82.53 vs 80.65/81.38). This is supported by lower training loss and accuracy gains, indicating a principled architectural advantage.

- Strong empirical validation with targeted ablations: Comparisons against AMPCNN, BiCNN char-3-gram, single-layer BiLSTM, and CNN variants are fair and informative. The WebQSP CNN drop aligns with the need to model relation chains and longer dependencies, reinforcing the architectural choices.

- End-to-end impact on KBQA: The two-step use of relation detection for entity re-ranking and subsequent relation scoring produces consistent 2–3 point improvements over matched pipelines with baseline detectors, reaching state-of-the-art on SimpleQuestions while keeping the pipeline simple. The ablation showing the importance of entity re-ranking indicates a meaningful system-level contribution.

- Attention to KBQA-specific challenges: The work explicitly tackles large relation vocabularies, unseen relations (zero-shot tendencies), and relation chains, which differ from standard relation extraction setups. The design choices (name tokenization, chain handling, entity position via <e>) are well-motivated by these constraints.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of zero-shot generalization: While SimpleQuestions contains unseen relations and the combined representation helps, there is no breakdown of performance on seen vs unseen relations or by chain length. Such analysis would substantiate the claimed robustness and clarify where gains come from.

- Reproducibility and dataset construction details: For WebQSP relation detection, the paper constructs a new task (creating positive/negative candidates from topic entities and relation chains). Precise construction rules (maximum chain length, candidate explosion control, counts) and a public release timeline are not fully specified. Similarly, key hyperparameters for the two-step pipeline (K, K′, l, α, λ, threshold θ) and how they were tuned across datasets are under-described.

- Loss formulation and training clarity: The ranking loss is written as l = max{0, s(r+;q) + s(r−;q)} “where γ is a constant,” which appears inconsistent with standard margin ranking losses (typically max{0, γ − s(r+) + s(r−)}). Clarification is needed to avoid ambiguity. Additionally, initialization of relation-sequence LSTMs with word-sequence states for unseen relations is an interesting choice that deserves more explanation and ablation.

- Constraint detection is heuristic and under-specified: The character-overlap n-gram matching (with special rules for dates/answer types) drives substantial gains on WebQSP, yet implementation details, parameter settings, and error modes are not deeply analyzed. A small ablation on thresholds or alternative text-matching methods would strengthen the claims.

- Novelty is moderate at the conceptual level: Residual connections and hierarchical matching are established ideas; the primary novelty lies in adapting them thoughtfully to KB relation detection and integrating them into a practical KBQA pipeline. This is a solid engineering/scientific contribution but less theoretically novel.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Zero-shot and compositionality: What is the breakdown of relation detection accuracy on seen vs unseen relations on SimpleQuestions? On WebQSP, can results be stratified by chain length (1 vs 2), and by whether the chain or any component relation was unseen during training?

- WebQSP relation detection dataset: What maximum chain length is allowed when generating candidates, and how large is the candidate pool per question on average? Will the exact generation code and the derived dataset be released with train/dev/test splits?

- Loss function: Please clarify the ranking loss formula and the role/sign of the margin. Was hinge loss with margin γ − s(r+) + s(r−) used in implementation?

- Two-step pipeline hyperparameters: What values of K, K′, l, α (entity re-ranking tradeoff), and λ (final combination) were used per dataset? How sensitive is end-task accuracy to these choices?

- Efficiency: What are the average inference times per question for the entity re-ranking step and the relation detection step, and how do they scale with the number of candidate entities/relations?

- Constraint detection: What threshold θ was used, and how was it tuned? Could a brief error analysis be provided, particularly for false positives introduced by the overlap heuristic?

- Generality across KBs: How would the method handle KBs where relation IDs are non-lexicalized (opaque IDs)? Would relying solely on word-level representations be sufficient, or is a mapping to descriptive names required?

Overall, the work is well-motivated, experimentally solid, and offers a practical contribution to KBQA with clear improvements. Addressing the clarity and analysis points above would further strengthen the paper.
