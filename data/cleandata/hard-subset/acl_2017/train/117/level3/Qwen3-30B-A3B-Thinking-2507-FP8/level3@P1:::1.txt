

This paper addresses the critical challenge of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), where the task differs significantly from general relation extraction due to large relation sets (thousands of types), zero-shot learning requirements, and multi-relation chains. The main contribution is a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that combines word-level and relation-level representations for improved matching between questions and KB relations. The secondary contribution is a simple KBQA system that leverages this improved relation detector for entity re-ranking, which significantly boosts overall performance.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a compelling case for the distinct challenges of KB relation detection compared to general relation extraction, clearly identifying three key differences: large relation sets (6,000+ vs. <100), zero-shot learning requirements (14% unseen relations in SimpleQuestions), and multi-relation chains. This strong problem statement justifies the need for their approach.

2. The HR-BiLSTM model is well-designed with clear motivation for hierarchical matching. The authors demonstrate that combining word-level (local phrase matching) and relation-level (global phrase matching) representations significantly outperforms using either representation alone (93.3% vs. 91.2% on SimpleQuestions). The residual learning component effectively addresses training difficulties in deep hierarchical matching.

3. The entity re-ranking step is a novel and practical contribution that directly tackles a real bottleneck in KBQA systems. By using relation detection scores to re-rank initial entity candidates (e.g., distinguishing "Mike Kelley" the TV writer from "Mike Kelley" the baseball player), the system achieves significant performance gains without adding major complexity.

4. The experimental results are convincing, showing consistent improvements over strong baselines on both SimpleQuestions (93.3% vs. 91.2% for best baseline) and WebQSP (82.53% vs. 79.32%). The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the value of each component, particularly the residual learning and hierarchical matching.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation is limited to two datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), which may not fully represent the diversity of KBQA challenges. The paper would benefit from additional evaluation on other KBQA datasets or more diverse question types.

2. The paper does not sufficiently explain why the model performs better on WebQSP than SimpleQuestions (82.53% vs. 93.3%), despite noting that WebQSP benefits more from residual learning and deeper architectures. A more detailed analysis of these differences would strengthen the paper.

3. The ablation studies focus primarily on the relation detection component but lack deeper analysis of how different hyperparameters (e.g., LSTM hidden size, learning rate) impact performance across both datasets. The paper mentions tuning but doesn't provide sufficient justification for the chosen values.

4. The organization of Section 3 could be improved. Section 3.1 ("Different Granularity in KB Relations") would be better placed in related work, as it primarily reviews existing approaches rather than presenting the authors' novel contribution. Section 3.2 should be the main section on the proposed model.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper notes that WebQSP benefits more from residual learning than SimpleQuestions. Could you elaborate on why this difference exists, particularly given that SimpleQuestions has more unseen relations?

2. The entity re-ranking step appears crucial to the overall system performance (removing it causes significant drops). Could you provide more details about the specific question types where entity re-ranking provides the most benefit?

3. How does your model handle relations that don't have explicit names (e.g., relations inferred from question structure rather than named in KB)? This is a common challenge in KBQA.
