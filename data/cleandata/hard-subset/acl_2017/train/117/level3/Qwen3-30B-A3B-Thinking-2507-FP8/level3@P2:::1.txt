

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions
This paper addresses a critical challenge in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA): relation detection when dealing with large relation sets, unseen relations, and multi-relation chains. The authors propose a hierarchical recurrent neural network enhanced by residual learning for relation detection. Their key contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A hierarchical matching approach that combines both word-level (relation name as sequence) and relation-level (relation name as single token) representations, addressing the limitations of each approach individually.
2. A residual learning method that enables deeper hierarchical matching by making training more stable and effective, which significantly improves performance on KBQA relation detection tasks.
3. A simple yet effective KBQA system that leverages the improved relation detector through an entity re-ranking step, demonstrating that relation detection can enhance the overall QA pipeline.

## Strengths
1. **Significant Practical Impact**: The paper correctly identifies the gap between general relation extraction and KBQA-specific relation detection, which has been largely overlooked in prior work. The authors convincingly argue that KBQA requires handling thousands of relations, zero-shot learning for unseen relations, and multi-relation chains, making standard relation extraction approaches insufficient. Their solution directly addresses these challenges with practical improvements.

2. **Effective Hierarchical Matching with Residual Learning**: The core contribution of hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) is well-motivated and empirically validated. The ablation studies in Table 2 clearly demonstrate that both the hierarchical matching (combining word-level and relation-level representations) and the residual learning component are crucial for performance. The HR-BiLSTM achieves state-of-the-art results on both SimpleQuestions (93.3% vs. 91.2% for best baseline) and WebQSP (82.53% vs. 79.32% for best baseline), with statistically significant improvements.

3. **Novel Entity Re-ranking Mechanism**: The paper's most practical contribution is the entity re-ranking step that leverages relation detection to resolve ambiguities in entity linking. This simple yet effective mechanism (Section 4.1) significantly improves KBQA performance without requiring complex joint inference or feature engineering. The results in Table 3 show that removing this step causes a substantial performance drop, demonstrating its importance.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Dataset Evaluation**: The paper evaluates on only two datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), which don't fully represent the diversity of KBQA tasks. The authors don't demonstrate the approach on other benchmarks with different characteristics (e.g., more complex relation chains, different KBs), limiting the generalizability of their claims.

2. **Insufficient Novelty Contrast**: While the paper mentions prior work on relation detection for KBQA (e.g., Yin et al., 2016), it doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from these prior methods. For example, Yin et al. (2016) also used word-level relation representations and achieved good results on SimpleQuestions. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison explaining why their hierarchical residual approach is superior.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper doesn't sufficiently explain *why* residual learning improves hierarchical matching. The empirical results show it works, but the paper doesn't provide a clear theoretical or mechanistic explanation for how residual connections specifically help with the hierarchical matching problem in this context.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that the entity re-ranking step is crucial for performance (Table 3, third block), but doesn't provide examples of cases where it successfully resolves entity ambiguity. Could you provide a specific example from the test set where entity re-ranking corrects an error from the initial entity linker?

2. The authors mention that their approach benefits more from residual learning on WebQSP than SimpleQuestions. Could you elaborate on why this difference in effectiveness occurs, given that both datasets require relation detection?

3. The paper states that the hierarchical matching approach outperforms attention-based methods (Table 2), but doesn't provide a clear explanation for this. Given that attention mechanisms are commonly used for matching tasks, could you clarify why the residual learning approach works better in this specific context?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable contribution to KBQA by addressing a critical gap in relation detection with a practical and effective approach. The hierarchical residual matching method is well-motivated and empirically validated, and the entity re-ranking mechanism provides a simple yet powerful way to integrate relation detection into the QA pipeline. The results on both relation detection and KBQA tasks demonstrate significant improvements over previous work. With minor revisions to address the limited dataset evaluation and better contrast with prior work, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ACL. I recommend acceptance.
