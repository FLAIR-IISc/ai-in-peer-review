Summary and Contributions
This paper addresses relation detection for KBQA in settings with large relation vocabularies, limited supervision, and frequent zero-shot relations. The authors propose a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations represented at two granularities: (i) relation-as-token (relation-level) and (ii) tokenized relation name (word-level). They integrate this detector into a simple KBQA pipeline with a novel entity re-ranking step that uses relation scores to disambiguate entity linking, followed by relation detection conditioned on the re-ranked topic entity and optional constraint detection. Experiments on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP show improvements in both relation detection and end-to-end QA.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A practical KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection to re-rank entity linking candidates, yielding measurable end-to-end gains without complex joint inference or heavy re-ranking.
2) A hierarchical residual matching architecture that combines relation-level and word-level representations, improving robustness to unseen relations while preserving global relational cues.
3) A thorough empirical study, including ablations (residual vs weighted-sum/attention, word-level vs relation-level, CNN vs LSTM) and a constructed relation detection benchmark for WebQSP.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results with clear ablations:
  - Relation detection improves over strong baselines on both datasets (SQ: 93.3% vs 91.3%; WQ: 82.53% vs ~79–81%). The ablations convincingly demonstrate that both levels of relation representation and residual connections contribute to the gains. The LSTM-based encoder also outperforms CNNs on multi-relation chains, aligning with the longer dependency structure.
- End-to-end impact via entity re-ranking:
  - Using relation scores to re-rank entity linking candidates is simple yet effective, directly addressing a known bottleneck (entity ambiguity). The approach yields 2–3% QA accuracy gains and achieves SOTA or competitive results without heavy feature engineering or multi-stage re-rankers. This shows the detector’s utility beyond standalone relation classification.
- Addresses zero-shot and large-vocabulary challenges:
  - Splitting relation names into words improves generalization to unseen relations; complementing with relation-level tokens captures global semantics (e.g., longer phrases, skip-grams). This balance is valuable in KBQA where many test relations are unseen.
- Methodological clarity and plausibility:
  - The hierarchical residual design is well-motivated: deeper BiLSTMs capture broader context, and residual connections make layers complementary and training more stable than naive multi-layer similarity aggregation. The ranking loss and entity replacement token are reasonable choices for this task.
- Resource contribution:
  - Constructing and planning to release a relation detection setup for WebQSP is useful for the community, enabling more systematic evaluation of relation detection independent of full QA pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental:
  - Combining relation-level and word-level representations and adding residual connections to deep BiLSTMs are natural extensions of known techniques. The core modeling idea, while effective, feels incremental rather than conceptually new.
- Limited evaluation scope and analysis:
  - Only two datasets are used; both are Freebase-centric and widely studied. There is limited analysis of generalization to other KBs or open IE settings (e.g., ParaLex), robustness to paraphrase, or domain transfer. More detailed breakdowns (e.g., zero-shot vs seen relations, relation chain length, error categories) would strengthen claims.
- Dependency on external entity linker and lack of joint learning:
  - The pipeline still depends on an off-the-shelf linker; while re-ranking helps, the approach does not explore joint training or tighter integration, which could further reduce error propagation (especially since constraint detection is performed after selecting a single top query).
- Baseline coverage for matching architectures:
  - While attention (Parikh et al.) is included, stronger cross-attentive encoders or interaction models (e.g., token-wise cross-alignment beyond decomposable attention) are not explored. It’s unclear how HR-BiLSTM compares to more expressive cross-encoders under similar parameter budgets.
- Clarity/organization issues:
  - Section 3.1 is largely motivation/background and could be consolidated into Related Work; making 3.2 the focal modeling section would improve readability. Some training details (margin, α, λ, thresholds, K/K′ sensitivity) are scattered or omitted, affecting reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Zero-shot analysis: What fraction of test relations in SQ and WQ are unseen during training, and what is HR-BiLSTM’s accuracy on unseen vs seen subsets?
- Entity re-ranking impact: Please report entity linking top-1/top-K accuracy before vs after re-ranking, and its downstream effect on QA for both datasets.
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to K, K′, l (top relations for re-ranking), α (entity vs relation score mixing), and λ (final combination)? Provide tuning ranges and curves if possible.
- Residual choice: Which residual variant performed best (hidden-state skip vs pooled skip), and why? Did you try gating or layer normalization?
- Efficiency: What are training/inference costs vs baselines (parameters, throughput)? Any training stability issues (e.g., vanishing gradients without residuals)?
- Resource release: Will you release code, trained models, and the WQ relation detection dataset? Timeline?

Additional Comments
- Consider restructuring: move 3.1 (granularity motivation) to Related Work or combine with 2; make 3.2 the main Section 3, with a clearer split between representations, encoders, and matching/scoring.
- Provide more explicit details: margin value, optimizer, batch sizes, early stopping criteria; clarify whether word embeddings are fine-tuned; how relation-level tokens are built for chains; handling OOV relation words/names.
- A brief qualitative error analysis would be valuable (e.g., failure modes on compositional questions, type constraints, rare relations).
- Minor: fix reference formatting inconsistencies and figure/table captions for readability.
