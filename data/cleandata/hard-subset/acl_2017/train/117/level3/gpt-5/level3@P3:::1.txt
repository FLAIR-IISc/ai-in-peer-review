Summary
The paper tackles the core KBQA subproblem of mapping natural language questions to KB relations under two realistic constraints: (i) only partial argument information is present in the question (typically a single topic entity), and (ii) the relation inventory is very large, with a substantial portion unseen at training time. It proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions against two complementary relation views: a segmented, word-level view (to generalize to unseen relations) and an unsegmented relation-as-single-token view (to capture global, relation-id–level signals). The model is embedded in a simple KBQA pipeline that crucially uses relation detection to re-rank entity linking candidates before final relation selection, thereby mitigating entity ambiguity. Experiments on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP show consistent improvements in both standalone relation detection and end-to-end QA.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A dual-view relation representation with hierarchical residual matching (HR-BiLSTM), combining word-level and relation-as-token representations to address both zero-shot generalization and global phrase matching.
2) A practical pipeline contribution to KBQA: using relation detection to re-rank entity linker outputs, yielding a stronger entity–relation coupling and better downstream QA.
3) Solid empirical evidence with ablations demonstrating the value of both the dual-view design and residual connections, and improvements over strong baselines on two benchmarks.

Strengths
- Relevance and problem framing: The paper squarely addresses critical KBQA challenges—large relation spaces, unseen relations, and missing argument information. The motivation for mixing word-level and relation-level inputs is compelling, and the analysis (e.g., where word-level helps zero-shot while relation-level provides global cues) is well argued and evidenced.
- Methodological insight: The hierarchical residual design is more than an engineering tweak. Ablations show that (a) either view alone underperforms the combination, and (b) naive multi-layer or attention-based combinations underperform residual matching. This supports the claim that residual connections ease optimization and produce complementary abstractions useful for matching.
- Practical KBQA impact via entity re-ranking: Using relation detection signals to re-rank entity candidates is simple, effective, and targets a well-known bottleneck in KBQA. The reported gains and the analysis (e.g., where ambiguous surface forms are disambiguated by relation connectivity) substantiate this point.
- Strong empirical results with careful comparisons: The model improves relation detection (2–3 points over strong BiLSTM/CNN baselines) and yields end-to-end QA gains on both SimpleQuestions (single-relation) and WebQSP (multi-relation). The ablations are thoughtful (residual vs weighted sum vs attention; word vs relation name; CNN vs LSTM; depth).
- Clarity and reproducibility: The architecture, training objective, and KBQA pipeline are clearly described, with specific hyperparameters and data handling (entity replacement). Promised dataset release for the WebQSP relation task increases utility.

Weaknesses
- Dataset scope is limited: Evaluation is confined to two Freebase-centric datasets. While they cover single- and multi-relation settings, both share KB/domain characteristics; results on additional KBs (e.g., Wikidata) or other benchmarks would strengthen generality claims, especially for zero-shot behavior.
- Dependence on off-the-shelf entity linking and sensitivity analysis: Although entity re-ranking is a core contribution, the paper relies on a fixed EL system and K/K′ settings. A more systematic analysis of sensitivity to EL quality (e.g., when gold entity is not in top-K), K/K′, and α would clarify robustness.
- Limited analysis of unseen relations and coverage: The paper motivates zero-shot generalization but provides limited targeted analysis (e.g., performance stratified by seen vs unseen relations, or by chain length on WebQSP). Such breakdowns would better isolate where the dual-view and residual design help most.
- Computational considerations: Scoring “all relations connected to entities” at both re-ranking and final selection can be heavy for high-degree nodes. The paper does not quantify runtime or propose pruning strategies beyond K/K′, which matters for scaling to full KBs.
- Pipeline limitations: The system is still a pipeline without joint inference or beam-based query construction. While results are strong, the lack of comparison to joint models on the same EL outputs leaves some uncertainty on how much of the gain comes from better relation modeling vs pipeline design.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Unseen relations: Can you report relation detection accuracy split by whether the gold relation (or chain) appears in training? How does each view (word vs relation-id) contribute in the unseen subset?
- Entity linking sensitivity: What fraction of questions have the gold entity outside the initial top-K? How does performance vary with K and K′, and with α in entity re-ranking?
- Efficiency: What is the average number of relation candidates scored in step 1 (re-ranking) and step 2 (per-entity detection), and the end-to-end latency per question? Any batching or pruning used?
- Residual variants: Which residual variant performed best (hidden-state vs post-pooling), and how sensitive are results to hidden size/depth? Did you try layer normalization or highway connections?
- Multi-relation chains: On WebQSP, can you break down performance by chain length, and illustrate cases where relation-id tokens helped disambiguate vs where word-level matching dominated?
- Generalization: Have you attempted transfer to another KB (e.g., Wikidata) or to paraphrased relation labels to stress-test the word-level view?
