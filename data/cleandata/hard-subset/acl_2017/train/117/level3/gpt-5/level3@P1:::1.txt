Summary
The paper targets relation detection in knowledge-base question answering (KBQA), a setting with thousands of relations, entity ambiguity, and frequent zero-shot relations. It proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM model that matches questions to relations at two granularities: (a) relation-as-word-sequence (to generalize to unseen relations), and (b) relation-as-single-token (to capture global relation semantics). The model uses deep BiLSTMs for question encoding and residual connections to stabilize training and enable complementary abstraction levels to contribute to matching. The authors also build a simple KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection twice: once to re-rank entity linker candidates using question-only relation scores, and then to score relations conditioned on the (replaced) topic entity. Results show improved relation detection on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, and end-to-end QA gains, achieving SOTA on SimpleQuestions and competitive results on WebQSP.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstrating that strong relation detection can substantially improve KBQA via entity re-ranking: a simple two-step use of relation detection (pre-EL re-ranking, then entity-conditioned relation scoring) yields consistent end-task gains with minimal engineering.
2) A hierarchical residual matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines relation word-level and relation-name representations and exploits multi-level question abstractions; residual connections ease training and improve performance over weighted sums and attention baselines.
3) A carefully controlled empirical study with ablations (removing residuals, removing word or relation-name views, swapping BiLSTM for CNN, single-layer encoders) and a derived WebQSP relation detection benchmark, clarifying where the gains come from.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains on both relation detection and QA end-task: HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection accuracy by 2–3% over strong baselines on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP; the two-step use of relation detection translates into 2–3% QA accuracy gains over comparable pipelines. The improvements are consistent across datasets and settings.
- Thoughtful handling of zero-shot and large relation sets: The word-level relation representation addresses unseen relations; combining with relation-name tokens recovers global semantics that word-only models miss. The ablations convincingly show both views are necessary, especially on SimpleQuestions where unseen relations are common.
- Effective, simple pipeline contribution: Using relation scores to re-rank entity linker outputs is practical, addresses a real bottleneck (entity ambiguity), and materially improves downstream relation detection. The paper quantifies this effect and shows it is a major contributor to the end-task gains.
- Solid ablation and comparative analysis: The paper tests removal of residual connections, replacement with attention, shallow vs deep encoders, and CNN vs LSTM. The evidence that residual matching helps WebQSP more (longer dependencies, relation chains) is compelling and aligns with intuition about modeling sequences and chains.
- Reasonable generality and scalability: The model supports thousands of relations, relation chains, and large candidate sets; it does not rely on fragile joint inference or heavy feature engineering, making it easier to reproduce and extend.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: Only SimpleQuestions and WebQSP are used. Both are Freebase-centric and well-trodden; broader validation (e.g., ParaLex/OpenIE KBs, other KBs like Wikidata, more multi-hop datasets) would better support generality claims, especially the zero-shot story.
- Modest methodological novelty: While effective, HR-BiLSTM’s novelty is incremental—combining relation word/name views and applying residual connections across BiLSTM layers. Prior work has explored multi-view matching and deep encoders; the contribution is more in the combination and careful engineering than in a fundamentally new modeling idea.
- Comparisons and fairness concerns: The pipeline differs from prior QA systems (e.g., STAGG) in structure and losses; the authors do not perform matched comparisons where only the relation detector is swapped within the same end-to-end framework for all baselines. Also, reliance on off-the-shelf entity linkers may confound attribution of end-task gains.
- Constraint detection is heuristic: The character-overlap-based constraint attachment is simple and dataset-specific (with special rules). More robust approaches (neural matching with types/time normalization) might further improve multi-relation QA; as is, the component may limit generalization.
- Reproducibility gaps: Some training details (e.g., sizes of candidate relation pools per entity, alpha/beta trade-off parameters, exact l and K/K′ choices), data preprocessing (entity replacement rules), and resource release timelines are underspecified. The paper mentions datasets “will be available soon,” which weakens immediate reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Zero-shot analysis: Can you break down relation detection (and QA) accuracy by whether the gold relation appears in training vs unseen? How does HR-BiLSTM compare to word-only baselines in the unseen subset?
- Candidate set sizes: What are typical sizes of (a) ELK and EL′K′ after re-ranking, and (b) the per-entity relation candidate set on SQ and WQSP? How sensitive is performance to K, K′, and l?
- Residual design: Which residual variant performed best (hidden-state vs pooled-vector shortcuts)? Did you explore layer-wise normalization or highway connections?
- Comparisons: Can you report end-task results replacing only the relation detector within a fixed pipeline shared with prior work (e.g., STAGG), to isolate the detector’s contribution?
- Runtime and memory: What are training/inference costs for relation detection per question, and how do they scale with relation vocabulary size and chain length?

Additional comments (clarity/organization)
- The exposition can be improved by moving Section 3.1 (granularity discussion) into Related Work or as a brief motivation subsection, and making Section 3.2 the core of Section 3. Clearly split the new Section 3 into (a) representations, (b) hierarchical matching, (c) residual learning, and (d) training objective.
- Include more error analysis (e.g., typical failure types, attention/activation visualizations for the two granularities).
- If possible, release the WebQSP relation detection construction and code to strengthen reproducibility.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper addresses a central KBQA bottleneck with a well-engineered model and a simple, effective pipeline design. While the modeling novelty is moderate, the empirical gains, clear analysis, and practical contribution to entity disambiguation in KBQA make it a valuable addition.
