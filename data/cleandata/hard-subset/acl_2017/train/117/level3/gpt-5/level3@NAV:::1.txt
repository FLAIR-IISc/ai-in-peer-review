Summary
This paper tackles the important problem of mapping natural-language questions to knowledge-base (KB) relations under sparse argument information and very large relation vocabularies. It introduces a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that jointly leverages two complementary relation inputs—segmented word-level tokens and unsegmented relation-as-single-token representations—to improve relation detection. The method is integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline that crucially uses relation detection to re-rank entity candidates after initial entity linking, yielding consistent gains on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, with strong ablation evidence.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A practical KBQA pipeline innovation that uses relation detection for entity re-ranking post entity linking, substantially reducing ambiguity and improving end-task accuracy.
- Secondary: A hierarchical residual sequence matching model that combines relation word-level and relation-level inputs, with residual connections enabling effective multi-level matching and better optimization than attention or naive layer summation.
- Tertiary: Empirical evidence with thorough ablations demonstrating 2–3% absolute improvements in both relation detection and end-task QA over prior state of the art.

Strengths
- Clear end-task impact: Incorporating relation-informed entity re-ranking addresses a known bottleneck in KBQA; the reported 2–3% absolute gains over strong baselines on both datasets underscore practical significance.
- Sound modeling insight: Combining word-level generalization (zero-shot robustness) with relation-level global cues addresses coverage vs specificity; residual connections make multi-level matching trainable and effective.
- Comprehensive ablations: The paper isolates contributions of relation representations, residual learning, encoder choice (BiLSTM vs CNN), and pipeline components, providing convincing evidence.
- Relevance and scalability considerations: The approach directly targets large relation sets and partial argument settings common in real KBQA.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: Results are restricted to SimpleQuestions and WebQSP; broader validation (e.g., Wikidata, other multi-hop sets, unseen-relation stress tests) would strengthen generality claims.
- Entity re-ranking analysis is light: While end-task gains are reported, more granular metrics (EL recall/precision before/after, error breakdowns) would clarify where improvements originate.
- Architectural comparisons: Only one attention baseline is explored; stronger cross-attention or pretraining-based encoders (e.g., contextualized models) are not considered.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is entity re-ranking to the initial EL recall? Do gains persist when the gold entity is missing from the top-K list?
- How does HR-BiLSTM perform under heavier zero-shot conditions (e.g., higher unseen relation ratios)?
- What is the computational cost of relation-on-entity-set scoring at scale, and how does it grow with candidate set sizes and chain length?
