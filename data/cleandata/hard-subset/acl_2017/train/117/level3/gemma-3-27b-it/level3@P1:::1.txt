This paper addresses the challenging task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), specifically focusing on the complexities introduced by large knowledge bases with many relations, and the frequent occurrence of unseen relations during testing. The authors propose a hierarchical recurrent neural network enhanced by residual learning to improve relation detection performance, and demonstrate its effectiveness within a complete KBQA system.

The primary contribution of this work is a novel hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for relation detection. This model effectively combines word-level and relation-level representations of relations, leveraging deep BiLSTMs with residual connections to learn different levels of abstraction in question representations and facilitate matching with relation information. This approach proves particularly effective for dealing with the large number of relations and zero-shot learning scenarios inherent in KBQA. A secondary contribution is the integration of this improved relation detector into a complete KBQA pipeline, which includes an entity re-ranking step. This re-ranking, guided by the relation detector, significantly improves the overall accuracy of the system. Finally, the experimental results demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) QA benchmarks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical challenge in KBQA:** The paper tackles a key bottleneck in KBQA systems: accurate relation detection within the context of large, complex knowledge bases. The issues of scale and zero-shot learning are clearly articulated, and the proposed method directly addresses these concerns.
2. **Novel and well-motivated architecture:** The HR-BiLSTM model is a thoughtful approach. The hierarchical matching of question and relation representations at different granularity levels is well-justified, and the use of residual learning demonstrably improves performance. The motivation for combining word-level and relation-level representations is compelling and effectively implemented.
3. **Strong experimental validation:**  The authors provide thorough experimental results on two standard KBQA benchmarks, demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed model (residual learning, different relation representations). The inclusion of results from a complete KBQA pipeline further strengthens the findings.
4. **Practical system integration:**  The entity re-ranking step integrated with the relation detection model is a valuable addition. It highlights the importance of considering the end-to-end KBQA system, rather than focusing solely on individual components. This demonstrates a practical approach to building a more robust and accurate KBQA system.
5. **Clear and thorough explanation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The diagrams and tables are helpful in understanding the architecture and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited dataset diversity:**  The evaluation is primarily limited to the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets. While these are standard benchmarks, they may not fully represent the diversity of real-world KBQA scenarios. Performance on additional datasets or in more challenging settings (e.g., with noisy knowledge bases) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
2. **Complexity of the system:** The system, while achieving strong results, appears somewhat complex, relying on several components (entity linking, relation detection, constraint detection).  While each component is valuable, a simpler, more streamlined approach might be desirable for practical deployment.  A more detailed analysis of the computational cost of each component would be useful.
3. **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters, but doesn't provide extensive details about the sensitivity of the model to different parameter settings. A more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter tuning could help understand the robustness of the approach.
4. **Lack of error analysis:** While quantitative results are provided, a qualitative error analysis could provide valuable insights into the types of errors the system makes and potential areas for improvement. Understanding the failure cases would be beneficial for future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the HR-BiLSTM model to the quality of the initial entity linking? Would using a more accurate (but potentially slower) entity linker further improve performance?
2.  Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process? How did you select the optimal values for the learning rate, hidden layer size, and other key parameters?
3.   Regarding the entity re-ranking step, what is the relative contribution of the relation detection score versus the original entity linker score?  Could you provide a breakdown of cases where re-ranking significantly improved performance?
4.   The paper mentions constraint detection as being crucial for the system. What percentage of questions actually benefit from constraint detection and by how much?



**Additional Comments:**

- The initial presentation of the hierarchical matching approach could be made more concise. Moving Section 3.1 (Different Granularity in KB Relations) to the related work section would streamline the paper’s core technical contribution.
- The relationship between relation level and word-level representations needs to be clearer, maybe a simpler diagram illustrating the difference and how the model exploits this information.
- Consider adding a discussion of potential future work, such as exploring alternative architectures for relation detection (e.g., transformers) or investigating methods for handling noisy or incomplete knowledge bases.
