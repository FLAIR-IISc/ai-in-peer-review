This paper addresses the challenging problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), particularly focusing on the challenges posed by large relation sets and the need for zero-shot learning capabilities. The authors propose a hierarchical recurrent neural network enhanced by residual learning to compare questions and relation names at different levels of abstraction. Furthermore, they integrate this relation detector into a simple KBQA system, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving overall QA performance.

The primary contribution of this work is the proposed hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for relation detection. The key idea of combining word-level and relation-level representations, coupled with residual connections to facilitate training deeper networks, appears to be a significant improvement over existing approaches. This allows the model to better handle the complexities of KB relation detection, including unseen relations and the sheer scale of potential relations in a knowledge base. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration that improving relation detection, specifically through entity re-ranking *after* initial entity linking, can substantially benefit KBQA performance. The proposed system architecture, which incorporates this re-ranking step, achieves state-of-the-art results on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) QA benchmarks. Finally, the thorough ablation studies provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the different components of the proposed model.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets. The gains over previous methods are statistically significant and highlight the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies further solidify these results, showcasing the individual contributions of the hierarchical architecture and residual learning.
2. **Novel Approach to Relation Representation:**  The combination of word-level and relation-level representations is a particularly strong aspect of this work. This addresses the limitations of existing methods that rely solely on either approach. The hierarchical matching scheme effectively leverages the strengths of both granularities, leading to improved performance.
3. **Practical KBQA System Integration:**  The paper doesn’t just focus on relation detection in isolation.  The integration of the improved detector into a complete (albeit simple) KBQA pipeline and the demonstrated performance gains are valuable. The entity re-ranking step is a practical addition that addresses a known bottleneck in KBQA systems—the accuracy of initial entity linking.
4. **Thorough Analysis and Ablation Studies:** The authors provide a detailed analysis of the proposed model, including thorough ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of each component. This makes the paper more convincing and allows for a better understanding of the underlying mechanisms driving the performance improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The experiments are primarily conducted on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets. While these are established benchmarks, they may not fully represent the diversity of real-world KBQA scenarios.  Evaluating the model on additional datasets, or exploring its performance on more complex question types, would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Reliance on Pre-existing Entity Linkers:**  The KBQA system relies on external entity linkers (S-MART for WebQSP and a provided linker for SimpleQuestions). The performance of the system is therefore partially dependent on the quality of these linkers. An end-to-end system that integrates entity linking and relation detection could potentially achieve even better results. While the authors acknowledge the entity linking limitations, further exploration of a jointly trained system would be beneficial.
3. **Complexity of HR-BiLSTM:** While effective, the HR-BiLSTM model is somewhat complex.  The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the residual connections and how they contribute to the model’s performance. More detailed visualization of the learned representations might further improve understanding.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the system to the accuracy of the initial entity linker? Have you explored methods to mitigate the impact of linker errors, beyond the proposed re-ranking step?
2.  The ablation studies demonstrate the effectiveness of the residual learning. Could you provide more insight into *why* residual learning is particularly beneficial in this context? Is it primarily related to facilitating training of the deeper networks, or does it also contribute to the learning of more informative representations?
3.  The paper mentions that constraint detection is crucial for performance, yet it's presented as an optional step. Could you elaborate on the conditions under which constraint detection is applied, and what strategies are used to determine the appropriate threshold (✓) for filtering constraint entities?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The organization of the paper could be improved. Section 3.1 feels more like related work and would be better placed earlier in the paper. The current Section 3 should be the core of the technical contribution and expanded.
*   It would be helpful to include a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed model, compared to existing approaches.
*   Consider adding a qualitative analysis of some example questions and their corresponding relation detections to further illustrate the model’s strengths and weaknesses.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to KB relation detection. The experimental results are compelling, and the thorough analysis provides valuable insights. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the contribution and make the paper even more impactful. I recommend acceptance.
