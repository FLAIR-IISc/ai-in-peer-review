This paper addresses a relevant problem in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) by proposing a novel KB relation detection model that performs hierarchical matching between questions and KB relations. The authors' main contribution lies in their improved relation detection model, which uses deep residual bidirectional LSTMs to compare questions and relation names via different hierarchies of abstraction. Additionally, they propose a simple KBQA system that integrates entity linking and their proposed relation detector to enable one to enhance the other.

The paper's strengths include its ability to tackle the challenging problem of KB relation detection, which is a key step in KBQA. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the differences between general relation extraction tasks and KB relation detection, highlighting the need for a more robust model that can handle large relation sets and unseen relations. Their proposed HR-BiLSTM model is well-motivated and effectively addresses these challenges.

The experimental results show significant improvements over the state-of-the-art on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) QA benchmarks. The authors also provide an ablation study that demonstrates the effectiveness of their hierarchical matching approach and residual learning method.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. One major limitation is that the model is evaluated on a limited dataset, which may not be representative of the broader KBQA task. Additionally, the authors could have provided more analysis on the interpretability of their model and the types of relations that it is able to detect.

In terms of contributions, the authors' main claim is that their improved relation detection model enables their simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results on both single-relation and multi-relation KBQA tasks. However, it is not entirely clear whether the model itself is a key contribution or whether the data and evaluation are more significant contributions.

To strengthen the paper, the authors could consider the following suggestions:

* Move Section 3.1 to the related work section, as it provides a comprehensive overview of previous work on KB relation detection.
* Make Section 3.2 the main Section 3, as it describes the authors' proposed HR-BiLSTM model in detail.
* Split the new Section 3 more cleanly, perhaps by separating the description of the HR-BiLSTM model from the experimental results.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of KBQA by proposing a novel KB relation detection model that achieves state-of-the-art results on both single-relation and multi-relation QA benchmarks.

Questions to Authors:

* Can you provide more analysis on the interpretability of your model and the types of relations that it is able to detect?
* How do you plan to extend your model to handle more complex KBQA tasks, such as those involving multiple entities and relations?
* Can you provide more details on the entity linking component of your KBQA system and how it interacts with the relation detection model?

Additional Comments:

* The paper could benefit from more visualizations of the HR-BiLSTM model and its components, such as the hierarchical matching approach and the residual learning method.
* The authors could consider providing more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameter settings.
