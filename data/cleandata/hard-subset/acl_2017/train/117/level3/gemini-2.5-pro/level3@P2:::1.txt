**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), a challenging problem due to the very large number of potential relations and the need to generalize to relations unseen during training. The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual Bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM), which performs matching between a question and a candidate KB relation. The key idea is to represent relations at two levels of granularity—as a sequence of words and as a single, holistic token—and to match them against corresponding hierarchical representations of the question, which are learned using a deep BiLSTM with residual connections. The paper also demonstrates the effectiveness of this model within a simple KBQA pipeline where the relation detector is used first to re-rank candidate entities from an initial linker and then to identify the correct relation for the refined entity list.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective relation detection model (HR-BiLSTM) that jointly learns to match questions against both word-level and relation-level representations of KB relations. The use of a deep architecture with residual connections for this hierarchical matching is a key part of this contribution.
2.  A practical demonstration of how a powerful relation detector can be leveraged to improve the full KBQA pipeline. The proposed two-step process, which uses relation scores to perform entity re-ranking, effectively mitigates ambiguity and errors from the upstream entity linking module.
3.  State-of-the-art results on both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end QA task for two standard benchmarks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), demonstrating the model's effectiveness on both single-relation and multi-relation questions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Model Architecture:** The HR-BiLSTM model is technically sound and well-motivated. The core idea of handling the trade-off between generalization (word-level representations for unseen relations) and learning global semantics (relation-level representations for seen relations) is intuitive and addresses a real challenge in KBQA. The architectural choice of using deep BiLSTMs with residual connections to create hierarchical question representations for this matching is a clever and effective adaptation of recent deep learning advancements. The ablation studies in Table 2 provide strong evidence for the design, showing that the combination of hierarchical representations and residual learning yields significant gains.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results, achieving new state-of-the-art performance on two widely-used benchmarks. The improvements over strong prior work (AMPCNN, STAGG) are significant and are demonstrated on both the isolated relation detection task and the end-to-end KBQA task. The fact that this performance is achieved with a relatively simple KBQA pipeline (i.e., without complex feature-based re-ranking) further underscores the power of the core relation detection model.
3.  **Impactful Application to the KBQA Pipeline:** A key strength is the effective integration of the relation detector into the larger KBQA system. The proposed entity re-ranking step is a simple but elegant method for creating synergy between the entity linking and relation detection components. As the ablation study in Table 3 shows, this step is crucial for the system's overall performance, contributing significantly to the final accuracy. This provides a valuable insight into building more robust KBQA pipelines.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Evaluation Across Different Knowledge Bases:** The experiments are conducted on two datasets, SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, which are both based on Freebase. While these are standard benchmarks, the characteristics of relation names in Freebase (often compositional, e.g., "episodes_written") may be particularly well-suited to the proposed word-level/relation-level factorization. It is unclear how the approach would generalize to other knowledge bases (e.g., DBpedia, Wikidata) where relation names can be less transparent or more like opaque identifiers. Evaluating the method on a benchmark built on a different KB would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of general applicability.
2.  **Unclear Interaction with Other Advanced KBQA Techniques:** The authors rightly highlight the strength of their model by showing it performs well within a simple pipeline. However, this simplicity makes it difficult to assess how the gains from HR-BiLSTM would combine with other advanced components common in state-of-the-art systems, such as beam search over query graphs or sophisticated feature-based final re-rankers. For instance, it is possible that a powerful re-ranker could partially compensate for a weaker initial entity linking/relation detection stage, potentially reducing the relative impact of the proposed entity re-ranking step.
3.  **Clarity of Model Presentation:** The presentation of the model in Section 3 could be improved for better readability. Section 3.1, which motivates the different relation granularities, feels like it belongs in the introduction or related work section. The main model description in Section 3.2 is quite dense, combining the description of relation representations, the question encoder, and the hierarchical matching mechanism into a single block of text. Breaking this section into more clearly delineated subsections (e.g., one for relation representation, one for the question encoder, and one for the residual matching mechanism) would make the architecture easier to follow and appreciate.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The entity re-ranking step scores all relations associated with the top-K entity candidates. For a large K and for popular entities connected to many relations, this could become computationally expensive. Could you comment on the scalability of this step and report the values of K (initial entity candidates) and K' (re-ranked entity candidates) used in your experiments?
2.  You motivate the use of residual connections by stating that the second layer fits the "residues from the first layer of matching." This is an intuitive analogy to the original ResNet motivation. However, given that the final question representation is a sum (`hq = h_max^(1) + h_max^(2)`) which is then used in a single cosine similarity, could you clarify how this is technically fitting a residual? Is the benefit primarily from enabling easier gradient flow through the deep architecture, or is there a more direct "residual fitting" process at play?
