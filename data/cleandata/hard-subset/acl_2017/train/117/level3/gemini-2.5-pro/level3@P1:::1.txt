### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), a challenging problem due to the large number of potential relations and the presence of relations unseen during training. The authors propose a Hierarchical Residual Bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for this task. The core idea is to perform hierarchical matching by comparing a multi-layered question representation against two different granularities of relation representations: a fine-grained word-level view (tokenized relation name) and a coarse-grained relation-level view (the entire relation name as a single token). Residual connections are used between the BiLSTM layers to facilitate the training of deeper networks and learn more abstract question features.

The authors then integrate this improved relation detector into a simple but effective KBQA pipeline. A key aspect of this pipeline is a two-step application of the relation detector: first, it is used to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates from an off-the-shelf entity linker, and second, it is used to identify the correct relation for the refined set of entities. The proposed system is evaluated on both a single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and a multi-relation (WebQSP) KBQA benchmark, achieving state-of-the-art results on both.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective KBQA system architecture demonstrating the value of improved relation detection for entity disambiguation.** The paper's primary strength is its demonstration of how a powerful relation detector can be used to mitigate errors from an upstream entity linker. The entity re-ranking step, which leverages relation signals from the raw question text, is a simple yet impactful idea that directly leads to significant performance gains on the end-to-end QA task. The state-of-the-art results validate this system design.
2.  **A novel hierarchical matching model for relation detection.** The HR-BiLSTM model effectively combines multiple views of the relation with multiple levels of abstraction of the question. The use of residual learning to make the hierarchical representations comparable and complementary is well-motivated and empirically validated through thorough ablation studies, showing its particular benefit on the more complex WebQSP dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The most compelling argument for this paper is its strong performance. The proposed system achieves new state-of-the-art results on SimpleQuestions and results comparable to the state-of-the-art on WebQSP (Table 3). This is particularly impressive given that the system architecture is relatively simple and does not rely on complex feature engineering or joint inference models common in prior work. The ability to perform well on both single-relation and multi-relation benchmarks demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of the approach.
2.  **Effective and Novel System Design:** The two-step application of the relation detector is a clever contribution to the KBQA pipeline. By first using the model to re-rank entity candidates, the system directly addresses the well-known bottleneck of entity linking ambiguity. The ablation study ("w/o entity re-ranking" in Table 3) clearly shows that this step is responsible for a significant portion of the performance gain (e.g., a drop of over 2.5% on SimpleQuestions and 4% on WebQSP). This highlights a practical and impactful way to improve KBQA systems.
3.  **Thorough Model Analysis and Ablation Studies:** The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of their proposed HR-BiLSTM model in Table 2. They systematically dissect the model to show the contribution of each key component: the necessity of both word-level and relation-level views, the benefit of residual learning over simpler aggregation methods (weighted sum) or more complex ones (attention), and the advantage of deep BiLSTMs over a single-layer model or CNNs. This rigorous evaluation strengthens the claims and provides valuable insights into why the model is effective.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the combination of components is effective for the task, the core architectural elements (BiLSTMs, residual connections) are well-established in the literature. Residual connections were popularized in computer vision (He et al., 2016) and have been explored in NLP. The hierarchical matching idea, while well-motivated, is a fairly direct approach to combining different input granularities. The main novelty is in the specific application and successful integration of these ideas for KB relation detection, rather than a fundamental architectural breakthrough.
2.  **Dependence on Specific External System Outputs:** The KBQA system's evaluation relies on the entity linking outputs from specific prior works (Yin et al., 2016 for SimpleQuestions; Yih et al., 2016 for WebQSP). While this is a common practice for comparability, it makes it difficult to assess the performance of the proposed system in isolation or to understand how it would perform with a different entity linker. The reported end-to-end performance is a combination of the entity linker's quality and the authors' model, and it is not clear how sensitive the entity re-ranking step is to the quality or nature of the initial candidate set.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation in Section 3:** The organization of the model description could be improved for better clarity. Section 3.1 motivates the use of two different relation representations, but this feels like background that could be integrated into the introduction or related work. The core technical description of the proposed model begins in Section 3.2. Restructuring this part of the paper, perhaps by making Section 3.2 the main Section 3 and splitting it into cleaner subsections (e.g., Relation Representation, Question Representation, Hierarchical Matching with Residuals), would make the central technical contribution easier to follow.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The entity re-ranking step introduces a hyperparameter α to balance the initial linker score and the relation detection score. How sensitive is the overall system performance to the choice of α? Could you provide a brief analysis of its effect based on your development set experiments?
2.  The paper shows that the final constraint detection step is crucial for the WebQSP task, improving the score from 58.0% to 65.5%. Could you provide a brief error analysis of the remaining 34.5% of questions? What are the primary sources of error in the final system—are they persistent entity linking errors, core relation detection failures, or failures in the constraint detection module?
