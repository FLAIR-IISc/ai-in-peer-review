**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), particularly in scenarios with very large relation sets and ambiguous entity mentions. The authors propose a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that performs matching by representing relations at two levels of granularity: as a sequence of words and as a single, unsegmented token.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel application of a relation detection model within a KBQA pipeline to perform entity re-ranking. By using the relation detector to score potential relations before finalizing the topic entity, the system effectively disambiguates entity candidates from an initial linker, addressing a common bottleneck in KBQA systems.
2.  The HR-BiLSTM architecture itself, which combines word-level and relation-level representations to improve matching accuracy. The model demonstrates strong performance by leveraging the generalizability of word-level matching for unseen relations and the global context captured by relation-level matching for familiar ones.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper tackles a highly relevant problem. Mapping natural language questions to KB relations, especially with ambiguous or partial information, is a critical step for modern QA systems.
2.  The primary contribution—using the relation detector to re-rank entities—is a simple yet powerful idea. It shows how different components of a QA pipeline can be leveraged to mutually enhance one another. The ablation study in Table 3, which shows a significant performance drop without this step, provides strong evidence for its effectiveness.
3.  The proposed method is empirically validated with strong results. The HR-BiLSTM model achieves state-of-the-art performance on relation detection benchmarks, and the full KBQA system is competitive with or exceeds prior state-of-the-art systems on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. The thorough ablation studies clearly demonstrate the benefits of the model's key components, including the dual representation and the use of residual connections.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The evaluation, while strong, is limited to two standard datasets. The paper claims to address challenges like zero-shot learning and large-scale, open-domain relations. While the dual-representation approach is designed for this, its effectiveness would be more convincingly demonstrated on datasets with a more pronounced long-tail distribution of relations or a greater train-test vocabulary mismatch.
2.  The paper's organization could be improved for clarity. Section 3.1, which motivates the different relation granularities, reads more like background and could be integrated into the introduction or related work. This would allow Section 3.2 to become the main Section 3, which could then be more cleanly subdivided (e.g., Relation Representation, Question Representation, Hierarchical Matching) to better articulate the model's architecture.
