This paper addresses the challenging problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), specifically focusing on scenarios with large relation sets and potential for unseen relations. The authors propose a hierarchical recurrent neural network (HR-BiLSTM) that leverages both word-level and relation-level representations of relations, enhanced with residual learning. A key contribution is integrating this improved relation detector into a KBQA pipeline to enable entity re-ranking, improving overall QA performance.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the effective combination of different relation representations and the use of residual learning to facilitate hierarchical matching. While the individual components (BiLSTMs, word/relation embeddings) are not novel, the specific architecture and the demonstrated improvement in both relation detection *and* downstream KBQA performance are significant. The entity re-ranking step, enabled by the improved relation detector, is a clever addition that addresses a common bottleneck in KBQA systems. A second contribution is the demonstration of the benefit of this approach on two established KBQA benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art results on SimpleQuestions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial challenge in KBQA – handling large and open relation sets – which is often overlooked in standard relation extraction research.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The HR-BiLSTM consistently outperforms baselines on both relation detection and KBQA tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component.
*   **Practical Integration:** The integration of the relation detector into a complete KBQA pipeline, with the entity re-ranking step, shows a clear path to practical application.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their method on two standard datasets and provide detailed ablation studies, strengthening the validity of their findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The evaluation is primarily conducted on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. While these are standard benchmarks, they may not fully represent the diversity of real-world KBQA scenarios. Expanding the evaluation to other datasets or KBs would strengthen the generalizability of the results.
*   **Architecture Complexity:** The HR-BiLSTM architecture, while effective, is relatively complex. A more detailed analysis of the learned representations and the impact of different hyperparameters would be beneficial.
*   **Section Organization:** As noted by the supervisor, the organization of Section 3 could be improved. Moving 3.1 to related work and restructuring 3.2 would enhance clarity.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the HR-BiLSTM, particularly in cases where it underperforms compared to the baselines?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the system to the quality of the initial entity linking step?
*   What is the computational cost of the HR-BiLSTM compared to the baseline models?
