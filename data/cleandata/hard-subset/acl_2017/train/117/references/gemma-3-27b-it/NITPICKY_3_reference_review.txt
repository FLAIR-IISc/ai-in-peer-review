This paper addresses the problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), focusing on the challenges posed by large relation vocabularies and zero-shot learning. The authors propose a hierarchical BiLSTM model with residual learning to better match questions and relations. They demonstrate improvements on both relation detection and KBQA tasks.

The primary contribution is the proposed Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) architecture for relation detection. While the combination of hierarchical matching and residual connections isn’t entirely novel in NLP, its application to the specific problem of KB relation detection, and the demonstration of its effectiveness, represents a solid incremental advance. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation on both relation detection and end-to-end KBQA, providing convincing evidence of the method’s benefits. The inclusion of ablation studies further strengthens the analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The HR-BiLSTM consistently outperforms strong baselines on both relation detection tasks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) and on the KBQA end-task. The gains, while not massive, are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies are well-designed and provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component of the HR-BiLSTM (hierarchical matching, residual learning). This helps to justify the design choices and isolates the key factors driving performance.
3. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of KB relation detection compared to general relation extraction, specifically highlighting the issues of large vocabularies and zero-shot learning. This provides a strong rationale for the proposed work.
4. **Practical KBQA System Integration:** Demonstrating the improvement within a complete KBQA pipeline, even a relatively simple one, reinforces the practical relevance of the work. The entity re-ranking step is a pragmatic addition.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Components:** While the combination is effective, the individual components (BiLSTMs, hierarchical matching, residual connections) are all well-established techniques in NLP. The paper doesn’t introduce fundamentally new building blocks. The novelty primarily lies in the application and adaptation of existing techniques to the specific KBQA relation detection problem.
2. **Overly Detailed Presentation:** The paper is excessively detailed in its description of the model and experimental setup. While thoroughness is appreciated, the level of detail often obscures the key ideas. For instance, the lengthy description of the query generation algorithm (Algorithm 1) adds little value compared to the core contribution of the HR-BiLSTM. The lengthy related work section also feels exhaustive rather than focused.
3. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the types of errors the model makes. Understanding *why* the model fails on certain examples would be valuable for future research. Are there specific relation types that are particularly challenging? Are there systematic biases in the training data?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters, but doesn't provide sufficient detail about the process. It's unclear how sensitive the results are to different hyperparameter settings. A more robust analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the findings.
5. **Formatting & Style:** Numerous minor inconsistencies in formatting and writing style detract from the paper's overall polish. The reference list is improperly formatted, with author names listed inconsistently (e.g. “Yao et al\.,? 2014” vs. “Fader et al\.,? 2013”). The lengthy repetition of citations in the reference list also feels redundant and cluttered.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors the HR-BiLSTM makes? Are there specific relation types or question structures that are particularly challenging?
2. How sensitive are the results to different hyperparameter settings, particularly the learning rate and the weight assigned to the entity re-ranking step?
3. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the HR-BiLSTM compared to the baseline models? Does the added complexity significantly increase training or inference time?
4. The paper mentions that the KBQA system does not use joint inference or feature-based re-ranking.  Have you explored these techniques, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

* Please carefully proofread the paper for formatting and stylistic errors. The reference list requires significant cleanup.
* Consider condensing the related work and query generation sections to focus on the most relevant contributions.
* A visual representation of the HR-BiLSTM architecture (beyond Figure 2) might be helpful to clarify the connections and data flow.



Despite the paper's strengths, the lack of fundamental novelty and the excessive detail, coupled with the numerous minor flaws, lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the work presents a solid incremental improvement, it does not meet the high bar for acceptance at ACL. A more concise, polished, and insightful presentation of the core contributions, along with a more in-depth error analysis, would be needed to warrant reconsideration.
