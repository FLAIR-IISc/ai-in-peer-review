This paper tackles the challenging problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), specifically addressing the issues of large relation vocabularies, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation queries. The core idea – hierarchical matching of questions and relations with residual learning – is a compelling direction.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Hierarchical Matching Approach:** The proposed HR-BiLSTM framework, leveraging both relation-level and word-level representations *and* residual connections, is a genuinely interesting architectural contribution. It moves beyond simple sequence matching and attempts to capture different levels of abstraction, a critical aspect for KBQA.
2. **Empirical Demonstration on KBQA:** The paper demonstrates that improvements in relation detection *do* translate to gains in end-to-end KBQA performance, validating the importance of this subtask. The state-of-the-art results, though incremental, are encouraging.
3. **Clear Problem Formulation & Analysis:** The paper provides a well-articulated analysis of the challenges specific to KB relation detection compared to general relation extraction. This framing is valuable for researchers in the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold Architectural Idea:** The HR-BiLSTM is not a marginal improvement; it's a fundamentally different way of approaching relation matching. The intuition behind hierarchical representation and residual learning is sound and the experimental results support its effectiveness.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently positive results across both relation detection *and* KBQA tasks demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach. While the gains aren’t massive, they are statistically significant and consistent.
3. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies are well-designed and provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component of the HR-BiLSTM.  The results clearly show the benefit of both hierarchical matching and residual learning.
4. **Well-Motivated Problem:**  The paper effectively highlights the specific challenges of KB relation detection (large vocabulary, zero-shot learning) and argues convincingly for the need for new approaches.
5. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the specifics of KBQA.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of hierarchical matching and residual learning is novel, the individual components (BiLSTMs, max-pooling, residual connections) are well-established in the deep learning literature. The novelty lies more in the application and integration than in groundbreaking algorithmic innovation.
2. **Evaluation Scope:** The experiments are primarily conducted on two datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP). While these are standard benchmarks, expanding the evaluation to more diverse KBs and question types would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper doesn't delve deeply into the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter settings. A more detailed analysis of how different hyperparameters affect performance would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis would help pinpoint the remaining challenges in relation detection and guide future research. Understanding *where* the model fails could reveal new avenues for improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the HR-BiLSTM compared to simpler models? Is the increased performance worth the added computational cost?
2.  You mention that the constraint detection step is crucial for your system.  Could you elaborate on why this is the case, particularly given the performance of the relation detection model itself?  Are there specific types of questions where constraint detection is particularly important?
3.  The residual connections seem crucial. Did you experiment with different types of residual connections (e.g., skip connections with learnable weights)? If so, what were the results?
4.  Regarding the ablation study with attention, why did the attention mechanism not perform as well as the residual connections in this particular setup?
