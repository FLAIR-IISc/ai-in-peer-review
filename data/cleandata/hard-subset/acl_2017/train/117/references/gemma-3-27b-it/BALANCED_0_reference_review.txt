This paper addresses the challenging task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), focusing on the issues of large relation vocabularies, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation question answering. The authors propose a hierarchical BiLSTM model with residual learning (HR-BiLSTM) to better match questions and relations at different granularities. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on both single- and multi-relation KBQA tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Relation Representation and Matching:** The core contribution lies in the hierarchical matching strategy between questions and relations. By combining word-level and relation-level representations and employing residual learning, the model effectively captures both local and global relation information, improving performance on unseen relations and complex queries. This is a significant improvement over simply treating relations as single tokens or relying on standard BiLSTM architectures.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets. The HR-BiLSTM model consistently outperforms strong baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness and achieving state-of-the-art performance. This strengthens the claim that the proposed approach is a valuable contribution to the field.
3. **System Integration and Analysis:**  The authors integrate the improved relation detector into a complete KBQA system and provide an analysis of its impact.  The inclusion of an entity re-ranking step and the ablation studies provide insights into the different components' contribution and guide future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges specific to KB relation detection compared to general relation extraction, establishing the importance of the research. The authors effectively explain why existing methods struggle with the scale and complexity of KBQA.
2. **Well-Designed Model:** The HR-BiLSTM architecture is thoughtfully designed to address the identified challenges. The use of residual connections is a particularly strong point, as it helps mitigate the vanishing gradient problem and facilitates the training of deeper networks. The combination of relation-level and word-level representations is a clever approach to capture different aspects of relation information.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons to multiple baselines and detailed ablation studies. The inclusion of both relation detection and end-to-end KBQA evaluation provides a holistic assessment of the proposed method. The chosen datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) are standard benchmarks in the KBQA community.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention the availability of datasets, facilitating potential reproducibility of the results. The detailed description of the model and experimental setup also aids in this regard.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The ablation studies and discussion of results are insightful. The authors don’t simply present numbers; they offer explanations for the observed performance differences and provide guidance for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the *combination* of techniques (hierarchical matching + residual learning) is effective, the individual components (BiLSTMs, residual connections) are not entirely novel. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the proposed approach differs from and builds upon existing work on each component.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters but doesn't provide a detailed account of the search process or the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the findings.
3. **Error Analysis:**  A more detailed error analysis would be valuable. What types of questions/relations does the model still struggle with?  Are there specific error patterns that could inform future improvements? Knowing the error distribution will help in designing better models.
4. **Complexity of Implementation:** While the paper describes the method clearly, the implementation appears complex, potentially making it difficult for others to adopt and extend. A simplified version or open-source code would be extremely beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what ranges of values were explored for each hyperparameter, and how was the best configuration selected?
2.  What types of errors does the HR-BiLSTM model make most frequently? Can you provide examples of questions or relations that the model consistently misclassifies?
3.  How does the performance of the model vary across different relation types (e.g., frequently occurring vs. rare relations)?
4.  You mention the S-MART entity linking results.  Did you consider using other entity linking systems, and if so, how did they impact performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing quality is generally good, but some sections could be more concise.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the proposed approach and related work.
*   Investigating the use of pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) to initialize the word embeddings might further improve performance.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of KBQA. The proposed HR-BiLSTM model is effective, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated. While there are a few areas for improvement, the paper is well-written and presents compelling evidence of its contributions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
