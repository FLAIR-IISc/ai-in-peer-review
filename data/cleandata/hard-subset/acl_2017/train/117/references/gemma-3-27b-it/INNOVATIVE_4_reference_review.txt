This paper tackles the challenging problem of relation detection in the context of Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), specifically addressing the issues of large relation vocabularies, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation question answering. The core idea – hierarchical matching between questions and relations, leveraging both relation-level and word-level representations with a residual learning mechanism – is a genuinely interesting and potentially impactful direction. While the empirical validation isn't overwhelmingly strong, the *idea* itself is novel and warrants acceptance.

The primary contribution of this work is the proposed hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for relation detection. The motivation to capture different granularities of relation information – whole relation names versus constituent words – is well-articulated, and the residual connections are a clever addition to facilitate training and potentially learn more abstract representations. The application of this to the specific challenges of KBQA relation detection (large vocab, zero-shot) is a good framing.

A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration, through ablation studies, of the benefits of both hierarchical matching *and* residual learning. While the gains aren't massive, the systematic evaluation helps to validate the design choices.

Finally, the KBQA system built around this improved relation detector, and the resulting state-of-the-art results, provide a compelling, albeit limited, validation of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The hierarchical matching with residual learning is a genuinely new idea for relation detection. Most existing work focuses on either direct matching or attention mechanisms, and this offers a distinct alternative that explicitly tackles the granularity issue. This is the strongest point of the paper.
2. **Well-Motivated Design:** The authors provide clear reasoning for their design choices, linking them directly to the specific challenges of KBQA relation detection (large vocabularies, unseen relations). This makes the approach feel grounded and not purely ad-hoc.
3. **Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies are well-conducted and offer valuable insights into the contribution of each component of the HR-BiLSTM model. This helps to establish the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **State-of-the-Art Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, even with a relatively simple KBQA system, demonstrates the practical value of the proposed relation detector.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed methods and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Empirical Gains:** While the results are state-of-the-art, the improvements over strong baselines are relatively modest (2-3%). This suggests that the HR-BiLSTM, while promising, may not be a *huge* leap forward in performance.
2. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters, but doesn't provide much detail on the process. It's unclear how sensitive the results are to different hyperparameter settings.
3. **Dependency on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The reliance on pre-trained word embeddings may limit the model’s ability to learn representations specific to the KBQA domain. Exploring KB-specific embeddings could be beneficial.
4. **KBQA System Simplicity:** The KBQA system itself is rather simple. A more sophisticated system could potentially unlock the full potential of the improved relation detector.
5. **Missing Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis would be helpful to understand the types of questions where the HR-BiLSTM still struggles and identify areas for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the criteria used for selection?
2. Have you experimented with KB-specific word embeddings, and if so, what were the results?
3. Could you provide a qualitative error analysis, highlighting examples of questions where the model fails and discussing the reasons for the failures?
4. How computationally expensive is the HR-BiLSTM compared to the baseline models?



**Additional Comments:**

The discussion of related work is reasonably comprehensive. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful for illustrating the task. The use of publicly available datasets and resources is commendable.
