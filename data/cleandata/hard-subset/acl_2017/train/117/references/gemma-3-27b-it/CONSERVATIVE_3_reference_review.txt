This paper addresses the problem of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), focusing on the challenges posed by large relation vocabularies, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation questions. The authors propose a hierarchical BiLSTM model with residual learning to better match questions and relations at different granularities. They demonstrate improvements on both relation detection and end-to-end KBQA tasks.

The primary contribution of this work is a reasonably well-engineered, if not groundbreaking, model for relation detection within a KBQA pipeline. While the individual components (BiLSTMs, residual connections, hierarchical matching) are not novel, their combination and application to the specific challenges of KB relation detection – particularly the handling of a large number of relations – appear to yield tangible benefits. The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation, demonstrating improvements over several baselines on standard datasets. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Experimental Validation:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation on both relation detection and KBQA tasks, using established datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP).  The ablation studies, while somewhat extensive, are useful in dissecting the contribution of the different components of the proposed model.
2. **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors clearly articulate the challenges specific to KB relation detection, contrasting it with general relation extraction.  The discussion of the issues stemming from large relation vocabularies and zero-shot learning is well-motivated.
3. **Practical System Integration:**  The paper doesn't simply propose a relation detector in isolation. It demonstrates how the improved relation detection module integrates into a complete KBQA system, providing a more realistic assessment of its value. The two-step process of entity re-ranking and relation detection seems sensible.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors state that datasets will be made publicly available, and baselines are well-defined. This is helpful for allowing others to build upon their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Conceptual Novelty:** The core ideas presented – BiLSTMs, residual connections, and hierarchical matching – are well-established in the deep learning literature. The combination, while effective, doesn’t represent a significant theoretical advance. The paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of how this work extends or refines existing techniques.
2. **Over-Reliance on Empirical Results:** The paper is heavily driven by empirical results.  While strong experimental validation is good, the lack of deeper analysis into *why* the proposed method works is a weakness. A more detailed analysis of the learned representations or attention weights would be beneficial. For example, do the representations truly capture different levels of abstraction as claimed?
3. **Limited Comparison to Strongest Baselines:** The comparison to existing KBQA systems is not entirely fair.  The baselines used (STAGG, AMPCNN) are not necessarily state-of-the-art *current* systems and may not reflect the latest advancements in the field. A more rigorous comparison against more recent and sophisticated KBQA approaches would strengthen the paper’s claims.  Furthermore, simply re-implementing baselines isn't enough; a fair comparison would involve tuning those baselines to their full potential.
4. **Complexity/Explainability:** The model has several moving parts (two BiLSTM layers, residual connections, pooling operations). While the ablation studies help, the overall complexity makes it difficult to understand precisely which components are most crucial and why.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned representations in your HR-BiLSTM model? For example, can you visualize the representations and demonstrate that they indeed capture different levels of abstraction as claimed?
2.  The paper mentions that the choice of hyperparameters was made via tuning on development sets. Can you provide details on the size of the development sets and the specific tuning procedure used?
3.  The impact of the entity re-ranking step is presented, but how sensitive is the system's performance to the choice of the α parameter in the re-ranking formula?
4.  Given the performance gains shown, have you explored using the HR-BiLSTM model as a feature extractor for a more complex KBQA system architecture (e.g., a sequence-to-sequence model)?
