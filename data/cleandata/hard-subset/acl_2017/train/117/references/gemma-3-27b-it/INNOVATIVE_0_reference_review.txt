This paper tackles the challenging problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), specifically addressing the issues of large relation vocabularies, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation chains. The authors propose a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that leverages both relation-level and word-level representations, combined with residual learning to improve training and representation quality. They demonstrate improved performance on both relation detection and the end-to-end KBQA task.

The most significant contribution of this work is the exploration of hierarchical matching with residual learning for relation detection in the context of KBQA. While the individual components (BiLSTMs, word/relation-level representations) aren’t novel in themselves, the *combination* and the specific framing for the KBQA task – particularly the residual connections to facilitate learning across levels of abstraction – is a genuinely interesting idea. The empirical results, showing improvements over strong baselines, suggest this approach holds promise. The clear articulation of the challenges specific to KBQA relation detection (compared to general relation extraction) is also a valuable contribution. 

Further strengths include: the comprehensive experimental evaluation on two standard datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), including ablation studies that validate the effectiveness of each component of the proposed HR-BiLSTM model, and the inclusion of an end-to-end KBQA system to demonstrate the practical benefits of the improved relation detection module. The focus on making a simple, yet effective, KBQA system is also appreciated; many KBQA papers get bogged down in complex architectures. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivations and technical details of the proposed approach.

However, several weaknesses prevent a higher score. The gains achieved, while statistically significant, are somewhat modest. A 2-3% improvement on the KBQA task, while positive, doesn't represent a radical breakthrough. The analysis of *why* the residual connections are helpful is limited; the paper states it improves training and abstraction but lacks a deeper dive into what specifically the network is learning. A more in-depth error analysis would be beneficial. The comparison to state-of-the-art is incomplete; while the authors compare to existing results, the implementation details of those results aren’t always clear, making a direct comparison difficult. The reliance on pre-existing entity linking results is a limitation – errors in entity linking will inevitably impact the downstream relation detection performance.

Finally, the paper lacks discussion of computational complexity and scalability. How does the HR-BiLSTM model scale to even larger knowledge bases and more complex questions? This is a critical consideration for real-world KBQA systems.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations within the HR-BiLSTM? Specifically, what types of information are captured at each layer, and how do the residual connections facilitate the flow of this information?
2. How does the performance of the HR-BiLSTM degrade as the knowledge base size increases? Have you explored techniques for scaling the model to handle larger KBs?
3. The paper mentions that constraint detection significantly improves performance. Could you provide more details on the implementation of the constraint detection module and its interaction with the relation detection component?
4. Given that the entity linking stage is a known bottleneck, have you considered integrating relation detection *into* the entity linking process to perform joint inference?



Additional Comments:
- The formatting of the table listing references is somewhat inconsistent.
- While the ablation studies are helpful, exploring different architectural variations (e.g., using attention mechanisms instead of residual connections) could further strengthen the analysis.
