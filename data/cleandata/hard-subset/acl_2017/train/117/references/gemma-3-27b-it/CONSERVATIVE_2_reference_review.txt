This paper addresses the challenging task of relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA), focusing on the issues of large relation vocabularies, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation questions. The authors propose a hierarchical BiLSTM model with residual learning (HR-BiLSTM) to better match questions to relations at different granularities. They demonstrate improved performance on both relation detection and the KBQA end-task on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets.

The primary contribution of this work is a relatively well-engineered, albeit incrementally advanced, model for relation detection within a KBQA pipeline. While the individual components (BiLSTMs, residual connections, hierarchical matching) are not novel in themselves, their combination and application to the specific challenges of KB relation detection, especially handling large relation sets and unseen relations, represents a useful contribution. The thorough experimental evaluation against several baselines, including reimplemented published methods, strengthens this contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The comparison against multiple baselines, including reimplementations of published methods (BiCNN, AMPCNN), is commendable. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the contribution of each component of the HR-BiLSTM model. This rigorous evaluation strengthens the claims made by the authors.
2. **Practical Focus & System Integration:** The work focuses on a critical component of a KBQA system – relation detection – and demonstrates how improvements in this subtask translate to gains in the end-to-end KBQA performance. The clear description of the KBQA pipeline and the integration of the relation detector are valuable.
3. **Addressing KBQA Specific Challenges:** The authors correctly identify the key differences between general relation extraction and KB-specific relation detection (large vocabulary, zero-shot learning, multi-relation queries). The proposed approach is specifically designed to address these challenges, particularly through the use of word-level and relation-level representations and hierarchical matching.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach, the experimental setup, and the results. The figures and tables are informative and contribute to understanding the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of existing techniques is effective, the paper lacks a truly novel algorithmic contribution. The core ideas – BiLSTMs, residual connections, hierarchical matching – are well-established in the NLP literature. The primary innovation lies in the application of these techniques to the specific problem of KB relation detection.  The incremental improvement achieved, while demonstrable, doesn’t represent a significant breakthrough.
2. **Over-reliance on Dataset-Specific Tuning:** The extensive hyperparameter tuning suggests that the performance gains are somewhat sensitive to the specific dataset and experimental setup. The paper does not adequately address the generalizability of the proposed approach to other KBs or QA datasets.
3. **Lack of Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper presents quantitative results, but lacks a qualitative analysis of the errors made by the system. Understanding the types of errors made by the model would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for future improvement. What types of relations are still difficult to detect?  What types of questions cause issues?
4. **Dependency on Existing Entity Linkers:** The performance is heavily reliant on the accuracy of the initial entity linking step. While the re-ranking component attempts to mitigate some of the errors made by the entity linker, the overall system performance is still constrained by the quality of the initial entity linking results. A more thorough investigation of the interaction between entity linking and relation detection would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the performance gains to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings?  Have you explored other embedding methods or fine-tuned the embeddings on the KBQA data?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the error cases? Specifically, what types of relations or questions does the model struggle with?
3.  How well does the proposed approach generalize to other knowledge bases or QA datasets? Do you have any preliminary results on other datasets?
4.  The paper mentions that constraint detection is crucial.  Could you elaborate on why your approach outperforms previous work in this area, specifically regarding constraint detection? What aspects of the HR-BiLSTM model contribute to the effectiveness of constraint detection?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is reasonably thorough but could benefit from mentioning more recent advances in neural relation extraction, such as graph neural networks.
*   The description of the KBQA pipeline in Algorithm 1 is helpful, but could be more detailed. For example, how are the weights  α and λ  in steps 1 and 3 determined?
*   Minor: There are a few typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper (e.g., "hrs" should be "embeddings").
