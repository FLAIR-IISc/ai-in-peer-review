This paper addresses the challenging task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), focusing on issues arising from large relation vocabularies and zero-shot learning scenarios. The core idea is to improve relation detection by hierarchically matching questions to relations at both the word-level and relation-level, using a residual learning mechanism within BiLSTMs. The authors also propose a KBQA system incorporating this improved relation detection component and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on several benchmarks.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Hierarchical Relation Matching with Residual Learning:** The primary contribution lies in the novel HR-BiLSTM model. Combining word-level and relation-level matching, coupled with residual connections, demonstrably improves relation detection accuracy, particularly in the challenging KBQA setting. The residual learning aspect appears particularly valuable for training deeper networks and enabling more abstract question representations.
2.  **End-to-End KBQA System with Improved Relation Detection:** The authors integrate the improved relation detector into a complete, albeit simplified, KBQA pipeline. This provides a strong validation of the effectiveness of the relation detection component in a realistic application and demonstrates improved performance over existing methods. The entity re-ranking step further contributes to the overall system performance.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation and Ablation Studies:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies on standard KBQA datasets. These results convincingly show the benefits of each component of the proposed approach and provide insights into the relative importance of different design choices.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addressing a Critical KBQA Challenge:** The paper directly tackles a significant bottleneck in KBQA systems – accurate relation detection in the face of a large number of relations and unseen relations. This makes the work highly relevant and valuable to the KBQA research community.
2.  **Technical Soundness of the Proposed Method:** The HR-BiLSTM model is well-motivated and technically sound. The use of residual connections is a clever adaptation from computer vision and seems particularly well-suited to the challenges of training deep sequence models for relation detection.
3.  **Strong Experimental Results:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art or competitive results on established KBQA benchmarks, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies are particularly helpful in understanding the contributions of different components.
4.  **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The technical details are clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The use of Figure 1 and Figure 2 aids in understanding the system architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of hierarchical matching and residual learning is effective, the individual components (BiLSTMs, word embeddings, residual connections) are well-established techniques in NLP. The novelty primarily resides in their specific application and combination within the context of KBQA relation detection.
2.  **Simplistic KBQA Pipeline:** The KBQA system described is relatively simple, relying on existing entity linkers and lacking more sophisticated query generation or answer ranking components. While this simplicity allows for a clear focus on the impact of the relation detector, it limits the overall performance potential of the system.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The number of hyperparameters tuned (learning rate, hidden state size) indicates a sensitivity to tuning. While standard practice, this implies the results may not generalize without similar tuning effort to different datasets or knowledge bases.  More discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are impressive, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors the system makes could provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the HR-BiLSTM model compared to simpler relation detection approaches?
2.  The paper mentions that constraint detection is crucial for their system. Could you elaborate on the specific types of questions where constraint detection provides the greatest performance improvement?
3.  What are the limitations of your approach, and what future research directions do you see for addressing these limitations? Specifically, how would you adapt the model to handle more complex semantic parsing scenarios or knowledge bases with different characteristics?
