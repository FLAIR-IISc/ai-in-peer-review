This paper tackles the challenging problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), specifically addressing the issues of large relation vocabularies, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation questions. The core idea – hierarchical matching between questions and relations using a residual BiLSTM – is a compelling approach.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) architecture for relation detection. This approach, while building on established techniques (BiLSTMs, residual connections), cleverly combines them to address the specific challenges of KBQA relation detection, notably dealing with both relation-level and word-level representations and mitigating training difficulties in deep models. The empirical results demonstrating improvements over strong baselines, particularly on the WebQSP dataset, suggest the effectiveness of this architecture. The system-level evaluation showcasing SOTA results further validates the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of hierarchical matching *with* residual connections is a genuinely interesting idea. While individual components (BiLSTMs, residual learning) are well-known, their application within this specific hierarchical structure for KB relation detection is a novel contribution. It directly addresses the complexities of representing and matching relations with varying levels of abstraction.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides solid experimental evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Performance gains on both relation detection tasks *and* the end-to-end KBQA task demonstrate a clear benefit from the HR-BiLSTM model.  The ablation studies are well-conducted and insightful, justifying the design choices.
3. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges specific to KBQA relation detection that differentiate it from general relation extraction.  Highlighting the large vocabulary size, zero-shot learning, and multi-relation scenarios effectively motivates the proposed work.
4. **System-Level Integration:** The paper doesn't stop at relation detection alone; it integrates the improved detector into a complete KBQA pipeline and demonstrates its benefit in a realistic setting. This provides a more convincing demonstration of the approach’s practical value.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the ablation studies are good, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *what* types of relations or questions the model still struggles with. Understanding the error patterns would offer valuable insights for future work and demonstrate a deeper understanding of the model's limitations.
2. **Dependency on Existing Entity Linkers:** The KBQA pipeline relies heavily on the performance of an external entity linker. While acknowledging this, the paper doesn't explore the interplay between linker errors and relation detection performance. A more in-depth analysis of this interaction would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but doesn’t provide specifics on the search space or the computational cost.  It would be helpful to know how sensitive the performance is to different hyperparameter settings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically categorizing the types of relations or question structures where the model performs poorly?
2.  How does the performance of the relation detector degrade when using a less accurate entity linker? Have you experimented with any techniques to mitigate the impact of linker errors?
3. What was the computational cost of the hyperparameter tuning process? Were any automated hyperparameter optimization techniques used?
