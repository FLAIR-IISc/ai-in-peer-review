Summary
The paper targets relation detection in knowledge base question answering (KBQA), a setting with thousands of relations, zero-shot cases, and multi-relation chains. It proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that matches questions to relations represented at two granularities: (i) relation-level tokens (relation IDs or whole names) and (ii) word-level tokens (tokenized relation names). Residual connections across BiLSTM layers aim to facilitate complementary matching at different abstraction levels. The paper integrates this relation detector into a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step use of relation detection: entity re-ranking based on relation evidence, and final relation (or core-chain) selection, with an optional constraint detection step. Experiments on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP show improvements in both relation detection and end-to-end QA, with ablations supporting the contributions.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection that jointly leverages word-level and relation-level representations with residual connections for hierarchical matching. This addresses zero-shot and large relation vocabulary challenges and yields consistent gains over strong baselines.
2) A two-step integration of relation detection into KBQA: using relation scores to re-rank entity linker outputs, then detecting relations/core-chains with the topic entity masked. This simple pipeline demonstrates that better relation detection translates to end-task gains.
3) Empirical evaluation and ablations showing: (a) the importance of combining word-level and relation-level representations; (b) the benefit of residual learning over weighted sums or attention; and (c) improved KBQA results without complex joint inference or heavy feature engineering.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and appropriate modeling choice: The paper articulates the gap between standard relation extraction (small, closed label sets with both arguments given) and KBQA relation detection (thousands of relations, zero-shot, chains). Factoring relation names into word sequences addresses unseen relations; retaining relation-level tokens preserves global signals. The hierarchical matching setup is well aligned with these needs.

- Solid empirical improvements with thorough ablations: On relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves over a BiLSTM with word-level relations by 2.1% (93.3 vs 91.2) on SimpleQuestions and 3.2% (82.53 vs 79.32) on WebQSP test sets. Ablations isolate the contributions: removing relation names or words reduces accuracy; replacing residual with weighted sum or attention reduces performance, especially on WebQSP; replacing BiLSTM with CNN harms chain detection. These support the paper’s claims about hierarchical information and residual connections.

- Demonstrated impact on end-to-end KBQA: Using the improved relation detector in a simple pipeline yields +2–3% absolute gains over comparable baselines and achieves state-of-the-art on SimpleQuestions and comparable-to-SOTA on WebQSP, despite not using joint inference or sophisticated rerankers. This evidences that relation detection quality is a key driver of KBQA performance.

- Practical simplicity and portability: The pipeline is easy to adopt (plug-in relation detector; entity re-ranking via relation evidence; simple constraint detection). This enhances the contribution’s utility beyond the specific datasets.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior sequence matching and residual learning: Factoring relation names to words and using deep BiLSTMs for hierarchical features are established ideas; residual connections across layers are standard. The specific combination (relation-level + word-level + residual hierarchical matching) is useful but incremental. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis of why residual learning helps this task compared to attention-based hierarchical matching beyond reporting numbers.

- Incomplete or ambiguous training details and objective formulation: The ranking loss is written as lrel = max{0, srel(r+;q) + srel(r−;q)}, which appears incorrect (missing margin and sign), potentially a typo. Details on negative sampling (how many negatives per positive), margin value, and training stability are not fully specified. Hyperparameters such as l (top relations used in entity re-ranking), α and λ in the scoring equations, and K/K′ choices are only described as tuned but without values, affecting reproducibility.

- Fairness and coverage of baselines: While the paper compares to AMPCNN and char-trigram BiCNN, other competitive recent KBQA relation detectors (e.g., methods leveraging pre-trained KB embeddings or stronger attention architectures) are not extensively compared, especially for WebQSP chain detection. The WebQSP relation detection dataset is newly derived; the paper reports only internal baselines there, making it harder to position against the broader literature for that subtask.

- Limited qualitative/error analysis and zero-shot breakdown: The paper argues benefits for unseen relations but does not provide per-split performance (seen vs unseen relation types) or detailed error categories (e.g., entity ambiguity vs relation synonymy vs chain composition). Constraint detection is described with a character-overlap heuristic; there is little analysis of its errors or comparison to learned alternatives.

- Reproducibility and resource release: The WebQSP relation detection dataset is noted as “will be available soon.” Code availability is not specified. Given the pipeline involves multiple components and hyperparameters, releasing code and data is important for verification.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Loss/objective: Please clarify the ranking loss. Is it a margin-based loss l = max(0, γ − s(r+;q) + s(r−;q))? What margin γ and how many negatives per positive are used? How are negatives sampled?

- Hyperparameters and pipeline settings: What values were used for l (top relations for entity re-ranking), α (entity re-ranking weight), λ (combination of steps 1 and 2), K and K′? How sensitive are results to these choices?

- Relation and chain representation: How are relation names tokenized (e.g., handling underscores, camelCase)? For chains, are relation-level tokens the individual relation IDs along the path, and word-level tokens the concatenation of all relation words? Any positional encoding across chain steps?

- Zero-shot analysis: Can you provide results broken down by whether the gold relation type is unseen during training (SimpleQuestions), and similarly for unseen chain components in WebQSP?

- Constraint detection: Why choose character-overlap heuristics instead of a learned matching model? How much does this step contribute across different question types, and what are typical failure modes?

- Reproducibility: Will you release code for HR-BiLSTM and the derived WebQSP relation detection dataset, along with scripts to reproduce the KBQA pipeline results?
