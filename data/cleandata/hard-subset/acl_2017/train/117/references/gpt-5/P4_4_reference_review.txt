Summary and contributions
- The paper targets relation detection in knowledge-base question answering (KBQA), arguing that KB-specific settings differ from standard relation extraction due to large relation vocabularies, zero-shot relations, and relation chains. It proposes a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: word-level tokens from relation names and relation-level tokens (whole relation names and relation chains). Residual connections across BiLSTM layers enable hierarchical matching between different abstractions of question representations and relation representations.
- The paper integrates the relation detector into a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection strategy: (1) entity re-ranking using relations predicted directly from raw questions, to mitigate entity-linking ambiguity, and (2) relation detection conditioned on the top entity candidates after replacing the entity mention with a special token. An optional constraint detection adds non-core constraints via simple surface matching.
- Experiments on SimpleQuestions (single relation) and WebQSP (relation chains) show improved relation detection accuracy over strong baselines (e.g., AMPCNN, BiCNN). The KBQA pipeline achieves state-of-the-art or comparable end-to-end QA accuracy. Ablations indicate both hierarchical matching and residual connections contribute, with larger gains on WebQSP.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A hierarchical relation detection model that combines relation-level and word-level representations with residual connections across question encoder layers; empirically stronger than baselines and non-residual combinations.
2) A practical two-step integration of relation detection into KBQA, using relation-driven entity re-ranking that yields consistent end-task improvements.
3) A derived relation detection benchmark from WebQSP and comprehensive ablations demonstrating where gains originate (residual learning, granularity, encoder choice).

Strengths
- Addresses KB-specific constraints in relation detection: The use of relation word sequences handles unseen relations better than id-only approaches, while combining with relation-level tokens recovers global cues; this is well-motivated for KBQA where many relations are rare or unseen. Empirical results on SimpleQuestions (with ~14% unseen relations) support this design.
- Clear architectural innovation and ablation support: The residual hierarchical matching is justified and tested. Ablations show that (a) using both relation granularities is superior to either alone; (b) residual connections outperform naive weighted sums and an attention baseline; (c) deeper BiLSTMs help more on WebQSP, consistent with the need to capture longer-range context and relation chains.
- End-to-end effectiveness with a simple pipeline: Despite avoiding joint inference or heavy feature engineering, the system obtains state-of-the-art or competitive KBQA accuracy on both datasets. The impact of relation-driven entity re-ranking is quantified and substantial, indicating a robust way to mitigate entity ambiguity.
- Empirical rigor across tasks and alternatives: Results are provided for both single-relation and multi-relation settings, include comparisons to strong baselines (AMPCNN, char-3-gram BiCNN), and test alternative encoders (CNN vs BiLSTM), strengthening the evidence for the modeling choices.
- Practicality and portability: The method uses only relation names and off-the-shelf embeddings, with no need for pre-trained KB-specific relation embeddings or joint training with entity linkers, which increases applicability.

Weaknesses
- Limited zero-shot analysis: While the method is motivated by unseen relations, the experiments do not isolate performance on unseen vs seen relations or quantify improvement specifically for zero-shot cases. This limits direct validation of the claimed advantage in open-relation settings.
- Incomplete training and loss specification: The ranking loss is presented in a way that appears to omit a margin term or sign (typically max{0, m − s(r+;q) + s(r−;q)}); the notation states a constant parameter is used but is not explicit, and negative sampling specifics are not provided. Several hyperparameters critical to reproducibility (e.g., α and λ weights in re-ranking and query scoring, l for top relations, K and K′) are not detailed in tuning or scale normalization.
- Constraint detection is heuristic: The optional constraint module relies on character-overlap matching and special rules for dates/types. While effective in the pipeline, it is relatively brittle and may not generalize across KBs or to paraphrastic mentions; no comparisons to learned constraint models are given.
- Dependency on descriptive relation names: The approach assumes relation names are meaningful and tokenizable. For KBs with opaque relation IDs or sparse/low-quality labels, performance may degrade; this limitation is not empirically evaluated.
- Scalability and candidate generation details: For multi-relation (WebQSP), details on candidate chain generation (e.g., maximum path length, pruning strategy, candidate pool sizes, computational cost) are under-specified. The efficiency and scalability implications for large KBs are not discussed.
- Fairness and breadth of comparisons: Comparisons focus on a subset of prior systems. While the baselines are strong and contemporary to the datasets cited, broader comparisons (e.g., other sequence matching or neural semantic parsing approaches of the time) would strengthen the claim of state-of-the-art.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot evaluation: What is the accuracy on test instances whose gold relations (or relation chains) never appear in training? Can the gains be decomposed by seen vs unseen relations?
- Loss and training details: Please clarify the exact ranking loss form (including the margin), negative sampling strategy, batch composition, and how many negatives per positive were used.
- Hyperparameters: How were α (entity re-ranking) and λ (final scoring) tuned? Were scores normalized to be combinable across modules? What values for l, K, and K′ worked best?
- Candidate chain generation: What is the maximum chain length considered on WebQSP? How are candidate chains enumerated and pruned, and what are the typical candidate set sizes?
- Constraint detection: How sensitive is the overall QA accuracy to the character-overlap threshold and special rules? Have learned constraint detectors (e.g., neural matching) been tried?
- Generalization to other KBs: How does the approach behave when relation names are non-descriptive or noisy? Would subword/character-level representations of relations mitigate this?

Overall, the paper is clear in motivation and design, presents a technically sound hierarchical model with residual matching, and demonstrates meaningful improvements on two standard KBQA benchmarks. Clarifications on zero-shot behavior, loss/parameter details, and candidate generation would further strengthen the work.
