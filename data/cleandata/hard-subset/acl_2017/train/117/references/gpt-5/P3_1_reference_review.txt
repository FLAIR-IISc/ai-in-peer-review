Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles relation detection for KBQA, arguing that KB relations pose unique challenges: large relation vocabularies, zero-shot relations, and relation chains. The core technical contribution is HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that:
- represents relations at two granularities (word-level tokenization of relation names and relation-as-a-token “relation-level”),
- encodes questions with a 2-layer BiLSTM and introduces residual connections across layers to facilitate hierarchical matching between question representations and relation representations,
- trains with a margin-based ranking loss.

The paper also proposes a simple KBQA pipeline where relation detection is used twice: (i) to re-rank entity linker outputs by checking compatibility with relations suggested by the question, and (ii) to score relations (or core-chains) conditioned on each candidate entity (with entity mention replaced by <e>). A lightweight constraint detection via character-overlap matching is optionally applied.

On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, the model improves relation detection accuracy over strong baselines (AMPCNN, char 3-gram CNN/LSTM) and yields competitive or state-of-the-art end-to-end KBQA performance.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) A practical, empirically strong relation detector that combines dual-granularity relation representations with hierarchical residual matching, leading to consistent gains across SQ and WQ relation detection tasks and better KBQA.
2) A simple but effective two-step use of relation detection to re-rank entity linking candidates and then detect relations/core-chains, highlighting the centrality of relation detection for KBQA.
3) Ablations and analyses demonstrating that residual connections improve training and that word- and relation-level representations are complementary.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains with solid baselines and ablations. HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection by 2–3% over best baselines on both datasets, with statistical significance, and these gains translate into 2–3% end-task improvements without heavy feature engineering or joint inference. The ablations (no relation names, no relation words, no residual connections, CNN vs LSTM) convincingly support the design choices.
- Addresses zero-shot and large-vocabulary aspects. Factorizing relation names into words and combining with relation-level tokens is a sound approach to mitigate sparsity while preserving global cues; the SQ vs WQ behavior in Table 2 illustrates this point.
- Sensible pipeline design. Using relation signals to re-rank entity candidates is a practical insight addressing a known bottleneck. The formulation that intersects top-l question-predicted relations with an entity’s outgoing relations is simple and effective.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility within scope. Architecture, training setup, and hyperparameter search ranges are reasonably described (word embeddings, BiLSTM sizes, learning rates, residual variants), and ablations isolate contributions.
- Broader relevance. The work cleanly bridges general relation classification and KBQA specifics (open relation sets, relation chains, missing argument entities), with implications beyond the two datasets.

Weaknesses
- Modest novelty; largely an engineering refinement. Dual-granularity relation representations (word-level relation tokenization) and residual connections are known ideas; the innovation lies mainly in their combination and application to KB relation detection. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis of why residuals help here beyond training stability claims, or a more principled hierarchical interaction (e.g., learned gating rather than additive residuals).
- Limited analysis of zero-shot generalization. The paper motivates unseen relations and large vocabularies but doesn’t directly quantify performance on held-out relations or on varying proportions of unseen relation names/words. A controlled split showing zero-shot relation performance would strengthen claims.
- Entity re-ranking may be degree-biased and under-specified. The re-ranking score uses the maximum score among intersecting relations with top-l predicted ones; entities with higher degree (more relations) may benefit disproportionately. No normalization by entity degree or calibration analysis is presented. Sensitivity to l, K, K′, and α is not reported.
- Constraint detection is ad hoc and under-evaluated. The character-overlap heuristic is simple but brittle; there’s little quantitative isolation of its contribution (besides an ablation stating it’s “crucial”). Details and thresholds are deferred to the appendix, and there’s no error analysis contrasting constraint errors vs entity/relation errors.
- Reproducibility caveats for WQ relation detection. The paper constructs a new relation detection task from WebQSP; although it states the data will be released, comparability to prior work on exactly the same task is limited. Also, some implementation details are missing or ambiguous (e.g., margin in ranking loss formula appears omitted in the text; initialization of “relation sequence LSTMs with the final state of the word sequence” needs clarification).

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot and OOV analysis: Can you report performance on test relations unseen in training (at relation-level token granularity) and on OOV relation words? How does HR-BiLSTM compare to word-only or relation-only encoders in this setting?
- Residual design: Why simple additive residuals rather than concatenation plus projection or learned gating? Did you try layer-wise normalization to ensure comparable scales across layers?
- Re-ranking sensitivity and degree effects: How sensitive is entity re-ranking to l, α, K, K′? Did you control for entity degree (e.g., normalize by |Re|) to avoid favoring high-degree entities?
- Loss and negatives: What margin value is used in the ranking loss? How many negatives per positive are sampled, and what is the negative sampling strategy (in-batch, hard negatives among entity-adjacent relations)?
- Relation chain handling: What is the maximum chain length considered on WebQSP, and how are chains represented (concatenated tokens)? Any analysis of performance vs chain length?
- Attention baseline: The Parikh-style attention baseline underperforms. Could you share more details (tokenization, whether entity replacement was used, parameter count) to ensure a fair comparison?
- Resource parity: For KBQA comparisons, are KB snapshots, entity linker outputs, and candidate generation identical to STAGG/AMPCNN? Any differences that might affect comparability?

Additional comments
- Provide an error analysis partitioned by (i) entity linking vs relation vs constraint errors, (ii) lexical vs syntactic mismatch, and (iii) degree and ambiguity of entities/relations.
- Include calibration analysis of combining stage-1 and stage-2 scores (tuning β) and report robustness to that choice.
- If possible, release the WebQSP relation detection dataset and code to facilitate replication.
