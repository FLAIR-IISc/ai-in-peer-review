Summary and contributions
- The paper targets KB-specific relation detection within KBQA, focusing on the large, open relation inventory and zero-shot settings. It proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual matching model that jointly matches questions to relations at two granularities: (a) relation-level tokens (whole relation names/IDs and chains) and (b) word-level tokens (tokenized relation names). Residual connections across BiLSTM layers enable training stability and encourage complementary abstractions.
- It further integrates relation detection into the KBQA pipeline in two steps: (1) relation-aware entity re-ranking to resolve entity-linking ambiguity, and (2) relation detection per (re-ranked) entity with an optional heuristic constraint module. This simple pipeline yields SOTA or competitive results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP.
- Empirically, the model outperforms strong CNN/LSTM baselines for relation detection on both datasets, and the ablations isolate the gains from hierarchical matching and residual learning.

Strengths
- Clear, problem-driven modeling for KB relation detection at scale. The paper squarely addresses two KBQA peculiarities (thousands of relations and unseen relations), and the proposed word-level vs relation-level representations address generalization and global semantics in a principled way. This design choice is well motivated by realistic KBQA constraints.
- Hierarchical residual matching is effective and simple. The shortcut-based aggregation across two BiLSTM layers is a clean idea that avoids pitfalls seen in naive weighted combinations. The reported improvements over baselines (e.g., +2–3% absolute on SimpleQuestions/WebQSP relation detection) and over attention alternatives suggest a non-trivial modeling contribution.
- Strong empirical evidence and thorough ablations. The paper evaluates both relation detection and end-to-end QA. The ablations (removing relation names/words, removing residuals, replacing LSTM with CNN, altering depth) convincingly demonstrate where the gains come from. Significance is claimed and differences are non-trivial.
- Practical KBQA contribution: relation-aware entity re-ranking. The two-step use of relation detection to re-rank entities is simple yet impactful, addressing a known bottleneck (entity ambiguities). The ablation shows large drops without this step, underscoring its utility. This is a useful, reproducible trick for the community.
- Competitive end-to-end performance with a lightweight pipeline. Despite eschewing complex joint-inference or heavy re-ranking stacks, the system achieves SOTA on SimpleQuestions and competitive results on WebQSP. This suggests the relation detector is robust and the pipeline design is strong.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. While effective, the core ingredients—BiLSTMs, residual/shortcut connections, relation-name tokenization—are established. Combining relation-level IDs and word-level tokens is intuitive and has appeared in related forms. The residual application here is incremental rather than a fundamentally new concept or model class.
- Heuristic constraint handling and partial end-to-end story. Constraint detection relies on character-overlap heuristics and special rules (e.g., dates), which weakens the learning narrative and may understate difficulty in multi-constraint questions. The pipeline largely assumes strong off-the-shelf entity linking and heuristic constraint attachment rather than a unified learned model.
- Missing or incomplete analyses. The paper motivates zero-shot generalization but does not report a breakdown on seen-vs-unseen relations or explicit zero-shot settings; this would strengthen the main claim. There’s also little analysis of chain length (beyond 2), how often relation IDs vs tokens dominate, or why attention underperforms (beyond brief remarks).
- Comparisons and fairness. End-to-end comparisons are limited to STAGG and AMPCNN; other pertinent baselines (e.g., CFO, neural semantic parsing/programmer models) are discussed but not uniformly compared in the same KB and supervision regime. This makes it hard to position the approach against the most recent alternatives. Also, the attention comparison uses a decomposable NLI model variant; more tailored sequence matching baselines (e.g., BiDAF-style, cross-attention Transformers) might be fairer.
- Reproducibility gaps. Several key hyperparameters for the KBQA pipeline (top-l relations for re-ranking, K/K’, α, λ, thresholds) are tuned but not reported with values or sensitivity. The enumeration strategy for relation chains, max length, and candidate pool sizes are not fully specified, making it harder to assess scalability.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot breakdown: Can you report performance separately on test questions with unseen relations (w.r.t. training) vs seen, for both datasets? How much does the word-level representation contribute specifically in the unseen case?
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: Please provide the chosen values and sensitivity for l (top relations for re-ranking), K and K′, α in entity re-ranking, λ in the final score mix, and the constraint threshold θ. How robust is the pipeline to these choices?
- Chain enumeration: What is the maximum chain length considered on WebQSP? How are relation-chain candidates enumerated and pruned per entity, and what are the typical candidate set sizes?
- Ablation on entity replacement: Does replacing the entity mention with <e> ever hurt when the surface form conveys type or semantic cues? Have you tried retaining a type token or appending the surface form?
- Attention baseline details: Which exact attention architectures were tried for hierarchical matching? Could cross-attention between relation tokens (IDs+words) and question tokens be revisited to ensure a strong attention baseline?
- Speed/scalability: What are training/inference times and memory usage when scoring all relations connected to top-K entity candidates? How does this scale with larger KBs or richer graphs?

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is well executed, empirically strong, and practically useful. However, the central ideas feel incremental rather than opening a new direction. If the authors can strengthen the novelty case (e.g., deeper zero-shot analyses, more competitive baselines, and clearer evidence that hierarchical residual matching yields qualitatively different behavior), I would lean toward acceptance. As it stands, the contribution is solid but not sufficiently bold.
