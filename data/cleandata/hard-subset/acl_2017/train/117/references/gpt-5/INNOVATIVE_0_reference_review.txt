Summary and contributions
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA at scale (many relations, zero-shot, and relation chains). It introduces:
1) A hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) matcher that combines word-level and relation-name-level representations of KB relations and performs residual (shortcut) fusion across BiLSTM layers on the question side to better align short/long phrasal evidence.
2) A two-step relation detection pipeline for KBQA where relation scores are first used to re-rank entity linker outputs (relation-aware entity disambiguation), followed by relation (or relation-chain) selection with entity placeholders and a lightweight constraint detection.
3) Solid empirical validation: state-of-the-art relation detection on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, and improved end-to-end KBQA; thorough ablations isolating the effects of hierarchical granularity, residual connections, depth, and encoders.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with clear impact: The core idea—represent relations at two granularities and align them with multi-level question abstractions via residual connections—is straightforward yet effective. It directly addresses zero-shot generalization (via word-level) while preserving global cues (via relation-name tokens). The residual design meaningfully improves training and complementarity across layers.
- Strong empirical gains and breadth: On both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves by 2–3% over strong baselines. Gains translate to the KBQA end task despite a deliberately simple pipeline (no heavy joint inference or feature-rich reranking), indicating the relation module’s practical value.
- Thorough ablations and analysis: The paper disentangles the contributions of relation-name vs word-level representations, residual vs weighted-sum vs attention-based hierarchical fusion, deep vs shallow encoders, and LSTM vs CNN. Notably, residual connections help most on WebQSP, consistent with the need to handle longer contexts and relation chains.
- Pipeline contribution that addresses a bottleneck: The relation-aware entity re-ranking step is a neat and useful idea. It leverages relation evidence to disambiguate entities (e.g., TV writer vs baseball player), improving the whole KBQA pipeline with minimal engineering.
- New evaluation setup for relation detection on WebQSP: Framing relation detection (including chains) as a standalone task for WebQSP is a helpful contribution for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Splitting relation names into words and matching with deep encoders is established; residual connections are standard. The novelty lies in their specific combination and hierarchical residual fusion. While impactful, it feels like a careful system improvement rather than a bold new modeling paradigm.
- Analysis of zero-shot behavior is underdeveloped: The paper motivates zero-shot as a key challenge but does not provide breakdowns for seen vs unseen relations or detailed analysis of generalization (e.g., to novel chains), which would strengthen the core claim.
- Comparisons could be broader/deeper: While some baselines (char-3-gram BiCNN, AMPCNN) are included, related approaches that leverage pretrained relation embeddings (e.g., TransE-based) or sequence-to-sequence relation generators (e.g., character-level) are discussed but not thoroughly compared in controlled settings. A stronger head-to-head on zero-shot would be valuable.
- Constraint detection and search are simplistic: The character-overlap matching and top-1 core-chain decoding risk error propagation and may underuse available context. Although the system still performs well, this module feels heuristic compared to learned constraint handling or beam search with final reranking found in prior work.
- Some missing details and clarity: Key hyperparameters for entity re-ranking (α, l, K′, λ) and their tuned values, initialization effects (initializing relation LSTMs from word-level encodings), and runtime/scalability (cost of scoring all relations for an entity set) are not fully specified. The construction of relation-name tokens for chains (single token per chain vs per edge) could be clearer.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot analysis: Can you report performance stratified by seen vs unseen relations (and chains) on SQ and WQ? How much does the word-level representation help on truly unseen relation names?
- Relation-chain representation: For chains, do you treat the chain as a single relation-name token, or use multiple relation-name tokens (one per edge)? How are word-level tokens formed for chains?
- Residual fusion variants: You propose two shortcut strategies (between hidden states vs after max-pooling). Which is used in the main results? Can you provide ablations isolating each setting and the margin parameter values in the ranking loss?
- Entity re-ranking hyperparameters: What values are used for α, l, K′, and λ in practice? How sensitive is the pipeline to these choices? Do you share HR-BiLSTM parameters between step 1 and step 2, and is the model trained jointly or independently?
- Runtime and scalability: What is the average number of relations per candidate entity and per question on WQ? What is the inference time per question for relation detection in both steps?
- Constraint detection: Did you try learned constraint models or beam search with a final reranker? Any evidence that simple character-overlap is close to optimal, or cases where it clearly fails?

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. While the architectural novelty is moderate, the paper presents a clean, cohesive idea—hierarchical residual matching across relation granularities—that yields consistent, meaningful gains and a practical pipeline improvement (relation-aware entity re-ranking). The empirical work is careful and compelling, and the contributions will likely be useful to the KBQA community. Addressing the zero-shot analysis and clarifying hyperparameters/runtime would further strengthen the paper.
