Summary and contributions
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA and proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: relation words (tokenized relation names) and relation IDs (full relation names). The model uses deep BiLSTMs with residual (skip) connections to enable “hierarchical matching” between question representations at different abstraction levels and relation representations at both granularities. The relation detector is then used in a simple KBQA pipeline with a relation-aware entity re-ranking step and a lightweight constraint detection heuristic. The authors report improved relation detection on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, and corresponding gains on the KBQA end task.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: Multi-granularity relation representations combined with a residual two-layer BiLSTM yields improvements in relation detection and downstream KBQA on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, with ablations to support the design.
2) Practical pipeline contribution: A relation-aware entity re-ranking step that reduces entity linking ambiguities and improves end-task performance.
3) Dataset engineering: A derived WebQSP relation detection benchmark (from labeled parses), intended to evaluate relation and chain detection.

Strengths
- Solid empirical gains with ablations: On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP relation detection, HR-BiLSTM outperforms strong CNN/LSTM baselines (e.g., +2–3% absolute over the best BiLSTM/AMPCNN), with ablations showing that (i) combining word- and relation-level representations helps; and (ii) adding residual connections improves over a naïve two-layer sum. The improvements carry over to the KBQA end task (+2–3% within the same pipeline), which is valuable.
- Addresses a real KBQA pain point (entity ambiguity): The relation-aware entity re-ranking step is simple and appears effective. Given that entity linking often dominates errors in KBQA, this is a pragmatic addition with measurable impact (the ablation removing re-ranking significantly hurts).
- Sensible, established modeling choices: The approach builds on well-understood components (BiLSTMs, residual connections, max-pooling, cosine scoring) and a margin-based ranking loss, which lowers the risk of implementation instability and eases adoption.
- Coverage of zero-shot issues: Factorizing relations into word sequences helps handle unseen relations, a legitimate challenge in KBQA. The empirical trends (larger benefit on SimpleQuestions) are consistent with dataset properties.
- Competitive KBQA performance with a lean system: Despite avoiding heavy joint inference or feature-rich re-ranking, the pipeline achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results, underscoring the utility of better relation detection.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Combining word-level and relation-level representations and adding residual connections across BiLSTM layers is a fairly incremental adaptation of standard sequence matching techniques. The hierarchical matching is implemented as residual summation and max-pooling; the methodological lift over prior relation-word/char n-gram matchers (e.g., Yin et al., Yih et al.) is modest. Gains, while meaningful, are not transformative.
- Evaluation gaps and reproducibility risks: For WebQSP relation detection, the benchmark is newly derived; at submission time it is “to be released,” which limits verification. The end-to-end comparison set is narrow: stronger contemporaneous KBQA systems (e.g., CFO, neural symbolic approaches) are mentioned but not directly compared under comparable settings, and the claim of “state-of-the-art” is sometimes softened to “comparable,” which is ambiguous. The pipeline depends on external entity linkers; robustness to different linkers is not studied.
- Incomplete methodological details: Several critical specifics are underspecified, making reproduction difficult. The ranking loss omits the margin term in the equation; negative sampling strategy/size is unclear; hyperparameters for entity re-ranking (K, K’, l, alpha), the final query score interpolation, and constraint thresholds are not fully reported; details of handling relation chains (order/direction, composition beyond concatenation) are brief; no runtime/latency or scalability analysis for “relation detection on entity set.”
- Potential confounds in entity re-ranking: Re-ranking entities by the top-l relation scores associated with them can bias toward high-degree entities or those connected to generic, frequent relations. There is no control analysis normalizing for entity degree or relation popularity, nor an ablation varying l or using frequency-matched baselines to rule out such biases.
- Limited robustness/error analysis: There is no explicit zero-shot split evaluation to quantify unseen-relation generalization; sensitivity to entity linker quality (e.g., degraded linker) is not evaluated; attention-based alternatives were tried superficially (decomposable attention), but it is unclear whether these were thoroughly tuned. Statistical significance is reported for headline improvements but not for key ablations.

Questions to authors (to clarify during response)
- Loss and negatives: What margin value is used in the ranking loss? How are negative relations sampled—are all candidate relations used per instance, or is there subsampling? For WebQSP relation chains, how are negatives constructed (same length/different length)?
- Hyperparameters and settings: Please report exact values for K, K’, l, alpha in entity re-ranking, and the interpolation weight(s) used in final query scoring. How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Relation chains: How exactly are relation chains represented (order/direction), and how is composition handled beyond concatenating tokens? Did you consider sequence encoders over chains rather than pooling?
- Re-ranking confounds: Did you normalize for entity degree or relation frequency when re-ranking? Can you provide performance stratified by entity ambiguity and degree?
- Robustness: Do results hold with different entity linkers or when the linker’s top-1 accuracy is degraded? Can you report zero-shot performance where test relations (at ID level) are unseen in training?
- Reproducibility and resources: Will you release the WebQSP relation detection dataset, code, and all hyperparameters to fully reproduce Tables 2 and 3? What are training/inference runtimes for the relation detection and re-ranking steps on both datasets?

Overall, the paper offers a useful, practical improvement using established techniques, with clear empirical benefits. However, the methodological novelty is limited and several evaluation/detail gaps reduce confidence. With clarifications and resource release, the work would be a solid incremental contribution.
