Summary
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA and proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that jointly matches questions to relations at two granularities: word-level (tokenized relation names) and relation-level (whole relation identifiers). Residual connections across stacked BiLSTMs are used to enable hierarchical matching between question representations of different abstraction levels and relation representations. The model is integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection strategy: (i) a relation-aware entity re-ranking that disambiguates entities using relations inferred from the raw question; and (ii) relation (or relation-chain) detection with the topic entity replaced by a marker. On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, HR-BiLSTM achieves state-of-the-art relation detection results and improves end-to-end QA accuracy; ablations substantiate the effectiveness of hierarchical matching and residuals.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A strong relation detection architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines relation word-level and relation-level representations and uses residual hierarchical matching to improve robustness to unseen relations and longer contexts.
2) A simple yet effective KBQA pipeline that leverages relation-aware entity re-ranking and relation detection to achieve competitive or state-of-the-art end-to-end results without heavy joint inference or final answer re-ranking.
3) A clear formulation and evaluation setup for multi-relation (relation-chain) detection on WebQSP, with ablations that isolate the contributions of granularity, residuals, and encoders.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains on two benchmarks: The model improves relation detection accuracy by 2–3% over competitive BiLSTM/CNN baselines on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, with statistically significant margins. These upstream gains transfer to 2–3% end-to-end QA improvements in the proposed pipeline, matching or surpassing specialized systems (e.g., STAGG, AMPCNN) despite minimal pipeline engineering.
- Well-motivated hierarchical design: The paper convincingly argues and demonstrates that relation-level tokens capture global cues (e.g., longer phrases, skip-grams) while word-level tokens provide generalization to unseen relations; combining them outperforms using either alone, especially on SimpleQuestions where unseen relations are common.
- Residual matching improves training and performance: The residual connections across BiLSTM layers enable effective hierarchical matching; simple weighted-sum or attention alternatives underperform. The ablation showing a notable drop on WebQSP without residuals indicates training/representation benefits beyond mere model size.
- Practical entity re-ranking: Using relation signals to disambiguate entities in the initial candidate list is simple and impactful. The example and aggregate results show this step is important where entity ambiguity is a bottleneck; the system’s improvements are not solely from better relation scoring but from better entity-choice facilitation.
- Thorough ablations and clear setup: The paper compares against several sensible baselines (BiCNN with char n-grams, AMPCNN, CNN encoders), tests removing relation-level or word-level components, and explores residual vs attention, deep vs shallow encoders. Hyperparameter choices and training details are described, and a WebQSP relation detection dataset is promised for release.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of zero-shot generalization: While the motivation emphasizes unseen relations, the evaluation does not break down performance by seen vs unseen relations or by relation-chain length. It is therefore hard to isolate how much of the gain stems from better zero-shot behavior, rather than overall stronger matching.
- Scalability and efficiency underexplored: The approach scores all relations attached to candidate entities (potentially large sets in Freebase), both for re-ranking and for final detection. There is little reporting on candidate pool sizes, runtime, or memory footprint, which are important for real-world KBQA.
- Loss formulation clarity: The ranking loss is presented without an explicit margin term (the equation appears to omit it while mentioning a constant parameter). The exact negative sampling strategy (all-candidate vs sampled negatives) and margin value are unclear; this affects reproducibility and interpretation of training stability claims.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and under-evaluated: The constraint step uses character-overlap matching with thresholds and special rules (appendices). Its contribution is stated as crucial in the pipeline, but the method is brittle and not integrated with the neural model; more analysis of error types, threshold sensitivity, and alternatives (e.g., learned constraint scoring) would strengthen the claim.
- Comparisons and contemporaneous baselines: While the paper compares to several prior approaches, it does not consider stronger contextual encoders or pretrained models. If applicable, comparisons to modern encoders (e.g., contextual embeddings) or to more recent neural symbolic parsers would be valuable. At minimum, a discussion of expected interactions with pretrained encoders would help.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot and compositionality: Can you report relation detection accuracy split by (i) relations seen vs unseen in training (SimpleQuestions), and (ii) chain length on WebQSP? Do residual connections help more on unseen relations or longer chains?
- Loss and negatives: What is the exact ranking loss with margin, and how are negatives selected during training? Do you use all candidate relations per question or sample hard negatives?
- Efficiency: What are the average numbers of candidate entities (K, K’), candidate relations per entity, and relations considered during entity re-ranking and final scoring? Please provide runtime and memory usage for training and inference on SQ and WQ.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters: How sensitive are results to α (entity re-ranking interpolation), λ (final combination), l (number of top relations for re-ranking), and the residual connection variant (hidden-state vs pooled)? Which variant is used in the main results?
- Constraint detection: Can you quantify the standalone gain from the constraint module and provide an error breakdown (e.g., date constraints, type constraints, spurious matches)? Have you tried a learned constraint scorer using the same HR-BiLSTM or a lightweight encoder?
- Generality: Would HR-BiLSTM still help if relation texts include aliases/descriptions rather than only names, or if the KB uses verbose schema labels? Any experiments on alternative KBs?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed paper with clear motivation, careful ablations, and meaningful gains in both relation detection and end-to-end KBQA. Addressing the analysis and clarity gaps (especially zero-shot breakdown, scalability, and loss/negative sampling) would further strengthen the contribution.
