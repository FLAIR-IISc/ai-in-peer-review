Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA. It proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations using two granularities of relation representation: word-level tokens (split relation names) and relation-level tokens (entire relation names), with residual connections across stacked BiLSTMs on the question to “ease” optimization and enable multi-level matching. The model is used in a two-step KBQA pipeline: (i) relation-aware entity reranking, then (ii) relation detection with the entity mention masked. The system optionally attaches constraints via a character-overlap heuristic. Evaluations are on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP; the authors also construct a relation detection benchmark for WebQSP.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical finding that combining word-level and relation-level representations with residual connections yields modest but consistent gains for KB relation detection (especially for relation chains on WebQSP).
2) A simple but effective use of relation detection to rerank entity linking outputs, improving end-to-end KBQA.
3) A constructed relation detection benchmark derived from WebQSP.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements with ablations: The paper demonstrates 2–3% absolute improvements in relation detection over strong baselines on both SQ and WQ, and correspondingly 2–3% gains on end-to-end KBQA in their pipeline. The ablations (w/o rel words, w/o rel names, no residuals, CNN vs BiLSTM) are informative and suggest each component contributes.
- Sensible modeling choices grounded in prior work: Using split relation names for zero-shot robustness and whole-relation tokens for global cues is reasonable and addresses known issues (unseen relations and short-context biases). The observation that LSTMs help with relation chains compared to CNNs is plausible and supported by results.
- Practical pipeline impact: Leveraging relation detection to rerank entities is a pragmatic idea that addresses a known bottleneck in KBQA. The reported drop when removing reranking indicates this step is impactful.
- Some statistical testing: The paper reports significance (p-values) for the main gains, which is appreciated.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and incremental methodology: HR-BiLSTM combines known ingredients—multi-granularity inputs, stacked BiLSTMs, residual connections (He et al., 2016), and cosine similarity—with standard max pooling. Prior KBQA relation detection has already used word/char sequence representations for relations (e.g., Yih et al., 2015; Yin et al., 2016; Golub and He, 2016), and the hierarchical/residual design is a straightforward adaptation of established techniques. The methodological advance feels incremental rather than conceptually new.
- Methodological clarity issues: Key training details are underspecified or potentially incorrect. The ranking loss is written as l = max(0, s(r+;q) + s(r−;q)) with “a constant” mentioned but no margin term in the formula; standard pairwise hinge should be max(0, m + s(r−;q) − s(r+;q)). Negative sampling strategy, batch composition, dropout/regularization, and optimizer details are missing. It’s unclear how the model is trained for step 1 (raw question) vs step 2 (entity-masked) without a domain shift. The construction of relation-level tokens for chains and their embeddings initialization are not fully specified. These omissions hinder assessment of soundness and reproducibility.
- Evaluation gaps and baselines: Comparisons focus on AMPCNN and a char-3-gram BiCNN; stronger contemporary KBQA/relation detection baselines (e.g., CFO; models using pre-trained KB embeddings; joint neural semantic parsers) are not thoroughly compared under matched settings. Claims of state-of-the-art would be more convincing with broader baselines and clearer protocol parity (same preprocessing, tuning budgets, and embeddings).
- Lack of targeted analyses: The paper motivates zero-shot robustness but does not report performance split by seen vs unseen relations on SQ, or by chain length on WQ. There is little error analysis. The reported significance lacks description of the test procedure (paired bootstrap, stratified permutations, etc.).
- Reproducibility and resource release: The constructed WebQSP relation detection dataset is “to be released,” and no code is provided. Many crucial hyperparameters (K, K′, ℓ, α, λ, thresholds) and candidate set sizes are not fully documented nor is a sensitivity analysis included. This is problematic for verifying the claims.
- Potential confounds in end-to-end gains: The end-to-end improvements might be partly due to heuristic constraint detection and entity reranking rather than the core hierarchical residual matching. Although ablations are mentioned, the precise quantitative impact of removing each (with full Table 3 numbers) is not shown here, making it hard to attribute gains solely to the relation detector.
- Scalability and efficiency: Scoring “all relations associated” with entity candidates on full Freebase can be large; there is no runtime analysis, memory footprint, or discussion of scalability.

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- Please correct/clarify the ranking loss: what is the exact formula, margin m, and sign convention? How are negative samples chosen per query? What optimizer, batch size, dropout, and regularization are used?
- Training/data mismatch: Is the model trained on raw questions, entity-masked questions, or both? How do you avoid distribution shift between step 1 (raw) and step 2 (<e>-masked)?
- Relation tokenization details: How do you tokenize relation names (underscores, camelCase)? For chains, do you include both the chain as a single token and individual relation names? How are unseen relation-level tokens initialized and updated?
- Can you report SQ results split by seen vs unseen relations and WQ by chain length? This would directly support the zero-shot and chain claims.
- Please provide the full Table 3 numbers, including ablations: removing entity reranking, removing constraint detection, and using different top-L relation sets for reranking. Also report the exact K, K′, ℓ, α, λ, and threshold values and sensitivity.
- Baseline parity: How were AMPCNN and BiCNN reimplemented/tuned? Did you use the same embeddings and preprocessing? Can you add comparisons to CFO or models using pre-trained relation embeddings for zero-shot?
- Efficiency: What are the average sizes of candidate entity sets and relation pools per question for SQ and WQ? What is the runtime per question and training time on your hardware?
- Resource release: Will you release code, trained models, and the WebQSP relation detection dataset to ensure reproducibility?

Overall, the paper addresses a relevant problem and shows consistent empirical gains, but the technical novelty is limited and several methodological and reproducibility details are missing. I encourage the authors to clarify the loss/training, broaden and equalize baselines, add targeted analyses (zero-shot, chain length), and release resources.
