Summary and Contributions
- The paper targets relation detection for KBQA and proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: word-level (tokenized relation names) and relation-level (whole relation names as tokens). Residual connections across stacked BiLSTMs are used to ease optimization and enable hierarchical matching.
- The model is embedded in a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection scheme: (i) relation-aware entity re-ranking of the initial entity linker output; (ii) relation detection conditioned on each re-ranked entity with entity placeholdering, plus an optional heuristic constraint detection via string matching on a local subgraph.
- Empirically, the approach improves relation detection accuracy on SimpleQuestions (SQ) and a derived WebQSP (WQ) relation detection task, and yields strong end-to-end KBQA results (SOTA on SQ with ensemble; competitive on WQ).

Strengths
- Empirical gains on two widely used KBQA benchmarks. The reported 2–3% absolute improvements in relation detection over strong neural baselines (AMPCNN, BiCNN) translate into better end-to-end QA, including a new SOTA on SimpleQuestions (using top-3 model ensemble).
- Sensible decomposition and thorough ablation. The paper carefully ablates: removal of relation-name tokens, removal of word-level tokens, replacing residual with weighted-sum and attention, single-layer vs deep, and replacing BiLSTM with CNN. These support the claim that both granularity and residual hierarchical training matter, especially on WQ (multi-relation setting).
- Relation-aware entity re-ranking is a simple, general idea with clear practical impact. The ablations show it significantly contributes to end-to-end accuracy by mitigating entity linking ambiguity, a known bottleneck in KBQA.
- The method itself relies on established components (BiLSTMs, residual/skip connections, cosine similarity with max-pooling). This reduces complexity relative to fully joint systems and plausibly aids reproducibility and adoption.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. Using both word-level and relation-level representations has precedent (e.g., character/word n-grams and tokenized relation names in prior KBQA), and residual/skip connections across stacked sequence encoders are standard. The main novelty is their specific combination and application, which feels incremental.
- Claims about zero-shot/generalization are insufficiently substantiated. While the motivation focuses on unseen relations, the evaluation does not report performance breakdowns on seen vs unseen relations (e.g., the 14% unseen in SQ). The observed gains from adding relation-name tokens likely benefit seen relations; for unseen ones, relation-level tokens provide no signal. A stratified analysis is needed.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines. For WQ, comparisons to other strong KBQA systems that leverage relation embeddings or joint inference (e.g., CFO and contemporaneous semantic parsers/neural programmers) are missing or only qualitatively referenced. For relation detection, a baseline using pre-trained KB relation embeddings (TransE-style) is mentioned in related work but not compared.
- The WebQSP relation detection benchmark is constructed by the authors and “will be made available soon.” This undermines reproducibility and makes it hard to validate absolute numbers. Precise construction details (candidate pool definition, negatives sampling, chain formation) should be fully specified and released.
- Loss function and training details are underspecified/possibly incorrect. The ranking loss is written as l = max{0, s(r+;q) + s(r−;q)} without a margin and sign; typically one uses max{0, m − s(r+;q) + s(r−;q)}. It is unclear whether a margin was used, how negatives were sampled, whether embeddings were fixed or fine-tuned, and how many epochs or early stopping criteria were applied.
- Efficiency and scalability not discussed. Relation detection is run over all relations connected to candidate entities (in the first step) and over all entity-associated relations (in the second). There is no reporting of candidate pool sizes, runtime, memory, or scalability on larger KBs. Practicality is important for adoption.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and task-specific. The string-overlap heuristic on subgraphs likely yields gains on WQ, but feels ad hoc and may not generalize. A comparison to simple type/datetime detectors or to prior constraint modules would help.

Questions to Authors
- Zero-shot analysis: Please report relation detection performance separately for (i) test relations seen in training vs (ii) unseen relations (and for WQ, seen vs unseen core-chains). How much of the gain comes from each?
- Loss/optimization: What exact ranking loss was used (margin value, sign)? How were negative relations sampled (all candidates vs sampled), and how many per instance? Were word embeddings fine-tuned? What regularization (dropout, weight decay) was applied?
- Candidate sizes and efficiency: What are typical K, K’, and l, and average sizes of relation candidate pools per step on SQ and WQ? Provide runtime per question and GPU/CPU resources used.
- Baselines: Can you add comparisons with a relation-embedding baseline (e.g., TransE-initialized relation encoders, or CFO), and/or plug your relation-aware entity re-ranking into those systems to isolate the effect of the re-ranking step?
- Fairness of ensemble: Your SQ SOTA uses an ensemble of top-3 detectors. What is the single-model performance under the same settings? Do baselines benefit similarly from ensembling?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, trained models, and the constructed WQ relation detection dataset (with scripts to re-derive negatives and chains)?
- Constraint detection: How sensitive are results to the threshold θ? Can you compare against a simple learned constraint classifier (e.g., type/date detection) to show robustness?

Additional Comments
- Some notation and descriptions are hard to follow (e.g., initializing relation LSTMs with “final state of the word sequence” needs clarification). Proofread equations and symbols for correctness.
- Please clarify whether the attention baseline was tuned comparably (single- vs two-way attention, size, dropout). Also specify the significance test methodology.
