Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA. It proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that matches questions against two granularities of relation representations: (i) word-level tokens from the relation’s name, to better handle unseen relations; and (ii) relation-level tokens (whole relation names and chains), to capture global/long-span matching signals. Residual connections across BiLSTM layers stabilize learning and enable complementary abstraction levels. The model is integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection: first, relation scores are used to re-rank entity-linking candidates; second, relations are scored conditioned on the top entity (with <e>-replacement). A lightweight constraint detection module further filters candidates. The approach achieves state-of-the-art relation detection results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP relation-detection tasks, and strong end-to-end KBQA results.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical residual sequence matching model (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection that fuses word-level and relation-level representations, with residual training across BiLSTM layers. Primary contribution lies in the empirical effectiveness and careful ablations demonstrating the importance of both granularity and residual connections.
2) A simple two-step KBQA pipeline leveraging relation detection to re-rank entity candidates before relation scoring, yielding strong end-to-end results with minimal feature engineering.
3) Empirical evidence across two datasets (SQ and WQ) and ablation studies (residual vs weighted sum vs attention; word-level vs relation-level; LSTM vs CNN) clarifying which choices matter.

Strengths
- Strong, consistent empirical gains with clear ablations:
  - Relation detection: HR-BiLSTM improves over solid baselines by 2–3% on both SQ (93.3% vs 91.2% best baseline) and WQ (82.53% vs 79.32%). Ablations quantify contributions of hierarchical matching and residual training (e.g., 82.53% vs 80.65% without residual).
  - End-to-end: The pipeline with HR-BiLSTM improves 2–3% over replacing it with prior detectors, reaches SOTA on SQ, and is competitive on WQ despite not using heavy beam search or feature-rich re-rankers.
- Sensible modeling of relation granularity and abstraction:
  - Splitting relation names into word tokens improves generalization to unseen relations, while preserving relation-level tokens provides global matching signals often missed by word-level-only representations. The two-level question encoder (two BiLSTM layers) aligns well with these granularity needs, and residual connections make the two levels complementary and trainable.
- Practical pipeline innovation: Using relation detection to re-rank entity-linker candidates is simple and effective for resolving entity ambiguity (e.g., Mike Kelley writer vs baseball player). The paper shows this step materially contributes to end-to-end gains.
- Comparisons and alternatives: The paper compares against character trigram (BiCNN), AMPCNN, CNN encoders, and attention-based hierarchical matching (Parikh et al.), providing a convincing case for the chosen architecture.
- Minimal engineering and KB-agnostic design: The approach integrates with existing entity linkers and requires no specialized features, suggesting good portability.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: The components (word-level decomposition of relation names, deep BiLSTMs, residual/skip connections) are known; prior KBQA works already used character/word-level relation decompositions and relation-chain encoders. The novelty is mainly in their specific combination with residual hierarchical matching, which, while useful, may be viewed as an engineering advance rather than a conceptual leap.
- Clarity and missing training details:
  - The ranking loss is written incorrectly (missing the negative-minus-positive structure and margin term); please provide the exact formula and margin value.
  - Several key hyperparameters and settings are not reported (K/K’ for entity lists, l for top relations in re-ranking, alpha and lambda in score combination, threshold theta for constraints). Sensitivity analysis is missing.
  - The description of “initializing the relation sequence LSTMs with the final state of the word sequence” is unclear. Are there two separate BiLSTMs with shared parameters? How exactly are initial states set for BiLSTMs (both directions)?
- Zero-shot claims not directly evaluated: While the motivation emphasizes unseen relations, there is no breakdown of performance on seen vs unseen relations (e.g., on SQ where ~14% of test relations are unseen). This weakens the zero-shot generalization claim.
- Fairness and scope of comparisons:
  - For SQ there exist other strong models (e.g., character-level generative models, TransE-based relation embeddings) that are not compared. For WQ, STAGG uses more sophisticated search; your system uses a different pipeline. Although you discuss this, a more controlled comparison (same candidate sets/beam) would strengthen claims.
  - The “top-3 relation detectors” result is an ensemble; comparisons to single-model SOTA should be distinguished clearly.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and under-specified: The character-overlap matching and special rules (dates/types) can be brittle; no precision/recall analysis is provided. It’s unclear how often constraints help vs hurt, and which constraint types are covered.

Questions to authors
- Please provide the exact ranking loss, margin, and negative sampling strategy. How many negatives per positive?
- What values did you use for K, K’, l, alpha, lambda, and threshold theta on each dataset? Any sensitivity plots?
- How are relation chains enumerated (max length, number of candidates per entity, pruning criteria)? What is the average candidate set size per question in WQ?
- How are relation names tokenized for both word-level and relation-level inputs (schema labels vs IDs, normalization)? How do you handle multi-word or alias names?
- Can you report performance on seen vs unseen relations (e.g., on SQ) to substantiate the zero-shot argument?
- Provide runtime/throughput for relation scoring and the full pipeline on SQ and WQ.

Overall
This is a solid empirical paper with a well-motivated, simple architecture and pipeline that deliver clear gains on two standard KBQA settings, supported by informative ablations. While the novelty is moderate, the work is carefully executed and practically useful. I recommend acceptance after clarifying training details, adding a seen/unseen analysis, and tightening the methodological description.
