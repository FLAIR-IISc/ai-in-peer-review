Summary and claimed contributions
- A hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) for KB relation detection that jointly matches questions to relations at two granularities: (i) treating relation names as atomic tokens (relation-level) and (ii) decomposing relation names into words (word-level). Residual connections across stacked BiLSTMs are used to “hierarchically” combine question representations.
- A simple KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection twice: first to rerank entity linker outputs via relation scores, then to detect the final relation (or chain) given the top entities, followed by a lightweight constraint detection step.
- Empirical results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP showing improvements on relation detection (2–3% absolute) and competitive end-to-end KBQA, with ablations suggesting both granularity and residual connections help.

Strengths
- Empirical gains with careful ablations. On relation detection, the proposed model outperforms strong baselines (AMPCNN, BiCNN char-trigram, BiLSTM w/ words) by 2–3% on two datasets, and ablations indicate both (a) combining relation-level and word-level and (b) residual connections contribute. The end-to-end KBQA improvements (2–3% over your re-implemented baselines) are consistent with these gains.
- Practical pipeline that addresses a real bottleneck. The entity reranking using relation signals is a sensible, low-overhead addition to standard KBQA pipelines that rely on off-the-shelf entity linkers. The paper shows that this step helps mitigate entity ambiguity and benefits downstream relation detection.
- Reasonable coverage of alternatives and controls. The paper compares residual vs. attention-based hierarchical matching, single- vs. two-layer encoders, and LSTM vs. CNN encoders, and reports that the residual formulation is more stable and effective, especially on WebQSP where relation chains appear.
- Attention to zero-shot challenges. Factoring relation names to word sequences and initializing name-level encoders from word-level states are reasonable design choices for unseen relations; the SimpleQuestions improvements support this motivation.
- Reproducibility alignment with prior setups. For SimpleQuestions, the use of AMPCNN’s question–relation pairs and entity linking outputs, and for WebQSP, the use of S-MART linker, helps ensure comparability to published baselines.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. Combining word-level and relation-level representations and using residual connections over stacked RNNs are established ideas; similar decompositions (word-level, char-level) for relations and hierarchical matching have been explored. The proposed “hierarchical residual matching” is an incremental architectural tweak rather than a fundamentally new method.
- Incomplete transparency of experimental setup. Several critical hyperparameters are unspecified or under-described: the top-l relations used for reranking, K and K′, α and λ mixing weights, margin, negative sampling, optimizer, and early stopping criteria. Without these, reproduction is difficult and the gains may be sensitive to these choices.
- Constructed WebQSP relation detection task and data availability. The WebQSP relation detection dataset is newly created and “will be released soon.” This hampers verification. It is unclear how candidate relation chains were enumerated, how large the candidate pools are, and whether the negatives include challenging confounders. Since this task underpins key claims (82.53% accuracy), release is important.
- Fairness and scope of comparisons. For relation detection on WebQSP, the baselines are relatively limited (BiCNN char-trigram, BiLSTM, CNN). Stronger sequence matching/attention baselines (e.g., more competitive hierarchical attention or alignment models) are not included; the chosen attention baseline (decomposable attention) is not tailored to this task. For end-to-end KBQA, comparison is mainly to STAGG and AMPCNN; other joint entity–relation linking or weakly supervised semantic parsers are not evaluated in the same setting.
- Evaluation gaps and analysis. There is no breakdown of performance for seen vs. unseen relations, single vs. multi-relation chains, or by candidate pool size. Statistical significance is reported for relation detection but not for end-to-end KBQA. The constraint detection is a heuristic character-overlap matcher; its contribution is presented but not rigorously analyzed (e.g., precision/recall of constraints, error types, or sensitivity to threshold).
- Reliance on ensembling to reach top numbers. The best SimpleQuestions results require ensembling multiple relation detectors, blurring how much the core model alone contributes versus model averaging.

Questions to authors
- Please specify all missing hyperparameters and settings: l (top relations for reranking), K and K′, α for reranking combination, λ for combining steps 1 and 2, the margin in the ranking loss, optimizer, batch size, negative sampling strategy, and early stopping criteria.
- For the WebQSP relation detection dataset, how are candidate relation chains constructed? What are the average candidate pool sizes (single- vs. multi-hop), and how many chains per example? Do you include chains not observed in training (true zero-shot)?
- Can you report performance broken down by seen vs. unseen relations on SimpleQuestions, and by chain length on WebQSP? Also, the impact of reranking as a function of initial linker top-1 accuracy.
- Are the end-to-end KBQA gains statistically significant? Please provide significance testing and confidence intervals.
- How sensitive are results to α, λ, and l? Provide a small sensitivity analysis.
- When will the WebQSP relation detection dataset and code be released? Will you also release the constraint detection module and all preprocessing needed to reproduce Tables 2–3?
