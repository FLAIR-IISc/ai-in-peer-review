Summary and key contributions
This paper targets relation detection within KBQA and proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that matches questions to both relation-level tokens (whole relation names/cores chains) and word-level tokens (tokenized relation names). The model builds multi-granularity relation representations and multi-level question representations, and uses residual connections to stabilize and improve hierarchical matching. The authors also integrate the detector into a simple KBQA pipeline with an additional entity re-ranking step driven by relation scores, and a lightweight constraint detection heuristic. They report state-of-the-art relation detection on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, and strong end-to-end KBQA results.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A hierarchical multi-granularity matching model (HR-BiLSTM) with residual connections for relation detection, combining relation-level and word-level representations and demonstrating consistent gains over competitive baselines.
2) A simple but effective KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection to re-rank entities, yielding clear improvements in end-to-end accuracy without complex joint inference or heavy feature engineering.
3) Empirical evaluation with ablations demonstrating the value of residual connections, multi-granularity relation representations, and deep question encoders; and showing strong results on both single- and multi-relation datasets.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains on two standard benchmarks. On relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves over strong baselines (e.g., 93.3% vs 91.2% on SimpleQuestions and 82.53% vs 79.32% on WebQSP for BiLSTM w/ words), with reported statistical significance. The improvements translate to 2–3% absolute gains in end-to-end KBQA and SOTA on SimpleQuestions, comparable to SOTA on WebQSP.
- Well-motivated multi-granularity design. The paper identifies a real tension: word-level representations generalize to unseen relations but miss global cues; relation-level tokens capture global cues but suffer from sparsity. Jointly matching both and aligning them with different levels of question abstractions is a sensible and effective design, substantiated by ablations showing drops when either granularity is removed.
- Residual connections materially help hierarchical matching. The ablation replacing residual by simple weighted sums or decomposable attention degrades performance (e.g., 82.53% to 80.65/81.38 on WebQSP), suggesting the residual formulation eases optimization and encourages complementary use of layers rather than collapsing to one.
- Practical KBQA pipeline contribution. The entity re-ranking step using relation scores is simple yet impactful, addressing a documented bottleneck in entity linking via entity–relation collocation. Ablations indicate notable drops without this step, and the pipeline achieves strong performance despite avoiding complex final re-rankers/joint inference.
- Comprehensive ablations and comparisons. The paper contrasts relation-name vs word-level encodings, deep vs shallow encoders, BiLSTM vs CNN, residual vs attention, and reports dataset-dependent behaviors (e.g., WebQSP benefits more from depth/residuals than SimpleQuestions), offering useful insight.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior sequence matching and multi-granularity ideas. Splitting relation names into words is known in KBQA; deep encoders for multi-level representations are common; residual connections are broadly adopted. The contribution is a careful combination tailored to KB relation detection, but the architectural novelty is incremental. Stronger comparisons to contemporary multi-level cross-attention or highway/gated residual variants would strengthen the case.
- Incomplete training objective specification and clarity issues. The ranking loss is written as max{0, s(r+;q)+s(r−;q)} without a margin or the usual sign; presumably it should be max{0, γ − s(r+;q) + s(r−;q)}. Several implementation details are underspecified: negative sampling strategy, candidate pool sizes, exact residual variant used in final runs (hidden vs pooled), margin γ, l for top-l relations in re-ranking, K and K′, α and λ weights, and the threshold θ for constraints. This limits reproducibility.
- Limited analysis of zero-shot generalization and error modes. A main motivation is unseen relations; yet there is no explicit breakdown of performance on seen vs unseen relations, or per relation-chain length. The paper asserts SimpleQuestions benefits more from word-level due to unseen relations, but quantitative evidence is not provided. A small qualitative/error analysis would aid understanding.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and underdeveloped. The method relies on character overlap thresholds and special rules for dates/types; while effective in their pipeline, it is brittle and dataset-specific. There is no comparison to stronger neural constraint/linking baselines or to leveraging KB type signals. The claim that constraint detection is “crucial” would be stronger with more detailed ablation numbers and sensitivity analyses.
- Baseline coverage and fairness. The relation detection baselines are competitive (AMPCNN, BiCNN char-ngrams, BiLSTM), but newer (at the time) cross-attention or joint models could be included or at least discussed more thoroughly. For WebQSP KBQA, comparisons to more recent multi-hop neural semantic parsers or strong graph-based rankers would help contextualize results.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the ranking loss: what is the exact formula, margin value, and negative sampling strategy? Are all relations connected to the topic entity used as negatives, or do you include cross-entity negatives?
- Which residual variant is used for the main reported results (hidden-state skip vs max-pooled skip), and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- How do you handle unseen relation-level tokens at test time? When a relation name was unseen during training, is its embedding initialized from the word-level encoder’s final state every time, or precomputed? Do you update such embeddings during inference?
- Can you report performance breakdowns for seen vs unseen relations on SimpleQuestions, and for different chain lengths on WebQSP? Also, how does HR-BiLSTM perform when the correct relation is absent from training entirely?
- Please quantify the effect of the entity re-ranking step on entity linking accuracy (e.g., top-1/top-5 EL before vs after re-ranking) and on downstream QA, and provide sensitivity to l, K, K′, and α.
- For constraint detection, can you report the proportion of questions where constraints are added, the gains on those, and robustness to the threshold θ? Have you tried neural encoders or type-aware scoring for constraints?

Overall, this is a solid, empirically strong paper with a pragmatic, well-motivated architecture and pipeline. While the novelty is incremental, the technical ideas are executed well and bring consistent gains. Addressing clarity/reproducibility gaps and providing deeper analyses of zero-shot behavior and constraint handling would strengthen the work further.
