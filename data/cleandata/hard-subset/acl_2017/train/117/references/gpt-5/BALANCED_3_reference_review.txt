Summary
This paper targets relation detection in knowledge base question answering (KBQA), a setting with thousands of relations, zero-shot relations at test time, and relation chains. The authors propose HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that matches questions to relations at two granularities: (i) word-level (tokenized relation names) to handle unseen relations and local cues, and (ii) relation-level (relation names as single tokens) to capture global/idiosyncratic patterns. Deep BiLSTMs are used to produce different abstraction levels of question representations, and residual connections enable effective hierarchical matching. The model is integrated into a simple two-step KBQA pipeline: use relation detection to re-rank entities from an external linker, then perform relation detection with the topic entity masked, followed by a lightweight constraint detection step. On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection accuracy and yields state-of-the-art or competitive end-to-end KBQA results, with extensive ablations.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines relation-word and relation-name representations, with residual connections across question-encoding layers to stabilize training and improve performance.
2) A simple but effective KBQA pipeline leveraging relation detection to re-rank entities before core relation selection, yielding 2–3% gains and strong end-task results without heavy joint inference.
3) Thorough empirical study with ablations demonstrating the value of multi-granularity relation representations and residual hierarchical matching on both single- and multi-relation benchmarks.

Strengths
- Solid empirical gains with careful ablations: On relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves over strong baselines (e.g., BiLSTM with words, AMPCNN) by 2–3% on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. The ablations are informative: removing relation names or relation words hurts; replacing residual with weighted sum or attention degrades performance; swapping BiLSTM for CNN especially hurts on chain-heavy WebQSP. These results support the paper’s central claims.
- Addresses key KBQA challenges: The design (factorizing relation names to words + retaining relation-level tokens) explicitly targets zero-shot and data sparsity issues, while deep encoders help with longer contexts and relation chains. The model yields particularly notable gains on WebQSP, where longer dependencies and chains matter.
- Practical pipeline contribution via entity re-ranking: Using relation scores from the raw question to re-rank entity linker outputs is simple yet impactful, mitigating entity ambiguity (e.g., “Mike Kelley”). The ablation shows removing this step significantly reduces end-task performance, underscoring the method’s practical importance.
- Clarity of methodology and reproducible setup: The architecture is clearly described with two residual variants; training relies on standard pre-trained word vectors; experiments use widely used datasets and public entity linking outputs. The creation of a relation detection task from WebQSP is sensible and the authors indicate plans to release it.
- Competitive end-to-end results with a light-weight system: Despite avoiding joint inference and heavy re-ranking, the system achieves new SOTA on SimpleQuestions and competitive results on WebQSP. The multi-detector ensemble further boosts SimpleQuestions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in components: The core ideas—multi-granularity matching, deep BiLSTMs, residual connections—are adaptations of well-known techniques. The contribution is more in engineering and integration than fundamentally new modeling. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical or analytical insight into why residual hierarchical matching helps beyond empirical gains.
- Loss function and training clarity: The ranking loss is written as lrel = max{0, srel(r+;q) + srel(r−;q)} without a margin or the conventional sign; this appears to be a typo (standard is max(0, γ − s+ + s−)). Also unclear: how relation-name “tokens” are embedded—are they separate learned embeddings or composed from word pieces? How are unseen relation-name tokens handled? This ambiguity hinders reproducibility.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and under-analyzed: The character-overlap-based constraint module is brittle and task-specific. Given its substantial contribution on WebQSP (authors note it is “crucial”), a deeper evaluation (types of constraints handled, error modes, comparison to stronger neural constraint models) is needed to assess robustness and generality.
- Insufficient analysis of zero-shot and scalability: While the motivation emphasizes unseen relations and large candidate spaces, the experiments lack targeted analysis (e.g., performance split on seen vs unseen relations, relation frequency buckets, chain lengths). Computational cost is not reported (per-question latency, effect of l and K, degrees in WebQSP), which matters when scoring relations for multiple entities twice.
- Presentation issues and incomplete references: There are several formatting problems (footnotes, missing years, inconsistent citation formatting) and small notational ambiguities (e.g., initialization “back-off” for unseen relations). Statistical significance reporting (p-values) lacks details on tests and setup.

Questions to authors
- Loss and training: Please clarify the exact ranking loss (including margin and signs) and negative sampling strategy. How many negatives per positive are used?
- Relation-name embeddings: Are relation-name tokens part of the same vocabulary as words or a separate embedding table? How are embeddings initialized and updated? How are unseen relation-name tokens handled at test time?
- Residual variant: Which residual variant (pre-max-pooling vs post-max-pooling) performs best per dataset? Any intuition from training dynamics (e.g., layer-wise norms) supporting hierarchical complementarity?
- Zero-shot analysis: Can you report performance on unseen relations (w.r.t. training) for SimpleQuestions and your WebQSP split, and across relation/chain frequency buckets?
- Computational efficiency: What are average candidate set sizes per step (|ELK|, |R_e|, l), and end-to-end inference time per question on each dataset? How sensitive is performance to l, K, K′, α, and λ?
- Constraint module: Which constraint types (dates, types, locations, names) benefit most? Could you compare against a simple neural string/mention encoder or type-aware filtering?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, trained models, and the WebQSP relation detection split? How are ambiguous topics handled in entity replacement when the mention is not a contiguous span?

Overall, this paper offers a well-executed, practically useful approach that meaningfully improves relation detection and KBQA performance. Addressing the clarity issues, adding targeted zero-shot/scalability analyses, and strengthening the constraint modeling and its evaluation would further solidify the contribution.
