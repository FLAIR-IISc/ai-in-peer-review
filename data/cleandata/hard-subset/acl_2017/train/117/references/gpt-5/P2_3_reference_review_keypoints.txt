- Summary: Proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual sequence-matching model using word-level and relation-name-level representations plus multi-layer question encoders, applied in a two-step KBQA pipeline (entity re-ranking then relation scoring) with 2–3% gains on relation detection and improved end-to-end KBQA.

- Contribution 1: HR-BiLSTM combines relation-word and relation-name tokens with multi-layer question encoders via residuals, improving relation detection for single- and multi-relation settings.

- Contribution 2: Introduces a practical two-step pipeline that re-ranks entity candidates by relation evidence before relation scoring, reducing entity-linker ambiguity and improving end-to-end performance.

- Contribution 3: Provides empirical ablations (granularity, residual vs alternatives, LSTM vs CNN) and evaluates on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP to isolate what design choices matter.

- Strength: Clear motivation and modeling for relation granularity and zero-shot scenarios; word-token decomposition helps unseen relations while relation-name tokens preserve global cues.

- Strength: Strong empirical gains with significance testing; HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection by ~2–3% and end-to-end KBQA by ~2–3% with a minimal pipeline.

- Strength: Ablations systematically show both granularities matter, residuals outperform weighted-sum/attention variant, and LSTM > CNN for relation chains.

- Strength: Practical entity re-ranking using relation scores effectively disambiguates entities and is a major contributor to end-task improvement.

- Strength: Method generalizes across single-triple (SQ) and multi-relation chain (WQ) QA, increasing applicability.

- Strength: Minimal dependencies—uses only surface forms (questions and relation names), no KB embeddings or heavy features—easy to adopt.

- Weakness: Architectural novelty is limited; combining granularities and residuals is incremental and alternatives (e.g., layer attention) are only lightly explored.

- Weakness: Training details are under-specified (ranking loss margin, negative sampling, hyperparameter choices K, K′, ℓ, α, λ, θ) and reproducibility is hindered.

- Weakness: Baseline coverage is incomplete; missing comparisons to other relation-detection/KBQA methods (e.g., CFO relation module, char-level seq2seq, KG-embedding approaches).

- Weakness: Zero-shot/unseen-relation claims are not rigorously quantified (no seen vs unseen performance breakdown or analysis of back-off initialization effects).

- Weakness: Constraint detection is heuristic and loosely integrated (character-overlap rules, date/type heuristics) and may be brittle with potential error propagation from core-chain reliance.

- Question: Provide seen vs unseen relation/chain breakdowns for relation detection on SQ and WQ and quantify how often relation-name tokens are unseen.

- Question: Specify ranking loss margin and negative sampling strategy—how many negatives and from which pools; how candidate relations are constructed during training.

- Question: Report chosen hyperparameter values (K, K′, ℓ, α, λ, θ) and a sensitivity analysis demonstrating robustness.

- Question: Provide computational cost: average candidate set sizes for re-ranking and per-entity relation detection, and runtime impact of the two-step approach versus a single-step baseline.

- Question: Clarify residual vs attention: which residual variant worked best (hidden-state vs post-pooling), exact performance differences, and whether more advanced attention (e.g., multi-head/cross-layer) was tried or outperformed residuals.

- Question: Quantify constraint detection behavior: frequency of added constraints, precision/recall, error breakdown (missed vs incorrect constraints), and concrete handling of dates/types.

- Question: Will the WebQSP relation detection dataset and code be released at camera-ready for reproducibility?
