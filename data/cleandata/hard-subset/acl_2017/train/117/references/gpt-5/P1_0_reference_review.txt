Summary and contributions
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA and proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM model that matches questions against relations represented at two granularities: word-level (tokenized relation names) and relation-level (relation identifiers or whole names, including relation chains). The model uses deep BiLSTMs to encode questions with different abstraction levels and employs residual connections between layers to facilitate training and enable complementary matching. The authors further integrate this detector into a simple KBQA pipeline that uses relation detection twice: first to re-rank entity linking candidates, then to select the relation/core-chain for the topic entity, followed by a lightweight constraint detection step. Experiments on SimpleQuestions (single-relation) and WebQSP (multi-relation) show improved relation detection accuracy and strong end-to-end KBQA performance.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An effective relation detection architecture that combines word- and relation-level representations with hierarchical residual matching, yielding consistent gains over strong neural baselines on both SQ and WQ.
2) A simple yet effective use of relation detection to re-rank entity candidates, which alleviates entity ambiguity and improves end-to-end KBQA without heavy joint modeling.
3) An extensive ablation demonstrating the value of multi-granularity relation representations and residual learning for multi-relation matching, especially on WebQSP.

Strengths
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements on relation detection. On SQ, HR-BiLSTM reaches 93.3% vs the best baseline ~91.3%; on WQ, 82.53% vs 79.32% with detailed ablations. The improvements hold across model families (BiLSTM/CNN/char-3gram) and design alternatives (weighted sums, attention, CNN encoders), supporting the approach’s robustness.
- Clear motivation and evidence for multi-granularity relation representations. The paper convincingly shows the trade-off between word-level generalization (important for unseen relations) and relation-level global cues, and that combining them matters (SQ: 93.3% vs 91.2% words-only and 88.8% relation-only; WQ: 82.53% vs 81.69%/79.68%).
- Residual learning improves deeper matching, notably for multi-relation WQ. Residual connections yield +1.9% absolute over weighted-sum and +1.15% over attention, suggesting the training benefits are substantive, not just architectural convenience.
- Practical KBQA pipeline improvements with minimal engineering. Using relation detection to re-rank entities is simple and intuitive, addresses a well-known bottleneck in entity linking ambiguity, and yields measurable end-task gains. Despite omitting joint inference/beam search/final re-ranking, the system achieves state-of-the-art on SQ and competitive results on WQ.
- Comprehensive ablations and comparisons. The paper compares with AMPCNN, BiCNN char-trigram, single-layer vs deep, residual vs attention, and BiLSTM vs CNN encoders; it also analyzes the effect of removing entity re-ranking and constraint detection.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate. Using word-level relation sequences and whole relation tokens has appeared in prior work; hierarchical matching and residual connections are known techniques. The main technical novelty is in combining these ideas with residual summation across deep BiLSTM layers for relation-question matching. While effective, the conceptual step is incremental rather than groundbreaking.
- Limited analysis of zero-shot/generalization claims. A core motivation is unseen relations and large relation vocabularies. However, the paper does not provide a breakdown of performance on seen vs unseen relations (e.g., on SQ where ~14% are unseen). It would strengthen claims to quantify gains specifically on unseen relations and long chains.
- Sparse methodological details hinder reproducibility. Important hyperparameters and settings are under-specified: values of K/K′ for entity lists, l (top-l relations for re-ranking), α and λ weights in scoring, the margin in ranking loss, which residual variant was chosen (hidden-state vs pooled), chain length limits, and optimization details beyond hidden sizes and learning rate. Computational cost and latency of the two-step detection are not discussed.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and may be dataset-specific. The string-overlap heuristic (plus special rules for date/answer types) is brittle; yet the end-task gains rely significantly on this step (authors note it is “crucial”). This raises questions about portability to other KBs or domains with noisier surface forms and whether a learned matcher would be preferable.
- Fairness and scope of end-to-end comparisons. While the pipeline is intentionally simple, comparisons to stronger joint systems (e.g., with beam search, global inference, or re-rankers) are limited. The “multiple relation detectors” ensemble improves SQ further; the impact on WQ is less clear. An explicit comparison isolating the contribution of the entity re-ranking step vs the improved detector would be helpful (Appendix C is referenced but not shown).

Questions to authors
- Can you report relation detection accuracy split by seen vs unseen relations (and by chain length) on SQ and WQ? This would directly substantiate the zero-shot motivation.
- What are the exact values (and sensitivity) of K, K′, l, α, λ, margin, and residual variant used in the best models? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- What is the maximum core-chain length supported/trained on WQ? How does performance vary with chain length?
- What is the computational cost (per question) for the two-step relation detection and the constraint detection stage? Any pruning strategies beyond top-l?
- How does the approach handle KBs where relation IDs are opaque (no meaningful tokenization)? Would character n-grams or descriptions replace relation words?
- Will you release code and the constructed WQ relation detection dataset? If not, can you provide full details to reproduce the candidate relation pools and training pairs?

Additional comments
- The claim of statistical significance is appreciated; please describe the test used and sample sizes.
- Minor clarity: explicitly state whether residual connections are applied over hidden states or pooled representations in the best setting, and provide a brief training curve or loss comparison to support the “training easier” claim.
