Summary
This paper targets relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). It argues that KB relation detection differs from standard RE due to large relation vocabularies, zero-shot relations, and relation chains. The core technical contribution is a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to both relation-level tokens (entire relation names or chains) and word-level tokens (tokenized relation name words), with residual connections across stacked BiLSTM layers on the question to ease training and enable multi-granularity matching. The model is integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline that (i) re-ranks entity linker candidates using relation scores predicted from the raw question and (ii) runs relation detection conditioned on each candidate entity (with entity mention replaced by <e>), followed by a lightweight constraint detection heuristic. The approach achieves strong gains on relation detection (SimpleQuestions, WebQSP) and competitive or SOTA results on end-to-end KBQA.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical, multi-granularity relation detection model (HR-BiLSTM) that combines relation-level and word-level representations with residual learning across question encoder layers, yielding consistent 2–3 point absolute gains over strong baselines.
2) A simple but effective two-step KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection to re-rank entity candidates, substantially mitigating entity linking errors and improving overall QA accuracy without heavy joint inference or feature engineering.
3) A thorough empirical evaluation with ablations (granularity, residual vs weighted sum vs attention, CNN vs LSTM encoders) on both single-relation (SQ) and multi-relation (WQ) benchmarks; and a new relation-detection formulation for WebQSP (to be released).

Strengths
- Clear motivation and principled design for multi-granularity matching: The distinction between relation-level and word-level matching is well-motivated by zero-shot needs (unseen relation names) versus global semantic cues (entire relation tokens). Table 1 and Figure 2 concretely justify why both levels are needed. Ablations show using both substantially outperforms either alone (e.g., 93.3 vs 91.2/88.8 on SQ).
- Residual learning materially improves optimization and accuracy: The hierarchical residual connections across question layers are not just architectural polish; they address training difficulties the authors identify (imbalanced layer contributions). The residual variant outperforms weighted-sum and attention alternatives (e.g., 82.53 vs 80.65/81.38 on WQ), with a plausible explanation that WQ benefits from deeper abstraction for longer contexts/relations.
- Strong empirical results on two complementary datasets: On SimpleQuestions, HR-BiLSTM reaches 93.3 relation detection accuracy and improves end-to-end QA by 2–3 points, setting SOTA with model ensembling. On WebQSP, it improves relation detection (82.53 vs 79.32) and achieves competitive end-to-end performance, despite using a relatively simple pipeline.
- Practical KBQA pipeline with minimal engineering: The two-step relation detection, especially entity re-ranking via relation scores, is simple and effective. The constraint detection heuristic is lightweight yet yields meaningful gains, suggesting a good balance between simplicity and performance. This makes the approach appealing for practitioners.
- Solid ablations and comparisons: The paper re-implements strong baselines (char trigram BiCNN, AMPCNN), controls for encoder type (CNN vs LSTM), and examines architectural choices (depth, residual placement), giving credibility to claims about where the improvements come from.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: Splitting relation names into words, using relation-name tokens, and applying attention/CNN/LSTM encoders for question–relation matching are well-explored. The main novelty—residual hierarchical matching across BiLSTM layers combined with dual granularity relation representation—is a sensible composition of known ideas rather than a fundamentally new paradigm. The empirical gains are meaningful but not transformative.
- Heavy reliance on relation name quality: The approach assumes meaningful, human-readable relation names. In KBs where relation labels are opaque/normalised or noisy, or in domains with limited lexical overlap, the gains from word-level matching may diminish, and relation-level tokens may be too sparse. The paper does not evaluate robustness to such conditions.
- Limited analysis and reporting details: Key hyperparameters and design choices (e.g., values of α, λ, l, K, K′; candidate pool sizes; details of initializing relation sequence LSTMs from word sequence states) are not fully specified, making reproducibility harder. Runtime/memory costs and scalability with very large relation/chain spaces are not discussed.
- Fairness and breadth of comparisons: On WebQSP, comparison is primarily to STAGG and an in-house BiCNN baseline. More recent neural semantic parsers or embedding-based zero-shot relation baselines (e.g., TransE-initialized models) are not included. The constraint detection component is heuristic and dataset-specific; it is unclear how much of the end-to-end gain is attributable to the relation model vs. constraint heuristics. A controlled ablation isolating constraint detection across systems would help.
- Zero-shot claims not directly quantified: While improvements on SQ suggest better handling of unseen relations, the paper does not explicitly report performance on seen vs unseen relations, nor does it measure generalization to novel relation surface forms or longer chains.

Questions to authors
- Could you report seen vs unseen relation performance on SimpleQuestions to substantiate the zero-shot generalization claim? Similarly, for WebQSP, how often do test core-chains contain unseen components?
- Please clarify the “initialize the relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence” step: Which sequence initializes which (relation-name by relation-word, or vice versa), and how exactly is this implemented?
- What are the chosen values for α, λ, l, K, and K′, and how sensitive are results to them? What are the average candidate set sizes before and after entity re-ranking?
- How does the method scale with larger KBs and longer chains (e.g., >2 hops)? Any results beyond length-2 chains?
- Can you release the WebQSP relation detection dataset and code to ensure reproducibility? Also, training/inference time and parameter counts would be helpful.
- How robust is HR-BiLSTM to noisy or opaque relation labels (e.g., Wikidata Q-properties)? Have you tried character-level or subword representations to mitigate this?

Overall, the paper is well-motivated, methodologically sound, and empirically strong, with a practical KBQA impact. The novelty is moderate but the execution is careful and convincing.
