Summary
The paper targets relation detection within knowledge base question answering (KBQA), a sub-problem that differs from standard relation extraction due to large relation vocabularies, zero-shot relations, and multi-hop chains. The authors propose HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that matches questions to relations at two granularities: word-level tokens derived from relation names and relation-level tokens (entire relation names or chain segments). Residual connections across stacked BiLSTMs on the question side are used to stabilize training and encourage complementary abstraction levels. The model is integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline with two-step relation detection: (i) entity re-ranking using relation signals from the raw question and (ii) relation detection conditioned on a chosen topic entity, followed by an optional constraint detection module based on string overlap. Experiments on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP show state-of-the-art or competitive results for relation detection and improved end-to-end QA.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical relation matching formulation combining word-level and relation-level representations with residual learning on deep BiLSTMs for questions, yielding consistent gains over strong baselines on two datasets.
2) A simple yet effective KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection to re-rank entity candidates and to select core chains, demonstrating that stronger relation detection substantially boosts overall QA without complex joint inference.
3) Thorough ablations dissecting the contributions of relation granularity, residual connections, encoder choice (BiLSTM vs CNN), and attention-based alternatives.

Strengths
- Solid empirical improvements with careful ablations. The HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection by 2–3% absolute over strong baselines (e.g., BiLSTM with word-level relations) on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. Ablations convincingly show that (a) using both relation-level and word-level tokens matters, and (b) residual connections outperform both naïve weighted sums and a decomposable attention baseline.
- Addresses KBQA-specific challenges. Splitting relation names into words mitigates unseen relation issues common in KBQA; adding relation-level tokens preserves global cues that word-only models may miss. This directly targets the large-vocabulary and zero-shot nature of KB relations.
- Practical integration into a KBQA pipeline. The two-step relation detection, especially entity re-ranking based on relation signals, is a simple and effective way to reduce the search space and alleviate entity ambiguity. The paper shows measurable downstream gains (2–3% absolute on QA) attributable to the relation detector, not to heavy feature engineering or beam-search.
- Analysis across datasets with differing characteristics. The paper contrasts SimpleQuestions (single hop, more unseen relations) vs WebQSP (multi-hop chains), and the results substantiate the design choices: LSTMs help chains; residual learning helps when longer contexts matter; relation-name tokens help capture longer patterns.
- Reproducibility-enhancing details and comparisons. The authors re-implement prior baselines (BiCNN, AMPCNN), tune hyperparameters, and provide meaningful ablations (e.g., replacing BiLSTM with CNN, removing residuals). This strengthens the credibility of claims.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental. Hierarchical matching of word- and relation-level signals and residual connections are reasonable adaptations of known ideas rather than fundamentally new architectures. Prior work has already leveraged subword/word relation representations and character- or word-level matching for KBQA; the main novelty is their particular combination with residual BiLSTMs.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and may obscure the relation detector’s contribution. The constraint module relies on character-level overlap with special rules (e.g., for dates). While effective, it is ad hoc and may limit generalization to other KBs or languages. More principled neural or learned constraint modeling would strengthen the end-to-end story.
- Missing or unclear technical details. The ranking loss is specified as l = max{0, s(r+; q) + s(r−; q)} with a reference to a margin parameter, but the margin does not appear explicitly; this looks like a typo and raises reproducibility concerns. It is also unclear how negative samples are selected and how large candidate pools are per instance in both steps. Optimization details (optimizer, batch size, early stopping criteria) are sparse.
- Limited analysis of zero-shot behavior and chains. The paper argues that word-level representations help unseen relations, but does not provide a breakdown by seen vs unseen relations on SimpleQuestions, or by chain length on WebQSP. Such analyses would better substantiate claims about generalization and the benefits of residual hierarchical representations.
- Dataset and resource availability. The WebQSP relation detection formulation is constructed by the authors; at submission time they state it will be released “soon”. For an ACL submission, concrete release plans (URLs, licensing) are important for reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Loss function: Please clarify the exact ranking loss, including the margin value and how it is applied. Is it l = max(0, margin + s(r−; q) − s(r+; q))? What negatives are used per instance (all candidates vs sampled)? How is class imbalance handled?
- Candidate pools: In step 1 (entity re-ranking), how many relations per entity are typically considered, and what is l (the number of top relations from the global detector)? How sensitive are results to K, K′, l, and α?
- Zero-shot analysis: Could you report performance on seen vs unseen relations on SimpleQuestions? Also, a breakdown on WebQSP by chain length would help understand where HR-BiLSTM helps most.
- Residual design: Which of the two residual variants (residual over hidden states per position vs over max-pooled layer outputs) is finally used per dataset? Any insights into why one works better?
- Constraint detection: How many questions benefit from constraint attachment, and what are the most common constraint types? Have you tried learned neural matching for constraints, and if so, how did it compare?
- Resources: Will you release code, pretrained models, and the constructed WebQSP relation detection dataset, including scripts to reproduce candidate generation and all hyperparameters?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide seen/unseen and chain-length breakdowns, plus error analyses contrasting cases where relation-level vs word-level tokens dominate.
- Replace or complement heuristic constraint detection with a learned module (e.g., contextual encoders over neighboring nodes/relations) and measure its impact.
- Compare to using KB-embedded relation vectors (e.g., TransE/RotatE) concatenated with token-level representations to test whether structural embeddings yield further gains.
- Report computational costs (training/inference time, parameter counts) and analyze the trade-off between depth and performance.
- Ensure public release of the new relation detection datasets and code to facilitate benchmarking.
