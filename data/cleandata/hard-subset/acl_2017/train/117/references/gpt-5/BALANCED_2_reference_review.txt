Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA), a setting with thousands of relations, zero-shot test relations, and relation chains. The authors propose a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: (i) relation-level tokens (whole relation names) to capture global cues and (ii) word-level tokens (tokenized relation names) for compositional generalization. Residual connections over a two-layer BiLSTM are used to facilitate hierarchical matching. The model is embedded in a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection strategy: using relation signals to re-rank entity linking candidates, then detecting relations conditioned on each candidate entity, with an optional heuristic constraint detection step. Experiments on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP show gains in relation detection and KBQA, with ablations analyzing the impact of components.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A hierarchical relation detection model (HR-BiLSTM) that jointly leverages relation word tokens and relation-name tokens with residual connections, yielding consistent gains over strong sequence-matching baselines.
2) A simple but effective two-step integration of relation detection into KBQA that improves entity disambiguation via relation cues and yields state-of-the-art or near-SOTA end-task performance without heavy joint inference.
3) An explicit formulation and evaluation of relation detection on WebQSP (including chains), with ablations that clarify why residual hierarchical matching helps more on multi-relation settings.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements with solid ablations: HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection on SimpleQuestions (93.3% vs. 91.3% AMPCNN) and WebQSP (82.53% vs. 79.32% BiLSTM words). Ablations isolate the contribution of relation-name tokens, residual connections, and deeper encoders. Replacing BiLSTM with CNN hurts especially on WebQSP, supporting the claim that sequence modeling helps chain composition.
- Practical pipeline benefit via entity re-ranking: The first-step relation detection to re-rank entity linking candidates is simple and effective, addressing a known bottleneck. The paper provides a concrete mechanism and shows that removing this step degrades KBQA substantially, indicating that relation signals are highly informative for entity disambiguation.
- Addresses zero-shot generalization intuitively: By tokenizing relation names to word sequences and retaining relation-level tokens, the model can generalize to unseen relations while still exploiting global co-occurrence patterns when available. The SimpleQuestions results (larger gains when many test relations are unseen) support this motivation.
- Clear problem framing for multi-relation detection: Treating relation chains as sequences and comparing the effect of deeper/residual architectures on WebQSP is well motivated and empirically validated. Residual connections notably help on WebQSP, consistent with the need to aggregate longer-range context.
- Methodological transparency of key design choices: The paper articulates how hierarchical matching is performed (two residual variants), what representations are compared (cosine with max-pooled question and relation vectors), and contrasts residual with naive weighted-sum and attention alternatives.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to current standards: Combining relation word-level and relation-name tokens, and adding residual connections over a stacked BiLSTM, are incremental extensions of established sequence matching approaches. Prior KBQA work also tokenizes relations, uses character or word n-grams, and applies deep encoders with attention. The paper’s novelty rests on a specific combination and training recipe rather than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm.
- Missing strong contemporary baselines: There is no comparison to transformer-based encoders (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa/DeBERTA) for question–relation matching, either as bi-encoders or cross-encoders, which are now standard for text matching and often outperform RNN/CNN architectures. Without such baselines, it is difficult to assess the competitiveness of HR-BiLSTM today.
- Zero-shot and compositional generalization not quantified: Although the paper motivates unseen relations as a core challenge, it does not provide a breakdown of performance on seen vs. unseen relations, nor an analysis by chain length or relation-token overlap with training. This makes it hard to attribute gains to the hierarchical design versus better fitting on seen relations.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and underdeveloped: The final constraint step relies on character-overlap matching with special rules for dates. While effective in this pipeline, it is brittle and may not generalize beyond the evaluated datasets; stronger learned constraint/relation-argument models or type-aware reasoning are not explored.
- Reproducibility and evaluation details: Several important implementation choices are underspecified: negative sampling strategy for the ranking loss, the value/range for top-l relation aggregation and the mixing weights (alpha, lambda), candidate pool sizes per entity, runtime/efficiency, and how relation names are tokenized across KBs. The WebQSP relation detection dataset is stated as “to be released,” and code is not provided. Comparability on the end-task may be affected by differences in KB subsets, candidate recall, and pipeline choices; statistical significance is only reported for subtask results, not for KBQA.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot analysis: What is the accuracy on test relations unseen during training (and on partially seen relations)? Can you report performance by chain length and by lexical overlap between relation names and questions?
- Transformer baselines: How does HR-BiLSTM compare to (i) a bi-encoder with BERT/RoBERTa encoders for question and relation tokens and (ii) a cross-encoder that jointly encodes question–relation pairs? Can your hierarchical residual idea be layered on top of transformer encoders?
- Negative sampling and training: How are negatives constructed per question (all candidate relations vs. sampled across KB)? What margin was used in the ranking loss, and how sensitive are results to it?
- Entity re-ranking specifics: What values of top-l relations and mixing weight alpha worked best? How large is the candidate entity set before and after re-ranking on each dataset, and what is the time cost?
- Relation representation: How are relation names tokenized (e.g., handling camelCase, punctuation, mid identifiers)? For chains, do you include both individual relations and chain-level tokens, and how are chain names formed?
- Constraint detection: What proportion of final correct answers rely on the constraint step? Can you provide an error analysis of failures due to constraint detection, and have you tried learned models for constraint matching?

Decision
Weak reject. The paper is well-motivated, empirically solid for its design choices, and practically useful; however, the methodological novelty is limited and the absence of transformer-based baselines and detailed zero-shot analyses weakens the contribution under current standards. Strengthening with modern baselines, deeper generalization analyses, and improved constraint modeling would significantly improve the paper.
