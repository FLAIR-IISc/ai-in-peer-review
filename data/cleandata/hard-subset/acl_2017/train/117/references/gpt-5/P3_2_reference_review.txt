Summary and contributions
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA, a setting with large relation vocabularies, zero-shot relations, and relation chains. The core technical contribution is a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) for question–relation matching that:
- Represents relations at two granularities: word-level tokens derived from relation names and relation-level tokens (full relation names, including chains).
- Learns multi-level question representations with a 2-layer BiLSTM and uses residual connections to perform hierarchical matching between question layers and relation representations.
The model is used twice in a simple pipeline KBQA system: first to re-rank entity candidates by scoring relations connected to each entity, then to score relations (or short chains) given a chosen entity, followed by an optional constraint detection step. On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, the method improves relation detection accuracy by 2–3% over strong baselines and yields SOTA or comparable end-to-end KBQA.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A hierarchical residual matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines relation word-level and relation-level representations with multi-level question encodings, yielding consistent gains over CNN/LSTM/attention baselines for relation detection.
2) A practical two-step use of relation detection to re-rank entity candidates before relation prediction, improving disambiguation in KBQA and boosting end-to-end accuracy without heavy joint inference.
3) Ablations that diagnose where gains come from (granularity combination, residual connections, LSTM vs CNN, attention vs residual).

Strengths
- Addresses KBQA-specific challenges with principled design: The dual granularity of relation representation directly targets zero-shot/large-vocab issues, and the residual hierarchical matching aligns well with the observation that relation tokens align with variable-length question phrases. The initialization of relation-name encoders from word-level encoders is a thoughtful back-off for unseen relations.
- Solid empirical gains with careful ablations: On both SQ and WebQSP, HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection by 2–3% over strong baselines (AMPCNN, BiCNN, word-level BiLSTM). Ablations show clear contributions from (i) using both granularities, (ii) residual hierarchical matching vs weighted-sum or attention, and (iii) LSTM over CNN especially for relation chains. Statistical significance is reported.
- Simple KBQA pipeline yet competitive: Despite avoiding complex joint inference or heavy re-ranking with hand-crafted features, the system reaches SOTA on SQ and is comparable on WQ. The entity re-ranking via relation scores is a simple, effective idea that concretely addresses entity ambiguity, a known bottleneck.
- Clearer analysis on dataset differences: The paper discusses why SQ benefits more from word-level generalization (more unseen relations) while WQ benefits more from deeper residual structure (longer contexts and chains), matching empirical patterns in the ablations.
- Reusability: The approach is modular (plug-in relation detector), making it useful beyond the specific pipeline.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to existing ideas: Breaking relations into words has been explored (e.g., Yin et al., Liang et al.), and residual connections for sequence models are known. The main novelty is their specific residual hierarchical matching between two question layers and mixed relation granularities. While the combination is well-motivated and executed, the conceptual advance is moderate rather than fundamental.
- Limited zero-shot analysis: The paper claims advantages for unseen relations but does not provide explicit breakdowns by seen vs unseen relation performance, nor analyses of how much of the gain comes from word-level generalization vs relation-level tokens. A targeted evaluation would strengthen the zero-shot claim.
- Potential popularity/degree bias in entity re-ranking: The first-step re-ranking scores entities by the max relation score over the intersection of predicted top-l relations and each entity’s neighbors. High-degree entities may be favored simply because they have more associated relations, inflating the chance of overlap. A normalization by degree or analysis controlling for degree is missing.
- Incomplete details hinder reproducibility: Several hyperparameters and training details are underspecified (e.g., K, K′, l, α, λ, margin value, dropout/regularization, negative sampling, chain length distribution, tokenization of Freebase predicates). The WebQSP-derived relation detection dataset is “to be released,” and code availability is not stated.
- Constraint detection is simplistic and under-analyzed: The character-overlap matching on subgraph entities is heuristic. While it helps, there is no ablation comparing stronger neural constraint/linking modules, and no error analysis to understand failure modes (e.g., dates, types). The reported large impact suggests this component deserves deeper treatment.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot evaluation: Can you report relation detection performance split by whether the gold relation (or chain) was seen in training? On SQ, how much of the gain comes from word-level relation representation vs residual matching?
- Degree bias: Did you observe a correlation between entity degree and re-ranking success? Have you tried normalizing the max-overlap score by |Re| or using a soft aggregation to mitigate degree effects?
- Chains: What is the maximum chain length considered on WebQSP? How are chains constructed and pruned? Do gains persist for longer chains?
- Hyperparameters and training: Please provide the exact values of K, K′, l, α, λ, margin, optimizer, batch size, dropout/regularization, early stopping, and number of epochs used per dataset. How are relation names tokenized from Freebase predicates (e.g., handling slashes/underscores/camelCase)?
- Runtime/scalability: What are the average numbers of relations scored per question in step 1 and step 2? What are training and inference times, and memory footprints?

Additional comments
- A qualitative error analysis (e.g., when attention helps vs residual, or typical confusions between semantically close relations) would enrich the paper.
- Consider reporting results with character-level inputs for relations/questions as an additional robustness check for OOV tokens.
- Please fix minor notation issues in the ranking loss (missing margin symbol) and ensure consistent citation formatting.
