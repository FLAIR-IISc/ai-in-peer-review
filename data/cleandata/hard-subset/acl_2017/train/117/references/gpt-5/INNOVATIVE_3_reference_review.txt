Summary and contributions
- A hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) for KB relation detection that jointly matches questions to two granularities of relation representation: (i) word-level tokenization of relation names for zero-shot generalization, and (ii) relation-level IDs to capture global signals. Residual connections across BiLSTM layers support “hierarchical matching” of question representations to relation representations.
- A simple, effective two-step use of relation detection in a KBQA pipeline: first to re-rank entity linker outputs by relation evidence, then to score relations given the context with an <e> marker, followed by a lightweight constraint detection on a local subgraph. This yields strong overall QA results without heavy joint inference or complex reranking.
- Empirical validation with thorough ablations on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, showing 2–3% gains in relation detection over strong baselines and corresponding gains on end-to-end KBQA; state-of-the-art on SQ and competitive on WQ.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with a useful modeling twist: The combination of relation word-level matching (for zero-shot) and relation-level IDs (for global semantics), coupled with residual “hierarchical” matching of question encodings, is a clean idea that directly targets the KBQA-specific challenge of a large, partially unseen relation set. The ablations convincingly demonstrate that both granularities and the residual connections matter (e.g., 93.3 vs 91.2/88.8 on SQ; residual improves 80.65→82.53 on WQ).
- Clear, measurable advances on relation detection: On two datasets with different characteristics (single-relation SQ vs multi-relation WQ), HR-BiLSTM improves over strong baselines (AMPCNN, char-3-gram BiCNN, word-level BiLSTM) by 2–3% absolute, with statistical significance. Replacing BiLSTM with CNN hurts especially on WQ, aligning with the hypothesis that chains require longer-range modeling.
- Pipeline leverage of relation detection for entity disambiguation: Using relation scores to re-rank entity linker outputs is effective and practical. The paper shows that removing this step significantly reduces KBQA performance, highlighting a nice, low-cost integration point that many systems overlook. This is particularly valuable given the known ambiguity in entity linking (e.g., “Mike Kelley”).
- Strong ablation and diagnostic coverage: The paper systematically checks the contribution of (i) relation-level vs word-level, (ii) residual vs weighted sum vs attention, (iii) depth (single-layer vs deep), and (iv) encoder family (BiLSTM vs CNN). This strengthens the causal claims behind the improvements.
- End-to-end gains with minimal engineering: Despite avoiding joint training or heavy hand-crafted features, the method achieves SOTA on SQ and competitive results on WQ. The optional constraint detection is lightweight yet provides meaningful gains in the multi-relation setting.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to contemporaneous work: Breaking relation names into word sequences and matching with neural encoders is established (e.g., Yin et al., Yih et al., Golub & He). The main novelty is the specific hierarchical residual matching between question layers and relation granularities. While effective, this feels like an incremental architectural refinement rather than a fundamentally new direction.
- Some methodological details are underspecified or contain inconsistencies: The ranking loss is written as max{0, s(r+;q)+s(r;q)}, which appears incorrect without a negative sign or margin term; the margin is referenced but not shown in the equation. Initialization and sharing between relation word/name encoders, negative sampling strategy, and exact hyperparameters for l, K, K’ in the pipeline are not fully detailed, which can hinder reproducibility and interpretation.
- Generality and scalability questions: The entity re-ranking step requires scoring all relations connected to top-K entities; the computational cost and scalability to larger KBs or higher K are not analyzed. Similarly, the approach relies on relation names being tokenizable into meaningful words—less clear for KBs with opaque relation IDs or multilingual KBs.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and brittle: The character-overlap matching with special rules for dates/types is a pragmatic choice, but feels ad hoc compared to the otherwise neural approach. There is no analysis of precision/recall trade-offs or error cases introduced by constraints.
- Evaluation scope and fairness: End-to-end comparisons rely on different entity linkers across datasets (S-MART vs resources from prior work), and some baselines are re-implemented. While common, it leaves room for confounds. The newly derived WQ relation detection dataset is not fully documented in the main text (selection policies, chain composition, negatives), and “will be released soon” is not a substitute for reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Loss function: Please clarify the exact ranking loss, margin value, and negative sampling/mining strategy used. The current formula appears to omit the margin and sign.
- Residual design: Which residual variant performed best—connecting hidden states vs max-pooled layer? Are the residuals applied only on the question side? Did you try residuals on the relation encoders?
- Computational cost: What are the average numbers for K, K’, and l, and the runtime per question for entity re-ranking and relation detection? How does performance trade off with K and l?
- Robustness to KB naming: How do you tokenize relation names (handling camelCase, snake_case, punctuation)? What happens when relation names are uninformative (IDs) or contain OOV tokens?
- Constraint detection: Can you report precision/recall of constraint attachment, and ablations on threshold θ and date/type handling? Could a learned constraint scorer further help?
- WebQSP relation detection setup: Please provide more details on constructing negatives, chain length distribution, and whether chains are treated compositionally or as concatenated tokens. Will you release this resource and code?

Rating recommendation: Weak accept. The architectural idea—hierarchical residual matching across relation granularities—and its simple integration into a two-step KBQA pipeline deliver consistent, meaningful gains with solid ablations. While the novelty is moderate, the approach is practical, addresses a real KBQA pain point (large, partially unseen relation sets and entity ambiguity), and is likely to influence follow-up work. Addressing the methodological clarifications and releasing resources would further strengthen the paper.
