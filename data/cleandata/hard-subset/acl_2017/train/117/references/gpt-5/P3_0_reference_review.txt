Summary and contributions
This paper tackles relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA), emphasizing challenges specific to KBs: large relation vocabularies, zero-shot relations, and multi-hop (relation-chain) queries. The core technical contribution is a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: (i) relation-level tokens (entire relation names, and relation chains treated as single tokens), and (ii) word-level tokens (relation name words). For questions, two stacked BiLSTMs produce representations at different abstraction levels and residual connections are used to facilitate learning and hierarchical matching. The paper integrates this detector into a simple KBQA pipeline with two-step relation detection: first using relation signals to re-rank entity linker outputs, then detecting relations per top entity with entity mention replacement, plus a lightweight constraint detection module. On SimpleQuestions (single-hop) and WebQSP (multi-hop), the method improves relation detection accuracy and yields strong end-to-end KBQA performance, achieving new SOTA on SQ and competitive results on WQ with a relatively simple pipeline.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A practical hierarchical matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines relation-level and word-level representations with residual connections to improve relation detection under large-vocabulary and zero-shot conditions, with clear ablations.
2) A simple but effective two-step KBQA pipeline where relation detection aids entity disambiguation, demonstrating measurable end-task gains without heavy joint inference or re-ranking.
3) An empirical analysis on both single-hop and multi-hop datasets, including ablations (residual connections, granularity, encoders), showing robustness across settings.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated modeling of relation granularity: The paper convincingly argues that relation names carry complementary global signals to word-level tokens, especially for multi-word paraphrases and skip-grams, while word-level tokens help with zero-shot generalization. The hierarchical matching design directly addresses this, and ablations (w/o rel names vs w/o rel words) validate the complementarity, particularly on SQ where unseen relations are common.

- Solid accuracy gains with careful ablations: On relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves 2–3 points over strong BiLSTM/CNN baselines on both datasets. The residual connections show consistent benefits over naive multi-layer matching and attention baselines, especially on WebQSP (multi-hop), supporting the claim that residual training helps learn complementary abstraction levels rather than simply deeper encoders.

- Simple pipeline with tangible end-to-end impact: The entity re-ranking step that exploits relation signals is both intuitive and impactful. Removing it significantly hurts QA accuracy, indicating that relation-aware re-ranking meaningfully alleviates entity ambiguity. The pipeline remains lightweight (no joint inference or feature-based global re-ranking), yet reaches SOTA/competitive performance, suggesting the proposed relation detector is the main driver.

- Attention to multi-hop: Representing relation chains and encoding them with sequence models is important for WebQSP. The finding that LSTM encoders outperform CNNs here aligns with the need to model longer dependencies, and the method scales to relation chains without bespoke graph modules.

- Reproducibility elements and comparisons: The paper reimplements baselines (BiCNN with char tri-grams, AMPCNN) and reports a variety of ablations (encoder choice, hierarchical combination strategies). Using released resources for SQ and off-the-shelf entity linkers improves comparability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing ideas: While the hierarchical residual matching is effective, the core ingredients are incremental: (i) mixing relation word-level and id-level signals has precedents (e.g., word-level relations in Yin et al., 2016; character-level in Yih et al., 2015; word-level in Liang et al., 2016), and (ii) residual/skip connections for deeper RNNs are established. The novelty resides mainly in the specific combination and application to KB relation detection; a stronger theoretical or architectural justification beyond additive residuals would strengthen the contribution.

- Missing targeted evaluation for zero-shot/generalization: The paper motivates zero-shot relations as a key challenge (e.g., 14% unseen in SQ) but does not report separate performance on seen vs unseen relations, nor analyze which component (word-level vs relation-level) drives zero-shot gains. Such analysis would substantiate the claimed benefits.

- Baseline coverage and fairness: For SQ, comparison to character-level seq2seq relation generation (Golub & He, 2016) is missing; for WQ, comparison to CFO (Dai et al., 2016) or other recent relation detectors is not included. The attention baseline adapts the decomposable attention model, but more targeted cross-attention architectures (e.g., token-level bi-attention between question and relation) might be stronger baselines. The ensemble (“top-3 relation detectors”) boosts results but details on what differs across the three models are sparse.

- Ambiguities in architecture details and loss: The hierarchical residual matching describes two variants (pre-pooling vs post-pooling addition) but does not specify which variant yields reported main numbers. The ranking loss is written without an explicit margin term; it should be clarified (and margin tuned) since otherwise the hinge reduces improperly. The “initialize relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence” as a back-off is interesting but underexplained: how is the state used when the relation-name token is unseen, and how are embeddings for relation-name tokens initialized/updated?

- Efficiency and scalability not discussed: Inference requires scoring all relations connected to entities in the candidate pool (and in step 1, the union over ELK(q)). For KBs like Freebase, degrees can be large. There is no analysis of runtime, candidate pool sizes, or pruning strategies. Similarly, the constraint detection module relies on character-overlap heuristics; its precision/recall and effect on latency are not quantified.

- Reproducibility gaps: The WebQSP relation detection dataset is “to be released.” Until then, the community cannot verify those results. Some key hyperparameters (choice of residual variant, margin, l, K/K’, alpha, lambda, thresholds) are only qualitatively described.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot analysis: Can you report relation detection accuracy separately for relations unseen in training vs seen (on SQ and WQ), and the relative contributions of word-level vs relation-level signals in each subset?

- Residual variant and loss: Which residual variant (per-timestep vs post-pooling) is used for the headline results? What margin value is used in the ranking loss? Please correct the loss formulation in the paper and specify negative sampling strategy and batch construction.

- Baselines: Can you include results against Golub & He (2016) on SQ, and CFO (Dai et al., 2016) or a cross-attention matching baseline on both datasets? For the “top-3 relation detectors,” what differs across models (random seeds, hyperparams, encoders), and how are scores combined?

- Efficiency: What are average numbers of candidate relations in step 1 and step 2, and average inference time per question? How does performance vary with K and K’? Any pruning heuristics beyond degree cutoff?

- Constraint detection: What is its precision/recall on WQSP constraints? How sensitive is end-task accuracy to the character overlap threshold? Could you compare against a simple neural mention linker on the subgraph?

- Reproducibility: When will the WQSP relation detection dataset, code, and trained models be released?
