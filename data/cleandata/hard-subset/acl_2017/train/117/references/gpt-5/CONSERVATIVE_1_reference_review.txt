Summary and claimed contributions
- A hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) for KB relation detection that jointly matches questions to relations at two granularities: relation-name tokens (relation-level) and tokenized relation words (word-level), with residual skip connections across BiLSTM layers to ease training and enable “hierarchical matching.”
- A simple KBQA pipeline that uses the relation detector twice: first to re-rank entity linker candidates via relation evidence, then to score relations given a selected topic entity; plus a heuristic constraint detection module. The system yields improved end-to-end results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP.
- Ablations showing benefits of multi-granularity relation representation and residual connections versus weighted sums or attention.

Strengths
- Empirical gains on both relation detection and end-to-end KBQA. The model improves relation detection accuracy by 2–3% over solid BiLSTM/CNN baselines on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP and translates into consistent end-task gains of similar magnitude. The improvements persist in multiple ablations, suggesting robustness of the core idea.
- Practical insight: using relation signals to re-rank ambiguous entity-linking candidates. The first-step relation detection for entity re-ranking is simple yet effective and tackles a well-known bottleneck in KBQA pipelines.
- Clear problem framing around large relation vocabularies and zero-shot issues. The use of word-level relation tokenization is standard but well-motivated for unseen relations; combining it with relation-name tokens to regain global cues is sensible and practically useful.
- Ablation breadth. The paper contrasts relation-level vs word-level, residual vs weighted-sum vs attention, BiLSTM vs CNN, and single vs deep encoders. Even if not exhaustive, these variants help attribute where gains come from.
- Dataset coverage. Evaluating on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) settings strengthens claims of generality, and the pipeline handles relation chains.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established techniques. The core architecture is a straightforward deep BiLSTM with skip (residual) connections and late fusion of multi-granularity relation encodings. Residual connections for sequence models and multi-view matching are well-known; the contribution feels incremental without new theoretical insights.
- Missing stronger baselines. There is no comparison to transformer-based encoders (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa, pretraining over questions and relation strings), which are now standard for text matching and KBQA submodules. Without these, it is difficult to assess competitiveness; the reported SOTA claims are limited to older baselines.
- Claims about zero-shot generalization are not directly validated. While the motivation is unseen relations, there is no explicit analysis separating seen vs unseen relations on test sets (e.g., per-relation coverage breakdowns on SimpleQuestions), nor a dedicated zero-shot setup. This weakens the central claim.
- Reproducibility and missing details. The WebQSP relation detection dataset is “to be released,” and several crucial implementation choices are under-specified: the exact residual variant used in final results, values for l (number of top relation scores used for re-ranking), α and λ, K and K′, threshold θ for constraints, negative sampling strategy, and seed/variance across runs. Appendices are referenced (A/B/C) but not available; significance testing methodology is not described. These gaps impede replication.
- Heuristic constraint detection and unclear attribution of end-task gains. Constraint detection relies on character-overlap heuristics with a tuned threshold. The paper states it is “crucial,” yet the comparison against stronger constraint models or a fair ablation within competing systems is missing. It is unclear how much of the end-task gain comes from the proposed relation detector versus constraint heuristics and the two-step pipeline design.
- Scalability and efficiency are not discussed. The approach ranks all relations attached to each entity candidate (and, for chains, composes relation sequences), which can be large. There is no runtime, memory, or candidate-pool size analysis, nor pruning strategies beyond K/K′.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot analysis: Please report performance on unseen relations vs seen ones (per Bordes et al. 2015 statistics on SimpleQuestions) and analogous breakdowns for WebQSP. How does HR-BiLSTM compare to word-only and relation-only encodings specifically on unseen relations?
- Baselines: Can you add transformer-based encoders (e.g., BERT-base/large) as question/relation encoders in your framework for both relation detection and end-task, with the same candidate sets? If not feasible, justify and discuss expected effects.
- Residual variant: Which residual connection (hidden-state-level vs pooled-level) is used in the main results? Did you average over multiple random seeds? Please provide mean±std to support the p-values and describe the statistical test.
- Hyperparameters and implementation details: Provide exact values for l, α, λ, K, K′, θ, negative sampling, optimizer, batch size, and early stopping criteria. How large are candidate relation pools per entity on average for WebQSP (including chains)?
- Constraint detection: How sensitive are results to θ and the choice of character-overlap metric? Can you compare to a simple learned string/embedding matcher or type-aware constraints to isolate the contribution of this module?
- Efficiency: Please report training and inference time, and memory usage for SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, including the entity re-ranking pass.
- Resources: Will you release the WebQSP relation detection dataset, code, and exact entity-linking outputs used? Without them, how can others reproduce your numbers?

Overall, this submission presents a well-executed, practically useful increment with solid empirical gains, but lacks stronger baselines, zero-shot validation, and sufficient detail for replication.
