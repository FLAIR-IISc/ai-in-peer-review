Summary
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA. It proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual sequence-matching model that (i) represents relations at two granularities—word-level tokens from relation names and whole relation-name tokens—and (ii) learns multi-level question representations with a 2-layer BiLSTM, combined via residual connections to facilitate complementary matching. The model is used twice in a simple KBQA pipeline: first to re-rank entity candidates by how well their incident relations match the question, then to score relations (or chains) for each shortlisted entity; an optional heuristic constraint detection module refines answers. The approach yields 2–3% gains on relation detection over strong baselines on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, and improves end-to-end KBQA to state-of-the-art on SimpleQuestions and competitive on WebQSP. Ablations support the importance of relation granularity and residual hierarchical matching.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective hierarchical residual matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines relation word-level and relation-name-level representations with multi-layer question encoders, yielding consistent gains in relation detection across single- and multi-relation KBQA settings.

2) A practical two-step use of relation detection to re-rank entities before relation scoring, reducing entity linker ambiguity and materially improving end-to-end KBQA.

3) An empirical study with ablations (removing relation names/words; replacing residual with weighted sum or attention; LSTM vs CNN) demonstrating which parts of the design matter, and application to both SQ and WQ.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated modeling of granularity and zero-shot challenges. The decomposition of relation names into word tokens helps unseen relations; including whole relation-name tokens preserves global cues. The hierarchical question encoder matches these granularity levels. This addresses known failure modes and is corroborated empirically, especially on SimpleQuestions where unseen relations are common (word-only >> relation-only; combining both is best).

- Strong empirical gains with solid ablations. On relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves 2–3% over the best baselines on both datasets; on end-to-end KBQA, gains of 2–3% are reported even with a minimal pipeline (no joint inference or heavy re-rankers). Ablations systematically isolate contributions: both granularities help; residual connections outperform weighted-sum and a decomposable-attention variant; LSTM > CNN for multi-relation chains (WebQSP). Significance tests are reported.

- Practical entity re-ranking via relation evidence. Using relation scores to disambiguate entities is effective and conceptually simple. The ablation shows a significant drop without this step, indicating it is a primary source of end-task improvement. This is a useful engineering insight for KBQA systems where entity linking is a bottleneck.

- Generality across single- and multi-relation QA. The same detection approach works on single triples (SQ) and relation chains (WQ), with evidence that LSTMs better model chain composition. This strengthens the applicability of the method beyond a single benchmark.

- Minimal dependencies and ease of adoption. The approach relies only on surface forms (question text, relation names), without KB embeddings or heavy hand-crafted features, making it easy to integrate into existing pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Limited architectural novelty. Combining word-level and relation-name-level representations and adding residual connections between LSTM layers are incremental adaptations of established ideas. The paper positions residuals as enabling hierarchical matching, but alternatives (e.g., attention over layers) are only superficially explored. The conceptual gap to prior hierarchical/shortcut architectures is modest.

- Missing or underspecified training details hinder reproducibility. The ranking loss is incompletely specified (margin not clearly defined), negative sampling/candidate pools during training are not detailed, and several key hyperparameters (K, K′, ℓ, α, λ, θ) and their selection/sensitivity are only briefly mentioned. The constructed WebQSP relation detection dataset is “to be released,” and code availability is not stated.

- Baseline coverage could be broader. Comparisons omit some relevant relation-detection/KBQA baselines that also target unseen relations or relation-name modeling (e.g., CFO’s relation module; char-level seq2seq relation prediction; approaches with KG embeddings like TransE). This makes it harder to position the gains against alternative design choices (e.g., leveraging KB structural embeddings).

- Zero-shot claim insufficiently quantified. A core motivation is handling unseen relations. While the design and overall results support this, there is no explicit breakdown of accuracy on seen vs. unseen relations/chains, or analysis of how the back-off initialization helps for truly unseen relation-name tokens.

- Constraint detection is heuristic and loosely integrated. The character-overlap matching and special rules (for dates/types) are pragmatic but brittle, and the approach starts from the top-1 core-chain, risking error propagation. While the paper shows gains from this step, attribution between improved relation detection and constraint matching is not fully disentangled.

Questions to Authors
- Seen vs. unseen analysis: Please report relation detection performance broken down by whether the gold relation/chain was seen in training (for both SQ and WQ). How often do whole relation-name tokens appear unseen, and how much does initializing the relation-name BiLSTM from the word-level encoder help in those cases?

- Training specifics: What margin and negative sampling strategy are used in the ranking loss? For each training instance, how many negatives are included and from which pool (all relations of the topic entity, or larger)? For SQ, how are candidate relations constructed during training?

- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: Provide the chosen values for K, K′, ℓ (top relations for re-ranking), α (entity vs. relation weight), λ (final combination), θ (constraint threshold), and a sensitivity analysis showing robustness.

- Computational cost: What are the average candidate set sizes for the entity re-ranking step and the per-entity relation detection (SQ/WQ)? What is the runtime impact of the two-step detection compared to a single-step baseline?

- Residual vs. attention: Which residual variant performed best (hidden-state vs. post-pooling), and by how much? Did you try multi-head or cross-layer attention tuned for short sequences? Any failure cases where attention outperforms residuals?

- Constraint detection details: Please quantify how often constraints are added and their precision/recall. Provide an error breakdown (missed constraints vs. incorrect constraints). How are dates/types handled concretely?

- Resources: Will the WebQSP relation detection dataset and code be released by camera-ready?
