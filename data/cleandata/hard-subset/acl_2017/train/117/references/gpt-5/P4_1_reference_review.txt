Summary and main contributions
The paper targets relation detection within KBQA, where the number of relations is large, relations may be unseen at training, and multi-hop relation chains are needed. It proposes a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: (i) word-level tokens from relation names, to generalize to unseen relations; and (ii) relation-level tokens (whole relation names or relation-chain elements), to capture global cues. The question is encoded by a deep (two-layer) BiLSTM; hierarchical matching is facilitated by residual connections across layers to stabilize training and encourage complementary abstractions. The relation detector is used twice in a simple KBQA pipeline: first to rerank entity-linking candidates using relation scores (to reduce ambiguity), then to score relations (or chains) for the selected topic entity; optional heuristic constraint detection handles additional entities/dates.

Contributions as assessed:
1) A hierarchical residual matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines word-level and relation-level relation representations and residual connections across question encoder layers, yielding consistent gains on relation detection (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP).
2) A simple, two-step KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection to rerank entities and then select relations, achieving strong end-to-end results with minimal system complexity; comprehensive ablations attribute gains to both hierarchical matching and entity reranking.
3) A constructed relation-detection benchmark for WebQSP (core-chain classification given a topic entity), plus ablations comparing residual vs weighted-sum/attention and LSTM vs CNN encoders.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements with thorough ablations: On relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves over strong baselines by 2–3% absolute (e.g., 93.3% vs ~91% on SimpleQuestions and 82.53% vs ~79–81% on WebQSP). Ablations isolate the contribution of hierarchical matching (using both word- and relation-level inputs) and residual connections (notably beneficial on WebQSP), and show LSTMs outperform CNNs for multi-relation chains. The comparisons include attention-based alternatives and single-layer controls.
- Sound handling of zero-shot and large relation spaces: Factorizing relations into words (with shared encoders and initialization of relation-level encoders from word-level encodings) is a principled way to cope with unseen relations and vocabulary size, and the results on SimpleQuestions (where 14% of test relations are unseen) support the approach. The motivation and design choices are well reasoned and connected to the task’s constraints.
- Simple yet effective KBQA pipeline: The two-step use of the same relation detector (for entity reranking and relation selection) provides a clean, low-engineering system. The entity reranking step demonstrably improves end-to-end accuracy; the paper quantifies the impact of removing it, indicating the relation detector’s utility beyond relation scoring.
- Clarity of core model description and evaluation framing: The hierarchy of representations, residual variants (position-wise vs pooled), and training objective are described with sufficient detail to understand the mechanism. The creation of a WebQSP relation detection task (core-chain discrimination) is explained, aligning relation-detection evaluation with the end task.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: Using both word-level and relation-level representations for relations has been explored (e.g., Yin et al., 2016; Yih et al., 2015; Liang et al., 2016), and residual connections are adapted from ResNet. The main novelty lies in combining these with hierarchical residual matching across BiLSTM layers for relation detection and integrating the detector twice in a pipeline. While effective, the conceptual advance is incremental.
- Missing or ambiguous training and system details hinder reproducibility: Several key hyperparameters are unspecified or only named (e.g., K, K0, l, α, λ, θ), and the ranking loss is ambiguously written (typical margin form is max(0, γ − s+ + s−), but the text shows max{0, s+(r+) + s(r−)} with an undefined constant). Details of negative sampling, candidate pool sizes, optimization schedule, and statistical testing (test type for p-values) are not provided. Some steps refer to appendices for crucial procedures (e.g., constraint detection scoring, date handling), which limits self-contained clarity.
- Zero-shot claims are not directly evaluated: Although the approach is motivated by unseen relations, there is no breakdown of performance on seen vs unseen relations or analysis by relation frequency. This weakens the empirical support for the zero-shot generalization argument.
- Comparability and component confounds in end-to-end results: The WebQSP system uses full Freebase and S-MART linking; SimpleQuestions uses FB2M and another linker. It is unclear whether all baselines are run under identical KB and entity-linking settings. The constraint detection module uses heuristic string overlap matching; while effective, it may introduce dataset-specific gains and complicates attribution of improvements solely to relation detection.
- Scope of baselines: Although the paper compares to strong CNN/LSTM/attention baselines and STAGG, it does not report comparisons to contemporaneous character-level/generative relation prediction systems in end-to-end KBQA on the same settings (e.g., Golub & He, 2016), nor to TransE-initialized relation models (Dai et al., 2016) in the relation detection setting. This limits conclusions about general superiority.

Questions to authors
- Loss and training: What is the exact ranking loss (margin γ, sign), number of negatives per positive, and negative sampling strategy? Are embeddings and encoders fully fine-tuned? What optimizer, learning-rate schedule, batch size, and early stopping criteria were used?
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: What are the exact values of K, K0, l (top-l relations), α (entity reranking mixture), λ (combination in query scoring), θ (constraint threshold)? Please include a sensitivity analysis.
- Residual variants: Which residual connection (position-wise vs pooled) yields the reported best numbers on each dataset? Did you try residuals on the relation encoders?
- Unseen relations analysis: What is the performance on test relations unseen during training on SimpleQuestions and on WebQSP? How does accuracy vary with relation frequency and chain length?
- Relation chain representation: For multi-hop chains, do you treat each hop as a separate relation-level token and also include all words from all hop names in the word-level sequence? Any ordering or separator handling?
- Initialization of relation-level encoders: Initializing the relation-name BiLSTM with the final state of the word-level sequence is unusual. Please clarify precisely how this is implemented for bidirectionality and whether it materially affects performance (ablation without this back-off).
- Runtime and scalability: What are the average numbers of entity candidates and relation candidates per question in the reranking step, and the total inference time per question? How does the method scale with KB size?

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated improvement to relation detection for KBQA, with clear gains and careful ablations. Its novelty is moderate, and some experimental details and zero-shot analyses are missing, but the work is likely to be useful to the community.
