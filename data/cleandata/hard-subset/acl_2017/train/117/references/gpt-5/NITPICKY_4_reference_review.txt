Summary
The paper targets relation detection in knowledge base question answering (KBQA). It proposes a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: (i) word-level (tokenized relation names) to improve zero-shot generalization and (ii) relation-level (relation IDs) to capture global semantics. Residual connections across BiLSTM layers are used to ease training and encourage complementary abstraction. The model is further embedded into a simple KBQA pipeline with a novel entity re-ranking step driven by relation scores. Experiments on SimpleQuestions (SQ) and WebQSP (WQ) report gains in relation detection and end-to-end KBQA.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines word- and relation-level representations with residual connections, yielding consistent improvements for relation detection on SQ and WQ.
2) A practical KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection for entity re-ranking and downstream relation selection, achieving strong results with minimal extra components.
3) An ablation study suggesting residual connections help deep sequence matching and that both granularity levels are complementary.

Strengths
- Solid empirical gains on two benchmarks: The HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection over competitive BiLSTM/CNN baselines (2–3% absolute) and translates into 2–3% end-to-end KBQA gains. The ablations on residual connections, granularity removal, and encoder variants are informative.
- Targeted design for KB-specific challenges: Using tokenized relation names explicitly addresses zero-shot relations; handling relation chains on WQ is practically important. The architecture choice (BiLSTM over CNN) is substantiated by WQ results.
- Simple yet effective pipeline: The entity re-ranking step guided by relation scores is a well-motivated way to mitigate entity-linking ambiguity, a known bottleneck, and the paper quantifies its impact.
- Clear link between modeling choices and the task: The hierarchical matching rationale (word-level ↔ local phrases; relation-level ↔ longer phrases/skip-grams) is compelling and supported by examples and ablations.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and technical precision issues: Several equations and definitions are underspecified or contain apparent typos:
  • Ranking loss defined as lrel = max{0, srel(r+;q) + srel(r−;q)} is likely incorrect (missing margin and sign; standard is max(0, γ − s+ + s−)). Please clarify margin γ and exact form used.
  • In Section 3.2, r is written with an unusual “[” union; definition of how relation chains are represented (concatenated tokens? pooled?) is not fully clear. How are embeddings initialized for relation “name tokens” (IDs) that are unseen? If they are random, how is the claimed back-off realized?
  • The “initialize relation sequence LSTMs with the final state of the word sequence” needs precise description (which sequence initializes which; for unseen relations how does this help if the token embedding is random?).
- Missing/underspecified hyperparameters and calibration: Key quantities for reproducibility and fairness are not given or not justified: K, K′, l (top-l relations), α (entity re-ranking weight), λ/β (combination weights in Eq. for s(ê, r̂; q)), batch size, optimizer, regularization (dropout, weight decay), margin, negative sampling protocol. The paper sums s_linker and s_rel but does not discuss score calibration; how are ranges normalized to avoid one term dominating?
- Comparability and fairness of baselines: End-to-end results depend on external entity linkers (different sources for SQ and WQ). It is unclear whether baselines were re-run with identical entity-linking to ensure fair comparison. Some strong contemporary systems (e.g., CFO for SQ; neural programmer/NSM variants for WQ) are not compared or only cited; relation detection comparisons omit character-level seq2seq (Golub & He) in a comparable setting. The WQ relation detection dataset is “to be released,” which hinders verification.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and under-specified: The character-overlap matching (Appendix A/B) and thresholds are not described in sufficient detail in the main text. It is unclear how sensitive end-to-end gains are to this module. No comparison to alternative constraint handling (e.g., type-aware pruning, textual evidence) is provided, yet the module materially affects WQ results.
- Writing/formatting quality: Numerous formatting inconsistencies and bibliographic errors (missing years, “?” placeholders, repeated author names, backslashes, misnumbered footnotes, broken figure/table references, inconsistent math notation). This detracts from clarity and raises concerns about carefulness. Examples: missing years in several references; “We will make the datasets publicly available soon”; “BiLSTM w/ relation names rel names”; brackets/typos in equations; inconsistent use of relation tokens (“episodes written” vs “episode written”).

Questions to Authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
1) Please provide the exact ranking loss with margin γ and the negative sampling/mining strategy. How many negatives per positive per batch?
2) How are embeddings for relation ID tokens initialized and updated? What happens for unseen relation IDs at test time?
3) What optimizer, batch size, dropout/regularization, and early stopping criteria were used? Please report training time and inference latency, and the average number of candidate relations per entity/entity set in steps 1–2.
4) How were K, K′, l, α, and the combination weight in s(ê, r̂; q) chosen? Are s_linker and s_rel calibrated/normalized?
5) For WQ relation detection, please release the constructed dataset and detail candidate pool construction (chain length limits, pruning). What is the distribution of chain lengths and pool sizes?
6) Can you report zero-shot breakdown on SQ (seen vs unseen relations at training) to substantiate the “unseen relations” claim?
7) What statistical test was used for p-values in Table 2? Over how many runs? Report mean±std over seeds.
8) How sensitive are end-to-end results to the constraint detection threshold and rules? Provide an ablation varying θ and removing special date/type rules.

Overall recommendation
The idea—hierarchical matching across relation granularities with residual connections—is reasonable and empirically useful. However, the paper currently suffers from significant clarity, reproducibility, and formatting issues, and several methodological details are under-specified, making it hard to fully assess novelty and fairness. I lean toward rejection in the current form. With a thorough revision addressing the technical specification gaps, fairer and broader comparisons, and improved presentation, this work could be a solid contribution.
