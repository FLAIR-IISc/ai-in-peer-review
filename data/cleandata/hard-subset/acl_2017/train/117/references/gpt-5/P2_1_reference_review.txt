Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles relation detection for KBQA, a setting with thousands of relations, frequent unseen relations, and relation chains. It proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that matches questions to relations at two granularities—relation-level tokens (full relation names/IDs) and word-level tokens (tokenized relation names). Residual connections enable effective training of multi-layer question encoders and hierarchical matching. The model is used in a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection: (i) relation-driven entity reranking, then (ii) relation (or relation-chain) selection given a topic entity, with an optional constraint detection stage.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, effective relation detection architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that combines relation-level and word-level representations with residual connections for hierarchical question-relation matching, yielding consistent 2–3% absolute gains over strong baselines on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP relation detection.
2) A KBQA pipeline demonstrating that stronger relation detection materially improves the end task via relation-driven entity reranking and relation-chain selection, achieving state-of-the-art or comparable results.
3) Empirical analysis/ablations showing the value of (a) using both relation- and word-level representations, and (b) residual connections vs. naive multi-layer or attention-based alternatives.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains on two benchmarks. On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP relation detection, HR-BiLSTM improves over strong baselines (BiLSTM, AMPCNN, BiCNN) by 2–3% absolute (Table 2), with significance tests. In end-to-end KBQA, replacing the relation detector with HR-BiLSTM yields 2–3% gains and overall SOTA/comparable results (Table 3), despite a relatively simple pipeline.
- Addresses key KBQA challenges (scalability and zero-shot generalization). Using both relation-level and word-level representations allows better generalization to unseen relations (especially helpful on SimpleQuestions) while preserving global cues from relation IDs to disambiguate lexical matches (e.g., “play” vs. “starring roles”). The method directly targets issues that standard RE settings often ignore.
- Sound architectural choices and informative ablations. The ablations isolate the sources of improvement: removing relation names or words hurts; replacing residual with weighted-sum or decomposable attention degrades performance; CNN underperforms LSTM for relation chains on WebQSP. The comparison against a single-layer encoder additionally supports the claim that residual connections ease deeper training and foster complementary abstractions.
- Practical pipeline improvement via entity reranking. The first-step relation detection to rerank entity linker outputs is simple yet effective, addressing the known bottleneck of entity disambiguation in KBQA. The ablations (Table 3) show the reranking step substantially boosts end-to-end performance, strengthening the case for the proposed relation detector’s utility beyond a standalone metric.
- Fair and relevant baselines/settings. Experiments reuse commonly used entity linkers (S-MART for WebQSP; released linker for SQ), and compare with prior SOTA systems (STAGG, AMPCNN, BiCNN), making improvements credible.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in core ideas. Combining tokenized relation names with relation IDs and using residual connections over stacked BiLSTMs are individually well-known strategies. The main novelty lies in how these are composed for hierarchical matching in KBQA. While the empirical results are solid, the conceptual contribution is incremental.
- Insufficient analysis of zero-shot and chain-specific performance. The paper motivates the setting by emphasizing unseen relations and relation chains, yet provides no breakdown by seen vs. unseen relations, relation length, or candidate pool size. Such analyses would better support the claims about generalization and the particular strengths on WebQSP.
- Loss function and training details are under-specified. The ranking loss is written as max{0, s(r+;q) + s(r;q)} with a constant mentioned but not clearly included as a margin, and no minus sign; negative sampling strategy, number of negatives, batch composition, regularization (dropout, L2), and early stopping criteria are not fully described. This hampers reproducibility.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and underspecified. The character-overlap matching (plus special rules for dates/answer types) is ad hoc. While the ablation suggests it helps, there is limited detail (Appendix referenced) and little discussion on robustness, error modes, or how it interacts with relation-chain errors. More principled approaches or stronger justification would strengthen this component.
- Reproducibility/datasets. The WebQSP-derived relation detection dataset is said to be released “soon,” and there is no code release. Given the pipeline’s sensitivity to entity linker outputs and candidate generation, releasing code and data is important for verification.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the ranking loss: should it be max{0, margin + s(r−;q) − s(r+;q)}? How are negatives sampled, and how many per positive?
- How many relations per candidate entity are scored in practice on WebQSP? What are the average candidate pool sizes before/after reranking?
- Can you report breakdowns for (a) seen vs. unseen relations on SimpleQuestions; (b) single-step vs. multi-step relation chains on WebQSP; and (c) effect sizes vs. candidate pool size?
- How sensitive is performance to the residual placement (hidden-state vs. pooled vectors) and to the margin/alpha/beta hyperparameters in the pipeline scoring equations?
- Could you release the WebQSP relation detection dataset and code to ensure reproducibility?

Overall assessment
This is a solid engineering contribution with clear empirical gains on important KBQA benchmarks. While methodological novelty is moderate, the work is well-motivated, addresses practical bottlenecks, and includes meaningful ablations. With clarifications on training details, analyses that substantiate claims about zero-shot and chain handling, and released resources, I would lean toward acceptance (weak accept).
