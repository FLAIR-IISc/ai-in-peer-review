Summary and contributions
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA and proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: word-level (tokenized relation names) for generalization to unseen relations and relation-level (whole relation names) for capturing global cues. On the question side, deep BiLSTMs with residual connections enable complementary abstractions to be pooled and compared to relation representations. The model is further embedded in a simple yet effective KBQA pipeline that uses relation detection twice: first to re-rank entity linker outputs via relation cues, and then to detect the core relation/chain, followed by optional constraint matching.

As I see it, the main contributions (in order of perceived strength) are:
1) A hierarchical residual matching architecture that jointly exploits word-level and relation-level representations and residualized multi-layer question encoders for robust relation detection, especially under unseen relations and relation chains.
2) A relation-aware entity re-ranking step that links relation detection with entity disambiguation, yielding a compact, effective KBQA pipeline without heavy joint inference.
3) Empirical results with solid ablations showing 2–3% gains on relation detection (SimpleQuestions, WebQSP) and competitive-to-SOTA end-to-end KBQA, demonstrating the practical value of the approach.

Strengths
- Conceptual integration of granularity and hierarchy: Explicitly combining word-level and relation-level representations with residualized multi-layer question encodings is a clean idea tailored to KBQA’s zero-shot and compositionality challenges. The residual design addresses known training issues in deep sequence encoders and empirically outperforms naive weighted sums and attention baselines.
- Clear, measurable gains with ablations: On both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, HR-BiLSTM improves over strong baselines (BiLSTM w/ words, char-3-gram BiCNN, AMPCNN) by 2–3% for relation detection. Ablations (removing relation names/words, removing residuals, using CNNs, single-layer encoders) support the modeling choices and show where the gains come from (e.g., residuals matter more for WebQSP).
- Practical KBQA pipeline impact via relation-aware entity re-ranking: Using relation scores to re-rank entity linker candidates directly addresses a common bottleneck (name ambiguity). The end-task improvements and the analysis that SimpleQuestions benefits more from unseen relation handling, while WebQSP benefits more from deeper context, are consistent and convincing.
- Handles relation chains: Encoding relation chains and showing LSTMs outperform CNNs in this setting is practically important for multi-hop KBQA; the model appears to capture longer dependencies better than convolutional alternatives.
- Simplicity and portability: The pipeline avoids heavy joint inference and complex re-rankers yet achieves competitive or SOTA results, suggesting the approach can be adopted easily and benefit many KBQA stacks.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond thoughtful combination: While well-motivated and effective, the architectural ideas (mixing token granularities, residual connections, deep BiLSTMs, max-pooling and cosine scoring) are evolutions of existing components rather than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm. The “hierarchical” aspect lives mainly in residually combining two BiLSTM layers and mixing relation token granularities into a single pooled relation vector, rather than explicit multi-level alignment.
- Insufficient zero-shot evaluation and analysis: The paper motivates zero-shot settings (e.g., 14% unseen relations in SimpleQuestions), but does not provide a targeted evaluation isolating unseen relations vs. seen relations. A split-level breakdown, or explicit zero-shot benchmarks, would strengthen claims that word-level relation representation drives generalization.
- Loss formulation and training clarity: The ranking loss is written as l = max{0, s(r+;q)+s(r−;q)} without a margin or the conventional subtraction; this looks like a typo or an unconventional choice that needs clarification. Details on negative sampling strategy, candidate pool sizes, and the exact margin or scaling would help reproducibility and interpretation.
- Incomplete comparisons on end-to-end QA: While the pipeline is intentionally simple, comparisons against recent joint or beam-based systems (and/or adding a lightweight final answer re-ranker) would clarify how much headroom remains. The constraint detection is character-overlap based; a comparison to stronger constraint models would contextualize its contribution.
- Some implementation details are underspecified: How relation names are segmented and normalized, how relation chains are tokenized (e.g., delimiter choices), whether the two relation token types are concatenated or processed in parallel before pooling, and which residual variant (pre- vs post-pooling) was best, are not fully specified in the main text. The effect of hyperparameters (α, λ, l, K, K′) is not reported.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot analysis: Can you report performance separately on test questions whose gold relations (or chains) are unseen during training vs. seen, for both datasets? How do the ablations (w/o relation words vs. w/o relation names) behave under that split?
- Loss function: Is the ranking loss missing a margin and sign (e.g., γ − s(r+;q) + s(r−;q)) in the text, or did you actually optimize the stated form? Please clarify the exact loss, margin value, and negative sampling.
- Residual variant: Which residual variant (shortcut between hidden states vs. between pooled vectors) performed best, and by how much? Did you try residuals on the relation encoder as well?
- Entity re-ranking sensitivity: How sensitive are end-to-end gains to α, l, K, and K′? Can you provide precision/recall for topic entity identification before/after re-ranking, and error types corrected?
- Constraint detection: What fraction of WebQSP gains come from constraint detection? Have you tried a learned constraint/scorer or attention-based matching and how does it compare?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, the WebQSP relation detection split, and all hyperparameters? Are there any dependencies on proprietary resources?
