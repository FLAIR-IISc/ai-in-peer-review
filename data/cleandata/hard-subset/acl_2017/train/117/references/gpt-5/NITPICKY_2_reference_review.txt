Summary
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA and proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that matches questions against two granularities of relation representations: (i) word-level tokens from relation names and (ii) relation-level tokens (entire relation names, including multi-hop “relation chains”). Residual/shortcut connections across BiLSTM layers are used to make different abstraction levels of question representations comparable and easier to optimize. The paper also introduces a simple KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection both to re-rank entity linker outputs and to pick relations, with an optional constraint detection step based on string overlap in a small subgraph. Experiments on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP show improvements in relation detection accuracy and competitive or SOTA end-to-end QA accuracy, supported by ablations.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical KBQA pipeline that uses relation detection to re-rank entity candidates and select relations/core-chains, leading to strong end-task performance with relatively simple components.
2) A hierarchical relation detection model combining relation-level and word-level representations with residual connections, yielding consistent improvements and reasonable ablation evidence.
3) A constructed WebQSP relation detection benchmark (derived from labeled parses), enabling direct evaluation of relation detection on multi-hop settings.

Strengths
- Solid empirical gains on two benchmarks. The model improves relation detection accuracy by ~2–3% over strong baselines on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, and the KBQA pipeline reaches SOTA or near-SOTA end-to-end accuracy despite its simplicity. Ablations (no rel-name vs no rel-words; residual vs weighted-sum vs attention; CNN vs BiLSTM) are informative and support the design claims.
- Well-motivated handling of zero-shot relations and multi-hop chains. Splitting relation names into words increases coverage and zero-shot capacity, while retaining relation-level tokens captures global co-occurrence signals. This addresses genuine KBQA pain points (large relation sets, unseen relations, chain composition).
- Relation-aware entity re-ranking is effective and simple. Using the top relation scores from the raw question to re-rank EL candidates directly addresses entity ambiguity and provides a clear win in the pipeline, as corroborated in the ablations.
- Architecture decisions are validated. The residual trick across BiLSTM layers appears to stabilize training and improve WebQSP more than SimpleQuestions, which aligns with the intuition that longer contexts and chains benefit from deeper abstractions; the comparison against attention and single-layer encoders is useful.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. Combining word-level and relation-level tokens and using residual/shortcut connections in deep RNN encoders are well-established ideas. Prior KBQA work has leveraged subword/word-level representations for relations (e.g., char n-grams, word sequences), and residual/highway connections for sequence modeling are known. The contribution feels largely incremental engineering rather than a substantial conceptual advance.
- Methodological ambiguities and a likely error in the loss. The ranking loss is written as l = max(0, s(r+;q) + s(r−;q)) “where is a constant,” which appears incorrect; standard pairwise ranking uses max(0, γ − s(r+;q) + s(r−;q)). The missing margin and sign strongly suggest a typo or implementation mismatch. Additionally, details on relation encoding are unclear: how exactly are word-level and relation-level sequences ordered/concatenated for the BiLSTM; what does “initialize the relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence” mean operationally; why are the two relation encoders “shared parameters”; and are order and directionality preserved for relation chains?
- Reproducibility gaps. Many critical hyperparameters are unspecified (K, K′, l, α for entity re-ranking, the margin γ, optimizer choice, batch size, negative sampling strategy, dropout/regularization). Constraint detection depends on Appendices A/B (not provided) and ad hoc thresholds. For WebQSP relation detection, the candidate pool construction (max chain length, CVT handling, pruning) is under-specified; the dataset is “to be released,” which prevents verification.
- Fairness and statistical testing. Several baselines are re-implemented (e.g., BiCNN with char 3-grams), which may be suboptimal; it is unclear that training setups, candidate pools, and tokenization are strictly aligned. Reported p-values lack a description of the test (e.g., paired bootstrap, McNemar), and no confidence intervals are given. The multiple-detector ensemble for final KBQA further blurs attribution of gains.
- Heavy reliance on heuristic constraint detection. The subgraph string-overlap matcher is simplistic, dataset-specific, and depends on several rules relegated to appendices. Given that the claim of strong end-to-end results partially hinges on this component (noted as “crucial”), more principled modeling or clearer analysis is needed.

Questions to Authors
- Ranking loss: Please clarify the exact loss used (margin γ, correct sign, negatives per positive, sampling strategy). Is the equation a typo? What optimizer, batch size, and regularization (dropout, L2) were used?
- Relation representation: How are Freebase relations converted into word sequences (tokenization, casing, separators, mapping from IDs)? For chains, what is the maximum length considered and how is order encoded? Do you traverse CVT nodes explicitly?
- Relation encoder: Do the BiLSTMs over word-level and relation-level tokens share parameters? In what sequence are they applied, and what does initializing the “relation sequence LSTMs with the final state of the word sequence” concretely mean? Did you try separate parameters?
- Entity re-ranking: What are the exact values of K, K′, l, α, and how sensitive are results to these choices? How many relations are typically in Rl_q?
- Evaluation: What statistical test was used for significance? For WebQSP relation detection, please report candidate set sizes and distribution (relations and chains per question), and release the dataset to verify claims.
- Constraint detection: Please provide details of the overlap scoring and date/type rules, and quantify its isolated impact (with/without) on WebQSP with error analysis.

Additional comments (minor but numerous)
- The paper has many typos/grammar issues (e.g., “golded test relations,” “opendomain,” “question texts,” repeated “the” in Section 4.2) and inconsistent notation. Figures/tables have formatting problems; Table 1 and Figure 2 captions are hard to parse; some symbols are not defined.
- Several references are incomplete or missing years (e.g., Bordes et al., ., Golub and He ., multiple entries with “?”), and citation formatting is inconsistent.
- Specify whether residual connections are at hidden-state level or pooled vector level in the final model used for results, and report the other variant’s performance.
- Please detail tokenization, casing, and OOV handling; whether word embeddings are fine-tuned; and the handling of unseen relation tokens during training/inference.

Overall, while the empirical results are promising and the pipeline is practical, the paper’s novelty is modest, and clarity/reproducibility issues (including a likely loss function error) and numerous presentation problems are substantial. I lean toward rejection unless the authors can correct the methodological ambiguities, fix the loss, release resources, and significantly improve clarity.
