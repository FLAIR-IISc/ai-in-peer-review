Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA with large relation vocabularies and occasional zero-shot relations. It proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual sequence matching model that compares questions to relations at two granularities: (i) relation-level tokens (whole relation names) to capture global cues, and (ii) word-level tokens (tokenized relation names) to handle unseen/rare relations. Deep BiLSTMs encode questions at two abstraction levels; residual connections are used to stabilize training and make the layers complementary. The model is embedded in a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection: relation-driven entity re-ranking followed by per-entity relation detection and lightweight constraint detection. The approach yields SOTA or competitive results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, with ablations suggesting each component contributes.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A practical, multi-granularity relation matching formulation with residual hierarchical question encoding that consistently improves relation detection and KBQA on two benchmarks, including relation chains on WebQSP.
2) A simple yet effective relation-driven entity re-ranking step that measurably mitigates entity ambiguity and boosts end-task accuracy.
3) A thorough empirical study with ablations (removing residuals, removing one granularity, replacing LSTMs with CNNs, attention alternative), demonstrating where the gains come from.

Strengths
- Clear, pragmatic formulation for large/open relation sets. Combining relation-level and word-level representations is well motivated for zero-shot generalization and long-phrase matching; the residual hierarchical question encoder is a simple mechanism that avoids training instabilities seen with naive multi-layer scoring. The method yields 2–3% absolute gains over strong baselines in relation detection on both datasets.
- Effective use of relation detection to improve entity linking. The two-step design (relation-driven entity re-ranking then per-entity relation detection) is a clever, low-overhead way to inject relation evidence into entity disambiguation, addressing a known bottleneck. The ablation shows a significant drop when this step is removed, supporting its importance.
- Handles relation chains. Encoding relation chains as sequences and showing LSTMs outperform CNNs on WebQSP strengthens the case that the proposed architecture captures longer dependencies relevant to multi-hop questions.
- Solid ablations and statistical tests. The paper compares against AMPCNN, BiCNN with char 3-grams, attention-based decomposable matching, and tests removing residual connections and granularities. Reported p-values and controlled comparisons increase confidence in the findings.
- Simplicity and applicability. The pipeline relies on off-the-shelf entity linking and light scoring rather than heavy joint inference, yet achieves SOTA or near-SOTA, making it attractive for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited conceptual novelty. Splitting relation names into words and contrasting relation-level vs. word-level matching are established ideas; residual connections for deep sequence models are also standard. The hierarchical residual matching here reads as a well-engineered combination rather than a fundamentally new concept. Given the paper’s positioning, the novelty feels incremental.
- No explicit zero-shot analysis. Although the motivation is unseen relations, there is no breakdown of performance on seen vs. unseen relations or a dedicated zero-shot setting. It’s thus unclear how much the multi-granularity design concretely helps when test relations are absent from training.
- Dependence on meaningful relation surface forms. The approach presumes relation labels decompose into semantically meaningful tokens; it’s unclear how well it would transfer to KBs with opaque IDs, non-English labels, or sparse/alias-poor schemas. There is no experiment probing robustness when relation names are noisy or masked.
- Comparisons and scope. While CFO/TransE-style pretraining is cited, there is no direct comparison or hybrid with KB-embedding-based relation representations. Likewise, the end-to-end baseline set is limited; more recent joint entity–relation or semantic parsing models could contextualize the contribution better.
- Reproducibility/experimental detail gaps. Several training specifics are under-specified (e.g., margin value, negative sampling strategy/size, choice/sensitivity of α, λ, l in re-ranking, K/K′, learning rate schedules). The constraint detection heuristic is only briefly described (appendix), and resources are promised “soon.” Reference formatting is also inconsistent.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot behavior: What is the accuracy on test instances whose gold relations (or chains) never appear in training? Please report seen vs. unseen splits for both datasets and quantify the contribution of relation-level vs. word-level in that regime.
- Tokenization of relation names: How exactly are Freebase properties normalized into “words” and “relation names” (e.g., splitting slashes/underscores, camel case)? How would the method handle opaque or multilingual relation labels?
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: What values did you use for the ranking loss margin, number of negatives per query, top-l relations in entity re-ranking, α (entity vs. relation score), λ (step-1 vs. step-2 combination), K/K′, and threshold θ in constraint detection? Any sensitivity curves?
- Efficiency: What are training/inference runtimes and memory footprints for WebQSP with full Freebase? How many relation candidates per entity/chain are processed on average?
- Complementarity with KB embeddings: Did you try initializing/augmenting relation representations with KB embeddings (e.g., TransE) and combining with your multi-granularity matching? If so, what was the effect?
- Robustness to beam size: You use top-1 for subsequent steps. How does performance change with a small beam (e.g., 5) and a lightweight final re-ranker?
