Summary and main contributions
This paper targets relation detection in KBQA and proposes a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that jointly matches questions against two granularities of relation representations: (i) relation-level tokens (whole relation names or relation chains) and (ii) word-level tokens (tokenized relation names). The model learns multi-level question representations via stacked BiLSTMs and introduces residual connections to stabilize hierarchical matching. The paper further integrates relation detection into a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation use: first, re-ranking entity candidates by relation signals from the raw question; second, detecting relations given a chosen topic entity (with entity mention replaced by <e>). An optional heuristic constraint detection prunes candidates for multi-constraint questions.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A hierarchical matching architecture for relation detection that combines relation-level and word-level representations using residual connections, yielding consistent gains over strong baselines on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP.
2) A simple, effective KBQA pipeline where relation detection is used for entity re-ranking and relation selection, producing state-of-the-art (or competitive) end-to-end results with minimal engineering.
3) Empirical analysis and ablations demonstrating the benefit of multi-granularity relation representation and residual matching vs. weighted-sum and attention-based alternatives.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements on two standard benchmarks: The proposed HR-BiLSTM achieves 93.3% on SimpleQuestions and 82.53% on WebQSP relation detection, improving over competitive baselines (e.g., BiLSTM w/ words, AMPCNN) by 2–3% with significance, and yields 2–3% gains in end-to-end KBQA within the same pipeline. An ensemble of the top-3 detectors sets a new SOTA on SimpleQuestions and remains competitive on WebQSP.
- Clear benefit of multi-granularity relation representations: Treating relations as both whole tokens and tokenized words addresses the zero-shot/large-vocabulary challenge while preserving global relation cues. Ablations show both levels contribute; using only relation names or only words degrades performance (notably on SQ).
- Residual hierarchical matching improves deep sequence modeling: Residual connections across question encoder layers outperform naive weighted-sum and attention-based hierarchical matching, especially on WebQSP (+1.9–2.0 points over weighted-sum/attention), indicating better training dynamics and complementary abstraction levels.
- Practical, simple KBQA pipeline with meaningful interaction between components: Using relation scores to re-rank entities addresses a common bottleneck (entity ambiguity) without complex joint inference. Ablations confirm the reranking step materially boosts final QA performance.
- Thorough ablations and baselines: The paper compares to multiple model classes (BiCNN char n-grams, CNN, BiLSTM, attention), dissects residual variants, and reports significance testing, which helps attribute gains to specific design choices.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: Decomposing relation names to words is well-established in KBQA; treating entire relation ids as tokens is also common; residual connections are standard. The main novelty is their specific combination and residual formulation for hierarchical matching, which, while effective, is incremental rather than conceptually new.
- No explicit zero-shot analysis: A key motivation is handling unseen relations, yet there is no breakdown of performance on seen vs. unseen relations (or on unseen chains). SimpleQuestions is known to have unseen relations; quantifying gains specifically on those cases would substantiate the claim.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and ad hoc: The optional constraint module relies on character overlap and special rules (e.g., for dates), which may not generalize to broader constraint types or other KBs. End-to-end gains partially depend on this component, but it is not learned or integrated into the neural model.
- Reproducibility and missing details: Important hyperparameters (e.g., margin in the ranking loss, alpha for reranking, lambda for score fusion, l for top relations used, K and K0 sizes, threshold for constraints) are not specified; candidate core-chain generation (max length, branching) and computational costs are not detailed. The WebQSP relation-detection dataset is said to be released “soon.” These omissions hinder replication.
- Fairness and scope of comparisons: The paper compares to strong contemporaneous baselines, but does not report robustness to different entity linkers or provide runtime/efficiency analysis. Also, while the ensemble result is strong, it is unclear how it compares in complexity to prior multi-model systems (e.g., STAGG’s multiple detectors), and whether a single-model setting would remain SOTA.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot/generalization: Can you report relation detection accuracy broken down by seen vs. unseen relations (and by chain length) on both datasets? How much does the word-level representation help on truly unseen relations?
- Architectural choices: Which residual variant (hidden-state shortcuts vs. post-pooling) performs best, and by how much? Did you explore deeper than 2-layer BiLSTMs, and how did training behave?
- Candidate generation and efficiency: How are candidate core-chains generated (max length, branching factor, pruning)? What is the average number of candidate relations/chains per topic entity on WebQSP, and the runtime impact of the entity re-ranking step?
- Hyperparameters and training: Please specify the margin used in the ranking loss, α for entity reranking, λ for query score fusion, l, K, and K0, and the constraint threshold. How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Constraint detection: How often does the constraint module change the top answer, and what types of constraints does it capture vs. miss? Could a learned constraint module improve further?
