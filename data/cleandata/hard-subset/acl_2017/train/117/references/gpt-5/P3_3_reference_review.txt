Summary and contributions
- A hierarchical relation detection model (HR-BiLSTM) that jointly matches questions against two granularities of relation representation: (i) relation-level tokens (relation ids / chains treated as tokens) to capture global semantics, and (ii) word-level tokens (tokenized relation names) to enable generalization to unseen relations. Residual connections across BiLSTM layers on the question encoder are used to facilitate hierarchical matching.
- A simple but effective KBQA pipeline that uses the relation detector twice: first to re-rank entity-linking candidates by collocation with high-confidence relations inferred directly from the question; then to detect relations for each candidate entity after replacing the entity mention with a special token. A lightweight constraint detection on a subgraph adds filtering constraints.
- Empirical gains on SimpleQuestions (single-hop) and WebQSP (multi-hop), with 2–3% improvements in relation detection and corresponding gains in end-to-end KBQA. Ablations support the contribution of both granularities and residual connections.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated modeling of relation representation granularity. Splitting relation names into words addresses zero-shot and large-vocabulary issues; retaining relation-level tokens preserves global cues that word-level alone loses. The hierarchical matching story is coherent and supported by ablations showing each view matters (notable drops when either is removed).
- Residual connections in the question encoder enable effective deep hierarchical matching. The paper convincingly shows that naïve layer weighting or attention underperform, and that residuals particularly help on WebQSP where longer contexts and chains are common. The control with two independent single-layer BiLSTMs helps attribute gains to depth plus residuals rather than mere ensembling.
- Practical KBQA pipeline improvement via entity re-ranking with relation cues. This is a simple, general mechanism to mitigate entity-linking ambiguity, and the ablation shows a sizable impact on end-to-end QA. It is an attractive trade-off versus more complex joint inference/re-ranking pipelines.
- Comprehensive evaluation setup for relation detection and end-to-end QA. The paper evaluates on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, reports p-values, and includes multiple baselines (char 3-gram, CNNs, attention). The WebQSP-specific relation detection task is constructed in a principled way (all relations/chains attached to the topic entity), and ablations explore encoder type, residual variants, and attention alternatives.
- Sensible engineering: sharing parameters between relation encoders, initializing relation-name encoders from word-level encodings as back-off for unseen relations, and using entity placeholder tokens in questions are small but thoughtful design choices that likely contribute to robustness.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior art. Representing relations as words has been explored (e.g., Yin et al., 2016; Liang et al., 2016), and deep BiLSTMs with residual/skip connections are now standard. The main technical increment is the particular combination—hierarchical dual-view matching plus residual addition across question layers—which, while effective, is a relatively incremental architectural tweak.
- Comparisons to stronger/alternative baselines are incomplete. The paper cites but does not compare against models leveraging pre-trained KB relation embeddings (e.g., TransE/CFO) or more expressive interaction models for sequence matching. The attention baseline is limited to decomposable attention; other cross-attention/match-LSTM style architectures or cross-encoders could provide a fairer point of comparison for “hierarchical matching.”
- The WebQSP relation detection benchmark is created by the authors. While the construction is reasonable, the lack of a widely adopted standardized split can complicate comparability; details such as candidate pool sizes per question, chain length distributions, and the ratio of seen/unseen relations are not reported. Releasing the resource is promised but not guaranteed; reproducibility depends on it.
- Zero-shot generalization is claimed but not quantified. The paper motivates unseen relations (e.g., 14% in SimpleQuestions) but does not report performance broken down by seen vs unseen relations or by novel chains on WebQSP. This analysis is critical to substantiate the importance of word-level relation modeling and the proposed back-off initialization.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and under-specified as a core enabler. The reported importance of the constraint module for WebQSP suggests it plays a large role in end-to-end gains, yet it uses character-overlap scoring and special rules. More analysis (e.g., precision/recall of constraint detection, failure modes) is needed to understand its impact and fairness relative to systems using learned constraint modules or joint decoding.
- Practicality and efficiency details are missing. The approach scores relations across all neighbors of top-K entities in step 1; for WebQSP/full Freebase this could be large. The paper does not report candidate set sizes, runtime, or memory footprint. Hyperparameters for entity re-ranking (alpha, l, K, K′, lambda) are not fully specified and may affect reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot analysis: What fraction of test relations (and chains) are unseen during training on each dataset? Can you report relation detection accuracy separately for seen vs unseen relations and for novel chains?
- Relation-chain representation: For multi-hop chains at word level, do you simply concatenate the words of each relation along the chain? Do you include separators or directionality markers? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Back-off initialization: How much does initializing the relation-name BiLSTM with the word-level encoding help? Please report an ablation removing this initialization.
- Candidate pool sizes and efficiency: What are the average numbers of (a) initial entity candidates K, (b) re-ranked entities K′, and (c) relation candidates per entity (and per question after union) on WebQSP? What are training/inference times per question?
- Entity re-ranking hyperparameters: How were alpha, l, and lambda chosen? Please include sensitivity curves. Does the re-ranking step ever hurt due to spurious high-scoring relations?
- Constraint module: What are its precision/recall on detecting constraints vs. adding spurious constraints? How many questions benefit from constraints, and by how much? Could HR-BiLSTM be adapted to learn constraints as well?
- Baselines: Did you attempt a comparison to models that use KB embeddings for relations (e.g., CFO), or stronger cross-attention encoders? If not, can you justify why your chosen baselines are sufficient?
