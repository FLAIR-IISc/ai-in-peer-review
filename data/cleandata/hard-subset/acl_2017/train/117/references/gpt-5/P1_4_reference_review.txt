Summary and contributions
This paper targets relation detection within KBQA and proposes: (1) a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities—relation-name tokens (relation-level) and tokenized relation words (word-level)—and combines two layers of question representations via residual connections to ease optimization and enable complementary matching; (2) a simple, effective KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection twice: first to re-rank entity-linker candidates by scoring relations connected to them, and second to select the core relation/chain, with an optional rule-based constraint detection step; (3) empirical evidence on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, with ablations, showing consistent improvements in both standalone relation detection and end-to-end KBQA.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated modeling of relation granularity and hierarchical matching. The observation that relation names encode global cues while tokenized words support generalization to unseen relations is sound. Combining both with deep question representations is intuitive and effective, and ablations confirm each component’s contribution (e.g., 93.3 vs. 91.2/88.8 on SQ when removing one view; +1.9–2.9 points over strongest baselines).

- Residual connections for multi-level sequence matching improve optimization and performance. The paper shows that naive weighted sums of layers underperform (92.5 vs. 93.3 on SQ; 80.65 vs. 82.53 on WQ), and that attention-based alternatives also lag. This is a pragmatic adaptation of residual learning to sequence matching with measurable gains, particularly on WebQSP where longer contexts and relation chains matter.

- Practical KBQA pipeline that directly leverages relation detection to mitigate entity-linking ambiguity. The entity re-ranking via “relation detection on entity sets” is simple yet impactful, addressing a common bottleneck (ambiguous surface forms). Ablations show that removing re-ranking substantially hurts end-task scores, indicating the module’s utility beyond the relation classifier itself.

- Solid empirical evaluation and analysis. The paper evaluates on two widely used datasets, uses public EL outputs for fair comparison, and provides informative ablations: removing residuals, removing one relation view, single vs. deep encoders, CNN vs. LSTM, and attention variants. The WebQSP relation detection setup is described, and performance deltas are statistically significant where claimed.

- Competitive to state-of-the-art with a relatively lightweight system. Despite avoiding joint inference or heavy rerankers, the approach achieves SOTA on SimpleQuestions and competitive results on WebQSP, and further gains by ensembling multiple relation detectors.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental. Hierarchical matching, multi-view representations (relation words + names), and residual connections are well-known ideas adapted to this setting. The main advance is their particular combination and the two-step use in KBQA, rather than a fundamentally new modeling approach to relation detection.

- Limited analysis of zero-shot and scalability aspects. A key motivation is handling unseen relations; however, the paper does not break down performance by seen vs. unseen relations or by relation frequency, nor does it quantify how much the relation-name view helps when test relations never appear in training. Similarly, efficiency/scalability of scoring “all relations connected to EL candidates” is not analyzed; degrees in Freebase can be large, and the choice of l (top relations) and its impact on runtime/accuracy is under-specified.

- Comparisons and fairness details are incomplete. For WebQSP, the relation detection dataset is constructed by the authors; while reasonable, it complicates comparability without public release and clear statistics (candidate pool sizes, chain-length distribution). For the KBQA end task, some recent strong baselines that exploit textual evidence or joint inference are not compared under matched settings, and the system benefits from ensembling top-3 relation detectors without detailing the ensemble protocol.

- Constraint detection is heuristic and brittle. The optional constraint module relies on character-overlap string matching and special rules (dates), operates only on the top-1 core chain, and could suffer from error propagation. While the overall system still performs well, this component seems ad hoc; more robust neural or type-aware constraint modeling could be explored or at least analyzed via error cases.

- Some implementation details are underspecified. It is unclear which residual variant (hidden-state vs. post-pooling) yields the reported best results; how negative sampling/candidate pools are formed during training; how relation chains are represented and composed for variable lengths; exact values and tuning ranges for α, λ, l, K, K′, and thresholds; and whether/when relation embeddings are shared or initialized across views.

Questions to authors
- Zero-shot analysis: What is the accuracy on relations unseen during training vs. seen, and how do the two relation views contribute in each regime? Could you report per-frequency buckets?

- Residual variant and hyperparameters: Which residual connection (between hidden states vs. after max-pooling) is used in the main reported numbers? Please provide the chosen α (entity re-ranking), λ (query combination), l (number of top relations for re-ranking), K/K′, and the margin in the ranking loss.

- Relation chains: How are chains encoded on the relation side (concatenation vs. learned composition), and how is chain length handled? Do you observe different gains for length-1 vs. length-2+ chains?

- Efficiency: What is the average number of relations scored per question in the entity re-ranking step and the per-question runtime on SQ/WQ? How sensitive are results to l and K′?

- Ensemble: What are the “top-3 relation detectors” combined for the final gains? How are their scores combined (simple average, learned weights)? What is the single-model best vs. ensemble?

- Dataset release: Will the WebQSP relation detection dataset and code be released to ensure comparability?

Additional comments (minor)
- Please clarify how relation names are tokenized (handling of separators/underscores), and how you initialize relation-name embeddings when unseen. Some qualitative examples of correct/incorrect matches for each view would be helpful.
