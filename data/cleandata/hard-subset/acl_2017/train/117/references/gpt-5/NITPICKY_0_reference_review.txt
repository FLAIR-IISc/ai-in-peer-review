Summary
The paper targets relation detection for KBQA and proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that matches questions to relations using two granularities of relation representation: word-level (tokenized relation names) and relation-level (whole relation identifiers). The model adds residual connections across BiLSTM layers on the question to ease hierarchical matching. The authors also integrate the detector into a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection: (i) use relation signals to rerank entity candidates and (ii) detect relations conditioned on the (re-ranked) topic entity, plus a simple constraint detection by string matching on a KB subgraph. Reported results are strong on SimpleQuestions and competitive on WebQSP; ablations attribute gains to the hierarchical + residual design.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) combining word-level and relation-level relation representations with residual connections across question encoders, yielding solid gains on relation detection (primary).
2) A simple, modular KBQA pipeline that leverages relation detection for entity reranking and relation prediction, achieving competitive end-to-end results without heavy joint inference (secondary).

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains on relation detection: On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, HR-BiLSTM improves 2–3 points over strong BiLSTM and CNN baselines; ablations indicate both hierarchical granularity and residual connections matter, particularly on WebQSP (82.53 vs 80.65 without residuals).
- Practical KBQA improvements with minimal engineering: The two-step relation detection plus simple constraint matching yields state-of-the-art or competitive end-task performance despite avoiding joint decoding or hand-crafted features. This is a strong engineering contribution for reproducible systems.
- Sensible modeling intuition: Combining relation-level tokens to capture long-range lexical patterns with word-level tokens for generalization addresses zero-shot relations and large relation vocabularies. Residual connections for hierarchical sequence matching are a plausible and effective architectural choice.
- Thorough ablations: The paper evaluates several ablations (w/o relation words, w/o relation names, no residual, attention in lieu of residuals, single-layer encoders, CNN variants), giving a decent causal story for the gains.
- Relevance and timeliness: KBQA relation detection under zero-shot and large-vocabulary settings is important, and the proposed methods address practical pain points (entity ambiguity; chain composition).

Weaknesses
- Technical correctness and clarity issues in core formulas: The ranking loss is written as max(0, s_pos + s_neg) with an unnamed margin; this is likely incorrect (should encourage s_pos > s_neg by a margin). The entity reranking formula uses R_l(q) \ R_e (set difference) where intersection is intended (text says “associated to the entity”). These are not cosmetic: they cast doubt on implementation correctness.
- Insufficient detail for reproducibility: Several critical hyperparameters are undefined or not reported (margin value; negative sampling strategy and pool size; l, K, K′, α, β in the pipeline; how relation-name embeddings are initialized/regularized; dropout, weight decay, early stopping, number of filters for CNN). Significance testing (p-values) is reported without test description. Appendices A–C are referred to for key components (constraint matching, date handling, and entity reranking metrics) but are not provided.
- Novelty is incremental: Using relation word sequences is common; matching both word- and relation-level representations has precedent; residual connections for sequence encoders are well-known. The conceptual advance is mainly the particular residual fusion for hierarchical matching; the pipeline entity reranking by relation signals is also not new. The contribution is thus more empirical/systemic than conceptual.
- Evaluation gaps: No breakdown for seen vs unseen relations (central to the motivation). No analysis of chain-length effects, candidate pool sizes, or robustness to entity linker errors beyond anecdotal examples. No runtime or scalability analysis despite operating on full Freebase for WebQSP. The WebQSP relation detection dataset is “to be released,” hindering verification.
- Numerous presentation/formatting errors: Frequent typos (“golded/golden,” “co-occurs,” inconsistent hyphenation), unresolved LaTeX symbols (alpha, margin, arrows), misnumbered/inline footnotes, malformed equations/symbols, inconsistent terminology (relation/core-chain), incomplete references (missing years, venues), and unclear figures/tables. These issues impede readability and confidence in precision.

Questions to Authors
- Please correct and clarify the loss function and provide the exact margin value and negative sampling strategy. Also confirm the actual set operation used in entity reranking (intersection vs difference) and provide the precise implemented formula.
- What are the sizes of K, K′, and l; how are α and β tuned; and how sensitive are results to these? Please provide full hyperparameter tables and training details (optimizer, schedule, dropout/regularization).
- How are relation-level token embeddings initialized for unseen relations? Do you share embeddings between word-level tokens and relation-level tokens or keep separate vocabularies?
- Provide seen/unseen relation breakdowns on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. How does HR-BiLSTM perform specifically on truly unseen relations and on multi-relation chains of different lengths?
- For the attention baseline, which exact variant(s) were implemented, with what hyperparameters, and how was parameter count matched? Please report variances over multiple runs and the statistical test used for p-values.
- Please release the WebQSP relation detection dataset, the code, and the constraint detection details (Appendix A/B/C) to enable reproducibility.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The empirical gains are solid and the approach is sensible, but the accumulation of technical, clarity, and reproducibility issues (including likely formula errors) undermines confidence. With corrected formulas, complete experimental details, released resources, and clearer writing, this could be a strong contribution.
