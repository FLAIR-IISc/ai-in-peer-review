Summary and contributions
This paper targets relation detection for KBQA. It proposes HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that matches questions to relations at two granularities: word-level tokens from relation names and relation-level IDs, combined via residual connections across BiLSTM layers. The model is then used in a simple pipeline KBQA system with an additional entity re-ranking step that leverages relation scores and an optional heuristic constraint detection. On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, the model improves relation detection accuracy and yields competitive or state-of-the-art KBQA results.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical relation detector that combines word-level and relation-level representations with residual connections and yields consistent gains.
2) A simple yet effective entity re-ranking step driven by relation detection, improving robustness to entity linker ambiguity.
3) Empirical evaluation with ablations across datasets, indicating the utility of multi-granularity and residual connections.

Strengths
1) Consistent empirical gains with ablations. The relation detector improves over strong baselines (AMPCNN, BiCNN, BiLSTM variants) on both SQ and WQ, with ablations that isolate the effects of word vs. relation tokens and residual connections. Performance improvements (2–3% absolute) are non-trivial for these tasks.

2) Multi-granularity design addresses practical KBQA constraints. Factoring relations into words helps generalization to large vocabularies; relation-level IDs capture broader patterns. The hierarchical matching idea is well motivated and supported by results.

3) Simple entity re-ranking step is effective. The use of relation detection to re-rank entity linker outputs is intuitive and seems to materially improve end-to-end performance where entity ambiguity is a bottleneck.

4) Broad comparison space. The paper compares against CNN/LSTM/attention variants and includes ablations (weighted sum vs residual, attention variant, CNN replacement), strengthening the empirical case.

5) Handling relation chains on WebQSP. The approach covers single relations and chains, and shows stronger benefits of residual/deeper architectures on multi-relation settings, which aligns with the claim that longer dependencies matter.

Weaknesses
1) Clarity and correctness issues in the learning objective and notation. The ranking loss is written as l_rel = max{0, s_rel(r+;q) + s_rel(r−;q)} “where is a constant,” missing the margin term/sign (should be something like max(0, γ − s+ + s−)). Several equations mix ambiguous symbols (e.g., “↵,” “,”) without definitions. In §4.1, Rl_q \ R_e is likely meant to be intersection but "\" is set difference. Such issues impede reproducibility and raise concerns about correctness.

2) Limited novelty relative to prior work. Using word-level relation tokens and relation IDs has precedents in KBQA; residual/shortcut connections in sequence models are also well-known. The main novelty is their combination for this task and the specific aggregation (residual on layers and on max-pool), which is incremental. The paper overstates the gap to prior sequence matching/attention models without stronger analysis.

3) Missing experimental details and calibration. Many crucial hyperparameters are not reported: margin γ, α for srerank, λ in final combination, K/K′/l values, negative sampling strategy, optimizer/batch size/regularization/dropout, tokenization and OOV handling for relation names, normalization of cosine scores when mixing with entity-linker scores. There are no runtime/complexity analyses for the “relation detection on entity set” step, which could be large. Significance testing is mentioned (p-values) but the test and repeated runs/variance are not reported.

4) Claims about unseen relations are not substantiated. Although zero-shot generalization is emphasized, there is no stratified evaluation on seen vs. unseen relations nor an explicit zero-shot split; conclusions rely on aggregate accuracy. This weakens the central motivation.

5) Comparability and reproducibility concerns. WebQSP uses full Freebase; SimpleQuestions uses FB2M; different linkers are used across systems. It is unclear if comparisons to STAGG/AMPCNN are fully comparable. Several key details are deferred to appendices (A/B/C), which are not available; the WebQSP relation detection dataset is “to be released,” making verification difficult.

6) Constraint detection is heuristic and under-specified. The n-gram character-overlap matching lacks detail (thresholds, handling of dates/types) in the main text and may be brittle. Yet it is claimed to be crucial for final performance; more rigorous formulation or analysis is desirable.

7) Numerous formatting and citation issues. Many references have missing/garbled years; LaTeX artifacts and footnote markers persist; figures/tables have inconsistent labels; minor typos (“the the”) and symbol inconsistencies abound. These detract from professionalism and readability.

Questions to authors
1) Please correct and fully specify the ranking loss (margin value, sign, negative sampling strategy) and all hyperparameters used in §4 (α, λ, K, K′, l, threshold θ), including how they were tuned and the final chosen values per dataset.

2) How are s_rel (cosine) and entity-linker scores calibrated before combination? Are scores normalized per question or per candidate set?

3) What are the average sizes of candidate relation sets in §4.1 and §4.2, and the computational cost (per question) for relation scoring and the full pipeline? Any pruning strategies?

4) Can you report performance split by seen vs. unseen relations (and by chain length) to support the zero-shot/generalization claims?

5) For relation-level tokens: how are multiword relation names tokenized and embedded? How are OOV relation IDs handled? Are embeddings updated during training?

6) How is entity replacement handled for multiple mentions or partial matches? What if the entity surface form is not present verbatim in the question?

7) Please detail optimizer, batch size, regularization (dropout/L2), number of epochs, and variance across multiple runs. How were p-values computed?

8) Will you release code, trained models, and the constructed WebQSP relation detection dataset upon publication to ensure reproducibility?

Additional comments (minor)
- Fix notation in §4.1 (intersection vs difference), define all symbols on first use, and standardize Greek letters.
- Improve figure/table captions and legibility; ensure consistent model names (HR-BiLSTM vs Hier-Res-BiLSTM).
- Provide brief error analysis to illustrate typical failure modes (e.g., lexical ambiguity vs long-range paraphrases).
- Consider calibrating scores or using a learning-to-rank combiner for s_rel and s_linker.
