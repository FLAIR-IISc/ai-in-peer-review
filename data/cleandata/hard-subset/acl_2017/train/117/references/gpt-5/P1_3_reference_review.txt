Summary
This paper targets relation detection in knowledge-base question answering (KBQA). It argues that KB relations exhibit multiple granularities: whole relation names (useful for global cues) and their constituent words (useful for local cues), and that questions require representations at multiple abstraction levels to match these. The authors propose HR-BiLSTM, a hierarchical residual BiLSTM that (i) encodes relations with both word-level tokens and relation-name tokens, (ii) encodes questions with a 2-layer BiLSTM, and (iii) uses residual shortcuts to combine layers for robust matching. They further integrate relation detection into a simple KBQA pipeline with a new relation-aware entity re-ranking step, and an optional constraint detection module. On SimpleQuestions (SQ) and WebQSP (WQ), HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection by 2–3% absolute over strong baselines and yields state-of-the-art or comparable end-to-end QA accuracy.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A hierarchical residual matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) that fuses relation word-level and relation-name representations with multi-layer question representations, achieving strong gains in relation detection on SQ and WQ.
- Secondary: A simple yet effective KBQA pipeline that uses relation detection twice—first to re-rank entities, then to score relations per entity—leading to improvements in end-to-end QA without heavy joint inference.
- Tertiary: Empirical analysis/ablations showing: (a) both relation-name and word-level signals are necessary; (b) residual connections materially help hierarchical matching (especially on WQ); (c) constraint detection helps more when upstream entity/chain selection is strong.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains on relation detection: HR-BiLSTM achieves 93.3% on SQ and 82.53% on WQ relation detection, improving by 2–3% absolute over the best baselines (BiLSTM w/ words and AMPCNN), with reported statistical significance. Ablations convincingly show that removing either granularity (name or word) hurts (e.g., 93.3 → 91.2/88.8 on SQ), and residual connections outperform weighted-sum and attention-based hierarchical matching.
- Effective, practical integration into KBQA: The relation-aware entity re-ranking step addresses a real bottleneck (entity ambiguity) and significantly boosts end-task performance. Removing re-ranking causes notable drops in QA accuracy, indicating the practical value of relation signals for disambiguation.
- Well-motivated modeling of granularity and abstraction: The paper clearly articulates why relation-name tokens capture global cues (longer phrases/skip-grams) while word tokens capture local cues, and why deeper question encoders produce varying abstraction levels. The residual design helps training stability and complementarity between layers; ablations replacing LSTMs with CNNs or removing residuals support these design choices, especially on multi-relation WQ.
- Minimal engineering with competitive SOTA: Despite a simple pipeline (no heavy joint inference or global feature re-ranking), the approach reaches SOTA on SQ and is competitive on WQ. This simplicity aids adoption and reproducibility.
- Comprehensive comparisons and ablations: The paper re-implements competitive baselines (AMPCNN, char tri-gram BiCNN), evaluates alternative encoders (CNN vs LSTM), and explores hierarchical matching variants (weighted sum, attention), providing a thorough view of what matters.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond judicious combination: Using relation-name and word-level tokens and adding residual connections to a multi-layer BiLSTM are known ideas. The contribution is primarily in combining these for KB relation detection, rather than introducing a fundamentally new modeling paradigm. The attention-based hierarchical matching explored is relatively shallow; more modern cross-attention or pre-trained contextual encoders are not compared.
- Weak validation of “zero-shot/unseen relation” claims: While the motivation emphasizes unseen relations, there is no explicit evaluation split (seen vs unseen relations) or analysis of performance vs relation frequency. WQ is largely close-domain; SQ has unseen relations, but quantitative evidence isolating zero-shot generalization is missing.
- Incomplete algorithmic details and clarity: The ranking loss lacks a margin parameter description; combination weights (alpha for re-ranking, lambda for final scoring) and candidate set sizes (K, K', l) are not fully specified; the initialization of relation-name encoders “with final state of word sequence” is under-explained. Notational inconsistencies (e.g., missing symbols) and typos hinder reproducibility.
- Fairness and scope of end-task comparison: The QA comparisons rely on different underlying resources (FB2M vs full Freebase across datasets), and the best numbers are obtained by ensembling top-3 relation detectors. Constraint detection uses heuristic character-overlap; stronger text-evidence models (as in prior work) are not compared, making it hard to attribute gains solely to relation detection.
- Efficiency/scalability not addressed: The approach scores many relations per entity and also “relation detection on entity sets” for re-ranking. There is no report on candidate pool sizes, runtime, or memory, which matters for large KBs with thousands of relations and high-degree entities.

Questions to authors
- Can you report performance broken down by seen vs unseen relations (especially on SQ), and by relation frequency buckets? Similarly, a breakdown for single vs multi-relation chains on WQ.
- What are the exact values and tuning ranges for the ranking loss margin, alpha (entity re-ranking weight), and lambda (final combination), and for K, K', and l? Please report sensitivity analyses.
- How are relation names tokenized (handling underscores, slashes, camelCase, aliases)? For relation chains, do you include individual relation names as separate relation-level tokens, or concatenate the chain as a single token?
- Please clarify the “initialize the relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence” mechanism and provide an ablation isolating its effect.
- What are the typical candidate pool sizes per step (relations per entity, entities per question pre/post re-ranking), and end-to-end inference time? How does HR-BiLSTM scale to higher-degree nodes?
- Could the entity re-ranking be biased when multiple entities share similar relation neighborhoods? Have you evaluated on ambiguous-name subsets to quantify gains from re-ranking?
- Will you release the constructed WQ relation detection dataset and code to reproduce both relation detection and QA results?
