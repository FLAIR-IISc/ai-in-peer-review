Summary
This paper tackles relation detection for KBQA, aiming to handle large relation vocabularies, zero-shot relations, and relation chains. The core technical contribution is a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: (i) word-level (tokenized relation names) for generalization to unseen relations, and (ii) relation-level (whole relation identifiers) for capturing global signals. Residual connections across stacked BiLSTMs are used to ease training and promote complementary abstractions. The authors further integrate relation detection into a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection approach: relation-guided entity re-ranking followed by entity-aware relation detection, plus an optional heuristic constraint detection module. On SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection and yields strong end-to-end QA performance, with ablations supporting the design choices.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical residual sequence matching model (HR-BiLSTM) that combines word-level and relation-level representations and exploits residual connections to improve relation detection for KBQA.
2) A simple but effective KBQA pipeline where relation detection re-ranks entity candidates and improves downstream relation prediction, leading to state-of-the-art or competitive results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP.
3) Construction of a WebQSP-based relation detection benchmark and a thorough ablation showing the importance of hierarchical matching and residual connections.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains on two benchmarks with clear, interpretable ablations. HR-BiLSTM outperforms competitive baselines for relation detection (e.g., +2–3% accuracy over strong BiLSTM/AMPCNN baselines on both datasets) and improves end-to-end QA by 2–3% when plugged into the pipeline. The ablations convincingly isolate the impact of (a) using both word- and relation-level views, and (b) residual connections vs. weighted-sum/attention.
- Well-motivated design for zero-shot and large relation spaces. Splitting relation names into words addresses unseen relations; retaining relation-level identifiers captures global co-occurrence signals beyond local word matches. The hierarchical question encoder aligns with the need to match phrases of varying lengths (e.g., single tokens vs. longer paraphrases).
- Practical integration with entity linking. The entity re-ranking step, driven purely by relation signals from the question, addresses a real bottleneck in KBQA and yields measurable end-task gains. This is a simple, general idea that should transfer across linkers and KBs.
- Broad applicability and simplicity. The pipeline avoids heavy joint inference or complex re-rankers yet achieves state-of-the-art or near SOTA results, suggesting the relation detector is robust and generally useful.
- Sensible analysis choices. The paper tests CNN vs. LSTM encoders, attention vs. residuals, single- vs. multi-layer encoders, and with/without each relation view; it also discusses why WebQSP benefits more from deeper/residual architectures (longer dependencies and relation chains).

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty. The two core ideas—(a) word-level decomposition of relation names and (b) residual connections for deep sequence encoders—are established. The architectural novelty is a careful combination tailored to relation detection. While the empirical gains are solid, the conceptual leap beyond prior attention-based matching or hierarchical encoders is moderate.
- Limited comparisons to alternative relation encoding strategies. Prior KBQA work uses pre-trained relation embeddings (e.g., TransE/KB embeddings) or character-level matching. Although the paper mentions these, there is no direct comparison to a strong TransE-initialized relation matcher or to recent neural semantic parsers on WebQSP. This makes it harder to attribute gains to the proposed hierarchical residual design versus simply better relation representations.
- Heuristic constraint detection and missing details. The constraint detection module is largely rule/overlap-based, not learned, and lacks detailed evaluation or error analysis. It is claimed to be “crucial” for the system on WebQSP, but quantitative breakdowns (e.g., precision/recall of constraint linking, ablations per constraint type) are sparse, and some key settings are deferred to appendices.
- Reproducibility and fairness concerns. Several hyperparameters critical to the two-step pipeline (K, K’, l, α, λ, thresholds) are not explicitly reported in the main text, and the promised datasets/code are not available within the paper. The WebQSP relation detection benchmark is newly constructed; although reasonable, the comparison to baselines relies on the authors’ reimplementations and may reflect setup differences. Efficiency and scalability (e.g., number of relations per entity, number of chains considered, runtime) are not discussed.
- Scope and generality. The approach assumes informative relation names; performance may degrade for KBs with opaque IDs or noisy naming conventions. It is unclear how well the method adapts when relation names do not align lexically with question phrasing, or when the KB uses schema-level abstractions.

Questions to authors
- Please report the exact hyperparameters used in the KBQA pipeline (K, K’, l, α for entity re-ranking; λ for combining steps; chain length limit; matching thresholds for constraints), plus average candidate set sizes and runtime per question on both datasets.
- How are relation chains enumerated and represented during training/inference? What is the maximum chain length and average number of candidate chains considered per entity?
- Did you evaluate a variant that incorporates KB embeddings (e.g., TransE) for relation initialization or as an additional view? If so, how does it compare to HR-BiLSTM; if not, can you comment on expected gains or challenges?
- Can you provide an error analysis contrasting failures that require global relation-level cues vs. local word-level cues, and how residual connections change these error modes?
- How sensitive are results to the quality of the initial entity linker? If the top-K recall drops, does relation-guided re-ranking still help? Please include quantitative improvements in entity linking recall/precision after re-ranking.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Release the WebQSP relation detection dataset and code to strengthen reproducibility and adoption.
- Include a more detailed evaluation of the constraint detection module (per constraint type, ablations, and learned alternatives).
- Discuss applicability to KBs with less informative relation names and consider augmenting with schema/type signals or KB embeddings.
- Consider end-to-end training of the entity re-ranking and relation detection components, or a lightweight joint inference step, to reduce error propagation.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper presents a carefully engineered, well-motivated improvement to relation detection with solid empirical impact on two established KBQA benchmarks, supported by ablation studies. While the novelty is moderate and some comparisons/details are missing, the method is practical and advances the state of the art. Addressing the questions above would further strengthen the contribution.
