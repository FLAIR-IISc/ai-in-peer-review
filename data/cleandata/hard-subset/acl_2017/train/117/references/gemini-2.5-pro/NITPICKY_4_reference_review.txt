### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The authors argue that relation detection in KBQA is distinct from general relation extraction due to the large number of relations, the need for zero-shot learning, and the presence of relation chains. To tackle these challenges, they propose a novel model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM). This model performs hierarchical matching by representing relations at both the word-level and relation-level, and questions at multiple levels of abstraction using a deep BiLSTM. A residual learning mechanism is introduced to facilitate the training of this deep architecture. The authors embed this improved relation detector into a simple KBQA pipeline that features a two-step relation detection process: first for re-ranking entity candidates and second for identifying the final relation for a chosen entity. Experiments on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets show that the proposed model improves relation detection accuracy and that the overall KBQA system achieves state-of-the-art or comparable results.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Hierarchical Matching Model for Relation Detection (HR-BiLSTM):** The primary contribution is a neural architecture that matches a question against a KB relation using multiple levels of granularity. It combines a multi-level question representation (from a deep BiLSTM) with a dual-view relation representation (word-level and relation-level tokens) using a residual connection scheme.
2.  **A Two-Step Relation Detection Pipeline for KBQA:** The paper proposes using their relation detector in a two-stage process. First, it is used to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates by scoring their associated relations against the raw question. Second, it is used to find the specific relation for the top re-ranked entities. This entity re-ranking step is shown to be beneficial for the end-to-end KBQA task.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of distinguishing relation detection for KBQA from general relation extraction. It clearly outlines the key challenges (large/open relation vocabulary, zero-shot requirements, relation chains) that motivate the need for a specialized approach.
2.  **Intuitive Model Architecture:** The core idea of matching multi-granularity representations of questions and relations is sound. Representing relations as both a sequence of words (for generalization to unseen relations) and as a single token (to capture global semantics) is a sensible design choice. The hierarchical approach to matching these representations is logical.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed HR-BiLSTM model demonstrates significant improvements over several baselines on two distinct relation detection tasks derived from SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. Furthermore, the complete KBQA system, despite its relative simplicity (e.g., no feature-based re-ranker), achieves state-of-the-art results, underscoring the effectiveness of the improved relation detection component. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the utility of the key components of the model (hierarchical representation, residual learning).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Clarity:** Several critical parts of the methodology are described vaguely or contain apparent errors.
    *   The description of "residual learning" is imprecise. The authors claim the second layer "is fitting the residues from the first layer of matching," but the implementation (`h_i' = h_i^(1) + h_i^(2)` or `hq = h_max^(1) + h_max^(2)`) is an additive combination of two different representations rather than the standard `H(x) = F(x) + x` formulation of residual networks. The connection is more metaphorical than technical, which is misleading.
    *   The entity re-ranking score formula in Section 4.1 appears to have a critical error. The formula uses `max r∈Rlq\Re srel(r; q)`, where `\` is the set difference operator. This implies that an entity is scored higher if its associated relations *do not* include the top-predicted relations for the question, which is counter-intuitive. This should almost certainly be an intersection (`∩`), and this confusion undermines a key component of the proposed KBQA system.
    *   Key implementation details are missing, hindering reproducibility. The initialization of the relation-level LSTM with the final state of the word-level LSTM is an interesting idea but is not explained in sufficient detail. The final values for numerous crucial hyperparameters (`α`, `β`, margin `γ`, `l`, `K'`, `K'0`) are not reported.

2.  **Superficial Analysis and Experimental Gaps:**
    *   The ablation study analysis is weak. For instance, the claim that WebQSP benefits more from deeper architectures because it requires handling a "larger scope of context matching" is an unsubstantiated hypothesis. The paper would be much stronger with qualitative analysis and concrete examples illustrating *why* and *how* the different model components contribute to success on different datasets.
    *   The relation detection task for WebQSP was created by the authors. While this is a reasonable contribution, the dataset is not available at the time of review ("will make... available soon"), and the description of its creation lacks detail (e.g., number of negative examples per positive, handling of class imbalance). This makes it difficult to fully assess the validity of the results on this task.

3.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper is marred by a significant number of formatting errors and a general lack of polish, which detracts from its scientific merit.
    *   The bibliography is completely broken. It is littered with formatting errors, extraneous characters (`\`, `?`), and inconsistent formatting, making it difficult to read and unprofessional. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   Figure 2, illustrating the main model, is cluttered, and the use of symbols like `!"#$%&` is unconventional and distracting. The arrows for "Shortcut connections" are ambiguous.
    *   Important implementation details and limitations are relegated to footnotes (e.g., Footnote 5), whereas they should be discussed in the main body of the paper.
    *   There are numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout the text (e.g., "golded test relations," "few work," awkward phrasing). Citations are also formatted inconsistently within the text (e.g., improper line breaks).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the entity re-ranking score equation in Section 4.1? The use of the set difference operator `\` seems incorrect. Was this intended to be an intersection (`∩`)? Please provide the correct formula.
2.  Could you provide a more precise technical explanation of how your use of "shortcut connections" relates to the canonical formulation of residual learning? The current description is largely based on intuition.
3.  For reproducibility, could you please report the final hyperparameter values used for the experiments (e.g., `α`, `β`, `l` for re-ranking, `K`, `K'`, LSTM hidden sizes, learning rate, etc.) for both datasets?
