**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The authors argue that existing methods do not adequately handle the challenges of KBQA relation detection, such as the large number of relations and the need for zero-shot generalization. They propose a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that performs hierarchical matching. The model creates two levels of relation representations (word-level and relation-name-level) and matches them against two levels of question representations generated by a deep BiLSTM. To facilitate training, a residual connection is added between the BiLSTM layers. This relation detector is then embedded into a KBQA pipeline that first uses it to re-rank entity linker candidates and then to detect the final relation for the top-ranked entity. The authors evaluate their method on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets, claiming state-of-the-art results for both the standalone relation detection task and the end-to-end KBQA task.

**Main contributions (as I see them)**

1.  A novel entity re-ranking step that uses a relation detection model to disambiguate entity candidates prior to core relation detection. This shows a tangible benefit for the overall KBQA pipeline.
2.  A hierarchical matching architecture for relation detection that combines multi-granularity representations of relations (words and full names) with multi-level representations of the question.
3.  Strong empirical results on two standard KBQA benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed relation detection model within a simple, feature-light KBQA system.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated model architecture:** The core idea of matching hierarchical representations of questions and relations is intuitive and directly addresses a key challenge in KB relation detection: the trade-off between the generalization of word-level matching and the specificity of matching against full relation names. This is a clear and sensible approach.
2.  **Effective entity re-ranking:** The proposed two-step application of the relation detector, particularly the entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1), is a clever and practical contribution. It directly tackles the known bottleneck of entity linking ambiguity. The ablation study in Table 3 confirms its substantial impact (+6.5% on SQ, +5.3% on WebQSP), demonstrating that this is a significant part of the paper's contribution to the final KBQA performance.
3.  **Strong empirical performance:** The proposed HR-BiLSTM model shows a clear and significant improvement over several strong baselines on the isolated relation detection task (Table 2). Furthermore, the full KBQA system, despite its relative simplicity (e.g., no beam search or feature-based re-ranker), achieves excellent results, suggesting that improvements in the core relation detection module can yield substantial end-to-end gains.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of methodological clarity and rigor:** Several key components of the method are poorly described.
    *   The term "Residual Learning" is used loosely. The paper claims the second layer "is fitting the residues from the first layer of matching," but the described mechanism is a simple summation of representations (`hq = h(1)max + h(2)max`). This is not the standard formulation of residual learning (`F(x) + x`) and the paper provides no theoretical or empirical justification for why this specific operation encourages learning a "residue." The connection to He et al. (2016) feels superficial.
    *   The description of the model architecture is ambiguous. Section 3.2 presents two ways of applying residual connections (on hidden states or on max-pooled outputs) but fails to specify which was used for the final experiments. This is a critical detail for reproducibility.
    *   The ablation for the "single-layer BiLSTM" in Table 2 is confusing. It is unclear how a single-layer question encoder performs hierarchical matching against both word-level and relation-level relation inputs. This makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the deep, hierarchical architecture.

2.  **Oversights in experimental design and reporting:**
    *   The state-of-the-art results reported in the last row of Table 3 are based on an ensemble of three models. While ensembling is a valid technique, presenting this as the primary result can be misleading. The performance of the single HR-BiLSTM model, which is the paper's core contribution, is more modest (52.5 on WebQSP, compared to STAGG's 52.5) and should be the focus of comparison.
    *   The KBQA system eschews beam search, processing only the top-1 query path greedily (Footnote 5). This is a significant simplification compared to the STAGG system (Yih et al., 2015) it is compared against, which relies heavily on staged search with a beam. This makes the comparison less direct and potentially masks brittleness in the proposed system.
    *   The description of the constraint detection module (Section 4.4) is relegated to an appendix and appears overly simplistic, based on "maximum overlapping sequence of characters." Given that this step is responsible for a large performance jump on WebQSP (from 58.0% to 65.3%), its rudimentary nature and scant description are concerning.

3.  **Numerous formatting and presentation errors:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish that detracts from its credibility.
    *   The reference section is duplicated, with a properly formatted list followed by a garbled, unprocessed list of bib entries. This is a major formatting error that should have been caught during proofreading.
    *   Citations in the introduction are inconsistent in their formatting (e.g., "Bast and Haussmann, 2015" vs. "Berant et al., 2013").
    *   There are minor but noticeable grammatical errors and awkward phrasings (e.g., "golded test relations," "no previous used entity information").
    *   Figure 2 is not self-contained; the function of the crucial "Shortcut connections" is not explained in the figure or its caption, requiring the reader to hunt for the explanation in the text.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents a well-motivated model and a clever KBQA pipeline with strong results, the numerous methodological ambiguities, questionable experimental comparisons, and significant formatting errors prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The lack of clarity in the core "residual learning" mechanism and the over-reliance on an ensemble for SOTA claims are particularly problematic. The work has potential, but requires a major revision to address the issues of clarity, rigor, and presentation. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**.
