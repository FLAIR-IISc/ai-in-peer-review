### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for the task of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The core idea is to perform a hierarchical match between the question and the candidate relation. To achieve this, relations are represented at two granularities (word-level for generalization and relation-level for global context), and questions are encoded with a two-layer BiLSTM to capture different levels of abstraction. Crucially, the authors adapt the concept of residual learning to connect the two BiLSTM layers, which they argue simplifies training and produces more complementary question representations. This improved relation detector is then integrated into a simple but effective KBQA pipeline that introduces a novel entity re-ranking step, using the relation detector's output to disambiguate initial entity linking results.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Architecture for Relation Detection (HR-BiLSTM):** The primary contribution is the model itself, which creatively combines three key ideas: hierarchical representation of relations, multi-level abstraction of questions via a deep BiLSTM, and the adaptation of residual connections for a sequence matching task. This represents a bold and conceptually interesting approach to modeling the question-relation similarity space.
2.  **A Novel KBQA System Design:** The paper proposes an elegant two-step application of their relation detector. The first step, using the model to re-rank entity candidates *before* selecting a topic entity, is a very clever method for mitigating entity linking errors by leveraging relational context. This is a significant system-level insight.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model and system achieve state-of-the-art performance on two distinct benchmarks, SimpleQuestions (single-relation) and WebQSP (multi-relation), demonstrating the general applicability and effectiveness of the approach.

### Strengths

1.  **High Degree of Novelty and Creativity:** This work stands out for its novelty. The idea of applying residual connections, inspired by computer vision, to a deep BiLSTM for a hierarchical matching task in NLP is not an obvious step and represents a significant conceptual leap. It moves beyond simply stacking layers and proposes a principled way to make deep recurrent models more effective for this type of matching problem. This is exactly the kind of bold, cross-domain inspiration that pushes the field forward.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Core Idea:** The central premise—that relations benefit from being viewed at both a granular word-level and a holistic relation-level, and that a question encoder should produce representations to match both—is compelling and clearly articulated. The HR-BiLSTM architecture is a direct and elegant implementation of this idea. The problem framing is excellent, and the solution is thoughtfully designed to address the specific challenges of KB relation detection (e.g., unseen relations).
3.  **Clever Integration into the End Task:** The entity re-ranking step is a major strength. It shows that the authors have not just designed a better standalone component, but have also thought carefully about how to best leverage it to solve a key bottleneck (entity ambiguity) in the broader KBQA pipeline. The fact that their simple system, without complex feature engineering or joint inference, can achieve state-of-the-art results is a powerful testament to the strength of their core relation detection model.
4.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence across two different datasets. The gains over strong baselines are significant. More importantly, the ablation studies are thorough and effectively demonstrate that each novel component—the hierarchical representation, the residual connections, and the choice of BiLSTM over CNN—contributes meaningfully to the final performance.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. I view these as minor points that do not detract from the overall significance of the work.

1.  **Limited Analysis of the Residual Mechanism:** The paper shows that residual connections work well, but the explanation for *why* they outperform a simple weighted sum or an attention mechanism is somewhat brief. The intuition that the second layer models the "residue" is appealing, but a more in-depth analysis—perhaps through representation visualization or a discussion of gradient flow during training—would provide deeper insight and make the contribution even more impactful.
2.  **Simplicity of the Constraint Detection Module:** The constraint detection component relies on a fairly basic text-matching method. While the overall system performs well, this final step feels less sophisticated than the core model. This is less of a flaw and more of a missed opportunity; it suggests that even greater performance could be unlocked by developing a more powerful constraint handler. However, I appreciate that the focus of the paper is on relation detection, and the current design effectively isolates and highlights the contribution of the HR-BiLSTM model.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your ablation study shows that residual learning and the deep architecture provide a much larger boost on WebQSP than on SimpleQuestions. Your explanation is that WebQSP requires handling a larger scope of context. Could you elaborate on this? Is it primarily due to the presence of relation chains in WebQSP, which the deeper, compositional nature of the HR-BiLSTM is better able to model?
2.  The two-step relation detection process (first for entity re-ranking, second for relation prediction) seems effective but potentially computationally expensive. Could you comment on the computational overhead of this approach compared to a standard pipeline, and whether this might be a concern for real-world applications?
