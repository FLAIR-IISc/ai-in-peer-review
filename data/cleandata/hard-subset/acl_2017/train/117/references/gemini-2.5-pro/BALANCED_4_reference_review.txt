**Summary of the paper**
This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), arguing that it differs significantly from standard relation extraction due to the large number of relations, the need to handle unseen relations (zero-shot learning), and the presence of relation chains. To tackle these challenges, the authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM). The model performs hierarchical matching by encoding the question with a deep, 2-layer BiLSTM and representing the target relation at two granularities: as a sequence of words and as a single relation-level token. A key aspect of the model is its use of residual connections to combine the representations from the two BiLSTM layers, which the authors claim improves training and enables better matching of different question abstractions to different relation granularities.

The authors embed this improved relation detector into a simple but effective KBQA pipeline. A distinctive feature of this pipeline is a two-step application of the relation detector: first, it is used to re-rank an initial set of candidate entities by scoring their associated relations against the raw question text; second, after selecting a topic entity, it is used again to identify the specific relation or relation chain. The paper shows through extensive experiments on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets that this approach achieves state-of-the-art results on both the isolated relation detection task and the end-to-end KBQA task.

**Main Contributions**
1.  **A novel hierarchical matching architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection.** The primary contribution is the model itself, which thoughtfully combines multi-granularity relation representations (word-level for generalization, relation-level for semantics) with multi-level question representations from a deep BiLSTM. The use of residual connections to fuse the layers of the question encoder is a clever and effective method for this task.
2.  **A demonstration of how an improved relation detector can drive a simple and effective KBQA system.** The paper presents a straightforward KBQA pipeline that leverages the HR-BiLSTM for both entity re-ranking and relation prediction. This design, particularly the entity re-ranking step, elegantly uses relation semantics to resolve entity ambiguity, a common bottleneck in KBQA.
3.  **Comprehensive empirical validation and analysis.** The work is supported by strong results on two standard KBQA benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art performance. The ablation studies are thorough and provide convincing evidence for the effectiveness of each component of the proposed model, including the hierarchical representations and the residual learning mechanism.

**Strengths**
1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper provides an excellent framing of the KB relation detection problem, clearly differentiating it from general relation extraction and articulating the specific challenges involved. The proposed model is directly and logically motivated by these challenges, making the work easy to follow and appreciate.
2.  **Sound and Well-Justified Model Design:** The core ideas of the HR-BiLSTM model are intuitive and well-justified. Combining word-level representations to handle unseen relations with relation-level representations to capture holistic meaning is a strong design choice. Furthermore, applying residual learning to combine different layers of the question encoder is a novel contribution in this context that appears to be empirically successful, especially on the more complex WebQSP dataset.
3.  **Rigorous and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are a major strength of this paper. The authors evaluate on two different datasets that cover single-relation and multi-relation questions. They report results for both the subtask of relation detection and the end-to-end KBQA task, providing a complete picture of the model's impact. The ablation study in Table 2 is particularly strong, as it systematically dismantles the model to quantify the contribution of each design choice (hierarchical inputs, residual learning, LSTM vs. CNN encoder).
4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility for KBQA:** The paper shows that focusing on and improving a core component can lead to a state-of-the-art system without the need for complex, heavily-engineered features or joint inference mechanisms. The entity re-ranking strategy is a simple, effective method that directly addresses the ambiguity inherent in entity linkers and could be easily adopted in other modular KBQA systems.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Ambiguity in the Residual Connection Implementation:** In Section 3.2, the paper describes two distinct ways to implement the residual connections for hierarchical matching: (1) summing the hidden states at each position before max-pooling, and (2) summing the max-pooled representations from each layer. Section 5.1 mentions this as a tunable hyper-parameter, but the paper never clarifies which method was ultimately used for the reported results or whether one consistently outperformed the other. This lack of detail hinders reproducibility and a full understanding of the final model.
2.  **Potential Scalability Issues are Not Discussed:** The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) involves using the HR-BiLSTM model to score all relations connected to the initial top-K entity candidates. In a large-scale knowledge base like Freebase, prominent entities can be connected to hundreds or thousands of relations. This step could therefore be a significant computational bottleneck at inference time. The paper lacks any discussion of the practical efficiency or scalability of this approach, which is an important consideration for a KBQA system.
3.  **The Simplicity of the KBQA Pipeline:** While the simplicity of the KBQA system is used to highlight the strength of the relation detector, it also introduces limitations. The system is a greedy, staged pipeline where errors can easily propagate. For example, an error in the entity re-ranking or core relation detection step cannot be recovered later. The authors note this in a footnote, but a more direct discussion of this limitation and how their powerful relation detector might be integrated into more robust frameworks (e.g., using beam search with a final re-ranker, as in STAGG) would strengthen the paper. This is particularly relevant for WebQSP, where the single-model performance (58.0) lags considerably behind the ensemble result (64.0).

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the two methods for implementing residual connections described in Section 3.2 (summing hidden states vs. summing pooled vectors): Which of these two methods was used in the final model that produced the reported results? Did you observe a notable performance difference between them during your hyper-parameter tuning?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the computational overhead of the entity re-ranking step? For the WebQSP dataset, for instance, what is the average number of relations scored per question during this step, and what is the impact on the overall inference time compared to a baseline system without this re-ranking component?
3.  The performance lift from the single model to the ensemble model is substantial on WebQSP (58.0 to 64.0). Does this suggest that the task is particularly sensitive to model initialization/variance, or do the different models in the ensemble (e.g., HR-BiLSTM vs. HR-CNN) learn highly complementary signals for relation detection on this dataset?
