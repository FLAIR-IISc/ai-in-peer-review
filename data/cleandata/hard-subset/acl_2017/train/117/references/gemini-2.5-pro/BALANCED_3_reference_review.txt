### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), a challenging problem due to the large number of relations, the presence of unseen relations at test time (zero-shot learning), and the need to detect relation chains. The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), which performs hierarchical matching. It compares a multi-layered BiLSTM representation of the question against a dual-granularity representation of the relation (as a single token and as a sequence of words). To facilitate this hierarchical matching, residual connections are introduced between the BiLSTM layers. The authors then integrate this improved relation detector into a simple but effective KBQA pipeline that uses the model in two stages: first, to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates, and second, to identify the correct relation for the top-ranked entities. The system is evaluated on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, achieving state-of-the-art results on both the isolated relation detection task and the end-to-end KBQA task.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective neural architecture for relation detection (HR-BiLSTM).** The core ideas of representing relations at multiple granularities (word-level and relation-level) and using residual connections to enable hierarchical matching with a deep question encoder are well-motivated and empirically validated.
2.  **A demonstration of how a strong relation detection component can significantly improve and simplify a KBQA system.** The proposed two-step pipeline, which uses the relation detector to aid entity disambiguation before final relation prediction, is a simple yet powerful design that achieves impressive results without complex, feature-rich re-rankers or joint inference.
3.  **A thorough and convincing empirical evaluation.** The paper presents strong results on two standard benchmarks with different characteristics. The claims are well-supported by extensive ablation studies that clearly isolate the benefits of each component of the proposed model.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant model design.** The paper provides a clear justification for its core architectural choices. The trade-off between word-level (better generalization) and relation-level (captures global context) representations is a key challenge in KB relation detection, and the proposed hierarchical matching approach is an intuitive solution. Furthermore, the application of residual learning to solve the training instability of a deep, multi-level matching architecture is a clever and effective adaptation of a known technique to this new context.
2.  **Strong empirical results and rigorous analysis.** The proposed HR-BiLSTM model significantly outperforms strong baselines and prior work on the relation detection subtask on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP (Table 2). The end-to-end KBQA system also achieves state-of-the-art or comparable performance (Table 3). The strength of these results is bolstered by a comprehensive set of ablation studies that systematically validate the contributions of using both relation views, employing residual learning, and using a deep LSTM architecture. This analysis provides convincing evidence for the authors' claims.
3.  **Demonstrates high impact on the full KBQA pipeline.** A key strength is showing that improving a core module (relation detection) can have a cascading positive effect on the entire system. The entity re-ranking step, which leverages the relation detector, is shown to provide a significant performance boost (Table 3 ablation), highlighting the model's ability to resolve ambiguities early in the pipeline. This makes a strong case for the importance of focusing on core sub-tasks. The overall system is also refreshingly simple compared to prior work like STAGG, which relies on multiple models and complex features.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The KBQA system design is susceptible to error propagation.** The paper frames the simplicity of its pipeline as a strength, but this sequential design (entity linking -> re-ranking -> relation detection -> constraint detection) can propagate errors. Specifically, the constraint detection step operates only on the single top-ranked query from the previous stage (as noted in footnote 5). This is a greedy approach that contrasts with prior work (e.g., STAGG) that uses beam search to maintain multiple hypotheses, which is often more robust. While the final results are strong, this design choice limits the system's ability to recover from an early mistake in identifying the topic entity or core relation chain.
2.  **Lack of clarity on key implementation and hyperparameter details.** The paper omits several details that are crucial for reproducibility. For example, the final values for the weighting hyperparameters `α` (Eq. 1) and `β` (Eq. 2), the margin `γ` for the ranking loss, and the various candidate list sizes (K, K', l) are not specified. It is also unclear which of the two proposed methods for implementing residual connections (summing hidden states vs. summing max-pooled outputs) was used in the final model. Including these details, perhaps in an appendix, would significantly strengthen the paper.
3.  **The final ensemble result is underdeveloped.** The best results reported in Table 3 come from an ensemble of the top-3 relation detectors. This is presented in a single sentence without any detail on the ensembling method (e.g., averaging scores, voting) or analysis. While ensembling is a common practice, its inclusion here feels ad-hoc and slightly detracts from the paper's main contribution, which is the single HR-BiLSTM model. The paper would be stronger if it either focused solely on the performance of the single model or provided a more thorough motivation and analysis of the ensemble.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the KBQA system design: Have you experimented with maintaining a beam of N-best `(entity, relation)` candidates through the pipeline and applying constraint detection to all of them before a final re-ranking? It seems this might mitigate the error propagation issue and could potentially lead to further gains, making the comparison to systems that use beam search more direct.
2.  In Section 3.2, you propose two ways to implement the residual connection: summing hidden states at each position `i` before max-pooling, or summing the max-pooled representations from each layer. Could you clarify which variant was used in the final experiments reported in Table 2, and what was the rationale for choosing it over the other?
3.  For reproducibility, could you please provide the final values for the key hyperparameters used in the KBQA system, including `α`, `β`, and the candidate list sizes `K`, `K'`, and `l` for both datasets?
