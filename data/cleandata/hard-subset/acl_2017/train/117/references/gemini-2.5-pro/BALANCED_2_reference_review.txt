### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA), arguing that it is distinct from general relation extraction due to the large number of relations, the presence of unseen relations at test time (a zero-shot problem), and the need to predict relation chains. The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for this task. The model matches a question against a KB relation using hierarchical representations of both. Relations are represented at both the word-level (for generalization to unseen relations) and the relation-level (to capture global semantics). Questions are encoded with a deep, two-layer BiLSTM to capture representations at different levels of abstraction. Crucially, the model uses residual connections between the BiLSTM layers to facilitate effective training and hierarchical matching. The authors then integrate this improved relation detector into a KBQA pipeline where it is used in two stages: first, to re-rank entity candidates from an initial linker, and second, to identify the correct relation for the top-ranked entities. Experiments on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets show that the HR-BiLSTM model significantly outperforms previous methods on the relation detection subtask, and the resulting KBQA system achieves state-of-the-art or comparable performance on both benchmarks.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective neural architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection.** The core contribution is the model itself, which thoughtfully combines multi-granularity representations of relations with a deep, residually-connected question encoder. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate that this specific combination of ideas is responsible for the model's strong performance.
2.  **A demonstration of how a powerful relation detector can improve and simplify a KBQA system.** The paper presents a simple KBQA pipeline that leverages the HR-BiLSTM for both entity disambiguation (via re-ranking) and relation prediction. This approach achieves excellent results without resorting to more complex system components like joint inference or heavily engineered re-ranking features, highlighting the impact of a strong core component.
3.  **Thorough empirical evaluation and analysis on two distinct KBQA benchmarks.** The authors validate their model on both a single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) task. The experiments are well-designed, including strong baselines and a comprehensive set of ablation studies that clearly isolate the impact of each architectural choice.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and technically sound model.** The design of the HR-BiLSTM is well-justified. The motivation for using both word-level and relation-level representations to balance generalization and specificity is clear and intuitive for the KBQA setting. The use of a deep BiLSTM to capture hierarchical question features is sensible, and the application of residual connections to enable effective hierarchical matching is a clever adaptation of an idea from computer vision to this NLP task. This represents a solid and non-trivial architectural contribution.
2.  **Strong empirical results and rigorous ablation studies.** The paper reports state-of-the-art performance on the relation detection subtask for both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, with significant margins over strong baselines (Table 2). The end-to-end KBQA results are also state-of-the-art on SimpleQuestions and competitive on WebQSP. The credibility of these results is greatly enhanced by the thorough ablation studies, which systematically dismantle the model to show the individual contributions of using hierarchical inputs (words + relation names), residual learning, and the BiLSTM architecture itself. This rigorous evaluation provides strong support for the authors' claims.
3.  **An elegant and effective KBQA pipeline.** The proposed KBQA system is simple but powerful. The idea of using the relation detector first to re-rank entity candidates is a simple and effective way to address the entity linking bottleneck, using semantic clues from the question to disambiguate entities. This two-step process demonstrates a practical way to leverage a high-quality component to boost overall system performance. The fact that this relatively simple pipeline can outperform more complex systems like STAGG (on SimpleQuestions) is a testament to the quality of the relation detection model.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The novelty of the overall KBQA system is limited.** While the HR-BiLSTM model is novel, the broader KBQA pipeline (entity linking -> relation detection -> constraint detection) follows a standard, staged architecture. The constraint detection module in particular appears quite simplistic, relying on character-level n-gram overlap. The authors rightly note that they omit components like beam search and feature-based re-rankers used in prior state-of-the-art systems (e.g., STAGG). While their system performs very well without them, this makes the comparison slightly less direct and suggests that the gains are almost exclusively from the relation detection module, rather than a new system design.
2.  **Lack of clarity on key model implementation details.** The description of the HR-BiLSTM in Section 3.2 could be more precise. For example, it is unclear which of the two proposed methods for residual connections (adding hidden states at each position vs. adding the final max-pooled vectors) was used in the final experiments. Additionally, the mechanism for processing the concatenated word-level and relation-level inputs with shared BiLSTMs and initializing the relation-level LSTM from the word-level one ("initialize... with the final state representations") is described too briefly to be easily reproducible.
3.  **Analysis could be strengthened with qualitative examples.** The paper presents strong quantitative results but would benefit from more qualitative analysis. For instance, showing a few concrete examples where the hierarchical model succeeds while the single-level baselines fail would provide valuable intuition. It would be insightful to see an example of an unseen relation on SimpleQuestions being correctly handled by the word-level component, or an ambiguous question on WebQSP being resolved by the relation-level representation. This would better substantiate the claims about why the hierarchical approach is effective.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify which of the two residual connection methods described in Section 3.2 (element-wise addition of hidden states before pooling, or addition of the max-pooled vectors) was used to obtain the results in Table 2? Could you also elaborate on the precise mechanism for processing the combined word-level and relation-level inputs and initializing the relation-level LSTM from the word-level one?
2.  Your KBQA system achieves state-of-the-art results without complex inference mechanisms like beam search, which were central to prior work like STAGG. Your high top-1 accuracy on topic entity and core-chain detection (mentioned as 77.5% in a footnote) is impressive. Could you elaborate on your error analysis? What are the primary remaining sources of error in your system after the improved relation detection and entity re-ranking steps?
3.  The motivation for the hierarchical relation representation is to handle both seen and unseen relations effectively. Could you provide a specific example from the SimpleQuestions test set of an unseen relation that your HR-BiLSTM model correctly identifies, and which a model using only word-level or relation-level representations fails on? This would provide a powerful, concrete illustration of your model's main strength.
