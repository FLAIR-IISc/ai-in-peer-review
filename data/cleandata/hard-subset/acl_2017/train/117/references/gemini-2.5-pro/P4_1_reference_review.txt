**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), highlighting challenges unique to this domain, such as the large number of relations, the zero-shot problem for unseen relations, and the need to detect relation chains. The authors propose a new neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for this task. The model performs hierarchical matching by representing relations at two levels of granularity (word-level and relation-name-level) and matching them against a multi-level question representation learned by a deep, two-layer BiLSTM. To facilitate the training of this deep architecture and improve matching, the model incorporates residual connections between the BiLSTM layers. The paper then integrates this improved relation detector into a KBQA pipeline that uses it in two stages: first, to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates to improve entity disambiguation, and second, to identify the correct relation for the top-ranked entities. The proposed methods are evaluated on both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task using the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art or comparable performance.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is a novel neural architecture, HR-BiLSTM, for relation detection. The model's key technical innovation is the combination of a hierarchical representation for relations (word-level and relation-level) with a deep, multi-level question representation, where residual connections are used to enable effective matching between these hierarchical representations.
2.  A second significant contribution is the demonstration that a strong relation detector can substantially improve a simple KBQA pipeline. The paper introduces a two-step application of the detector for both entity re-ranking and relation prediction, achieving competitive end-to-end results without complex joint inference or feature-based re-ranking modules common in prior work.
3.  The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis, including extensive ablation studies, on two distinct KBQA benchmarks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP). This evaluation validates the effectiveness of the proposed model for both single-relation and multi-relation chain detection and isolates the performance gains attributable to the specific architectural components (hierarchical representation, residual learning).

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed methods achieve state-of-the-art results. On the isolated relation detection task, the HR-BiLSTM model surpasses previous methods and strong baselines by a significant margin of 2.0% on SimpleQuestions and 3.2% on WebQSP (Table 2). This translates to strong performance on the end-to-end KBQA task, where the system achieves a new state-of-the-art on SimpleQuestions and is highly competitive on WebQSP (Table 3). These results provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.
2.  **Well-Motivated Technical Approach:** The architectural choices are well-justified and directly address key challenges in KB relation detection. The use of a dual word-level and relation-level view is a sound strategy to balance generalization to unseen relations (via words) and capturing global semantics for seen relations (via relation names). The application of residual learning to facilitate training and matching in a deep hierarchical model is a logical and effective adaptation of a successful concept from computer vision.
3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The paper includes a comprehensive set of ablation experiments (Table 2) that clearly demonstrate the individual contributions of the core components of the HR-BiLSTM model. The results validate the benefits of using a hierarchical relation representation, the superiority of residual learning over simpler aggregation methods (weighted sum, attention), and the advantage of BiLSTMs over CNNs for this task, particularly on the more complex WebQSP dataset. This analysis significantly strengthens the paper's claims.
4.  **Effective and Simple KBQA Pipeline:** The paper presents a KBQA system that is less complex than many competing approaches, forgoing feature-rich re-rankers or joint decoding. The proposed entity re-ranking step is a simple yet effective method to leverage the relation detector to mitigate errors from the initial entity linking stage. The fact that this streamlined pipeline achieves state-of-the-art results underscores the high impact of improving the core relation detection module.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Unclear Technical Details for Reproducibility:** Section 3.2 describes the core "Hierarchical Residual Matching" mechanism and proposes two distinct methods for implementing the residual connection: summing the outputs of each BiLSTM layer at each time step, or summing the max-pooled representations of each layer's outputs. However, the paper does not specify which of these two methods was used in the experiments or whether their performance differs. This ambiguity hinders the exact reproducibility of the work.
2.  **Incremental Novelty of Architectural Components:** While the overall architecture is novel in its application to KBQA, its constituent parts—deep BiLSTMs, multi-view representations, and residual connections—are established concepts from prior work in NLP and other machine learning domains. The primary novelty lies in the specific combination and application of these ideas, rather than the invention of a fundamentally new architectural primitive.
3.  **Dependence on an External Entity Linker:** The proposed pipeline relies on an initial set of candidates from a pre-existing entity linker. While the entity re-ranking step is designed to improve upon this initial set, the system's overall performance ceiling is still constrained by the recall of this external component. The evaluation does not explore the system's sensitivity to the quality or size (K) of the initial candidate set, which would provide a more complete picture of its robustness.
4.  **Creation of a Non-Standard Evaluation Task:** For the WebQSP dataset, the authors created a new relation detection task to evaluate their model, as one did not previously exist. While this is necessary for their component-wise evaluation, it establishes a new, non-standard benchmark. Direct comparison with future work on this specific sub-task will depend on others adopting the authors' exact data generation protocol.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the two methods for implementing residual connections described in Section 3.2 (summing hidden states vs. summing max-pooled representations), could you please clarify which method was used to obtain the reported experimental results? If both were tested, was there a notable difference in performance?
2.  The entity re-ranking step in Section 4.1 relies on two key hyperparameters: `α` (balancing the linker and relation scores) and `l` (the number of top-scoring relations to consider). Could you provide details on the tuning process for these parameters and comment on the sensitivity of the final KBQA performance to their values?
