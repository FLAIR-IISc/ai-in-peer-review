**Summary of the paper:**
This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The authors argue that relation detection for KBQA is distinct from standard relation extraction due to the large number of relations and the prevalence of unseen relations at test time. To tackle this, they propose a novel model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM). This model matches questions to relations by representing relations at both a word-level (sequence of words in the relation name) and a relation-level (the entire relation name as a token). The question is encoded using a two-layer BiLSTM with residual connections, intended to capture hierarchical information for matching against the multi-granularity relation representations. The authors then integrate this relation detector into a KBQA pipeline that first uses it to re-rank entity candidates and then to detect the final relation for the highest-ranked entities. The proposed system is evaluated on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets, where it achieves state-of-the-art or comparable results on both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task.

**Main Contributions:**
1.  An empirical demonstration that a KBQA system built around a strong neural relation detector can achieve state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks. The two-step application of the detector for entity re-ranking and final relation prediction is a key component of this system.
2.  A neural architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection that combines two well-established techniques: multi-granularity representations (representing relations as both words and full names) and residual connections applied to a stacked BiLSTM encoder.
3.  An ablation study that provides evidence for the utility of the different components of the proposed model and KBQA pipeline.

**Strengths:**
1.  The paper reports strong empirical results. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on SimpleQuestions and a competitive result on WebQSP demonstrates the effectiveness of the overall proposed system. The improvements are consistent, showing gains on the isolated relation detection task which translate to the final KBQA accuracy.
2.  The motivation is clear and well-grounded. The paper does a good job of differentiating KB-specific relation detection from general relation extraction and identifying the key challenges (large/open vocabulary, zero-shot learning) that motivate its architectural choices.
3.  The proposed KBQA pipeline, particularly the entity re-ranking step, is a methodologically sound approach to leveraging a strong relation detector to mitigate errors from upstream entity linkers. The ablation study in Table 3 confirms the significant impact of this component.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The novelty of the core technical contribution, the HR-BiLSTM model, is limited. The architecture is primarily a combination of existing ideas. Using word-level representations for unseen relations is a known strategy. Stacked LSTMs are standard. The main novelty appears to be the application of residual connections, an idea borrowed directly from computer vision. The justification for this choice—that the second layer learns "residues from the first layer of matching"—is vague and lacks sufficient theoretical or empirical support beyond improved metrics. It is unclear if this is a genuine modeling insight or simply a useful optimization technique for training a deeper network.
2.  The validation of the hierarchical and residual components against alternatives could be more rigorous. The comparison for hierarchical matching is made against a simple weighted sum of layer outputs and a decomposable attention model designed for NLI. These may not be the strongest possible baselines for this sequence matching task. A more direct comparison to other advanced sequence-matching architectures would strengthen the authors' claims. The improvement from residual learning, while present, is modest on SimpleQuestions, raising questions about the complexity-performance trade-off.
3.  The analysis of the model's behavior is superficial. The paper would be significantly improved with a qualitative analysis that demonstrates *why* the proposed components work. For example, providing specific cases where the word-level view correctly identifies a zero-shot relation that the relation-level view misses, or an instance where the deeper, residual-trained BiLSTM layer captures a complex phrasing that a single-layer model fails on. The claim that WebQSP benefits more from the deeper architecture because it requires matching "larger scope of context" is an assertion that requires more concrete evidence.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  The performance gain from your full HR-BiLSTM over a single-layer BiLSTM is quite small on the SimpleQuestions relation detection task (93.3% vs. 92.8%). Given the increased complexity of the hierarchical and residual architecture, how do you justify its necessity for this type of single-relation QA task? Does this suggest that much of the performance gain in the end-to-end system comes from the pipeline structure (i.e., entity re-ranking) rather than the novel aspects of the relation detector itself?
2.  The entity re-ranking step uses a relation detection model on the raw question, while the main relation detection step uses a model on a question where the entity has been replaced by a special token `<e>`. Are these two separate models, or is it a single model trained to handle both input formats? If it is a single model, could you please clarify the training procedure?
3.  The intuition for using residual connections is that the second layer models the "residue" of the first layer's matching. Can you provide a more concrete explanation of this? Have you performed any analysis of the hidden states from the two layers (e.g., via probing or visualization) to verify that they are indeed learning complementary, hierarchical representations as hypothesized?
