**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), a challenging problem due to the large number of relations, the presence of unseen relations at test time (zero-shot learning), and the need to detect chains of relations. The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for this task. The core idea is to perform a hierarchical match between the question and the candidate relation. This is achieved by (1) representing relations at two levels of granularity: as a sequence of words and as a single token representing the entire relation name, and (2) representing the question with a two-layer BiLSTM, where each layer captures a different level of abstraction. A residual connection is used to combine the question representations from both layers before matching against the relation representation.

The authors embed this improved relation detector into a simple yet effective KBQA pipeline. A key feature of this pipeline is a two-step application of the relation detector: first, it is used to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates to improve entity disambiguation; second, it is used to detect the final relation(s) for the top-ranked entities. The proposed system is evaluated on both the single-relation SimpleQuestions dataset and the multi-relation WebQSP dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results on both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A novel and effective neural architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for KB relation detection.** The primary contribution is the model itself, which thoughtfully combines hierarchical representations for both questions and relations. The motivation to match word-level relation tokens with local question context and relation-level tokens with more global context is compelling. The adaptation of residual connections to facilitate this multi-level matching is a clever solution to a potential training difficulty.
2.  **A simple KBQA system that effectively leverages the improved relation detector to achieve state-of-the-art performance.** The paper demonstrates how a powerful component can significantly boost a larger system. The proposed entity re-ranking step, which uses relation detection to disambiguate entities, is a valuable and practical contribution that addresses a known bottleneck in KBQA pipelines.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation with strong results and insightful ablations.** The work is validated on two standard benchmarks, demonstrating its versatility for both single- and multi-relation questions. The ablation studies clearly isolate the benefits of the key components of the HR-BiLSTM model (hierarchical inputs, residual learning) and the KBQA system (entity re-ranking).

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant model design.** The core idea of matching different levels of granularity is intuitive and well-argued in Section 3.1. Representing relations as both word sequences (for generalization to unseen relations) and whole tokens (for capturing global semantics) directly addresses the key challenges of KB relation detection. Using a deep BiLSTM to create corresponding hierarchical question representations is a natural fit. The use of residual connections to combine these layers is a clever technique that, as shown in the ablations, is crucial for making the hierarchical matching effective.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on multiple challenging benchmarks.** The method achieves impressive results, outperforming strong previous work on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. The gains are consistent across the isolated relation detection task (Table 2) and the end-to-end KBQA task (Table 3). This demonstrates the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3.  **Thorough and convincing ablation studies.** The authors perform a rigorous set of experiments to validate their design choices. Table 2 clearly shows that both hierarchical relation representations ("w/o rel name", "w/o rel words") and the residual learning mechanism ("w/o residual learning") contribute significantly to the final performance. This level of analysis strengthens the paper's claims and provides valuable insights for future work.
4.  **Clear presentation and reproducibility.** The paper is well-written, and the proposed method is described clearly. Figure 2 provides a helpful visualization of the model architecture. The authors use standard datasets and evaluation protocols, and plan to release the newly created relation detection version of WebQSP, which will be a valuable resource for the community.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational cost of the entity re-ranking step is unclear.** The proposed KBQA pipeline introduces an entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) where the relation detector scores all relations connected to the initial top-K entity candidates. For entities with a high degree in the knowledge graph and a moderately large K, this could be computationally prohibitive. The paper does not provide any analysis of the runtime or the number of relations scored per question in this step, making it difficult to assess the practical scalability of the approach.
2.  **The constraint detection module is simplistic and under-analyzed.** The constraint detection method, based on character overlap matching, feels disconnected from the sophisticated neural model at the core of the system. While effective, it appears brittle and may not generalize well. Given that this step is reported as "crucial" for performance on WebQSP, its simplicity is surprising. The paper would be stronger if it either justified this design choice more thoroughly or explored using the HR-BiLSTM model for this subtask as well.
3.  **Novelty is primarily in the combination of existing techniques.** The core components of the model—deep BiLSTMs, residual connections, and representing relations as word sequences—are not new in themselves. The novelty lies in their specific combination and application to KB relation detection. While this is a valid and valuable contribution, the paper could be more precise in positioning its work relative to prior uses of these techniques in NLP.
4.  **Potential sensitivity to hyperparameters in the KBQA pipeline.** The full system introduces several hyperparameters for combining scores (α, β) and selecting candidates (K, K', l). While the paper states these were tuned on development sets, there is no discussion of their sensitivity. The performance of a pipeline that relies on linearly interpolating scores from different models can often be sensitive to these weighting parameters, and some analysis would improve the reader's confidence in the system's robustness.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the computational overhead of the entity re-ranking step? Specifically, for the WebQSP experiments, what were the typical values for K, K', and the total number of candidate relations that needed to be scored per question in this initial step? How did its runtime compare to the main relation detection step that followed?
2.  The constraint detection module is based on a simple text-matching heuristic. Did you experiment with applying a neural model, perhaps a variant of your HR-BiLSTM, to this task? If so, how did it perform? If not, could you provide more intuition for why the simpler character-overlap method was sufficient and effective?
3.  In Section 3.2, you mention two ways of implementing the residual connections (on hidden states vs. on max-pooled outputs). Could you share the development set results for both variants and offer any insights into why one performed better than the other for this task?
