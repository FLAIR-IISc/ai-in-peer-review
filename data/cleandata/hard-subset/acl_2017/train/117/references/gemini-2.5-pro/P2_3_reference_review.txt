### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA), a task made challenging by the large number of relations and the need to handle unseen relations (zero-shot learning). The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for this task. The core idea is to perform a hierarchical match between the question and the candidate relation. To do this, they represent relations at two granularities (word-level for generalization and relation-name-level for global context) and match them against a multi-layered BiLSTM representation of the question. Residual connections are used to facilitate the training of this deep matching architecture. The authors then integrate this improved relation detector into a simple yet effective KBQA pipeline that uses the model in two steps: first to re-rank entity candidates from an initial linker, and second to detect the final relation for the top-ranked entity.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **The HR-BiLSTM Model:** A novel and well-motivated architecture for relation detection that effectively combines hierarchical representations of the question with multi-granularity representations of the relations. The application of residual learning to ease the training of this matching network is a clever and impactful contribution.
2.  **A Simple and Effective KBQA Pipeline:** The paper demonstrates how a powerful relation detector can be used to improve the end-to-end KBQA task, not just through better relation prediction but also by improving the upstream entity linking via a re-ranking step. This shows the practical utility of the core model.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The work is supported by extensive experiments on two standard benchmarks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), achieving state-of-the-art results on both the isolated relation detection task and the full KBQA task. The thorough ablation study convincingly validates the authors' architectural choices.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model:** The motivation for the HR-BiLSTM is very clear. The paper correctly identifies the trade-off between word-level relation representations (good for unseen relations, but potentially ambiguous) and relation-level representations (captures global semantics, but suffers from data sparsity). The proposed solution to match both granularities against different levels of abstraction in the question representation is intuitive and powerful. The use of residual connections to make this hierarchical matching trainable is a key insight that appears to be effective, especially on the more complex WebQSP dataset.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. The authors evaluate their model on two distinct datasets: SimpleQuestions, which tests the model's ability to handle a large number of single relations and the zero-shot problem, and WebQSP, which involves more complex relation chains. The HR-BiLSTM model shows significant improvements over strong baselines on both. The ablation study in Table 2 is excellent; it systematically demonstrates the contribution of each component of the model (hierarchical representation, residual learning, and the use of both relation granularities), providing strong evidence for the authors' claims.

3.  **Demonstrated Impact on the Full KBQA Task:** The paper successfully bridges the gap between improving a sub-task (relation detection) and achieving better performance on the end-task (KBQA). The proposed two-step usage of the relation detector, particularly for entity re-ranking, is a clever engineering choice that addresses a known bottleneck in KBQA systems: entity linking ambiguity. This re-ranking step, as shown in the ablation study in Table 3, provides a significant performance boost, underscoring the real-world value of the improved relation detector.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, the proposed method is explained with sufficient detail (aided by Figure 2), and the experimental setup is clear. The simplicity of the final KBQA system, which avoids complex feature engineering or joint inference, makes the work more likely to be reproducible and built upon by others.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Novelty of Components:** While the *combination* of ideas in the HR-BiLSTM model is novel and effective for this task, the individual components are not new. Deep BiLSTMs, hierarchical representations, and residual connections have been explored extensively in other areas of NLP and computer vision. The primary novelty lies in the specific application and integration of these techniques for KB relation detection. This is a minor weakness, as the successful application to a new problem is a valid contribution, but the paper could be slightly more explicit about positioning its novelty in this context.

2.  **Oversimplified KBQA System for SOTA Comparison:** The authors rightly praise the simplicity of their KBQA system. However, this simplicity can make direct comparisons to more complex systems like STAGG (Yih et al., 2015) slightly uneven. For instance, the paper notes that their system uses a greedy top-1 query generation approach, whereas STAGG employs beam search and a feature-based re-ranker. While the proposed system's comparable performance is impressive given its simplicity, it's unclear if the gains come purely from the relation detector or if the different system-level search strategies also play a role. It is possible that integrating the HR-BiLSTM into a more sophisticated search framework like STAGG's could yield even greater improvements.

3.  **Lack of Detail on Constraint Detection:** Section 4.4, which describes the crucial constraint detection step for WebQSP, is very brief and defers key details to an appendix. While this is often necessary for space, the description of the matching score as based on the "maximum overlapping sequence of characters" is somewhat vague. Given that this step is responsible for a large performance jump on WebQSP (from 58.0% to 70.3% when combined with entity re-ranking), providing more insight into this mechanism in the main body would strengthen the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The comparison with STAGG on WebQSP shows comparable, but not superior, performance. Given that your KBQA system uses a greedy search while STAGG uses beam search and re-ranking, do you believe the performance gap is primarily due to the different search strategies? In other words, could your superior relation detector, when integrated into the more complex STAGG framework, be expected to significantly outperform the original STAGG system?

2.  In Section 3.2, you mention an interesting initialization detail: "We initialize the relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence, as a back-off for unseen relations." Could you elaborate on the intuition here and perhaps quantify its impact? How much does this specific initialization contribute to performance on SimpleQuestions, where unseen relations are a significant issue?
