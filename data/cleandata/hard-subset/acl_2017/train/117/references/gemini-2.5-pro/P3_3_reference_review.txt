### **Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), a challenging task due to the large number of relations, the presence of unseen relations at test time (zero-shot), and the need to detect relation chains. The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for this task. The model's key idea is to perform hierarchical matching: it represents relations at two granularities (word-level and full relation name) and matches them against question representations from two layers of a deep BiLSTM, which are assumed to capture different levels of abstraction. To facilitate this matching and ease training, the model incorporates residual connections between the BiLSTM layers. The authors then integrate this improved relation detector into a simple KBQA pipeline that notably uses the detector in two stages: first, to re-rank entity candidates from an initial linker, and second, to identify the correct relation for the top-ranked entities. The proposed system achieves state-of-the-art or comparable results on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP benchmarks for both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A novel neural architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection.** The primary contribution is a well-motivated model that performs hierarchical matching between multi-granularity relation representations (word-level for generalization, name-level for specificity) and hierarchical question representations (from a deep BiLSTM). The application of residual connections to enable this matching is a clever and effective architectural choice.
2.  **A simple yet powerful KBQA pipeline demonstrating the impact of improved relation detection.** The paper presents a system that forgoes complex joint inference or feature-based re-rankers, instead relying on the strength of its relation detector. The proposed entity re-ranking step, which uses relation scores to disambiguate entities, is a practical and effective method that highlights how improvements in a core sub-component can significantly benefit the overall system.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thorough Evaluation.** The paper demonstrates significant performance gains on two standard datasets, SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. The evaluation is comprehensive, assessing performance on both the isolated relation detection task and the full KBQA pipeline. Achieving state-of-the-art results with a conceptually simpler KBQA system underscores the effectiveness of the core relation detection model.
2.  **Well-motivated and Intuitive Model Architecture.** The central idea of matching different levels of abstraction in the question and relation is well-justified. Representing relations at both word and name levels addresses the trade-off between generalization to unseen relations and capturing the specific semantics of full relation names. Using a deep BiLSTM to generate hierarchical question representations is a natural fit for this goal.
3.  **Excellent Ablation Studies.** The authors provide a detailed ablation analysis in Table 2, which substantially strengthens their claims. These experiments clearly isolate and quantify the benefits of the key components of their model: the hierarchical relation representation (using both words and names), the use of residual learning over simpler aggregation or attention mechanisms, and the choice of LSTM over CNN for more complex datasets like WebQSP.
4.  **Practical and Impactful System Design.** The entity re-ranking step is a simple but elegant contribution. It directly tackles the critical bottleneck of entity linking ambiguity in many KBQA systems. By showing that a strong relation detector can improve an upstream component, the authors provide a valuable insight for practitioners building modular KBQA systems.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Ambiguity in Key Architectural Details.** Section 3.2 proposes two different methods for implementing the residual connections (one adding hidden states before max-pooling, the other adding the max-pooled vectors). However, the paper never specifies which of these two methods was used in the final experiments, nor does it comment on their relative performance. This is a crucial missing detail about the core technical contribution.
2.  **Reliance on Ensembling for State-of-the-Art Claim on WebQSP.** The headline SOTA result on WebQSP (61.3%) is achieved with an ensemble of models. The reported performance of the single, novel HR-BiLSTM model (58.0%) is actually lower than the existing STAGG baseline (59.4%). While ensembling is common practice, the paper should be more transparent about this, as the primary contribution is the single model architecture. The strong single-model performance on SimpleQuestions is commendable and should be highlighted similarly.
3.  **Simplicity of KBQA System Complicates Comparison.** The authors rightly emphasize the simplicity of their KBQA pipeline as a strength. However, this also makes direct comparisons to more complex, jointly trained, or heavily feature-engineered systems (like STAGG) somewhat difficult. While the results are impressive, it is hard to fully attribute the performance gains *solely* to the relation detection module, as other parts of their simple pipeline (e.g., the constraint detector) might also be interacting in favorable ways.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the two methods for hierarchical residual matching proposed in Section 3.2: which method was used to produce the results in your experiments? Did you observe a significant performance difference between them on the development set?
2.  Your paper makes a strong case for the importance of the entity re-ranking step. Could you provide quantitative results on the impact of this step, for instance, by reporting the top-K accuracy of the entity linker before and after re-ranking on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP test sets?
3.  Could you clarify if the state-of-the-art claim on WebQSP is dependent on using an ensemble? Given the single HR-BiLSTM model's performance is below the STAGG baseline, it would be helpful to explicitly frame the single-model vs. ensemble performance in your conclusion.
