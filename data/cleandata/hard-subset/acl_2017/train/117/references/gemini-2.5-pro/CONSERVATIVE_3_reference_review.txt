### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The authors argue that existing methods struggle with the large number of relations and the zero-shot nature of the task. They propose a new neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for relation detection. The core idea is to perform hierarchical matching between the question and the candidate relation. This is achieved by representing relations at both the word-level (for generalization) and the relation-level (for global context) and matching them against a two-layer BiLSTM representation of the question. Residual connections are introduced between the BiLSTM layers, purportedly to ease training and create more complementary representations. The authors embed this improved relation detector into a simple KBQA pipeline that uses it in two stages: first, to re-rank initial entity candidates, and second, to detect the primary relation for the top-ranked entities. The system is evaluated on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets, where it achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results.

### Contributions

1.  **A strong empirical result for relation detection in KBQA:** The main contribution is the proposed HR-BiLSTM model, which achieves state-of-the-art performance on relation detection sub-tasks for both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the utility of the model's different components.
2.  **A simple and effective KBQA pipeline:** The paper presents a straightforward KBQA system that leverages the proposed relation detector to improve not only relation detection but also upstream entity linking through a re-ranking step. This system, despite its simplicity and lack of complex feature engineering, performs very well.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper reports state-of-the-art results on the SimpleQuestions KBQA task and results comparable to the state-of-the-art on WebQSP. The improvements on the relation detection subtask are particularly clear (Table 2), with gains of 2-3% over strong baselines, which is a significant margin on these benchmarks.
2.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of ablation experiments for their proposed HR-BiLSTM model. Table 2 clearly breaks down the contribution of using hierarchical relation representations (word + relation name), the deep architecture, and the residual connections. This analysis provides convincing evidence that each component contributes to the final performance, lending credibility to the overall model design. A similar ablation is provided for the full KBQA system in Table 3, highlighting the importance of the entity re-ranking step.
3.  **Well-Motivated Problem Formulation:** The paper provides a clear motivation for the problem, correctly identifying the challenges of KBQA relation detection compared to general relation extraction, such as the large number of relations and the zero-shot problem. The justification for using dual-granularity (word-level and relation-level) representations is intuitive and well-explained with examples.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core technical contribution, the HR-BiLSTM model, is an incremental combination of existing, well-established techniques. The architecture is composed of standard BiLSTMs. The idea of using multi-level representations is not new. The key novelty claim rests on the use of residual connections, an idea borrowed directly from computer vision (He et al., 2016). While effective, its application here feels more like a successful engineering choice than a fundamental advance in sequence matching. The paper lacks a deep analysis of why this specific mechanism is superior to other potential fusion strategies for a two-layer network.
2.  **Unconvincing Justification for Residual Learning:** The authors motivate residual connections by claiming they make training easier and that the second layer learns "residues from the first layer of matching." This is a rather hand-wavy analogy to the original motivation for ResNets, which were designed to combat gradient vanishing in much deeper networks (e.g., >100 layers). For a two-layer BiLSTM, it is not clear that this is the primary benefit. The improvement could stem from other factors, such as implicit regularization or simply providing a direct path for lower-level features to the final representation. The performance gain from residual connections over a simple weighted sum is also inconsistent: it is large on WebQSP (80.65% to 82.53%) but much smaller on SimpleQuestions (92.5% to 93.3%), which questions its general applicability and importance.
3.  **Incremental System Design:** The overall KBQA system follows a standard pipeline (entity link -> relation detect -> constrain). The entity re-ranking step is a sensible addition but is a straightforward application of the relation detector. The authors themselves note they "make minimal efforts beyond the training of the relation detection model." While this highlights the strength of their detector, it also underscores the limited novelty of the system as a whole. Furthermore, the best-reported results rely on an ensemble of models, a common practice but one that slightly dilutes the claimed contribution of the single HR-BiLSTM model.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The justification for using residual connections is primarily intuitive. Could you provide a more rigorous analysis of what the model is learning? For instance, what exactly are the "residues" in this sequence matching context? Have you explored other methods for combining the two layers of BiLSTM representations, such as concatenation followed by a dense layer, and how do they compare?
2.  On the SimpleQuestions relation detection task, the gap between a single-layer BiLSTM using both relation views (92.8%) and your full HR-BiLSTM model (93.3%) is quite small. This suggests the added complexity of the hierarchical residual architecture might not be fully justified for this dataset. Could you comment on this apparent trade-off between model complexity and performance gain?
3.  The constraint detection module is presented as a simple text-matching method, yet it provides a substantial boost on WebQSP (from 58.0% to 62.1% F1). Could you provide a more detailed description of this component and perhaps an error analysis? Understanding its strengths and weaknesses is crucial, as its performance significantly impacts the final KBQA results.
