### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), focusing on challenges like the large number of relations and the need to handle unseen relations (zero-shot learning). The primary contribution is a novel neural architecture, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for matching questions to KB relations. The model's key ideas are: (1) representing relations at two granularities (word-level and full relation-name-level) to handle both lexical matching and global semantics; (2) using a deep BiLSTM to generate hierarchical representations of the question; and (3) employing a residual learning mechanism to effectively combine and match these hierarchical representations.

As a second contribution, the authors embed this improved relation detector into a KBQA pipeline. This pipeline introduces a novel entity re-ranking step that uses relation detection scores to disambiguate and refine the output of an initial entity linker. The authors demonstrate that this relatively simple pipeline, driven by their powerful relation detector, achieves state-of-the-art or comparable performance on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) KBQA benchmarks.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The HR-BiLSTM model is a strong contribution. The motivation for using hierarchical representations for relations is very clear: word-level tokens provide robustness against unseen relations and capture lexical cues, while relation-level tokens capture more abstract semantic meaning. Combining this with a deep question encoder and a residual connection mechanism for matching is a clever and effective way to leverage information at different levels of abstraction. This design directly addresses key challenges in KB relation detection.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Generalizability:** The paper reports state-of-the-art results on the SimpleQuestions dataset and results comparable to the state-of-the-art on WebQSP. Achieving strong performance on both a single-relation and a more complex multi-relation dataset demonstrates that the proposed methods are effective and not over-tuned to a single task. The improvements over strong baselines are significant (2-3% absolute on the relation detection task).

3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of ablation experiments in Table 2 that convincingly validate their design choices. These experiments clearly demonstrate the benefit of using both word-level and relation-level views, the superiority of the proposed residual learning mechanism over simpler fusion methods or attention, and the advantage of BiLSTMs over CNNs for this task. This rigorous analysis greatly strengthens the paper's claims about why their model works.

4.  **Insightful Integration into a KBQA System:** The proposed KBQA pipeline effectively showcases the downstream impact of an improved relation detector. The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is an excellent idea, as it uses the semantic context provided by the relation detector to mitigate errors from the upstream entity linking module, a known bottleneck in many KBQA systems. The fact that this simple addition leads to significant performance gains underscores the practical value of the core contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Superficial Justification of "Residual Learning":** The paper's use of the term "residual learning" is potentially misleading and lacks technical depth. The implementation (`hq = h(1)max + h(2)max`) is a simple summation of representations from two layers, not a standard residual connection where a layer learns a function `F(x)` and the output is `F(x) + x`. While the motivation of making layers comparable and easing training is plausible, the paper does not provide analysis to show that the second layer is truly learning a "residue." The connection to the original ResNet work by He et al. (2016) feels tenuous, and the mechanism could be more accurately described as a form of deep feature fusion.

2.  **Lack of Detail on Crucial Components:** The constraint detection module (Section 4.4) is described very briefly, with key details deferred to an appendix. However, the ablation study in Table 3 shows this component is "crucial," providing a 4.5% absolute improvement on WebQSP. For a component with such a large impact on the final results, the lack of detail in the main paper on the matching function and thresholding hinders reproducibility and a full understanding of the system's capabilities.

3.  **The KBQA System is Not as Simple as Claimed:** The authors frame their KBQA system as simple, with "minimal efforts beyond the training of the relation detection model." However, the full pipeline involves an initial entity linker, an entity re-ranking step, a second relation detection step, and a final constraint detection step. This process introduces several new hyperparameters to tune (e.g., `K`, `K'`, `l`, `alpha`, `beta`, `theta`). Furthermore, the best results reported in Table 3 are achieved using an ensemble of three models, which adds significant complexity. While the system avoids heavy feature engineering, calling it simple is an overstatement that downplays the engineering effort involved.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify the connection between your residual learning formulation (`h_q = h_max^(1) + h_max^(2)`) and the canonical ResNet formulation (`F(x) + x`)? It seems more akin to an unweighted deep feature ensemble. Is there a more precise justification for terming this "residual learning," perhaps by analyzing what the second BiLSTM layer learns compared to the first?

2.  Given that constraint detection provides a significant boost on WebQSP, could you provide more detail on the character-overlap matching function used for entity linking within the sub-graph? How was the threshold `theta` determined, and how robust is this simple matching approach to different types of constraints (e.g., numerical values vs. named entities)?

3.  The entity re-ranking step introduces a hyperparameter `alpha` to balance the original linker score with the relation detection score. How sensitive is the final KBQA performance to the setting of this parameter? A sensitivity analysis would help understand the robustness of this component.
