### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents an improved neural model for relation detection in the context of Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The core of the contribution is a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model. This model addresses the challenge of matching natural language questions to KB relations by creating hierarchical representations for both. For relations, it uses both the full relation name (relation-level) and its constituent words (word-level). For questions, it uses a two-layer BiLSTM, and combines the outputs of both layers using a residual connection to capture information at different levels of abstraction. The paper then integrates this improved relation detector into a simple KBQA pipeline that uses it for two steps: first, to re-rank an initial set of candidate entities, and second, to detect the final relation for the top-ranked entities. The authors evaluate their approach on both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task for the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets, claiming state-of-the-art or comparable results.

### Contributions
1.  **A Hierarchical Relation Detection Model:** The primary contribution is the HR-BiLSTM model architecture. It is well-motivated by the nature of KB relations, which can be matched at both a coarse (full name) and fine-grained (individual words) level. The application of residual connections to combine hierarchical question representations for matching is a notable technical aspect.
2.  **A Simple and Effective KBQA System:** The paper demonstrates that a strong relation detection component can be the core of a high-performing KBQA system, even without complex feature-based re-rankers or joint inference mechanisms. The proposed two-step application of the relation detector (for entity re-ranking and relation selection) is an effective and straightforward pipeline.
3.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The work is supported by a thorough set of experiments and ablation studies on two distinct KBQA datasets. These experiments convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model over several strong baselines and dissect the contributions of its core components (hierarchical representation, residual learning, etc.).

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model and system achieve excellent results. The HR-BiLSTM model demonstrates a significant 2-3% absolute improvement over strong baselines on the relation detection subtask for both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. The end-to-end KBQA system subsequently achieves state-of-the-art results on SimpleQuestions and is competitive with the state-of-the-art on WebQSP, which is impressive for a system that is described as simple and without a complex final re-ranking stage.
2.  **Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The core idea of matching a question to both word-level and relation-level representations is intuitive and directly addresses a key challenge in KB relation detection, namely the trade-off between generalization to unseen relations (via words) and capturing global semantics (via full relation names). The use of residual connections to facilitate the training of a deep question encoder for this hierarchical matching is a clever application of a well-established technique.
3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The authors provide a detailed ablation analysis in Table 2. This analysis is crucial for understanding the model's behavior and validating the authors' architectural choices. It clearly shows that all major components—using both relation views, employing residual learning, and the choice of BiLSTMs over CNNs (for WebQSP)—contribute positively to the final performance. This level of analysis adds significant weight to the paper's claims.
4.  **Effective Use of Relation Detection for Entity Disambiguation:** The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is a simple but powerful idea. It leverages the relation detector to address the ambiguity in an upstream entity linking component, demonstrating a synergistic relationship between the two key KBQA subtasks. The ablation results in Table 3 confirm that this step is critical to the system's overall success.

### Weaknesses
Despite the strong results, the paper is marred by a significant number of minor—and some major—flaws in its technical description, presentation, and formatting. The accumulation of these issues raises serious concerns about the paper's polish and reliability.

1.  **Incorrect Technical Formulation:** The ranking loss function in Section 3.2 is described as `lrel = max{0, srel(r+;q) + srel(r-;q)}`. This formulation is incorrect for a max-margin loss. A standard ranking loss would be `max{0, margin - srel(r+;q) + srel(r-;q)}`. The presented formula would be minimized by simply driving all scores to be negative, not by creating a margin. This is a critical error in the description of the training objective.
2.  **Inconsistent Formulas and Descriptions:** In Section 4.1, the entity re-ranking score formula uses `max_{r∈Rlq\Re} srel(r; q)`. The notation `\Re` suggests set difference. However, the goal is to find a high-scoring relation that is *associated* with entity `e`. One would expect an intersection (`∩`), not a set difference. This appears to be a logical contradiction between the goal of the step and the formula provided.
3.  **Formatting and Typos:** The paper suffers from numerous formatting and typographical errors.
    *   The References section is completely broken, containing what appears to be a raw, unformatted dump from a bibliography management tool following the properly formatted list. This is a major formatting failure.
    *   There are several typos and grammatical errors, e.g., "golded test relations" (Introduction), "questions representations" (Section 3.2), "Many of the above relation detection research could" (Section 2).
    *   The caption for Table 1 is slightly vague ("give the position information").
    *   Figure 1 is cluttered and the text is too small, making it difficult to parse.
4.  **Missing Methodological Details:** Key details of the methodology are either missing or glossed over.
    *   Section 4.4 on Constraint Detection is extremely brief. It describes a text matching method based on "maximum overlapping sequence of characters," deferring details to an Appendix that is not provided. This makes a crucial part of the system (especially for WebQSP) unreproducible and difficult to evaluate. The claim that this simple string matching constitutes "entity-linking on a KB sub-graph" is an overstatement.
    *   The paper does not report the final hyperparameter values used (e.g., `α`, `β`, `l`, `γ`, `θ`). The sensitivity of the system to these parameters is not discussed, making it harder to assess the robustness of the approach.
5.  **Insufficient Justification for Certain Claims:** The paper claims that residual learning "ensures the vector spaces of two layers are comparable and makes the second-layer training easier." While this is the high-level intuition, the paper provides no empirical evidence (e.g., visualizations, training curves, analysis of vector spaces) to support this specific claim in their context, where the mechanism is slightly different from the original ResNet.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide the correct formulation for the ranking loss function used to train your model? The one presented in Section 3.2 appears to be incorrect.
2.  In the entity re-ranking formula in Section 4.1, the term `max_{r∈Rlq\Re} srel(r; q)` uses a set difference operator. Is this correct, or should it be an intersection? Please clarify the precise logic for scoring an entity based on its associated relations.
3.  Could you provide the full details of your constraint detection method as described in Section 4.4, which were deferred to a non-existent appendix? This component is critical for your results on WebQSP.
4.  What were the final values chosen for the key hyperparameters (`α` and `β` in Section 4.3, `l` in 4.1, `γ` in the loss function, and `θ` in 4.4)? How sensitive is the system's performance to these choices?
