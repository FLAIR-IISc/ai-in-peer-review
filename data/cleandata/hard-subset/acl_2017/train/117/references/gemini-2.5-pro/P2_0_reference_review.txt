### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), highlighting key challenges such as the large number of relations and the need for zero-shot generalization. The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for this task. The core idea is to perform hierarchical matching: it represents relations at two levels of granularity (as a sequence of words and as a single token representing the full relation name) and matches them against a multi-level question representation generated by a deep, two-layer BiLSTM. Crucially, it employs residual connections between the BiLSTM layers to facilitate training and improve matching. The authors then integrate this improved relation detector into a simple yet effective KBQA pipeline. A key feature of this pipeline is a two-step application of the relation detector: first, to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates based on the raw question, and second, to identify the final relation for the top-ranked entities. The system achieves state-of-the-art or comparable results on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **The HR-BiLSTM model architecture:** A well-motivated model for relation detection that combines hierarchical representations of relations with a deep, residual-connected question encoder. This architecture is specifically designed to handle the trade-off between the generalization power of word-level matching and the context-capturing ability of relation-level matching.
2.  **A simple and effective KBQA pipeline leveraging the relation detector for entity disambiguation:** The proposed two-step process, where the relation detector is first used to re-rank entity candidates, is an elegant solution to the common problem of entity linking ambiguity. It shows how a strong sub-component can improve the overall system in a synergistic way.
3.  **Strong empirical results and a thorough ablation study:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of its approach by achieving state-of-the-art results on two standard benchmarks. The comprehensive ablation studies convincingly validate the contributions of each component of the proposed model (hierarchical inputs, residual connections) and the KBQA pipeline (entity re-ranking).

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated model design:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating its architectural choices. The core problem of KB relation detection—needing to generalize to unseen relations while also capturing the semantics of full relation names—is clearly stated. The proposed solution of using both word-level and relation-level representations is a direct and intuitive response to this challenge. Furthermore, the application of residual learning to ease the training of the deep matching architecture is a clever adaptation from computer vision that is shown to be effective, particularly on the more complex WebQSP dataset.
2.  **Impressive empirical results and rigorous evaluation:** The paper reports state-of-the-art results on SimpleQuestions and strong, comparable-to-SOTA results on WebQSP. The evaluation is robust, covering both the isolated relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task. The ablation studies in Table 2 are particularly strong, as they systematically dissect the HR-BiLSTM model to show the impact of its key components: using both relation views is critical, and residual learning provides a significant boost over naive hierarchical matching or attention-based alternatives.
3.  **Elegant and practical KBQA system design:** The proposed KBQA pipeline is simple but highly effective. The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is a standout feature. By using the relation detector on the raw question to score relations connected to all initial entity candidates, the system effectively uses relational context to disambiguate entities (e.g., Mike Kelley the writer vs. the baseball player). This demonstrates a deep understanding of the practical bottlenecks in KBQA systems and offers a clean solution that outperforms more complex systems without requiring extensive feature engineering or joint inference.
4.  **Clarity and presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed model is explained with a helpful diagram (Figure 2), and the experimental setup and results are presented logically. This high level of clarity makes the paper's contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the combination and application are novel, the core components themselves are drawn from existing work. Deep BiLSTMs are standard encoders, residual connections are famously from computer vision (He et al., 2016), and representing relations as word sequences has been explored before (e.g., Yin et al., 2016). The paper's main novelty lies in the specific synthesis of these ideas into the HR-BiLSTM architecture for hierarchical matching. This is a minor weakness, as the intelligent combination leads to strong results, but the work is more of an excellent piece of engineering and adaptation than a fundamental breakthrough.
2.  **Potential scalability concerns of the entity re-ranking step:** The entity re-ranking step requires scoring all relations associated with the top-K initial entity candidates. For a large K and for entities with a high degree in a massive KB like the full Freebase, this step could be computationally prohibitive. The paper does not discuss the efficiency or scalability of this approach, such as the average number of relations scored per question or the inference time. While it is clearly effective on the provided datasets, its feasibility in a production system with a larger KB and more candidate entities is an open question.
3.  **The final SOTA results rely on an ensemble:** The best results reported in Table 3 are achieved by ensembling the top-3 relation detectors. While ensembling is a common practice, the performance of the single HR-BiLSTM model, while strong, is "comparable" to the state-of-the-art on WebQSP (58.0 vs. 59.4) rather than clearly surpassing it. The paper could be more transparent about the ensemble method (e.g., how predictions are combined) and should perhaps frame the single-model performance as the core result, with the ensemble as an additional improvement.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost and runtime performance of your KBQA system, particularly the entity re-ranking step? For the WebQSP experiments on the full Freebase, what was the typical number of relations that needed to be scored by the HR-BiLSTM model in this first step, and how did this impact overall query latency?
2.  The ensemble of the top-3 relation detectors provides a significant boost. Could you provide more details on this ensemble? Specifically, which three models were used, and how were their scores combined to produce the final ranking?
3.  In Section 3.2, you mention a clever detail: "We initialize the relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence, as a back-off for unseen relations." Could you provide more intuition on why this helps and whether you performed an ablation study to measure its impact on performance?
