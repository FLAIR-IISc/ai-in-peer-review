**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), focusing on the challenges of large relation vocabularies and zero-shot scenarios where relations are unseen during training. The authors propose a novel neural architecture, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for this task. The model's core idea is to perform hierarchical matching by representing relations at two granularities: as a sequence of words and as a single relation-level token. A deep, two-layer BiLSTM encodes the question, and residual connections are employed to combine the representations from both layers to match against the hierarchical relation representations. This improved relation detector is then integrated into a KBQA pipeline that performs a two-step relation detection. First, the model is used to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates by scoring their associated relations against the raw question. Second, for the top re-ranked entities, the model is used again to detect the most likely relation or relation chain. The authors evaluate their system on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on both the standalone relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a novel neural architecture for relation detection, HR-BiLSTM, which effectively combines word-level and relation-level representations of KB relations. The use of residual connections to enable this hierarchical matching by training a deep question encoder is a key technical aspect of this contribution.
2.  A second contribution is the design of a simple but effective KBQA pipeline that uses the proposed relation detector in a two-step process. The first step, which uses relation scores to re-rank and disambiguate entity candidates, is a notable architectural choice that directly addresses the entity linking bottleneck.
3.  The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation on two standard KBQA benchmarks. The comprehensive experiments, including detailed ablation studies, validate the effectiveness of the proposed model components and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) QA tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Technically Sound Model:** The core concept of hierarchical matching is clearly motivated by the trade-off between the generalization capability of word-level representations (for unseen relations) and the ability of relation-level representations to capture global context and resolve ambiguity. The application of residual learning, adapted from computer vision, is a technically sound and effective solution to the challenge of training a deep network to produce comparable, multi-level representations for matching. The ablation study in Table 2 provides strong evidence for this, showing that the full HR-BiLSTM model outperforms variants without hierarchical views (`w/o rel name`/`w/o rel words`) and without residual learning (`w/o residual learning`).
2.  **Effective System Design for KBQA:** The paper presents a clean and effective KBQA system architecture. The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is a particularly strong component, as it provides a principled way to couple entity linking and relation detection to address entity ambiguity. Instead of treating entity linking as a fixed upstream step, the system uses relation signals to refine its output. The ablation test in Table 3 ("- entity re-ranking") confirms the significant impact of this step, with performance dropping by 2.6% on SimpleQuestions and 5.1% on WebQSP. This demonstrates that the improved relation detector contributes to the overall system in multiple, meaningful ways.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and robust. By testing on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, the authors demonstrate the model's applicability to both single-relation and more complex multi-relation chain detection scenarios. The decision to evaluate the relation detection component in isolation (Section 5.2) and within the end-to-end system (Section 5.3) allows for a clear analysis of the model's direct and indirect contributions. The inclusion of strong baselines and extensive ablation studies for both the model architecture (Table 2) and the KBQA system components (Table 3) provides compelling evidence supporting the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity on Residual Learning Implementation:** The description of the residual learning mechanism in Section 3.2 is brief. Two methods for applying the shortcut connections are mentioned: (1) connecting hidden states `h_i` at each time step, and (2) connecting the final max-pooled representations `h_max`. However, the paper does not specify which of these two methods was used for the final reported results. In Section 5.1, the choice between these two methods is listed as a tuned hyperparameter, but the outcome of this tuning and its impact on performance are not discussed. This lack of detail obscures a key architectural choice of the proposed model.
2.  **Limited Scope of End-to-End System Comparison:** The proposed KBQA system is intentionally simple, omitting components like beam search and feature-based re-ranking that are common in other state-of-the-art systems (e.g., STAGG). While achieving SOTA results with a simpler system is an advantage, the direct comparison in Table 3 makes it difficult to fully isolate the impact of the HR-BiLSTM model from the impact of the surrounding pipeline architecture. A more controlled comparison, such as replacing the relation detection module in a more complex system like STAGG with HR-BiLSTM, would provide a clearer measure of the model's contribution relative to other architectural factors.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on a Newly Created Dataset:** For the WebQSP relation detection task, the authors created a new evaluation dataset. While making the data available is promised, the paper lacks basic statistics about this dataset, such as the number of training/development/test instances and the distribution of positive and negative labels per question. The process for generating negative examples ("select all the relations and relation chains... connecting to the topic entity") could result in a highly imbalanced dataset, and understanding this distribution is crucial for interpreting the reported accuracy scores in Table 2. This omission hinders the full assessment of the WebQSP relation detection results and their reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify which of the two residual connection methods described in Section 3.2 (connecting hidden states vs. connecting max-pooled vectors) was used for the final experiments in Tables 2 and 3? Did you find one method to be consistently superior?
2.  For the WebQSP relation detection dataset you created, could you please provide key statistics, including the number of question-relation pairs in the train/dev/test splits and the average number of negative candidates per positive instance?
3.  The entity re-ranking score (Section 4.1) is a linear interpolation between the linker score and the relation score, controlled by a hyperparameter `α`. Could you report the values of `α` used for the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP experiments and briefly comment on the sensitivity of the final KBQA performance to this parameter?
