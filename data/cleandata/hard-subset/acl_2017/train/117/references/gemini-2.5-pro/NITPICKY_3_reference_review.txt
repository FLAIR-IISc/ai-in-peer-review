**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for the task of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The model's key idea is to perform hierarchical matching between a question and a candidate relation. It achieves this by representing relations at two levels of granularity (word-level and relation-level) and encoding the question using a deep BiLSTM. To facilitate the matching between different representation layers, the model incorporates residual (shortcut) connections. The authors then integrate this improved relation detector into a simple two-step KBQA pipeline. This pipeline first uses the relation detector to re-rank entity candidates from an initial linker and then detects the final relation for the top-ranked entities. The proposed system achieves state-of-the-art results on both the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets.

**Contributions**

1.  **A novel neural architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection.** The main contribution is the model itself, which combines a dual-granularity representation of relations (word-level and relation-level) with a deep, residually-connected question encoder for hierarchical matching. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the utility of each component.
2.  **A simple and effective KBQA pipeline leveraging the proposed relation detector.** The paper demonstrates that a strong relation detector can significantly boost KBQA performance, particularly through an entity re-ranking step that disambiguates entities using relation signals. This pipeline achieves state-of-the-art results without complex, feature-based re-ranking modules common in prior work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper reports state-of-the-art or comparable results on two standard KBQA benchmarks, SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. The improvements on the isolated relation detection sub-task are also significant (2-3% absolute accuracy gain over strong baselines), which robustly supports the paper's core claims.
2.  **Well-Motivated Model Design:** The motivation for using both word-level and relation-level representations is clear and compelling. Word-level matching addresses the zero-shot problem for unseen relations, while relation-level matching captures more global semantic correspondence. The paper provides good illustrative examples (e.g., "starring roles" vs. "plays produced") for this design choice.
3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The ablation analysis in Table 2 is comprehensive and provides valuable insights. It successfully isolates the contributions of using both relation views, the residual learning component, and the choice of encoder (BiLSTM vs. CNN), strengthening the authors' claims about why their model is effective.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents promising results, it suffers from a significant number of minor flaws in its methodology, experimental details, and presentation that collectively undermine confidence in the work.

1.  **Imprecise Methodological Descriptions:**
    *   The use of the term "Residual Learning" is misleading. The proposed mechanism is an additive shortcut connection, either `h'_i = h_i^(1) + h_i^(2)` or `h_q = h_max^(1) + h_max^(2)`. This is architecturally different from the formulation in He et al. (2016), where a layer learns a residual function `F(x)` with respect to the input `x` (i.e., `H(x) = F(x) + x`). The justification that the second layer "is fitting the residues from the first layer of matching" is hand-wavy and lacks technical grounding.
    *   Crucial training details are missing. The ranking loss function `lrel = max{0, srel(r+;q) + srel(r-;q)}` is incorrectly specified; it should be a max-margin loss, likely `max{0, margin - s(r+) + s(r-)}`. More importantly, the strategy for selecting the negative sample `r-` (e.g., random, hard negative mining) is not mentioned, which is a critical detail for training ranking models effectively.
    *   The model description in Section 3.2 is ambiguous. It mentions two ways to implement residual connections (on hidden states or on max-pooled vectors) but never specifies which was used for the final experiments. Similarly, the detail about initializing the relation-level LSTM with the final state of the word-level LSTM is an interesting idea for handling unseen relations but is never ablated or elaborated upon.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Experimental Setup:**
    *   The comparison against the state-of-the-art KBQA system STAGG is not entirely direct. The proposed pipeline is much simpler and notably omits a final feature-based re-ranking stage over a beam of candidates, which is a core component of STAGG. While achieving SOTA with a simpler model is commendable, this architectural difference should be more explicitly discussed as a confounding factor.
    *   The final SOTA results rely on an ensemble of three models. While this is a common practice, the primary claims of the paper should rest on the performance of the single HR-BiLSTM model. The performance gain from ensembling is non-trivial (e.g., 58.0% -> 60.1% on WebQSP) and slightly overstates the contribution of the core model architecture.

3.  **Poor Formatting and Numerous Typos:** The paper exhibits a lack of care in its preparation.
    *   The reference section is exceptionally poorly formatted, with raw BibTeX-like entries, repeated author names, and inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Bao et al\.,? 2016, Bao et al\.,"). This is a major presentational flaw.
    *   There are several typos and grammatical errors throughout the text, such as "golded test relations" (Footnote 1), "no previous used entity information" (Sec 2), and an incorrectly rendered union symbol `[` instead of `\cup` in Section 3.2.
    *   Citations in the introduction are improperly formatted with line breaks inside the parentheses.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I lean towards rejecting this paper in its current form. The core idea is sound and the results are strong, suggesting a potentially valuable contribution. However, the paper is marred by a substantial number of small-to-medium issues, including imprecise methodological descriptions, missing experimental details, and egregious formatting errors. The lack of rigor and care in the presentation makes it difficult to fully trust the implementation and findings. The paper has the potential to be a strong submission after a thorough revision that addresses the clarity of the method and cleans up the numerous presentation flaws.
