### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), focusing on the challenges of handling a large number of relations and unseen relations during testing. The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for matching questions to KB relations. The core idea is to represent relations at two levels of granularity (word-level for generalization and full relation-level for specificity) and to create corresponding hierarchical question representations using a deep BiLSTM. To effectively combine these hierarchical representations, the model incorporates residual connections between the BiLSTM layers. The paper then integrates this improved relation detector into a KBQA pipeline where it serves two functions: first, to re-rank initial entity candidates by leveraging relational context, and second, to identify the core relation for the top-ranked entities. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Matching Architecture (HR-BiLSTM):** The primary contribution is the HR-BiLSTM model itself. The combination of dual-granularity relation representations with a deep, residual question encoder is a novel and well-motivated approach to balance generalization (for unseen relations) and specificity (for complex lexical matching). The use of residual connections to facilitate training and matching between different representation layers is a clever adaptation from other domains.
2.  **An Effective Two-Step Application of Relation Detection for KBQA:** The paper presents a simple but powerful KBQA pipeline that showcases how a strong relation detector can improve upstream components. The entity re-ranking step, which uses relational evidence from the question to disambiguate entity candidates, is a significant and effective contribution to improving the robustness of the overall system.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation on two standard benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art results. The comprehensive ablation studies compellingly demonstrate the value of each component of the proposed model (hierarchical representations, residual learning) and the KBQA system (entity re-ranking, constraint detection).

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Novel Model Architecture:** The motivation for handling relations at different levels of granularity is clear and directly addresses a key challenge in KBQA (the zero-shot problem). Treating relations as both a sequence of words and a single token is an intuitive way to capture both compositional and holistic semantics. The adaptation of residual learning to enable this hierarchical matching is the most innovative part of the model and appears to be effective, particularly on the more complex WebQSP dataset.
2.  **Excellent Experimental Results and Rigorous Ablation Studies:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance on two important and distinct KBQA datasets. The strength of the empirical evaluation lies in its thoroughness. The ablation study in Table 2 clearly isolates the impact of using word-level vs. relation-level views, the benefit of the residual connection over simpler aggregation methods (weighted sum, attention), and the superiority of BiLSTMs over CNNs for this task. This level of analysis strongly supports the authors' claims about their model's design.
3.  **Synergistic Integration into a Full KBQA System:** The paper goes beyond just proposing a new relation detector and demonstrates its impact on the end task. The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is a particularly strong point. It cleverly uses the proposed model to mitigate errors from the entity linking stage, a common bottleneck in KBQA systems. The ablation results in Table 3, which show a significant drop in performance when this step is removed, validate its importance.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed method is explained logically with a helpful diagram (Figure 2), and the experiments are presented clearly. This makes the contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Details on Constraint Detection:** The constraint detection module (Section 4.4) is described very briefly, with key details about the matching function ("maximum overlapping sequence of characters") deferred to an appendix that is not included. Given that the ablation study in Table 3 shows this component is "crucial" for performance on WebQSP (improving accuracy from 58.0% to 62.3%), this lack of detail hinders the reproducibility and full understanding of the system's success on complex questions.
2.  **Potential Scalability Issues are Not Addressed:** The entity re-ranking step requires scoring all relations connected to the top-K initial entity candidates. For a large KB like Freebase, where entities can have hundreds of relations, and for a reasonably sized K, this step could be a significant computational bottleneck. The paper provides no discussion of the efficiency of this approach, the values of K and K' used, or the average number of relations scored per question. This oversight makes it hard to assess the practical viability of the method for large-scale, real-world systems.
3.  **The KBQA Pipeline Architecture Lacks Novelty:** While the *use* of the relation detector within the pipeline is novel (especially for re-ranking), the overall pipeline structure (Entity Linking -> Re-ranking -> Relation Detection -> Constraint Detection) is a fairly standard staged approach, following prior work like Yih et al. (2015). The authors acknowledge this by calling it a "simple KBQA implementation," but it means the primary novelty of the paper is almost exclusively confined to the relation detection model itself.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the constraint detection mechanism? Specifically, how is the matching score based on the "maximum overlapping sequence of characters" computed, and is it the only feature used for linking constraint entities? Providing more detail would significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.
2.  Regarding the computational cost of the entity re-ranking step, what were the typical values for K (initial candidates) and K' (re-ranked candidates) in your experiments? Could you provide insight into the average number of relations that needed to be scored per question and the overall impact on inference time?
3.  Your analysis suggests that residual learning and the deeper architecture are more beneficial for WebQSP than for SimpleQuestions. You hypothesize this is due to matching a "larger scope of context". Could you expand on this? Do you believe this is because WebQSP questions are more syntactically complex, or is it specifically because matching relation *chains* (as opposed to single relations) benefits more from the abstract representations learned by the second BiLSTM layer?
