### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), arguing that it is distinct from general relation extraction due to the massive number of relations, zero-shot scenarios, and the need to handle relation chains. The central contribution is a novel neural architecture, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM). The key idea is to represent relations at two levels of granularity: as a sequence of words (e.g., "episodes", "written") and as a single token (e.g., "episodes_written"). The model then learns hierarchical representations of the input question using a deep BiLSTM and performs a hierarchical match against the multi-granularity relation representations. To make this deep architecture trainable and effective, the authors cleverly adapt residual connections. This improved relation detector is then integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline, where it is used not only for relation detection but also for an initial entity re-ranking step. The authors show that this approach achieves state-of-the-art results on both the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **A Novel and Conceptually Elegant Approach to Relation Representation:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel formulation of the relation detection problem. Instead of choosing a single representation for relations (e.g., word sequences, character n-grams, or holistic embeddings), the authors propose combining word-level and relation-level views. This is a bold and well-motivated idea that directly tackles the trade-off between generalization (word-level for unseen relations) and capturing the non-compositional semantics of full relation names (relation-level). This multi-granularity approach is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of KBQA.

2.  **Clever Architectural Innovation:** The proposed HR-BiLSTM architecture is an elegant solution to the hierarchical matching problem. The use of a deep BiLSTM to generate multi-level question representations corresponding to the multi-level relation representations is intuitive. More impressively, the application of residual learning to facilitate the matching between these layers is a creative adaptation of a powerful idea from computer vision. The ablation studies convincingly show that this is not just a minor tweak; the residual connections are crucial for making the hierarchical matching effective, leading to significant performance gains over naive combinations or standard attention mechanisms. This demonstrates strong architectural design.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Systemic Impact:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the proposed model is not just novel but also highly effective. HR-BiLSTM substantially outperforms previous state-of-the-art models on the isolated relation detection task for both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. Crucially, the authors demonstrate the model's practical value by integrating it into a KBQA system. The fact that their simple pipeline, powered by the new relation detector, achieves state-of-the-art results is a powerful testament to the importance of the core contribution. The entity re-ranking step, which leverages the relation detector to disambiguate entities, is a simple yet impactful addition that further underscores the model's strength.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis into the Learned Representations:** The paper's narrative rests on the appealing intuition that the deeper layers of the BiLSTM learn more abstract features from the question, which then match the more holistic, relation-level representations. While the performance gains support this hypothesis, the paper offers no direct evidence. A qualitative analysis—for example, visualizing which parts of a question are attended to or have higher activations at each layer—would have provided much deeper insight into *why* the hierarchical matching works. As it stands, the mechanism remains a bit of a black box. While the results are convincing on their own, this lack of analysis is a missed opportunity to fully validate the central claim about learning different levels of abstraction.

2.  **The KBQA System is Largely Conventional:** The authors are transparent that their KBQA pipeline is intentionally simple to highlight the impact of the relation detection module. However, this means the novelty is almost entirely contained within the HR-BiLSTM model. The pipeline itself, consisting of entity linking, relation detection, and constraint handling, follows a standard staging pattern. While the entity re-ranking step is a nice touch, the overall system architecture does not represent a new direction for KBQA system design. This is a minor weakness, as the paper's focus is clear, but it's worth noting that the primary contribution is a component, not a full system paradigm.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The core motivation for the hierarchical architecture is that different layers of the question encoder capture different levels of abstraction. Could you provide any qualitative examples or analysis to support this claim? For instance, for a question like "what tv show did grant show play on in 2008", what n-grams or patterns are captured by the first BiLSTM layer versus the second? This would greatly strengthen the paper's central story.

2.  The entity re-ranking step proves to be very effective. How sensitive is the final KBQA performance to the hyperparameter `alpha`, which balances the original linker score and the relation detection score? A brief analysis would help in understanding the robustness and interplay of these two components.
