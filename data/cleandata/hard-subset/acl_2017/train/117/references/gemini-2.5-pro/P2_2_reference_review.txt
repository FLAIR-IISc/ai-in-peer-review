### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA), arguing that it is a significantly more challenging problem than general relation extraction due to the large number of relations, the presence of unseen relations at test time (zero-shot), and the need to handle relation chains. The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual Bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM), to tackle these challenges. The core idea is to perform a hierarchical match between the question and the candidate relation. Relations are represented at two levels of granularity (full relation names and constituent words), while the question is encoded using a two-layer BiLSTM. A residual connection is used to combine the representations from both layers of the question encoder, which the authors claim makes training easier and improves matching. This improved relation detector is then embedded into a simple yet effective KBQA pipeline. A key feature of this pipeline is a two-step use of the relation detector: first, to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates based on the raw question, and second, to identify the final relation for the re-ranked, higher-confidence entities. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive results on both the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets for both the isolated relation detection task and the end-to-end KBQA task.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **The HR-BiLSTM model for relation detection.** The primary novelty lies in the hierarchical matching framework, specifically the use of residual connections to combine multi-level question representations for matching against multi-granularity relation representations. This is a well-motivated architectural choice that is shown to be effective.
2.  **A simple and effective KBQA system design.** The paper demonstrates how a powerful relation detection component can be leveraged to improve other parts of a KBQA pipeline. The proposed entity re-ranking step, which uses relation detection to disambiguate entities, is a clever and practical contribution.
3.  **Strong empirical results and analysis.** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on two standard benchmarks. The evaluation is thorough, with detailed ablation studies that convincingly demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed model components (hierarchical representations, residual learning).

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Model Architecture:** The motivation for hierarchical matching is very clear. Relations in KBs like Freebase have semantic meaning at both the word level (e.g., "written") and the full phrase level (e.g., "episodes_written"), which may match different spans in a question. The proposed HR-BiLSTM architecture directly addresses this by creating multi-granularity representations on both the question and relation sides. The application of residual learning to combine the hierarchical question representations is a novel and elegant solution to what the authors identify as a difficult training problem for a naive hierarchical model.

2.  **Excellent Empirical Results and Rigorous Ablation Studies:** The paper presents compelling results. The HR-BiLSTM model significantly outperforms strong baselines (including prior SOTA) on two distinct relation detection tasks (Table 2). The ablation studies are particularly strong, systematically dissecting the model to justify each design choice. For instance, removing either the word-level or relation-level view degrades performance, confirming the value of the hierarchical approach. Likewise, replacing residual learning with simpler combination methods (weighted sum, attention) leads to worse results, validating this specific architectural choice.

3.  **Effective Integration into a Full KBQA System:** The authors don't just present a model for an isolated subtask; they demonstrate its value in an end-to-end system. The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) is a particularly strong aspect of the system design. It directly addresses the common problem of entity linking ambiguity by using the relational context from the question as a powerful disambiguation signal. The fact that their relatively simple pipeline achieves state-of-the-art results underscores the power of their core relation detection module.

4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed method is explained logically with helpful figures and examples (Fig 1, Fig 2, Table 1), and the experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward and easy-to-understand manner.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Nature of the KBQA Pipeline:** While the entity re-ranking step is clever, the overall KBQA system is a staged, pipeline approach that is common in the literature. It relies on an external entity linker and separate steps for relation detection and constraint handling. While the authors frame this simplicity as a strength, it lacks the novelty of more integrated, end-to-end trainable systems. The system's success is almost entirely dependent on the strength of the HR-BiLSTM component rather than a novel system-level architecture.

2.  **Limited Justification for Residual Learning:** The paper borrows the idea of residual connections from computer vision, where it is primarily motivated by the need to train extremely deep networks (e.g., >100 layers) by mitigating vanishing gradients. Here, it is applied to a two-layer network. While the empirical results are clear, the theoretical justification feels somewhat post-hoc. The authors state it "makes the model training easier" and helps fit "residues from the first layer of matching." A more in-depth analysis of why a simple element-wise sum is superior to a learned combination (e.g., a gated or weighted sum) in this specific two-layer matching context would strengthen the paper's technical contribution.

3.  **Potential Hyperparameter Complexity:** The full KBQA system introduces several hyperparameters (e.g., `alpha` for entity re-ranking, `beta` for final query generation, `K`, `K0`, `l`, `theta`) whose tuning process is not described in detail. The performance of such a pipeline can be sensitive to these values, and a lack of discussion on their tuning or sensitivity could pose a challenge for reproducibility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The comparison to the attention mechanism from Parikh et al. (2016) is very interesting. Could you elaborate on why you think it underperformed compared to the residual connection? You suggest it may work better for longer sequences, but was there any analysis of error cases that might support this hypothesis? Did you experiment with other, perhaps simpler, attention mechanisms (e.g., Bahdanau-style attention)?

2.  In the final query generation step (Section 4.3), you combine the entity re-ranking score `s_rerank` and the relation detection score `s_rel` using a hyperparameter `beta`. How was this parameter tuned? Is the system's performance sensitive to this value, and did you find that different values were needed for SimpleQuestions versus WebQSP?

3.  The ensemble result reported in the final row of Table 3 gives a significant boost. Could you specify which "top-3 relation detectors from Section 5.2" were used in this ensemble and how their outputs were combined to produce the final prediction?
