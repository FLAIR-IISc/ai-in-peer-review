### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The authors argue that KB relation detection is distinct from general relation extraction due to the large number of relations, the zero-shot problem (unseen relations at test time), and the need to handle relation chains. To tackle these challenges, they propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual Bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM). The core idea is to perform hierarchical matching between a question and a candidate relation. Relations are represented at two granularities: as a sequence of words from the relation name (word-level) and as the full relation name token (relation-level). Correspondingly, the question is encoded using a two-layer BiLSTM to produce representations at different levels of abstraction. Residual connections are used to combine these hierarchical question representations effectively. The paper also presents a KBQA pipeline that integrates this improved relation detector, most notably by using it in an initial entity re-ranking step to disambiguate entity candidates. Experiments on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets show that the proposed model improves performance on the relation detection subtask and enables a simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  A novel neural architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for KB relation detection that effectively combines multi-granularity relation representations with a hierarchical, residual question encoder. The design is well-motivated by the specific challenges of KB relation detection, and its components are validated through thorough ablation studies.
2.  An effective application of the relation detection model to improve a key upstream task in KBQA: entity linking. The proposed entity re-ranking step leverages the model's ability to score relations from raw question text to disambiguate entity candidates, demonstrably improving end-to-end performance.
3.  Strong empirical results on two standard KBQA benchmarks for both single-relation and multi-relation questions. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the relation detection module in isolation and within a full KBQA system, establishing new state-of-the-art performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The HR-BiLSTM model is technically sound and its design is clearly motivated by the problem's characteristics. The paper provides a clear justification for using both word-level and relation-level representations (Section 3.1), addressing the trade-off between generalization to unseen relations and capturing global semantic correspondence. The use of a deep BiLSTM to create hierarchical question representations is a logical parallel, and the adaptation of residual learning to facilitate the training of this hierarchical matching framework is a non-trivial and effective contribution, as demonstrated by the ablation results in Table 2 (e.g., 82.53% vs. 80.65% on WebQSP).

2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed. The authors evaluate their method on two distinct and widely used datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) that cover both single-relation and more complex multi-relation questions. Crucially, they evaluate both the relation detection component in isolation and the end-to-end KBQA system. The inclusion of strong baselines and extensive ablation studies (Table 2) provides convincing evidence for the effectiveness of each proposed component: the hierarchical representation, the residual learning mechanism, and the choice of BiLSTMs over CNNs for this task. This rigor substantiates the paper's performance claims.

3.  **Effective System-Level Integration:** The paper demonstrates a practical system-level contribution beyond the core model. The proposed entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) addresses the well-known problem of entity linking ambiguity, a common bottleneck in KBQA systems. By using the relation detector to score relations associated with entity candidates, the system can better disambiguate entities like the "Mike Kelley" example. The ablation test in Table 3 ("w/o entity re-ranking") confirms the significant impact of this step on both datasets, showing that the improved relation detector provides benefits at multiple stages of the KBQA pipeline.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty in the Overall KBQA Pipeline:** While the relation detection model is novel, the overall KBQA system described in Algorithm 1 largely follows a standard staged pipeline architecture seen in previous work (e.g., Yih et al., 2015). The primary architectural novelty is the entity re-ranking step. The other components, such as the final relation detection and constraint detection, are presented as relatively standard applications of the core model or simple text-matching heuristics. The authors state they "make minimal efforts beyond the training of the relation detection model," which, while highlighting the strength of the detector, also limits the paper's contribution in terms of a new end-to-end KBQA framework.

2.  **Lack of Clarity on Implementation Details:** The description of the residual learning mechanism in Section 3.2 is slightly ambiguous. The authors propose two methods for implementing the shortcut connections: (1) summing the hidden state vectors at each position before max-pooling, and (2) summing the max-pooled representations of each layer. The paper does not explicitly state which of these two variants was used to produce the final reported results, though Section 5.1 mentions it was tuned as a hyperparameter. This lack of specificity hinders reproducibility and a complete understanding of which architectural choice was most effective.

3.  **Absence of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper presents strong quantitative results but lacks a qualitative analysis of the model's failures. An analysis of the types of questions or relations where the HR-BiLSTM still makes mistakes would provide valuable insight. For instance, does the model struggle with complex syntactic paraphrases, long relation chains, or specific relation types? Similarly, for the end-to-end system, an error analysis could clarify whether the remaining bottleneck is entity linking, relation detection, or constraint detection. This would strengthen the paper by contextualizing the results and highlighting promising directions for future research.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify which of the two proposed residual learning implementations (summing hidden states before pooling vs. summing after pooling) was used for the final reported results in Table 2? Was one method found to be consistently superior to the other on the development sets for both datasets?
2.  The entity re-ranking step provides a significant performance boost. Could you provide a quantitative analysis on the development set showing what percentage of initial top-1 entity linking errors are corrected by this re-ranking step? This would help to more directly measure the impact of the relation detector on the entity linking subtask.
