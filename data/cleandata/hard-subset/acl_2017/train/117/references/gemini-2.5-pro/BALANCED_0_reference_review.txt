### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), a problem made difficult by the large number of relations, the presence of unseen relations in test data (zero-shot), and the need to handle multi-relation chains. The authors propose a novel neural model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for matching questions to KB relations. The core idea is to perform hierarchical matching: it represents relations at two levels of granularity (word-level and full relation name) and matches them against question representations learned at two levels of abstraction using a deep BiLSTM. To facilitate this multi-level matching, the model incorporates residual connections between the BiLSTM layers, which eases training and helps learn complementary representations. This improved relation detector is then integrated into a simple but effective KBQA pipeline. A key feature of this pipeline is a two-step application of the relation detector: first, it is used to re-rank an initial set of entity candidates to improve entity disambiguation; second, it is used to identify the final relation for the top-ranked entity. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach with experiments on the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets, achieving state-of-the-art results on both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel neural architecture (HR-BiLSTM) for relation detection.** The key novelty lies in the combination of multi-granularity relation representations with multi-level question representations, enabled by a residual learning mechanism. This is a well-motivated and technically sound approach to capturing different types of lexical and semantic overlap between questions and relations.
2.  **A demonstration of how a strong relation detector can simplify and improve a KBQA system.** The proposed pipeline, particularly the entity re-ranking step, shows that a powerful, well-designed component can effectively address other sub-problems in the KBQA cascade (like entity ambiguity) without complex joint modeling or feature engineering.
3.  **Thorough empirical evaluation and analysis.** The paper presents strong results on two standard benchmarks. The claims are well-supported by extensive ablation studies that validate the contributions of each component of the proposed model (hierarchical representations, residual learning) and the KBQA system (entity re-ranking).

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and novel model architecture.** The central contribution, the HR-BiLSTM, is well-designed. The motivation for using both word-level and relation-level representations (Section 3.1) is clear and compelling, addressing the trade-off between generalization and specificity. The use of a deep BiLSTM to capture different levels of abstraction in the question is intuitive. The application of residual connections to enable this hierarchical matching is a clever and effective way to overcome the training difficulties of deep matching models, making the representations from different layers comparable and complementary.
2.  **Strong empirical results supported by rigorous ablation studies.** The paper reports state-of-the-art or comparable results on two important and distinct KBQA benchmarks. The strength of these results is significantly bolstered by the thorough ablation studies in Table 2. These experiments systematically dissect the proposed model, convincingly demonstrating the positive impact of using hierarchical representations (both word and relation level), the superiority of residual learning over simpler aggregation methods or a baseline attention model, and the advantage of BiLSTMs over CNNs for this task. This level of analysis provides strong evidence for the authors' claims.
3.  **Effective and elegant system design.** The proposed KBQA pipeline is simple, yet it demonstrates a powerful principle: improving a core component can have cascading benefits. The entity re-ranking step is a particularly strong aspect. It directly uses the relation detector to address the common and critical problem of entity ambiguity (e.g., the "Mike Kelley" example) in a simple and data-driven way, rather than relying on hand-crafted features. Showing that this simple pipeline can outperform more complex, feature-rich systems highlights the power of the core relation detection model.
4.  **Clarity and presentation.** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, the proposed solution is explained logically, and Figure 2 provides an excellent illustration of the model architecture.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The comparison to attention-based matching is brief.** The paper compares its residual learning approach to a single attention-based model from Parikh et al. (2016) and finds it to be inferior. However, given the success and variety of attention mechanisms in NLP, this comparison feels somewhat cursory. The dismissal of attention with the brief explanation that it "may work better for long sequences" is not fully convincing. A more thorough discussion or exploration of other attention variants (e.g., self-attention for question encoding, or different inter-sequence attention mechanisms) would have strengthened the claim that residual connections are the superior method for this specific hierarchical matching task.
2.  **Limited novelty in the overall KBQA pipeline structure.** While the use of the relation detector for entity re-ranking is a nice contribution, the authors themselves note that they make "minimal efforts beyond the training of the relation detection model." The overall four-step pipeline (entity linking, re-ranking, relation detection, constraint detection) is a fairly standard cascaded approach. The constraint detection module, in particular, is based on simple text matching and seems less sophisticated than in some prior work. While the paper's focus is clearly on the relation detection component, this limits the novelty of the system-level contribution.
3.  **Lack of detail on ensembling and hyperparameter tuning.** The final state-of-the-art results reported in the last row of Table 3 are achieved with an ensemble of three models. The paper lacks detail on how these models' outputs were combined (e.g., score averaging, voting). While ensembling is common, its contribution should be clearly distinguished from the gains of the single model. Furthermore, the paper has several key hyperparameters (e.g., `α` and `β` for scoring, `δ` for the ranking loss) whose tuning process is not described, making reproducibility more difficult.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the entity re-ranking step, how are the initial `s_linker` scores determined? Are they confidence scores provided by the external entity linker, or are they derived from the rank in the initial K candidates? Could you also comment on the sensitivity of the system to the weighting parameter `α`?
2.  The final state-of-the-art results rely on an ensemble. Could you please provide more details on the ensembling method used? Furthermore, what is the performance of the single best HR-BiLSTM model on the end-to-end KBQA task (Table 3), to allow for a clearer comparison against prior non-ensembled works?
3.  Could you elaborate on why you believe the decomposable attention model underperformed for hierarchical matching? Did you experiment with other attention mechanisms that might be more suitable for integrating information from different representation levels, as an alternative to the residual connection?
