

# Review of "Improved Neural Relation Detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems. The authors identify three key differences between KBQA relation detection and general relation extraction: (1) KBs contain thousands of relations (vs. hundreds in general tasks), (2) KBQA often requires zero-shot learning (unseen relations in test data), and (3) KBQA sometimes requires predicting chains of relations.

The main contributions of this work, as objectively assessed, are:

1. A novel hierarchical residual matching approach for KB relation detection that combines both relation-level and word-level representations to handle the unique challenges of KBQA, with residual learning to improve training stability and hierarchical matching performance.

2. Demonstrating that their improved relation detection model enables a simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) tasks without complex joint inference or feature-based re-ranking.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Well-Motivated Approach**: The paper provides a clear and thorough analysis of the specific challenges in KB relation detection compared to general relation extraction. The authors effectively demonstrate why previous approaches (using only relation names or only word sequences) are insufficient. Their proposed HR-BiLSTM model addresses these limitations through hierarchical matching with residual learning, which is well-justified and supported by comprehensive ablation studies (Table 2). The model's design directly addresses the specific problems they identify, such as the need to match different levels of abstraction in questions to relation representations.

2. **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation**: The paper evaluates the proposed approach on two distinct KBQA datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) with thorough ablation studies. The results clearly show the contribution of each component (hierarchical matching, residual learning, etc.) to performance. The evaluation is particularly strong because it assesses both the relation detection task (Table 2) and the KBQA end task (Table 3), demonstrating that improvements in relation detection translate to better overall KBQA performance. The comparison to multiple baselines (AMPCNN, BiCNN, etc.) on both datasets provides a solid benchmark.

3. **Practical Impact and Simplicity**: The paper demonstrates that their improved relation detection model can be integrated into a simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results without complex components like joint inference or feature-based re-ranking. The entity re-ranking step they introduce (Section 4.1) is particularly valuable as it addresses a common bottleneck in KBQA systems (entity linking ambiguities) by leveraging relation detection. The fact that their system outperforms specialized baselines on both datasets with minimal architectural changes is a strong practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Approach**: While the specific combination of hierarchical matching with residual learning for KB relation detection is novel, the individual components (BiLSTMs, hierarchical matching, residual learning) have been used in other NLP tasks. The paper does not sufficiently establish how this particular combination is novel or why it's necessary for KBQA specifically. For example, the use of residual learning in NLP has been explored in machine translation (He et al., 2016), and hierarchical attention has been used in relation extraction (Parikh et al., 2016). The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with these prior works to better establish the novelty of their approach.

2. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**: The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach. It doesn't address how their model might scale to extremely large knowledge bases (e.g., with millions of relations), how it would perform on particularly long or complex questions, or the computational cost of their approach compared to baselines. The paper claims their model is "simple" but doesn't provide any analysis of inference speed or resource requirements.

3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper reports performance improvements of 2-3% on both relation detection and KBQA tasks but does not provide statistical significance testing for these improvements. For example, the paper states "our proposed HR-BiLSTM outperformed the best baselines on both tasks by margins of 2-3% (p < 0.001 and 0.01 compared to the best baseline BiLSTM w/ words on SQ and WQ respectively)," but doesn't provide the statistical tests or confidence intervals that would support these p-values. This is particularly important for the WebQSP results where the improvement is smaller (about 1-2%).

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that SimpleQuestions has 14% of golden test relations not observed in training, but doesn't provide similar statistics for WebQSP. Could the authors clarify how many test relations in WebQSP are unseen during training, and how this compares to SimpleQuestions?

2. The paper claims that the number of relations in general relation extraction tasks is "normally smaller than 100," but the examples provided (ACE2005, SemEval2010, TACKBP2015) have 11-74 relations. Could the authors provide more comprehensive statistics about the typical number of relations in general relation extraction tasks to better justify their claim that KBQA's relation count is significantly larger?

3. The paper states that "the number of relations is usually not large" in general relation extraction, but the authors' own examples (TACKBP2015 with 74 relations) are closer to the lower end of the KBQA relation count (6,000+). Could the authors clarify how they define "small" and provide evidence that general relation extraction tasks consistently have fewer relations than KBQA tasks?

## Additional Comments

The paper's writing is clear and well-organized, with good use of examples (Figure 1) to illustrate key concepts. The technical details are well-explained, and the ablation studies are particularly strong. The paper makes a solid contribution to the KBQA field by addressing a specific, well-identified problem with a technically sound solution that demonstrates clear practical benefits.
