

# Review for ACL Submission: "Improved Neural Relation Detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenge of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems. The authors identify three key differences between KBQA relation detection and general relation extraction tasks: (1) KBs contain thousands of relation types (vs. hundreds in general relation extraction), (2) KBQA often requires zero-shot learning (unseen relations in test data), and (3) KBQA sometimes requires predicting chains of relations. The authors propose a novel Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that performs hierarchical matching between questions and relations at both relation-level (whole relation name as a token) and word-level (relation name split into words) representations. They also introduce a simple KBQA pipeline that incorporates their improved relation detection model, featuring an entity re-ranking step based on relation detection scores.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. A novel hierarchical residual matching approach for KB relation detection that overcomes training difficulties in deep architectures through residual connections, enabling effective matching at multiple abstraction levels.
2. Demonstration that the improved relation detection model significantly boosts KBQA performance on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results.
3. A simple but effective KBQA pipeline that uses relation detection for entity re-ranking, addressing a key bottleneck in KBQA systems without complex post-processing.

## Strengths

1. **Strong, well-motivated problem statement**: The authors clearly articulate the unique challenges of KBQA relation detection compared to general relation extraction, supported by concrete evidence (e.g., 14% of test relations unseen in training for SimpleQuestions). This provides a compelling rationale for their work.

2. **Novel technical contribution with solid justification**: The HR-BiLSTM model with hierarchical residual matching is a meaningful advancement over standard approaches. The authors provide a thorough explanation of why standard deep matching approaches fail (training difficulty, layer incompatibility), and how their residual connections solve these problems. The ablation studies in Table 2 provide strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of each design choice.

3. **Significant performance improvements**: The model achieves 2-3% absolute improvements over the best baselines on both relation detection and KBQA tasks (93.3% vs. 91.2% on SimpleQuestions, 82.53% vs. 79.32% on WebQSP), which is substantial in this domain. The improvements are statistically significant (p<0.001 for SimpleQuestions).

4. **Clear connection between relation detection and KBQA**: The paper effectively demonstrates how improved relation detection directly benefits the KBQA end task. The entity re-ranking step is particularly innovative and addresses a real bottleneck in KBQA systems (72.7% top-1 entity linking accuracy on SimpleQuestions).

5. **Thorough evaluation with ablation studies**: The authors provide detailed ablation studies that isolate the contributions of hierarchical matching, residual learning, and different representation levels, making the results highly interpretable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison to more recent methods**: The paper compares to methods from 2015-2016 (BiCNN, AMPCNN), but doesn't compare to more recent approaches (post-2016) for relation detection or KBQA. For example, they don't compare to transformer-based models or more recent KBQA systems that use graph neural networks or advanced attention mechanisms. This is a significant limitation given the rapid evolution of NLP models.

2. **Lack of failure analysis**: The paper doesn't provide qualitative analysis of cases where the model fails. Understanding the limitations and failure modes would help identify future research directions and strengthen the contribution. A few examples of challenging cases would be valuable.

3. **Incomplete implementation details**: The paper mentions using pretrained word embeddings but doesn't specify which ones (e.g., GloVe, Word2Vec). The hyperparameter tuning details are somewhat sparse (e.g., they mention tuning hidden state size but don't report the optimal value used).

4. **Insufficient analysis of zero-shot performance**: Since the paper emphasizes the zero-shot learning challenge in KBQA, it would be valuable to provide a breakdown of performance on seen vs. unseen relations, especially for SimpleQuestions which has 14% unseen test relations.

5. **Limited discussion of computational cost**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity or inference time of the proposed model compared to baselines, which is important for practical KBQA systems.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that SimpleQuestions has 14% of golden test relations not observed in training. Could you provide a breakdown of HR-BiLSTM's performance on seen vs. unseen relations to better understand how the model handles the zero-shot learning challenge?

2. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art results, but don't compare to more recent approaches (e.g., transformer-based models). Could you add a comparison to recent KBQA systems (post-2016) that use advanced architectures like transformers or graph neural networks?

3. In the constraint detection step, the paper mentions "special rules dealing with date/answer type constraints" but doesn't elaborate. Could you provide more details on these rules and how they impact the overall performance?

4. The ablation study shows residual learning helps significantly on WebQSP but less so on SimpleQuestions. Could you provide an analysis of why this difference exists between the two datasets (e.g., WebQSP's longer dependency chains requiring deeper representations)?

5. The paper states that their KBQA system achieves state-of-the-art results without joint-inference or feature-based re-ranking. How does this compare to more recent KBQA systems that use these techniques (e.g., those using graph neural networks or beam search with feature extraction)?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written paper with a strong motivation and a novel technical contribution. The proposed HR-BiLSTM model addresses a real problem in KBQA and demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods. The connection between relation detection and the KBQA end task is particularly strong and well-executed.

However, the lack of comparison to more recent methods and the limited analysis of zero-shot performance are notable weaknesses that should be addressed. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including these comparisons and analyses.

Given the strong technical contribution and clear empirical results, I recommend acceptance, but the authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above in the revision.

**Decision: Accept** (with minor revisions to address the questions raised)
