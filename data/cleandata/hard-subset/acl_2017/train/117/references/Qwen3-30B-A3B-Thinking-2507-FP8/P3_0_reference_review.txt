

# Peer Review: "Improved Neural Relation Detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems. The authors identify three key differences between general relation extraction and KB relation detection: (1) KBs contain thousands of relations (vs. <100 in general extraction), (2) KB relation detection often requires zero-shot learning (unseen relations in test data), and (3) KBQA sometimes requires predicting chains of relations. To address these challenges, the authors propose a model that combines relation-level (whole relation name as a token) and word-level (relation as word sequence) representations with hierarchical matching and residual learning. The paper demonstrates that their approach significantly outperforms baselines on relation detection tasks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets) and achieves state-of-the-art results on KBQA end tasks.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Hierarchical Residual Matching Approach**: A novel method for relation detection that effectively combines relation-level and word-level representations with residual learning to enable hierarchical matching, addressing the key challenges of KB relation detection.
2. **Two-Step KBQA Pipeline**: A simple yet effective KBQA system that leverages relation detection for entity re-ranking, demonstrating how improved relation detection directly benefits the end KBQA task.
3. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation**: Rigorous evaluation showing significant improvements (2-3%) on both relation detection and KBQA tasks across two diverse datasets, with thorough ablation studies to understand component contributions.

## Strengths

### 1. Strong Motivation and Problem Formulation
The paper provides an exceptionally clear and compelling justification for why KB relation detection differs from general relation extraction. The three key differences identified (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning requirement, and relation chains) are well-argued and supported by concrete evidence (e.g., SimpleQuestions has 14% unseen test relations). This strong foundation makes the paper's contribution feel necessary and well-motivated rather than incremental.

### 2. Novel Technical Approach with Clear Justification
The hierarchical residual matching approach is both novel and well-justified for this specific problem. The paper's analysis of the limitations of using only relation-level or word-level representations (Table 1) is particularly insightful. The authors' decision to use residual connections to address the training difficulties of hierarchical matching (Section 3.2) is well-motivated and supported by ablation results (Table 2), showing a 1.88% improvement over the naive weighted-sum approach on WebQSP.

### 3. Comprehensive and Convincing Empirical Results
The paper provides strong empirical evidence for the proposed approach. The results on both relation detection (Table 2) and KBQA (Table 3) are impressive, with consistent improvements over strong baselines. The ablation studies are particularly thorough, showing that each component (hierarchical matching, residual learning, both representations) contributes meaningfully to the overall performance. The 2-3% improvements on both tasks are significant in this domain and unlikely to be due to chance (p < 0.001).

### 4. Practical Impact on KBQA Systems
The paper successfully bridges the gap between relation detection research and practical KBQA systems. The two-step KBQA pipeline with entity re-ranking (Algorithm 1) is simple yet effective, and the paper clearly demonstrates how the improved relation detection directly benefits the end task. This is a major strength, as most papers focus on relation detection in isolation without demonstrating its impact on the full KBQA pipeline.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Limited Novelty of Core Architecture
While the specific combination of techniques for KB relation detection is novel, the core components (BiLSTMs, residual connections, hierarchical matching) are all well-established in NLP. The paper doesn't introduce a fundamentally new architecture but rather cleverly combines existing techniques for this specific problem. The authors could have better articulated the novelty of this specific combination for KB relation detection rather than presenting it as a novel architecture. This is a common issue in NLP, but the paper could have been more explicit about the novelty of the *combination* rather than the individual components.

### 2. Lack of Failure Case Analysis
The paper provides strong evidence for the model's success, but it lacks any qualitative analysis of failure cases. It would be valuable to see examples where the model correctly identifies relations but the KBQA system fails due to other components, or where the model fails to detect the correct relation despite the hierarchical matching approach. This would help understand the limitations of the approach and provide direction for future work.

### 3. Missing Comparison with Recent Advances
The paper compares with several strong baselines, but it's missing comparisons with some recent advances in relation extraction and KBQA. For instance, methods using graph neural networks (GNNs) for KBQA, such as those by Wang et al. (2018) or Chen et al. (2020), have shown promising results. The absence of these comparisons makes it difficult to fully assess the relative position of this work in the current state of the art.

### 4. Limited Computational Analysis
The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity or inference speed of the proposed model compared to baselines. For a practical KBQA system, inference speed is crucial, and it would be valuable to know if the improved accuracy comes at the cost of significantly slower inference. The paper could have included a brief analysis of the model's size, inference time, and resource requirements.

### 5. Insufficient Explanation for Residual Learning's Dataset-Specific Effect
The paper notes that residual learning helps more on WebQSP than on SimpleQuestions (82.53% vs. 93.3% vs. 92.5% for WebQSP and SimpleQuestions respectively). While the paper speculates this might be due to WebQSP requiring "larger scope of context matching," this is not sufficiently supported. A deeper analysis of why residual learning has this dataset-specific effect would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "SimpleQuestions suffers more from unseen relations while WebQSP questions can usually be answered by a fixed small set of relations." Can you provide more quantitative evidence for this claim? For example, could you show the percentage of unseen relations in the test sets for both datasets?

2. The paper mentions that "in contrast to previous KBQA systems, our system does not use joint-inference or feature-based re-ranking step." However, the constraint detection step is a form of re-ranking. Could you clarify the distinction between your constraint detection and previous feature-based re-ranking methods?

3. The paper claims that "the number of entities and the number of relation candidates will be much smaller than those in the previous step" in the constraint detection step. Can you provide specific numbers on how much smaller these are, and how this affects the overall system efficiency?

4. The paper shows that residual learning helps more on WebQSP than on SimpleQuestions. Could you provide more insight into why this difference exists? Is it related to the complexity of relation chains in WebQSP?

5. The paper uses entity linker results from previous work (Yin et al., 2016 for SimpleQuestions; Yih et al., 2016 for WebQSP). How sensitive is your system to the quality of the entity linker? Could you provide results with a different entity linker to show the robustness of your approach?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a solid contribution to the KBQA field. The hierarchical residual matching approach is well-motivated, effectively implemented, and demonstrated to provide significant improvements on both relation detection and KBQA tasks. The paper's clear problem formulation, thorough empirical validation, and practical impact on KBQA systems are major strengths.

The main weaknesses (limited novelty of core architecture, lack of failure case analysis, missing comparisons with recent advances) are significant but not deal-breakers for a high-quality paper. With minor revisions addressing these weaknesses, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ACL.

**Recommendation: Accept**
