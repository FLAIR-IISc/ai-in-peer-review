

# Peer Review: Improved Neural Relation Detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems, addressing key challenges that distinguish KB relation detection from general relation extraction: the large number of relation types (thousands vs. hundreds), zero-shot learning requirements, and multi-relation (chain) detection needs. The authors introduce a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that performs hierarchical matching between questions and both word-level and relation-level representations of KB relations, incorporating residual learning to improve training stability. They also propose a simple two-step KBQA pipeline that leverages this improved relation detector, including an entity re-ranking step to address entity linking ambiguities. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on both SimpleQuestions (93.3% relation detection accuracy) and WebQSP (82.53%) datasets, with significant improvements over previous methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **A novel hierarchical residual matching approach for KB relation detection**: The paper's most significant contribution is its effective combination of hierarchical matching (word-level and relation-level representations) with residual learning to overcome training difficulties in deep architectures. This provides a principled solution to the KB relation detection challenges.

2. **Thorough empirical analysis of the model components**: The paper provides detailed ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the contribution of each component (hierarchical matching, residual learning) and how they interact with different datasets (SimpleQuestions vs. WebQSP), offering valuable insights into the model's behavior.

3. **An effective KBQA pipeline with entity re-ranking**: The authors demonstrate how their improved relation detector can be integrated into a simple KBQA system, with the entity re-ranking step addressing a real bottleneck in KBQA (entity linking ambiguities) and significantly improving overall performance.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem definition and justification**: The paper excels at articulating the specific challenges of KB relation detection compared to general relation extraction. The three key differences (large relation sets, zero-shot learning, relation chains) are well-explained with concrete examples and data (e.g., 14% of test relations in SimpleQuestions are unseen in training). This strong foundation makes the paper's contributions feel necessary and well-motivated.

2. **Thoughtful model design with strong empirical validation**: The authors don't just propose a new model but provide a clear rationale for each design decision. The hierarchical residual matching approach directly addresses the training difficulties of naive hierarchical matching (as evidenced by the higher training loss in the weighted-sum baseline). The ablation studies in Table 2 and the detailed analysis in Section 5.2 provide compelling evidence for the model's effectiveness.

3. **Significant performance improvements**: The paper demonstrates clear, statistically significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods on both relation detection and KBQA end tasks. The 2-3% absolute improvement on SimpleQuestions (93.3% vs. 91.2%) and WebQSP (82.53% vs. 79.32%) is substantial for this domain and well-supported by the ablation studies.

4. **Practical integration with KBQA pipeline**: The authors go beyond just proposing a better relation detector—they demonstrate how it can be integrated into a KBQA system to achieve better end-task performance. The entity re-ranking step is particularly clever and addresses a real bottleneck in KBQA systems (entity linking ambiguities), with the ablation study (Table 3) showing that removing this step significantly degrades performance.

5. **Insightful dataset-specific analysis**: The paper provides valuable insights into how the model behaves differently on SimpleQuestions vs. WebQSP, explaining why residual learning helps more on WebQSP (which requires handling larger context matching) than on SimpleQuestions. This level of analysis is rare in NLP papers and adds significant value.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core methodology**: While the application to KB relation detection is novel, the core components (BiLSTMs, residual connections, hierarchical matching) are all standard in the deep learning literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently argue why these standard components, combined in this specific way, represent a significant advance for this problem. The residual learning component is directly borrowed from He et al. (2016), and the hierarchical matching idea is somewhat similar to Parikh et al. (2016). The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the novelty compared to related work.

2. **Insufficient comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares with a few baselines (BiLSTM, AMPCNN), but fails to include recent methods that have made significant advances in relation extraction using pretrained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa). For example, the paper doesn't compare with Zhang et al. (2020) or other recent BERT-based approaches. Without this comparison, the "state-of-the-art" claim is weakened, as the authors are not comparing against the current leading methods in the broader relation extraction field.

3. **Lack of error analysis and limitations discussion**: The paper provides no analysis of error cases or limitations of the approach. It's unclear why the model fails on certain examples, what the error patterns are, or how the model handles very long questions or complex relations. Without this, it's hard to assess the real-world applicability of the method or understand its boundaries. The paper should discuss how the model might perform on even larger KBs or with more complex question structures.

4. **Questionable necessity of the two-step pipeline**: While the entity re-ranking step improves performance, the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this is better than simply using a more accurate entity linker. The claim that "entity linking sometimes becomes a bottleneck" is supported only by a single statistic (72.7% top-1 accuracy on SimpleQuestions), but doesn't provide evidence that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art entity linkers on the same data. This weakens the justification for their two-step pipeline.

5. **Insufficient discussion of computational complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational costs of their approach compared to the baselines. Given that they use deep BiLSTMs with residual connections, it's important to know whether the performance gains come with a significant increase in training time or inference cost, which would be crucial for practical applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that residual learning helps more on WebQSP than on SimpleQuestions, with the authors speculating that "WebQSP benefits more from residual and deeper architecture, possibly because in this dataset it is more important to handle larger scope of context matching." Could you provide more concrete evidence for this hypothesis? For example, could you show how the model's attention patterns or hidden representations differ between the two datasets?

2. The paper doesn't compare with recent methods that use pretrained language models (e.g., BERT) for relation extraction. How would your HR-BiLSTM model compare with such approaches? Would the hierarchical residual matching still be beneficial when using pretrained language models as the base encoder?

3. The paper states that "the entity re-ranking step significantly decreases the scores" when removed (Table 3), but it's not clear why this step is better than using a more accurate entity linker. Have you tried using a state-of-the-art entity linker (e.g., one based on BERT) and then applying your relation detection model? How would the performance compare?

4. The paper mentions that "the KBQA end task benefits from this process" due to entity re-ranking, but doesn't provide concrete examples of how this improves the KBQA pipeline. Could you provide specific examples where the entity re-ranking step resolves ambiguity that would otherwise lead to incorrect answers?

5. The paper claims that the model handles zero-shot learning well due to the word-level representation. However, it doesn't provide a clear analysis of how many relations were truly unseen in training but successfully handled by the model. Could you provide statistics on the number of unseen relations in the test sets and how well the model handles them?

## Additional Comments

1. The related work section could be strengthened by discussing more recent work on relation extraction that uses pretrained language models, which have become the standard in the field.

2. The paper mentions "a simple KBQA implementation," but it's not clear why the authors consider this simple compared to other approaches. A brief comparison with the complexity of previous KBQA systems would help establish this claim.

3. The paper states that "our system does not use joint-inference or feature-based re-ranking step," but doesn't explain why this is an advantage. In many KBQA systems, joint inference is crucial for accuracy. The authors should better justify why their approach doesn't need these components and how it still achieves competitive results.

4. The paper mentions that "the number of relations is usually not large" in general relation extraction, but doesn't provide a clear comparison of the scale (e.g., 11/32 relations in ACE2005 vs. 6,000+ in Freebase). Adding a table comparing the number of relations in different datasets would make this point more concrete.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-motivated and well-executed approach to a significant problem in KBQA. The HR-BiLSTM model is a thoughtful combination of existing techniques applied to a specific problem, with strong empirical validation. While the core methodology isn't radically novel, the effective application to the KB relation detection problem and the thorough analysis of its components make it a valuable contribution to the field. The paper's main weakness is the insufficient comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods using pretrained language models, which would strengthen the "state-of-the-art" claim. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper would be a strong candidate for publication at ACL.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept**
