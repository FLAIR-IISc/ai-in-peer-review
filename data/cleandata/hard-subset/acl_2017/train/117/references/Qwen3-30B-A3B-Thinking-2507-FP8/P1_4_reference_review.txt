

# Peer Review for ACL Submission

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical but often overlooked aspect of Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA): relation detection. The authors identify three key differences between general relation extraction and KBQA relation detection: (1) KBQA deals with thousands of relations rather than hundreds, (2) it requires handling unseen relations (zero-shot learning), and (3) it sometimes needs to predict relation chains rather than single relations. To address these challenges, the authors propose a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that performs hierarchical matching between questions and relations at both word-level and relation-level representations. They also present a two-step KBQA system that leverages their improved relation detector, with an entity re-ranking step to address ambiguity in entity linking.

## Main Contributions (as evaluated by reviewer)
1. A well-motivated hierarchical residual matching approach for KBQA relation detection that effectively handles the unique challenges of large-scale relation sets and unseen relations.
2. Demonstrating that improved relation detection directly leads to state-of-the-art performance on KBQA end tasks, with a simple yet effective two-step KBQA system.
3. Providing thorough ablation studies that validate the importance of both hierarchical matching and residual learning components.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Gap Identification**: The paper clearly identifies and articulates a crucial gap in the literature between general relation extraction and KBQA-specific relation detection. The authors convincingly argue why standard relation extraction approaches fail for KBQA due to the scale of relations (thousands vs. hundreds), zero-shot learning requirements, and need for relation chains. This is an important contribution to the field as it shifts the focus from general relation extraction to the specific challenges of KBQA.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The proposed HR-BiLSTM model achieves state-of-the-art results on both relation detection tasks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) and KBQA end tasks, with significant improvements over strong baselines (2-3% absolute gain). The ablation studies in Table 2 provide compelling evidence that both hierarchical matching and residual learning are critical components, not just incremental improvements.

3. **Principled Approach with Theoretical Justification**: The authors provide a well-reasoned explanation for why their approach works better than alternatives. The connection to residual networks (He et al., 2016) is well-justified, and their analysis of the training difficulties with deep architectures provides valuable insights beyond just empirical results.

4. **Practical Impact on KBQA Pipeline**: The proposed two-step KBQA system with entity re-ranking is simple yet effective, directly addressing a known bottleneck in KBQA systems (entity linking ambiguity). The authors demonstrate that this simple addition significantly improves overall system performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Zero-Shot Learning Evidence**: While the paper mentions that 14% of test relations in SimpleQuestions are unseen during training, it doesn't provide specific performance metrics on these unseen relations. The authors claim their model handles zero-shot learning better, but without concrete evidence on how it specifically performs on unseen relations compared to baselines, this claim remains unsupported.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper references several recent works (Yin et al., 2016; Liang et al., 2016), but doesn't compare with the most recent state-of-the-art approaches. For example, the paper mentions Liang et al. (2016) but doesn't include their results in the comparison, which would strengthen the argument for their model's superiority.

3. **Superficial Treatment of Relation Chains**: While the paper acknowledges the need for relation chains (Figure 1b), it doesn't adequately explain how their model specifically handles chain prediction beyond the two-step process described in Section 4. The paper doesn't address how the model deals with multiple possible relation chains in the KB or how it selects between them.

4. **Lack of Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of their model compared to simpler baselines. Given the use of deep BiLSTMs with residual connections, the training and inference time might be significantly higher, which could be a practical limitation for real-world KBQA systems.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the proposed model handles zero-shot learning better, but doesn't provide performance metrics specifically on unseen relations in SimpleQuestions. Could you provide results showing how your model performs on the 14% unseen test relations compared to baselines?

2. The paper mentions that WebQSP benefits more from the residual learning component than SimpleQuestions. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, given that WebQSP questions can usually be answered with a fixed small set of relations?

3. How does the model handle cases where multiple valid relation chains exist in the KB for a single question? For example, if a question could be answered by two different paths in the KB, how does the model choose between them?

4. Could you provide a comparison of training and inference time between your HR-BiLSTM model and the simpler BiLSTM baseline to help assess the practical trade-offs of using your approach?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the KBQA field by addressing a critical but often overlooked component of the pipeline. The proposed HR-BiLSTM model is well-motivated, shows clear improvements over strong baselines, and leads to state-of-the-art KBQA performance with a simple system. While there are some weaknesses in the presentation of zero-shot learning results and comparison with recent work, these are relatively minor given the paper's overall strength. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions raised above. The work is clearly relevant to ACL, makes a substantive contribution to the field, and is well-executed.
