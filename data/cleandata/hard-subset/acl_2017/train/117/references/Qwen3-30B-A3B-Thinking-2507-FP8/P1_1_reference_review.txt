

# Peer Review: Improved Neural Relation Detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an improved neural relation detection model for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems. The authors identify three key challenges that differentiate KBQA relation detection from general relation extraction: (1) the large number of relation types (thousands vs. hundreds), (2) the zero-shot learning requirement (unseen relations in test data), and (3) the need to detect relation chains rather than single relations.

The authors propose a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that combines relation-level and word-level representations with hierarchical matching and residual learning. They also propose a simple KBQA system that uses two-step relation detection, including an entity re-ranking step based on relation detection scores.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. The HR-BiLSTM model for hierarchical matching between questions and relations, which effectively combines both relation-level and word-level representations with residual learning to handle the specific challenges of KBQA relation detection.

2. The demonstration that their improved relation detection model leads to state-of-the-art results on both relation detection tasks and the KBQA end task, with a simple two-step relation detection approach that outperforms previous more complex KBQA systems.

3. The thorough ablation studies that provide insights into why each component of their model (hierarchical matching, residual learning) is important for KBQA relation detection, particularly for handling unseen relations and complex relation chains.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Contribution**: The paper addresses a key bottleneck in KBQA systems - relation detection - with a practical solution that provides consistent improvements (2-3% absolute) over previous state-of-the-art methods on both relation detection and KBQA end tasks. The authors demonstrate that their approach, while relatively simple, significantly outperforms more complex systems that use joint inference or feature-based re-ranking. This practical contribution is highly valuable for the KBQA community.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The paper provides thorough experimental validation with comprehensive ablation studies. The authors carefully analyze each component of their model (hierarchical matching, residual learning, relation representation types) and demonstrate their importance through ablation tests. The results on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets show consistent improvements over previous methods, with statistical significance reported (p < 0.001 for SimpleQuestions). The ablation studies provide valuable insights into why their approach works well for KBQA.

3. **Clear Problem Formulation and Justification**: The paper clearly identifies the specific challenges of KBQA relation detection compared to general relation extraction, providing concrete examples (e.g., SimpleQuestions has 14% of test relations not seen in training). This strong problem formulation justifies their approach and differentiates their work from previous relation extraction research. The authors also correctly note that previous work often makes the "close domain assumption" that doesn't hold for many KBQA datasets.

4. **Novelty of Approach**: While residual learning and hierarchical matching have been used in other NLP tasks, their specific application to KBQA relation detection with the combination of relation-level and word-level representations is novel. The residual learning approach for hierarchical matching is particularly innovative for this specific problem, as evidenced by the 2-3% performance gains over baselines.

5. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the proposed model and experiments. The figures (especially Figure 2) effectively illustrate the model architecture, and the ablation studies provide valuable insights. The two-step relation detection approach is simple to understand and implement, making the contribution accessible to the community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the authors empirically demonstrate that their approach works well, they don't provide a strong theoretical justification for why hierarchical matching with residual learning is particularly beneficial for KBQA relation detection. They mention that deep architectures are harder to train, but don't provide a rigorous analysis of why their residual approach specifically solves this problem. A deeper theoretical analysis would strengthen the paper.

2. **Limited Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper compares to several previous methods, but misses some recent work on relation extraction that could be relevant. For example, there's a recent paper by (Zhang et al., 2020) on relation extraction with graph neural networks that might be worth comparing to, especially given the relational structure of KBs. The authors should discuss how their approach relates to or differs from these more recent methods.

3. **Computational Complexity**: The authors don't discuss the computational complexity of their model compared to baselines. Given that KBQA systems need to be efficient, it's important to understand whether their improved model is significantly more expensive to train or run. The paper mentions using "top-K" and "top-K0" candidates, but doesn't discuss how the model scales with K or how the computational cost compares to previous methods.

4. **Generalizability Concerns**: While the model works well on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, the paper doesn't demonstrate how well it generalizes to other KBQA datasets or to more complex relation chains. The authors mention WebQSP as a multi-relation task, but it's not clear if the model would scale to even more complex relations (e.g., chains of length 3+). The authors should discuss how their approach would handle longer relation chains.

5. **Data Leakage in Relation Detection Task**: The paper creates a new relation detection task from WebQSP data. However, they don't clearly explain how they avoid data leakage between the relation detection task and the KBQA end task. Since the relation detection task is derived from the semantic parses of the WebQSP questions, there might be some overlap between the training data for relation detection and the KBQA evaluation. The authors should clarify how they prevent this data leakage.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the SimpleQuestions dataset has 14% of test relations not seen in training, but doesn't provide details on how they handle these unseen relations in their relation detection model. Could you clarify how your model handles zero-shot relations during testing?

2. In the entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1), you mention using "top l best scoring relations" to re-rank entities. How did you determine the optimal value of l? Did you tune it on the development set?

3. The paper states that using CNNs in the hierarchical matching framework results in a large performance drop on WebQSP. Could you provide more insight into why LSTMs work better than CNNs for this specific task, given that CNNs have been successful in other relation extraction tasks?

4. In the constraint detection step (Section 4.4), you mention "special rules dealing with date/answer type constraints." Could you provide more details on these special rules, especially since they're not described in the main text?

5. The paper mentions that their approach outperforms previous work on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, but doesn't provide detailed error analysis. Could you share some examples where your model succeeds where previous methods fail, especially in handling zero-shot relations or complex relation chains?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the KBQA community by addressing a key bottleneck in relation detection with a practical, effective solution. The authors provide thorough empirical validation and insightful ablation studies that demonstrate the importance of their approach. The paper is well-written and clearly presents a valuable contribution that could be easily adopted by other KBQA researchers.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding theoretical justification, comparison to recent work, and computational complexity analysis. The paper has significant practical value and the results are compelling, making it a strong candidate for ACL.
