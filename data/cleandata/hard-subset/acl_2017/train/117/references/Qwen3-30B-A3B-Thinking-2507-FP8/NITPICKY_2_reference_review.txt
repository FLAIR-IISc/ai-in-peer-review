

# ACL Review: "Improved Neural Relation Detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for improved relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems. The authors claim to address three key challenges in KB relation detection: (1) the large number of relation types (thousands vs. hundreds in general relation extraction), (2) zero-shot learning requirements (unseen relations in test data), and (3) handling relation chains for complex questions. The main contributions are:

1. A hierarchical matching approach that combines relation-level (whole relation name as token) and word-level (relation name as word sequence) representations
2. A residual learning method to facilitate hierarchical matching and improve training
3. A simple KBQA system using two-step relation detection that achieves state-of-the-art results

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Definition**: The paper effectively identifies the key differences between general relation extraction and KB-specific relation detection (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, relation chains), which is a strong foundation for the proposed work.

2. **Well-Structured Experiments**: The experimental setup is comprehensive, with thorough ablation studies (Table 2) and evaluation on two standard datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP). The authors provide convincing evidence that their hierarchical matching approach outperforms baselines by 2-3% on both relation detection and KBQA tasks.

3. **Practical KBQA Implementation**: The two-step relation detection approach (entity re-ranking followed by relation detection) is well-motivated and provides a clear path to improve KBQA systems without requiring complex joint inference or re-ranking modules.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Formatting and Typographical Errors**: The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism. The reference list has numerous instances of "Bao et al.,? 2016" instead of "Bao et al., 2016" (with a missing space and incorrect punctuation). The phrase "the the candidate e's entity mention" (Section 4.2) contains a clear typo. The phrase "falls to a local optima" (Section 3.2) should be "falls into a local optimum." These are basic errors that should have been caught during proofreading.

2. **Methodological Ambiguity**: The paper fails to clearly explain key technical aspects. The description of "initializing the relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence" (Section 3.2) is confusing and lacks detail. How exactly are these "final state representations" used for initialization? The paper also doesn't clearly differentiate between the two proposed ways of Hierarchical Residual Matching, making it difficult to understand the actual model architecture.

3. **Lack of Specific Evidence for Key Claims**: The authors claim their model "copes with the above mentioned problems" including zero-shot learning, but they don't provide specific results on unseen relations. While they mention "SimpleQuestions has 14% of the golden test relations not observed in golden training tuples," they don't show how their model performs specifically on these unseen relations. The statistical significance claims (e.g., "p < 0.001 and 0.01") are not fully supported with appropriate statistical tests for all comparisons.

4. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses "relation-level" and "word-level" interchangeably with "relation names" and "relation words" without clear definition. For example, in Table 1, they refer to "relation-level" but then describe it as "relation names" in the caption. This inconsistency creates confusion about what exactly is being compared.

5. **Missing Implementation Details**: The paper lacks important implementation details that would help reproduce the results. For instance, they mention "entity replacement" in Section 4.2 but don't explain how they handle cases where the entity mention is not found in the question text. The paper also doesn't specify how they handle relation chains longer than two relations, which is a significant limitation for complex KBQA tasks.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the SimpleQuestions data set has 14% of the golden test relations not observed in golden training tuples." Could you provide specific results showing how your model performs on these unseen relations compared to baseline models?

2. In Section 3.2, you mention "we initialize the relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence." Could you clarify exactly how this initialization is performed, including the mathematical formulation?

3. The paper states that "WebQSP benefits more from residual and deeper architecture, possibly because in this dataset it is more important to handle larger scope of context matching." What specific evidence do you have to support this claim beyond the reported accuracy improvements?

4. The authors mention "constraint detection is crucial for our system" but note that "in contrast to the conclusion in (Yih et al., 2015)." Could you elaborate on why Yih et al. (2015) reached a different conclusion and how your results differ?

## Overall Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents a reasonable approach to KB relation detection with some promising results, the numerous formatting inconsistencies, methodological ambiguities, and lack of specific evidence for key claims are significant enough to warrant rejection. The paper contains basic errors that should have been caught during the writing process, and the technical details provided are insufficient for understanding the model's exact architecture and training process. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance when these fundamental issues remain unaddressed. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to correct all formatting issues, clarify the methodological details, and provide more specific evidence to support their claims before reconsideration.
