

# ACL Review

## Summary

The paper proposes an improved neural relation detection model for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems, called HR-BiLSTM (Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM). The authors argue that KB relation detection differs significantly from general relation detection due to the large number of relations (thousands vs. hundreds), zero-shot learning requirements, and the need to handle relation chains. The model combines relation-level and word-level relation representations with hierarchical matching using residual learning. The authors claim their improved relation detection enables a simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results on both single-relation and multi-relation KBQA tasks.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. A thorough empirical evaluation highlighting the differences between general relation detection and KBQA-specific relation detection
2. A slight performance improvement in relation detection through the combination of existing techniques (hierarchical matching, residual learning, BiLSTMs)
3. A simple KBQA pipeline that incorporates relation detection with an entity re-ranking step

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of the KBQA relation detection gap**: The paper effectively articulates the key differences between general relation detection and KBQA-specific relation detection, particularly regarding the large number of relations, zero-shot learning requirements, and relation chains. This is a valid observation that helps frame the problem.

2. **Thorough ablation studies**: Table 2 provides comprehensive ablation results showing the contribution of each component of the model (hierarchical matching, residual learning, relation-level vs. word-level representations). This demonstrates careful analysis of the proposed method.

3. **Evaluation on standard datasets**: The paper evaluates on two well-established KBQA datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), allowing for meaningful comparison with prior work and providing evidence of the model's effectiveness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of significant novelty**: The core contribution (HR-BiLSTM) is a combination of existing techniques (hierarchical matching from Parikh et al., 2016; residual learning from He et al., 2016; BiLSTMs for sequence matching) without clear evidence that this specific combination is fundamentally novel or significantly better than existing approaches. The marginal performance improvements (2-3%) do not justify the claimed "state-of-the-art" status in a top-tier venue.

2. **Insufficient evidence for zero-shot learning claim**: The paper claims to handle zero-shot learning (14% of test relations in SimpleQuestions are unseen in training) but doesn't provide specific evidence showing how their model specifically handles these unseen relations better than baselines. The ablation studies don't isolate this capability.

3. **Marginal performance improvements**: The claimed improvements (2-3% on relation detection, 2-3% on KBQA end-task) are consistent with incremental work in the field, not groundbreaking contributions. In a field where small improvements are common, this doesn't rise to the level of a significant contribution for ACL.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "zero-shot learning" capability but doesn't provide specific evidence showing how the model handles unseen relations compared to baselines. Can you provide a detailed analysis of the model's performance on the unseen relations subset of SimpleQuestions?

2. Why is your specific combination of hierarchical matching with residual learning better than simply using a single-layer BiLSTM with both relation-level and word-level representations (which is the baseline in your Table 2)? What is the theoretical justification for the residual connections in this specific context?

3. How does your approach compare to using pre-trained relation embeddings (e.g., from TransE as in Dai et al., 2016) for handling large relation sets? Why is your approach better for KBQA than these established methods?

4. The entity re-ranking step (Section 4.1) seems to be a significant addition to the KBQA pipeline. Can you provide more justification for this step and explain how it differs from previous approaches to entity disambiguation in KBQA?

5. The paper claims "state-of-the-art" results, but the improvements are marginal. How do you justify this as a significant contribution to the field, especially when compared to previous work that has made similar incremental improvements?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-executed empirical study with thorough ablation analysis, but the contributions lack sufficient novelty and the performance improvements are marginal. The combination of existing techniques doesn't constitute a significant methodological advance, and the paper fails to provide compelling evidence for the claimed zero-shot learning capabilities. While the evaluation is thorough, the marginal gains don't justify the claim of "state-of-the-art" results for a top-tier venue like ACL. I would recommend rejection.

**Decision: Reject**
