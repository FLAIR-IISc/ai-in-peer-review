

# Review of "Improved Neural Relation Detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems. The authors identify three key challenges that make KB relation detection more difficult than general relation extraction: (1) the large number of relation types (thousands vs. hundreds), (2) the need for zero-shot learning capability, and (3) the need to detect relation chains for complex questions. The proposed model uses both relation-level (as single tokens) and word-level (as sequences) representations to enable hierarchical matching between questions and relations, with residual learning to facilitate training of deeper architectures.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. A hierarchical matching approach for KB relation detection using both relation-level and word-level representations
2. A residual learning method to enable effective hierarchical matching
3. A two-step relation detection approach for KBQA that includes entity re-ranking to improve overall system performance

## Strengths

1. The paper correctly identifies and articulates the key differences between general relation extraction and KB relation detection, which is a valuable contribution to the field. The three challenges (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, relation chains) are well-motivated and well-explained.

2. The experimental evaluation on two standard datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) is thorough and compares with multiple strong baselines. The results demonstrate consistent improvements over previous methods (2-3% absolute gain on relation detection tasks).

3. The ablation studies provide useful insights into the effectiveness of different components (hierarchical matching vs. single-layer, residual learning vs. attention, etc.), which helps validate the authors' design choices.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of significant novelty**: The core technical contribution of using residual learning for hierarchical matching is not novel. Residual networks (He et al., 2016) are well-established in computer vision, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its application to relation detection from prior work. The hierarchical matching concept is also used in other NLP tasks (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016 for NLI). The paper fails to clearly articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon existing hierarchical matching techniques.

2. **Inconsistent terminology and formatting issues**: The paper uses inconsistent terminology throughout (e.g., "relation detection" vs. "relation extraction" without clear distinction), which is confusing. There are multiple formatting inconsistencies:
   - Inconsistent citation styles (some with "et al." and some without)
   - Table 2 has unclear abbreviations ("w/o rel name words" should be explicitly defined)
   - Figure 1 caption refers to "Love Will Find a Way" as the answer, but the KB example shows it as the episode title, not the answer (the answer should be "Love Will Find a Way" as the episode title)
   - Inconsistent use of "KBQA" vs. "KB question answering" in the text

3. **Insufficient explanation of key design choices**: The paper doesn't adequately justify why certain design choices were made:
   - Why is replacing the candidate entity with "<e>" necessary? The paper mentions it "helps the model better distinguish the relative position of each word compared to the entity" but doesn't provide evidence or explanation for this claim.
   - The entity re-ranking step is claimed to help with entity linking ambiguities, but the paper doesn't provide statistics or examples to support the claim that entity linking is a bottleneck (e.g., what is the accuracy of the entity linker used?).

4. **Incomplete ablation study**: While the paper performs an ablation study, it doesn't clearly identify which component contributes most to the performance gain. The comparison between residual learning and attention (Table 2) shows residual learning is better, but the paper doesn't explain why. The "w/o residual learning" and "replacing residual with attention" rows are confusingly labeled, making it difficult to interpret the results.

5. **Lack of failure analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss any failure cases or limitations of the proposed approach. This is a significant omission in a paper that claims to make a substantial contribution to the field.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your hierarchical residual matching approach specifically differ from the hierarchical matching approaches used in prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016)? What specific novelty does your approach provide?

2. The paper claims that entity linking is a bottleneck in KBQA systems, citing a 72.7% top-1 accuracy on SimpleQuestions. However, this statistic is not contextualized - what is the accuracy of the entity linker used in prior work, and how does this compare to the accuracy achieved by your system after entity re-ranking? Could you provide more specific evidence for this claim?

3. The paper mentions that "the number of relations is usually not large" in general relation extraction tasks, but the ACE2005 dataset has 11/32 coarse/fine-grained relations. Could you clarify how this dataset size compares to the relation counts in KBQA (e.g., Freebase2M has 6,000+ relations)?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a reasonable approach with some empirical improvements, the lack of significant novelty, multiple formatting inconsistencies, and insufficient explanation of key design choices are substantial flaws. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find that the paper's minor methodological details and technical nuances do not meet the high standards required for publication at ACL. The paper's contribution is incremental rather than substantial, and the presentation issues undermine confidence in the work's quality.

The paper makes a modest improvement over prior work, but the lack of clear novelty and the numerous technical and presentation flaws outweigh the contribution. The authors would need to significantly revise the paper to clarify the novelty, provide better justification for key design choices, and address the formatting inconsistencies before it would be suitable for publication.
