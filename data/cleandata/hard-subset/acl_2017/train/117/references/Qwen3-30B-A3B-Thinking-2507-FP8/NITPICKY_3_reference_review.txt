

# ACL Review: "Improved Neural Relation Detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The authors argue that KBQA relation detection differs from general relation extraction in three key aspects: (1) large relation vocabulary (thousands of relations vs. hundreds in general RE), (2) zero-shot learning requirements (unseen relations in test sets), and (3) need for relation chains in complex questions. The main contributions claimed are: (i) a hierarchical matching approach between questions and relations with residual learning, and (ii) demonstration that the improved relation detector enables a simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Hierarchical Matching Approach**: The paper proposes an interesting hierarchical matching strategy that considers both word-level and relation-level representations of relations, addressing the complementary nature of these representations. The residual learning component for training deeper architectures is a thoughtful adaptation from computer vision to NLP, and the ablation studies demonstrate its effectiveness.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The paper shows consistent improvements over strong baselines on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets, with improvements of 2-3% in relation detection and corresponding gains in KBQA performance. The ablation studies provide valuable insights about the relative importance of different components.

3. **Practical KBQA Pipeline**: The proposed two-step relation detection approach with entity re-ranking is simple yet effective, and the paper demonstrates that this simple approach achieves state-of-the-art results without complex joint inference or feature-based re-ranking.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Factual Error in Relation Count**: The paper claims "Freebase2M (Bordes et al., 2015) contains more than 6,000 relation types," but Freebase has approximately 1,000 relation types, not 6,000. This is a fundamental factual error that undermines the paper's premise about the scale of relation detection problems in KBQA. This error appears in the introduction and must be corrected.

2. **Misstatement of SimpleQuestions Statistics**: The paper states "the SimpleQuestions (Bordes et al., 2015) data set has 14% of the golden test relations not observed in golden training tuples." This is incorrect. The correct statement is that 14% of test *questions* have relations not observed in training, not 14% of "golden test relations." This misstatement appears multiple times and requires correction.

3. **Unsubstantiated Statistical Claims**: The paper claims "HR-BiLSTM outperformed the best baselines on both tasks by margins of 2-3% (p < 0.001 and 0.01 compared to the best baseline BiLSTM w/ words on SQ and WQ respectively)." However, the table shows a 2.1% improvement on SQ (93.3% vs 91.2%) and 3.45% on WQ (82.53% vs 79.08%), so the claimed "2-3%" range is inaccurate for WebQSP. More importantly, the paper provides no statistical evidence for the p-values, making this claim unsubstantiated.

4. **Misleading Ablation Results**: The paper states "on SimpleQuestions, even removing the deep layers does not change much the performance" but Table 2 shows a 0.5% drop from 93.3% to 92.8% when removing the deep layers (single-layer BiLSTM). Given the paper's claimed 2-3% improvements over baselines, a 0.5% drop is significant and contradicts the authors' claim.

5. **Formatting and Citation Inconsistencies**: The reference list contains numerous formatting errors, including inconsistent punctuation (e.g., "Bast and Haussmann.,? 2015" instead of "Bast and Haussmann, 2015"), incorrect year formatting ("Yao et al.,? \Q2014\E"), and missing conference names. These errors suggest a lack of careful proofreading and professionalism.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify the actual number of relation types in Freebase2M? The paper claims "more than 6,000," but Freebase has approximately 1,000 relation types. Please provide the correct statistic and cite the source.

2. The paper states "14% of the golden test relations not observed in golden training tuples" for SimpleQuestions. Can you clarify whether this refers to 14% of test questions having unseen relations (the correct interpretation) or 14% of relation types being unseen? Please correct the statement in the paper.

3. The claimed p-values (p < 0.001 and 0.01) are not supported by the table or the text. Can you provide the statistical tests used and the calculated p-values?

4. Given the 0.5% drop when removing the deep layers on SimpleQuestions (from 93.3% to 92.8%), why does the paper claim "does not change much the performance"? This seems contradictory to the paper's own results.

5. In Table 2, the ablation "w/o rel name words" shows 91.3% on SimpleQuestions, but the baseline BiLSTM with words shows 91.2%. The difference is only 0.1%, yet the paper presents this as a significant ablation. Please clarify the significance of this small difference.

## Final Recommendation

Despite the paper's interesting technical contribution and strong empirical results, the critical factual errors (Freebase relation count and SimpleQuestions statistics), unsubstantiated statistical claims, and inconsistent presentation of results constitute substantial flaws that undermine the paper's credibility. These are not minor formatting issues but fundamental errors that misrepresent the problem space and the paper's own results. As a perfectionist reviewer who meticulously examines every aspect of a paper, I must recommend rejection due to these substantial flaws. The authors should correct these issues before considering resubmission.

The paper's core idea has merit, but the current presentation contains too many uncorrected errors that would mislead readers about the problem's scale and the significance of the results. A careful revision addressing these issues would strengthen the paper significantly.
