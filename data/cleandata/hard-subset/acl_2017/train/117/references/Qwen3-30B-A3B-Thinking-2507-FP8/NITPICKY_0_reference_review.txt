

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel neural relation detection model called HR-BiLSTM for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The authors argue that KBQA relation detection differs significantly from general relation extraction due to large relation vocabularies (thousands of relations vs. hundreds in general tasks), zero-shot learning requirements, and the need to predict relation chains. Their model uses hierarchical matching between questions and relations at both word-level and relation-level representations, with residual learning to facilitate training. They demonstrate that their improved relation detector leads to state-of-the-art KBQA performance on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets.

## Main Contributions
1. A hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for KBQA relation detection that matches questions to both word-level and relation-level representations.
2. A two-step KBQA system that uses entity re-ranking based on relation detection scores to improve performance.
3. Empirical demonstration that their relation detection model improves KBQA performance on both single-relation and multi-relation tasks.

## Strengths
1. The paper correctly identifies an important gap between general relation extraction and KBQA relation detection, particularly regarding the scale of relations (thousands vs. hundreds) and the zero-shot learning requirement. This is a meaningful contribution to the field.

2. The hierarchical matching approach with residual learning is well-motivated and supported by ablation studies (Table 2), which show clear improvements over baselines and component removals.

3. The paper provides a clear connection between the improved relation detection and the KBQA end-task performance, demonstrating practical utility beyond just relation detection.

## Weaknesses
1. **Inconsistent and confusing terminology throughout the paper**. The authors interchangeably use "relation detection" with "relation extraction" and "relation classification" without clarifying distinctions. For example:
   - "In the information extraction field such tasks are usually called relation extraction or relation classification" (Section 1)
   - "relation detection" is used consistently in the paper, but the terminology in the related work section is inconsistent with the main paper's terminology.
   - The paper should clearly define the relationship between these terms to avoid confusion for readers.

2. **Technical inconsistencies in model description**:
   - Section 3.2 states: "We transform each token above to its word embedding then use two BiLSTMs (with shared parameters) to get their hidden representations." This is confusing because "two BiLSTMs with shared parameters" would imply the same BiLSTM is used for both representations, but the model description suggests separate processing for word-level and relation-level representations.
   - The description of "initializing relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence" (Section 3.2) is unclear and technically problematic, as the word sequence is part of the relation representation.
   - In Figure 2 caption, they refer to "dotted arrows" for shortcut connections, but in the figure, these connections are shown as solid lines.

3. **Numerous formatting and grammatical errors** that undermine the paper's professionalism:
   - "the the candidate e's entity mention" (Section 4.2)
   - Inconsistent reference formatting throughout (e.g., "Bast and Haussmann.,? 2015" vs. "Bast and Haussmann, 2015")
   - Poorly formatted Table 1 (text run together without proper separation)
   - Inconsistent use of "relation detection" vs. "relation extraction" in the title and throughout the paper
   - "w/o rel name words" and "w/o rel words rel names" in Table 2 are confusingly phrased and should be clarified.

4. **Lack of clarity in experimental results**:
   - The paper claims "improves from 2 to 3% the KBQA end task" (Section 5.3) but doesn't provide the exact accuracy numbers for the baseline and proposed method.
   - The claim "the gap is small on WebQSP" (Section 5.2) lacks numerical support.
   - The improvement of "2-3%" (Section 5.2) is vague without stating the baseline accuracy.

5. **Minor but pervasive technical inconsistencies**:
   - In Figure 1(b), the relation chain is described as "starring roles-series" but this appears to be a single relation name, while the text suggests it should be two separate relations ("starring roles" and "series").
   - The paper states "For example in Figure 1(b), when doing only word-level matching, it is difficult to rank the target relation 'starring roles' higher compared to the incorrect relation 'plays produced'." However, "plays produced" doesn't appear to be a relation name in the figure.
   - The paper claims "the number of relations is usually not large" for general RE, but doesn't provide specific numbers to back this up.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Section 3.2, you mention "two BiLSTMs (with shared parameters)" for relation representation. Please clarify whether you are using separate BiLSTMs for word-level and relation-level representations, or if you're using the same BiLSTM for both (with shared parameters). The current description is confusing.

2. In Table 2, the ablation studies include "w/o rel name words" and "w/o rel words rel names." Please provide clear definitions for these abbreviations to avoid confusion.

3. In Section 5.3, you claim "improves from 2 to 3% the KBQA end task." Please provide the exact accuracy numbers for the baseline and your model to make this claim concrete.

4. In Figure 1(b), the relation chain is described as "starring roles-series," but this appears to be a single relation name. Is "starring roles-series" intended to be a single relation name, or two separate relations ("starring roles" and "series")? Please clarify.

5. The paper uses inconsistent reference formatting throughout (e.g., "Bast and Haussmann.,? 2015" vs. "Bast and Haussmann, 2015"). Please ensure consistent reference formatting for the final version.

## Final Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper identifies a meaningful gap between general relation extraction and KBQA relation detection and proposes a technically interesting model, the numerous minor flaws throughout the paper - including inconsistent terminology, technical inconsistencies in model description, formatting errors, and unclear presentation of results - significantly undermine the paper's quality. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these issues are substantial enough to warrant rejection, even though the core idea has merit. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to address these issues before resubmission.

The paper's contribution is potentially valuable, but the current presentation is too flawed to be considered for publication. The numerous small errors and inconsistencies suggest a lack of careful proofreading and attention to detail, which is particularly problematic for a venue like ACL.
