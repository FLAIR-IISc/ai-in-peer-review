

# ACL Peer Review

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes an improved neural relation detection model for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) called Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM). The authors argue that relation detection for KBQA differs significantly from general relation extraction tasks due to the large number of relation types (thousands in KBs vs. hundreds in general tasks), the need for zero-shot learning (unseen relations), and the requirement to handle relation chains in complex questions. Their approach combines word-level and relation-level representations of relations, uses deep bidirectional LSTMs to learn hierarchical question representations, and employs residual learning for more effective hierarchical matching. The authors demonstrate that this improved relation detection enables a simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) tasks.

## Main Contributions

1. **The HR-BiLSTM model** with hierarchical residual matching that effectively combines word-level and relation-level representations for KB relation detection.
2. **A thorough empirical demonstration** that improved relation detection leads to better KBQA performance, with significant gains over previous state-of-the-art methods on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets.
3. **A simple yet effective KBQA pipeline** that incorporates the improved relation detection with an entity re-ranking step, showing that relation detection can be a key component in improving KBQA systems.

## Strengths

1. **The paper correctly identifies and addresses a significant gap in KBQA research.** The authors clearly articulate the differences between general relation extraction and KBQA-specific relation detection (large number of relations, zero-shot learning, relation chains), which is a crucial contribution. Previous work often treats KBQA as a general relation extraction task, but this paper properly frames the problem as one requiring specialized solutions. The motivation is strong and well-justified.

2. **The HR-BiLSTM model is well-motivated and thoroughly evaluated.** The authors provide a compelling rationale for each component of their model (hierarchical matching, residual learning), and the ablation studies (Table 2) convincingly show that each component contributes to the performance gains. The model outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods by 2-3% on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP datasets, with statistical significance (p < 0.001 on SimpleQuestions). This is a meaningful improvement in the field.

3. **The paper demonstrates the end-to-end impact of improved relation detection on KBQA.** Unlike many prior works that focus solely on relation detection, this paper shows how their improved relation detector leads to better overall KBQA performance. The results in Table 3 show that their approach improves KBQA performance by 2-3% over baseline relation detectors, even without complex joint inference or feature-based re-ranking. This is an important contribution to the KBQA community.

## Weaknesses

1. **The novelty of the approach is somewhat limited.** While the HR-BiLSTM model is well-executed, the core ideas (hierarchical matching, residual learning) are not entirely novel. Hierarchical matching is similar to other attention-based approaches (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), and residual learning is borrowed from computer vision (He et al., 2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how their specific implementation of these ideas is novel for KBQA relation detection. The most significant contribution is the empirical demonstration of effectiveness, not the model architecture itself.

2. **The paper lacks sufficient analysis of why the model performs better on WebQSP than SimpleQuestions.** The authors note that residual learning helps more on WebQSP (82.53% vs. 80.65% in ablation), but don't provide a detailed analysis of why this difference exists. Given that WebQSP involves multi-relation chains while SimpleQuestions is single-relation, understanding this difference could provide valuable insights about the model's strengths and limitations. This is a missed opportunity for deeper analysis.

3. **Computational complexity is not adequately addressed.** The HR-BiLSTM model is deeper and more complex than previous approaches (e.g., single-layer BiLSTM), but the paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of this model. How much slower is training and inference compared to simpler models? How does this affect scalability in real-world KBQA systems? Without this information, it's difficult to assess the practical impact of the proposed model.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that residual learning helps more on WebQSP than on SimpleQuestions. Can you provide more detailed analysis on why this difference exists? Is it because WebQSP requires handling longer dependency chains, or is there another factor?

2. The entity re-ranking step is described as a key contribution, but the paper doesn't show how much it improves entity linking accuracy. Could you provide more specific numbers on how much the re-ranking step improves the top-1 entity linking accuracy compared to using only the initial entity linker?

3. How does the computational complexity of HR-BiLSTM compare to previous models (e.g., single-layer BiLSTM or BiCNN)? Specifically, what are the training and inference time differences, and how do these impact the practical deployment of the model in real-world KBQA systems?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a meaningful contribution to KBQA research by properly addressing the unique challenges of relation detection in this domain and demonstrating that improved relation detection leads to better KBQA performance. The empirical results are strong and well-supported, and the paper is well-written and well-structured. While the novelty of the model architecture is somewhat limited, the thorough evaluation and clear demonstration of the end-to-end impact on KBQA make this a valuable contribution to the field. With the authors addressing the questions above in their response, this paper would make a strong contribution to ACL.
