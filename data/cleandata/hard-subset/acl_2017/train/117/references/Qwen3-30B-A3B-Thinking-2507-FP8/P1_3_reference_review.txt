

# Peer Review for ACL

## Summary

This paper addresses a key challenge in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA): relation detection. The authors identify three critical differences between general relation extraction and KB-specific relation detection: (1) the large number of relation types (thousands vs. hundreds), (2) the zero-shot learning requirement (unseen relations in test data), and (3) the need for chain relation detection in complex questions. They propose a Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that performs hierarchical matching between questions and relations at both word-level and relation-level representations, using residual learning to enable effective hierarchical matching.

The main contributions are: (1) an improved relation detection model addressing KBQA-specific challenges; (2) demonstration that this model enables a simple KBQA system to achieve state-of-the-art results; and (3) an entity re-ranking step that leverages relation detection to improve entity disambiguation.

## Main Contributions

1. **HR-BiLSTM model for hierarchical matching with residual learning** - This is the most significant contribution, addressing the core challenge of KB relation detection through a novel architecture that effectively handles both word-level and relation-level representations.

2. **Demonstration of end-task benefit** - The authors successfully show that their improved relation detection model directly translates to better KBQA performance, a crucial contribution that many NLP papers fail to demonstrate.

3. **Entity re-ranking approach** - The novel use of relation detection scores to improve entity disambiguation in KBQA systems represents a practical and effective contribution to the field.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and problem identification** - The authors correctly identify the key differences between general relation extraction and KB relation detection, which is essential for justifying their approach. Their analysis of the three main challenges (large relation vocabulary, zero-shot learning, and chain relations) is thorough and well-supported.

2. **Clear experimental results with strong statistical significance** - The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over state-of-the-art baselines on both relation detection tasks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) with statistically significant gains (2-3% on both tasks, p<0.001 and p<0.01). The KBQA end-task results also show meaningful improvements (2-3%) over previous work, demonstrating the practical value of their approach.

3. **Thorough ablation studies** - The ablation tests in Table 2 provide strong evidence for each component of their model. The authors systematically test the necessity of hierarchical matching, residual learning, and the different representation levels, which significantly strengthens their claims.

4. **Clear demonstration of end-task benefit** - Unlike many papers that focus solely on intermediate task improvements, the authors show how their relation detection improvements translate to better KBQA performance, which is a major strength for the KBQA community.

5. **Well-structured paper with clear technical contributions** - The paper is well-organized, with clear descriptions of the problem, proposed solution, and experimental evaluation. The figures (especially Figure 2) effectively illustrate the model architecture.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited analysis of zero-shot learning performance** - While the paper identifies zero-shot learning as a key challenge (SimpleQuestions has 14% unseen test relations), it doesn't provide specific performance metrics on these unseen relations. A comparison of performance on seen vs. unseen relations would strengthen the paper's claims about handling zero-shot scenarios.

2. **Lack of computational complexity analysis** - The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the HR-BiLSTM model compared to simpler baselines. Given that KBs can contain thousands of relations, understanding how the model scales with relation vocabulary size is important for practical deployment.

3. **Insufficient discussion of failure cases** - The paper doesn't analyze failure cases or provide examples where the model fails. Understanding the limitations would help future work and provide a more balanced view of the approach's effectiveness.

4. **Entity re-ranking formula lacks justification** - The specific formula for entity re-ranking (srerank(e; q) = α · slinker(e; q) + (1-α) · max_{r∈Rlq∩Re} srel(r; q)) is used without sufficient justification. The paper doesn't explore why this weighted combination is better than simpler alternatives (e.g., using only relation scores or a different weighting scheme).

5. **Comparison to more recent work** - The paper compares to several strong baselines, but doesn't include comparisons to more recent approaches like "Neural Symbolic Machines" (Liang et al., 2016) or other end-to-end KBQA systems published after the cited works.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper identifies zero-shot learning as a key challenge, yet doesn't provide specific performance metrics on unseen relations. Could you provide results on how your model performs specifically on the 14% of unseen relations in SimpleQuestions?

2. How did you determine the optimal value of α in the entity re-ranking formula? Did you perform a grid search over α, and how sensitive is the performance to this parameter?

3. Could you provide more details about the computational complexity of your model compared to baselines, particularly in terms of training time and inference time as the relation vocabulary size increases?

4. Could you share some examples of failure cases for your model, particularly where the entity re-ranking step failed to correctly disambiguate entities?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the KBQA field by addressing a key challenge with a well-motivated and effective solution. The hierarchical matching approach with residual learning is novel and well-justified, and the demonstration of end-task benefits is particularly valuable. While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of zero-shot analysis and computational considerations, these are relatively minor compared to the strength of the core technical contribution and experimental results. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions above.
