

# Review for "Improved Neural Relation Detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) systems. The authors argue that KBQA relation detection differs significantly from general relation extraction due to three key challenges: (1) the large number of relation types in KBs (thousands vs. hundreds in general tasks), (2) the zero-shot learning requirement for unseen relations, and (3) the need to handle relation chains in complex questions.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. **A novel Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for KB relation detection** that addresses the specific challenges of KBQA by combining relation-level and word-level representations with hierarchical matching and residual learning. This is the primary technical contribution.

2. **Demonstration of the end-to-end benefits of improved relation detection** for KBQA, showing that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on both relation detection and KBQA tasks without requiring complex additional components.

3. **Clear articulation of the specific challenges of KB relation detection** compared to general relation extraction, providing valuable insights for the community.

## Strengths

1. **Strong problem statement and clear articulation of the challenges**: The paper effectively highlights the differences between general relation extraction and KB-specific relation detection. The three points about the challenges (large number of relations, zero-shot learning, relation chains) are well-justified and clearly explained with concrete examples (Fig 1). This provides a solid foundation for why the authors' approach is necessary.

2. **Well-motivated and well-executed model architecture**: The HR-BiLSTM model is a thoughtful extension of existing sequence matching approaches. The hierarchical matching with residual learning is particularly well-motivated to address specific training challenges mentioned in the paper. The ablation studies in Table 2 provide strong empirical evidence for the necessity of each component (hierarchical matching, residual learning, word-level vs. relation-level representations).

3. **Significant and consistent empirical improvements**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on both relation detection tasks (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP) with statistically significant improvements over previous methods (2-3% absolute gains, p<0.001 for SQ). The KBQA results (Table 3) show meaningful improvements (2-3%) over baselines without requiring complex additional components, demonstrating the practical value of the improved relation detection.

4. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is well-written with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The examples (Fig 1, Table 1) are helpful for understanding the key concepts. The ablation studies are thorough and provide valuable insights into what components of the model are most important.

5. **Practical contribution to KBQA systems**: The paper demonstrates that their relation detection model can be integrated into a simple KBQA pipeline with meaningful improvements, making the approach accessible and practical for others to implement without requiring complex additional components.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to existing techniques**: While the HR-BiLSTM model is well-constructed, some of its components (hierarchical matching, residual learning) have been used in other NLP tasks before. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from prior work that has used similar techniques (e.g., the hierarchical matching concept is somewhat similar to attention mechanisms used in Parikh et al., 2016). The authors should more clearly articulate what is novel about their specific implementation and why it works better for KBQA specifically.

2. **Insufficient analysis of why residual learning works**: The paper demonstrates that residual learning improves performance but doesn't provide a deep analysis of why it works better. The explanation in Section 3.2 is somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis of the training dynamics or visualization of the learned representations would strengthen the paper significantly.

3. **Lack of failure mode analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the limitations of their approach or provide examples of cases where their model fails. Understanding the failure modes would be valuable for the community to know the boundaries of the approach and for guiding future work.

4. **Comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares to methods like AMPCNN (Yin et al., 2016) and BiCNN (Yih et al., 2015), but these are somewhat dated. The paper should compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods for relation extraction or KBQA, such as BERT-based approaches that have become standard in the field.

5. **No computational efficiency analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity or inference time of their model compared to baselines. This is important for practical deployment of the model in real-world KBQA systems, as the paper claims it's a simple pipeline.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that SimpleQuestions suffers more from unseen relations than WebQSP. Can you provide more specific analysis on how the distribution of seen vs. unseen relations affects model performance across the two datasets? Specifically, how much of the performance gap between your model and baselines can be attributed to handling unseen relations?

2. In the ablation study (Table 2), the "w/o residual learning" model shows a smaller performance drop on SimpleQuestions compared to WebQSP. Can you provide more insight into why residual learning is more beneficial for WebQSP? Is this due to the larger relation chains in WebQSP requiring deeper representations?

3. The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results on KBQA, but the comparison is limited to two baselines (STAGG and AMPCNN). How would your approach perform compared to more recent systems that use joint inference or feature-based re-ranking (e.g., the recent work by Zhang et al., 2021)? Would your relation detection model still provide the same relative improvement?

4. The paper uses a two-step relation detection approach (entity re-ranking followed by relation detection). How would you expect your model to perform in a one-step approach (i.e., directly predicting relations for entities without the re-ranking step)? What would be the trade-offs?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a meaningful contribution to the KBQA field. However, to strengthen the paper for ACL, I would recommend:

1. Adding a more detailed comparison to recent BERT-based relation extraction approaches (e.g., Zhang et al., 2021) to demonstrate the relevance of their approach in the current landscape.

2. Including a brief analysis of the computational complexity and inference time of the HR-BiLSTM model compared to baselines.

3. Adding a section discussing the limitations of the approach and providing examples of failure cases to help readers understand the boundaries of the method.

4. Expanding the discussion of the residual learning component to provide deeper theoretical justification beyond the empirical results.

Overall, this is a strong paper with meaningful contributions to the KBQA field. With the suggested improvements, it would be a valuable addition to ACL.
