This paper presents a novel method for mapping natural language text to relations in a Knowledge Base, a critical task for Question Answering. The proposed approach is designed to handle scenarios with a large number of candidate relations and incomplete information about the query's arguments. It achieves this by combining a word-level representation with a token-level representation for relations. The empirical results show an improvement over state-of-the-art methods. While the work is promising, its claims would be strengthened by a more extensive evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper addresses the relevant and challenging problem of mapping natural language questions to Knowledge Base (KB) relations. It specifically focuses on the Question Answering (QA) setting, which is characterized by two key difficulties: having only partial information for one of the arguments and navigating a very large space of potential target relations.
*   The core proposal is a novel method that combines two distinct representations of the input. One representation operates at the word level, segmenting both the input text and the target relation names. The other treats each relation as a single, unsegmented token. This hybrid approach is a well-motivated contribution.
*   A significant contribution of this work to the QA pipeline is its ability to effectively re-rank candidate entities following the initial Entity Linking step, which is a crucial component for improving overall accuracy.
*   The experimental results are promising, demonstrating a clear improvement over current state-of-the-art methods on the reported dataset.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The primary weakness of the current work is the limited scope of its evaluation. The approach has been evaluated on a single dataset, which may not be sufficient to fully demonstrate the generalizability of the findings. Evaluating the method on additional and more diverse datasets would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   For improved clarity and structure, the authors might consider reorganizing Section 3. Currently, Section 3.1 seems to describe a baseline or existing model, which would be more appropriately placed within the Related Work section.
*   This change would allow the core proposal, currently in Section 3.2, to become the new Section 3. This new, consolidated section describing the proposed method could then be subdivided more logically to better delineate its different components, enhancing readability.
