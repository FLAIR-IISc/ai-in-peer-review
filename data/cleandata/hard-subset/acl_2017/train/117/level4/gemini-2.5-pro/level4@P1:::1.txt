### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new neural model for relation detection in the context of Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The core of the contribution is a Hierarchical Residual Bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model. This model is designed to address the challenges of KBQA relation detection, such as the large number of relations and the presence of unseen relations at test time. It achieves this by matching a multi-level representation of the input question against a multi-level representation of candidate relations. Specifically, relations are represented at both the word-level (tokens from the relation name) and the relation-level (the entire relation name as a single token). The model uses a deep BiLSTM with residual connections to create hierarchical question representations to match these different relation granularities.

The authors also present a simple but effective KBQA system that integrates this improved relation detector. A key feature of this system is a two-step process where the relation detector is first used to re-rank entity candidates from an initial entity linker, thereby reducing ambiguity. After re-ranking, the model is used again to identify the correct relation for the top-ranked entities. The paper demonstrates that this approach achieves state-of-the-art results on both the single-relation SimpleQuestions dataset and the multi-relation WebQSP dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Effective Relation Detection Model:** The paper introduces a well-motivated HR-BiLSTM model that effectively tackles the problem of matching natural language questions to a very large set of KB relations. The core idea of creating hierarchical representations for both the question and the relation (at word and relation-name granularities) is compelling. The use of residual connections to facilitate the training of this deep matching architecture is a clever application of a recent technique to this problem. The strong results on the standalone relation detection tasks (+2-3% over strong baselines) validate the effectiveness of this architecture.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance on KBQA Benchmarks:** The ultimate goal of the work is to improve KBQA, and the paper convincingly demonstrates this. By integrating the proposed relation detector, the authors' relatively simple KBQA pipeline achieves state-of-the-art results on two standard benchmarks: SimpleQuestions (a single-relation task) and WebQSP (a multi-relation task). This shows the robustness of the approach across different types of question complexity.

3.  **Insightful System Design with Entity Re-ranking:** A significant contribution to the KBQA system is the introduction of an entity re-ranking step powered by the relation detection model. This is an elegant way to address the common problem of entity linking ambiguity, which is often a bottleneck in KBQA systems. By using preliminary relation detection to disambiguate entities, the system creates a synergistic loop where better relation detection improves entity linking, which in turn provides a better basis for final relation detection. The ablation study confirms that this component is crucial for the system's high performance.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The approach has been evaluated on two standard benchmarks, SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. While these are important datasets, demonstrating the model's effectiveness on a wider variety of KBQA datasets could further strengthen the paper's claims. For instance, testing on datasets with different KB schemas or linguistic properties would provide more evidence of the model's generalizability.

2.  **Clarity of Presentation in Section 3:** The organization of the methodology section could be improved. Section 3.1 ("Different Granularity in KB Relations") serves primarily as motivation by outlining the pros and cons of different relation representations. This content might fit more naturally within the Related Work section (Section 2) or as a preamble to the model description. This would allow Section 3 to be a more focused and streamlined presentation of the proposed HR-BiLSTM model, which could then be broken down into clearer subsections (e.g., Hierarchical Representations, Residual Matching, Training Objective).
