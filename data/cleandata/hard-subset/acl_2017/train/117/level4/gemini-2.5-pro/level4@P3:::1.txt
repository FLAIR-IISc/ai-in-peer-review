**Summary**

This paper presents a novel neural network model, the Hierarchical Residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM), for relation detection in the context of Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA). The core idea is to perform a hierarchical match between a question and a candidate relation. This is achieved by representing the question with a deep, two-layer BiLSTM and representing the relation at two levels of granularity: as a sequence of words and as a single, atomic token. Residual connections are used to effectively combine the different layers of the question representation for matching against the relation representation. The authors integrate this improved relation detector into a simple yet effective KBQA pipeline. A key aspect of this pipeline is a two-step application of the relation detector: first, to re-rank entity candidates from an initial linker, and second, to identify the final relation for the top-ranked entity. The proposed system achieves new state-of-the-art results on both the relation detection subtask and the end-to-end KBQA task for the SimpleQuestions and WebQSP benchmarks.

**Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Hierarchical Matching Model for Relation Detection:** The primary contribution is the HR-BiLSTM model. Its novelty lies in the sophisticated way it matches questions and relations. By representing relations at both word and whole-phrase levels and using a deep residual BiLSTM to create multi-level question representations, the model can capture both fine-grained lexical overlap and more abstract semantic correspondence. The application of residual connections to facilitate this hierarchical matching is a clever technique to improve the training of the deep architecture.
2.  **An Effective and Simple KBQA Pipeline:** The paper demonstrates how a powerful relation detector can be leveraged to build a high-performing KBQA system. The proposed two-step process, where the relation detector first helps disambiguate entities before selecting the final relation, is an elegant method to address the entity linking bottleneck and shows a strong synergy between the two components.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The submission provides strong empirical evidence of its effectiveness, establishing new state-of-the-art results on two standard but distinct benchmarks: SimpleQuestions (single-relation) and WebQSP (multi-relation). This demonstrates the model's robustness and its ability to generalize across different types of KBQA tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Effective Model Architecture:** The motivation for hierarchical matching is very clear. The authors correctly identify the trade-offs between word-level relation representations (good for generalization and unseen relations) and relation-level representations (good for capturing global semantics but suffering from data sparsity). The HR-BiLSTM architecture is a principled approach to combine the benefits of both. The use of residual connections is well-justified, and the ablation studies in Table 2 convincingly show that both the hierarchical representations and the residual learning contribute significantly to the model's success, particularly on the more complex WebQSP dataset.
2.  **Impressive Performance Across Multiple Tasks:** The paper demonstrates strong performance not only on the core relation detection task but also on the end-to-end KBQA task. Achieving state-of-the-art on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP is a significant accomplishment, as these datasets test different capabilities (handling a large number of unseen relations vs. handling relation chains). This shows the general applicability and power of the proposed methods.
3.  **Practical Insight on Improving KBQA Systems:** The entity re-ranking step is a simple but highly effective contribution. It directly tackles a well-known challenge in pipeline-based KBQA systems: the ambiguity and errors from the initial entity linking stage. By showing that a relation detector can be used to "globally" score entities based on the relations they are connected to in the KB, the authors provide a valuable and easily implementable technique for improving KBQA systems. The ablation study in Table 3 clearly shows the large impact of this re-ranking step.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Novelty of Components:** While the proposed *combination* of techniques is novel and effective for this task, the core architectural components themselves—BiLSTMs and residual connections—are well-established in the literature. Residual networks, in particular, are borrowed from computer vision. The work is therefore more of a successful and clever application of existing deep learning paradigms to the KBQA domain rather than a fundamental methodological breakthrough.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Model Behavior:** The paper provides strong quantitative results but lacks a deeper qualitative analysis of *why* the model works. For instance, the claim that the second BiLSTM layer learns "more general and abstract information" is intuitive but not empirically demonstrated. The paper would be strengthened by including qualitative examples or visualizations that provide insight into what the different layers of the hierarchy are learning and on which types of questions the residual architecture provides the biggest gains.
3.  **Potential for Error Propagation in Pipeline:** The KBQA system is a multi-stage pipeline (entity linking, re-ranking, relation detection, constraint detection). While simple and effective, this design is susceptible to error propagation. For example, an error in the entity re-ranking step cannot be recovered from later in the pipeline. The authors acknowledge this briefly in footnote 5. A discussion of this limitation and a comparison with more integrated, joint-inference models would make the work more complete.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance improvement from residual learning is substantially larger on WebQSP than on SimpleQuestions (Table 2). The paper hypothesizes this is due to WebQSP requiring the handling of a "larger scope of context matching." Could you elaborate on this? Are there specific linguistic structures or relation chain patterns in WebQSP that particularly benefit from the deeper, residual architecture?
2.  Could you provide any qualitative analysis to support the intuition that the second BiLSTM layer captures more abstract representations? For example, showing a few examples where the first-layer matching fails but the second-layer matching (i.e., the residual) succeeds could be very illustrative.
3.  Your KBQA pipeline makes hard decisions at each step (e.g., selecting the top-K' entities). Have you considered using a beam search approach to maintain a set of diverse hypotheses (e.g., multiple entity-relation pairs) throughout the pipeline to mitigate the risk of early-stage errors?

**Additional Comments**

*   The discussion in Section 3.1, which motivates the need for different relation granularities, could potentially be integrated more smoothly into the introduction or related work, making Section 3 more focused on the proposed model itself. This is a minor suggestion regarding presentation.
