

Summary: The paper proposes a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model for relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), addressing key challenges including large-scale relations (thousands vs. hundreds in general relation extraction), zero-shot learning requirements, and multi-relation chains. The main contributions are: (1) a hierarchical matching approach combining word-level and relation-level representations for better question-relation alignment; (2) residual learning that enables deeper hierarchical matching; and (3) a simple KBQA system with an entity re-ranking step that significantly improves performance.

Strengths:
1. The hierarchical matching approach is well-motivated and effectively addresses KBQA's unique challenges. The paper demonstrates that using only word-level or relation-level representations leads to significant performance drops (e.g., 91.2% vs 88.9% on SimpleQuestions), while combining both achieves state-of-the-art results (93.3% on SimpleQuestions, 82.53% on WebQSP). The authors provide clear explanations for why this works: word-level captures local phrase matching (e.g., "written" matching "was written by"), while relation-level captures global semantic matching (e.g., "episodes written" matching "what episodes were written by"). This dual-representation strategy is particularly valuable for handling unseen relations in zero-shot learning scenarios.

2. The residual learning mechanism for hierarchical matching is a significant technical contribution that enables deeper, more effective representation learning. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate that residual connections substantially improve performance compared to alternatives like weighted sum of layers (82.53% vs 80.65% on WebQSP) or attention-based methods (82.53% vs 81.38%). The paper shows that residual learning is especially crucial for WebQSP, where it enables the model to handle larger context matching requirements, while still providing benefits for SimpleQuestions.

3. The entity re-ranking step is a novel and practical contribution that effectively addresses a key bottleneck in KBQA systems. By using relation detection to re-rank entities from the initial entity linker, the system better handles entity ambiguities (e.g., distinguishing "Mike Kelley" the TV writer from the baseball player). The paper demonstrates this step alone leads to a 2-3% improvement in KBQA accuracy, and the authors provide a clear explanation for why it works: different entities connect to different relations, so high-scoring relations help disambiguate entities. This approach is simple yet effective, requiring minimal modifications to existing KBQA pipelines.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation is limited to two Freebase-based datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), which share similar characteristics. While these are standard benchmarks, the paper does not evaluate on alternative KBs or more diverse question types. It would be valuable to see how the model performs on datasets with different relation distributions, more complex question structures, or non-Freebase KBs to better establish the generalizability of the approach.

2. The paper does not thoroughly analyze the computational complexity of the proposed model. Given that HR-BiLSTM uses deep residual connections and hierarchical matching, it likely has higher computational requirements than simpler models. The authors should discuss this trade-off, particularly since real-world KBQA systems often have strict latency constraints. A comparison of inference time versus accuracy would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

3. The ablation study on the entity re-ranking step is limited in scope. While the paper shows removing this step leads to performance drops, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of when and why entity re-ranking is most beneficial. For instance, how does the effectiveness of this step vary with different levels of entity ambiguity in the dataset? This would help readers better understand the practical applicability and limitations of the approach.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more details on the computational cost of your HR-BiLSTM model compared to previous approaches, particularly in terms of inference time and memory requirements?

2. How does the entity re-ranking step perform on questions with varying levels of entity ambiguity? Would you expect it to be less effective for questions where entities are unambiguous?

3. Have you considered extending your hierarchical matching approach to handle more complex KB structures, such as those with nested relations or more elaborate constraint patterns?
