

**Strengths:**  
This paper tackles a significant challenge in open-domain question answering: learning robust mappings between natural language queries and knowledge base (KB) relations, particularly under realistic constraints of partial input information and high cardinality of target relations. The proposed method—combining two complementary text representations—is well-motivated and addresses a critical gap. Specifically, the integration of a word-level representation (with segmentation of target relation names and input text) alongside a relation-as-a-single-token representation (unsegmented) provides a nuanced way to capture both lexical and structural patterns. Crucially, the work demonstrates that this dual representation enables effective post-Entity Linking re-ranking of candidate entities, a contribution that directly enhances QA accuracy without requiring major pipeline modifications. Empirical results on WebQuestions show consistent improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art models, validating the approach’s practical utility. The method’s simplicity and effectiveness make it a compelling addition to the QA toolkit.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary limitation is the evaluation’s scope. The model is assessed solely on WebQuestions, a single dataset with relatively constrained relation coverage. While WebQuestions is a standard benchmark, the lack of multi-dataset validation (e.g., using FreeBase or SimpleQuestions) weakens claims about generalizability. For instance, performance on datasets with more complex relations or diverse question phrasing might differ significantly. Without broader empirical validation, the reported gains remain suggestive rather than conclusive. This narrow evaluation strategy also limits the ability to assess whether the method’s benefits are specific to WebQuestions’ characteristics or more widely applicable.

**General Discussion:**  
The paper’s structure would benefit from a minor reorganization to enhance clarity and logical flow. Section 3.1, which describes the dual representation framework, should be relocated to the Related Work section (e.g., as a discussion of prior segmentation approaches). This would allow Section 3 to focus exclusively on the *proposed* method, with subsections clearly delineating the model architecture, re-ranking mechanism, and training protocol. Currently, the current structure conflates background discussion with the core contribution, making it harder for readers to distinguish the novelty. Moving Section 3.1 to Related Work would streamline the narrative, positioning the proposed method as a direct response to gaps identified in the literature. Additionally, the authors should explicitly clarify how the unsegmented relation representation (e.g., treating "located_in" as a single token) interacts with the segmentation-based approach during inference—this detail is currently underdeveloped.

**Recommendation:**  
This paper presents a timely and technically sound contribution to QA with strong empirical support on a standard benchmark. The dual-representation strategy for relation mapping is novel and practical, and the post-Entity Linking re-ranking component is particularly valuable for real-world systems. However, the limited evaluation scope is a notable drawback that must be addressed before acceptance. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on:  
1. Expanding evaluation to at least one additional dataset (e.g., SimpleQuestions) to demonstrate broader applicability.  
2. Restructuring Sections 3.1 and 3 as advised to sharpen the presentation of the core contribution.  
3. Clarifying the interaction between segmented and unsegmented representations in the method description.  

The work’s strengths—relevance, simplicity, and measurable gains—outweigh its current weaknesses, especially with these targeted revisions. It would be a valuable addition to ACL 2017.
