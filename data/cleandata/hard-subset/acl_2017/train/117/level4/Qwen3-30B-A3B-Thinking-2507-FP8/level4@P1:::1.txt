

Summary: The paper proposes a hierarchical recurrent neural network enhanced by residual learning for KB relation detection, a core component of KBQA systems. The authors claim two main contributions: (1) an improved relation detection model using hierarchical matching with residual learning, and (2) a simple KBQA system that demonstrates how improved relation detection can significantly boost end-task performance.

Strengths:
1. The paper effectively addresses a critical gap in KBQA research: relation detection for KBQA differs significantly from general relation extraction due to the large number of relations (thousands vs. hundreds), zero-shot learning requirements, and the need to handle relation chains. The authors clearly articulate these differences and position their work to address them.

2. The proposed hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model is well-motivated and effectively combines two complementary representations of relations (word-level and relation-level). The model's use of residual learning to facilitate hierarchical matching between question representations and relation representations is a strong technical contribution that directly addresses the challenge of training deep matching networks.

3. The paper demonstrates the practical impact of improved relation detection through a simple yet effective KBQA system that includes an entity re-ranking step. This step leverages relation detection to resolve entity ambiguities from initial entity linking, which is a significant practical contribution. The results show that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP benchmarks without complex feature engineering or joint inference.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation is limited to two datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), both based on Freebase. While these are standard benchmarks, the authors would benefit from testing on additional KBQA datasets to better establish the generalizability of their approach.

2. The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of failure cases, particularly for the entity re-ranking step. Understanding when and why the entity re-ranking fails would provide valuable insights into the model's limitations and potential improvements.

3. The ablation studies are comprehensive but could better isolate the relative contribution of each component. For instance, it's not entirely clear how much of the improvement over baselines is due to hierarchical matching versus residual learning specifically.

General Discussion:
The paper would benefit from reorganizing Section 3.1 (Different Granularity in KB Relations) to appear in the Related Work section, where it would better contextualize the proposed approach within existing literature. This would allow Section 3.2 (Hierarchical Sequence Matching with Residual Learning) to become the main contribution section (Section 3), with a clearer presentation of the proposed model. The current organization makes it somewhat difficult to distinguish what is new and what is background.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more details on the entity re-ranking step, particularly how it handles questions with multiple possible topic entities? How does the system select the most appropriate topic entity when multiple entities could be relevant?

2. The paper mentions that entity linking is a bottleneck in KBQA systems. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges with entity linking in the SimpleQuestions dataset that motivated your approach?

3. Given that your model relies on relation names as input, how would it handle cases where relation names are ambiguous or contain multiple meanings?
