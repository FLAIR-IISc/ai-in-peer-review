

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses the critical challenge of relation detection in Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA), where standard relation extraction methods fall short due to the large number of relations (thousands), zero-shot learning requirements, and multi-relation chains. The authors propose a hierarchical residual bidirectional LSTM (HR-BiLSTM) model that improves relation detection through two key innovations: (1) hierarchical matching between questions and relations at both word-level (fine-grained) and relation-level (coarse-grained) abstractions, and (2) residual learning to enable deeper, more effective sequence matching. Crucially, they demonstrate how their improved relation detector directly enhances KBQA performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on both single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) benchmarks.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel hierarchical matching framework**: The paper effectively addresses the core challenge of KB relation detection by combining word-level and relation-level representations. The motivation is well-justified—relation names as single tokens suffer from data sparsity (e.g., unseen relations), while word-level representations lack global context. Their hierarchical approach (e.g., matching "episodes written" to both "episodes" and "written" while preserving the relation's semantic whole) resolves this tension. Ablation studies confirm that both levels are necessary, with the combined model outperforming single-level baselines by 2–3% on both datasets (p < 0.001 on SimpleQuestions).

2. **Residual learning for effective deep matching**: The authors' adaptation of residual networks to sequence matching is particularly compelling. Standard deep BiLSTMs struggle with hierarchical matching due to training instability (e.g., one layer dominating the other), but their residual shortcut connections enable complementary learning of question representations at different abstraction levels. This is validated by ablation results: removing residual learning reduces WebQSP accuracy by 1.87% (80.65% → 82.53%), while attention-based alternatives (e.g., Parikh et al.) underperform by 1.15%.

3. **Clear end-task impact**: The paper’s most significant contribution is demonstrating how relation detection improvements translate to KBQA gains *without* complex re-ranking or joint inference. Their simple two-step pipeline (entity re-ranking via relation scores followed by relation detection) achieves state-of-the-art results. Crucially, the entity re-ranking step (which relies on the relation detector) accounts for 2–3% of the KBQA improvement over baselines, proving that relation detection is a bottleneck in existing systems. This is evidenced by their ablation showing that removing entity re-ranking drops accuracy by 2.1% on SimpleQuestions.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Limited dataset scope**: The evaluation is confined to two datasets (SimpleQuestions and WebQSP), both derived from Freebase. While these are standard benchmarks, the paper lacks validation on other KBQA datasets (e.g., LC-QuAD or QALD) or non-Freebase KBs (e.g., Wikidata). This limits claims about generalizability, especially for open-domain QA where KB structure varies.

2. **Insufficient computational analysis**: The paper reports accuracy gains but omits training/evaluation runtime comparisons with baselines. For instance, how does HR-BiLSTM’s computational cost (e.g., GPU time, memory) compare to simpler models like BiLSTM or CNNs? This is critical for practical deployment, especially in resource-constrained settings.

3. **Ambiguity in entity re-ranking’s role**: While the authors claim entity re-ranking is key, they do not fully disentangle its contribution from the relation detector’s quality. The KBQA improvement could stem from better relation detection *alone*, not the re-ranking step. A controlled experiment (e.g., using a fixed entity ranking but replacing the relation detector) would strengthen this claim.

4. **WebQSP performance gap**: On WebQSP, the HR-BiLSTM still lags behind the state-of-the-art (82.53% vs. 83.6% reported by Yih et al. 2016). The paper attributes this to the need for constraint detection, but it does not explore why their model underperforms on this dataset. A deeper analysis of failure cases (e.g., long chains or ambiguous relations) would add valuable insight.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper states that residual learning helps WebQSP more than SimpleQuestions. Could you elaborate on why this difference exists? Is it due to the greater syntactic complexity of WebQSP questions, or the need for deeper context matching in multi-relation chains?  

2. In Table 3, your system achieves 82.53% on WebQSP, while Yih et al. (2016) report 83.6%. Given that your relation detector is the best-performing component, what factors might explain this gap?  

3. The entity re-ranking step relies on relation scores from the detector. If the detector is imperfect, how does this propagate errors? Could you share error analysis (e.g., precision/recall) for the re-ranking step versus the relation detector?  

**Additional Comments**  
Section 3.1 (on relation representations) would better fit under "Related Work" to avoid repeating background. The current structure (Section 3.1 as a separate section) disrupts the flow of the proposed method. The authors should merge this with Section 2 (Related Work) and reorganize Section 3 to focus solely on their method.  

Overall, this is a strong contribution to KBQA with clear technical innovations and compelling end-task results. The proposed HR-BiLSTM is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and directly addresses a key bottleneck in KBQA. With minor revisions to address the dataset scope and computational analysis, this paper would be a significant addition to ACL.
