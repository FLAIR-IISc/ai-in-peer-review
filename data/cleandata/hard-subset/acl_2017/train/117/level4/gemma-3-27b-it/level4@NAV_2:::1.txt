This paper addresses a relevant topic: learning the mapping between natural language and KB relations, in the context of QA – a setting where only partial information is available for one argument and the number of possible target relations is very large. The core proposal combines two different representations of the input: a word-level representation (segmenting both target relation names and the input text), and relation-level representations (treating relations as single tokens without segmentation). A key contribution appears to be the entity re-ranking mechanism applied after the entity linking step. Results demonstrate improvements over the state of the art.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a challenging problem:** The paper tackles a critical component of KBQA – relation detection – specifically in scenarios with large KBs and a high number of relations, where traditional methods struggle. This is a significant practical challenge.
2. **Novel combination of relation representations:** The hierarchical matching approach, leveraging both word-level and relation-level representations, is a well-motivated idea. Utilizing both granularities allows the model to capture different aspects of the relation and question, leading to improved performance, particularly in the face of unseen relations.
3. **Effective entity re-ranking:** The proposed entity re-ranking step, informed by relation detection, is a compelling contribution. The observation that entity linking ambiguities can be resolved by considering potential relations demonstrates a valuable insight into the interplay between these two core KBQA components.
4. **Strong empirical results:** The paper consistently outperforms baseline methods on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP, achieving state-of-the-art results. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed hierarchical residual BiLSTM and its components.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited dataset diversity:** The evaluation is primarily conducted on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. While these are standard benchmarks, they may not fully capture the diversity of real-world KBQA scenarios. Evaluation on additional datasets would strengthen the generalizability claims.
2. **Ablation study depth:** While the ablation studies are informative, a more in-depth analysis of the impact of individual components (e.g., the residual connections vs. weighted sum) would provide further insights into the model’s behavior.
3. **Complexity of the system:** Although the authors claim minimal effort beyond the relation detector, the complete KBQA pipeline, including entity linking and constraint detection, adds complexity that could make it harder to reproduce. A clearer breakdown of the contributions of each component would be helpful. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the model? Specifically, what types of relations or question structures are most challenging for the system?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the entity re-ranking step to the quality of the initial entity linking results?
3.  Have you explored alternative methods for combining the word-level and relation-level representations, such as attention mechanisms? If so, could you briefly discuss the results?
