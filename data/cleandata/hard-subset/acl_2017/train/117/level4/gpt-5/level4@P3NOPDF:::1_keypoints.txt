- Problem: Maps natural language questions to KB relations with partially specified subject entities after entity linking.
- Core idea: Combine word-level segmented representations with a single-token relation representation to balance lexical generalization and relation-specific discrimination.
- Integration: Positioned as a pragmatic re-ranker after entity linking, fitting common QA pipelines.
- Empirical result: Reports improvements over prior work, indicating practical effectiveness.
- Strength: Addresses a timely, central QA scaling challenge with a clear, intuitive methodological contribution.
- Strength: Practical design facilitates adoption in existing pipelines.
- Weakness: Limited evaluation on few datasets, weakening claims of generality, scalability, and robustness.
- Weakness: Missing ablation to quantify contributions of word-level vs single-token representations.
- Weakness: Insufficient comparisons to strong reranking baselines and alternative architectures (e.g., single-representation, subword/char models).
- Weakness: Dependency on entity linking not analyzed; sensitivity to EL accuracy and coverage is unclear.
- Weakness: Section organization and exposition (Section 3.1/3.2) disrupts narrative clarity.
- Suggestion: Expand evaluation across more datasets, vary candidate relation set sizes, and provide per-relation/per-question-type breakdowns and error analysis.
- Suggestion: Add ablations (each representation alone, composition variants, subword/char alternatives) and diagnostics for EL sensitivity (oracle/perturbed/stratified EL).
- Suggestion: Compare to competitive rerankers and direct-relation-prediction methods.
- Suggestion: Provide architectural specifics, hyperparameters, training objectives, compute/memory costs, and mitigation strategies for large relation vocabularies.
- Suggestion: Reorganize exposition (move related work, clarify method sections), define “single-token” early, use consistent notation and illustrative examples.
- Overall: Promising, practical contribution with encouraging gains, but requires broader evaluation, stronger baselines, and clearer ablations/exposition to validate generality and sources of improvement.
