Summary and Contributions
This paper tackles relation detection for knowledge base question answering (KBQA) where the relation space is large, often includes unseen relations at test time, and, in WebQSP, may require relation chains. The core technical contribution is a hierarchical residual BiLSTM (HR-BiLSTM) that matches questions to relations at two granularities: relation names treated as whole tokens (relation-level) and tokenized relation words (word-level). The model builds multi-level question representations via stacked BiLSTMs and introduces residual connections to stabilize training and enable complementary matching across abstraction levels. The paper further integrates relation detection into a simple KBQA pipeline with a two-step relation detection strategy: (i) using relation scores to re-rank entity linker candidates and (ii) relation detection conditioned on the top re-ranked entity. Empirically, the method improves relation detection and leads to state-of-the-art or competitive end-to-end results on SimpleQuestions and WebQSP.

Main contributions as I see them (ordered by strength):
1) A practical and effective relation detection model (HR-BiLSTM) that combines word-level and relation-level representations with residual connections for hierarchical sequence matching, yielding consistent gains and strong ablations.
2) A simple yet impactful integration of relation detection into KBQA via entity re-ranking that mitigates entity linker ambiguity and improves final QA accuracy without heavy joint inference or handcrafted features.
3) A constructed relation detection task for WebQSP (including chains) and a thorough ablation comparing relation representations, model architectures, and residual vs. attention-based hierarchical matching.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains with clear ablations. HR-BiLSTM improves relation detection by 2–3 points over strong baselines (AMPCNN and BiLSTM variants) on both SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. Ablations convincingly show that (a) using both relation-level and word-level views matters (especially on SimpleQuestions where unseen relations are common), and (b) residual connections enable better hierarchical matching than simple weighted sums or decomposable attention.
- Addresses real KBQA challenges: large relation spaces and zero-shot relations. Factoring relation names into word sequences increases coverage for unseen relations, while keeping relation-level tokens captures global cues that pure word-level matching can miss. This dual-view design is well motivated and validated empirically.
- Effective and minimal KBQA pipeline integration. The two-step relation detection design (entity re-ranking followed by relation scoring with an entity slot <e>) is simple and yields 2–3 point end-to-end gains over swapping in prior relation detectors. Notably, the system achieves state-of-the-art on SimpleQuestions and competitive results on WebQSP without complex joint models or feature engineering.
- Careful comparison across architectures and inputs. The paper compares relation inputs (relation-name tokens vs. word tokens), question encoders (LSTM vs. CNN), residual vs. attention-based hierarchical matching, and depth. Findings (e.g., LSTMs outperform CNNs on relation chains; residuals help more on WebQSP) are informative and likely generalize.
- Clarity on design choices tied to task properties. The paper connects dataset characteristics to model behavior (e.g., SimpleQuestions benefits more from word-level views due to many unseen relations; WebQSP benefits more from deeper/residual architectures due to longer dependencies), helping readers understand where gains come from.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental. While the combination of relation-level and word-level matching for relation detection is well justified, the core modeling ideas (stacked BiLSTMs and residual connections) are adaptations of known techniques. The attention-based hierarchical alternative underperforms here but is standard; the novelty rests more in a strong instantiation than a fundamentally new method.
- Limited evaluation breadth and potential setup bias. Experiments cover only SimpleQuestions and WebQSP. It would strengthen the claims to evaluate on additional KBQA datasets (e.g., ParaLex/OpenIE-style, FreebaseQA), or at least provide more detailed zero-shot analyses. For WebQSP relation detection, the authors construct the task; details on candidate generation and negatives are crucial to assess difficulty and to ensure fairness.
- Pipeline dependencies and potential leakage in entity re-ranking. The re-ranking step scores relations that are connected to candidate entities, which can create a coupling between candidate generation and relation priors from the KB. It is unclear how much of the gain stems from exploiting entity–relation co-occurrence vs. true question–relation matching, and whether this advantage holds if the candidate entity set is noisier or restricted.
- Constraint detection is heuristic and underexplained. The constraint handling relies on character-overlap matching and hand-written rules for dates/types. While effective, it is brittle and not deeply analyzed. The beam size of 1 in query generation increases susceptibility to early errors; the trade-off is acknowledged but not quantified.
- Reproducibility details are incomplete. Hyperparameters (e.g., l, K, K’, α, λ), candidate set sizes, and training details for WebQSP relation detection creation are scattered. The paper mentions releasing data “soon,” but code/model availability and exact preprocessing choices are vital for replication.

Questions to Authors
1) Entity re-ranking: What are the exact values (and sensitivity) of K, K’, l, and α? How does QA accuracy vary with these? How often does the true entity drop out after re-ranking?
2) WebQSP relation detection task: How are negative relation chains sampled and how large is the candidate set per question? Can you report zero-shot performance for relations/chains unseen during training?
3) Residual design: Which residual variant (hidden-state vs. max-pooled residual) performs best and why? Did you try layer normalization or highway connections?
4) Constraint detection: How much does each constraint type (dates, answer-type, entity mentions) contribute? Could a neural mention–entity linker on the subgraph further improve robustness?
5) Runtime and scalability: What are training/inference times and memory costs for HR-BiLSTM relative to AMPCNN/BiCNN, especially as the relation vocabulary scales?

Additional Comments
- Organization: Section 3.1 reads as motivation/related work on relation representations and could be folded into Related Work. Presenting the HR-BiLSTM as Section 3 and clearly separating model, training, and ablations would improve flow.
- Clarity: The description “initialize the relation sequence LSTMs with the final state representations of the word sequence” needs more detail (which sequence initializes which; how for multi-token relation names).
- Fairness: Please confirm that all baselines use identical candidate relation sets and the same entity linking outputs, and report candidate set recall for the gold entity/relations.
- Releasing code and the WebQSP relation detection dataset (with construction scripts) would increase the impact and reproducibility of the work.
