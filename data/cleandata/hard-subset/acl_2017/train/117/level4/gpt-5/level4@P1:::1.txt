Summary
The paper addresses relation detection for Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) under realistic constraints: only one argument (the topic entity) is typically observed, and the candidate relation space is very large. The authors propose a hierarchical recurrent model with residual connections that matches questions to relations at two granularities: (a) relation names treated as single tokens, and (b) relations decomposed into word sequences. They also build a simple KBQA pipeline in which relation detection is used twice—first to re-rank entities after initial entity linking, and then to select the final relation(s). The approach achieves improved results over prior work on both relation detection and end-to-end QA.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A hierarchical relation detection model that jointly leverages word-level and relation-as-token representations, enhanced with residual connections between BiLSTM layers to stabilize training and enable complementary abstraction levels.
2) A practical KBQA contribution: using relation detection to re-rank entity candidates after entity linking, which appears to be the main driver of end-to-end QA gains.
3) Empirical improvements over the state of the art on standard single-relation (SimpleQuestions) and multi-relation (WebQSP) benchmarks.

Strengths
- Relevance and scope: The paper tackles an important and challenging problem—mapping natural language to KB relations—under conditions common in KBQA: large and open relation vocabularies, partial observation of arguments, and potential zero-shot settings. This is a meaningful advance compared to general relation extraction setups with a small, closed set of relation types.
- Methodological clarity: The core idea of combining two complementary relation representations (relation-as-token for global cues, and word-level for generalization) is well motivated. The hierarchical residual BiLSTM encoder for the question side is a sensible way to align with these different granularities and mitigate training difficulties in deeper architectures.
- KBQA pipeline impact: The use of relation detection to re-rank entities after initial entity linking is simple and effective. Given that entity linking ambiguity is often a bottleneck (e.g., multiple entities sharing the same surface form), this step directly addresses a critical source of end-to-end error and, in my view, is the main contribution to the QA results.
- Empirical gains: The approach outperforms prior systems in relation detection and translates into stronger end-to-end QA. The improvements over strong baselines suggest the proposed modeling choices are beneficial in practice, not just in controlled settings.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: The experimental validation is restricted to a small set of datasets. Although SimpleQuestions and WebQSP are standard, they may not cover broader domains, KBs, or more diverse relation schemas. As a result, it is hard to assess generalizability beyond these benchmarks.
