Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a multigraph-based formulation for overlapping (nested/crossing) entity recognition. Edges are given explicit “entity separator” semantics (eight S/E/C combinations), yielding an edge-based representation with linear-time O(nT) inside–outside inference and Viterbi-style decoding.
- It presents an analytical comparison to mention hypergraphs (Lu & Roth, 2015), arguing that the latter are “deficient” due to spurious structures in the normalization term, while the proposed multigraph is “nondeficient.”
- Empirically, it reports small-to-moderate F1 improvements over mention hypergraphs and older baselines on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA; and shows competitive performance on CoNLL 2003.

Strengths
- Addresses an important setting (overlapping entities), where many sequence models fall short. The focus on representational adequacy and linear-time inference is well aligned with practical constraints.
- Clear, fair baseline against mention hypergraphs using identical feature sets. The split of test sets into overlapping vs non-overlapping portions provides useful diagnostic insight.
- The “edge semantics” view is interesting and arguably clarifies the roles that edges play in edge-based formulations. The entity-separator abstraction is intuitive and yields a compact constant-factor set of transitions per position.
- Theoretical discussion of spurious structures vs nondeficiency raises a real issue in some structured models. The toy example is instructive, and the training curves hint at improved optimization behavior.
- Reproducibility details are relatively thorough for a feature-based log-linear model (feature templates, regularization ranges, Brown clusters, and mention penalty tuning).

Weaknesses
- Missing comparisons to modern, stronger baselines. The evaluation omits recent nested NER methods (e.g., layered/stacked BiLSTM-CRF, span/region-based models, hypergraph/neural hybrids, MRC-style formulations), which have become standard on ACE/GENIA. Without such comparisons, the empirical significance is unclear; gains over Lu & Roth (2015) and older CRFs are not sufficient to establish state-of-the-art or practical relevance in 2025.
- The “nondeficiency” claim is not formally proven, nor is its impact quantified. The paper asserts a bijection between paths/subgraphs and entity sets (“can be verified”) but does not provide a rigorous proof in the main text. More importantly, there is no empirical isolation of this factor (e.g., controlled ablations) to show that the reported F1 gains stem from eliminating spurious structures rather than from alternative modeling choices (e.g., different edge factorization).
- Ambiguity on crossing entities and decoding. The paper states it resolves ambiguous structures “as nested instead of crossing,” suggesting the model or decoding cannot unambiguously represent/evaluate all crossing cases. This contradicts the claim to “handle overlapping entities” in general, since crossing mentions are common in ACE. The practical capability (and evaluation) on true crossing cases is not clearly demonstrated.
- Evaluation comparability concerns for GENIA. The paper changes tokenization (“further split all tokens at some punctuations”) and treats some discontiguous/coordinated mentions as single structures. This deviates from prior setups and may invalidate direct comparisons to Finkel & Manning (2009) and others. A replication with their tokenization/evaluation protocol (or reporting results under both) is needed.
- Limited analysis and reporting. No statistical significance tests; modest absolute gains (often ≤2 F1). The mention-penalty tuning for F1 optimization is applied, but fairness across baselines and risk of overfitting to dev are not fully addressed. Runtime is discussed only qualitatively (per-iteration +12%, fewer iterations) without end-to-end wall-clock or memory. No error analysis or ablation on separator set, edge features, and inter-type interactions.

Questions to Authors
- Nondeficiency and spurious structures:
  - Please provide a formal proof (or theorem with conditions) that shows a bijection between model subgraphs/paths and entity sets, ensuring the partition function sums over exactly the realizable outputs. How do you prevent multiple derivations per entity set in the multigraph? Are there corner cases with multiple edges (ES vs C) leading to ambiguity?
  - Quantify the effect: show likelihood differences, gradient mismatches, or controlled ablations demonstrating that removing spurious structures materially improves training dynamics and F1.
- Crossing entities:
  - Does the model uniquely represent crossing mentions? If so, please show explicit examples and evaluation on subsets with crossing entities. If ambiguous structures are mapped to nested during decoding, what is the measured impact on crossing cases in ACE?
- Baselines:
  - Please compare against recent nested NER systems (e.g., span-based neural models, layered BiLSTM-CRF, MRC-based nested NER, hypergraph neural models). If you position your work as a structural contribution, can you integrate your multigraph with neural features to test competitiveness?
- GENIA preprocessing and evaluation:
  - How do your tokenization and treatment of coordinated/discontiguous mentions affect comparability to published numbers? Can you report results under the standard GENIA/JNLPBA protocols used in F&M (2009) and Lu & Roth (2015)?
- Modeling details:
  - Do you model interactions across entity types at all? If not, can you quantify the impact (ablation) on ACE, where cross-type overlaps are frequent?
  - Please provide precise complexity constants and memory usage; report wall-clock training and decoding times vs mention hypergraph on all datasets.
  - Provide significance testing for reported improvements and an error analysis (especially on overlapping vs non-overlapping subsets) to substantiate the claimed advantages.

Overall, while the edge-semantic multigraph formulation is conceptually neat and the linear-time inference attractive, the paper’s empirical validation is not up to current standards and the theoretical claims need stronger support. Pending stronger baselines, formal proofs/quantification of nondeficiency benefits, and clearer handling of crossing cases and evaluation protocols, I am not convinced to recommend acceptance.
