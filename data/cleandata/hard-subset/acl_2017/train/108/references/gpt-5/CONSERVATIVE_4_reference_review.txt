Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a multigraph-based structured model for recognizing overlapping named entities. The core idea is to encode explicit semantics on edges (“entity separators”) between token-wise states, yielding a directed multigraph that supports nested/overlapping spans with O(nT) training/inference using an inside–outside style algorithm. The authors argue their formulation avoids the spurious-structures (deficiency) issue they attribute to the mention hypergraph model (Lu & Roth, 2015). Empirically, they report modest F1 gains over a reimplementation of mention hypergraphs and classic non-overlapping baselines on ACE2004/2005, GENIA, and CoNLL 2003.

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) A reparameterization of overlapping-entity modeling via edge-labeled multigraphs, claimed to be nondeficient while retaining linear complexity.
2) An analysis of “state-based” vs “edge-based” paradigms and an argument about spurious structures in mention hypergraphs.
3) An empirical comparison showing small F1 improvements on several datasets using matched feature sets.

Strengths
- Clear, implementable formulation with linear-time dynamic programming: The multigraph view is well described, and the mapping of edge semantics to “entity separators” is intuitive. The use of standard log-linear training with inside–outside and max-product decoding makes the method accessible and easy to reproduce conceptually.
- Focused comparison under matched features: The attempt to keep feature sets identical to mention hypergraphs strengthens the head-to-head comparison. The note on training dynamics (faster convergence despite slightly higher per-iteration cost) is a useful practical detail.
- Broad dataset coverage and some diagnostic analyses: Experiments on ACE, GENIA, and CoNLL 2003 (including splitting by sentences with/without overlaps) provide a baseline level of empirical grounding. The observation that the model remains competitive on non-overlapping data is valuable for understanding generality.
- Clarity of the edge-based paradigm discussion: The paper articulates how edges can encode role combinations that states alone cannot easily capture, which may be useful to others working on overlapping structures.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing formulations; missing strong baselines: The proposed multigraph with 8 “separator” edge types appears equivalent to labeling inter-token gaps with a small finite alphabet and running a first-order chain model (the authors themselves acknowledge this equivalence). Without formalizing why the multigraph brings unique modeling capacity, the contribution risks being a reparameterization. Crucially, the paper does not compare against modern nested NER approaches (e.g., span-based and neural segmental/hypergraph models; layered/stacked BiLSTMs; transformer-based baselines), which have become standard. As presented, the empirical improvements over a 2015 hypergraph baseline (and older CRF/constituency models) are modest and not obviously impactful in today’s landscape.
- Deficiency/spurious-structure claims are not rigorously established: The paper asserts nondeficiency for the multigraph and deficiency for mention hypergraphs, with an informal argument and an example in the appendix. There is no formal theorem/proof or precise conditions under which the normalization includes spurious structures, nor a quantification of the practical impact on learning/inference. The empirical link between “nondeficiency” and improved recall is speculative; no controlled ablation isolates this factor (e.g., synthetic data where spurious interpretations vary).
- Incomplete and potentially unfair evaluation details: For GENIA, the authors alter tokenization and compare against reported numbers from prior work, which can compromise comparability. Statistical significance is not reported. The reliance on a tuned “mention penalty” further complicates interpretation of gains; ablations on this tuning are missing. For CoNLL 2003, comparisons omit stronger baselines (e.g., Illinois NER with external resources, modern neural models), reducing the informativeness of the results.
- Clarity and formalism gaps: Some notation and definitions are confusing (e.g., Tt vs Ikt/Okt per token; whether Tt is token-specific or global; how crossing entities are handled vs “we resolved ambiguous structures as nested”). The model’s support for crossing entities is not demonstrated empirically, and the decoding choice to prefer nesting could bias results on datasets with crossing overlaps. Complexity claims are big-O only; constants and memory costs for multiple edge types per (k, t) are not analyzed.
- Lack of ablations and sensitivity analyses: There is no study of the contribution of particular separator types, the effect of the number of types T on speed/accuracy, or robustness to feature subsets. Since both models use hand-engineered features, isolating which aspects benefit from the multigraph factorization would strengthen the case.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a formal proof (or precise conditions) that your normalization excludes spurious structures while the mention hypergraph’s includes them? Under what assumptions on features and structure does the claim hold?
- How does the proposed multigraph compare to a gap-labeling linear-chain CRF with 8 labels (S, E, C, ES, CS, EC, ECS, X) trained with identical features? This seems the most direct, state-based reparameterization you discuss; an empirical comparison would clarify where the gains come from.
- Do your results on GENIA use the same tokenization as the baselines you cite? If not, can you re-run baselines under your preprocessing or report matched numbers?
- Are any improvements statistically significant (e.g., bootstrap or randomization tests) over mention hypergraphs across datasets?
- Do you support crossing entities in decoding, and if so, why are ambiguous cases mapped to nested structures? What fraction of ACE/GENIA test instances would be affected by this choice?

Additional comments
- Please report absolute training time, memory, and constant factors per token/type, not only iteration counts.
- Provide ablations on the mention penalty and on separator subsets. If the penalty is essential, it would be useful to understand its interaction with the “nondeficiency” property.
- The references and formatting contain several glitches; please clean up for camera-ready.

Overall, while the paper is clearly written and the formulation is neat, the methodological novelty seems incremental, the theoretical claims are not fully substantiated, and the empirical evaluation omits modern baselines and significance testing. I encourage the authors to strengthen the theoretical treatment and substantially broaden the empirical comparisons (including a gap-label CRF and contemporary neural nested NER models).
