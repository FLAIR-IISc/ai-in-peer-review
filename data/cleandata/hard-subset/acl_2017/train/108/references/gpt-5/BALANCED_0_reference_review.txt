Summary and contributions
- Proposes a multigraph-based structured model for overlapping entity recognition that assigns explicit semantics to edges (“entity separators”) and performs linear-time O(nT) inference/learning via an inside–outside style algorithm. The model is argued to be nondeficient (no spurious structures) unlike the mention hypergraph (Lu & Roth, 2015).
- Empirical evaluation on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA shows consistent, albeit modest, F1 improvements over an in-house reimplementation of mention hypergraphs; additional analysis splits results by sentences with/without overlaps and includes CoNLL 2003 to gauge performance on purely non-overlapping NER.
- Conceptual framing of “state-based vs edge-based” paradigms clarifies representational choices for overlapping structures and may inform future work.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive representation: Encoding “start/end/continue” combinations as edge semantics is elegant and compact. The mapping of overlapping spans to paths in a multigraph is easy to reason about, and the link to classic dynamic programming (inside–outside, max-product) is well explained.
- Theoretical claim of nondeficiency: The paper identifies and analyzes spurious structure issues in mention hypergraphs and argues the proposed model avoids them. The simplified examples in the appendix help build intuition.
- Efficiency preserved: Despite richer edge semantics, the algorithm maintains O(nT) time complexity with straightforward DP; this keeps the approach competitive with prior structured models for nested/overlapping NER.
- Empirical gains across datasets: On ACE2004/2005 and GENIA, the model generally outperforms a strong prior structured baseline (mention hypergraph), with the largest gain on ACE2004 (+2.1 F1 after tuning). The per-sentence-type breakdown provides useful diagnostic insight.
- Reproducibility details: The paper documents features, hyperparameters, tokenization decisions, and tuning procedures; it promises code release and provides dataset splits consistent with prior work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty over mention hypergraphs: The multigraph can be viewed as a reparameterization/edge-collapsing of the mention hypergraph with explicit edge semantics. While the nondeficiency argument is interesting, the modeling advance feels incremental relative to Lu & Roth (2015), and the empirical gains are modest.
- Insufficiently formal theory: The nondeficiency claim is argued via examples; a general, formal proof (conditions under which no spurious structures can arise and how scores factorize) is not provided. The paper says “it can be verified,” which weakens the theoretical contribution.
- Baselines are dated for current standards: No comparisons to modern nested/overlapping NER methods using neural encoders (e.g., span-based neural models, layered CRFs, region-based parsers, or transformer-based architectures). Even if the contribution is representational, showing competitiveness with contemporary neural baselines would strengthen significance.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics: There is no ablation isolating (i) the effect of nondeficiency vs representational change, (ii) the contribution of “separator” granularity (e.g., fewer separator types), (iii) the mention penalty feature, or (iv) alternative state-based re-encodings. It is therefore unclear what drives the gains. Error analysis is also absent.
- Evaluation caveats and reporting: Statistical significance is not reported. GENIA preprocessing (tokenization changes and handling of coordinated/discontiguous mentions) may diverge from prior work; it is unclear how this affects comparability to the Finkel & Manning results. The decoding resolves ambiguous structures as nested rather than crossing, which may bias outcomes on data with crossing mentions; the impact is not quantified. Runtime is reported qualitatively (iterations vs time/iteration) without end-to-end wall-clock comparisons for training and inference.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a general, formal proof that the multigraph model’s normalization excludes spurious structures under your factorization and feature definitions? What assumptions are required?
- How much of the empirical gain is attributable specifically to eliminating spurious structures? Can you quantify via an ablation that induces spurious structures in your model (or removes them from MH) while keeping everything else equal?
- How sensitive is performance to the set of 8 entity separators? Would a reduced or expanded set change accuracy or speed?
- What is the wall-clock training and inference time (and memory) comparison to mention hypergraphs on the reported datasets? Please include average sentence length and entity type counts to assess constant factors in O(nT).
- How does the decoding choice to resolve ambiguous structures as nested (not crossing) affect results on datasets with crossing mentions? Do ACE/GENIA contain crossing mentions, and if so, what fraction and what is the delta if you allow crossings?
- Could you compare to at least one modern neural baseline for nested/overlapping NER (e.g., span-based models with transformers), perhaps using your features + contextual embeddings or a neuralized version of your factor graph?
- Do you support discontiguous mentions beyond the GENIA coordination heuristic? If not, can the multigraph be extended to represent discontinuities, and what would be the complexity implications?
- Can you release code and preprocessing scripts to ensure comparability for GENIA tokenization decisions?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Strengthen the theoretical section with a rigorous, dataset-agnostic proof of nondeficiency and a clear statement of model class assumptions.
- Add statistical significance testing and error analysis (types and boundary errors, especially for overlapping cases).
- Include ablations on separators, mention penalty, and a state-based re-encoding baseline to validate the edge-based design choice.
- Provide wall-clock benchmarks and memory usage. A figure/table summarizing training and decoding speeds would help practitioners.
- If space permits, add qualitative examples showcasing cases where MH fails due to spurious structures but your model succeeds.
