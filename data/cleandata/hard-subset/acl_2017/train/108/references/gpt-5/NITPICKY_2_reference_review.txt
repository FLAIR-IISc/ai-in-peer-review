Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a multigraph-based, globally normalized model for overlapping entity recognition. It assigns explicit semantics to multiple edges between the same node pair (“entity separators”), performs inference via an inside–outside style dynamic program, and decodes via max-product. The authors argue the model belongs to an “edge-based” paradigm (vs. state-based) and claim it avoids the spurious-structure deficiency of mention hypergraphs (Lu & Roth, 2015) while retaining O(nT) complexity. Empirically, it outperforms a reimplementation of mention hypergraphs, a linear-chain CRF, and (on GENIA) a constituency parser baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Methodological: A multigraph formulation with edge semantics (“entity separators”) for overlapping entities, with linear-time inference and decoding.
2) Theoretical: A claim (with informal justification) that the model is non-deficient (no spurious structures) unlike mention hypergraphs.
3) Empirical: Moderate F1 improvements over a strong non-neural baseline on ACE04/05 and GENIA, plus a surprising win on CoNLL03 (non-overlapping entities).

Strengths
1) Clean edge-semantics formalization and clear connection to edge-based modeling. The “entity separators” are intuitive and lead to a compact representation with 8 interpretable transitions per gap. The paper helpfully situates this within state- vs edge-based paradigms and discusses equivalences.
2) Linear-time inference with a simple dynamic program. The construction appears DAG-structured and amenable to standard inside–outside/max-product, maintaining O(nT) like mention hypergraphs, which is attractive for scalability.
3) Non-deficiency discussion. The analysis contrasts inside-score semantics in the multigraph vs. mention hypergraph and provides a detailed appendix example. While not a full proof, it addresses a real issue in hypergraph normalizers.
4) Empirical results against competitive non-neural baselines. On ACE04/05 the multigraph gives +1.3–2.1 F1 over the reproduced mention hypergraph after tuning; on GENIA it slightly improves over the published parser and the reproduced hypergraph; on CoNLL03 it beats a CRF and a classic feature-rich NER system (without external resources).
5) Reasonable engineering: shared feature sets across models where possible, L-BFGS training, tuning of regularization and mention penalties, Brown clusters from training only, and some convergence analysis (fewer iterations than hypergraph, per-iteration +12% time).

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to existing formulations; equivalence to gap-tag CRFs not empirically addressed. The “entity separator” multigraph is very close to labeling gaps with boundary-transition tags (S, E, C, and combinations), i.e., a first-order linear-chain CRF over gap states. The paper acknowledges this equivalence but dismisses it without empirical or complexity evidence. A gap-tag CRF baseline (or a semi-Markov with boundary features) is essential to establish methodological novelty and practical benefit.
2) Non-deficiency claim lacks a formal proof and scope. The appendix uses a restricted toy hypergraph; it is not shown that (a) all spurious-structure cases of mention hypergraphs are excluded, and (b) the multigraph has no analogous ambiguity under all feature parametrizations. A precise definition and proof (e.g., path-unique decomposition and one-to-one mapping between subgraphs and entity sets) are missing.
3) Incomplete and sometimes unfair experimental comparisons. There are no neural nested NER baselines (e.g., layered BiLSTM-CRFs, region-based methods, hypergraph/neural span models), which have been standard for several years. The GENIA preprocessing deviates (hyphen splitting, coordinated entities treatment), so comparability to prior numbers is unclear; yet prior results are cited. No statistical significance tests are reported, and improvements are small on GENIA. The CoNLL03 result is interesting but lacks analysis; since the model can predict overlaps that don’t exist in gold, explain why this helps.
4) Missing details and ambiguities in modeling and decoding. It is stated that “ambiguous structures” are resolved as nested rather than crossing at decoding; this may bias outcomes on datasets with any crossing annotations. Constraints ensuring global consistency across entity types are not described (model seems type-independent with a shared root). The “mention penalty” is tuned to maximize dev F1 by adding a feature on start edges; this changes the objective post hoc and may bias results—clarify whether this is applied symmetrically across models and how it affects normalization.
5) Presentation and formatting issues. Multiple figures/tables are referenced but not properly shown (e.g., Figure 3; Tables 3 and 5 are missing in the provided text). Numerous typos and inconsistent terminology (“pervious,” “nondeficient”), broken hyphenation, and footnote markers without content (e.g., “hyperedges1”). References are inconsistently formatted with placeholders (e.g., “?, \Q2003\E”) and missing venues/years. These issues hamper reproducibility and clarity.

Questions to authors
1) Can you provide a formal proof that your normalization sums over a set in bijection with valid entity-structure interpretations (i.e., no spurious structures), and that decoding yields the MAP element of that set?
2) How does your model handle crossing entities at decoding? You state ambiguous structures are interpreted as nested; are any datasets with crossing entities affected? Please quantify impact and justify this design.
3) Please include a gap-tag linear-chain CRF baseline (states over gaps with 8 separator labels) and compare complexity, training stability, and accuracy. If you claim unnecessary dependencies arise, provide empirical evidence (performance vs. training time).
4) For GENIA, given your tokenization and coordination handling differ from prior work, please report numbers under both your preprocessing and a setting matching prior work, or clearly state non-comparability. Also provide per-type F1 and overlap-specific metrics.
5) Report significance tests (e.g., bootstrap) for all main comparisons, and wall-clock training/inference time. Also release code and exact feature templates to ensure reproducibility.
6) Can you clarify whether features are truly “identical” across models, given that the edge sets and semantics differ? Provide counts of feature types and active features per instance.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is neat and the empirical gains over a strong non-neural baseline are promising, but the novelty is limited without comparison to an equivalent gap-tag CRF, the non-deficiency claim lacks a formal proof, modern baselines are missing, and the paper suffers from substantial presentation and referencing issues. Addressing these concerns could make this a solid contribution.
