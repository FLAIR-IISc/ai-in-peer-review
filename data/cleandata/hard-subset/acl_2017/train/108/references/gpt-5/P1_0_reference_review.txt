Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a multigraph-based structured prediction model for overlapping NER. The core idea is to assign explicit semantics to edges between adjacent positions via “entity separators” (combinations of start/end/continue signals), yielding a directed multigraph with multiple edges between the same node pair. The model performs globally normalized inference (inside–outside and max-product) in O(nT), similar to the mention hypergraph (Lu & Roth, 2015), but claims to avoid the “spurious structures” (deficiency) that arise in mention hypergraphs. Experiments on ACE2004/2005, GENIA, and CoNLL 2003 indicate consistent (though modest) F1 improvements over strong structured baselines.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A conceptually clear edge-semantic multigraph formulation for overlapping (including nested and potentially crossing) entity recognition with O(nT) inference.
2) A theoretical analysis arguing the model is nondeficient (its normalization does not include spurious structures), contrasted with mention hypergraphs.
3) An empirical study showing small but consistent improvements across multiple datasets, plus analysis by overlap vs. non-overlap subsets and training convergence.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea with explicit edge semantics:
  The “entity separators” formalize the roles at token boundaries and map naturally to overlapping spans. The edge-based view is well-motivated, with a helpful comparison to state-based paradigms and to mention hypergraphs. The construction is intuitive and compact, yet expressive enough to capture overlapping structures while preserving linear-time inference.

- Theoretical clarification of deficiency:
  The paper articulates why mention hypergraphs count spurious structures in the partition function and argues the proposed multigraph avoids this by summing over distinct paths. The appendix provides a concrete example illustrating the phenomenon for mention hypergraphs. This conceptual clarity is valuable and likely of interest beyond NER.

- Empirical improvements and fair comparisons:
  Using identical feature sets and training protocols to compare against a reimplementation of mention hypergraphs makes the evaluation fair. The model consistently improves F1 on ACE2004/2005 and slightly on GENIA; the overlap vs. non-overlap breakdown adds insight. Reporting convergence behavior (fewer iterations, slight per-iteration overhead) is also useful.

- Same asymptotic complexity with practical feasibility:
  Maintaining O(nT) while improving modeling fidelity over a strong prior method is appealing. The use of standard log-linear training and familiar decoding reduces barriers to adoption.

- Broader framing:
  The state- vs. edge-based paradigm discussion is thought-provoking and may inspire applications to other overlapping-structure problems.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to simple transformations:
  The paper acknowledges that the separators could be represented as states in a linear-chain model. While it argues this would induce “unnecessary dependencies,” this claim is not empirically tested. Without a separator-as-state baseline (or theoretical analysis of the induced constraints), the novelty may feel incremental to some readers.

- Nondeficiency claim lacks a formal proof:
  The argument that the multigraph sums over unique paths and thus avoids spurious structures is plausible but informal. A proof of a bijection between valid mention sets and derivations counted by the partition function (including the impact of the root–T hyperedge and multiedges) would strengthen the contribution.

- Ambiguity about crossing mentions:
  The paper states the model supports crossing entities, but in decoding “ambiguous structures” are resolved as nested. This weakens the claim. It is unclear whether the decoding procedure can faithfully return crossing mentions when they are truly intended and how often ambiguities arise. The handling seems ad hoc and should be clarified and evaluated.

- Limited baselines and significance testing:
  The empirical gains (≈1–2 F1 on ACE, ≈0.4 on GENIA) are modest. There is no reporting of statistical significance, per-type or length-stratified performance, or detailed error analysis. In today’s landscape, comparisons to stronger/neural nested NER systems are missing. Even within the classic feature-based setting, including Semi-CRF and parser-based baselines on all datasets (not only GENIA) or more recent hypergraph/vatariant baselines would help.

- Reproducibility and dataset handling issues:
  Some preprocessing choices (e.g., tokenization for GENIA, coordinated entities treatment) meaningfully affect results but are only briefly described. The CoNLL-2003 results claim to beat strong baselines without external resources; this is surprising and warrants more detail (exact numbers, features, significance). Code is promised but not yet available.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a formal proof (or a precise statement with proof sketch) that the multigraph’s normalization is nondeficient, i.e., each valid entity combination maps to a unique derivation counted exactly once?
- Do you support decoding of crossing mentions in practice? If yes, how are ambiguous cases handled; if no, what subset of overlaps is actually returned? Please quantify how often decoding changes a crossing structure into nested in your test sets.
- Did you implement a “separator-as-state” linear-chain baseline? If so, how does it compare in accuracy and runtime? If not, can you justify, theoretically or empirically, why your edge-based formulation is preferable?
- Are the reported improvements statistically significant? Please include significance tests and per-entity-type/length breakdowns.
- How are dependencies across entity types handled? Are types conditionally independent given the structure, or are there cross-type constraints/features?

Additional comments
- Please clarify the exact constants in the O(nT) analysis given the 8 separator edges and multigraph structure, and report end-to-end runtime/memory comparisons per sentence.
- Improve clarity of figures and notations (some references and footnotes are hard to follow). Ensure all tables (e.g., CoNLL, GENIA) are fully reported with exact numbers.
