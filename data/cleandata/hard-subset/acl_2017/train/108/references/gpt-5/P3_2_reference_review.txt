Summary and contributions
The paper tackles overlapping/nested named entity recognition with a new structured model: a multigraph in which edges carry explicit semantics (“entity separators” S, E, C and their combinations) between adjacent positions for each entity type. Training uses a log-linear objective with edge features; inference is via an inside–outside-style dynamic program with linear time O(nT) and Viterbi-style decoding. The authors argue their model is “nondeficient” (no spurious structures) unlike mention hypergraphs (Lu and Roth, 2015), and they report modest F1 gains over that baseline on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA; they also include a CoNLL-2003 experiment.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Modeling: a compact multigraph formulation for overlapping entities based on separator-typed edges, with linear-time training/inference and straightforward feature design.
2) Analysis: a discussion and illustrative examples of spurious structures in mention hypergraphs and an argument that the proposed formulation avoids them.
3) Empirics: comparisons to mention hypergraphs and a linear-chain baseline on classic datasets, with modest but consistent improvements and some breakdown by sentence overlap and training convergence.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive modeling idea with explicit edge semantics. Encoding start/continue/end combinations as multiedges between I/O states per token is simple and expressive. The separator taxonomy yields a clean DP with a small, fixed set of transitions per position.
- Preserves favorable complexity. The linear O(nT) inference and training with standard inside–outside and max-product are attractive and on par with mention hypergraphs, making the method practically viable.
- Theoretical framing raises a real issue. The spurious-structure discussion helps clarify deficiencies of the hypergraph parameterization. Even if the empirical impact is modest, the formal perspective is valuable for structured modeling of overlapping outputs.
- Fair baseline and implementation. The comparison to Lu and Roth (2015) uses matched features and training objectives, and the authors include both “raw” and F1-tuned results. The additional training curve showing faster convergence (fewer L-BFGS iterations) is a nice touch.
- Diagnostic experiments. The split of test sets by presence/absence of overlap provides insight into where the model helps, and the CoNLL-2003 experiment suggests the approach is competitive even without overlap, hinting at a generally useful parameterization.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong and contemporary baselines. The nested NER literature has advanced substantially beyond feature-based models (e.g., layered/stacked sequence taggers, region-based/span-based neural models, transition systems, and modern hypergraph formulations). Comparisons to representative neural span-based or layered models (e.g., Ju et al., 2018; Katiyar and Cardie, 2018; Wang and Lu, 2018; Luan et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2021, among others) are absent. In 2025, improvements over a 2015 feature-based baseline are not sufficient to establish SOTA or broad impact.
- Nondeficiency claim needs formalization. The paper presents an example-driven argument that separators induce a one-to-one mapping between full structures and edge-sequences, thereby avoiding spurious structures. A rigorous proof (e.g., uniqueness-of-derivation and bijection between separator sequences and entity sets for all types and sentence lengths) is missing. In particular, when multiple events co-occur (ECS), or across sentence boundaries (start/end), it should be proven there is no alternative derivation or latent ambiguity. Similarly, the use of a root hyperedge connecting all T nodes deserves clarification vis-à-vis factorization and whether it could reintroduce ambiguity.
- Limited empirical validation of the theory. The hypothesized practical benefit of nondeficiency is only indirectly supported (slightly higher recall, faster convergence). There are no ablations quantifying the effect of removing multi-edges (collapsing ES/C, etc.), of removing the mention penalty, or of varying the separator set. Statistical significance tests are not reported; given the small gains (often ≤1 F1), such tests are important.
- Dataset preprocessing choices may confound comparability. For GENIA, nonstandard tokenization (additional splits) and special handling of coordinated/discontiguous mentions may shift difficulty compared to prior work. It would help to report performance on commonly used splits/tokenization or provide side-by-side results to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Scope and generality not demonstrated. While the paper motivates broader applicability of multigraphs to overlapping structures (e.g., equation parsing), no additional tasks are explored. Moreover, the model does not model cross-type interactions (admitted by the authors), which may limit applicability in settings where type correlations matter.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a formal proof that the multigraph induces a unique derivation (no spurious structures) for any sentence and entity set across all types, i.e., a bijection between complete separator assignments and entity sets? Please specify assumptions (e.g., allowance of crossing spans, simultaneous start/end events) and boundary conditions.
- How does your model handle genuinely crossing entities during decoding? You mention interpreting ambiguous structures as nested. Is the model capable of representing and decoding crossings uniquely, and if so, how frequent are such cases in your datasets and how are they evaluated?
- Please report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap resampling) of the F1 improvements over mention hypergraphs on test sets.
- Can you add ablations: (i) collapsing certain separators (e.g., merging ES and C), (ii) removing the mention penalty, and (iii) modeling/not modeling cross-type independence? This would help attribute gains to specific design choices.
- To situate the contribution in today’s landscape, could you provide results with a neural encoder (e.g., BiLSTM/Transformer features) and compare to representative modern nested NER baselines on at least one dataset with standard preprocessing? If not feasible, please justify.
- For GENIA, can you also report results under a commonly used tokenization/split to enable direct comparison with prior reports?
