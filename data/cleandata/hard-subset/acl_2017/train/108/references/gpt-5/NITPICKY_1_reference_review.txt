Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a log-linear, globally normalized multigraph model for overlapping (nested/crossing) entity recognition. It encodes local “entity separators” between adjacent tokens with 8 edge labels (S, E, C and their combinations), yielding a DAG with multiple parallel edges between the same node pair (hence a multigraph). Training/inference use inside–outside; decoding uses max-product.
- The authors claim: (1) a novel edge-based multigraph representation that handles overlapping entities in O(nT) time; (2) theoretical advantage over the mention hypergraph (Lu & Roth, 2015) by avoiding “spurious structures” (nondeficient normalization); (3) empirical improvements over mention hypergraph and several baselines on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA; they also report CoNLL 2003 results (non-overlapping).

Strengths
- Clear edge-based modeling idea. Encoding the start/end/continue events as explicit edge semantics is a neat, intuitive local representation. The separator taxonomy (E, C, S and combinations) is well motivated, and the use of directed multiedges is a reasonable formal mechanism to disambiguate identical state transitions with different semantics.
- Linear-time inference with global normalization. The model keeps O(nT) complexity comparable to mention hypergraphs while offering a different factorization. The inside–outside/Viterbi descriptions are standard and plausible given the left-to-right DAG structure.
- Competitive results against older structured baselines. On ACE and GENIA, the model appears to slightly outperform the authors’ implementation of mention hypergraphs; the additional partitioning analysis (w/ vs w/o overlapping sentences) is informative.
- Helpful discussion of state-based vs edge-based paradigms. The comparison section highlights representational trade-offs and clarifies why edges must carry semantics for overlapping structures.
- Reproducibility intent and feature transparency. The paper provides feature templates and hyperparameter grids; preprocessing of GENIA is described.

Weaknesses
- Questionable “nondeficiency” claim; unclear one-to-one mapping. The paper asserts the normalization contains no spurious structures because inside scores sum over path scores. However, the separator scheme is inherently lossy with respect to multiplicity: S/E/C are modeled as Booleans, not counts. When multiple entities (especially of the same type) start/end/continue at a boundary, distinct mention configurations map to the same sequence of separators (and thus to the same subgraph/path). The paper itself notes “we resolved ambiguous structures by considering them as nested entities instead of crossing,” which contradicts a one-to-one mapping and suggests ambiguity remains at decoding. The appendix provides illustrative cases for mention hypergraphs but no formal proof that the proposed factorization yields a bijection between subgraphs and entity sets. As written, the nondeficiency claim is unproven and likely incorrect in general.
- Insufficient empirical baselines. Contemporary nested NER approaches (span-based, layered, hypergraph/neural, and PLM-based models: e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with biaffine/span scoring, layered taggers, Seq2Seq-pointer, transformer-based nested NER) are absent. Improvements over a 2015 hypergraph baseline and linear-chain/semi-CRF do not establish state of the art in 2025. Lack of comparison to Finkel & Manning (2009) on ACE is also a gap.
- Decoding and expressivity underspecified. The entity recovery procedure is crucial in edge-based models but is relegated to “same interpretation as mention hypergraph” with an ad hoc tie-breaking toward nesting. It is unclear whether the 8 separators can express unbounded nesting depth of same-type entities starting/ending at the same positions, or how multiplicities are recovered without counts. A formal characterization of representational capacity is missing.
- Evaluation and statistical rigor. No statistical significance testing; no ablations (e.g., without mention penalty, without Brown clusters, without specific feature groups); no per-type or length-based analysis; hardware/time details are minimal (only one convergence plot; 12% per-iteration overhead claim lacks environment/configs). The CoNLL results are referenced but not shown in the main text excerpt; it’s unclear how much they add beyond demonstrating competitiveness in the non-overlapping regime.
- Clarity/notation/formatting issues. Numerous notational inconsistencies (e.g., Tt vs Tkt; mixing node indices/types; footnotes misplaced; missing spaces in “time complexityO(nT 2)”), references contain artifacts (“?, \Q2003\E”), missing or unclearly placed figures/tables (e.g., Fig. 3, Table 3/5 not visible), and ambiguous statements (“features defined for each output feature”). These hinder readability and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Nondeficiency: Provide a formal proof that every valid mention set corresponds to exactly one scored derivation (subgraph/path) and vice versa. Specifically: how does the separator representation encode multiplicities when multiple entities start/end/continue at the same boundary for the same type? If multiple mention sets map to the same separator sequence, how is deficiency avoided?
- Expressivity: Can your model represent arbitrarily deep nesting of same-type entities with coincident boundaries? If yes, give a constructive encoding and decoding algorithm that guarantees uniqueness and completeness. If not, characterize the restrictions.
- Decoding: Precisely define the mapping from the highest-scoring subgraph to the output mention set. When multiple mention configurations are consistent with the same path, what criterion yields a unique output? Does this choice influence training consistency (i.e., numerator vs denominator mismatch)?
- Comparisons: Please add baselines with modern nested NER methods (span-based and pretrained transformers) on ACE and GENIA, and report significance tests. Also report Finkel & Manning (2009) on ACE for completeness.
- Training/inference details: Provide exact hardware, runtimes per epoch, memory usage, implementation details for inside–outside on the multigraph (treatment of the root hyperedge; topological order; semiring), and confirm linear complexity constants. Include ablations of feature groups and the mention penalty.
- Data and evaluation: Clarify evaluation scripts, exact matching criteria, handling of crossing entities during decoding, and the impact of GENIA re-tokenization on comparability with prior work. Will you release code and the exact preprocessing pipeline?

Overall, while the core idea is interesting and potentially useful, the theoretical claim is not substantiated, empirical comparisons are dated, and there are numerous clarity and presentation issues. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
