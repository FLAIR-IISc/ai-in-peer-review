Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a multigraph-based structured prediction model for recognizing overlapping named entities. The key idea is to assign explicit semantics to multiple edges between the same pair of nodes (“entity separators”), enabling an edge-based representation of how entities start, continue, and end across tokens. Training uses a log-linear model with features on edges and generalized inside–outside inference with linear time complexity in sentence length and number of entity types.

Main contributions as I see them:
1) A conceptual and formal modeling contribution: an edge-based multigraph representation with explicit “entity separators” that compactly encodes overlapping spans with O(nT) inference, distinguishing it from both linear-chain and hypergraph approaches.
2) A theoretical analysis that identifies and explains spurious structures in the mention hypergraph model and argues that the proposed multigraph model is nondeficient (its partition function does not include unattainable structures).
3) An empirical evaluation on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA showing consistent, if modest, improvements over a strong mention hypergraph baseline using identical features; plus analysis separating overlapping vs non-overlapping sentences and a non-overlapping CoNLL2003 benchmark.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea with explicit edge semantics:
  The introduction of “entity separators” (E, C, S and their combinations) is intuitive, compact, and directly maps to the phenomena of overlapping entities. The multigraph formulation unifies roles (start/continue/end) across adjacent tokens and provides a clean alternative to prior hypergraph representations.
- Theoretical clarity on deficiency:
  The paper carefully explains why the mention hypergraph’s normalization sums over distinct entity combinations that collapse to the same subgraph, producing spurious structures. In contrast, the multigraph’s path-based inside computation enumerates valid structures without duplication. This is a meaningful conceptual advance that helps the community reason about objective-deficiency issues in structured models.
- Empirical improvements with controlled comparisons:
  On ACE2004/2005 and GENIA, the proposed model outperforms the reimplemented mention hypergraph baseline, often by 0.4–2.1 F1, using identical features and the same mention penalty tuning. The head-to-head setup isolates the effect of the modeling choice. The split analysis (sentences with vs without overlapping mentions) is also informative and shows gains extend beyond overlapping cases.
- Maintains linear-time inference and trains efficiently:
  The model preserves O(nT) complexity and shows faster convergence (fewer iterations) than the mention hypergraph, with only a modest per-iteration overhead (~12%). This makes the method practical and scalable.
- Reproducibility orientation:
  The authors describe features, tuning, preprocessing, and plan to release code. The supplementary material adds detail on the spurious structure argument and feature design.

Weaknesses
- Magnitude and statistical significance of gains:
  While consistent, improvements are modest on some datasets (e.g., ~0.4 F1 on GENIA after tuning). The paper does not report statistical significance tests or confidence intervals, making it difficult to judge robustness. Given the relatively small absolute gains, significance testing is important.
- Limited baselines relative to current practice:
  The evaluation omits more recent neural models for nested/overlapping NER, which are standard in contemporary ACL venues. Even if the focus is on structured formalisms, comparisons to strong neural encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/CRF or transformer-based nested NER) would contextualize the practical impact of the modeling contribution.
- Ambiguity handling and crossing structures:
  The paper notes that ambiguous outputs are “resolved as nested rather than crossing entities.” It remains unclear whether the model can truly produce crossing structures, how many such cases occur in the datasets, and whether this decoding choice biases results. Since one of the motivations is handling overlapping (including non-nested) entities, this requires clarification and possibly a dedicated evaluation.
- Interaction across entity types:
  The model appears to treat types largely independently (no cross-type features), connected only via a root hyperedge. While experiments suggest this may not hurt, the paper does not quantify or analyze cases where type interactions matter (e.g., consistency constraints, mutual exclusivity), nor does it compare to baselines that explicitly couple types.
- GENIA preprocessing and comparability:
  The tokenization strategy (splitting tokens at punctuation) deviates from Finkel and Manning’s approach. This can affect comparability to prior results and may change task difficulty. The paper should discuss how this impacts absolute numbers and ensure fairness in cross-paper comparisons.

Questions to authors
- Can the model produce crossing entities at decoding time? If yes, please provide examples and a quantitative breakdown across datasets. If not, how does the forced “nested” interpretation affect evaluation on datasets where crossing occurs?
- Are the reported improvements statistically significant (e.g., bootstrap or paired tests at sentence level)? Please include p-values or confidence intervals.
- How strong is the coupling across types? Is the joint normalization over all types or effectively a product across types? Did you try features that capture interactions between types, and what was the impact?
- Sensitivity to the entity separator set: Did you experiment with fewer/more separator labels or different groupings? How does this affect performance and speed?
- Can you provide more formal proof or sketch ensuring a one-to-one correspondence between entity combinations and paths in your multigraph (i.e., the nondeficiency claim), beyond the illustrative examples?

Overall evaluation
This paper presents a neat and principled modeling idea with a clear theoretical insight about spurious structures, maintains linear-time inference, and shows consistent empirical gains over a strong hypergraph baseline under matched conditions. The main limitations are the modest magnitude of gains, lack of significance testing, and missing comparisons to modern neural nested NER. With clarifications and strengthened experiments, this work would make a solid contribution on structured modeling for overlapping entities. I lean toward acceptance if the authors address evaluation concerns and clarify crossing/nesting handling.
