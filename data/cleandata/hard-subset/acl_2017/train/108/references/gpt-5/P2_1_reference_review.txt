Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a multigraph-based structured prediction model for recognizing overlapping (including nested and crossing) entities. The core idea is to assign explicit semantics to edges between adjacent token-position states via “entity separators” (S, E, C and their combinations), yielding a multigraph where multiple edges may connect the same node pair. Training uses a log-linear CRF-like objective with inside–outside inference over the directed multigraph; decoding uses max-product. The authors argue (i) the model achieves O(nT) time like mention hypergraphs (Lu & Roth, 2015), (ii) avoids “spurious structures,” hence is nondeficient, and (iii) improves F1 over mention hypergraphs and a linear-chain CRF on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA; they also show performance on CoNLL 2003.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Modeling contribution: A compact edge-semantic multigraph formalism for overlapping NER with linear-time inference and a clear edge-based interpretation via entity separators.
2) Theoretical claim: Analysis arguing the model is nondeficient (no spurious structures in the partition function), in contrast to mention hypergraphs.
3) Empirical evidence: Consistent, modest improvements over a strong structured baseline (mention hypergraph) on ACE and GENIA with the same feature sets; analysis of convergence behavior.

Strengths
1) Clear, principled modeling idea with explicit edge semantics. The separator-based multigraph is intuitive and cleanly captures start/end/continue events, enabling a compact representation of nested/crossing spans while keeping O(nT) complexity. The paper also situates the approach within a “state-based vs edge-based” paradigm discussion, which clarifies design choices.

2) Theoretical diagnosis of spurious structures. The paper provides a concrete explanation (with examples) of how mention hypergraphs aggregate multiple distinct output interpretations into the same subgraph in the normalization term, and argues the proposed multigraph avoids this by summing over path-wise edge choices. While concise, the argument is plausible and connects to observed empirical effects (higher recall, faster convergence in iterations).

3) Fair experimental setup versus the strongest comparable non-neural baseline. The authors reimplement mention hypergraphs, match features closely, and report with/without F1 tuning, which makes the observed +1.3–2.1 F1 gains on ACE credible. The split of test data into sentences with and without overlapping entities further diagnoses where gains come from.

4) Inference and training details are replicable. The objective, features (appendix), hyperparameters, and handling of decoding ambiguity are described; code release is promised. The runtime discussion (slightly slower per iteration but fewer iterations) is informative.

5) Generality and potential beyond NER. The separator idea is appealing for other overlapping structures (e.g., certain parsing tasks), and the paper articulates this broader vision.

Weaknesses
1) Missing comparisons to contemporary neural nested NER methods. By current standards, the empirical evaluation is dated: no comparisons to neural span-based or layered models (e.g., Ju et al., 2018; Sohrab & Miwa, 2018; Shibuya & Hovy, 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2020). Even if the contribution is modeling/formalism, a modernized experiment (e.g., plugging the multigraph into a neural feature extractor) or at least a head-to-head on standard nested benchmarks would substantively strengthen the case for impact.

2) GENIA preprocessing comparability. The paper alters tokenization for GENIA (splitting tokens at punctuation) and treats coordinated entities specially. Yet it compares to Finkel & Manning (2009), which used a different handling. This complicates direct comparison. A controlled ablation showing the effect of the new preprocessing or reporting numbers with the canonical JNLPBA/GENIA preprocessing would improve credibility.

3) Limited ablations and alternative formulations. The authors note that the separator edges could be recast as labels in a linear-chain “gap tagging” model; they argue this induces unnecessary dependencies, but provide no empirical evidence. A baseline with separator tags (first-order CRF over gaps) would be a strong and fair comparator to demonstrate the specific benefit of the multigraph formulation.

4) Theoretical rigor of nondeficiency claim. The supplement gives illustrative examples, but a formal proof that the partition function enumerates each distinct output exactly once (with a clear definition of the output space Y and the mapping from subgraphs to outputs) would make the claim more solid. Also, it would help to quantify how much spuriousness affects training likelihood and gradients in the mention hypergraph, and how that relates to the observed gains.

5) Scope of empirical gains and analysis. Improvements, while consistent, are modest. The paper attributes GENIA’s lower recall on overlapping cases to fewer overlapping examples, but this is speculative; a more detailed error analysis (types/lengths of overlaps, crossing vs nested, per-entity-type improvements) would clarify where the model truly helps and where it struggles.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a formal proof (or a precise theorem statement with assumptions) that your multigraph’s partition function sums over a one-to-one set of distinct outputs, i.e., no two distinct outputs share the same contributing subgraph?
- How would your model perform when paired with neural features (e.g., biaffine/span encoders or transformer contextual embeddings)? Can you report results versus recent nested NER baselines on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA?
- Can you include an ablation where you replace the multigraph with a gap-label CRF over the 8 separator types, and quantify the performance/runtime differences?
- For GENIA, can you report results under the canonical preprocessing used by prior work (or at least quantify the impact of your token splitting on performance)?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a neat, well-explained modeling contribution with reasonable theoretical motivation and careful classic-feature experiments, improving over a strong structured baseline at the same asymptotic complexity. However, the lack of comparisons to modern neural nested NER, limited ablations, and GENIA comparability issues weaken the case for broad impact. With added baselines/ablations and strengthened theoretical exposition, this work would be significantly more compelling.
