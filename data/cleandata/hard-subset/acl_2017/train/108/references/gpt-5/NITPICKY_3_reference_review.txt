Summary
The paper proposes a multigraph-based structured model for recognizing overlapping entities. Each token and entity type is represented with two token-level states (in-entity Ikt and out-of-entity Okt), and edges are assigned explicit semantics as “entity separators” capturing start/end/continue configurations between adjacent tokens (8 cases). The model is trained as a log-linear CRF-style model with features on edges; inference uses an inside–outside style algorithm with O(nT) complexity. The authors argue their formulation avoids the “spurious structures” (deficiency) present in the mention hypergraph model (Lu & Roth, 2015), while matching its complexity, and report improvements on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA; they also show results on CoNLL 2003.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A reparameterization of overlapping-entity modeling into a multigraph with semantically distinct multi-edges (“entity separators”), together with an inside–outside inference scheme that remains O(nT).
2) An analysis and discussion claiming nondeficiency (no spurious structures) compared to the mention hypergraph, with a worked example in the appendix.
3) Empirical comparisons to a strong pre-neural baseline (mention hypergraph), showing modest F1 improvements on ACE and GENIA.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity of edge semantics: The use of multi-edges with explicit “separator” semantics is clean and intuitively ties local boundary configurations to features. This helps reason about overlapping configurations without proliferating states.
- Maintains linear-time inference: The model appears to preserve O(nT) complexity while supporting overlaps, a practical requirement for sentence-level NER.
- Competitive results vs. a strong classical baseline: On ACE and GENIA, the model beats an in-house reproduction of the mention hypergraph baseline by up to ~2 F1 (ACE2004) and ~0.4 F1 (GENIA), suggesting the formulation is at least competitive in the classic feature-based setting.
- Useful discussion on state- vs edge-based paradigms: The paper offers a helpful taxonomy and highlights representational trade-offs, which can inform future work on structure encodings beyond NER.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and theory: The approach feels like a representational variant of Lu & Roth (2015). The “first multigraph” claim is tenuous given that factor graphs routinely allow multiple factors/potentials over the same variables, and structured models often attach semantics to edges. The nondeficiency claim is asserted informally (“can be verified”) without a rigorous proof (e.g., a bijection between subgraphs and valid mention-sets, or a measure-theoretic argument ruling out path aliasing). The appendix example is anecdotal and only addresses one restricted configuration.
- Potential representational limitation (multiplicity): The separator scheme is binary for S/E/C at each boundary. It is unclear how multiple same-type entities starting or ending at the same boundary (e.g., two nested spans with identical start) are represented; the 8-case encoding seems to lack multiplicity. The paper does not discuss whether datasets contain such cases, nor whether the model forbids them. This is a serious modeling ambiguity.
- Outdated empirical context: No comparisons to modern neural nested/overlapping NER models (e.g., layered/stacked models, span-based neural models, hypergraph/neural semi-CRFs). Without such baselines, the practical significance is unclear. The reported gains over a 2015 CRF-based model are modest and may fall within variance.
- Evaluation and preprocessing confounds: For GENIA, tokenization is altered (splitting on punctuation) and coordinated/discontiguous mentions are collapsed; yet the paper compares to prior results that may not share these choices. This risks apples-to-oranges comparisons. CoNLL 2003 results are discussed but the table is missing; experimental protocols (tagger, casing, gazetteers, embeddings) are not specified. There are no significance tests.
- Decoding choices and crossing mentions: The model resolves ambiguous structures as nested rather than crossing; the datasets’ treatment of crossing mentions is not clarified. This could bias evaluation if crossing mentions occur (ACE can have non-nested overlaps). More generally, the mapping from subgraph to entity set during decoding is underspecified and depends on ad hoc interpretation rules.

Questions to authors
- Multiplicity: Can your separator encoding represent multiple same-type entities starting (or ending) at the same boundary? If yes, please formalize how; if no, what constraints are imposed and how frequent are violated cases in ACE/GENIA?
- Nondeficiency: Please provide a formal proof (or at least a precise proposition with proof sketch) that your normalization sums over exactly the set of distinct mention-sets (no duplicates; no omissions), and clarify whether multiple edge-paths can yield the same entity set in your multigraph.
- Crossing vs nested: Do ACE/GENIA test sets contain crossing entities? If so, how does your forced nesting resolution affect recall and evaluation? Please quantify.
- Comparability on GENIA: Given altered tokenization and the treatment of coordinated/discontiguous mentions, how comparable are your numbers to Finkel & Manning (2009)? Can you report results under their preprocessing or replicate their setup?
- Statistical significance and variance: Are the reported improvements significant (e.g., bootstrap resampling)? Please report multiple runs or confidence intervals.
- Runtime constants: You note 12% more time per iteration but fewer iterations. Please report wall-clock training and decoding times per sentence for both models, and memory footprint.
- Modern baselines: Can you add comparisons to representative neural nested/overlapping NER methods to contextualize the contribution?

Additional comments (minor)
- Many typos/formatting/citation glitches (e.g., missing spaces in complexity, malformed references with “?” placeholders, inconsistent notation Tkt/Tt, footnotes inlined). Figures are referenced but some tables (CoNLL results) are missing. Please proofread carefully and standardize references.
- The description of features is high-level; consider adding a clear feature template table and ablation (e.g., impact of the mention penalty, Brown clusters, edge-type features).
- Clarify the independence assumptions across types. Does the root hyperedge couple types in any way beyond normalization, or are types effectively independent during inference? This matters for comparing to linear-chain CRF baselines that model inter-type transitions.
