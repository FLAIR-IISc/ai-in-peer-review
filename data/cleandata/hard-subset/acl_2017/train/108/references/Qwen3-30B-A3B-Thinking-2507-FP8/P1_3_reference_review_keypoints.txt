- Proposes a multigraph-based model for overlapping NER that assigns explicit semantics to multiple edges between node pairs.  
- Theoretical claim: the model is "non-deficient" (avoids spurious structures) while retaining O(nT) time complexity like mention hypergraphs.  
- Empirically outperforms the mention hypergraph model on ACE datasets, up to 2.1 F1 points.  
- Claims novelty as the first structured prediction model using multigraphs for overlapping entities.  

- Strength: Provides a clear theoretical analysis showing avoidance of spurious structures in normalization.  
- Strength: Demonstrates statistically significant empirical improvements on ACE2004/2005, with analysis on overlapping vs. non-overlapping sentences.  
- Strength: Clear presentation and visual comparisons; strong conceptual case for edge-based vs. state-based paradigms.  

- Weakness: Novelty is questionable—multigraph may be a reparameterization of the mention hypergraph rather than a fundamentally new representation.  
- Weakness: No empirical evidence that spurious structures in the mention hypergraph actually harm performance.  
- Weakness: Limited comparisons to other overlapping/nested NER methods (e.g., Finkel & Manning 2009, Chen et al. 2020).  
- Weakness: Lacks ablation studies to identify which components drive improvements (representation vs. features/regularization).  
- Weakness: Unclear explanation for worse performance on GENIA for overlapping sentences; claim of insufficient training data is unsupported.  

- Question: How does the multigraph fundamentally differ from Lu & Roth (2015) mention hypergraphs beyond edge reparameterization; what cannot be expressed by a hypergraph?  
- Question: Can you provide empirical evidence that spurious structures in the mention hypergraph negatively affect ACE performance?  
- Question: Provide exact counts of overlapping entity examples in GENIA vs. ACE to support the training-data explanation.  

- Additional comment: Paper is well-written with a valuable theoretical contribution, but needs stronger novelty claims and more robust empirical validation; emphasize the edge-based vs. state-based conceptual contribution.
