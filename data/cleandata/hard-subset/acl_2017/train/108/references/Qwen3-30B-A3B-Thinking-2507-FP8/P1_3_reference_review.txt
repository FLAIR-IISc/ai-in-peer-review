

# Peer Review: "A Multigraph-based Model for Overlapping Entity Recognition"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a multigraph-based model for overlapping entity recognition (NER), where entities can overlap (e.g., "China" within "Bank of China"). The authors claim their model represents overlapping structures through explicit semantics assigned to different edges between the same pair of nodes in a multigraph representation. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. **Theoretical analysis showing their model is "non-deficient"** (does not have the spurious structures issue) while maintaining the same time complexity (O(nT)) as the previous state-of-the-art mention hypergraph model.
2. **Empirical results demonstrating superior performance** over the mention hypergraph model on ACE datasets (with up to 2.1 F1-point improvement).
3. **Novel multigraph representation** for overlapping entities, which the authors claim is the first structured prediction model using multigraphs for this task.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical contribution regarding spurious structures**: The paper provides a clear and well-justified analysis of the "spurious structures" issue in the mention hypergraph model. The authors demonstrate that their model avoids this problem by having a normalization term that doesn't contain spurious structures, which is a significant theoretical contribution. This is particularly valuable because it addresses a fundamental limitation of previous approaches.

2. **Empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over the mention hypergraph model across multiple datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005), with up to 2.1 F1-point improvement on ACE2004. The results are statistically significant and well-presented. The additional analysis splitting the data into sentences with and without overlapping entities further supports the model's effectiveness.

3. **Clear presentation of the model and comparison**: The paper provides a well-structured explanation of the proposed multigraph model and its relationship to the mention hypergraph model. The visual comparisons (Figures 2, 4, and 5) effectively illustrate the differences between approaches. The authors also make a strong case for the edge-based paradigm versus the state-based paradigm, which is a valuable conceptual contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty of the multigraph representation**: The paper claims their model is "the first structured prediction model utilizing multigraphs" for overlapping structures, but the relationship between their multigraph and the mention hypergraph model is not sufficiently distinct. The paper itself states that "the edges in our model represent combinations of multiple edges in the mention hypergraph model." This makes the novelty claim questionable, as the approach appears to be a reparameterization of the hypergraph model rather than a fundamentally new representation.

2. **Lack of evidence linking spurious structures to performance**: While the paper provides a strong theoretical argument about the spurious structures issue, it doesn't demonstrate that this issue actually impacts performance in practice. The authors claim their model "resolves the spurious structures issue," but they don't show that the mention hypergraph model's performance is negatively affected by this issue. The empirical improvement could be due to other factors, such as different feature sets or model regularization.

3. **Limited comparison to other state-of-the-art approaches**: The paper primarily compares against the mention hypergraph model, but doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison to other recent approaches for overlapping NER. For example, the paper doesn't compare against the nested NER model by Finkel and Manning (2009) or more recent approaches like the one by Chen et al. (2020). This limits the significance of the claimed improvements.

4. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to determine which components of their model contribute to the performance improvement. For example, it's unclear whether the improvement comes from the multigraph representation itself or from other aspects of their implementation, such as the specific feature engineering or regularization parameters.

5. **Ambiguous interpretation of results on GENIA**: The paper reports that their model performs worse than the mention hypergraph model on the GENIA dataset for sentences containing overlapping entities (Table 4). The authors attribute this to "insufficient amount of training data on overlapping entities," but they don't provide evidence or analysis to support this claim. This weakens the overall argument for the superiority of their model.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their model is "the first structured prediction model utilizing multigraphs" for overlapping structures. Can you clarify how your model fundamentally differs from the hypergraph-based approach in Lu and Roth (2015), beyond the reparameterization of edges? What specific aspects of the multigraph representation cannot be expressed using a hypergraph?

2. The paper states that the mention hypergraph model has "spurious structures" while your model does not. Can you provide empirical evidence that this theoretical issue actually impacts performance? Specifically, can you show that the mention hypergraph model's performance on the ACE datasets is negatively affected by this issue?

3. The paper reports that your model performs worse than the mention hypergraph on GENIA for sentences with overlapping entities. Given that GENIA has a lower percentage of overlapping entities (24% compared to ACE's 32-58% as shown in Table 4), could this be due to the model being trained on a smaller number of overlapping entity examples, rather than a fundamental limitation of your approach? Can you provide the exact number of overlapping entity examples in the GENIA training data compared to ACE?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable theoretical contribution by identifying and resolving the spurious structures issue. However, the novelty claim needs strengthening, and the empirical evidence for the claimed advantages needs to be more robust. The authors should address the questions above to better establish the significance of their contribution.

The paper's contribution to the edge-based paradigm versus the state-based paradigm is conceptually valuable and deserves more emphasis in the paper. This conceptual distinction could be a significant contribution to the field beyond the specific application to NER.
