

# ACL Peer Review

## Summary

This paper proposes a multigraph-based model for overlapping entity recognition (OER), a task where entities can overlap with each other (e.g., "China" within "Bank of China"). The authors argue that previous approaches like linear-chain CRFs, tree-based models, and hypergraph-based models have limitations in handling overlapping entities. The paper introduces a novel "edge-based paradigm" for structured prediction, contrasting with the commonly used "state-based paradigm," and claims that their multigraph-based model avoids the "spurious structures" issue present in the previous state-of-the-art model (mention hypergraph) while maintaining the same inference time complexity.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Theoretical contribution**: The paper's most significant contribution is the identification of the "edge-based paradigm" as a new approach to structured prediction, with a clear theoretical analysis showing how their model avoids spurious structures that plague the mention hypergraph model. This is a substantial theoretical contribution to structured prediction literature.

2. **Empirical contribution**: The authors demonstrate that their model consistently outperforms the mention hypergraph model across multiple standard OER datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, and GENIA), with improvements of up to 2.1 F1 points.

3. **Methodological contribution**: The paper provides a clear framework for modeling overlapping entities through explicit semantics assigned to edges in a multigraph representation, which leads to a more natural representation of overlapping structures.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical insight on spurious structures**: The paper provides a clear and well-supported theoretical argument about why the mention hypergraph model contains "spurious structures" in its normalization term, while their model does not. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses a fundamental limitation of previous approaches that has not been adequately addressed before. The detailed analysis in Section 5.2 and the supplementary material (Section A) provide strong evidence for this claim.

2. **Clear presentation of the model**: The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the proposed multigraph-based model, with helpful visualizations (Figures 2-4) that effectively demonstrate how the model works. The description of the 8 possible entity separators (Figure 3) is particularly insightful and makes the model's approach intuitive.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The model consistently outperforms the mention hypergraph model across multiple datasets, with statistically significant improvements in F1 scores (Table 2 and 3). The additional experiments in Table 4 show that the model is particularly effective at recognizing overlapping entities when sufficient training data is available.

4. **Important distinction between paradigms**: The paper makes a valuable contribution by clearly differentiating between the "state-based paradigm" and "edge-based paradigm" for structured prediction. This conceptual distinction helps frame the field's understanding of how to model overlapping structures and provides a foundation for future research.

5. **Practical demonstration of broader applicability**: The paper demonstrates that the model can also outperform linear-chain CRF on the standard CONLL2003 dataset (Table 5), suggesting that the proposed approach has broader applicability beyond just overlapping entity recognition.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the paper introduces a "multigraph-based model," the approach is fundamentally similar to the mention hypergraph model (Lu and Roth, 2015), with the main difference being the explicit semantics assigned to edges. The paper doesn't sufficiently argue why this refinement is novel enough to warrant a new model rather than a modification of the existing one. The authors acknowledge this similarity but don't adequately justify the significance of their contribution.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent OER models**: The paper focuses primarily on comparing with the mention hypergraph model (Lu and Roth, 2015), but there are several more recent approaches to overlapping entity recognition that should be considered, such as neural network-based methods (Wang et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020). Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess the relative significance of the proposed model in the current landscape of OER research.

3. **Insufficient explanation of faster convergence**: The paper claims that their model converges faster than the mention hypergraph model (Figure 7), but doesn't explain why. The authors state that each training iteration requires 12% more time, yet the model converges in fewer iterations. A more detailed explanation of this phenomenon would strengthen the paper.

4. **Limited analysis of the GENIA performance gap**: The model performs worse than the mention hypergraph on the overlapping entities portion of the GENIA dataset (Table 4), and the authors attribute this to insufficient training data. However, they don't provide specific data on how much additional training data would be needed to close this gap, which would help readers understand the practical limitations of the approach.

5. **Lack of ablation study on the edge-based paradigm**: The authors claim that the edge-based paradigm is more suitable for overlapping entity recognition, but they don't provide an ablation study to support this claim. For example, they could show how a state-based version of their model performs compared to their edge-based model, which would provide stronger evidence for their paradigm shift argument.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the multigraph model is the first structured prediction model utilizing multigraphs for overlapping structures. However, Zhang et al. (2020) proposed a multigraph-based model for overlapping entity recognition. How does your model differ from theirs, and why is your approach more theoretically sound?

2. Could you provide a concrete example of how the spurious structures in the mention hypergraph model lead to suboptimal predictions in practice? The paper shows that the normalization term contains spurious structures, but it's not clear how this directly impacts the model's predictions.

3. The paper states that the model converges faster than the mention hypergraph model (Figure 7), but each training iteration requires 12% more time. What is the specific reason for this faster convergence? Is it related to the structure of the model or the optimization process?

4. In Table 4, your model performs worse than the mention hypergraph model on the overlapping entities portion of the GENIA dataset. Given that GENIA has a lower percentage of overlapping entities (24%) compared to ACE datasets (32-42%), what specific data augmentation or training strategies would you recommend to improve performance on GENIA?

5. The paper mentions that the model can be used for other problems involving overlapping structures. Could you provide a specific example of another NLP task where this model might be applicable, and how it would be adapted for that task?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of structured prediction for overlapping entity recognition. The identification of the "edge-based paradigm" is particularly insightful and could inspire further research in this direction. The empirical results are strong, and the paper is well-organized with helpful visualizations. 

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses listed above, particularly by comparing with more recent OER approaches and providing a more detailed explanation of the theoretical advantages of the edge-based paradigm. The claim of novelty could also be better supported by demonstrating how the proposed model differs fundamentally from existing approaches rather than just being a refinement of an existing one.
