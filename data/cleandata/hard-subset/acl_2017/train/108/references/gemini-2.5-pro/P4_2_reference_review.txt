### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for named entity recognition (NER), specifically designed to handle overlapping entities. The core of the model lies in its representation, where the transitions between states of adjacent tokens are modeled using a set of eight distinct edge types, termed "entity separators." Each separator explicitly encodes the structural role of the token boundary (e.g., an entity starting, ending, continuing, or a combination thereof). The model is formulated as a log-linear model and trained with a standard objective function. The authors provide both theoretical and empirical comparisons against previous state-of-the-art models, particularly the hypergraph-based model of Lu and Roth (2015).

In my assessment, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel multigraph formulation for representing overlapping structures.** The paper's primary contribution is the introduction of a new way to model overlapping entities using multigraphs. The concept of "entity separators" as distinct, semantically meaningful edges between pairs of nodes is an intuitive and powerful representational device for capturing complex structural dependencies in sequence labeling tasks.
2.  **A theoretical analysis identifying and resolving the "spurious structures" issue.** The paper provides a clear analysis of a deficiency in the prior state-of-the-art mention hypergraph model, where the normalization term incorrectly sums over structural interpretations that cannot be produced by the model. The proposed multigraph model is shown to be "nondeficient," representing a significant theoretical improvement in the soundness of the probabilistic objective function.
3.  **A strong empirical validation of the proposed model.** The paper demonstrates that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on three standard benchmark datasets for overlapping NER (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA), outperforming a strong reimplemented baseline. The evaluation is further strengthened by detailed analysis of performance on subsets of the data and on a standard non-overlapping NER task.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Clarity of the Proposed Model:** The multigraph-based formulation is a novel contribution to structured prediction for NLP. The paper clearly articulates the distinction between "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms, situating its work within the latter. The core idea of using semantically explicit edges ("entity separators") to model the complex interactions at token boundaries is both elegant and intuitive. The model is presented with clarity, and the illustrations (e.g., Figure 2) are effective in communicating the core concepts.

2.  **Significant Theoretical Insight:** The analysis in Section 5.2 regarding "spurious structures" is a key strength. The authors identify a subtle but important flaw in the objective function of the mention hypergraph model, where the partition function Z aggregates scores from structures that do not have a one-to-one mapping with the model's output space. The proposed multigraph model rectifies this issue, making it "nondeficient." This demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying models and provides a strong theoretical motivation for the new formulation, beyond just being an alternative representation.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive and rigorous. The authors compare their model against strong baselines, including their own implementation of the mention hypergraph model, on three widely used datasets for this task. The use of an identical feature set for both their model and the primary baseline allows for a fair comparison of the underlying model structures. The consistent F1-score improvements shown in Tables 2 and 3 provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model. The additional analyses, including the breakdown of performance on sentences with and without overlapping entities (Table 4) and the experiment on the non-overlapping CoNLL-2003 dataset (Table 5), add significant depth to the evaluation.

4.  **Good Scientific Practice and Reproducibility:** The paper provides substantial detail regarding the experimental setup. The data splits, feature sets (Appendix B), and hyperparameter settings (Appendix C) are clearly documented. The authors' commitment to releasing their code further strengthens the paper's contribution to the community by facilitating reproducibility and future work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The link between the "nondeficient" property and empirical gains is not well established.** While the theoretical analysis of spurious structures is a major strength, the paper does not provide a clear explanation for *why* being nondeficient leads to better empirical performance. In Section 7, the authors "speculate this is due to the fact that as a nondeficient model... our model is more confident in making its predictions," which is not a sufficiently rigorous explanation. It is unclear if the deficient normalization term in the baseline systematically biases the gradients during training in a way that hurts performance (e.g., recall). A more thorough discussion of the practical implications of correcting the partition function would significantly strengthen the paper's central argument.

2.  **The model could be framed as an incremental refinement of the hypergraph model.** While the multigraph perspective is presented as novel, the underlying model can be interpreted as a clever re-parameterization or refactoring of the mention hypergraph model. The eight entity separators in the multigraph model correspond to specific combinations of hyperedges in the mention hypergraph model. For example, the `ECS` separator appears to be a compact representation of a hyperedge that simultaneously models an entity ending and a new one starting. The paper would be stronger if it more directly addressed this relationship and clarified whether the main contribution is the multigraph formalism itself, or the identification of a more effective set of composite factors (represented as edges) that also happens to solve the spurious structures problem.

3.  **The explanation of spurious structures could be improved for clarity.** The detailed example of spurious structures provided in Appendix A is difficult to follow. The example involves 9 possible entity combinations, 7 of which are claimed to map to the same subgraph, but the reasoning is dense. The crucial point from Section 5.2—that distinct interpretations like "crossing entities" (`w0w1`, `w1w2`) and "nested entities" (`w0w1w2`, `w1`) can map to the same subgraph—is a much more direct and understandable example. Using a simplified graphical illustration for that specific case would make this important theoretical point more accessible to a broader audience.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your primary theoretical argument is that the proposed model is "nondeficient," unlike the mention hypergraph model. You speculate that this leads to higher recall. Could you please provide a more concrete explanation for this connection? For instance, does the deficient partition function in the mention hypergraph model lead to gradients that systematically penalize the prediction of multiple entities, thereby suppressing recall?

2.  Could you elaborate on the relationship between your multigraph model and the mention hypergraph model? Is it possible to formulate the mention hypergraph model with a more complex set of hyperedges that would be equivalent to your "entity separators," and would such a formulation also solve the "spurious structures" problem? This would help clarify whether the key innovation is the multigraph formalism itself or the specific factorization of the problem that your formalism enables.

3.  The results on CoNLL-2003, a non-overlapping dataset, are intriguing. You note that your model can predict overlapping entities even when none exist in the training data, and that this leads to improved performance. Could you provide a qualitative analysis of this? What kinds of overlapping entities (if any) did your model predict on the CoNLL-2003 test set, and how did these predictions contribute to a higher F1 score compared to the linear-chain CRF baseline?
