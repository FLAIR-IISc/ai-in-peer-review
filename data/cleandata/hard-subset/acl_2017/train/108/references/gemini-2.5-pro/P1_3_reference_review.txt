### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping named entities. The standard approach of treating NER as a sequence labeling task with models like linear-chain CRFs fails in this setting. The proposed model represents the problem using a multigraph where nodes represent whether a token is inside or outside an entity of a certain type, and a set of 8 distinct, semantically rich edges (called "entity separators") connect nodes at adjacent time steps. These edges explicitly model whether an entity is starting, continuing, or ending at a given position, allowing for the representation of complex overlapping structures. The model is trained as a standard log-linear model and uses an efficient inside-outside algorithm for inference, maintaining the same linear time complexity as the state-of-the-art mention hypergraph model. The authors claim their model is theoretically "nondeficient," as it resolves a "spurious structures" issue present in the mention hypergraph model's normalization term. Experiments on ACE, GENIA, and CoNLL 2003 datasets show that the proposed model outperforms strong baselines, including the mention hypergraph model.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel and Elegant Multigraph Formalism:** The paper introduces a new and intuitive way to model overlapping structures by assigning explicit semantics to the edges of a graph rather than encoding complexity in the nodes or hyperedges. This "edge-based" paradigm is a valuable contribution to structured prediction.
2.  **Theoretical Analysis of Model Deficiency:** The identification and explanation of the "spurious structures" problem in the prior state-of-the-art mention hypergraph model is a significant theoretical insight. Demonstrating that the proposed multigraph model is "nondeficient" provides a strong motivation for the new formulation beyond just empirical gains.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results, demonstrating consistent and sometimes significant improvements over the strong mention hypergraph baseline on multiple standard benchmarks for overlapping NER.

### Strengths

1.  **Model Novelty and Intuitiveness:** The proposed multigraph representation is novel and elegant. The concept of "entity separators" (edges representing combinations of Start, Continue, End) is a very clean and understandable way to decompose the complex problem of overlapping entities. This contrasts with the mention hypergraph model, which uses more abstract hyperedges and node types. This clear formalism is a definite strength.
2.  **Sound Theoretical Motivation:** The paper's strongest point is the theoretical analysis in Section 5. The authors clearly articulate the "spurious structures" problem in the mention hypergraph model, where the normalization term sums over entity combinations that map to the same subgraph, making the objective function a loose proxy for the true data likelihood. By designing a "nondeficient" model that avoids this issue, the authors provide a compelling theoretical justification for their approach. This analysis elevates the paper from a simple proposal of a new architecture to a work with deeper modeling insights. The observed faster convergence (in iterations) provides empirical support for this cleaner objective.
3.  **Comprehensive and Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on three standard overlapping NER datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) and one non-overlapping dataset (CoNLL 2003). The proposed model consistently outperforms the primary baseline, the mention hypergraph model, with a notable F1-score improvement of 2.1 points on ACE2004. The detailed breakdown of performance on sentences with and without overlapping entities, as well as the strong performance on the non-overlapping CoNLL dataset, demonstrates the model's robustness and general applicability.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model is explained clearly with helpful illustrations (Figures 2 and 4). The distinction between "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms is insightful. The authors provide sufficient detail on features and promise to release their code, which strengthens the paper's potential impact and facilitates reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Discussion on Decoding Ambiguities:** Footnote 3 mentions a critical detail: "we resolved ambiguous structures by considering them as nested entities instead of crossing entities." This heuristic seems important for turning a highest-scoring subgraph into a final set of entities. The paper would be much stronger if it included an analysis of this step. How often do such ambiguities arise? Is this heuristic a standard practice, or is it specific to this work? Could this choice systematically favor one model over another? Given that the ability to handle various overlapping types (nested, crossing) is a core challenge, this decoding choice warrants a more prominent discussion.
2.  **The Link Between "Nondeficiency" and Performance is Speculative:** While the theoretical analysis of spurious structures is a major strength, the paper only speculates on its empirical consequences. The authors suggest that being nondeficient is the reason for higher recall and faster convergence. This is plausible, but not directly proven. A more direct analysis—for instance, by examining the gradients during training or quantifying the mass assigned to spurious structures by the baseline—could have made the connection between the theoretical advantage and the empirical gains more concrete.
3.  **The Model Appears to Treat Entity Types Independently:** The model structure, as depicted in Figure 4, seems to consist of parallel, independent chains for each entity type (T0, T1, etc.), all joined at the root. This suggests the model does not capture dependencies between different entity types (e.g., a GPE entity is unlikely to be part of an ORG entity in a specific way). While the strong CoNLL results suggest this may not be a critical limitation, many NER models benefit from modeling tag-to-tag transitions. The paper should acknowledge and discuss this potential limitation of its current formulation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the decoding heuristic in Footnote 3: Could you please quantify how frequently ambiguous subgraphs (which could be decoded as either nested or crossing entities) were encountered on the test sets? Furthermore, could you elaborate on the impact of your "prefer nested" heuristic on the final reported scores?
2.  In your discussion, you speculate that the nondeficient nature of your model leads to higher recall. Could you provide more intuition or analysis to support this specific claim? Does the model become more "confident" in its predictions by not wasting probability mass on structures that are valid mathematically but can never be decoded?
3.  As noted in my weaknesses, your model appears to assume independence between entity types. Was this a deliberate design choice for simplicity and efficiency? Do you see a straightforward way to incorporate cross-type dependencies into the multigraph framework, and do you believe it would lead to further improvements?
