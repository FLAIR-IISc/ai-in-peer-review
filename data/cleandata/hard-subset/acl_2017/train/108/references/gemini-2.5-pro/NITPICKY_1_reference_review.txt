### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for named entity recognition, specifically designed to handle overlapping entities. The core idea is to represent the different roles a token can play with respect to entity boundaries (e.g., starting, ending, continuing) as distinct edge types, which they term "entity separators." This formulation allows multiple, semantically different edges to connect the same pair of nodes, hence the "multigraph" designation. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) the novel multigraph model itself; (2) superior empirical performance against a state-of-the-art hypergraph model on multiple datasets; and (3) a theoretical advantage, showing their model is "non-deficient" by resolving a "spurious structures" issue present in the hypergraph baseline, while maintaining the same linear time complexity.

### Main Contributions (Reviewer's View)

1.  **A Novel Edge-Centric Model for Overlapping NER:** The primary contribution is the formulation of overlapping NER using a multigraph where different edge types directly encode the combinatorial roles of tokens at entity boundaries. This "entity separator" concept is an elegant and intuitive way to model complex, overlapping spans.
2.  **Theoretical Analysis of the "Spurious Structures" Problem:** The paper provides a valuable, albeit imperfectly explained, analysis of a deficiency in the baseline hypergraph model's objective function, where the normalization term incorrectly sums over scores of structures that are conflated by the model's representation. Demonstrating that their proposed model is "non-deficient" is a significant theoretical contribution.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation showing consistent, albeit modest, F1-score improvements over a strong baseline on several standard datasets, including those with and without overlapping entities.

### Strengths

1.  **Model Novelty and Elegance:** The core idea of using semantically explicit edges ("entity separators") to model overlapping structures is novel and compelling. It offers a potentially cleaner and more direct formalism compared to the complex node-and-hyperedge combinations of the Lu and Roth (2015) model. This "edge-based paradigm" is an interesting conceptual contribution.
2.  **Sound Experimental Design:** The authors conduct a rigorous comparison against the state-of-the-art mention hypergraph (MH) model. Crucially, they use an identical feature set for both their model and the MH baseline, ensuring a fair comparison of the underlying model structures. The inclusion of multiple datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) and a standard non-overlapping dataset (CONLL2003) provides a comprehensive performance evaluation.
3.  **Important Theoretical Insight:** The identification and resolution of the "spurious structures" problem is a key strength. This deficiency in the baseline model, where the partition function Z includes scores for entity combinations that cannot be decoded, is a subtle but important flaw. The claim that the proposed model is "non-deficient" while matching the `O(nT)` time complexity of the baseline is a strong theoretical result.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in presentation, clarity, and execution, which collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Severe Presentation and Clarity Issues:** The paper is exceptionally difficult to follow due to missing information and poor explanations.
    *   **Missing Figure:** The text explicitly refers to Figure 3 for an "illustration of these separators," which are the absolute core of the proposed model. This figure is not present in the paper, leaving the reader to guess at the visual representation of this central concept.
    *   **Unclear Theoretical Justification:** The argument that the model is "non-deficient" (Section 5.2) is asserted rather than explained. The provided justification—"This can be verified by the fact that the inside score of each node...is calculated as the sum of the scores associated with each path..."—is merely a description of the inside algorithm and does not constitute a proof. A clear, formal argument showing a one-to-one mapping between entity configurations and valid subgraphs is needed but absent. The appendix (Section A) that is meant to clarify this is convoluted and poorly written.
    *   **Unreadable Visualizations:** Figure 4, intended to illustrate the overall model, is excessively dense and cluttered, with illegibly small labels, rendering it almost useless.

2.  **Extremely Sloppy Formatting and Proofreading:** The paper exhibits a concerning lack of attention to detail, which calls into question the rigor of the entire work.
    *   **Broken Bibliography:** The reference section is a complete mess. Entries are malformed with stray characters and incorrect BibTeX outputs (e.g., `Alex et al\.,? 2007, Alex et al\.,`, `\Q2003\E`). A stray paragraph from Appendix D appears at the end of the reference list. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference.
    *   **Inconsistent and Imprecise Language:** Footnote 1 states, "we may also use edge to refer to hyperedge," which is imprecise. In a paper distinguishing its graph-based formalism from a hypergraph one, this casual conflation of terms is confusing.

3.  **Weakened Empirical Claims:** The detailed analysis in Table 4 slightly contradicts the main narrative. On the GENIA dataset, the proposed model performs slightly *worse* than the baseline on the subset of sentences containing overlapping entities. While the difference is small, it undermines the central claim that the model's primary advantage lies in its handling of overlapping structures. The authors' post-hoc explanation of "insufficient training data" is speculative.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Can you provide the missing Figure 3? This is essential for understanding your model's core "entity separator" mechanism.
2.  Could you provide a much clearer and more formal argument for why your model is "non-deficient"? A simple example illustrating how a specific entity combination maps to a unique subgraph in your model (and not in the MH model) would be highly beneficial. The current explanation is insufficient.
3.  The paper suffers from extensive and severe formatting issues, particularly in the bibliography. Can you confirm that the paper will be thoroughly proofread and corrected for the final version? The current state detracts significantly from the work's credibility.
4.  How do you reconcile the result in Table 4 (lower performance on overlapping GENIA sentences) with the primary claim that your model is superior for overlapping entity recognition?
