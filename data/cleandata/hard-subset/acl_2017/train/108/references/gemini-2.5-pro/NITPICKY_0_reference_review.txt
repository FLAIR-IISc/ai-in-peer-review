### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping named entities. The core idea is to model the transitions between words using "entity separators," which explicitly encode whether an entity is starting, ending, or continuing at a given position. This formulation results in a multigraph where multiple directed edges can exist between the states of adjacent tokens. The authors claim this model resolves the "spurious structures" issue present in the state-of-the-art mention hypergraph model of Lu and Roth (2015), making it theoretically "nondeficient" while maintaining the same linear time complexity. The paper presents empirical results on ACE, GENIA, and CoNLL 2003 datasets, showing consistent F1-score improvements over strong baselines.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  A novel edge-centric formulation for overlapping NER that assigns explicit semantics to transitions between tokens. This provides an interesting alternative to traditional state-based or hypergraph-based approaches.
2.  A theoretical analysis that identifies a deficiency (the "spurious structures" problem) in a prior state-of-the-art model and demonstrates how the proposed formulation resolves it.
3.  A thorough empirical evaluation across four datasets that demonstrates consistent, though modest, performance improvements over the mention hypergraph baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the important and challenging task of overlapping NER. The proposed "entity separator" concept is intuitive and provides a novel way to decompose the problem. The distinction drawn between this approach and prior work (simple graphs, trees, hypergraphs) is clear.
2.  **Valuable Theoretical Insight:** The analysis in Section 5.2 regarding "spurious structures" in the mention hypergraph model is a significant insight. Identifying that the normalization term Z(x) incorrectly sums over multiple interpretations of the same subgraph is a subtle but important finding. The authors convincingly argue that their multigraph formulation is "nondeficient" and does not suffer from this issue.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model consistently outperforms the primary baseline (mention hypergraph) across all tested datasets, including standard overlapping NER benchmarks (ACE, GENIA) and a non-overlapping NER benchmark (CoNLL 2003). The gains, while not dramatic (ranging from +0.4 to +2.1 F1 points), are consistent and suggest the proposed formulation is effective.
4.  **Efficiency:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed model achieves these improvements while maintaining the same O(nT) time complexity as the mention hypergraph model, making it a practical and efficient alternative.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish that detracts from its overall quality and credibility.
    *   **Reference Formatting:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. It contains BibTeX artifacts (`\.,?`, `\Q2003\E`), inconsistent formatting, and a malformed, duplicated entry for "GENIA" at the very end. Such carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Inconsistent Terminology:** In Section 4, the authors state "a designated root node R is added and is connected to all T-nodes by a single hyperedge". This is confusing, as the paper's main claim is to propose a multigraph model as an alternative to hypergraphs. This phrasing blurs the very distinction the paper seeks to establish.
    *   **Unclear Figures:** Figure 2 (Top) is meant to illustrate the multigraph model but fails to show any graph structure (nodes, edges), instead using only abstract brackets. This makes it difficult to compare directly with the clear hypergraph depiction in Figure 2 (Bottom). Figure 4 is overly dense and difficult to interpret as a concrete "example".
    *   **Hidden Details:** An important implementation choice—"we resolved ambiguous structures by considering them as nested entities instead of crossing entities"—is relegated to footnote 3. The rationale and impact of this heuristic are not discussed in the main text.

2.  **Weak Link Between Theory and Empirical Gains:** The central theoretical claim is that the model is "nondeficient." The authors speculate that this makes the model "more confident" and leads to higher recall (Section 7). However, this connection is not rigorously established. The modest F1 gains could be attributed to other factors, such as the explicit separator features creating a more amenable optimization landscape for L-BFGS. The paper would be much stronger if it could more directly link the resolution of the spurious structures problem to the performance improvements.

3.  **Contradictory Results and Overstated Claims:**
    *   In Table 4, the proposed model achieves a lower F1 score (67.3) than the mention hypergraph baseline (67.7) on the subset of GENIA sentences that actually contain overlapping entities. The authors' post-hoc explanation ("insufficient amount of training data") is unconvincing, as the baseline model was trained on the exact same data. This result directly challenges the claim that the model is superior at handling overlapping entities.
    *   Based on the CoNLL 2003 results, the authors suggest that "modeling the interactions between distinct entity types may not be crucial." This is a strong and overly broad conclusion to draw from a single experiment where their model, which lacks this capability, happens to perform well.

4.  **Framing and Novelty:** While the "entity separator" idea is clever, framing it as a "novel multigraph-based structured prediction framework" feels somewhat overstated. The model can be viewed as a re-parameterization of a sequence labeling model where labels are assigned to the transitions between tokens. The authors acknowledge this possibility in Section 5.1 but dismiss it as "less intuitive and less concise" without sufficient justification. The core novelty lies in the specific state-space decomposition, not necessarily in the use of multigraphs as a general framework.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify the use of a "single hyperedge" to connect the root node R in your model (Section 4)? How does this fit within the proposed multigraph-based formalism, and why was this choice made over alternative graph structures?
2.  In Table 4, your model underperforms the MH baseline on GENIA sentences containing overlapping entities. Your explanation is a lack of data, but the baseline faced the same condition. Do you have an alternative hypothesis or a more detailed error analysis that could explain this specific result?
3.  The link between your model being "nondeficient" and the observed empirical gains is presented as speculation. Is there any further analysis you can provide to substantiate this claim? For instance, does the model assign lower probability mass to invalid or ambiguous structures compared to the MH model during inference?
4.  The reference list is severely malformed and requires a complete overhaul. Will you commit to thoroughly correcting this before the camera-ready version?
