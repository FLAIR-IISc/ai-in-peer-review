### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping named entities, a known challenge for traditional sequence labeling approaches. The core idea is to shift the representational complexity from the nodes (states) to the edges of the graph. Each edge between adjacent tokens is assigned an explicit semantic label, an "entity separator," which describes the combination of entities starting, continuing, or ending at that boundary. This formulation allows a single pair of nodes to be connected by multiple edges, hence the "multigraph" designation. The model is trained as a standard log-linear model and maintains the same linear-time inference complexity as the prior state-of-the-art hypergraph model.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:

1.  **A strong theoretical analysis identifying the "spurious structures" problem in a prior state-of-the-art model.** The paper provides a clear and compelling argument that the mention hypergraph model of Lu and Roth (2015) is "deficient," meaning its normalization term sums over entity combinations that the model can never actually predict. The authors show that their proposed model is "nondeficient," providing a powerful theoretical motivation for their new approach beyond simple empirical gains.
2.  **A novel and elegant multigraph formalization for overlapping structures.** The concept of using edges as "entity separators" with explicit semantics ({Start, End, Continue}) is an intuitive and powerful way to model complex, overlapping spans. This "edge-based" approach is a clean and principled contribution to structured prediction.
3.  **Thorough empirical validation demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.** The proposed model is shown to consistently outperform the strong mention hypergraph baseline across three standard datasets for overlapping NER (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA), as well as on the non-overlapping CoNLL 2003 dataset. The comprehensive experiments and analysis add significant weight to the paper's claims.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Theoretical Grounding:** The paper's strongest aspect is its theoretical analysis. The identification of the "spurious structures" issue in the Lu and Roth (2015) model is a significant insight. It provides a clear, principled reason why that model might be suboptimal and why the proposed multigraph model is fundamentally better designed. This moves the paper beyond a simple "here is a new model that gets a 2% F1 boost" to "here is a new model that fixes a demonstrable flaw in previous work, leading to better performance."
2.  **Novel and Intuitive Model Formulation:** The multigraph representation is elegant. Shifting the complexity to the edges (separators) while keeping the node states simple (`I_kt`/`O_kt`) is a very effective design choice. It makes the model's operations transparent and easy to understand, as exemplified by the clear diagram in Figure 2. This clarity makes the contribution more impactful and potentially applicable to other structured prediction tasks with overlapping phenomena.
3.  **Rigorous and Fair Experimental Comparison:** The empirical evaluation is well-executed. The authors compare their model against its most direct and powerful predecessor (Lu & Roth, 2015) by reimplementing it and using the *exact same feature set*. This ensures that the observed performance gains are due to the model architecture itself, not differences in feature engineering. The inclusion of multiple datasets, analysis of performance on overlapping vs. non-overlapping sentences, and even a check on training convergence speed (Figure 7) demonstrate a high degree of experimental rigor.
4.  **Clarity of Writing and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The concepts are introduced logically, and the distinction between "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms provides a useful framework for understanding the work's place in the literature. The figures are instrumental in conveying the core ideas.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Ambiguity in Decoding Is Under-discussed:** Footnote 3 states that "we resolved ambiguous structures by considering them as nested entities instead of crossing entities." This is a crucial detail that seems to contradict the model's purported representational completeness. It implies that the highest-scoring subgraph produced by the model can still correspond to multiple entity interpretations, requiring a post-hoc heuristic for disambiguation. This detail should be moved from a footnote to the main body and discussed more thoroughly. How frequent is this ambiguity? How does this heuristic affect the results for crossing vs. nested entities? This weakness slightly undermines the claim of having a perfectly non-deficient model, as the ambiguity is shifted from the training objective to the decoding step.
2.  **Limited Exploration of Crossing Entities:** While the model is presented as a general solution for overlapping entities, the examples, discussion, and the decoding heuristic all seem to favor nested entities. Crossing entities (e.g., "[European] computer [science conference]") are a distinct and challenging subtype of overlapping entities. A dedicated example showing how the "entity separators" represent a crossing structure and how it is decoded without ambiguity would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of generality.
3.  **The "Paradigm Shift" Framing Is a Minor Overstatement:** The distinction between "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms in Section 5.1 is an interesting conceptual lens, but the authors themselves note that one can be converted into the other. The argument that the edge-based formulation is more "intuitive" or "concise" is subjective. The real strength of the model comes from its non-deficiency (Section 5.2), not the paradigm it belongs to. This section feels slightly like post-hoc justification and could be toned down to focus on the more concrete technical contributions.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the decoding heuristic in Footnote 3: Could you provide statistics on how often ambiguous structures (which could be interpreted as either nested or crossing) are predicted on the ACE and GENIA test sets? Have you experimented with an alternative heuristic (e.g., preferring crossing entities or disallowing one interpretation), and how would it impact the final scores?
2.  The "spurious structures" analysis convincingly explains why the mention hypergraph's normalization term is flawed. Could you offer more intuition on how this flaw practically impacts the learned weights during optimization? For instance, does it lead the model to be overly conservative and assign lower weights to features associated with complex overlapping structures, potentially explaining your model's improved recall?
3.  Could you walk through how your model would represent and decode a canonical example of crossing entities, such as "[San Francisco]-based [company]" where the entities are `ORG: San Francisco-based company` and `LOC: San Francisco`? This would help clarify the model's handling of non-nested overlap.
