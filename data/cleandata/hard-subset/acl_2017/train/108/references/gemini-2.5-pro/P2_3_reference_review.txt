### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping named entities. The core idea is to move the semantic complexity from a large set of states (as in some prior work) to the edges of the graph. Specifically, the model uses a small, fixed set of states per token (indicating if a token is inside or outside an entity of a certain type) and defines eight distinct types of edges, called "entity separators" (e.g., Start, End, Continue, and their combinations), that can connect the states of adjacent tokens. This multigraph formulation allows it to naturally represent complex overlapping structures.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:

1.  **A Novel and Elegant Model Formulation:** The proposed multigraph model is a genuinely novel approach for this task. The "entity separator" concept is an intuitive and powerful way to decompose the problem, allowing for a simpler state representation while maintaining the ability to model complex interactions. It presents a compelling alternative to hypergraph-based methods.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Analysis and Justification:** The paper provides a clear theoretical advantage over the previous state-of-the-art (Lu and Roth, 2015). The analysis of "spurious structures" in the mention hypergraph model, and the demonstration that the proposed multigraph model is "nondeficient," offers a principled reason for why this new model should perform better. It suggests the model is optimizing a more correct objective function.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments on three standard overlapping NER datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) and one standard non-overlapping dataset (CoNLL 2003). The model consistently outperforms a strong, reimplemented baseline across these datasets, validating its effectiveness.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Clear Model:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for a new model by clearly outlining the limitations of previous approaches. The multigraph model itself is described clearly, and Figure 2 provides a very helpful comparison with the mention hypergraph baseline, effectively illustrating the new formulation's relative simplicity and interpretability. The idea of assigning explicit semantics to edges is the key strength.
2.  **Principled Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The theoretical analysis in Section 5, particularly the discussion on "spurious structures," is a major strength. It moves beyond a simple "our model is different and works better" argument to provide a concrete, technical reason for its superiority. The authors argue that the mention hypergraph's normalization term `Zw(x)` incorrectly sums over scores of distinct entity combinations that map to the same underlying subgraph, which their model avoids. This is a sophisticated and compelling argument.
3.  **Rigorous and Fair Experimental Design:** The empirical evaluation is exemplary. The authors reimplement the main baseline (Mention Hypergraph) and, crucially, use an identical feature set for both models to ensure a fair comparison. This isolates the performance gains to the model architecture itself. The inclusion of experiments on CoNLL 2003 demonstrates the model's robustness and generalizability even on non-overlapping data. Furthermore, the detailed breakdown of performance on sentences with and without overlapping entities (Table 4) adds valuable insight.
4.  **Efficiency and Practicality:** The paper demonstrates that its novel formulation does not come at a computational cost. It maintains the same linear-time inference complexity, O(nT), as the state-of-the-art baseline. The empirical finding that the model converges in fewer iterations (Figure 7) is another practical advantage. The commitment to release the code is also a welcome contribution to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Qualitative Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth qualitative error analysis. The discussion in Section 7 notes that the model has lower recall on overlapping entities in GENIA and speculates this is due to less training data. However, a few examples of cases where the multigraph model succeeds and the hypergraph fails (or vice versa) would provide deeper insight into the practical differences between the two formalisms. For instance, is the model better at handling nested entities versus crossing entities? Such an analysis would strengthen the connection between the theoretical "nondeficient" property and its practical impact.
2.  **The "State-based vs. Edge-based" Dichotomy Feels Slightly Overstated:** The classification of models into "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms in Section 5.1 is an interesting framing device. However, the distinction may not be as fundamental as presented. As the authors themselves acknowledge, one could transform their edge-based model into a state-based one by representing each "entity separator" as a state between words. While their argument that this would be less intuitive and potentially less efficient is valid, it suggests the paradigms are more of a modeling choice on a continuum rather than a strict dichotomy. This part of the analysis feels less impactful than the excellent "spurious structures" argument.
3.  **Novelty in the Context of Re-parameterization:** While the model is presented as a novel multigraph approach, one could also view it as a clever re-parameterization of the problem space previously modeled by hypergraphs. The "entity separators" (e.g., CS, EC) seem to correspond to specific combinations of hyperedges in the Lu and Roth (2015) model. While the resulting formulation is simpler and avoids the spurious structure problem, the conceptual leap might be seen as incremental by some. A more direct discussion of this relationship could help to better situate the work. This is a minor weakness, as the benefits of the new formulation are clearly demonstrated, but it's worth noting.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the formal relationship between your multigraph model and the mention hypergraph model? Specifically, can each "entity separator" edge in your model be mapped to a unique subgraph (i.e., a specific combination of hyperedges) in the mention hypergraph? Clarifying this could help to precisely pinpoint whether your model restricts the hypothesis space of the hypergraph model or if it represents the same space more efficiently and correctly.
2.  Regarding the lower recall on overlapping entities in the GENIA dataset, have you analyzed the specific types of errors? For example, does your model tend to predict a single, larger entity instead of two overlapping ones, or does it miss one of the overlapping entities entirely? This could help verify your hypothesis about insufficient training data and provide more insight into the model's failure modes.
