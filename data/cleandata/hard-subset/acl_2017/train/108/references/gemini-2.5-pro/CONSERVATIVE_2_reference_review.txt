**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a novel multigraph-based model for named entity recognition, specifically targeting the challenge of overlapping entities. The core idea is to represent entity boundaries and transitions using a set of eight "entity separators" which are modeled as distinct, directed edges between nodes representing token states. This contrasts with prior work, particularly the hypergraph model of Lu and Roth (2015), which the authors classify as an "edge-based" paradigm. The authors provide a theoretical analysis arguing that their model is "non-deficient" as it avoids the "spurious structures" problem present in the mention hypergraph model's normalization term. Empirically, the proposed model is evaluated on ACE2004, ACE2005, and GENIA datasets, demonstrating modest F1 score improvements over the hypergraph baseline.

**Contributions**
1.  An alternative log-linear model formulation for overlapping NER based on multigraphs. The primary theoretical contribution is the model's "non-deficient" property, which resolves an ambiguity in the partition function calculation of the prior state-of-the-art hypergraph model by Lu and Roth (2015).
2.  Empirical results showing consistent, though modest, improvements over the Lu and Roth (2015) model on several standard overlapping NER datasets, as well as on the non-overlapping CoNLL 2003 dataset.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper is well-written and the proposed model is described clearly. The conceptual framing of "state-based" versus "edge-based" paradigms is a helpful way to position the work, and the comparison to the mention hypergraph model is detailed and direct, which facilitates understanding.
2.  The theoretical analysis of the "spurious structures" issue is a definite strength. The authors clearly identify a potential deficiency in the formulation of a strong, established baseline model and propose a solution. While the practical impact is debatable (see Weaknesses), the methodological rigor in identifying and correcting this theoretical issue is commendable.
3.  The experiments are conducted carefully. The authors make a fair comparison to their primary baseline by re-implementing the mention hypergraph model and using an identical feature set for both systems. The evaluation across multiple datasets, including an analysis on subsets with and without overlapping entities, provides a reasonably comprehensive picture of the model's performance.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The proposed multigraph model appears to be a relatively small, incremental step from the mention hypergraph model of Lu and Roth (2015). The core idea of using "entity separators" (e.g., CS, EC) as edges seems to be a re-representation that essentially pre-enumerates and collapses certain combinations of hyperedges from the prior model. The distinction between a "multigraph" and a "hypergraph" in this specific application feels more like a change in terminology and graph construction rather than a fundamental shift in modeling paradigm. It is not clear that this formulation enables modeling any phenomena that were impossible to capture with the hypergraph approach.
2.  **Unclear Practical Impact of the "Spurious Structures" Correction:** The paper places heavy emphasis on its model being "non-deficient". However, the practical benefit of fixing this theoretical issue is not convincingly demonstrated. The baseline model, despite being "deficient," achieves very strong performance. The empirical gains reported in this paper are modest (e.g., 2.1 F1 on ACE2004, 0.4 F1 on GENIA). It is difficult to ascertain whether these small improvements are a direct result of the non-deficient formulation, or if they stem from other subtle differences in optimization or model specifics. The paper speculates that higher recall is a result of this correction, but this claim is not substantiated with any targeted analysis.
3.  **Limited Empirical Gains and Potential Robustness Issues:** The performance improvements, while consistent, are not substantial enough to be considered a major advance. An F1 improvement of 0.4 on GENIA is quite minor. More concerningly, the analysis in Table 4 shows that on the subset of GENIA sentences that actually contain overlapping entities, the proposed model performs slightly worse than the baseline (67.3 vs 67.7 F1). The authors' explanation that this is due to insufficient training data is speculative and could equally suggest that the proposed model is less robust or data-efficient than the established baseline it aims to improve upon.
4.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern Architectures:** The evaluation is exclusively focused on log-linear models with hand-crafted features. While comparing to Lu and Roth (2015) is essential, the field of NER has largely moved towards neural architectures based on pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT). Without at least a discussion of how this structured prediction approach compares to, or could be integrated with, more modern methods, the work feels somewhat disconnected from the current state of the art in the broader NER field.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide a more concrete analysis of the practical impact of the "spurious structures" issue? For example, can you perform an error analysis to show specific instances or types of errors that are made by the mention hypergraph model due to its deficient nature, which are correctly handled by your model? This would be more compelling than attributing the general trend of higher recall to this factor.
2.  The paper frames the multigraph model as a novel approach. Could you elaborate on why this should not be considered simply a re-factorization or different implementation of the mention hypergraph model? Given that the "entity separator" edges seem to correspond to combinations of hyperedges from the previous work, it seems plausible to view this as a clever re-representation rather than a fundamentally new class of model.
3.  Regarding the lower performance on the GENIA subset with overlapping entities (Table 4), you suggest a lack of training data is the cause. Could this instead be due to the way features are combined for your more complex edge types (e.g., 'ECS')? Have you investigated if the model struggles to learn weights for these rarer, composite edge types, and could a different feature strategy or regularization approach mitigate this?
