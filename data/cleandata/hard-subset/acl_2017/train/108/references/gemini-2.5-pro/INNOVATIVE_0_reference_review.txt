### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for the task of overlapping named entity recognition. The core idea is to move beyond traditional graph or hypergraph structures by assigning explicit, distinct semantics to the edges connecting states. These "entity separators" (e.g., Start, Continue, End, and their combinations) allow a single pair of nodes to be connected by multiple types of edges, thus forming a multigraph. The authors frame this as an "edge-based paradigm" for structured prediction, in contrast to the more common "state-based paradigm" where nodes carry the primary semantic load.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Modeling Paradigm:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a multigraph formalism for structured prediction. The idea of using semantically rich edges ("entity separators") to model complex, overlapping structures is a genuinely new and elegant conceptual advance over existing graph-based methods.
2.  **Theoretical Improvement over Prior Art:** The paper provides a compelling theoretical analysis demonstrating that its model is "nondeficient," meaning it does not suffer from the "spurious structures" problem present in the state-of-the-art mention hypergraph model. This provides a strong justification for the new formalism beyond just being a different representation.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The proposed model is shown to outperform previous state-of-the-art methods on several standard benchmarks for overlapping NER (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA), while maintaining the same efficient linear-time inference complexity.

### Strengths

This is an excellent paper that I believe makes a significant and lasting contribution to the field.

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness:** The paper's core strength lies in its conceptual novelty. The multigraph formulation is not merely an extension of existing models but a fundamentally new way to think about representing overlapping structures. The distinction drawn between "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms is insightful and clearly articulates the contribution. In a field that often sees incremental improvements, this work presents a bold, well-motivated, and elegant new direction. This kind of foundational modeling work is precisely what our community should encourage.

2.  **Theoretically Sound and Well-Motivated:** The authors don't just propose a new model; they provide a strong theoretical motivation for it. The analysis of the "spurious structures" issue in the mention hypergraph model is sharp and convincing. By demonstrating that their multigraph model is nondeficient and correctly accounts for all structures in its normalization term, they show that their proposed formalism is not just different, but fundamentally more correct. This theoretical soundness elevates the paper from a clever engineering trick to a principled modeling advance.

3.  **Potential for Broad Impact:** While demonstrated on overlapping NER, the proposed multigraph framework seems highly generalizable to other NLP tasks involving overlapping or complex structures, such as relation extraction, event extraction, or parsing. The authors' hope that this work will inspire further research in this direction is well-founded. This paper has the potential to open up a new line of inquiry into edge-based structured prediction models.

4.  **Solid and Convincing Experiments:** The novel idea is backed by thorough empirical validation. The model consistently outperforms a strong state-of-the-art baseline (mention hypergraph) across multiple standard datasets. The authors are careful to use the same feature sets for a fair comparison. The detailed breakdown of performance on sentences with and without overlapping entities, and the additional experiment on CoNLL 2003, further strengthen the empirical case and provide valuable insights into the model's behavior. The faster convergence noted in Figure 7 is another practical advantage.

### Weaknesses

My enthusiasm for this paper is high, and I consider the following points to be minor weaknesses that do not detract from the overall strength of the submission.

1.  **Relationship to Mention Hypergraph Could Be Formalized:** The paper states that "the edges in our model represent combinations of multiple edges in the mention hypergraph model." This is a crucial point that could be elaborated on. While the conceptual difference is clear, a more formal characterization of this relationship would be beneficial. For example, is the multigraph model a re-parameterization of the hypergraph, or is it strictly more expressive? Clarifying this would further sharpen the paper's theoretical contribution.

2.  **Modest F1 Gains:** An overly critical reviewer might point out that the F1 score improvements, while consistent, are not dramatic (e.g., ~2 points on ACE2004, ~0.4 on GENIA). However, I view this differently. On a well-established task like NER, achieving any consistent improvement over a strong, state-of-the-art baseline is significant. More importantly, the value of this paper lies in its introduction of a novel, theoretically superior framework. The fact that this new framework *also* leads to better performance is strong validation of its merit. The novelty and theoretical correctness are the main story here, not just the F1 score.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The analysis of "spurious structures" is a key argument for your model's superiority. You note that in the mention hypergraph, different structural interpretations (e.g., nested vs. crossing) can map to the same subgraph. You also state that the scores of these interpretations might differ. Could you elaborate on how these differing scores would arise if they share the same underlying subgraph and features are defined on edges? Does the deficiency in the MH model's normalization term have practical negative consequences during training, such as leading to poorly calibrated probabilities or inefficient learning?

2.  Following up on the weakness I noted, could you provide more intuition on whether your model can represent entity combinations that are simply impossible or ambiguous to represent in the mention hypergraph model? For example, can the explicit "entity separator" edges disentangle ambiguities that the hypergraph model cannot?
