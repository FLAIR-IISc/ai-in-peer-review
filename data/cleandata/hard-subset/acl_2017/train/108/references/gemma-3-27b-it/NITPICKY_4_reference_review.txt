This paper introduces a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER). The authors claim three primary contributions: (1) a novel multigraph-based model for overlapping entity prediction, (2) empirical evidence demonstrating superior F1-scores compared to previous models on multiple datasets, and (3) a theoretical demonstration that their model avoids the “spurious structures” issue present in a state-of-the-art baseline while maintaining comparable inference complexity. 

I believe the primary contribution lies in the exploration of a multigraph representation for structured prediction in the context of overlapping NER, even if the core idea isn't entirely groundbreaking. The empirical results are positive, though not overwhelmingly so. The theoretical analysis regarding spurious structures is interesting, but potentially overstated in its practical impact.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Representation:** The use of a multigraph to explicitly represent different semantic relationships between tokens in relation to entity boundaries is a thoughtful approach. While graphs and hypergraphs have been used in NER before, the multigraph formulation offers a potentially more flexible and expressive representation for overlapping structures.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on the ACE and GENIA datasets, demonstrating its practical effectiveness. The reported improvements over the mention hypergraph baseline, while modest, are statistically significant and suggest a genuine advancement.
3. **Detailed Analysis:** The paper provides a relatively thorough analysis of the proposed model, including a comparison with the mention hypergraph model, a discussion of spurious structures, and a clear explanation of the training and inference procedures. The inclusion of Figure 2 is helpful for visualizing the multigraph representation.
4. **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Significant Novelty:** While the multigraph representation is a reasonable extension of existing work, the core idea feels incremental rather than transformative. The jump from hypergraphs to multigraphs, in this context, isn't as substantial as the authors imply. The edge-based vs state-based paradigm discussion feels somewhat contrived and doesn't strongly justify the multigraph approach.
2. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The model’s performance is heavily dependent on carefully engineered features. The description of these features (Appendix B) is quite extensive, suggesting a significant amount of manual effort was required. A more robust model would ideally rely less on hand-crafted features and more on learned representations.
3. **Spurious Structures – Overstated Impact:** The argument about spurious structures seems overly critical of the mention hypergraph model. The practical consequences of these structures aren’t convincingly demonstrated. The analysis feels more theoretical than empirically grounded. How much *does* this spurious structure problem actually affect performance? A more compelling argument would involve ablation studies or error analysis showing a direct link between spurious structures and prediction errors.
4. **Inconsistent Formatting & Typos:** Throughout the paper, there are inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., capitalization of "Figure"), and several minor typos (e.g., "pervious" instead of "previous"). While these are minor issues, they detract from the overall polish and credibility of the work. Specifically, the spacing around equations is not uniform.
5. **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components of the model. For example, it’s unclear how much each type of entity separator contributes to the overall performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more concrete explanation of how the "spurious structures" in the mention hypergraph model manifest in practice? What specific types of errors are caused by these structures, and how does your model avoid them?
2.  What is the computational overhead of representing edges as multigraphs versus hypergraphs, particularly in terms of memory usage during training and inference?
3.  Can you discuss the limitations of your model? For instance, how well does it scale to larger datasets or more complex entity types? What types of overlapping structures does it struggle with?
4. The paper mentions both models (yours and the mention hypergraph baseline) share the same time complexity, O(nT). However, the multigraph representation inherently increases the number of edges. Could you detail any optimizations made to maintain this complexity, and what impact, if any, this has on constant factors and practical runtime?



**Additional Comments:**

* The phrase "to the best of our knowledge" is somewhat redundant and should be removed or justified.
* The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., sometimes "et al." is used, sometimes full author lists).
* Please review the paper carefully for typos and formatting errors before submitting the final version.
* The description of the hyperparameter tuning process could be more detailed. What specific criteria were used to select the best lambda value?
