This paper introduces a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER). The primary contribution is the novel application of multigraphs to represent overlapping entities, enabling explicit semantic assignment to edges and offering a potentially more expressive structure than previous hypergraph-based approaches. A secondary contribution is demonstrating improved F1-scores over existing methods on standard datasets, and a theoretical analysis showing the model avoids spurious structures present in a prior state-of-the-art approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Multigraph Approach:** The core idea of using multigraphs to represent overlapping entities is genuinely novel. Existing approaches rely on hypergraphs or tree structures, which can become cumbersome or restrictive when dealing with complex overlapping scenarios. The explicit semantics assigned to edges within the multigraph offer a conceptually clean way to encode relationships between entities. This is a significant departure from established techniques.
2. **Theoretical Justification:** The analysis demonstrating the absence of “spurious structures” is valuable. This provides a theoretical underpinning for the observed performance improvements, suggesting the model’s inference process is more robust and less prone to suboptimal solutions. Identifying and addressing deficiencies in prior work is a strong point.
3. **Empirical Results:** Achieving higher F1-scores on standard datasets is positive, though the gains aren't massive. Importantly, the consistent improvement across multiple datasets lends credibility to the approach. The inclusion of results on both ACE and GENIA is commendable.
4. **Clear Presentation & Code Availability:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The diagrams illustrating the multigraph structure are helpful. The commitment to releasing code is essential for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While improvements are reported, the magnitude of these improvements seems somewhat modest. A more comprehensive evaluation, perhaps including more challenging datasets or variations of the task (e.g., nested entities with greater depth), would strengthen the claims.
2. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set appears fairly standard, and the paper doesn't delve into the specifics of feature selection or ablation studies. Investigating the impact of different feature combinations or exploring more sophisticated feature representations could lead to further performance gains and a better understanding of the model’s capabilities.
3. **Complexity Overhead (Potential):** Although the paper states the inference time complexity remains linear, the practical overhead of handling multigraphs compared to hypergraphs isn’t thoroughly discussed. Multigraphs introduce the possibility of increased memory usage and computational cost due to the multiple edges between nodes.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying common failure cases would provide valuable insights into the model’s limitations and guide future research. What types of overlapping entities does the model struggle with, and why?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead associated with using multigraphs compared to hypergraphs, both in terms of memory usage and inference time, especially for larger sentences?
2.  Could you elaborate on the process of assigning semantics to the different edge types in the multigraph? Was this done heuristically, or through a more systematic approach?
3.  What types of errors does the model make most frequently? Can you provide examples of sentences where the model fails to correctly identify overlapping entities?
4. Have you explored different initialization strategies or regularization techniques to improve the convergence speed of the model?

**Additional Comments:**

The discussion of related work is solid. The comparison to the mention hypergraph model is well-articulated. The theoretical analysis in Section 5 is a strong element of this submission. While the empirical results are not groundbreaking, the novelty of the multigraph approach, coupled with the theoretical justification, makes this a worthwhile contribution.
