This paper introduces a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER). The core contribution lies in representing overlapping structures using a novel multigraph formalism, assigning distinct semantics to edges connecting the same node pair. The authors also provide theoretical analysis demonstrating the absence of “spurious structures” compared to a prior state-of-the-art model, and present competitive empirical results on standard datasets.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Multigraph Representation:** The introduction of multigraphs for representing overlapping entities is genuinely interesting. It moves beyond traditional graph/hypergraph approaches and offers a potentially more expressive way to encode structural relationships, particularly for scenarios with complex overlaps. This is the strongest aspect of the paper.
2. **Theoretical Analysis of Deficiency:** Identifying and demonstrating the "spurious structures" issue in the prior hypergraph model is a valuable contribution. This provides a solid theoretical justification for the proposed multigraph approach.
3. **Empirical Validation:** Showing competitive performance against existing methods, especially the strong baseline of Lu & Roth (2015), lends credibility to the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The multigraph representation is a significant departure from existing NER models. The idea of assigning semantics to edges is promising and addresses a key limitation of traditional structured prediction models in handling overlapping structures.  This is a bold step, prioritizing a new conceptual framework.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of overlapping NER and the shortcomings of previous approaches. The motivation for exploring alternative representations is well-justified.
3. **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis of the "spurious structures" issue is well-explained and provides a compelling argument for the benefits of the multigraph-based model. The comparison to the hypergraph model is thorough and insightful.
4. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental setup, including comparisons to strong baselines and ablation studies, is well-designed and provides strong evidence of the model's effectiveness.
5. **Code Availability:** Committing to releasing code and the system is a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation:** While the comparisons to baselines are strong, the ablation studies are somewhat limited. Exploring the impact of *specific* features or components of the multigraph model (e.g., the different edge types) would strengthen the analysis.
2. **Complexity of Multigraph Interpretation:** While the multigraph representation is novel, it might be difficult to intuitively understand and debug. The authors could benefit from providing more visual examples illustrating how the multigraph encodes complex overlapping scenarios.
3. **Minor Empirical Gains:**  The performance improvements over the baseline, while statistically significant, are not dramatic in all datasets. More substantial gains could further solidify the contribution. While novelty is prioritized, a clear performance advantage is still important.
4. **Feature Engineering:** The features used, while standard, feel somewhat conventional. The potential to design features specifically tailored to exploit the multigraph structure could be explored.


**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation (perhaps with additional visual examples) of how the different entity separator types (ECS, EC, etc.) capture distinct overlapping patterns?
2.  What is the computational overhead associated with maintaining the multiple edges in the multigraph compared to a standard hypergraph representation? Is this overhead significant in practice?
3.  Have you explored different methods for learning the edge weights in the multigraph model? Could a more sophisticated learning algorithm further improve performance?
4.  The paper mentions that the spurious structures issue is resolved. Could you elaborate on how this translates into practical benefits in terms of improved prediction accuracy, especially in challenging cases with complex overlaps?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The supplementary material would benefit from clearer descriptions of the feature sets used for each dataset. The discussion of related work is thorough. Despite the minor empirical gains, I find the novelty of the multigraph approach and the associated theoretical analysis compelling. The willingness to release code will also be helpful for the community. I lean towards acceptance, as the paper presents a genuinely new direction for research in overlapping NER.
