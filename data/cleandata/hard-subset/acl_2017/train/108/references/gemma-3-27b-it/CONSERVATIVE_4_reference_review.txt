This paper presents a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER), motivated by the limitations of sequence labeling and existing graph-based approaches like hypergraphs. The authors claim three primary contributions: a novel multigraph representation, empirical improvements in F1-score on multiple datasets, and a theoretical demonstration of the model’s non-deficiency (avoiding spurious structures) compared to a prior hypergraph model while maintaining comparable inference complexity.

The most significant contribution, as I see it, is the attempt to formalize overlapping entity representations through a multigraph structure and to analyze its theoretical properties. While the concept isn’t revolutionary—graph-based structured prediction for NER is well established—the explicit modeling of entity separators within a multigraph context, and the associated analysis regarding spurious structures, represent a potentially useful, though incremental, advance. The empirical results, demonstrating improvements over a strong baseline (Lu & Roth, 2015), are a positive sign, though not overwhelmingly convincing (see weaknesses). The inclusion of code is also a benefit.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of overlapping NER and positions the proposed approach as a response to limitations in existing methods. The discussion of spurious structures is a valuable analytical contribution.
2. **Theoretical Analysis:** The comparison to the mention hypergraph model and the identification of the spurious structure issue provide a compelling justification for the proposed approach. The demonstration of maintained computational complexity is also positive.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The results on ACE and GENIA datasets show improvements over the baseline, suggesting that the proposed model is at least a viable alternative. The ablation study, comparing with and without optimization, further demonstrates the benefit of the proposed model.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors' commitment to releasing code promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea of using a graph-based representation for structured prediction in NER is not novel. The primary contribution lies in the specific multigraph formulation and the analysis of spurious structures, which feels somewhat limited in scope. The improvement in performance (around 2-4 F1 points) is noticeable but not substantial enough to justify a major paradigm shift.
2. **Limited Feature Engineering Details:** While the authors mention the feature sets used, the descriptions are high-level. A more detailed explanation of the features, and a discussion of their impact on performance, would be beneficial. It's unclear if the gains are primarily due to the multigraph structure or clever feature engineering.
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The datasets used (ACE, GENIA) are relatively small and may not fully represent the diversity of real-world text. Evaluating the model on larger and more challenging datasets would strengthen the findings. The observed performance difference on GENIA suggests the model might be sensitive to data characteristics.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparison to Lu & Roth (2015) is appropriate, including results against more recent and sophisticated NER models (e.g., those leveraging pre-trained language models like BERT or RoBERTa) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model's performance. A linear chain CRF is also included, but it is not a strong competitor on this task.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the features used in your experiments, including examples and an ablation study to quantify their individual contributions?
2.  How does the performance of your model degrade on datasets with significantly different characteristics from ACE and GENIA (e.g., larger datasets, different entity types, different levels of overlap)?
3.  Have you explored incorporating pre-trained language model embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) into your model? If so, what was the impact on performance?
4.  Can you elaborate on the practical implications of avoiding spurious structures? Does this translate into more interpretable or robust predictions?
5.  The discussion of edge-based vs state-based paradigms is interesting, but somewhat abstract. Could you provide concrete examples of how the edge-based paradigm facilitates modeling complex relationships that are difficult to capture with a state-based approach?



**Additional Comments:** The manuscript is generally well-written and organized. The inclusion of figures illustrating the multigraph representation is helpful. The discussion of related work is adequate but could be expanded to include more recent advances in NER.
