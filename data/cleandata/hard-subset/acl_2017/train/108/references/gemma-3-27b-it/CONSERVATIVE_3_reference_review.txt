This paper proposes a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER), motivated by the limitations of existing approaches like linear-chain CRFs, tree-based parsers, and hypergraphs. The authors claim three primary contributions: a novel multigraph representation for overlapping structures, empirical improvements in F1-score over previous models, and a theoretical demonstration of the model’s non-deficiency (avoidance of spurious structures) compared to a state-of-the-art baseline.

A primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the formalization of edge semantics in structured prediction for overlapping entities. While the idea of using multiple edges isn’t entirely novel, explicitly assigning meaning to these edges (the “entity separators”) provides a potentially useful framework for representing complex relationships between spans. The authors correctly identify and articulate the limitations of previous methods concerning spurious structures. 

Strengths:

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper effectively highlights the challenges of overlapping NER and positions the proposed model as a direct response to the shortcomings of existing approaches. The issue of spurious structures is well-defined and serves as a strong motivation for the proposed method.
2. **Thorough Comparison to Baseline:**  The authors provide a reasonably comprehensive comparison to a recent and strong baseline (Lu & Roth, 2015).  The experimental setup is well-defined, and the inclusion of standard datasets (ACE, GENIA) facilitates comparison with prior work. The comparison against a linear-chain CRF provides a useful, albeit expected, performance benchmark.
3. **Theoretical Analysis:** The discussion of “deficiency” and spurious structures offers a valuable theoretical insight. The authors make a convincing argument that their model avoids this issue, which is a significant advantage. The explanation and justification, while a little dense, appear sound.
4. **Empirical Results:** The reported improvements in F1-score on ACE datasets, though not dramatic, are positive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The consistent outperformance of the baseline is encouraging. The analysis further breaking down results into overlapping vs. non-overlapping entities is insightful.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the explicit edge semantics are a nice addition, the core idea of using a graph-based representation for NER is well-established. The multigraph itself feels like an incremental extension rather than a fundamentally new approach. The authors need to better articulate *why* this specific multigraph structure is superior, beyond simply avoiding spurious structures.
2. **Feature Engineering:** The reliance on manually engineered features is a significant limitation. Modern NER systems increasingly leverage contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT) which automatically learn relevant features. The lack of experiments with such representations makes it difficult to assess the true potential of the multigraph model. It's unclear if the gains observed are due to the multigraph structure itself or simply to the chosen feature set.
3. **Scalability Concerns:**  Although the authors claim linear time complexity, the constant factors associated with multigraph operations could become a bottleneck for very large datasets or complex entity types.  A more detailed discussion of the computational cost, and perhaps some scalability experiments, would be beneficial.
4. **GENIA Results:** The performance gains on the GENIA dataset are relatively small. The authors acknowledge potential issues with training data size for overlapping entities, but further investigation and analysis are needed to understand why the model doesn’t perform as well on this dataset.

Questions to Authors:

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the inference procedure, including a discussion of the constant factors involved?
2.  Have you experimented with incorporating pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., BERT, GloVe) into your model? If not, could you discuss the potential benefits and challenges of doing so?
3.  Can you elaborate on the specific types of spurious structures that are avoided by your model and provide concrete examples of how they affect the predictions of the baseline model?
4.  Why do you believe the model performs better on ACE datasets than GENIA? Is it possible that the types of overlapping entities in GENIA are more complex or require different feature representations?



Additional Comments:
The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. The inclusion of Figure 2 and 4 illustrating the multigraph structure is helpful. The supplementary material is appreciated and should be reviewed.  I suggest the authors address the concerns raised regarding novelty, feature engineering, and scalability to strengthen their submission.
