This paper proposes a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER). The core idea is to represent overlapping structures using a multigraph where different edges connecting the same nodes have explicit semantics, termed “entity separators”. The authors claim (1) a novel multigraph-based model, (2) improved F1-scores on multiple datasets, and (3) a theoretical advantage over a prior state-of-the-art model (Lu & Roth, 2015) – specifically, avoiding “spurious structures” and maintaining comparable inference time complexity.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Multigraph Representation:** The paper introduces a potentially valuable way to represent overlapping entities using multigraphs. Assigning explicit semantics to edges (entity separators) offers a more flexible and expressive approach compared to traditional graph-based models. This is the strongest contribution.
2. **Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, F1-scores on the ACE and GENIA datasets compared to the mention hypergraph baseline (Lu & Roth, 2015) and other established methods. 
3. **Theoretical Analysis:** The analysis of "spurious structures" in the mention hypergraph model and the demonstration that the proposed model avoids this issue is a solid theoretical contribution. This highlights a potential limitation of the previous state-of-the-art and provides a clear justification for the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of overlapping NER and motivates the need for more sophisticated modeling approaches. The introduction effectively establishes the context and highlights the limitations of existing methods.
2. **Well-Defined Model:** The multigraph-based model is described in a detailed and understandable manner. The explanation of entity separators and their mapping to edges is particularly clear, accompanied by illustrative figures (Figs 2 & 4).
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors evaluate their model on standard datasets (ACE, GENIA) and compare it against relevant baselines. They also provide ablation studies (reporting results with and without optimization) and additional analysis regarding different types of sentences (with and without overlap).
4. **Theoretical Insight:** The analysis of spurious structures is a valuable contribution that demonstrates a deeper understanding of the underlying principles of structured prediction models. The argument that their model is “non-deficient” is well-supported.
5. **Code Release:** The promise of code availability is a strong point, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Idea:** While the application to overlapping NER is novel, the concept of multigraphs is not entirely new. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work on multigraphs and their application in other domains, to better position the contribution.  It's not clear how much of the performance gain is due to the multigraph representation *itself* versus the specific features and training procedure.
2. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set is relatively standard and not significantly different from those used in the baseline model.  It's unclear if the performance gains are directly attributable to the multigraph representation or primarily driven by careful feature engineering. Exploring more advanced feature representations tailored to the multigraph structure could further enhance the model’s performance.
3. **Ablation Studies are Limited:** While the paper presents results with and without F1-score optimization, a more detailed ablation study evaluating the impact of individual components of the model (e.g., specific types of entity separators) would be beneficial. This would provide further insight into the model's behavior and the importance of each component.
4. **Overstatement of Theoretical Advantage:** The claim that the model is "non-deficient" seems somewhat strong. While the analysis of spurious structures is valid, it doesn't necessarily mean the model is fundamentally superior in all cases. A more nuanced discussion of the potential trade-offs would be appropriate.
5. **GENIA Performance:** While improvements are shown, the gains on GENIA are less substantial than on the ACE datasets.  The authors acknowledge a potential issue with insufficient training data for overlapping entities in GENIA, but a more in-depth investigation of this issue and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., data augmentation) would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the novelty of using multigraphs in this specific context, referencing relevant work on multigraphs in other domains?
2.  What specific strategies were used for feature engineering, and how were the features selected? Could you provide more details about the feature importance?
3.  Could you perform a more detailed ablation study, evaluating the impact of different entity separator types on the overall performance?
4.  Can you discuss the limitations of the theoretical advantage of avoiding spurious structures? Are there scenarios where this advantage might be less significant?
5.  What steps could be taken to address the lower performance gains observed on the GENIA dataset? Have you considered data augmentation or other techniques to improve performance on this dataset?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The inclusion of example multigraphs (Figs 2 & 4) is extremely helpful.
*   The detailed explanation of the mention hypergraph model (Section 3) provides a good foundation for understanding the proposed approach.
*   The code release is highly anticipated.

**Overall:**

This is a solid paper with a clear contribution to the field of overlapping NER. While the core idea of using multigraphs is not entirely novel, the application to this specific problem is well-motivated and the experimental results are promising. The theoretical analysis adds valuable insight.  The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed through further experimentation and clarification. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the "Weaknesses" and "Questions to Authors" sections.
