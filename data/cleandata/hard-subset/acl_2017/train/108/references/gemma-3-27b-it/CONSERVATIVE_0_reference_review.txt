This paper proposes a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER). The authors claim three primary contributions: (1) a novel multigraph representation for overlapping structures, (2) empirical improvements in F1-score over existing models on multiple datasets, and (3) a theoretical demonstration that their model avoids a "spurious structures" issue present in a prior state-of-the-art model, while maintaining comparable inference complexity. 

The most significant contribution of this work, if validated, is the identification and formalization of the "spurious structures" problem in the Lu and Roth (2015) hypergraph model. The authors convincingly argue that the normalization term in the hypergraph model includes invalid combinations of entities, potentially leading to suboptimal inference. This is a valuable theoretical insight. A second notable contribution is the relatively comprehensive empirical evaluation. The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, and GENIA) and compare against several baselines, including a linear-chain CRF and the hypergraph model of Lu and Roth (2015). The consistent performance gains across these datasets are encouraging. Finally, the proposal of the multigraph representation, while perhaps not entirely groundbreaking, offers a viable alternative to hypergraphs for modeling overlapping entities and deserves consideration.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the novelty of the multigraph representation itself is questionable.  The idea of assigning distinct semantics to edges, while presented as novel, feels like a natural extension of existing graph-based structured prediction methods. The authors don't sufficiently motivate *why* a multigraph is inherently better than simply adding more features to a hypergraph model to disambiguate different overlapping scenarios.  Simply stating it addresses "spurious structures" doesn’t justify the architectural change without a more thorough comparative analysis of model capacity. Second, the feature engineering details are insufficient. The authors mention using features similar to those in previous work, but provide limited specifics, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison. Were the feature sets *identical* between the multigraph model and the hypergraph baseline? Any differences could significantly impact the reported results. Third, the discussion of the spurious structures issue, while theoretically sound, lacks a compelling empirical demonstration. The authors state that their model avoids these structures, but don’t provide any analysis of *how* this translates to improved performance in specific cases or examples.  A visualization of the differing subgraphs generated by the two models for a representative sentence would be highly beneficial. Fourth, the gains reported are often modest. While statistically significant improvements are valuable, the 1-2 point F1 increases in some cases don't represent a dramatic advancement over existing methods. The practical impact of these gains needs to be contextualized.

Here are some questions for the authors:

1. Could you provide a more detailed description of the feature set used in both the multigraph model and the hypergraph baseline, including a list of specific features? Specifically, were all feature templates identical across models?
2. Can you provide concrete examples illustrating the "spurious structures" issue in the hypergraph model and how the multigraph representation avoids them? A visualization would be helpful.
3. You mention that the inference complexity is the same for both models (O(nT)). However, do the constant factors differ significantly? Does the multigraph representation lead to higher computational costs during training or inference?
4. Have you explored adding features to the hypergraph model that might mitigate the spurious structures issue, rather than switching to a multigraph representation?  What was the performance of such an augmented hypergraph model?
5. The analysis in section 5.1 categorizes approaches as state-based vs. edge-based. This seems like a useful distinction, but is it a strict categorization, or a spectrum? Could the hypergraph model also be interpreted as having an "edge-based" component?



Additional Comments:
The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, the writing could be more concise in places. The discussion of related work is thorough. The inclusion of the code and system is appreciated.
