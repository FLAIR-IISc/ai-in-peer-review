This paper introduces a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER). The authors argue that existing approaches struggle with overlapping entities and propose a novel representation using multigraphs, allowing explicit semantics to be assigned to edges connecting the same node pair. They claim contributions in proposing the model, demonstrating improved F1-scores on multiple datasets, and theoretically showing its non-deficiency compared to a previous state-of-the-art model while maintaining comparable inference complexity.

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of a multigraph-based representation for overlapping NER. While the idea of using multigraphs isn't entirely novel in machine learning, its application to the specific problem of overlapping entity recognition and the formalization of "entity separators" as edges within the multigraph are valuable. The analysis comparing their approach to the mention hypergraph model (Lu and Roth, 2015) and identifying the "spurious structures" issue is insightful and contributes to a better understanding of the limitations of existing methods. A secondary contribution is the empirical evaluation on standard datasets, providing a benchmark for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Multigraph Representation:** The use of multigraphs to explicitly represent overlapping entities through "entity separators" is a novel approach. This allows the model to capture relationships that are difficult to represent with simpler graph structures like those used in CRFs or hypergraphs. The concept of assigning semantics to edges representing different overlap scenarios is particularly strong.
2. **Theoretical Analysis of Spurious Structures:** The authors’ identification and analysis of spurious structures in the mention hypergraph model and demonstration that their model avoids this issue is a significant strength. This provides a theoretical justification for the potential performance improvements and offers a valuable insight into the behavior of different structured prediction models.
3. **Empirical Results:** The empirical evaluation on ACE2004, ACE2005 and GENIA datasets demonstrates that the proposed model achieves competitive and in some cases improved F1-scores compared to the mention hypergraph baseline. The experiments on separating overlapping vs. non-overlapping entity sentences provide further insights into the model’s behavior.
4. **Clear Explanation & Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation for the work, the proposed model, and the experimental setup. The visualizations (Figures 2, 3, and 4) are helpful in understanding the multigraph representation and the entity separators.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the multigraph *representation* is novel in this context, the underlying inference algorithm (generalized inside-outside) and the overall log-linear framework are standard techniques in structured prediction. The feature set also largely follows existing work. This reduces the overall impact of the paper.
2. **Feature Engineering Details:** The description of the features could be more comprehensive. While they state they follow previous works, a more detailed table or explanation of the exact feature set used would be helpful for reproducibility and understanding the model’s capabilities.
3. **Minor Performance Gains:** While statistically significant improvements are reported, the performance gains over the strong baseline (mention hypergraph) are relatively modest in some cases. It would be valuable to explore ablation studies to determine which components of the multigraph representation contribute most to the performance improvement.
4. **CoNLL 2003 Results Discussion:** The strong performance on the non-overlapping CoNLL 2003 dataset is noted, but the discussion lacks depth. It’s not entirely clear *why* the model performs well on this dataset, and how this relates to the design choices made for handling overlapping entities.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed feature set listing (e.g., a table) for each dataset, including the specific features used for both models?
2. You mention that the “spurious structures” issue in the mention hypergraph model can be resolved by considering alternative interpretations as nested entities. How does this resolution strategy affect the performance of your multigraph model, and have you explicitly evaluated this aspect in your experiments?
3. Could you provide an ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of individual components of the multigraph representation (e.g., the different entity separator types) to the overall performance?
4. Why does the model perform so well on the CoNLL 2003 dataset, which does not contain overlapping entities? Does this suggest that the multigraph structure is beneficial even in the absence of overlapping structures, and if so, what is the underlying reason?

**Additional Comments:**

* The phrase "to the best of our knowledge" in the introduction is common but unnecessary.
* The references are generally well-cited, but some are abbreviated (e.g., "Alex et al\.,? 2007"). Standardize the citation format.
* The supplemental material mentioned in the text would be beneficial to review.
