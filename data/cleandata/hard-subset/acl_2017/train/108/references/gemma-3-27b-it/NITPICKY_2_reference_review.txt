This paper introduces a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER). The authors claim three primary contributions: a novel multigraph representation for overlapping structures, empirical improvements in F1-score on multiple datasets, and a theoretical demonstration that their model avoids the “spurious structures” issue present in a prior state-of-the-art model while maintaining comparable inference complexity. 

I find the primary contribution to be the formalization and exploration of an edge-centric representation for overlapping entities, contrasting it with state-based paradigms. While the multigraph concept itself isn’t entirely novel in graph theory, its application to this specific structured prediction problem, and the comparison to the hypergraph approach, provides a distinct perspective. The empirical results offer some support for the approach, and the theoretical analysis is a welcome attempt to understand the properties of the proposed model.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Representation:** The explicit semantic assignment to edges within the multigraph offers a conceptually clear way to represent overlapping entities, moving beyond simpler graph structures. The detailed explanation and illustration in Figures 2 & 4 are particularly helpful.
2. **Comparative Analysis:** The paper diligently compares the proposed model to the recent mention hypergraph approach (Lu & Roth, 2015). The analysis regarding “spurious structures” is insightful, and the claim of maintaining linear time complexity is a significant advantage.  The distinction between state-based and edge-based paradigms is a useful categorization.
3. **Thorough Experimentation:** The evaluation across multiple datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) demonstrates the generalizability of the approach. The ablation studies showing performance on sentences with/without overlapping entities strengthen the analysis.
4. **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The inclusion of detailed feature descriptions and training procedures enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement:** While the empirical results show improvements over the baseline, the gains are relatively modest (e.g., ~2.1 F1 point on ACE2004). The paper doesn’t sufficiently justify *why* the multigraph representation consistently outperforms the hypergraph approach, beyond the theoretical argument about spurious structures. A deeper investigation into feature contributions or error analysis is needed.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Table 6 reveals significant variation in the optimal regularization parameter (λ) across datasets. This suggests the model’s performance is sensitive to hyperparameter tuning and may not generalize well to unseen data without careful optimization. The paper lacks a discussion of hyperparameter search strategies or robustness.
3. **GENIA Results Discrepancy:** The performance on the GENIA dataset is less convincing. The authors acknowledge potentially insufficient training data for overlapping entities in GENIA. This should be addressed with either data augmentation or a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the model in this domain. The improvement on GENIA is also significantly lower than on ACE datasets.
4. **Minor Implementation Details:** The description of preprocessing for GENIA (Appendix D) involving token splitting introduces a potentially confounding factor. While mentioned, the impact of this step on the results isn’t thoroughly discussed.
5. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, missing spaces, redundant phrases). While not critical, these detract from the overall polish of the submission. For example, in section 6.3, the word “lin-CRF” should be "Lin-CRF", in section 6.2 the term “w/ o.l.” appears with inconsistent spacing, and there are inconsistent indentation patterns throughout.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more in-depth error analysis to understand *where* the multigraph model outperforms the hypergraph model?  Specifically, what types of overlapping entity patterns are better handled by your approach?
2. What strategies were used for hyperparameter tuning?  Would a more systematic hyperparameter search (e.g., using grid search or Bayesian optimization) lead to more consistent performance across datasets?
3. How sensitive are the results to the token splitting preprocessing step applied to the GENIA dataset? Could this step be avoided or modified to improve performance?
4. The paper mentions the possibility of applying the proposed framework to other problems. Can you elaborate on specific problem domains and the potential benefits of using a multigraph representation in those contexts?



**Additional Comments:**

* Please carefully proofread the paper to address formatting inconsistencies and typos.
* In Appendix A, the figure is referenced but not actually included in the submitted materials. Please ensure all figures are present.
* The sentence “However, such results give us some idea on how much performance increase we can gain by properly recognizing overlapping entities.” (Section 7) is somewhat vague and could be phrased more precisely.
