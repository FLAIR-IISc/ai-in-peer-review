This paper introduces a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER), arguing it overcomes limitations of previous approaches like hypergraphs by explicitly representing semantic relationships between edges. The core contribution is the novel multigraph representation and its application to a structured prediction task. A secondary contribution is the theoretical analysis demonstrating the model’s lack of “spurious structures,” a deficiency identified in the compared hypergraph model, alongside maintained computational efficiency. Finally, the paper provides empirical validation on standard datasets, demonstrating improved F1-scores.

The most significant strength of this work lies in the **novelty of the multigraph representation for structured prediction**. While hypergraphs have been used for overlapping NER, the authors convincingly argue for the benefits of assigning explicit semantics to edges, a concept that hasn't been explored in this context before. This represents a potentially impactful shift in how structured prediction problems with overlapping structures are modeled. The presented approach feels genuinely different and offers a fresh perspective.

A second strength is the **clear theoretical analysis and comparison to the state-of-the-art**. Identifying and explaining the "spurious structures" issue in the hypergraph model is valuable. Demonstrating that the proposed multigraph model avoids this problem, while retaining the same time complexity, is a strong argument for its adoption. This isn't just about improved empirical results; it's about a fundamentally better model design.

The **empirical results are promising**, showing consistent improvements over the hypergraph baseline on multiple datasets. While the gains aren’t enormous, they are statistically significant and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of results on CoNLL 2003, demonstrating performance even on non-overlapping NER, is a positive sign of the model's generalizability.

Finally, the authors clearly articulate the **potential broader applicability** of the multigraph-based framework beyond NER. Positioning this as a general approach for modeling overlapping structures is a smart move and could inspire further research.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses. The **feature engineering section is relatively light on detail**. While the authors mention following previous works, a more thorough discussion of the specific features used, and their rationale, would strengthen the paper. It's not immediately clear why these specific features were chosen, or if any feature selection/ablation studies were performed.

A second weakness is the **lack of detailed ablation studies focused on the multigraph aspects**. How much of the performance gain is attributable *specifically* to the semantic edge representation, versus other aspects of the model? A targeted experiment, perhaps by simplifying the edge representations, would help isolate the contribution of the core innovation.

The **paper’s presentation could be improved**. Some sections feel somewhat dense and could benefit from clearer explanations and more intuitive examples. The diagrams, while helpful, are a little cluttered. Specifically, the explanation of the edge types (S, E, C, etc.) could be more visually accessible.

Finally, the **impact of the spurious structures issue is not fully demonstrated in the empirical results**. While theoretically sound, the paper doesn’t definitively *show* how this issue hinders the hypergraph model's performance on specific examples. Providing a case study illustrating this difference would be impactful.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide a more detailed description of the features used in your experiments, including specific examples and a discussion of feature selection/ablation?
2. Could you conduct an ablation study specifically focused on the contribution of the semantic edge representation in the multigraph model?
3. Can you provide a specific example where the “spurious structures” issue in the hypergraph model demonstrably leads to incorrect predictions, and show how your model avoids this error?
4. Could you provide a discussion on how this multigraph-based formalism can be used to solve other structured prediction problems involving complex structures?



Additional Comments:
The formatting is generally good. The references are complete and well-formatted. The authors' commitment to releasing code is commendable.
