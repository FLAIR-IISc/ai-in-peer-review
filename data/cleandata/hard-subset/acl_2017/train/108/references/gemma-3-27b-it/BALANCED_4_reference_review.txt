This paper introduces a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER). The authors argue that existing methods struggle with overlapping entities and propose a novel representation using multigraphs to explicitly encode semantic relationships between entities. They claim three main contributions: a novel multigraph model, empirical improvements over previous models on multiple datasets, and a theoretical analysis demonstrating the model's non-deficiency compared to a state-of-the-art baseline.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Multigraph Representation:** The core idea of representing overlapping structures using a multigraph, where edges explicitly encode semantic relationships, is a strong point. This allows for a more nuanced representation than traditional graph-based models, which may struggle to capture the intricacies of overlapping spans. The visualization in Figure 2 helps clarify this concept.
2. **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates improvements over existing models (including the mention hypergraph baseline) on ACE and GENIA datasets. While the improvements aren't massive, they are consistent and suggest the proposed model is a viable approach for overlapping NER. Reporting results on multiple datasets is good practice.
3. **Theoretical Analysis of Spurious Structures:** The analysis identifying and addressing the "spurious structures" issue in the mention hypergraph model is valuable.  Demonstrating that the proposed model avoids this problem provides a theoretical justification for its improved performance and highlights a key advantage over the baseline. The explanation and accompanying figures in Section 5.2 and Appendix A are helpful.
4. **Comprehensive Comparison:** The authors conduct a thorough comparison against relevant baselines, including a linear-chain CRF and the mention hypergraph model.  This helps contextualize the contribution of the proposed model and demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Improvement Magnitude:** While statistically significant, the performance improvements reported are relatively modest. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of *why* the improvements are limited. Is it due to the datasets, the features used, or inherent limitations of the multigraph representation?
2. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set appears fairly standard and could benefit from more exploration of features specifically designed to leverage the multigraph structure. The current feature description is also a bit sparse.  More detail on the specific features used would be helpful.
3. **Complexity of the Model:** The introduction of multigraphs adds complexity to the model. The paper needs to more clearly articulate the trade-offs between this increased complexity and the resulting performance gains. A discussion of the computational cost compared to other methods, beyond simply stating the time complexity is the same as the baseline, would be beneficial.
4. **GENIA Results & Analysis:** The results on the GENIA dataset are less convincing, with some cases showing a slight *decrease* in performance. The authors attribute this to insufficient training data on overlapping entities. A more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the model on GENIA would be insightful. The conclusion that the model can still do well on non-overlapping entities, while interesting, feels somewhat tacked on and could be strengthened with more focused analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the feature set used, including examples of specific features that leverage the multigraph structure?
2.  What is the computational overhead of performing inference with a multigraph compared to a hypergraph, in practice (e.g., memory usage, runtime on a specific hardware configuration)?  The theoretical time complexity being the same is not the full picture.
3.  Why does the model perform relatively worse on the GENIA dataset? What types of errors is it making on this dataset that it doesn't make on ACE?
4. The claim that the model is "nondeficient" is strong. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of why spurious structures are a problem and how the multigraph representation specifically avoids them, beyond the technical description in Section 5.2?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*  Providing the code and system will be valuable for the research community.
*  Consider adding a discussion of potential applications of the multigraph-based approach beyond NER.
*  The formatting of the figure captions could be improved for readability.
