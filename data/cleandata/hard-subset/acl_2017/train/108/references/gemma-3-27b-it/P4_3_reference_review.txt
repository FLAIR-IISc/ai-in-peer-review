This paper introduces a multigraph-based model for overlapping named entity recognition (NER). The core idea is to represent entity structures using a multigraph where edges explicitly encode semantic relationships between tokens, allowing for representation of overlapping spans. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a novel multigraph-based model for overlapping NER, (2) empirical improvements in F1-score on multiple datasets, and (3) a theoretical demonstration that the model avoids spurious structures present in a previous state-of-the-art model (Lu & Roth, 2015) while maintaining comparable inference complexity.

I assess the primary contribution to be the exploration of multigraphs for structured prediction in the context of overlapping NER, and the insightful analysis comparing it with the hypergraph-based approach. While the idea of using multigraphs isn't entirely groundbreaking (multigraphs exist as a mathematical construct), applying them specifically to address the challenges of overlapping spans in NER and demonstrating its properties relative to existing methods is novel. The empirical results provide support for the effectiveness of the proposed model, and the theoretical analysis concerning spurious structures adds significant value. The data and experimental setup are well-defined, leveraging standard datasets and comparable baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Multigraph Application:** The application of multigraphs to represent overlapping entities is a key strength. The authors convincingly argue that the explicit semantics assigned to edges in the multigraph can effectively capture the relationships inherent in overlapping structures, a limitation of simpler graph-based or hypergraph-based models. This represents a conceptually different approach to modeling the problem.
2. **Theoretical Analysis of Spurious Structures:** The analysis comparing the multigraph model to the mention hypergraph model (Lu & Roth, 2015) regarding spurious structures is a significant contribution. Identifying and explaining this deficiency in the prior work, and demonstrating how the proposed model avoids it, provides a strong theoretical justification for the approach. The detailed explanation and example (Appendix A) clarifies this important difference.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents comprehensive experimental results on standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA). The reported F1-score improvements over the mention hypergraph baseline are statistically significant and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed model. The ablation study shown in Table 4 offers additional insight into performance gains on sentences *with* and *without* overlapping entities.
4. **Well-defined Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is well-defined, using standard datasets, a strong baseline (Lu & Roth, 2015), and a reasonable feature set. The careful consideration of hyperparameters and the inclusion of detailed results further strengthen the empirical evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions tuning the l2-regularization parameter and Brown cluster size, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the model to these hyperparameters would be beneficial. A sensitivity analysis would provide a better understanding of the model's robustness.
2. **Feature Set Description Could Be More Detailed:** The description of the feature set, while generally adequate, could be more detailed. Providing a more complete list of the features used (as hinted at in Appendix B) would improve reproducibility. It's unclear how the features are combined or weighted, and a more thorough explanation would be helpful.
3. **Complexity of the Model:** While the inference complexity is claimed to be linear, the multigraph representation adds inherent complexity compared to simpler models. A more detailed discussion on the practical implications of this complexity in terms of training and inference time, beyond simply stating the theoretical time complexity, would be valuable. The empirical observation in Section 6.3 that training takes slightly longer with the proposed model supports the need for further discussion on this.
4. **Limited Ablation Studies:** While Table 4 provides some insight into the model’s performance on sentences with overlapping entities, further ablation studies investigating the impact of individual components of the model (e.g., different types of entity separators) would be useful for understanding the contributions of each part.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (training and inference time) associated with the multigraph-based model compared to the mention hypergraph model, perhaps using a table with runtime measurements on different datasets?
2. The paper identifies spurious structures in the mention hypergraph model. Could you elaborate on how these spurious structures might manifest in practical applications, and what impact they could have on downstream tasks?
3. You mention the potential for applying the multigraph framework to other problems with overlapping structures. Can you provide more specific examples of such problems and how the proposed model might be adapted to address them?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-organized, although some sections could benefit from more concise explanations.
* The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
* The supplementary material (mentioned in the paper) is crucial for understanding the details of the analysis and experimental setup and should be made available alongside the submission.
