- Strength: The problem of detecting overlapping (especially crossing) entities is interesting and relevant.
- Strength: The proposed multigraph technique appears to work empirically, though gains are not large.
- Strength: The paper discusses efficiency improvements compared to a previous system.
- Strength: The paper is generally well-written but needs polishing in several details.

- Weakness: The motivation for detecting nested entities (e.g., “China” inside “Bank of China”) is weak and under-explained.
- Weakness: Key algorithmic details are missing—e.g., the decision criterion for including edges and which I^k_t node variants are used.
- Weakness: Empirical improvements are small (often a few F1 points); claiming “outperformed” is overstated without effect-size discussion.
- Weakness: The paper lacks analysis of practical impact—how many important new entities are found and whether small F1 gains matter to users.
- Weakness: Missing baseline comparisons to simple dictionary-based methods and commercial systems (e.g., Google NLP) to contextualize results.
- Weakness: Insufficient focus on crossing entities in experiments and error analysis—unclear if gains come from nested detection only or also improved crossing detection.
- Weakness: The paper would benefit from detailed error comparisons with prior systems and concrete examples of successes/failures.

- General: The reviewer remains lukewarm and leans toward rejection because the paper does not convincingly emphasize the important problem of crossing entities.

- Minor: Define “multigraph” on first use for readers unfamiliar with the term.
- Minor: Numerous wording, grammar, and stylistic issues (typos, awkward phrases, punctuation).
- Minor: Several unclear terms and notations (e.g., “spurious structures,” “state” vs. “node,” CS-edge selection) need clarification.
- Minor: Figures (especially Fig.2) are unclear in black-and-white, use of colors/gray nodes and highlighted edges is not explained, and the example’s goals are not justified.
- Minor: Many presentation issues in math and text (commas/periods, equation punctuation, inconsistent reference formatting, footnote placement).
- Minor: Inconsistent reference usage and capitalization in bibliography; avoid using references as nouns.
- Minor: Clarify experimental details and reporting (what “significant”/“better” means, exact effect sizes, specific recall/precision issues on datasets).
