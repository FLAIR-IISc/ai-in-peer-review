### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for named entity recognition (NER), with a specific focus on handling overlapping entities. The core idea is to model the transitions between words using a set of 8 explicit "entity separators" (e.g., Start, End, Continue, and their combinations), which are represented as distinct directed edges in a multigraph. This "edge-based" approach contrasts with traditional sequence labeling models where states (nodes) carry the primary semantic information. The model is formulated as a log-linear model, trained with a standard objective function, and uses an efficient inside-outside algorithm for inference with linear time complexity in sentence length. The authors demonstrate experimentally that their model achieves state-of-the-art results on several benchmark datasets for overlapping NER (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) and also performs strongly on a standard non-overlapping NER task (CoNLL 2003).

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:

1.  **A compelling theoretical analysis identifying the "spurious structures" problem in a previous state-of-the-art model.** The paper provides a clear and insightful argument that the mention hypergraph model of Lu and Roth (2015) is "deficient." Specifically, its normalization term Z(x) incorrectly sums over scores of distinct entity combinations that are conflated into a single subgraph representation. The proposed multigraph model is "nondeficient" by design, ensuring a one-to-one mapping between entity structures and paths in the graph, which provides a principled motivation for the new architecture beyond just empirical gains.
2.  **The proposal of a novel and elegant multigraph formulation for structured prediction.** The use of explicit, semantically rich edges (the "entity separators") to connect a small set of simple node states is an innovative way to model complex, overlapping structures. This approach is intuitive and provides a clean separation between the status of a token (in/out of an entity) and the structural role it plays (starting, ending, or continuing an entity).
3.  **Strong and thorough empirical results that validate the proposed model.** The paper shows consistent and significant improvements over the strong mention hypergraph baseline across four different datasets, demonstrating the model's effectiveness and generalizability for both overlapping and non-overlapping entity recognition.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent motivation and theoretical grounding.** The paper's most significant strength is its clear diagnosis of a flaw in the prior state-of-the-art. The discussion of "spurious structures" and the "deficient" nature of the mention hypergraph model is well-explained and convincing. It provides a strong theoretical justification for why the proposed model should be better, as it optimizes a more correct objective function. This elevates the paper from a simple proposal of a new architecture to a work that provides deeper insight into the problem.
2.  **Novel and intuitive model architecture.** The concept of "entity separators" as edges in a multigraph is elegant. It directly models the combinatorial actions that occur at the boundary of each token (e.g., an entity ends AND another continues). This "edge-based paradigm," as the authors frame it, is a clean and powerful way to represent overlapping structures without resorting to the complexity of constituency parsing or the ambiguity of the mention hypergraph. The linear-time complexity makes it practical.
3.  **Comprehensive and convincing experimental validation.** The authors conduct a thorough empirical study on three standard datasets for overlapping NER (ACE2004/2005, GENIA) and one for non-overlapping NER (CoNLL 2003). Crucially, they compare against their own strong implementation of the main baseline (mention hypergraph) using the same feature sets, ensuring a fair comparison. The consistent F1-score improvements across all datasets (e.g., +2.1 on ACE2004, +0.5 on CoNLL 2003) strongly support their claims. The additional analysis breaking down performance on sentences with and without overlapping entities further strengthens the results. The observation of faster convergence (in iterations) is also a valuable practical finding.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The distinction between "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms feels somewhat artificial.** While this framing helps position the work, the argument against a state-based equivalent is not fully developed. The authors claim a state-based model using separators as states would model "unnecessary dependencies," but this is not substantiated. One could easily imagine a first-order sequence model over the 8 separator states placed in the gaps between words. It is not immediately obvious that this would be computationally prohibitive or model dependencies that are more "unnecessary" than those in the proposed model. A more concrete comparison would make this argument more compelling.
2.  **Lack of qualitative error analysis.** The paper demonstrates *that* the model performs better, but provides little insight into *why* beyond the high-level theoretical argument. An error analysis comparing the outputs of the proposed model and the mention hypergraph baseline would be very valuable. For instance, are the gains primarily from better boundary detection, better handling of deeply nested entities, or fewer false positives? Showing a few concrete examples where the nondeficient nature of the multigraph model leads to a correct prediction while the hypergraph model fails would provide a powerful empirical link to the theoretical claims.
3.  **The model's ability to handle crossing entities is unclear and potentially misaligned with the evaluation.** The introduction implies that handling various types of overlap is a key challenge. While the model seems structurally capable of representing crossing entities, Footnote 3 states that during decoding, "ambiguous structures" are resolved as nested entities. This suggests the evaluated system may not produce crossing entities, which are a known, albeit rare, type of overlap. This ambiguity should be clarified. If the model can handle them, it would be a notable advantage, but this capability is not demonstrated in the experiments.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Footnote 3 states that ambiguous structures are decoded as nested entities. Could you clarify the nature of these ambiguities and the reason for this decoding choice? Does this mean your evaluated model cannot predict crossing entities (e.g., "[Bank of [China] announced...]" vs. "[San Francisco] [Bay Area]")? How prevalent are crossing entities in the datasets used?
2.  The theoretical argument regarding spurious structures is a cornerstone of the paper. Could you provide a concrete example from one of the test sets where the mention hypergraph model makes an error that can be plausibly attributed to its deficient objective, and which your nondeficient model correctly identifies? This would powerfully connect your theory to your empirical results.
