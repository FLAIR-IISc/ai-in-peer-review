### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing named entities, with a specific focus on handling overlapping entities (both nested and crossing). The core idea is to model the transitions between tokens not with single edges, but with a set of parallel edges, each corresponding to a specific "entity separator" that describes the boundary condition (e.g., an entity starts, an entity ends, an entity continues). This formulation falls into what the authors term an "edge-based paradigm," where the edges, not just the nodes, carry explicit semantic meaning for interpreting the output structure. The authors provide a theoretical analysis arguing that their model is "non-deficient" as it does not suffer from the "spurious structures" problem present in the prior state-of-the-art mention hypergraph model of Lu and Roth (2015). They conduct experiments on three standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) and one non-overlapping dataset (CoNLL 2003), demonstrating consistent performance improvements over strong baselines, including a reimplementation of the mention hypergraph model, while maintaining the same linear-time inference complexity.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Multigraph Formulation for Overlapping Structures:** The primary contribution is the elegant and intuitive multigraph model itself. The concept of using parallel, semantically distinct edges ("entity separators") to explicitly model all possible boundary combinations (start, end, continue) between adjacent tokens is a novel and powerful way to represent complex overlapping structures within a sequence model.
2.  **Theoretical Analysis of Model "Deficiency":** A key contribution is the identification and analysis of the "spurious structures" problem in the mention hypergraph model. The authors clearly articulate how the normalization term in that model sums over interpretations that cannot be decoded, making it "deficient." They then demonstrate that their multigraph formulation is "non-deficient," providing a strong theoretical motivation for their approach beyond it just being an alternative architecture.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation on multiple standard datasets, showing consistent, statistically meaningful improvements over a strong and directly comparable state-of-the-art baseline. The analysis extends to non-overlapping entities and provides a breakdown of performance on different sentence types, adding robustness to the empirical claims.

**Strengths**

1.  **Conceptual Elegance and Novelty:** The proposed multigraph model is a very clean and intuitive way to tackle the overlapping entity problem. Instead of complicating the state space or relying on complex hyperedge compositions, it localizes the complexity into the parallel edges between simple states. This "entity separator" idea is novel for this task and represents a significant conceptual contribution to structured prediction for NLP.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The paper's strength is significantly enhanced by the analysis in Section 5. The distinction drawn between the proposed model and the mention hypergraph model regarding "spurious structures" is insightful and provides a compelling theoretical argument for why the multigraph model might be superior. This is not just a new model that works; it's a new model that is motivated by fixing a subtle but important theoretical flaw in prior work. This level of analysis is commendable.
3.  **Rigorous and Fair Experimental Comparison:** The experimental setup is excellent. The authors reimplemented the main baseline (mention hypergraph) and used an identical feature set for a fair comparison. The evaluation on multiple datasets with varying characteristics (e.g., proportion of overlapping entities) and the inclusion of a non-overlapping dataset (CoNLL 2003) demonstrate the model's general applicability and robustness. The consistent gains across these varied conditions strongly support the authors' claims.
4.  **Efficiency and Practicality:** A crucial aspect of the work is that the proposed model achieves superior performance and a better theoretical footing without sacrificing computational efficiency. By maintaining the same O(nT) time complexity as the mention hypergraph model, the authors present a solution that is not only more effective but also equally practical.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Qualitative Analysis and Error Discussion:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper would benefit greatly from a qualitative error analysis. The discussion connects the non-deficient property to higher recall, but this remains a hypothesis. Showing a few concrete examples where the multigraph model succeeds and the hypergraph model fails (or vice versa) would provide invaluable intuition into the practical differences between the two "edge-based" approaches. For instance, does one model handle nested entities better while the other is better at crossing entities?
2.  **Modest Performance Gains on Some Datasets:** The F1 score improvements, while consistent, are quite small on the GENIA dataset (~0.4 F1). While the gains on ACE are more substantial (~2.1 F1), the variable margin of improvement suggests that the practical impact of fixing the "spurious structures" issue might be data-dependent. A discussion on why this might be the case (e.g., type of overlaps, data size) would strengthen the paper. A statistical significance test would also be necessary to confirm the importance of these gains.
3.  **The State-based vs. Edge-based Framing:** The classification of models into "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms is an interesting framing device, but its contribution to the field is debatable. One could argue that in any log-linear graphical model, both node (state) and edge features contribute to the score, and the interpretation is always a function of the entire chosen subgraph. While the multigraph and hypergraph models certainly place more semantic weight on the edges, the paradigm distinction feels somewhat forced and serves primarily to position this work rather than to offer a fundamental new taxonomy for structured prediction models.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The connection between the non-deficient property of your model and the empirically observed higher recall is a central and compelling argument. Can you provide any more direct evidence to substantiate this claim? For example, have you analyzed the model posteriors or the value of the partition function (Z) on specific examples to show that the mention hypergraph model is "distracted" by spurious structures, leading it to assign lower probability to the correct path?
2.  Could you please report on the statistical significance of your results, particularly for the GENIA dataset where the margin of improvement over the mention hypergraph model is smallest?
3.  Regarding the faster convergence shown in Figure 7, you note that each iteration takes 12% longer. Could you clarify the overall wall-clock training time? Is the multigraph model faster to train to convergence in absolute time, or does it simply require fewer L-BFGS iterations?
