Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a multigraph-based structured prediction model for recognizing overlapping named entities. It assigns explicit semantics to multiple edges between the same pair of nodes (entity separators S, E, C and their combinations), enabling an edge-based representation of overlapping structures with linear-time inference in sentence length and number of types.
- It provides an analysis contrasting state-based vs. edge-based paradigms and argues that the proposed multigraph model is nondeficient (no spurious structures) unlike the mention hypergraph model of Lu and Roth (2015), while retaining O(nT) inference.
- Empirically, it reports consistent F1 gains over a reimplementation of mention hypergraphs on ACE 2004/2005 and GENIA, and shows competitive performance on non-overlapping CoNLL 2003.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1) A clear edge-semantic multigraph formulation for overlapping entities with linear-time inference and decoding.
2) An analysis of deficiency/spurious structures and a claim of nondeficiency for the proposed model.
3) Empirical improvements over a strong prior model across multiple datasets.

Strengths
- Clear core modeling idea with explicit edge semantics. Encoding boundary/continuation combinations as multi-edges (“entity separators”) is intuitive and directly targets the source of ambiguity in overlapping mentions. The representation is compact and easy to relate to span boundaries, and the edge-based perspective is well argued.
- Strong empirical results with appropriate baselines. The model consistently outperforms the mention hypergraph baseline on ACE2004/2005 (up to +2.1 F1 after dev tuning) and slightly on GENIA. Including a non-overlapping dataset (CoNLL 2003) strengthens the claim that the approach does not hurt standard NER and may help even without overlaps.
- Time complexity preserved with reasonable constants. The paper details inside–outside and max-product procedures and argues O(nT) complexity, matching the hypergraph model, with reported per-iteration overhead of only ~12%. The learning curve suggests faster convergence in iterations, which is practically relevant.
- Useful conceptual framing (state-based vs. edge-based). The discussion helps situate prior work and clarifies why modeling semantics on edges enables compact handling of overlaps without inflating the state space. This may inspire applications in other overlapping-structure tasks.
- Issue identification in prior work (spurious structures). The paper thoughtfully analyzes how the mention hypergraph’s normalization can include structures never produced at decoding, and connects this to reduced recall/overconfidence empirically.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to mention hypergraphs is incremental. The proposed multigraph factors many of the same structural constraints as the mention hypergraph into multi-edges with explicit semantics. While the edge semantics are elegant, the core idea (edge-based inference on a directed structure with linear complexity) and training objective are close to Lu & Roth (2015). The paper would benefit from sharper articulation of what this buys fundamentally beyond removing spurious structures and minor constant-factor changes.
- Nondeficiency claim is only informally justified. The argument that “inside equals sum over path scores” guarantees a one-to-one mapping between entity combinations and paths needs a formal proof: uniqueness of path decomposition for any valid mention set; proof that no two distinct mention sets share exactly the same set of active edges/paths; and that decoding always selects a structure that is counted in the normalization. Currently, the reasoning is persuasive but not rigorous.
- Ambiguity handling and coverage of overlap classes need clarification. The paper states it “resolved ambiguous structures by considering them as nested instead of crossing entities,” which suggests the model or decoding post-processing may not faithfully produce all crossing combinations even if representable. This design choice could affect metrics and comparability. A precise statement of the class of structures representable and decodable, and the impact of the nested-as-default interpretation, is missing.
- Limited experimental analysis. No statistical significance testing is reported for the relatively small margins (e.g., +0.4 F1 on GENIA). Ablations are absent: (i) effect of removing the mention penalty, (ii) effect of each separator type, (iii) input feature subsets, (iv) decoding choices for ambiguous cases. A more detailed error analysis of overlapping vs. non-overlapping cases would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Fairness and reproducibility concerns. The GENIA preprocessing involves a nontrivial tokenization change (splitting at punctuation), which complicates direct comparison with prior results; the paper should quantify how this affects performance and ensure that baselines use the identical preprocessing. For ACE, only the authors’ reimplementation of mention hypergraphs is compared; reporting the original Lu & Roth numbers (or explaining differences) would help. Also, while complexity is discussed, wall-clock training and inference times and memory usage are not reported.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a formal proof (or at least a precise lemma with proof sketch) that your multigraph normalization enumerates each distinct mention set exactly once (i.e., no spurious structures), including a uniqueness argument for path-factorization?
- What exact class of overlapping structures are representable and decodable under your model and interpretation procedure? Can you decode crossing structures? If not, how often does the nested-as-default heuristic change the gold structure and how does it affect F1?
- How do your mention hypergraph results compare to those reported by Lu & Roth (2015) on the same ACE splits and features? If they differ, what accounts for the gaps?
- Are the gains over mention hypergraphs statistically significant across seeds? Please report variance and significance tests. Also provide ablations for the mention penalty and key feature groups.
- Please report wall-clock times and memory usage for training and decoding on ACE (per epoch/per sentence), to complement the complexity claim.

Additional comments
- The model section would benefit from a more formal specification: the factorization of p(y|x), exact edge potential definitions, and the mapping from separators to feature functions.
- The references list has formatting inconsistencies; please clean up to ACL style.
- Releasing code (as promised) will help verify the nondeficiency claim and reproduce the preprocessing.
