Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses recognition of overlapping/nested entities by introducing an edge-labeled multigraph representation with linear-time dynamic programming for training and decoding. The model assigns explicit semantics to parallel edges (entity “separators” S/E/C combinations) between the same pair of nodes, and performs inside–outside over a directed acyclic multigraph. The authors compare primarily to the mention hypergraph model (Lu & Roth, 2015), argue their approach avoids “spurious structures” (nondeficiency), and report modest improvements on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA, plus competitive results on CoNLL2003.

Contributions as I see them (strongest first)
1) A reparameterization of overlapping NER into an edge-based multigraph with linear-time inference and training, including an explicit separator taxonomy and a concrete DP for inside/outside and max-product decoding.
2) An analytical comparison to mention hypergraphs highlighting and exemplifying the “spurious structures” issue; an argument (but not a full proof) that the proposed multigraph avoids deficiency.
3) Empirical evaluation on multiple datasets showing small but consistent gains over a strong hypergraph baseline using matched features, with some convergence advantages.

Strengths
- Clear, compact inference with linear complexity: The model achieves O(nT) training/decoding while supporting general overlapping spans. The separator design provides an intuitive, local decomposition and a clean DP over a DAG. This retains the favorable complexity of mention hypergraphs without hyperedges across many children.
- Head-to-head comparison with a strong specialized baseline under matched features: The experiments compare directly to the Lu & Roth hypergraph with identical feature templates and mention penalty tuning. On ACE2004/2005, multigraph improves F1 by about 1.3–2.1 points after dev tuning; on GENIA the gains are smaller (≈0.4). Such controlled comparisons are valuable given the sensitivity of nested NER to feature engineering.
- Useful analysis of edge- vs state-based paradigms: The paper articulates the representational choice—roles encoded in states versus in edges—and argues the multigraph can avoid unnecessary dependencies that arise when converting to gap-states. This perspective is likely to stimulate work on edge-factored structured prediction in other overlapping settings.
- Discussion of deficiency and training behavior: The spurious structures example for mention hypergraphs is carefully walked through, and the paper reports faster convergence in iterations (albeit slightly slower per-iteration time), which is a practical win if consistent.
- Broader applicability: The separator abstraction is generic for overlapping spans. Although not empirically demonstrated beyond NER, the formulation plausibly extends to other overlapping-structure prediction tasks.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to existing formalisms is somewhat incremental: Representing overlap via edge-labeled DAGs/multigraphs is closely related to finite-state transducer lattices, factor graphs with multiple factors between variables, and segmental/skip-chain CRFs. The “first structured prediction model utilizing multigraphs” claim is too strong without surveying lattice-CRF style work and layered/stacked CRFs that effectively create parallel arcs. The paper itself notes the equivalence to a state-based gap-labeling model; the empirical or computational benefit over such a baseline is not demonstrated.
- Nondeficiency claim lacks a formal proof and uniqueness guarantees: The argument that summing over paths yields a one-to-one correspondence with valid mention sets is intuitive but informal. Ambiguity remains: decoding requires an “interpretation process” and resolves ambiguities as nested rather than crossing. Without a formal bijection between path multisets and structured outputs (and a proof that no two distinct path sets yield the same output), the nondeficiency claim is not fully established.
- Limited baselines and ablations: Beyond mention hypergraph and a non-overlapping CRF, the study omits a direct “gap-state” linear-chain CRF baseline that would test the claimed advantage of edge-based modeling. There are no ablations on the separator inventory, feature groups, or type interactions. The CoNLL result (non-overlapping) is interesting but does not isolate why the multigraph helps there.
- Evaluation comparability and significance: For GENIA, the paper changes tokenization (further splits at punctuation) and groups coordinated/discontiguous mentions; this may not be directly comparable to prior numbers. No statistical significance testing is reported; given small gains on GENIA, the improvements may not be robust. Crossing-entity handling is resolved as nested; the prevalence of true crossings in each dataset is not quantified.
- Clarity gaps in formal specification: The node/edge sets and dynamic programming recurrences are described informally. Precise definitions (scores over parallel edges, aggregation at nodes with multiple children, root hyperedge semantics) and proof of acyclicity would aid reproducibility. The decoding-to-entity mapping is delegated to prior work and to a disambiguation heuristic.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a formal proof (or a precise construction) that establishes a bijection between (multi)set(s) of paths in your multigraph and valid entity sets, thereby proving nondeficiency? Under what conditions could two distinct path decompositions yield the same set of entities?
- How many crossing entities (non-nested overlaps) exist in ACE/GENIA, and how does your decoding’s “nested resolution” affect evaluation on sentences where true crossings occur?
- Please report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap) for the F1 differences vs mention hypergraph on all test sets.
- Can you include a “gap-state” linear-chain CRF baseline with the 8 separator labels on token gaps (your own suggested equivalence) to empirically validate the claimed modeling/computational benefits? Even a first-order model would be informative.
- What is the exact inside/outside recurrence (equations) for nodes with multiple outgoing parallel edges and for the root’s hyperedge? Is the structure always a DAG for arbitrary sentence length and type set?
- Did you try modeling cross-type interactions (e.g., simple pairwise coupling) in the multigraph? If so, how did it affect complexity and accuracy?

Additional comments
- Please clarify whether the mention penalty was tuned identically for both models and whether it was per-type or global.
- Wall-clock runtimes and memory per sentence/type would strengthen the practical claim.
- An error analysis contrasting typical gains/failures vs the hypergraph baseline would help readers understand where the multigraph helps (e.g., short nested mentions, adjacent boundaries, etc.).
