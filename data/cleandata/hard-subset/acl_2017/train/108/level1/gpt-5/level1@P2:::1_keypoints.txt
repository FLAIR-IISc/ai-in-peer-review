Summary: Proposes a multigraph-structured prediction model encoding explicit edge semantics for overlapping entities, with log-linear training and O(nT) inference, claiming nondeficiency and showing consistent F1 gains on ACE/GENGIA and competitive CoNLL-2003 results.

Main contributions:
- Multigraph formulation encoding entity separators as parallel edges enabling linear-time inference for overlapping entities.
- Theoretical analysis that the multigraph avoids spurious structures in the partition function compared to the mention hypergraph.
- Empirical evidence of modest but consistent F1 improvements and faster convergence than prior structured models.

Strengths:
- Simple, intuitive edge-semantics representation that cleanly captures nested and crossing entities without inflating the state space.
- Convincing nondeficiency argument linking edge semantics to a better match between training objective and predictor.
- Fair experimental setup using identical feature templates and tuning, showing consistent gains across datasets.
- Retains O(nT) time complexity and empirically requires fewer optimizer iterations to converge.
- Helpful framing contrasting edge-based vs. state-based design choices for structured models.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is incremental relative to the mention hypergraph; the “multigraph” label may be more terminological than fundamentally new.
- The impact of eliminating spurious structures is not empirically isolated or quantified.
- Limited baselines and potential fairness issues (e.g., altered GENIA tokenization) versus newer neural nested/crossing NER methods.
- Some modeling and DP details are underspecified; formal derivations and discussion of cross-type interactions are lacking.
- Evaluation breadth limited: crossing entities are rare and breakdowns by nested vs. crossing cases, decoding policy, and statistical significance are missing.

Questions for authors:
- Please provide formal derivations/proofs for the DP recurrences, complexity, and the nondeficiency bijection between structures and DP paths.
- How frequent are crossing entities in the datasets, and how does performance differ on nested vs. crossing cases?
- Can you empirically isolate the effect of nondeficiency (e.g., constrain mention hypergraph or inject spuriousness)?
- How sensitive are results to the chosen separator set, and would pruning separators affect performance or speed?
- How does your GENIA tokenization differ from prior work, and can you report results under standard tokenization?
- Were statistical significance tests run for reported F1 gains; if so, please include them?
- What are the practical constant-factor runtime and memory overheads vs. the mention hypergraph per sentence/token, and how do they scale with T?
