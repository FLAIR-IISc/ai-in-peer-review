- Addresses overlapping entities with a multigraph method, motivated by crossing-entity challenges.  
- Technique appears to work but yields only modest empirical improvements.  
- Paper includes a useful efficiency comparison to a prior system.  
- Overall writing is good but needs refinement in many places.  

- Motivation for the task is unclear, especially the practical utility of nested entities.  
- Missing technical details about edge inclusion and the construction of I^k_t nodes.  
- Empirical gains are small; effect sizes and practical significance are not reported.  
- Lacks comparisons to simple baselines (e.g., dictionary lookup) or commercial systems (e.g., Google NLP).  
- Insufficient focus on crossing entities: need per-type performance, counts of newly detected entities, and detailed error analysis.  
- Unclear whether improvements come from nested or crossing entity detection.  
- Paper reads as incremental and does not convincingly tackle the core crossing-entity problem.  
- Reviewer leans slightly toward rejection due to limited novelty/impact.  

- First mention of multigraphs needs a brief explanation for non-experts.  
- Multiple awkward phrasings and unclear referents (e.g., "they", "this task").  
- Inconsistent or incorrect use of articles and plurals throughout the text.  
- Typos and misspellings (e.g., "Pervious") need correction.  
- Ambiguous terms: "spurious structures", "state" in (hyper-)graphs, "NP chunking".  
- Figure 2 is unclear (why a hypergraph, grayscale issues, gray nodes/edges, selection criteria).  
- Unclear why both short and long overlapping entities in Figure 2 should be detected instead of just the longer span.  
- Inconsistent notation and punctuation (brackets, footnote placement, commas after enumerations, comma after Equation 1).  
- Several algorithmic choices are unexplained (how the CS-edge is chosen, how separators are determined, why certain edges are absent per Footnote 2).  
- Many wording suggestions and grammar fixes (e.g., "We see that our model", "Used in this experiments", "The following five types").  
- Request to report effect sizes and clarify claims like "outperforms" and "significantly better" given small F1 differences.  
- Dataset and result descriptions need clearer phrasing (e.g., "On the GENIA dataset", specify which recall is low).  
- Redundant or unclear sentences should be streamlined (e.g., "Allows us to use to make use").  
- Clarify experimental choices and priorities (why favor nested entities over crossing ones; footnote 3 concern).  
- Table 5 and other results show similar F1 scores—avoid overstating improvements.  
- Ensure consistency in reference formatting and capitalization of paper titles.
