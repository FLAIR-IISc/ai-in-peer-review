The paper introduces a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping entities.

**Strengths:**

The problem addressed – identifying overlapping entities, particularly crossing entities – is potentially interesting, especially for resolving ambiguity in text. The proposed technique appears functional, although the empirical results demonstrate only modest improvements. I appreciate the discussion of efficiency compared to prior work. The paper is generally well-written, though some minor polishing is needed (detailed below).

**Weaknesses:**

The motivation for this work is not fully convincing. The example of detecting “China” within “Bank of China” raises the question of practical application. While the value for crossing entities is apparent, the use case for nested entities remains unclear and requires further justification to engage the reader. Regarding the approach itself, key details are missing. Specifically, the criteria for including edges in the multigraph are not defined. Lines 229–233 present multiple options for the *I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub>* nodes without clarifying which edges are ultimately selected. 

The empirical evaluation shows improvements over existing methods, but these are not substantial. The claim of “outperforming” these methods feels overstated given the small margin of improvement. The effect size is not reported; a two-percentage-point increase in F<sub>1</sub> may not be practically significant.  It would be valuable to understand the real-world impact of these improvements – how many previously undiscovered “important” entities are now identified? Furthermore, the performance of a simple dictionary-based approach, or a commercial entity linking system (e.g., Google’s NLP cloud), on the same datasets should be included for context.

The discussion of results would benefit from a greater focus on crossing entities, which I believe represent the more compelling aspect of this work. How does the system perform specifically on crossing entities? How many more are detected compared to previous methods, and why were they previously missed? Is the performance gain attributable to improved nested entity detection, or to better identification of crossing entities? A comparative error analysis, contrasting the errors made by the proposed system with those of existing methods, would also strengthen this section.

**General Discussion:**

I recognize the relevance of the problems related to named entity recognition and the potential benefits of recognizing crossing entities. However, the rationale for focusing on nested entities is not adequately explained in the paper, nor is this addressed in the evaluation. A discussion of the advantages and disadvantages, illustrated with examples, and a greater emphasis on results concerning crossing entities might increase my confidence in the work.

Currently, I have a lukewarm impression of the paper, leaning towards rejection. It feels like another iteration on an existing problem without a clear emphasis on the crucial aspect of crossing entities.
