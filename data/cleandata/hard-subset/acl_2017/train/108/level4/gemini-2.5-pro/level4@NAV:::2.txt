This paper proposes a method based on multigraphs for the detection of potentially overlapping entities.

**Strengths:**
The problem addressed, particularly the detection of crossing entities within text, is both relevant and interesting. The proposed technique appears to be effective, even if the empirical results do not demonstrate a substantial improvement over baselines. The inclusion of a discussion on computational efficiency in comparison to prior work is a commendable addition. Overall, the paper is well-written, though it would benefit from further polishing as detailed in the minor remarks.

**Weaknesses:**
The manuscript's primary weakness is its insufficient motivation. The importance of detecting nested entities (e.g., identifying "China" within the entity "Bank of China," as per the introduction's example) is not well-established. While the use case for crossing entities is clear, the practical application for nested entities needs to be more thoroughly explained to engage the reader.

Regarding the methodology, the description of the proposed approach lacks critical details. For instance, the decision criterion for including an edge in the multigraph is not specified. Similarly, while several configurations for the I^k_t nodes are mentioned (lines 229-233), the paper does not clarify which configuration is ultimately used or how edges are formed in this context.

The empirical evaluation could be more robust. The reported performance gains over previous methods are marginal, making the term "outperformed" seem like an overstatement. The evaluation would be more convincing with an analysis of the effect size to demonstrate the practical significance of a few percentage points of F1 improvement. It would be beneficial to know how many critical entities are now discovered that were missed by other methods. Furthermore, the experiments lack crucial context that could be provided by including simpler baselines (e.g., a dictionary-based method) and state-of-the-art commercial systems (e.g., Google Cloud NLP), which would help situate the paper's contribution.

The results discussion would benefit from a deeper analysis with a specific focus on crossing entities, which represents the more compelling aspect of the problem. The paper should report performance specifically on this subset to clarify whether the gains come from improved nested entity detection, crossing entity detection, or both. A comparative error analysis, highlighting the types of errors the proposed model corrects (and introduces) relative to previous methods, would also significantly strengthen this section.

**General Discussion:**
While the problem of named entity recognition for crossing entities is significant, this paper does not adequately motivate its focus on nested entities. This lack of motivation carries through to the evaluation, which fails to provide a detailed analysis of performance on the crossing entity subset or a compelling error analysis. Without a clearer demonstration of the advantages for the more challenging crossing entity problem, with illustrative examples, the contribution feels incremental. Therefore, my assessment of the paper is lukewarm, with a slight inclination toward rejection. The work seems like another attempt in the area without sufficiently emphasizing the most critical research questions.
