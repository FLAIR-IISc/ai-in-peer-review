### Summary

This paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping named entities, including both nested and crossing configurations. The core idea is to model all possible entity spans and their relationships within a multigraph structure, which is then decoded to identify the final set of entities.

### Strengths

1.  **Interesting Problem:** The paper addresses the challenging problem of recognizing overlapping entities. The task of disambiguating crossing entities (e.g., "University of Washington" and "Washington Huskies") is particularly well-motivated and important for downstream applications.
2.  **Sound Approach:** The proposed technique appears technically sound, and the empirical results demonstrate a consistent, albeit modest, improvement over the methods used for comparison.
3.  **Efficiency Considerations:** I appreciate that the authors discuss the efficiency of their model in comparison to a previous system, which is an important practical consideration.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, it would benefit from further polishing to address several minor issues (detailed below).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Motivation:** The problem, specifically regarding nested entities, is not sufficiently motivated. Using the paper's introductory example, why is it critical to detect "China" as an entity within "Bank of China"? While the utility of identifying crossing entities is clear, the use case for nested entities is not well-established. A stronger motivation would help engage the reader and clarify the contribution's impact.
2.  **Methodological Clarity:** The description of the approach is missing some crucial details. The paper mentions several options for representing token-internal nodes (I^k_t) but never clarifies the exact decision criterion used to determine which edges are included in the multigraph. This is a critical detail for understanding and replicating the method.
3.  **Empirical Evaluation:**
    *   **Magnitude of Improvement:** The reported improvements are marginal. The term "outperformed" seems overly strong for gains of a few tenths of a percentage point in F1-score. The paper would be more convincing if it discussed the effect size or practical significance of these improvements. Does a 2-point increase in F1 correspond to the discovery of many important entities missed by other systems?
    *   **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The results discussion lacks depth. A dedicated analysis of the model's performance on crossing entities—arguably the more interesting subset—is needed. How many more crossing entities are detected compared to prior work? Is the overall F1 improvement primarily due to better nested entity detection, or does the model excel at crossing entities as well?
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The evaluation would be strengthened by including baselines that provide broader context. For instance, what performance would a simple dictionary-based method achieve? Furthermore, comparing the results to a state-of-the-art commercial system (e.g., Google's Cloud NLP API) would help situate the contribution within the landscape of currently available tools.
    *   **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. Comparing the types of errors made by the proposed system against those from previous methods could provide valuable insights into the model's specific advantages and remaining limitations.

### Overall Recommendation

I appreciate the paper's focus on named entity recognition and see the value in developing better methods for recognizing crossing entities. However, the motivation for nested entities remains unclear, and this ambiguity carries through to the evaluation, which fails to shed light on this aspect. The paper currently feels like an incremental improvement without a compelling narrative or a deep analysis of its core strengths, particularly concerning the challenging crossing entities. A more thorough discussion of errors, example-driven analysis of advantages, and a specific focus on performance gains for crossing entities would have been more persuasive.

As it stands, I am lukewarm on the paper with a slight tendency toward rejection. It appears to be another attempt at the problem without sufficiently emphasizing or analyzing the most critical and interesting questions in this domain.

### Detailed Comments & Minor Remarks

*   **Multigraph Definition:** The term "multigraph" could be briefly defined upon its first use for readers who may be unfamiliar with it.
*   **Phrasing:**
    *   "previously noted by ... many previous": This phrasing is awkward.
    *   "Solving this task": It is unclear which specific task is being referred to.
    *   "to find out": This phrasing is too informal. Consider "to determine" or "to investigate."
    *   "we make two": Consider a more formal alternative, such as "we conduct two experiments."
    *   "the simple first order assumption": This assumption should be explicitly defined.
    *   "we see that our model": Consider "we have shown" or "our results demonstrate."
    *   "tried to follow as close": Rephrase for clarity, e.g., "we followed the feature design of previous work as closely as possible."
*   **Formatting & Typos:**
    *   "e.g.": This abbreviation should not be in italics.
    *   "pervious" -> "previous".
    *   "of Lu and Roth~(2015)" -> "of Lu and Roth (2015)".
    *   Small integers in prose (e.g., "the following five types") should be spelled out.
    *   Punctuation should be placed after footnotes, not before.
    *   Citations as Nouns: Please avoid using references as nouns (e.g., "Following (Lu and Roth, 2015)"). Rephrase to "Following the work of Lu and Roth (2015), we...".
    *   "In GENIA dataset" -> "On the GENIA dataset".
    *   "used in this experiments" -> "used in these experiments".
    *   "allows us to use to make use" -> "allows us to make use".
    *   **References:** The capitalization of paper titles in the references is inconsistent (some are lowercased, others are in title case). Please ensure consistency.
*   **Technical & Experimental Clarity:**
    *   "time linear in n": When n is sentence length, the distinction between linear and cubic complexity is highly significant. The claim should be precise.
    *   "spurious structures": This term is used in the introduction without a clear definition in this context.
    *   "NP chunking": It would be helpful to spell this out as "Noun Phrase chunking" on first use.
    *   "Since they set": It is unclear who "they" refers to.
    *   "types of states": The term "state" is used interchangeably with "node" later. Please clarify what a "state" is in the context of the hypergraph.
    *   **Figure 2:**
        *   The figure is not friendly to black-and-white printing due to its reliance on color.
        *   It is unclear why some nodes/edges are grayed out. The selection criteria for highlighted vs. grayed-out edges are not explained.
        *   The figure does not immediately convey why it represents a hypergraph.
        *   The motivation for detecting both entities in the example is not clear. What is the benefit of knowing "Bank of China" *and* "China" versus only the longer entity?
    *   "child nodes in a hypergraph": The concept of "child nodes" in a hypergraph is not standard and should be defined.
    *   "CS-edge": How is the separator CS-edge chosen algorithmically?
    *   **Footnote 2:** "due to the missing edge": How was it determined that this specific edge *should* be missing?
    *   **Footnote 3:** The claim that not favoring crossing over nested entities is a "good property" needs justification. Why not prioritize the more complex crossing cases? Examples would be helpful.
    *   "this low recall": Which specific low recall value is being referred to?
    *   **Table 4:** The use of "highlighted in bold" is not sufficient. Please discuss the effect size or statistical significance of the differences. A 0.4 point F1 difference is too small to be described as "outperforming" without further justification.
    *   **Table 5:** The F1 scores appear very similar. The "outperform" claim seems like a stretch here as well.
    *   "is more confident": How does a model being "more confident" lead to an increase in recall? This connection should be explained.
