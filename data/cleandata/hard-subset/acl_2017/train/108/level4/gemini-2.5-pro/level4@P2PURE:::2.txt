This paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping named entities. A multigraph, where multiple edges can connect the same pair of nodes, is used to model this phenomenon.

### Strengths

The problem of identifying overlapping entities is compelling, particularly for the challenging case of crossing (discontinuous) entities. The proposed technique appears sound, and the discussion of its efficiency relative to a previous system is a welcome inclusion. The paper is generally well-written, although it would benefit from further polishing to address several minor details (see below).

### Weaknesses

The motivation for the problem is underdeveloped. While the utility of identifying crossing entities is clear, the paper fails to present a compelling use case for detecting nested entities. For instance, using the paper's introductory example, why is it important to detect "China" as an entity within "Bank of China"? A stronger motivation for the nested entity sub-problem would significantly increase the paper's impact.

Key details of the approach are missing. The paper does not specify the decision criterion for including an edge in the multigraph. Furthermore, while lines 229-233 mention several options for the I^k_t nodes, it is not clarified which of these options are used to construct the edges, leaving the model's implementation underspecified.

The empirical evaluation could be more robust. The reported results, while showing an improvement over previous approaches, are marginal. The use of terms like "outperformed" feels overstated for gains of a few percentage points in F1-score. An analysis of the effect size is needed to contextualize these improvements and argue for their practical significance. The evaluation would also be strengthened by a qualitative analysis. For example, how many crucial entities does this model discover that were missed by previous methods?

The evaluation also lacks crucial baselines. It is unclear how the proposed method compares to a simple but plausible dictionary-based approach for finding overlapping entities. Additionally, a comparison against a state-of-the-art commercial system (e.g., Google's Cloud NLP API) would provide a valuable benchmark for the model's performance.

The results discussion lacks depth, particularly concerning crossing entities, which is arguably the more interesting sub-problem. The analysis should be stratified to show performance specifically on crossing entities versus nested ones. Is the overall performance gain driven by improvements in detecting nested entities, crossing entities, or both? A detailed error analysis comparing the types of errors made by the proposed system against those of previous methods would also significantly strengthen this section. For instance, which crossing entities were newly identified, which were missed, and what are the potential reasons?

### General Discussion

In summary, while the problem of identifying crossing entities is important, the paper's contribution is weakened by two main factors. First, it fails to adequately motivate the need for identifying nested entities. Second, the empirical evaluation does not provide a focused analysis of performance on crossing entities, which is the most compelling aspect of the problem. A more thorough evaluation, including specific examples, error analysis, and a clearer focus on the advantages for crossing entity recognition, would be needed to make a stronger case for the paper's contributions. As it stands, the paper feels like an incremental effort that does not sufficiently address the most critical and interesting questions in this problem space.

### Minor Remarks

**Clarity and Phrasing:**
*   When first introducing the term "multigraph," a brief definition would benefit some readers.
*   The phrasing "previously noted by ... many previous" is awkward and should be revised.
*   When using phrases like "Solving this task," please ensure the antecedent task is unambiguous.
*   Explain why "e.g." is italicized, or format it consistently with the surrounding text.
*   It is not clear why spurious structures are a concern in the introduction. Please clarify what is meant by this.
*   Phrases like "regarded as a chunk" and "to find out" could be rephrased more formally.
*   The phrase "we make two" should be revised to something like "we conduct two."
*   In "Since they set," clarify who "they" refers to.
*   In "the combination of states alone does not," the sentence is incomplete.
*   Please define what is meant by "the simple first order assumption."
*   The phrase "allows us to use to make use" contains a redundant "to use."
*   Rephrase "tried to follow as close" to something more formal, such as "we followed the feature design of previous works as closely as possible."
*   Avoid using references as nouns (e.g., "Following (Lu and Roth, 2015)"). Instead, write "Following Lu and Roth (2015)."
*   Typo: "pervious" should be "previous."

**Technical and Methodological Details:**
*   Define "NP chunking" (presumably Noun Phrase chunking) on first use.
*   In the discussion of hypergraphs, clarify what "state" refers to. It appears to be used interchangeably with "node."
*   Clarify the definition of "child nodes" in the context of a hypergraph.
*   In the last paragraph before Section 4.1, the algorithm for choosing the CS-edge to represent an entity separator is not explained.
*   The remark in footnote 3 that the model does not distinguish between nested and crossing entities is presented as a positive, but this is unclear. Why is this preferable to favoring the more challenging crossing entities? Please clarify with examples.

**Figures and Tables:**
*   Figure 2 is difficult to interpret. It is not immediately clear why it represents a hypergraph. The use of color is not effective for black-and-white printing, and the distinction between gray and black nodes/edges is not explained. The selection criteria for the highlighted edges are also unclear.
*   Regarding Figure 2, the motivation for detecting both entities in the example is not provided. What is the advantage of detecting the shorter entity in addition to the longer one that contains it?
*   In Table 4, claims of being "significantly better" should be supported by statistical tests or a discussion of effect size. A 0.4 point F1 improvement is marginal and arguably does not constitute "outperforming" the baseline.
*   In Table 5, the F1 scores appear very similar. The term "outperform" seems to be a stretch here as well. Please justify this claim or tone down the language.
*   The claim that the model "is more confident" needs justification. How does this property increase recall?

**Formatting and Style:**
*   For numbered lists (e.g., end of page 2), please place a comma after each item and a period after the last one.
*   Small numbers in prose (e.g., "the following five types") should be spelled out.
*   Footnotes should be placed after punctuation marks, not before them.
*   The formatting of parenthetical phrases like "(denoting...)" is inconsistent. Please make it uniform.
*   In the References section, please ensure consistent capitalization for paper titles.
