### Summary

The paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping named entities.

### Strengths

*   The paper addresses the interesting and challenging problem of identifying overlapping entities. The potential to handle crossing entities, in particular, is a valuable direction of research.
*   The proposed technique appears sound and demonstrates an empirical improvement over the evaluated baselines.
*   The discussion of computational efficiency in comparison to a previous system is a welcome inclusion.
*   The paper is generally well-written, although it would benefit from further polishing to improve clarity and precision in several places (see Minor Remarks).

### Weaknesses

A number of weaknesses in the paper's motivation, methodology, and evaluation currently limit its impact.

*   **Problem Motivation:** A primary weakness is the lack of clear motivation, particularly concerning nested entities. Using the paper's introductory example, the utility of identifying "China" within "Bank of China" is not explained. While the case for identifying crossing entities is more intuitive, the paper needs to articulate a compelling use case for nested entity detection to fully engage the reader.
*   **Methodological Clarity:** The description of the method is missing key details that are crucial for reproducibility and understanding. Specifically, the decision criterion for including an edge in the multigraph is not defined. Furthermore, while lines 229-233 mention several options for the I^k_t nodes, the paper does not specify which configuration is used in the experiments or how edges connected to these nodes are determined.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation could be significantly strengthened in several ways:
    *   **Magnitude of Improvement:** The reported improvements over prior work are marginal. The term "outperformed" seems too strong for gains of a few percentage points in F1-score. The paper should discuss the effect size and the practical significance of these improvements. For a user, what is the tangible benefit of this small increase in F1?
    *   **Baselines:** The evaluation would be more convincing with the inclusion of additional baselines. For example, what performance would a simpler dictionary-based method achieve for finding overlapping entities? To place the results in a broader context, a comparison against a state-of-the-art commercial system (e.g., Google's Cloud NLP API) that performs entity recognition and linking would be highly informative.
    *   **Analysis of Results:** The discussion of results should place more emphasis on the more challenging case of crossing entities, which is arguably the more interesting aspect of this problem. The analysis does not separate performance on nested versus crossing entities, making it unclear if the reported gains come from the more straightforward nested case or from genuine improvements in detecting crossing entities. The reviewer would like to see a breakdown of performance on this subset, including how many more crossing entities are detected and an analysis of those that are still missed.
    *   **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis comparing the proposed system with baselines would significantly strengthen the evaluation. Such an analysis could highlight which specific types of entities the model now correctly identifies and which ones it still misses, providing valuable insights into the model's behavior.

### General Discussion

The problem of named entity recognition for complex cases like crossing entities is important. However, this paper's contribution is weakened by its failure to sufficiently motivate the need for detecting nested entities, both in the introduction and in the evaluation. The empirical results, while showing modest gains, lack the depth needed to be truly convincing. A more thorough analysis—with a focus on performance for crossing entities, stronger baselines, and a qualitative discussion of errors and advantages with concrete examples—would be required to demonstrate a significant contribution.

As it stands, the reviewer is not fully convinced of the paper's impact. The work appears to be an incremental improvement without a clear demonstration of its practical or theoretical significance, particularly for the important sub-problem of crossing entities.
