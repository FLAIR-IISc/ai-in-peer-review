### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel structured prediction model for named entity recognition that is capable of handling overlapping entities. The model is based on a multigraph representation, where multiple distinct edges (termed "entity separators") can connect the same pair of nodes, each with explicit semantics related to whether an entity is starting, continuing, or ending. The authors present the training and inference procedures for this model, which maintain a linear time complexity in the sentence length, similar to a recent state-of-the-art hypergraph model. They also provide a theoretical analysis arguing that their model does not suffer from the "spurious structures" issue present in the hypergraph baseline, making it non-deficient. Experiments on standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) show that the proposed model achieves modest improvements in F1-score over previous methods.

### Main Contributions

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Multigraph-based Model:** The primary contribution is the proposal of a novel structured prediction model that uses a multigraph to represent and predict overlapping entities. The explicit modeling of entity boundaries through semantic "entity separator" edges is a creative approach.
2.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a useful analysis contrasting its "edge-based" paradigm with prior "state-based" models. More importantly, it identifies the "spurious structures" issue in the mention hypergraph model and demonstrates that the proposed multigraph model is non-deficient, which is a solid theoretical advantage.

### Strengths

1.  **Interesting Problem and Novel Formulation:** The paper addresses the challenging and important problem of overlapping entity recognition. The proposed multigraph-based method is technically sound and presents a novel application of this graph structure to a structured prediction task in NLP.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness and Efficiency:** The analysis of the model's theoretical properties is a key strength. The authors clearly show that their model avoids the deficiency of a key baseline (the mention hypergraph model) while maintaining the same efficient O(nT) time complexity for inference. This is a significant conceptual improvement.
3.  **Well-Written Paper:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, making the core ideas and the distinction from prior work relatively easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Motivation for Nested Entities:** The paper does not adequately motivate the problem, particularly for nested entities. The introduction uses the example of finding "China" within "Bank of China," but it fails to explain the downstream use case or practical benefit of identifying the nested entity. While the case for crossing entities is more intuitive, a stronger motivation for all types of overlapping entities is needed to frame the importance of this work.
2.  **Lack of Detail on Model Construction:** Crucial details about the model's implementation appear to be missing. Section 4 describes 8 types of "entity separators" that correspond to edges, but it is not clear what decision criterion is used to determine which edges should be present in the graph for a given input sentence. Without this information, the approach is not fully reproducible.
3.  **Marginal Empirical Gains and Lack of Significance Analysis:** The empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art are quite small. While the model shows consistent gains, an improvement of 2 F1 points on ACE2004 and 0.4 points on GENIA is modest. The claim of "outperforming" previous work feels overstated. The paper would be much stronger if it included an analysis of the practical significance (effect size) of these improvements.
4.  **Limited Evaluation and Error Analysis:** The experimental evaluation could be more thorough.
    *   A qualitative error analysis is missing. Comparing the types of errors made by the proposed model versus the mention hypergraph baseline would provide valuable insight into the practical effects of the non-deficient model structure. For example, how many "important" entities are now correctly identified that were missed before?
    *   The evaluation lacks comparisons to other relevant baselines. A simple dictionary-based method could provide a useful lower bound, while a comparison to a modern commercial system (e.g., Google's NLP Cloud API) would help place the results in a broader, practical context.
5.  **Insufficient Focus on Crossing Entities:** The evaluation does not specifically analyze the model's performance on crossing entities, which are arguably a more complex and interesting sub-problem than nested entities. A performance breakdown on crossing vs. nested entities is needed to understand where the model's advantages truly lie. A discussion of which specific crossing entities were newly detected or still missed would greatly strengthen the results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the use case for recognizing nested entities? For example, in what scenario is it critical to identify "China" as a separate entity when "Bank of China" is also present?
2.  Could you please clarify the decision process for including edges in the multigraph? How does the model decide which of the 8 entity separators (e.g., CS, EC) is active between two tokens during decoding?
3.  Given the modest F1-score improvements, could you provide a more qualitative analysis of the errors your model corrects compared to the mention hypergraph baseline? Are there specific patterns of overlapping entities that your model is uniquely able to capture?
4.  Does your model's improved performance stem from better recognition of nested entities, crossing entities, or both? A performance breakdown on these subsets would be very helpful.

### Additional Comments

*   **Introduction:**
    *   It would be helpful to provide a brief definition of a "multigraph" when it is first introduced for readers unfamiliar with the term.
    *   The phrase "previously noted by... many previous" is slightly redundant.
    *   The term "spurious structures" is introduced without definition, which may confuse readers.
    *   The paper emphasizes its linear time complexity. Could you comment on the practical impact of this for typical sentence lengths in these datasets compared to a cubic-time model?
*   **Modeling:**
    *   In Section 3, when defining the 5 types of states, it is unclear what a "state" is in the context of a hypergraph. It seems to be used interchangeably with "node." Please clarify.
    *   What are "child nodes" in a directed hypergraph? This concept could be defined more formally.
    *   Footnotes should generally be placed after punctuation, not before it (e.g., "...extract entities. ^3" instead of "...extract entities^3 .").
    *   Footnote 3 mentions that ambiguous structures are resolved as nested rather than crossing entities. Could you justify this choice and provide examples?
*   **Figures and Tables:**
    *   Figure 2: The distinction between the multigraph and hypergraph is not immediately obvious. The use of color is lost in black-and-white printing, and it is unclear why some nodes/edges are greyed out. A more detailed caption would be beneficial.
    *   Table 5: The F1-scores on CoNLL 2003 are very close. The term "outperforms" seems like a stretch here as well.
*   **Minor Wording and Typos:**
    *   "pervious" -> "previous"
    *   "used in this experiments" -> "used in these experiments"
    *   "allows us to use to make use" -> "allows us to make use"
    *   "Following (Lu and Roth, 2015)" -> References should not be used as nouns. Please rephrase to "Following the work of Lu and Roth (2015)..." or similar.
    *   "converge than the mention hypergraph" -> "converge faster than the mention hypergraph model"
    *   The capitalization in the titles of the references is inconsistent (some are in sentence case, others in title case).
