This paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping named entities.

### Strengths

The problem of identifying overlapping entities is interesting, particularly for the case of "crossing" entities, where it is necessary to determine which of the overlapping mentions is the intended one. The proposed technique appears to be effective, although the empirical results do not demonstrate a dramatic improvement over the state of the art. The discussion of the model's efficiency compared to prior work is a welcome inclusion. Overall, the paper is well-written, though it would benefit from further polishing (see minor remarks below).

### Weaknesses

The motivation for the problem is not fully developed. Using the paper's introductory example, it is unclear why it is important to detect "China" as an entity within "Bank of China". While the use case for resolving crossing entities is clear, the practical application for identifying nested entities is not sufficiently explained. A stronger motivation would help to engage the reader and highlight the paper's contribution.

The description of the approach is missing some important details. Specifically, the criterion for including an edge in the graph is not defined. Furthermore, lines 229-233 mention several options for the I^k_t nodes, but it is not specified which edges are actually used in the final model.

Regarding the empirical evaluation, the results, while showing an improvement over previous approaches, are not substantially better. The paper's claim to have "outperformed" prior work seems overstated given the slight improvements in F1 score. The paper would benefit from a discussion of the effect size. Do the reported improvements of a few percentage points in F1-score translate to a meaningful difference in practice? For instance, how many critical entities does the new model discover that were missed by previous methods?

To better contextualize the results, it would be useful to include a simpler baseline, such as a dictionary-based method, which could also identify overlapping entities. Similarly, a comparison against a state-of-the-art commercial system (e.g., Google's Cloud NLP API) would provide a valuable benchmark and help situate the proposed method's performance within the broader landscape of available tools.

The results discussion would be more compelling with a greater focus on crossing entities, which represent a more challenging and arguably more interesting subproblem than nested entities. What is the model's performance specifically on this subset? How many more crossing entities are detected compared to previous work? A qualitative analysis of which entities were missed and why would be insightful. It is also unclear whether the overall performance gains stem from improved nested entity detection, crossing entity detection, or both. A general error analysis comparing the types of mistakes made by the proposed system versus previous ones would significantly strengthen this section.

### General Discussion

This paper addresses an interesting problem in named entity recognition, with the potential to handle crossing entities being particularly noteworthy. However, the motivation for detecting nested entities is not well-established, and this is not clarified in the evaluation. The paper would have been more convincing with a detailed error analysis, illustrative examples of its advantages, and a specific evaluation of its performance on crossing entities. As it stands, the contribution feels incremental, particularly because it does not sufficiently explore the most compelling aspect of the problem (crossing entities). For these reasons, my overall assessment is lukewarm, with a slight inclination towards rejection.

### Minor Remarks

*   When "multigraph" is first introduced, a brief definition (i.e., a graph where multiple edges can connect the same two nodes) would be helpful for some readers.
*   The phrasing "previously noted by ... many previous" is awkward.
*   In "Solving this task," it is unclear which task is being referred to.
*   The term "spurious structures" in the introduction is ambiguous.
*   Please expand the abbreviation "NP chunking" to "noun phrase chunking" on its first use.
*   In "Since they set," the pronoun "they" is ambiguous.
*   Typo: "pervious" should be "previous".
*   Numbers below ten should generally be spelled out (e.g., "five types").
*   The term "state" is used in the context of the (hyper)graph. Please define what a "state" is. It appears to be used interchangeably with "node" later on.
*   The concept of "child nodes" in a hypergraph is not standard. Please provide a definition.
*   Figure 2 is difficult to interpret. It is not immediately clear why it represents a hypergraph. The use of color is problematic for black-and-white printing, and the graying out of some nodes/edges is not explained. The rationale for highlighting certain edges is also missing.
*   In the Figure 2 example, what is the practical benefit of detecting both the nested and the longer entity, as opposed to only identifying the longer one?
*   Footnotes should be placed after punctuation marks, not before them.
*   Footnote 2 mentions a "missing edge". How was it determined that this edge should be missing?
*   In the last paragraph before Section 4.1, it is stated that a CS-edge represents an entity separator. How is this edge chosen algorithmically?
*   The phrase "to find out" is too informal. Consider "to determine" or "to investigate".
*   The phrasing "we make two" is awkward. Please rephrase to "we conduct two experiments" or similar.
*   Footnote 3 states the model does not distinguish between nested and crossing entities. Why is this considered a positive trait? An argument could be made for prioritizing crossing entities. An example would be helpful here.
*   Please define the "simple first-order assumption".
*   Do not use citations as nouns (e.g., change "Following (Lu and Roth, 2015)" to "Following the work of Lu and Roth (2015)").
*   While the best results are highlighted in bold, the practical significance (i.e., effect size) of these small improvements is questionable.
*   When claiming results are "significantly better," please clarify if this is in a statistical sense and report the effect size.
*   An improvement of 0.4 F1 points is too small to be described as "outperforming" a baseline.
*   In Table 5, the F1 scores are very close. The claim of "outperforming" seems exaggerated.
*   The paper claims the model "is more confident," which increases recall. This connection is not intuitive; please explain the reasoning.
*   The capitalization of titles in the reference list is inconsistent. Please format them uniformly.
