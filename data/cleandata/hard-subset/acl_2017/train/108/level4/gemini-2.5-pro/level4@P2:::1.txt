### Summary

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping named entities. The core idea is to represent entity structures using a graph where multiple, semantically distinct edges (termed "entity separators") can connect the same pair of nodes. These separators explicitly model the various roles a token can play, such as starting, ending, or continuing an entity, or a combination thereof. The authors claim this approach resolves a key theoretical limitation in a previous state-of-the-art model, the mention hypergraph (MH), which they term the "spurious structures" issue. Through extensive experiments on standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA), the proposed model is shown to achieve superior F1-scores compared to the MH model and other baselines.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Nondeficient Model for Overlapping NER:** The primary contribution is the development of a multigraph-based structured prediction model that is provably "nondeficient." The paper provides a theoretical analysis showing that, unlike the prior state-of-the-art mention hypergraph model, its objective function does not suffer from "spurious structures," where multiple distinct entity interpretations map to the same underlying subgraph during normalization. This provides a sounder theoretical foundation for modeling.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating that the proposed model consistently outperforms the mention hypergraph model and other strong baselines across multiple benchmark datasets for overlapping entity recognition. The gains in F1-score are significant, and the authors include detailed analyses of performance on sentences with and without overlapping entities.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Theoretically Grounded Method:** The proposal to use a multigraph with semantically rich edges ("entity separators") is an elegant and novel way to handle the complexity of overlapping entities. The theoretical analysis in Section 5.2, which identifies and resolves the "spurious structures" issue present in the mention hypergraph model, is a significant strength. This provides a clear and compelling justification for the new model beyond just empirical performance.
2.  **Solid Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are thorough and convincing. The proposed model achieves consistent and noteworthy improvements over the previous state-of-the-art across several standard datasets. The inclusion of experiments on a non-overlapping NER dataset (CoNLL 2003) further strengthens the claim that the model is a robust and general improvement.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is well-motivated, and the core concepts of the model are explained clearly, aided by helpful figures (e.g., Figure 2 and Figure 4) that effectively illustrate the proposed graph structure.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Motivation and Positioning of the Core Contribution:** The paper's key theoretical contribution—solving the "spurious structures" problem of the mention hypergraph (MH) model—is not introduced until late in the paper (Section 5.2). Section 3 provides a neutral description of the MH model without highlighting the specific limitations that the proposed model aims to solve. The paper's narrative would be much stronger if the motivation was established earlier, for instance by briefly mentioning the deficiency of the MH model at the end of Section 3. This would better frame the introduction of the multigraph model in Section 4 as a direct solution to a known problem.
2.  **Abstractness of the "Spurious Structures" Argument:** The discussion of spurious structures in Section 5.2, while being the central theoretical argument, is presented in a highly abstract manner. The authors defer the concrete example to the supplementary material. The main paper would benefit immensely from a small, self-contained example illustrating how two different sets of entities can map to the same subgraph in the MH model. Without this, it is difficult for the reader to fully appreciate the nature and severity of the problem that this work so effectively solves.
3.  **Limited Justification for Model Design Choices:** In Section 4, the "entity separators" are introduced as the core modeling mechanism. However, the rationale for why these 8 specific separators are the necessary and sufficient components is not fully elaborated. It is unclear why this specific edge-based formulation is superior to, for example, a state-based approach that might enrich the node definitions (T/I/O) to capture similar information. A brief discussion of alternative designs and why the proposed edge-based approach is preferable would strengthen the paper's claims.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The concept of "spurious structures" is central to your paper's theoretical contribution. Could you provide a minimal, concrete example in the main text (perhaps a simplified version of the one in the appendix) to illustrate this issue? For example, showing a two- or three-word phrase where the MH model ambiguously represents two distinct entity sets with the same subgraph, and how your multigraph representation resolves this.
2.  The paper argues that the proposed model is "nondeficient" while the MH model is "deficient." Does this theoretical advantage explain the consistent recall improvement of your model over the MH model, as speculated at the end of Section 7? Could you elaborate on the connection between avoiding spurious structures during training and the model's tendency to predict more entities at test time?
