### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach for named entity recognition that handles overlapping entities, including both nested and crossing mentions. The core of the method is a multigraph representation of a sentence, where nodes correspond to tokens and multiple types of edges capture different structural and semantic relationships. An inference algorithm is then applied to this graph to identify entity boundaries.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of identifying overlapping named entities. The focus on crossing entities, in particular, is valuable as this is a known limitation of standard sequence labeling models.
2.  **Novel Representation:** The proposed multigraph representation is an interesting and intuitive way to model the complex, non-sequential relationships required to detect such entities.
3.  **Efficiency:** The inclusion of an efficiency comparison with a previous system is a welcome addition and strengthens the practical case for the proposed method.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is, for the most part, well-written and easy to follow.

### **Weaknesses**

My main concerns with the paper relate to its motivation, methodological clarity, and the depth of the empirical evaluation.

1.  **Underdeveloped Motivation:** The motivation for detecting all types of overlapping entities, particularly nested ones, is not well-established. While the importance of handling crossing entities is clear, the paper does not provide a compelling use case for identifying a shorter entity (e.g., "China") that is a sub-span of a longer, recognized entity (e.g., "Bank of China"). A stronger motivation explaining the downstream benefits would help the reader understand the significance of this aspect of the problem.

2.  **Missing Methodological Details:** Key details of the proposed method are missing, which hinders reproducibility and full comprehension. Specifically, the paper mentions several options for representing tokens (the I^k_t nodes, lines 229-233) but does not state which representation is used or how edges are deterministically added to the graph. The decision criterion for including an edge is fundamental to the approach and needs to be explicitly defined.

3.  **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation, while showing positive results, could be significantly strengthened in several ways:
    *   **Marginal Improvements and Overstated Claims:** The empirical gains over prior work, while consistent, are quite small (e.g., <2 F1 points on several datasets). The paper's claims of having "outperformed" previous methods feel overstated given the small effect size. A more nuanced discussion of the results, perhaps including significance tests or an analysis of the practical impact of these gains, would be more convincing.
    *   **Lack of Informative Baselines:** The evaluation would benefit from including additional baselines. For instance, a simple but competitive dictionary-based method could help contextualize the complexity and performance of the proposed model. Furthermore, comparing against a state-of-the-art commercial system (e.g., Google Cloud NLP, spaCy) would provide a valuable benchmark against widely-used, practical tools.
    *   **Insufficient Analysis of Overlapping Entities:** The evaluation misses a key opportunity to analyze the model's performance on the most interesting subset of the problem: crossing entities. The paper should provide a breakdown of performance on nested vs. crossing entities separately. This would clarify whether the observed improvements are driven by better handling of crossing mentions (the more challenging case) or simply by better detection of nested ones.
    *   **Lack of Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis comparing the types of errors made by the proposed model versus previous systems would be highly insightful. Are certain types of entities now correctly identified that were missed before? What are the remaining failure cases?

### **Overall Recommendation**

I appreciate the paper's focus on the challenging problem of crossing entities and find the multigraph approach promising. However, the work in its current form is weakened by a lack of clear motivation for nested entities, missing technical details in the methodology, and an evaluation that is not deep enough to be fully convincing. In particular, the failure to analyze performance specifically on crossing entities leaves the reader wondering if the core challenge has been effectively addressed.

Because of these concerns, my recommendation leans toward rejection at this time. I believe the paper could be significantly improved by strengthening the motivation, providing the missing methodological details, and conducting a more thorough evaluation with deeper analysis and more comprehensive baselines.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

**Clarity and Presentation:**
*   A brief definition of a "multigraph" upon its first mention would be helpful for readers not familiar with the term.
*   The term "spurious structures" in the introduction is vague; please clarify what is meant.
*   Please expand "NP chunking" to "noun phrase chunking" on first use.
*   In the discussion of state types, it is unclear what a "state" corresponds to in the hypergraph. It seems to be used interchangeably with "node" later on; please be consistent.
*   The term "child nodes" is not standard terminology for hypergraphs. Please clarify this concept or define it for the reader.
*   Figure 2 could be improved for clarity. In black-and-white printing, color distinctions are lost. The use of gray for certain nodes and edges is not explained, nor is the logic for why the highlighted edges were selected. It would also be helpful to explicitly state the value of detecting both entities in the example.
*   Please be consistent with the use of parentheses around phrases starting with "denoting...".
*   Footnotes should be placed after punctuation marks, not before them.

**Wording and Phrasing:**
*   "previously noted by ... many previous": This phrasing is awkward. Consider rephrasing.
*   "Solving this task": It's unclear which specific task is being referred to.
*   "time linear in n": When n is the sentence length, the practical difference between linear and cubic complexity is significant. This point seems understated.
*   "Since they set": It is unclear who "they" refers to.
*   "to find out": Consider a more formal alternative like "to determine" or "to investigate."
*   "we make two": Awkward phrasing. Consider "we conduct two experiments" or "we perform two analyses."
*   "highlighted in bold": Instead of just noting statistical significance, please discuss the effect size. A 0.4 point improvement, even if statistically significant, may not be practically meaningful. The term "outperforms" should be reserved for more substantial gains.
*   "is more confident": How does confidence relate to an increase in recall? This claim needs further explanation.

**Technical Questions:**
*   Footnote 2: How was it determined that the missing edge "should be" missing? What is the oracle or rule here?
*   "on whether the separator defines...": What is the algorithm or heuristic for this determination?
*   Last paragraph before 4.1: How is the CS-edge chosen algorithmically?
*   Footnote 3: The claim that not favoring crossing entities is "good" needs justification. Why would this be a desirable property? An example would be helpful.
*   Please explicitly state the "simple first-order assumption" for the reader's benefit.

**Formatting:**
*   Please spell out small numbers (e.g., "five" instead of "5") in prose.
*   The capitalization in the titles of references is inconsistent. Please format them uniformly.
