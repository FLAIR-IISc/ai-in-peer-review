**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of recognizing overlapping named entities, a known challenge for standard sequence labeling models. The authors propose a novel structured prediction model based on a multigraph representation. In this model, each token is associated with states indicating whether it is inside or outside an entity of a certain type. The key innovation lies in the edges, which explicitly represent one of eight "entity separators" that describe the state transitions between tokens (e.g., an entity ending while another continues). This formulation allows multiple, semantically distinct edges to connect the same pair of nodes, hence the term "multigraph." The authors provide a theoretical analysis contrasting their model with the prior state-of-the-art mention hypergraph (MH) model, arguing that their approach is "nondeficient" as it avoids the "spurious structures" problem present in the MH model's normalization term. The proposed model is evaluated on several standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) and is shown to outperform the MH baseline and other previous methods.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The primary contribution is the demonstration of superior performance on multiple benchmark datasets for overlapping entity recognition. The proposed model consistently outperforms the strong mention hypergraph baseline, achieving significant F1-score improvements. The thorough experimental analysis, including a breakdown of performance on sentences with and without overlapping entities, provides solid evidence for the model's effectiveness.
2.  **A Nondeficient Model for Overlapping Structures:** The paper provides a valuable theoretical analysis identifying the "spurious structures" issue in the mention hypergraph model, where the objective function's normalization term incorrectly sums over structurally ambiguous representations. The authors show that their proposed multigraph formulation is "nondeficient," meaning each structure has a unique interpretation. This provides a compelling theoretical justification for the new model and may explain its improved empirical results and faster convergence.
3.  **A Novel Multigraph Formulation:** The paper introduces a new multigraph-based formulation for structured prediction. While it shares conceptual similarities with the mention hypergraph model, its explicit modeling of entity boundary semantics through "entity separators" as distinct edges offers a novel and intuitive way to handle complex, overlapping structures.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation that consistently demonstrates the superiority of the proposed model over previous state-of-the-art methods across several datasets. The improvements in F1-score are significant (e.g., a 2.1 point gain on ACE2004), and the results are well-supported by detailed tables and analysis. The inclusion of experiments on the CoNLL2003 dataset further strengthens the claim that the model is a robust entity recognizer in general, not just for overlapping cases.
2.  **Insightful Theoretical Analysis:** The discussion of "spurious structures" and "deficient" models in Section 5.2 is a key strength. This analysis provides a clear and principled reason why the proposed model might be superior to the mention hypergraph baseline. By identifying a potential flaw in the previous model's objective function, the authors offer a deeper understanding of the problem and motivate their solution beyond simple architectural novelty. The empirical finding that the model converges in fewer iterations further corroborates this theoretical advantage.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The authors effectively use figures (e.g., Figure 2) to illustrate the differences between their model and the mention hypergraph. The introduction of the "state-based vs. edge-based" paradigm, while not central to the model's superiority, helps to contextualize the work within the broader field of structured prediction and highlights a relatively unexplored modeling approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Delayed Motivation for the Model's Design:** The motivation for the proposed multigraph model is not clearly established upfront. The paper describes the mention hypergraph (MH) model in Section 3 but only presents the critique of its "spurious structures" in Section 5.2. Consequently, when the multigraph model and its core "entity separators" are introduced in Section 4, the reader lacks the necessary context to understand *why* this specific formulation is necessary or advantageous. The paper would be much stronger if the limitations of the MH model were discussed first, directly motivating the design choices of the new model.
2.  **Abstract Presentation of the "Spurious Structures" Argument:** The central theoretical argument regarding "spurious structures" in Section 5.2 is presented in a highly abstract manner, making it difficult to fully grasp without referring to the appendix. Deferring the concrete example to the appendix weakens this crucial part of the paper. Including a simplified, illustrative example directly within Section 5.2 would significantly improve the clarity and impact of this argument, making the theoretical contribution more accessible and convincing.
3.  **Unclear Contribution of the State- vs. Edge-based Paradigm Taxonomy:** The discussion in Section 5.1 categorizes models into "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms. While an interesting classification, its relevance to the paper's core thesis is limited. Since both the proposed model and the primary baseline (MH) fall into the "edge-based" category, this distinction does not serve to differentiate them or argue for the superiority of the proposed method. The section feels somewhat disconnected and does not sufficiently strengthen the paper's main claims.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the design rationale for the eight "entity separators"? Section 4 introduces them as the model's core component, but it is not immediately clear why this specific decomposition of start, continue, and end signals is the optimal or most natural choice. How is this design choice directly linked to resolving the "spurious structures" problem found in the mention hypergraph model?
2.  Your theoretical analysis of "spurious structures" is a key contribution. To improve clarity, would you consider moving a simplified version of the example from Appendix A into the main body of the paper (Section 5.2)? This would make your central argument much easier for readers to follow.
3.  In Section 5.1, you claim that a state-based version of your model would be "less intuitive" and model "unnecessary dependencies." Could you provide a concrete example of what these unnecessary dependencies would be? Clarifying this point would strengthen your argument for the advantages of the edge-based paradigm in this context.
