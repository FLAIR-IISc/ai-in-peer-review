

Strengths:  
The problem is potentially interesting, particularly for identifying crossing entities to determine which are mentioned in text. The technique appears functional, though empirical results lack significant impact. The discussion of efficiency relative to prior systems is appreciated. The paper is well-written but requires minor refinements (detailed below).  

Weaknesses:  
The problem lacks strong motivation. Why detect "China" within "Bank of China"? While crossing entities have merit, the use case for nested entities remains unclear and needs stronger justification. Crucially, the edge inclusion criterion is undefined: lines 229–233 list options for $I^k_t$ nodes but never specify which edges should be present. Empirically, results marginally exceed prior work but are not substantially better. A 2-point F₁ improvement is not meaningfully "outperforming," and the effect size, practical significance, and number of newly discovered "important" entities are unaddressed. A dictionary-based baseline and comparison to commercial systems (e.g., Google NLP Cloud) would contextualize the gains. The evaluation neglects to emphasize crossing entities—the more compelling subset of overlapping entities—failing to report: (a) detection rates for crossing entities versus nested ones, (b) missed examples and reasons, and (c) whether performance gains stem from nested or crossing entity handling. A comparative error analysis would strengthen this.  

General Discussion:  
While crossing entities are a valid focus, the paper inadequately motivates nested entities. A clearer discussion of why nested entities matter, coupled with deeper analysis of crossing entity performance (e.g., "How many more crossing entities are detected?"), would have been more persuasive. The lukewarm assessment leans toward rejection, as the work feels incremental without sufficiently highlighting the critical question of crossing entities.
