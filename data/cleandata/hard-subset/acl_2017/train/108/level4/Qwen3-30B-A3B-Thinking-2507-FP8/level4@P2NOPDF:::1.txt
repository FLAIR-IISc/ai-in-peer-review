

Strengths: The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with only minor issues noted below. The problem of detecting overlapping entities in text is well-motivated and relevant. The proposed multigraph-based model extends the Multigraph-based model (MH) framework, and the empirical results demonstrate robust improvements over prior work.

Weaknesses:  
1. Section 3 lacks a clear articulation of MH’s limitations that necessitate the new model. The authors should explicitly identify specific shortcomings of MH (e.g., handling overlapping entities or structural ambiguities) and explain how the proposed approach addresses them.  
2. Section 4 fails to justify the introduction of separators. It is unclear what additional information separators provide beyond the existing T/I/O (token/instance/offset) representation. Without this rationale, the design choice appears arbitrary.  
3. Section 5.1 does not establish why the new model outperforms MH. The reported metrics alone do not demonstrate superiority; the authors should link results to specific model capabilities (e.g., how the multigraph structure resolves ambiguities).  
4. Section 5.2’s discussion is overly abstract and fails to convey concrete insights. The authors should provide specific examples of spurious structures (e.g., ambiguous entity boundaries or overlapping spans) that MH struggles with but the new model resolves.  

General Discussion: The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art results on benchmark datasets, but the contribution lacks mechanistic clarity. While the model improves upon MH empirically, the paper does not explain *how* or *why* the multigraph approach achieves this—whether through better structural modeling, reduced spurious edges, or enhanced representation of entity overlaps. Without such insights, the novelty and practical significance of the approach remain unclear. The authors should strengthen the discussion by connecting model design choices to tangible performance gains, ideally with illustrative examples.
