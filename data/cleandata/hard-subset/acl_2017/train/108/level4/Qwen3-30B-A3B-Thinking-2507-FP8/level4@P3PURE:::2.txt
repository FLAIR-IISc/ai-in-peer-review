

Strengths:  
The problem of detecting potentially overlapping entities, particularly crossing entities, is inherently interesting for determining which entity is actually referenced in text. The proposed multigraph-based technique demonstrates empirical efficacy, though the results do not show dramatic improvements. The paper appropriately addresses efficiency concerns relative to prior systems, and the overall writing is clear, though some minor refinements are needed (detailed below).

Weaknesses:  
The problem lacks sufficient motivation. Why is detecting "China" as a nested entity within "Bank of China" practically meaningful? While crossing entities present a compelling use case, the utility of nested entities remains unclear—this should be explicitly justified to engage readers. The approach also omits critical implementation details: the decision criterion for including edges is unspecified, and Section 3.1 (lines 229–233) lists multiple options for $I^k_t$ nodes without clarifying which edges should be present.  

The empirical evaluation shows marginal improvements over prior methods (e.g., +0.4 F₁ on GENIA), which should not be described as "outperforming." The effect size is negligible—does a 2% F₁ gain matter to end-users? How many *new* important entities are discovered compared to prior work? Crucially, the paper omits baselines against simplistic dictionary-based approaches or commercial systems (e.g., Google Cloud NLP), which would contextualize the results meaningfully.  

The discussion neglects to emphasize performance on crossing entities—the more interesting subset of overlapping entities—failing to answer: how many more crossing entities are detected? Which were missed, and why? Is the improvement driven by better nested detection, crossing detection, or both? A comparative error analysis between the proposed system and prior work would strengthen this section.  

General Discussion:  
While crossing entities are a valid focus for NER, the paper fails to justify nested entity detection. The evaluation provides no insight into this distinction, and the discussion would benefit from concrete examples, error analysis, and explicit emphasis on crossing entity performance. The paper reads as a marginal incremental contribution without sufficient focus on the core problem of crossing entities.
