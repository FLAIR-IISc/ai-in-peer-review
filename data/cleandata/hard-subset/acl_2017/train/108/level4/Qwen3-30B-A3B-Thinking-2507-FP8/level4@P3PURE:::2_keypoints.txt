- Strength: The task of detecting overlapping, particularly crossing, entities is inherently interesting and relevant for disambiguating referenced entities in text.  
- Strength: The proposed multigraph-based method shows empirical effectiveness, though improvements are modest.  
- Strength: The paper addresses efficiency concerns relative to prior systems.  
- Strength: Writing is generally clear, with only minor refinements needed.

- Weakness: Insufficient motivation for nested-entity detection (e.g., why detecting “China” inside “Bank of China” is practically meaningful).  
- Weakness: Critical implementation details are missing—criteria for including edges and which I^k_t node options/edges are actually used are unspecified.  
- Weakness: Empirical gains are marginal (e.g., +0.4 F1 on GENIA); claiming to “outperform” is overstated without effect-size justification or user-impact analysis.  
- Weakness: No analysis of how many new or important entities are discovered compared to prior work.  
- Weakness: Missing baselines against simple dictionary-based or commercial systems (e.g., Google Cloud NLP) to contextualize results.  
- Weakness: Evaluation and discussion neglect the most interesting subset—crossing entities—failing to report how many more crossing entities are detected, which were missed, and whether gains come from nested or crossing detection.  
- Weakness: Lack of comparative error analysis between the proposed system and prior work to explain improvements or failures.

- General: The paper fails to justify nested-entity detection and does not distinguish nested vs. crossing entities in evaluation; needs concrete examples, focused error analysis, and emphasis on crossing-entity performance to avoid seeming like a marginal incremental contribution.

- Minor: Define “multigraph” at first mention and standardize/clarify terminology (e.g., “state” vs. node, “child nodes,” NP chunking).  
- Minor: Specify algorithmic details and notation choices clearly (e.g., what n denotes for time complexity, CS-edge selection, decision criteria for separators).  
- Minor: Clarify and improve Figure 2 and hypergraph visuals (B&W readability, gray nodes/edges explanation, why both entities are highlighted).  
- Minor: Add quantitative focus and clear phrasing in results (report effect sizes, avoid overstatements like “outperforms,” use “0.4-point F1 improvement,” and specify recall rates).  
- Minor: Include baseline comparisons, dataset statistics, and concrete examples of nested vs. crossing cases (Footnote 3 justification).  
- Minor: Numerous stylistic and editorial fixes requested (phrasing choices, punctuation, citation formatting, consistent use of schemes like BILOU, and reference capitalization).
