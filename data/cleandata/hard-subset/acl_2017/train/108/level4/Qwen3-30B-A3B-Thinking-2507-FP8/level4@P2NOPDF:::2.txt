

The paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting overlapping entities (including nested and crossing cases). While the core problem is relevant for scenarios like identifying "China" within "Bank of China," the paper’s motivation for nested entities remains underdeveloped. The crossing entity case is compelling, but the utility of nested detection is unclear without concrete use cases. The method shows marginal improvements over prior work (e.g., +0.4 F₁ on GENIA), yet the claim of "outperformance" is overstated for such small gains. Crucially, the evaluation lacks context: How do these results compare to simple dictionary-based methods or commercial systems (e.g., Google Cloud NLP)? Without this, the significance of the improvements is ambiguous.

Strengths include a well-structured presentation and a welcome efficiency discussion. The approach appears functional, though key technical details are missing. For instance, the criteria for adding edges to the multigraph (lines 229–233) are never clarified—how are edges selected? Similarly, the decision process for including specific I^k_t nodes is opaque.

Weaknesses require significant attention:
1. **Problem Motivation**: The paper fails to justify why nested entities matter. Why prioritize detecting "China" as a separate entity within "Bank of China" over crossing entities? This undermines the paper’s core premise.
2. **Methodological Gaps**: Critical implementation details are absent. How are edges determined? What defines a valid multigraph structure? The lack of clarity on these points weakens the technical contribution.
3. **Evaluation Deficiencies**: 
   - Marginal F₁ gains (e.g., +0.4 on GENIA) lack effect size analysis. Is a 0.4-point improvement meaningful for users?
   - No comparison to dictionary-based baselines or commercial NLP tools (e.g., Google Cloud) dilutes the results’ significance.
   - The focus on nested entities neglects the more valuable crossing entity case. Performance on crossing entities—where the method’s novelty should shine—is underreported. How many more crossing entities are detected? Which were missed, and why?
   - Error analysis comparing this method to prior work is absent, missing an opportunity to highlight tangible improvements.

The paper’s conclusion leans heavily on nested entities without addressing why they are prioritized over crossing ones. A stronger discussion of crossing entity performance (e.g., "Our method detects X additional crossing entities compared to [prior work]") would better justify the contribution. As it stands, the work feels like a minor incremental step rather than a compelling advancement.

Minor issues require polishing:
- Clarify "multigraph" on first mention (e.g., "a graph allowing multiple edges between nodes").
- Fix typos ("pervious" → "previous," "BILOU" → "BILOU scheme").
- Standardize terminology (e.g., "state" vs. "node" in hypergraphs; "chunking" → "noun phrase chunking").
- Address inconsistent punctuation (e.g., commas after enumerated items, footnote placement).
- Clarify ambiguous claims (e.g., "spurious structures" in intro; "CS-edge" selection algorithm in §4.1; why both entities in Fig. 2 must be detected).
- Remove redundant phrasing ("we make two," "to find out," "allows us to use to make use").
- Correct citation style ("Following Lu and Roth (2015)" not "Following (Lu and Roth, 2015)").
- Ensure consistent capitalization in references.

Overall, the paper tackles a meaningful problem but lacks clear motivation, technical transparency, and rigorous evaluation. The marginal gains and unaddressed commercial baselines make the contribution feel incremental. While the writing is generally sound, the core argument needs substantial strengthening to justify publication at ACL. The focus should shift from nested entities to the more impactful crossing entity case, with concrete evidence of real-world utility. Without this, the paper’s contribution appears limited. I recommend rejection.
