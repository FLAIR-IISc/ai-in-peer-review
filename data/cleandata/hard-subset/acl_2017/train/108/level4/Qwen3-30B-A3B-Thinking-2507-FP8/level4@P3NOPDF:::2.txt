

The paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting overlapping entities, addressing both nested and crossing cases. While the problem is relevant for complex entity recognition scenarios, the work’s contributions and significance require stronger justification and evaluation.

**Strengths:**  
The focus on overlapping entities is timely, and the multigraph formalism offers a novel structural representation. The efficiency discussion (linear in sentence length) is a valuable addition compared to prior work. The writing is generally clear, though minor grammatical and phrasing issues need correction (see detailed remarks).

**Major Weaknesses:**  
1. **Insufficient Motivation:** The core problem lacks convincing justification. Why prioritize detecting "China" within "Bank of China" (as in the introduction)? The paper conflates nested entities (e.g., "Bank of China" containing "China") with crossing entities (e.g., "Bank of China" and "China" co-occurring as distinct mentions). Crossing entities are genuinely challenging and useful (e.g., in coreference resolution), but nested entities have unclear practical value. The evaluation does not distinguish between these cases, obscuring the method’s real impact.  
2. **Ambiguous Methodology:** Key design choices are undefined. The paper lists multiple options for "I^k_t" nodes (lines 229–233) but never specifies *which* edges are included in the multigraph or the criterion for edge inclusion. This undermines reproducibility and the claimed technical contribution.  
3. **Underwhelming Evaluation:**  
   - Results show marginal gains (e.g., +0.4 F1 on GENIA, +0.2 on CoNLL). The claim of "outperforming" prior work is overstated; such small improvements lack practical significance (e.g., "two percentage points" is unlikely to impact real-world applications).  
   - No comparison to simple baselines (e.g., dictionary-based methods for overlapping entities) or commercial systems (e.g., Google Cloud NLP) contextualizes the results. How would a state-of-the-art commercial tool perform?  
   - The evaluation focuses on overall F1 without analyzing *crossing entities specifically*—the paper’s purported focus. It fails to answer: *How many more crossing entities are detected? Which were missed, and why?* Error analysis comparing the proposed method to prior work (e.g., Lu & Roth, 2015) is absent.  
   - The paper claims "improved detection of nested entities" but does not isolate this from crossing-entity gains. Performance improvements could stem solely from better nested recognition, not the core crossing-entity problem.  

**Recommendation:**  
The work is a marginal incremental advance. The lack of clear motivation for nested entities, ambiguous methodology, and negligible empirical gains prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. While the approach is technically sound, its significance is overstated. The paper would benefit from:  
- A focused evaluation on *crossing entities* (the more meaningful challenge),  
- Concrete comparisons to baselines/commercial tools,  
- Clearer justification for nested entity relevance (or removal of this focus),  
- Explicit edge-inclusion criteria and error analysis.  

**Minor Remarks (for author’s attention):**  
- Define "multigraph" on first use (e.g., "a graph allowing multiple edges between nodes").  
- Correct typos: "pervious" → "previous," "BILOU" → "BILOU scheme," "used in this experiments" → "used in these experiments."  
- Clarify ambiguous terms: "state" (in hypergraphs), "child nodes," "CS-edge" (Section 3.2).  
- Figure 2: Colors are unusable in grayscale; explain gray vs. highlighted edges algorithmically. Why highlight *those specific edges*?  
- Avoid phrases like "we make two" → "we conduct two"; "to find out" → "to determine."  
- Footnotes: Place after punctuation; clarify "CS-edge" selection (Section 3.2).  
- Table 5: Avoid "outperform" for small F1 differences; specify effect sizes.  
- References: Standardize title capitalization.  
- "Nested vs. crossing" footnote: Explain *why* this distinction matters (e.g., "crossing entities are more common in news text").  

**Overall:** The paper addresses an interesting problem but fails to convincingly argue its importance or demonstrate meaningful progress. The evaluation lacks depth and context, and the methodology is under-specified. Without significant revisions to clarify the problem’s relevance, justify nested entity focus, and strengthen empirical claims, the contribution remains limited. I recommend *rejection* due to insufficient novelty and impact.
