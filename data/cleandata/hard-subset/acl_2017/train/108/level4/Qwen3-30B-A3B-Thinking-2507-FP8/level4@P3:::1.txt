

**Summary:** This paper proposes a multigraph-based model for overlapping entity recognition, claiming it is the first structured prediction model to use multigraphs for this task. The main contributions are: (1) a novel multigraph-based model for predicting overlapping entities; (2) empirical demonstration of superior performance over previous models (including the mention hypergraph model) on standard datasets; (3) theoretical demonstration that the model avoids spurious structures present in the previous state-of-the-art model while maintaining the same inference time complexity.

**Strengths:**  
1. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed. The authors conduct experiments on three standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) and show consistent improvements over the mention hypergraph (MH) baseline, with the most significant improvement (2.1 F1 points) on ACE2004. The additional analysis of performance on sentences with and without overlapping entities (Table 4) provides valuable insights into the model's behavior.

2. The theoretical analysis of spurious structures is a strong contribution. The authors clearly demonstrate that their model avoids the spurious structures issue present in the MH model, which is a non-trivial theoretical insight. This explanation helps clarify why their model might outperform MH in practice, going beyond the empirical results.

3. The paper is generally well-written with clear descriptions of the model mechanics. The examples and figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively illustrate the model's representation of overlapping entities, making the approach more accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. The motivation for the new model is insufficiently articulated. The paper describes the MH model but fails to explicitly state its limitations that the authors aimed to address. The spurious structures issue is mentioned as a theoretical advantage, but the paper doesn't clearly explain why this is problematic for entity recognition performance, which would better motivate the proposed solution.

2. The introduction of entity separators (Section 4) lacks justification. The paper describes the 8 possible separators but doesn't explain why they're necessary or what additional information they convey beyond the T/I/O states. This omission makes it difficult to understand the novelty of the approach and why the multigraph representation is superior to alternatives.

3. The theoretical analysis (Section 5.2) is too abstract without concrete examples. The authors should provide specific examples of spurious structures in MH and how their model avoids them. For instance, they could show a simple 3-word example where MH would predict a spurious structure that their model correctly resolves, demonstrating the practical significance of their theoretical contribution.

4. The connection between the theoretical advantages and empirical results is weak. While the paper shows that their model outperforms MH, it doesn't clearly link this improvement to the avoidance of spurious structures or other specific aspects of the model. The paper claims their model is "nondeficient" but doesn't demonstrate how this translates to better performance in practice.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. Could you provide a concrete example of a spurious structure in the MH model that your model avoids, and how this specific structure would affect entity recognition performance?  

2. Why are the entity separators necessary? What specific information do they convey that couldn't be captured by the T/I/O states alone, and how would alternative representations (e.g., modeling each separator as a separate state) lead to modeling issues as mentioned in Section 5.1?  

3. In Table 4, your model performs slightly worse than MH on the overlapping entities portion of the GENIA dataset. Given that GENIA has a lower percentage of overlapping entities (24% vs. ACE's 39-68%), could you elaborate on why this might occur and how you would address this in future work?  

**Additional Comments:**  
The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of the problem with MH that motivates the new approach. The current draft describes MH but doesn't explicitly state why it's inadequate for overlapping entity recognition. A more direct comparison of MH's limitations versus the proposed model's advantages would strengthen the paper significantly. The discussion of the edge-based versus state-based paradigm (Section 5.1) is interesting but doesn't directly address why the new model is superior, which is a critical gap.
