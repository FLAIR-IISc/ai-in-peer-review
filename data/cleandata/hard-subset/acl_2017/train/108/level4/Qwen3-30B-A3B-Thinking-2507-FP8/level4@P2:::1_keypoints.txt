- Summary: Proposes a multigraph-based structured prediction model for overlapping entity recognition, claiming novelty and contributing a model, improved F1 results, and theoretical analysis avoiding spurious structures in the mention hypergraph.

- Strength: Empirical evaluation is comprehensive on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA, showing consistent improvements (e.g., +2.1 F1 on ACE2004 vs mention hypergraph).

- Strength: Theoretical analysis identifies spurious structures in prior mention hypergraph models and highlights problematic normalization terms.

- Strength: Addresses a meaningful, practical problem since overlapping entities are common in real datasets.

- Weakness: Fails to clearly articulate the specific limitations of the mention hypergraph that motivated the new approach; Section 3 needs explicit statements tying limitations to the solution.

- Weakness: Introduction of entity separators is under-explained—unclear what extra information they provide beyond T/I/O states and how they help.

- Weakness: The state-based vs edge-based paradigm discussion (Section 5.1) does not convincingly connect the claimed paradigm to tangible performance or advantages.

- Weakness: Discussion of spurious structures (Section 5.2) is too abstract and lacks concrete examples in the main text; relevant examples are buried in the supplement.

- Question: Provide specific examples of spurious structures in the mention hypergraph, show how they appear in the normalization term, and demonstrate their practical effect on predictions.

- Question: Clarify why entity separators are necessary beyond T/I/O states and what exact information they encode.

- Question: Explain how the theoretical property of being "non-deficient" vs "deficient" leads to practical performance improvements beyond reported F1 gains.

- Question: Justify whether the modest improvements (typically 0.4–2.1 F1) warrant the additional complexity of the multigraph model versus being a variant of the mention hypergraph.

- Additional comment: Paper needs a clearer connection between theoretical contributions and empirical gains, and should fix grammatical issues and minor inconsistencies before publication.
