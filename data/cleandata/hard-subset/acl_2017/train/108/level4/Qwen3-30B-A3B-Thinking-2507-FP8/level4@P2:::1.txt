

Summary: The paper introduces a multigraph-based model for overlapping entity recognition, claiming it is the first structured prediction model using multigraphs for this task. The authors present three main contributions: (1) a novel multigraph-based model for overlapping entity recognition, (2) empirical results showing improved F1 scores over previous models on standard datasets, and (3) theoretical analysis demonstrating that their model avoids "spurious structures" present in a previous state-of-the-art model (mention hypergraph).

Strengths:
1. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed, with experiments on three standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) showing consistent improvements over baselines. The results demonstrate a significant 2.1-point F1 improvement on ACE2004 over the mention hypergraph model, which is a strong empirical contribution.

2. The theoretical analysis of "spurious structures" in previous models is interesting and potentially valuable for the field. The authors correctly identify that the normalization term in the mention hypergraph model contains structures that would never be predicted, which is a meaningful theoretical contribution.

3. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with clear practical relevance, as overlapping entities are common in real-world datasets (ACE, GENIA, clinical texts) and previous approaches have struggled with this challenge.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper fails to clearly articulate the specific limitations of the mention hypergraph model that motivated the proposed approach. Section 3 should explicitly state these limitations to justify the new model. The authors mention that their model "does not present the spurious structures issue" but don't clearly explain why this issue matters for real-world performance.

2. The introduction of "entity separators" in Section 4 is not well-explained. It's unclear what additional information they convey beyond the T/I/O states, and how this contributes to the model's superiority. The paper should provide concrete examples showing how the separators improve performance.

3. Section 5.1's discussion of "state-based vs edge-based paradigm" doesn't effectively demonstrate why the new model is superior. The authors claim their model falls into the "edge-based paradigm," but don't connect this to tangible improvements in performance or explain why this paradigm is better for overlapping entity recognition.

4. Section 5.2's discussion of "spurious structures" is too abstract without concrete examples. The authors should provide specific examples of spurious structures and explain how their model avoids them, making it easier to understand the theoretical advantage. The supplementary material mentions these structures but doesn't incorporate them into the main text where they would be most helpful.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide specific examples of spurious structures in the mention hypergraph model, and how they manifest in the normalization term? How do these structures affect the model's predictions in practice?

2. In Section 4, could you clarify why the entity separators are necessary beyond the T/I/O states? What specific information do they encode that the T/I/O states alone cannot capture?

3. The paper claims the multigraph model is "non-deficient" while the mention hypergraph is "deficient." Could you explain how this theoretical property translates to practical performance improvements, beyond the reported F1 scores?

4. The paper mentions that the multigraph model "outperforms previous models," but the improvements are relatively modest (typically 0.4-2.1 F1 points). Could you discuss whether the additional complexity of the multigraph model is justified by these performance gains, especially considering the model is only a variant of the mention hypergraph?

Additional Comments: The paper would benefit from a clearer connection between the theoretical contributions and empirical results. While the model achieves better F1 scores, the review is unclear on exactly why the new model works better in practice, which is a significant gap in the paper. The writing is generally clear but contains some grammatical issues and minor inconsistencies that should be addressed before publication.
