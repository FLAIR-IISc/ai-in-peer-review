

The paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping entities, where multiple edges may connect nodes.  

**Strengths:**  
The problem of detecting overlapping entities is inherently interesting, particularly for resolving crossing entities (e.g., "China" within "Bank of China"). While the empirical results show modest gains rather than dramatic improvements, the method demonstrates reasonable effectiveness. The paper devotes attention to efficiency—a welcome contrast to prior work—and overall writing is clear, though minor refinements are needed (see below).  

**Weaknesses:**  
**Motivation:** The paper fails to convincingly justify nested entity detection. Why is identifying "China" as an entity within "Bank of China" practically useful? While crossing entities have clear utility (e.g., resolving ambiguous references), the rationale for nested entities remains unclear. The introduction’s example does not address this gap.  

**Methodology:** Critical details are missing. The decision criterion for including edges is unspecified (lines 229–233 list options for *I^k_t* nodes but never clarify which edges should be present). Without this, the approach lacks reproducibility.  

**Evaluation:**  
- Results marginally outperform prior work (e.g., +0.4 F1 on GENIA), but the paper’s claim of "outperformance" is overstated. A 0.4-point F1 improvement is negligible in practice—does it matter to users?  
- Effect size and real-world impact are unaddressed. How many *newly discovered* "important" entities does the method yield compared to prior systems?  
- Crucially, the paper omits comparisons with:  
  (a) Simple dictionary-based baselines for overlapping entities, and  
  (b) Commercial systems (e.g., Google Cloud NLP), which would contextualize results against existing tools.  
- The evaluation neglects crossing entities—the more compelling subset of overlapping entities. Performance on crossing entities (vs. nested ones) is unreported. How many crossing entities are now detected? Which were missed, and why? Is the improvement due to better nested detection, crossing detection, or both? A comparative error analysis would strengthen this.  

**General Discussion:**  
While crossing entities are a valid focus, nested entity detection lacks justification. The paper’s evaluation fails to highlight gains on crossing entities or explain why nested cases matter. A discussion of concrete error cases and a sharper emphasis on crossing-entity performance would have better convinced me of the work’s significance.  

**Verdict:** The paper presents a technically sound but under-motivated approach. It addresses an interesting problem but fails to clarify the utility of nested entities or demonstrate meaningful gains. I remain cautiously skeptical and lean toward rejection.
