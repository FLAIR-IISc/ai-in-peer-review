

Summary: This paper proposes a multigraph-based model for overlapping entity recognition, claiming it is the first structured prediction model using multigraphs for this task. The main contributions are: (1) A theoretical analysis demonstrating that the proposed model resolves the spurious structures issue present in the previous state-of-the-art mention hypergraph (MH) model while maintaining the same inference time complexity (O(nT)), (2) Empirical results showing consistent improvements in F1-scores over the MH model across multiple standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA), and (3) A novel multigraph-based framework for structured prediction of overlapping entities.

Strengths: The theoretical contribution is strong, clearly demonstrating that the model avoids spurious structures present in the MH model, which is a significant limitation of the previous state-of-the-art. This resolves a fundamental issue in the previous approach while maintaining the same computational efficiency. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines on three standard datasets with appropriate splits and careful feature engineering. The results show consistent improvements (up to 2.1 F1 points) across all datasets, with particularly strong performance on ACE datasets where overlapping entities are more prevalent. The implementation details and experimental setup are well-described, making the results reproducible.

Weaknesses: The paper fails to clearly articulate the specific limitations of the MH model that motivated the proposed approach. The authors should explicitly state what issues with MH they are addressing before introducing their new model, rather than leaving this for the reader to infer. The introduction of "entity separators" lacks justification; it's unclear what additional information they convey beyond the existing T/I/O states, making the model's novelty difficult to assess. The discussion of the edge-based paradigm (section 5.1) does not provide concrete evidence of superiority over the state-based paradigm, making it difficult to understand the conceptual contribution. The explanation of the spurious structures issue (section 5.2) is too abstract without specific examples, making it challenging to understand why the proposed model resolves this problem.

Questions to Authors: Can you provide specific examples of spurious structures in the MH model that your approach resolves, to better illustrate the theoretical advantage? What is the exact role of entity separators beyond what can be achieved with the T/I/O states, and why is this representation necessary for resolving the spurious structures issue? Could you clarify how the edge-based paradigm leads to better performance compared to state-based approaches, with concrete evidence from the experimental results?
