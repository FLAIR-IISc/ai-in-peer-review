The paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping entities, which can be an interesting problem, particularly for crossing entities. The technique appears to work, although the empirical results do not demonstrate a dramatic effect. The paper is well-written, but some details require further polishing.

Strengths of the paper include the problem itself, which could be interesting, especially for crossing entities. The approach seems to work, and the authors have devoted some discussion to efficiency compared to a previous system. The paper is generally well-written, but it requires further polishing in some details.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The problem itself is not well-motivated. It is unclear why detecting China as an entity within the entity Bank of China is important, to use the example from the introduction. While there is a point to be made for crossing entities, the use case for nested entities is not clearly explained. This lack of motivation makes it difficult for the reader to become interested in the problem.

Regarding the approach, some important details are missing. The decision criterion for including an edge or not is not clearly explained. In lines 229-233, several options for the I^k_t nodes are mentioned, but it is never clarified which edges should be present.

The empirical evaluation of the approach is also lacking. While the results are better than some previous approaches, the margin of improvement is not substantial. The authors claim that their approach "outperforms" previous methods, but this is an exaggeration. The effect size of the improvement is not clearly explained, and it is unclear whether the improvement is significant enough to matter to users. Furthermore, the authors do not provide a comparison to a simplistic dictionary-based method or a commercial system like Google's NLP cloud, which would be useful for putting the results into context.

The discussion of the results also lacks emphasis on actual crossing entities. The performance of the approach on this subset of overlapping entities is not clearly explained, and it is unclear how many more crossing entities are detected than were possible before. The authors also do not provide a general error discussion comparing errors made by the suggested system and previous ones, which would strengthen the paper.

In general, while the problem of named entity recognition is interesting, the paper does not convincingly motivate the scenario of nested entities. The evaluation also sheds no light on this point, and the emphasis on crossing entities is lacking. Discussing errors and advantages with example cases and an emphasis on the results on crossing entities compared to other approaches would be more convincing.

Minor remarks include the need for a short description of the notion of a multigraph, clarification of some sentences and phrases, and correction of typos and formatting errors.

Recommendation:
The paper requires significant revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. The authors should provide a clearer motivation for the problem, including the use case for nested entities. They should also clarify the decision criterion for including edges in the multigraph and provide a more detailed comparison to previous approaches and commercial systems. The discussion of the results should emphasize actual crossing entities and provide a general error discussion. Finally, the authors should address the minor remarks mentioned above to improve the clarity and formatting of the paper.
