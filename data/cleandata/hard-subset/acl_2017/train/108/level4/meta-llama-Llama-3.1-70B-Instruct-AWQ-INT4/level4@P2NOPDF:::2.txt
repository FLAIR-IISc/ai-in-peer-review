This paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping entities, which is a topic of interest, especially when dealing with crossing entities. The technique appears to be effective, although the empirical results do not demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods. I appreciate the discussion on efficiency compared to a previous system, and the paper is generally well-written, but requires further polishing in some areas.

The paper's strengths include its well-structured presentation and the authors' effort to address efficiency concerns. However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. Firstly, the problem statement lacks clear motivation, particularly for detecting nested entities. The example in the introduction, where "China" is identified as an entity within "Bank of China," does not convincingly demonstrate the importance of this task. The authors should provide more context and emphasize the use cases for nested entities to make the reader more interested.

Regarding the approach, some crucial details are missing. For instance, the decision criterion for including an edge in the multigraph is not clearly explained. The authors mention several options for the I^k_t nodes in lines 229-233, but it is unclear which edges should be present. This omission makes it difficult to fully understand the proposed method.

The empirical evaluation shows that the achieved results are better than some previous approaches, but the improvement is not substantial. I would not describe the slight improvements as "outperforming" existing methods, as the effect size is not significant. The authors should discuss the actual impact of the improvement, such as the number of "important" entities discovered that were not detected by previous methods. Additionally, it would be beneficial to compare the performance of the proposed method to a simplistic dictionary-based approach and commercial systems like Google's NLP cloud.

The result discussion could be improved by emphasizing the performance on actual crossing entities, which is a more interesting subset of overlapping entities than nested ones. The authors should provide more insights into the number of crossing entities detected, the ones that were missed, and the reasons behind the performance improvement.

In general, I appreciate the problems related to named entity recognition and see the value in recognizing crossing entities. However, the paper does not convincingly motivate the scenario of nested entities, and the evaluation does not shed light on this aspect. Discussing errors, advantages, and example cases, with an emphasis on the results on crossing entities, would strengthen the paper.
