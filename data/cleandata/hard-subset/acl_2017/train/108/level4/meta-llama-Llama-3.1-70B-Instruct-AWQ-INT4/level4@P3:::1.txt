**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping entities in text. The model is capable of handling overlapping entities, which are common in many existing datasets. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model through extensive experiments on standard datasets, including ACE2004, ACE2005, and GENIA, and show that it outperforms previous models.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a novel multigraph-based model for predicting overlapping entities.
2. They empirically show that their model achieves higher F1-scores compared to previous models in multiple datasets.
3. Theoretically, they show that their model does not present the spurious structures issue in its inference procedure and is therefore non-deficient.

**Strengths**

1. The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
2. The problem the paper tackles is useful and relevant to the field of natural language processing.
3. The proposed approach, multigraph-based model, is a variant of the mention hypergraph (MH) model and offers a new perspective on handling overlapping entities.
4. The empirical results are solid and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model.

**Weaknesses**

1. Clarification is needed in several places, including:
	* Section 3: The authors should point out the issues of the MH model that motivate them to propose a new model.
	* Section 4: The authors should explain why separators are introduced and what additional information they convey beyond T/I/O.
	* Section 5.1: The discussion on state-based vs edge-based paradigm does not seem to provide useful information regarding why the new model is superior.
	* Section 5.2: The discussion on spurious structures is abstract and lacks concrete examples.
2. The authors should provide more insights on why and how the new model works better than the MH model.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the issues of the MH model that motivated you to propose a new model?
2. How do the separators introduced in Section 4 contribute to the effectiveness of the proposed model?
3. Can you provide concrete examples of spurious structures and explain how the proposed model avoids them?
4. How do you plan to extend the proposed model to other structured prediction problems involving complex structures?
