- Proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping entities, including crossing entities.  
- The problem (especially crossing entities) is interesting and the technique appears effective, though empirical gains are modest.  
- Authors compare efficiency to a previous system and make a commendable effort on runtime discussion.  
- Paper is generally well written but requires polishing and clearer exposition in places.  
- Motivation for nested-entity detection is weak; importance of cases like "China" within "Bank of China" is not well justified.  
- Decision criterion for including edges in the multigraph is unclear, and the description of I^k_t nodes/edges is ambiguous.  
- Empirical improvements are small (e.g., ~2 F1 points); effect size and user relevance are not established.  
- Recommend comparing to simple baselines (dictionary lookup) and commercial systems (e.g., Google NLP) to contextualize results.  
- Results discussion should emphasize crossing-entity performance: how many more detected, which were missed, and why.  
- Lack of error analysis comparing proposed and prior systems weakens the empirical claims.  
- Several algorithmic details are missing or unclear (CS-edge selection, mention hypergraph, separator determination).  
- Complexity claim ("time linear in n") needs clarification about what n denotes and practical significance.  
- Figure 2 and examples need clearer labeling and explanation; color usage may not print well.  
- Notation and phrasing are inconsistent (bracketed vs. unbracketed notation, awkward sentences, typos like "pervious").  
- Numerous minor editorial issues: odd phrasings, inconsistent italics, unclear referents ("they", "In the previous section"), and needed expansions (e.g., NP chunking, BILOU).  
- Footnotes need repositioning and clearer content (footnotes 2 and 3 require explanation).  
- Experimental setup descriptions (datasets, statistics, features used) are vague and should be specified more clearly.  
- Claims of "significantly better" should report effect sizes and statistical significance.  
- References and citation styles should be standardized for consistency.
