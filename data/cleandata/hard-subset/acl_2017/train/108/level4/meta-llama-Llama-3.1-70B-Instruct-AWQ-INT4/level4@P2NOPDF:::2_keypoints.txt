- Proposes a multigraph approach for overlapping (especially crossing) entity detection; effective but yields only marginal empirical gains over prior methods.
- Strength: well-structured presentation and explicit discussion of efficiency compared to a prior system.
- Overall writing is decent but requires further polishing in many places.
- Weak motivation for detecting nested entities; the intro example ("China" in "Bank of China") does not justify the task or its use cases.
- Missing methodological detail: unclear decision criterion for including edges in the multigraph and ambiguous specification of I^k_t node options (lines 229–233).
- Empirical improvements are small—should not be described as clearly "outperforming"—and the paper lacks discussion of practical effect size or impact.
- Recommend reporting how many important entities the method finds that previous approaches miss.
- Suggest additional baselines: simple dictionary-based methods and commercial NLP systems (e.g., Google Cloud NLP).
- Evaluation should emphasize crossing entities (a more interesting subset than nested ones): report counts, misses, and reasons for performance differences.
- Paper needs more error analysis, concrete examples, and discussion of advantages to strengthen claims.
- Figure 2 and its explanation are unclear; colors/gray nodes and example importance are not convincingly presented.
- Numerous clarity/notation issues throughout: inconsistent notation (brackets), unclear terms ("state", "spurious structures", "child nodes"), and vague references to sections/agents.
- Many minor writing/formatting problems: awkward phrases, punctuation/footnote placement, numeral/word usage, and typos (e.g., "pervious").
- Inconsistent citation and reference formatting (e.g., Lu and Roth citations), unclear dataset descriptions/statistics, and unclear experimental detail (feature use, BILOU scheme).
- Specific equation/algorithmic clarifications requested: comma after Eq.1, how CS-edge is chosen, how missing edges were determined (footnote 2), and how separators/states are defined algorithmically.
