The paper proposes an approach based on multigraphs for detecting potentially overlapping entities. 

Strengths:
The problem of detecting overlapping entities is interesting, particularly for crossing entities, where the system must decide which entity is actually mentioned in a given text. The proposed technique appears to be effective, although the empirical results do not show a dramatic effect. The authors have made a commendable effort to discuss efficiency compared to a previous system. The paper is generally well-written, but some details require further polishing (see minor remarks below).

Weaknesses:
The problem itself is not well-motivated. The authors could have provided a clearer explanation of why detecting entities like "China" within "Bank of China" is important. While the authors make a case for crossing entities, the use case for nested entities is less clear. This lack of motivation may make it difficult for readers to become invested in the problem. Furthermore, some important details are missing from the approach. For instance, the decision criterion for including an edge in the multigraph is not clearly explained. In lines 229-233, the authors mention several options for the I^k_t nodes, but it is unclear which edges should be present.

The empirical evaluation shows that the proposed approach outperforms some previous methods, but the margin of improvement is not substantial. The authors claim that their approach "outperforms" previous methods, but the effect size is not clear. It is also unclear whether the slight improvements in F_1 score (e.g., 2 percentage points) are significant or meaningful to users. To put the results into perspective, it would be helpful to compare the performance of the proposed approach to that of a simplistic dictionary-based method or a commercial system like Google's NLP cloud. This comparison would help to contextualize the results and demonstrate the actual effect of the proposed approach.

In the result discussion, the authors could have placed more emphasis on the performance of the system in detecting crossing entities, which is the more interesting subset of overlapping entities. It would be helpful to know how many more crossing entities are detected by the proposed approach compared to previous methods, which entities were missed, and why. Additionally, an error analysis comparing the errors made by the proposed system and previous systems would strengthen the discussion.

General Discussion:
While the problem of named entity recognition is interesting, the authors could have better motivated the scenario of recognizing nested entities. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of why nested entities are important, and the evaluation does not shed light on this point. Discussing errors and advantages with example cases and emphasizing the results on crossing entities compared to other approaches would have made a stronger case for the proposed approach. As it stands, the paper seems like just another attempt at solving the problem without adequately addressing the important question of crossing entities.
