The paper proposes a method based on multiple graphs (where several edges can connect two nodes) to identify potentially overlapping entities.

Strength:

The problem itself can be quite interesting, especially for crossed entities, which can be mentioned in a text. The technique seems to work, although empirical results do not show some "dramatic" effects. I like that some word skills have been spent compared to previous systems. In general, the paper is well-written, but some detailed information needs further improvement (see small comments below).

Weaknesses:

The problem itself is not very well explained. Why is it important to identify China as an entity within the Bank of China, consistent with the example role? There is a rationale for crossed entities, but what about the use of nested entities? This could be more inspiring for the reader. Speaking of the method, I think some important details are missing: what is the criterion for including an edge? Several different options for I^k_t nodes are mentioned in lines 229-233, but it is never clarified which edges should be present!

In terms of empirical evaluation, the results achieved are better than some previous methods, but not by a very large margin. I would not call minor improvements "outperforming," as the paper does. What is the size of the effect? Is a slight improvement of two percent points in F_1 really important for some users? What effect can a person actually see? How many "significant" entities have been discovered that were not discovered in the previous method? Furthermore, what performance will some common dictionary-based methods achieve that can also be used to find overlapping things? And in a similar vein: what can some commercial systems like Google's NLP cloud achieve in datasets that are capable of identifying and connecting entities? Only to compare the results with existing "commercial" systems.

While discussing the results, I wanted to emphasize crossed entities more. What is the performance like there? In my opinion, it is more attractive in overlapping entities than in nested entities. How many crossed entities have been identified beforehand? Which ones are missing and perhaps why? Has the improvement in performance only been due to better nested identification or also due to crossed entity identification? That part would be stronger if we discussed some common errors, comparing the proposed system and the errors made by previous systems.

General Discussion:

I like the problems related to named entity identification and there is a rationale for crossed entity identification. However, why are nested entities of interest? The current paper does not encourage the situation and does not shed any light on that matter in the evaluation. Discussing some examples and errors compared to other methods and emphasizing the results on crossed entities may make me more understandable.

So, I am only slightly interested in the paper, perhaps with a slight tendency to reject it. It seems just another attempt without emphasizing the important question of crossed entities.
