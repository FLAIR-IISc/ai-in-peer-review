- Proposes a multigraph-based method for identifying potentially overlapping entities allowing multiple edges between nodes.
- Addresses an intriguing problem of intersecting entities where entity reference is ambiguous.
- Technique appears effective and efficiency comparison to prior work is commendable.
- Empirical results show only marginal improvements and effect size/significance is unclear.
- Motivation for detecting nested entities (e.g., "China" in "Bank of China") is weak and underexplained.
- Key methodological details are missing: criteria for including/excluding edges and which I^k_t node options are chosen.
- Unclear how CS-edge and other graph construction choices are selected algorithmically.
- Request for more targeted evaluation: quantify how many critical or new entities are discovered compared to prior methods.
- Suggests comparison to simple baselines (dictionary) and commercial systems (e.g., Google NLP) for context.
- Results discussion should emphasize intersecting entities (crossing) rather than nested ones and quantify gains on that subset.
- Calls for an error analysis comparing errors made by the proposed system and previous approaches with examples.
- Reviewer’s overall enthusiasm is lukewarm with a slight tendency toward rejection due to insufficient emphasis on intersecting entities.
- First mention of multigraph should include a brief description for unfamiliar readers.
- Multiple awkward phrasings and unclear sentences throughout (e.g., "Solving this task", "to find out", "we make two").
- Several typos and minor language issues (e.g., "pervious" → "previous"; numerals vs spelled-out numbers).
- Unnecessary formatting choices (italics for "e.g.") and punctuation/footnote placement problems.
- Unclear terminology: "spurious structures", "state" in (hyper-)graph, and "child nodes" in a hypergraph need clarification.
- Figure 2 is unclear in black-and-white, uses colors/greys unclearly, and needs explanation of why both entities should be detected.
- Inconsistent entity notation (brackets vs none) and inconsistent reference formatting (e.g., Lu and Roth citations).
- Numerous clarity and style fixes suggested (rephrasings, comma/period placement, grammar corrections).
- Claims of being "significantly better" should discuss effect sizes; small F1 differences (e.g., 0.4 points) may not be meaningful.
- Dataset/reporting language needs fixing (e.g., "In GENIA dataset" → "On the GENIA dataset") and clearer discussion of low recall sources.
- Table and result claims (Table 5 similarity, convergence statements) need clearer justification and explanation.
- References list formatting should be consistent (paper title casing).
