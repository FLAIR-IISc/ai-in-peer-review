This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for overlapping entity recognition, addressing a known challenge in named entity recognition where entities can share spans. The authors claim three main contributions: a novel multigraph-based model, empirical improvements over previous models on multiple datasets, and a theoretical demonstration that their model avoids the “spurious structures” issue present in a state-of-the-art baseline while maintaining comparable inference complexity. I assess the primary contribution to be the exploration of multigraphs for structured prediction in this context, and the empirical validation provides a solid foundation for the approach. The theoretical analysis, while potentially valuable, requires further clarification to fully appreciate its impact.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Multigraphs:** The core idea of leveraging multigraphs to represent overlapping entities is a significant contribution. Representing different edge semantics for the same node pair provides a flexible framework for modeling complex relationships between entities, moving beyond the limitations of simple or hypergraphs. This representation allows for a more nuanced encoding of overlapping structures.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements in F1-score across multiple datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, and GENIA) compared to the mention hypergraph (MH) baseline. This empirical validation provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model. The inclusion of results with and without F1-score optimization on the development set is a good practice.
3. **Comprehensive Comparison to Baseline:** The paper provides a detailed comparison to the mention hypergraph model, a recent and strong baseline. The thoroughness of the experimental setup and feature engineering, matching the baseline as closely as possible, strengthens the validity of the results. The exploration of performance on sentences *with* and *without* overlapping entities is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Motivation for Separators is Unclear:** The introduction of “entity separators” in Section 4 feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the concept is explained, the justification for *why* these specific separators are necessary—what information they convey beyond the standard T/I/O states—is not sufficiently articulated. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how these separators enhance the model's ability to capture overlapping structures.
2. **Insufficient Elaboration on State- vs. Edge-Based Paradigms:** Section 5.1, discussing the state-based versus edge-based paradigms, lacks concrete implications. While the categorization is presented, the practical benefits of shifting towards an edge-based paradigm are not fully explored. This section feels more like a conceptual observation than a driving force behind the model’s design.
3. **Spurious Structures Explanation Lacks Concreteness:** The discussion of "spurious structures" in Section 5.2 is abstract and difficult to follow. The explanation relies heavily on the mention hypergraph model and requires a deeper, more intuitive explanation. Providing concrete examples of how spurious structures arise in MH and how the multigraph model avoids them would significantly improve understanding. Appendix A attempts to clarify with an example, but it still feels overly complex and doesn't fully convey the practical impact. A visual illustration would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Analysis of Feature Contributions:** The paper lists the features used but does not provide any ablation studies or analysis of their relative importance. Understanding which features contribute most to the performance gains would provide valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the “entity separators” and illustrate with examples how they specifically address the challenges of overlapping entity recognition?
2. Could you provide concrete examples of "spurious structures" that arise in the mention hypergraph model and illustrate how your multigraph model avoids creating these structures? A visual example would be especially helpful.
3. Have you considered performing an ablation study to assess the contribution of different features to the overall performance of the model?
4. Could you discuss the computational cost of training and inference, beyond simply stating the time complexity is the same as MH? What are the practical implications of using multigraphs in terms of memory usage and processing time?

**Additional Comments:**

- The formatting in Appendix A is difficult to read.
- The use of acronyms (e.g., MH) could be explicitly defined upon first use within each section.
- The sentence "Although this has the advantage of simplifying the tokenization procedure, while also making the task slightly more difficult due to the higher number of tokens" in Section D is slightly awkward and could be rephrased for clarity.
