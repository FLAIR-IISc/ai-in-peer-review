The paper proposes an approach based on multigraphs (where multiple edges can link two nodes) for detecting potentially overlapping entities.

**Strengths:**

The problem the paper addresses is interesting, particularly regarding crossing entities and determining which one is actually mentioned in a given text. The technique appears to work, although the empirical results don’t demonstrate a dramatic improvement. I appreciate the discussion of efficiency compared to a previous system. The paper is generally well-written, but could benefit from further polishing in certain details (see minor remarks below).

**Weaknesses:**

The motivation for the problem isn’t entirely clear. Why is it important to detect “China” as an entity *within* the entity “Bank of China,” as illustrated in the introduction? I understand the use case for crossing entities, but what is the application for nested entities? Stronger motivation would increase reader engagement. Regarding the approach itself, important details are missing. What is the decision criterion for including an edge? In lines 229–233, several options for the *I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub>* nodes are mentioned, but it’s never clarified which edges should be present.

The empirical evaluation shows results that are better than some previous approaches, but not by a substantial margin. I would hesitate to characterize the slight improvements as “outperforming,” as the paper does. What is the effect size? Is a two percentage point improvement in F<sub>1</sub> meaningful to an end user? What observable effect does this have? How many previously undiscovered “important” entities are identified? Furthermore, what performance would a simple dictionary-based method, which could also identify overlapping entities, achieve? Also, what performance would a commercial system, such as Google’s NLP cloud (which should be capable of entity detection and linking), achieve on these datasets? This would provide context for the results.

In the results discussion, I would have liked to see more emphasis on crossing entities. What is the performance on this subset, which I believe is more interesting than nested entities? How many more crossing entities are detected compared to previous methods? Which ones were missed, and why? Is the performance improvement due to better nested entity detection, or also to improved detection of crossing entities? A general error analysis comparing the errors made by the proposed system and previous ones would also strengthen this section.

**General Discussion:**

I find the problems related to named entity recognition compelling, and I see merit in recognizing crossing entities. However, the motivation for focusing on nested entities remains unclear. The paper doesn’t sufficiently justify this scenario, nor does the evaluation shed light on it. Discussing errors, perhaps with illustrative examples, and emphasizing the results for crossing entities relative to other approaches might be more persuasive.

Therefore, I currently have a lukewarm assessment of the paper, with a slight lean towards rejection. It appears to be another attempt in this area, but it doesn’t strongly emphasize what I consider the important issue of crossing entities.
