The paper presents a multigraph-based approach for detecting potentially overlapping entities.

**Strengths:**
The problem addressed – identifying overlapping entities, particularly crossing entities – is potentially interesting, especially for resolving ambiguity in text. The proposed technique demonstrates functionality, though the empirical results are not dramatically impactful. I appreciate the inclusion of a discussion regarding efficiency improvements compared to prior work. Overall, the paper is well-written, but would benefit from additional refinement in certain areas (detailed in the minor remarks below).

**Weaknesses:**
The motivation for the problem is somewhat lacking. The example of identifying “China” within “Bank of China” raises the question of practical application; while the potential for crossing entities is clear, the use case for nested entities is less so. A stronger motivation would enhance reader engagement. Regarding the approach itself, critical details are missing. Specifically, the criteria for edge inclusion are not clearly defined. Lines 229-233 introduce multiple options for the *I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub>* nodes without specifying which edges should be present.

The empirical evaluation shows improvements over existing approaches, but the margin is modest. The characterization of these improvements as “outperforming” feels overstated. An assessment of effect size is needed; a two-percentage point improvement in F<sub>1</sub> may not be practically significant. What tangible benefits are realized? How many previously undiscovered "important" entities are identified? Furthermore, the results should be contextualized by comparison to a simple dictionary-based baseline and to the performance of a commercial entity recognition and linking system (e.g., Google's NLP cloud). 

The result discussion would be strengthened by a greater emphasis on crossing entities, which I believe represent the more compelling aspect of this research. What is the performance specifically on crossing entities? How many more are detected compared to previous methods, and what specific examples were previously missed, and why? Is the performance gain attributable to improved nested entity detection, or to better identification of crossing entities? A broader error analysis, comparing errors made by the proposed system to those of prior systems, would also be valuable.

**General Discussion:**
I recognize the relevance of problems related to named entity recognition and the potential benefit of recognizing crossing entities. However, the rationale for investigating nested entities remains unclear. The paper does not adequately motivate this aspect of the research, nor does the evaluation shed light on its practical value. A discussion of errors, illustrative example cases, and a focus on results related to crossing entities could strengthen the paper’s argument. 

Currently, my assessment is lukewarm, with a slight inclination towards rejection. The work appears as another iteration on an existing problem, without sufficiently emphasizing the, in my opinion, more important issue of crossing entities.
