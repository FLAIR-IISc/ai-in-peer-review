- Addresses an interesting problem (detecting overlapping entities) and proposes a multigraph-based approach.
- Technique appears to function and is more efficient than prior systems.
- Empirical gains over prior work are modest; claims of “outperforming” are overstated.
- Motivation for detecting nested entities (e.g., “China” inside “Bank of China”) is weak and needs stronger justification.
- Crucial methodological details are missing—unclear criterion for including edges in the multigraph.
- Ambiguity about which edges should connect I^k_t nodes; descriptions present options without specification.
- Effect size and practical impact of the reported F1 improvements are not analyzed or justified.
- Request for evaluation of simple baselines (dictionary-based) and comparisons to commercial NER/NEL systems (e.g., Google NLP).
- Need focused results and analysis specifically for crossing entities, which are the more compelling use case.
- Lack of detailed error analysis comparing proposed system’s errors to previous systems; unclear whether gains are from nested or crossing entity detection.
- Quantitative questions: how many additional crossing entities are detected, which were missed, and why.
- Overall assessment is lukewarm and leans toward rejection due to insufficient emphasis on crossing-entity significance.
- Paper is generally well-written but requires refinement and clearer terminology/definitions (e.g., define “multigraph”).
- Many unclear or awkward phrasings and referent issues (e.g., “they,” “Solving this task,” italicized “e.g.”).
- Terminology confusion: clarify “state” vs. “node” in (hyper-)graph context and explain “spurious structures.”
- Clarify algorithmic choices: how CS-edge is chosen, how separator defines edges, and how missing edges are determined.
- Figure 2 is unclear—hypergraph representation, gray nodes/edges, color issues, and selection/highlighting criteria need explanation.
- Explain why both nested entities should be detected in examples and what benefit this provides over choosing the longer entity.
- Clarify modeling assumptions (e.g., “simple first order assumption”) and complexity claims (linear vs. cubic w.r.t. sentence length).
- Many specific editorial and formatting fixes requested (commas, footnote placement, wording, dataset phrasing).
- Request to report effect sizes and statistical significance properly rather than vague “significantly better” claims.
- Inconsistencies in references and dataset descriptions should be corrected; ensure consistent capitalization and phrasing.
- Suggested improvements: stronger motivation for nested cases, focused crossing-entity evaluation, detailed error analysis, and more complete methodological descriptions.
