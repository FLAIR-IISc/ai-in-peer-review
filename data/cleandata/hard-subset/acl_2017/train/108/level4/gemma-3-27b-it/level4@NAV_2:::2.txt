The paper proposes a multigraph-based approach—utilizing graphs with multiple edges between nodes—for recognizing potentially overlapping entities.

**Strengths:**

The problem of overlapping entity recognition is a valuable one, particularly for handling crossing entities and resolving ambiguities in text. The proposed technique demonstrates improved performance compared to several previous approaches, and the discussion of efficiency gains relative to a prior system is commendable. The paper is generally well-written and presents a clear description of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

The motivation for this work could be strengthened. While the example of “China” within “Bank of China” illustrates nested entities, the practical importance of identifying such relationships is not well established. The use case for nested entities specifically is unclear; the problem would be more compelling if the focus were on crossing entities. Several important details regarding the implementation are lacking. Specifically, the criteria for including edges in the multigraph are not clearly defined; the discussion in lines 229–233 outlining different options for the *I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub>* nodes does not clarify the selection process.

The empirical results demonstrate improvement, but not by a substantial margin. The gains are not presented with effect sizes, leaving it unclear whether the improvements are practically significant. It would be valuable to assess the performance against a simple dictionary-based approach or established commercial NLP systems (e.g., Google’s Cloud NLP) to provide context. The discussion of results would benefit from a greater emphasis on crossing entities—the more intriguing aspect of overlapping entity recognition—with analysis of performance on this subset and comparison of errors made by the proposed system and previous methods.

**General Discussion:**

I appreciate the focus on named entity recognition and the potential of addressing crossing entities. However, the lack of justification for the relevance of nested entities and the limited empirical impact diminish the overall strength of the work. A more focused discussion of errors, accompanied by illustrative examples, and a specific evaluation of performance on crossing entities could strengthen the paper's contribution. I currently have a lukewarm assessment, leaning slightly toward rejection, as the paper feels like an incremental step without highlighting a truly critical issue—crossing entities.
