## Review for ACL 2017 - A Multigraph-based Model for Overlapping Entity Recognition

This paper introduces a multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping entities, presenting it as a novel approach compared to existing methods like mention hypergraphs (MH). The authors claim three primary contributions: a novel multigraph-based model, improved F1-scores on multiple datasets, and a theoretical demonstration of the model's non-deficiency compared to a prior state-of-the-art approach. 

I believe the primary contribution lies in the exploration of multigraphs for structured prediction, specifically in the context of overlapping entity recognition. While the performance improvements are valuable, the novelty of applying multigraphs to this problem is more significant. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of the proposed model, demonstrating its competitive performance on standard datasets. The theoretical analysis, while attempting to highlight an advantage over the MH model, requires further clarification to be fully convincing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Multigraph Application:** The paper explores a relatively unexplored representation – multigraphs – for structured prediction tasks involving overlapping structures. This is a potentially impactful direction, offering a different perspective on modeling complex relationships between entities.  Applying multigraphs, where edges can carry distinct semantics, seems like a logical extension to traditional graphical models.
2. **Solid Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive and often superior performance compared to existing state-of-the-art methods, including the MH model, on standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, and GENIA). This provides evidence of the practical effectiveness of the approach. The inclusion of results on both datasets with and without overlapping entities further strengthens the analysis.
3. **Comprehensive Comparison to Mention Hypergraphs:** The paper positions itself well within the existing literature by focusing on a direct comparison with the MH model. The authors attempt to analyze the differences between the two approaches, which demonstrates a clear understanding of the related work and highlights the potential advantages of their method.
4. **Availability of Code and System:** The authors' commitment to releasing their system and code for research purposes is a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further investigation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Motivation for Separators is Unclear:** The introduction of "entity separators" in Section 4 feels somewhat arbitrary. The paper doesn’t adequately explain *why* these separators are needed beyond the existing T/I/O states.  What specific information do they encode that is not already captured by the states themselves? A clearer explanation of the design choices behind these separators is crucial.
2. **Insufficient Justification for Model Superiority:**  While Section 5.1 attempts to categorize existing approaches, it doesn’t strongly motivate why the multigraph-based approach is fundamentally superior. The distinction between "state-based" and "edge-based" paradigms feels abstract and doesn't directly translate into performance benefits.
3. **Abstractness of “Spurious Structures” Discussion:** The explanation of “spurious structures” in Section 5.2 is overly theoretical and difficult to grasp. Providing concrete examples of these structures and illustrating how the multigraph model avoids them would greatly improve understanding. The current explanation relies heavily on abstract descriptions of the normalization term and doesn’t offer a clear intuition for why this difference matters in practice. The reference to Figure 8 in the Appendix is helpful, but it could be integrated directly into the main text.
4. **Limited Analysis of Feature Importance:** The paper mentions the features used but doesn't provide any analysis of their relative importance or contribution to the overall performance. A brief discussion on which features were most effective would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind introducing entity separators? What specific challenges or limitations of existing approaches do they address?
2. Could you provide a concrete example of a sentence and the corresponding entity structure where the multigraph model correctly identifies an entity that the MH model would incorrectly predict due to spurious structures?
3. What is the computational overhead (in terms of training and inference time) associated with using multigraphs compared to hypergraphs? The paper states comparable inference complexity, but a more detailed comparison would be useful.

**Additional Comments:**

*   In section 3, explicitly state the limitations of the MH model that motivate the proposed approach.
*   Consider adding a visual example to Section 4 illustrating how the multigraph represents overlapping entities in a concrete sentence.
*   Clarify the meaning of "non-deficient" in the context of the model’s theoretical properties.
*   The table referencing hyperparameters would benefit from including a brief explanation of why those values were chosen.
