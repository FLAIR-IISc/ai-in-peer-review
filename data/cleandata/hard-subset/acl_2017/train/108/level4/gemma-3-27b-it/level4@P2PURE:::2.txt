The paper proposes an approach based on multigraphs—graphs allowing multiple edges between nodes—for detecting potentially overlapping entities.

**Strengths:**
The problem addressed is potentially interesting, particularly for resolving which entity is referenced when entities cross. The technique appears to function, though the empirical results do not demonstrate a substantial effect. I appreciate the attention given to efficiency compared to prior systems. The paper is generally well-written, though it would benefit from further refinement in certain details (see minor remarks below).

**Weaknesses:**
The motivation for this problem is not fully convincing. Why is it important to identify “China” as an entity *within* “Bank of China,” as exemplified in the introduction? While the benefit for crossing entities is clear, the use case for nested entities remains unclear and requires stronger justification to engage the reader. Regarding the approach itself, crucial details are missing: What criterion determines whether to include an edge? In lines 229–233, several options for the I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub> nodes are presented without clarification of which edges should be present.

The empirical evaluation shows improvements over previous approaches, but the margin is not substantial. The paper’s claim of “outperforming” these approaches is overstated given the modest gains. What is the effect size? Would a two-percentage-point improvement in F<sub>1</sub> be meaningful to a user? What is the practical impact of these results? How many previously undiscovered “important” entities are identified? Additionally, what performance would a simple dictionary-based method achieve for finding overlapping entities? Furthermore, how would a commercial entity recognition and linking system, such as Google’s NLP cloud, perform on these datasets to provide a benchmark against existing industry solutions?

The discussion of results would be strengthened by focusing more on crossing entities—which, in my opinion, represents the more compelling use case. What is the performance specifically on crossing entities? How many more are detected compared to previous methods? Which ones were missed, and why? Is the performance improvement attributable solely to better nested entity detection, or also to improved detection of crossing entities? A more detailed error analysis comparing the errors made by the proposed system to those of previous systems would further strengthen this section.

**General Discussion:**
I recognize the relevance of the problems related to named entity recognition and the potential value of recognizing crossing entities. However, the rationale for focusing on nested entities is less apparent. The paper does not adequately motivate this scenario, nor does the evaluation shed light on its benefits. A discussion of errors and advantages, accompanied by example cases and a concentration on the results for crossing entities compared to other approaches, might increase my confidence in the work. 

Currently, my assessment is lukewarm, leaning towards rejection. The paper feels like another iteration on the problem without sufficiently emphasizing the critical question of crossing entities.
