The paper suggests an approach based on multigraphs (several edges may link two nodes) for detecting potentially overlapping entities.

**Strengths:**

The problem itself is potentially interesting, especially for resolving crossing entities to determine which one is actually mentioned in a given text. The technique appears to work, although the empirical results do not demonstrate a dramatic effect. I appreciate the discussion of efficiency compared to a previous system. The paper is generally well-written but would benefit from further polishing in some details (see minor remarks below).

**Weaknesses:**

The problem’s motivation is not entirely clear. Why is it important to detect “China” as an entity within “Bank of China,” as exemplified in the introduction? While a use case exists for crossing entities, the value proposition for nested entities remains unclear. This aspect requires stronger motivation to engage the reader. Regarding the approach itself, important details are missing. Specifically, what is the decision criterion for including an edge in the multigraph? In lines 229–233, several options for the *I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub>* nodes are mentioned, but it is never clarified which edges should be present.

The empirical evaluation shows results that are better than some previous approaches, but not by a substantial margin. The slight improvements are not significant enough to warrant claims of “outperforming” other methods. What is the effect size? Would a two-percentage-point improvement in F<sub>1</sub> be meaningful to a user? What tangible effect can be observed? How many “important” entities are discovered that were previously missed? Furthermore, what performance would a simplistic dictionary-based method achieve in finding overlapping entities? Additionally, what performance would a commercial system, such as Google’s NLP cloud (which should be capable of entity detection and linking), achieve on the datasets, providing a contrast to existing “commercial” systems?

The results discussion would be strengthened by greater emphasis on crossing entities. What is the performance specifically on this subset, which is, in my opinion, more interesting than nested entities? How many more crossing entities are detected compared to previous methods? Which ones were missed and why? Is the performance improvement solely attributable to better nested entity detection, or does it also stem from improved detection of crossing entities? A general error analysis comparing errors made by the proposed system and previous ones would further strengthen this section.

**General Discussion:**

I acknowledge the challenges related to named entity recognition and recognize the potential value of recognizing crossing entities. However, the motivation for recognizing nested entities is less clear. The paper does not adequately address this question, either in its motivation or in the evaluation. Discussing errors, providing illustrative examples, and focusing on the results for crossing entities compared to other approaches might strengthen the paper and increase my confidence in its contributions.

Currently, I hold a lukewarm opinion of the paper, leaning toward rejection. It feels like another attempt without sufficiently emphasizing the critical issue of crossing entities.
