- Problem is interesting, particularly for resolving crossing entities; the proposed multigraph technique appears to work and is relatively efficient.  
- Paper is generally well-written but needs polishing in several details.

- Motivation for detecting nested entities (e.g., “China” inside “Bank of China”) is unclear and needs stronger justification.  
- Key methodological detail missing: the decision criterion for including edges in the multigraph (which edges should be present is not specified).  
- Empirical improvements are small; claims of “outperforming” prior work are overstated without effect-size analysis.  
- Need to show practical significance: would a ~2-point F1 gain matter, and how many important entities are newly discovered?  
- Missing simple baselines: report performance of a dictionary-based method and of a commercial system (e.g., Google NLP) on the datasets.  
- Results should highlight crossing-entity performance specifically: report metrics on that subset and how many more crossing entities are detected vs prior work.  
- Provide detailed error analysis comparing the proposed system and previous systems to show where gains originate (nested vs crossing).  
- Clarify whether improvements stem mainly from nested entity handling or from better crossing-entity detection.

- Define multigraph and clarify terminology (state/node, CS-edge, entity separator) earlier and consistently.  
- Specify algorithmic choices: how CS-edge is chosen, how missing edges were detected, and what the “simple first order assumption” entails.  
- Figure 2 needs clearer depiction (not rely on color, explain gray vs highlighted edges) and justification for why detecting both entities is beneficial.  
- Explain selection and meaning of node types (I^k_t) and which edges link them (lines 229–233 ambiguity).  
- Clarify complexity claims (time linear in n) in context of sentence length and alternatives.  
- Add illustrative examples showing beneficial/important crossing and nested entity cases.

- Experimental/reporting fixes: report effect sizes, avoid overstating small gains (e.g., 0.4 F1), clarify which recall is low and why, and show dataset statistics and baseline feature usage clearly.  
- Include concrete examples of errors and of entities recovered by the new method but missed by others.  
- Address whether prioritizing crossing entities is beneficial (footnote 3) with examples.  
- Improve figure/table captions and ensure all F1 comparisons are interpreted cautiously.

- Numerous language, formatting, and typographical issues to fix (phrasing, commas/periods, footnote placement, typos like “pervious,” consistent reference formatting, spell out small numbers).
