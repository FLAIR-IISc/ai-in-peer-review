In this paper, the authors propose a novel approach based on multigraphs (where multiple edges may connect two nodes) for detecting potentially overlapping entities.

**Strengths:**

The problem addressed is interesting, particularly regarding crossing entities and resolving ambiguities in text. The proposed technique appears to function effectively, although the empirical results do not demonstrate a substantial improvement. I appreciate the discussion of efficiency compared to a previous system. The paper is generally well-written but would benefit from some polishing (see minor remarks below).

**Weaknesses:**

The motivation for the problem is not fully convincing. While the example of “China” within “Bank of China” is provided, the practical importance of detecting such nested entities is not clearly articulated. I recognize the value in handling crossing entities, but the use case for nested entities requires further justification to engage the reader. Regarding the approach itself, important details are lacking: What criteria are used to include an edge? Lines 229–233 mention several options for the I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub> nodes, but it remains unclear which edges should be present.

The empirical evaluation reveals improvements over previous approaches, but these are not substantial. The authors’ claim of “outperforming” seems overstated given the modest gains. An analysis of effect size is needed; does a two-percentage-point improvement in F<sub>1</sub>-score have practical significance? What is the tangible effect of this improvement – how many previously undetected important entities are now identified? Furthermore, what performance would a simplistic dictionary-based approach achieve, and how do the results compare to those of commercial NLP systems like Google’s Cloud NLP, which should also be capable of entity detection and linking?

The results discussion would benefit from a greater focus on crossing entities. How does the model perform on this subset of overlapping entities? This, in my opinion, is the more compelling aspect of the problem. How many more crossing entities are detected compared to previous methods? Which ones were missed, and why? Is the performance improvement attributable to better nested entity detection or improved crossing entity detection? A more thorough error analysis comparing the errors made by the proposed system and previous methods would strengthen this section.

**General Discussion:**

I acknowledge the importance of research on named entity recognition and see merit in addressing the challenge of crossing entities. However, the motivation for investigating nested entities remains unclear. The paper does not adequately explain the scenarios where this is valuable, nor does the evaluation shed light on this point. Discussing errors and providing illustrative examples, with a particular emphasis on crossing entity results, could have been more convincing. Therefore, I am only cautiously optimistic about this paper, leaning slightly towards rejection. It appears as another incremental contribution without clearly emphasizing the crucial question of crossing entities.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more compelling use case for the detection of nested entities?
*   What criteria are used for selecting edges in the multigraph?
*   What is the effect size of the observed improvements in F<sub>1</sub>-score?
*   How does the model perform specifically on crossing entities compared to previous approaches?
*   Could you provide an error analysis comparing the types of errors made by your system and previous methods?
*   How would a simple dictionary-based method perform on these datasets?
*   What performance would a commercial entity recognition system (e.g., Google Cloud NLP) achieve on these datasets?
