The paper introduces a multigraph-based approach (allowing multiple edges between the same pair of nodes) for recognizing entities that may overlap in text.

Strengths:
The topic is potentially compelling, particularly for handling crossing entities and deciding which mentions are actually present. The method appears to function as intended, although the empirical gains are modest rather than striking. I appreciate the discussion of inference efficiency relative to prior work. The writing is generally clear, though several details would benefit from additional polish (see minor remarks).

Weaknesses:
The motivation is underdeveloped. For example, the introduction’s example of nested entities (China within Bank of China) does not by itself justify why nested recognition is important; crossing entities seem more compelling, but the paper does not make a strong case for nested mentions. Several technical details are underspecified. In particular, what is the precise decision rule for including or excluding an edge? In lines 229–233, multiple options for the Ikt states are enumerated, but it remains unclear which edges are actually instantiated. Empirically, the improvements over prior systems are small. I would hesitate to describe the results as “outperforming” in the sense implied by the paper; please contextualize effect sizes. What is the practical impact of a two-point F1 increase? Which additional, meaningful mentions are recovered that prior systems missed? It would also help to compare against simple dictionary-based methods that can detect overlapping spans, and to include a reference point from a commercial NER offering (e.g., a cloud API) to situate the results. In the analysis, I would like to see a dedicated focus on crossing entities: performance specifically on crossings, which crossings were newly detected, which were still missed and why, and whether the gains stem mainly from nested or also from crossing cases. A comparative error analysis versus the baselines would strengthen the discussion.

General Discussion:
I am interested in NER and agree that crossing entities are a worthwhile challenge. However, the motivation for nested entities remains weak in this paper, and the evaluation does not illuminate that use case. A deeper error analysis with concrete examples, and a clear emphasis on crossing entities relative to prior work, would make the contribution more convincing. As is, my overall assessment is lukewarm, with a slight lean toward rejection, as the work feels incremental and does not fully address the central question of crossing entities.
