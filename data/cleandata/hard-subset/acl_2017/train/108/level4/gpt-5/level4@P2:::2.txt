Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a structured prediction model for recognizing overlapping (nested and crossing) entities using a multigraph representation that assigns explicit semantics to multiple edges between the same node pair. The authors present training and inference procedures with O(nT) complexity and argue the model avoids the spurious-structure deficiency of mention hypergraphs. Experiments on ACE2004/2005, GENIA, and CoNLL 2003 show modest improvements over strong baselines.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A multigraph-based formulation for overlapping entity recognition with edge semantics (primary).
2) A theoretical analysis claiming nondeficiency (no spurious structures) while retaining linear-time inference (secondary).
3) Empirical comparisons showing small but consistent gains on several datasets and a discussion of training convergence (secondary).

Strengths
- Novel representation within the edge-based paradigm: Defining entity separators as edges is a clear and intuitive way to encode overlapping spans. The mapping between separators and edges is well motivated and connects to prior work on mention hypergraphs.
- Sound inference with practical complexity: The inside–outside and max-product procedures are presented clearly, and the O(nT) complexity matches prior work. The nondeficiency argument addresses a known issue (spurious structures) with mention hypergraphs.
- Competitive empirical performance: The model yields consistent, if modest, F1 improvements on ACE and GENIA and performs well on non-overlapping data (CoNLL 2003), suggesting reasonable robustness.
- Training behavior: The reported faster convergence in terms of iterations (despite slightly slower iterations) is informative and useful for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient motivation for nested entities: The introduction does not convincingly argue why nested entities matter in downstream applications. The China vs. Bank of China example is illustrative but not tied to real use cases. A stronger case for practical value (information extraction, entity linking, biomedical IE) is needed. Crossing entities are more compelling; the paper should emphasize them.
- Missing technical detail on edge inclusion: The description around Ikt (e.g., lines 229–233 in the draft) lists several options but does not specify the decision criterion for including a particular edge/separator in the structure during inference and training. It remains unclear how, algorithmically, separators like CS are selected and how ambiguity is resolved.
- Limited analysis of effect size and significance: Improvements are mostly small (often ≈0.4–2 F1). The paper repeatedly uses “outperform,” but there is no statistical testing, effect size reporting, or user-centered interpretation (e.g., number of additional correct entities, especially for “important” mentions). It is unclear whether such gains matter in practice.
- Baseline coverage: There is no comparison to simple dictionary/heuristic approaches that can capture overlaps, nor to widely used commercial systems (e.g., Google Cloud NLP) for context. While not definitive baselines for research, they help ground the practical impact. Also, the paper focuses on hypergraph and linear-chain CRF baselines; additional structured baselines (e.g., semi-CRF variants or span-based neural models) would help.
- Limited error and subset analysis: The paper does not break out performance on crossing vs. nested entities, nor provide qualitative error analysis. It is unclear whether gains are driven by nested, crossing, or both; which types are missed; and why. Such analysis would strengthen the empirical case and illuminate the benefits of the multigraph design.

Questions to authors
- Edge selection: Precisely how are entity separators (e.g., CS, EC) instantiated between tokens? What is the algorithmic decision rule for including or excluding an edge? How is ambiguity handled when multiple separators connect the same state pair?
- Crossing vs. nested: What is the model’s performance on crossing entities specifically (precision/recall/F1), and how does it compare to baselines? How many more crossing cases are recovered?
- Effect size and significance: Are the reported gains statistically significant? Can you report absolute counts of additional correct entities, especially for high-value types?
- Practical baselines: How would a dictionary-based approach perform on overlapping/nested spans? Any sense of how commercial NER/linking systems fare on the same data?
- Efficiency: In practice, when sentence lengths are moderate, how much does O(n) vs. O(n^3) matter? Can you provide wall-clock comparisons for realistic settings?

Additional comments (minor)
- Define multigraph on first mention; avoid assuming graph-theory familiarity.
- Clarify “spurious structures” in the introduction; give a brief intuitive definition.
- “Previously noted by … many previous” sounds awkward; rephrase for flow.
- Specify the task when writing “Solving this task.”
- Avoid italics for “e.g.”
- Be precise: “time linear in n” (state n is sentence length); discuss why this matters in practice.
- Consistently use “state” vs. “node”; if “state” means node, state that explicitly.
- Use words for small numbers in running text (e.g., “five types”).
- Add commas in enumerations and a period at the end.
- Explain “child nodes” in a hypergraph context.
- Figures: Ensure grayscale readability; explain why Figure 2 is a hypergraph, how highlighted edges were chosen, and why others are gray.
- Explain why both entities in Figure 2 should be recognized; what additional utility does the shorter one provide?
- Be consistent with parenthetical “denoting …”; standardize bracket usage.
- Place footnotes after punctuation; in footnote 2, justify how the missing edge is determined.
- Clarify “on whether the separator defines …”: how is this determined?
- Before Section 4.1: explain how the CS-edge is selected algorithmically.
- Style: Consider a comma after “Equation (1),” avoid “to find out,” and prefer “we conduct” over “we make” when introducing analyses.
- Nested vs. crossing: briefly motivate why nested are retained when resolving ambiguity; provide examples.
- Clarify “first-order assumption” when discussing dependencies in a state-based reformulation.
- Dataset phrasing: “used in these experiments”; “published on their website”; “The statistics of each dataset are shown …”
- Wording: remove duplicated “use” in “allows us to use to make use”; rephrase “tried to follow as close …” to “we followed as closely as possible …”
- Reference style: “Following Lu and Roth (2015) …” (no parentheses as a noun); consistent capitalization in titles; fix typos (e.g., “pervious” → “previous”).
- Reporting: Avoid overclaiming (“significantly,” “outperform”) for small gains; if used, support with tests/effect sizes.
- Miscellaneous typos and consistency (BIO/BILOU capitalization, comma usage, section cross-references).
