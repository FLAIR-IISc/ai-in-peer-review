The paper proposes a multigraph-based approach (allowing multiple edges between two nodes) for detecting potentially overlapping entities.

Strengths:
- The problem is interesting, particularly for crossing entities, where one must decide which entity is actually expressed in the text. The technique appears to work, although the empirical results do not show a dramatic effect.
- I appreciate the discussion of efficiency relative to a previous system.
- The paper is generally well written, though it needs additional polishing in several places (see minor remarks below).

Weaknesses:
- The motivation is not sufficiently developed. For example, why is it important to detect China as an entity within Bank of China (as in the introduction)? I see the case for crossing entities, but what is the use case for nested entities? This needs stronger motivation to engage the reader.
- Important details of the approach appear to be missing. What is the decision criterion for including an edge? In lines 229–233, several options for the I^k_t nodes are mentioned, but it is never clarified which edges should be present.
- Empirically, the results are better than some prior approaches, but not by a large margin. I would not describe small gains as having “outperformed,” as the paper does. What is the effect size? Does a two-point improvement in F1 matter to an end user? What tangible differences can one observe? How many “important” entities are newly discovered that previous methods missed?
- What would a simple dictionary-based baseline achieve for overlapping entities? Similarly, how would a commercial system (e.g., Google’s Cloud Natural Language, which can detect and link entities) perform on these datasets? Such comparisons would help contextualize the results against existing “commercial” systems.
- In the discussion of results, I would have liked more emphasis on crossing entities. How does the system perform there? This subset is, in my view, more interesting than nested entities. How many additional crossing entities are detected compared to before? Which ones are still missed, and why? Is the improvement driven by better detection of nested entities, crossing entities, or both? A focused error analysis comparing the proposed system with prior ones would strengthen the evaluation.

General Discussion:
I am interested in named entity recognition and see a strong case for recognizing crossing entities. However, the paper does not adequately motivate the importance of nested entities, nor does the evaluation illuminate this point. A discussion of errors and advantages with concrete examples—especially emphasizing results on crossing entities relative to other approaches—would have been more persuasive. Overall, I am lukewarm about the paper, with a slight leaning toward rejection. It feels like yet another attempt that does not sufficiently emphasize, in my view, the key question of crossing entities.
