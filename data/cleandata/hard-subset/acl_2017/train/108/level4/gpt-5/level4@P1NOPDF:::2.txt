The paper proposes a multigraph-based approach (allowing multiple edges between two nodes) for detecting potentially overlapping entities.

Strengths:
- The problem is interesting, especially for crossing entities, where one must decide which mention(s) are actually present in a text. The technique appears to work, though the empirical gains are not dramatic.
- I appreciate the discussion of efficiency relative to prior work.
- The paper is generally well written, though it still needs polishing in several places (see minor remarks).

Weaknesses:
- Motivation is weak. Why is it important to detect “China” as an entity within “Bank of China,” as in the introduction? I can see a clearer need for handling crossing entities, but what is the concrete use case for nested entities? Stronger motivation would better engage the reader.
- Important technical details are missing. What is the decision criterion for including an edge? In lines 229–233, several options for the I^k_t nodes are listed, but it is never clarified which edges should exist among them.
- Empirically, the results improve over some prior work, but only marginally. I would not call such small gains “outperforming.” What are the effect sizes? Do 1–2 F1 points matter to end users? How many “important” entities are newly discovered compared to prior methods? What would a simple dictionary-based baseline achieve for overlapping cases? Similarly, how would a commercial system (e.g., Google Cloud Natural Language) perform on these datasets? Such baselines would help contextualize the gains.
- The results discussion underemphasizes crossing entities. How does the system perform specifically on crossing cases? How many more crossing entities are detected than before? Which ones were missed, and why? Are improvements driven mainly by better nested detection, or also by crossing detection? An error analysis contrasting this system with baselines would strengthen the evaluation.

General discussion:
I am interested in NER with overlapping mentions, and I particularly see the value in recognizing crossing entities. However, the paper does not convincingly motivate nested entities, and the evaluation does not clarify their practical value. A more thorough error analysis with illustrative cases, and a focused comparison on crossing entities against prior approaches, would be more persuasive. Overall, I am lukewarm, with a slight lean toward rejection: the work feels incremental and does not adequately address what I see as the most compelling aspect—crossing entities.
