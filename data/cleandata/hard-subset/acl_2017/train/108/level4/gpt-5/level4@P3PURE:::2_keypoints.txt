- Paper proposes a multigraph-based method to detect potentially overlapping (nested/crossing) entities.  
- Strength: The problem is interesting, especially for handling crossing entities and deciding which mentions are present.  
- Strength: The technique appears to work empirically, though gains are modest.  
- Strength: Discussion of efficiency relative to prior systems is appreciated.  
- Strength: Paper is generally well written, but needs polishing on details.  
- Weakness: Motivation is weak—unclear why detecting nested entities (e.g., “China” inside “Bank of China”) is practically important.  
- Weakness: Methodological details missing—unclear decision criterion for including edges in the multigraph and which I^k_t node options are used.  
- Weakness: Empirical gains are small; claims of “outperforming” seem overstated without effect-size context or user-impact discussion.  
- Weakness: Lack of baseline/context—no simple dictionary baseline or comparison to commercial systems (e.g., Google Cloud NLP) to position contributions.  
- Weakness: Results need targeted analysis on crossing entities—how many detected, missed, and whether improvements stem from nested or crossing cases.  
- Weakness: Would benefit from a focused error analysis comparing the proposed system’s errors to prior systems’.  
- General: Reviewer is interested in NER and crossing entities but is lukewarm overall with a slight tendency toward rejection due to insufficient emphasis on crossing-entity motivation/evaluation.  
- Minor: Define “multigraph” on first mention for unfamiliar readers.  
- Minor: Rephrase awkward phrases (e.g., “previously noted by … many previous”) and avoid informal wording like “to find out.”  
- Minor: Clarify which specific task is meant by phrases like “solving this task” (overlapping/nested/crossing).  
- Minor: Explain practical impact of time complexity claims (e.g., linear vs cubic in sentence length).  
- Minor: Clarify terms like “spurious structures,” “regarded as a chunk,” “state” vs “node,” “child nodes” in hypergraph context.  
- Minor: Improve Figure 2: justify why it’s a hypergraph, make grayscale-visible distinctions clear, and explain selected/gray nodes and whether detecting both entities is necessary.  
- Minor: Many phrasing/typo/citation issues (e.g., “pervious,” spacing in citations, use references as nouns, inconsistent casing in bibliography).  
- Minor: Fix footnote placement and completeness of several sentences/phrases that are currently incomplete or unclear.  
- Minor: Clarify experimental details and reporting—explain small F1 differences, report effect sizes/confidence intervals, and specify what “significantly better” means.  
- Minor: Explain algorithmic choices (e.g., how CS-edge is chosen, separator determination) and assumptions (e.g., the “simple first order assumption”).  
- Minor: Ensure consistent terminology and style (spell out acronyms on first use, place punctuation correctly in lists, use consistent “denoting”/bracketing).
