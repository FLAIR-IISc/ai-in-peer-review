Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a multigraph-based structured prediction model for recognizing overlapping entities in text. Instead of simple graphs or hypergraphs, it assigns explicit semantics to multiple edges between the same pair of nodes (“entity separators”) to represent whether entities start, end, or continue between adjacent tokens. The authors present training and inference using an inside–outside style algorithm with linear time complexity in sentence length and the number of entity types. They evaluate on ACE2004, ACE2005, and GENIA, and report improvements over prior work, notably the mention hypergraph (MH) model.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A multigraph-based formulation for overlapping entity recognition that uses edge semantics (entity separators) to represent overlapping structures.
2) Empirical improvements over strong baselines (including MH) on ACE and GENIA under comparable feature sets and decoding regimes.
3) A theoretical claim that the proposed model is nondeficient, avoiding spurious structures that arise in MH, while keeping the same O(nT) time complexity.

Strengths
1) Empirical performance: The model achieves solid gains over MH on ACE2004 and competitive or better results on ACE2005 and GENIA under matched features and tuning protocols. The improvements are meaningful for a well-studied task and are reported with precision/recall/F1, with additional analyses split by sentences with/without overlaps.

2) Sound problem focus: Overlapping entities are common in ACE and GENIA-style corpora and are inadequately handled by standard sequence models. Targeting this setting is practically useful and of clear interest to the community.

3) Comparable experimental setup: The implementation parity with MH (feature sets defined over edges, mention penalty tuning, linear-time inference) makes the comparison credible. The analysis by sentence type (with vs. without overlaps) helps interpret when the model yields benefits.

4) Theoretical angle: The discussion of spurious structures and nondeficiency is a valuable perspective. Maintaining MH’s linear complexity while arguing away a source of modeling bias is promising, if substantiated.

5) Writing and organization: Overall the paper is clearly written, with helpful figures connecting the multigraph edges to entity separators, and an explicit training/inference description.

Weaknesses
1) Motivation relative to MH is underdeveloped (Section 3). The description of MH is clear, but the paper does not explicitly articulate what limitations of MH drove the new design. Beyond the spurious-structure argument (Section 5.2), please clarify the specific modeling or inference shortcomings in MH that the multigraph model addresses, and how those shortcomings plausibly affect empirical performance.

2) Rationale for entity separators is unclear (Section 4). The paper introduces eight separators (E, C, S and their combinations) but does not convincingly explain what additional information they carry beyond the T/I/O state definitions. Please show, with a concrete example, how these edge semantics capture overlapping configurations that would be ambiguous or conflated using only node states. A small side-by-side example contrasting MH edges/hyperedges vs. multigraph separators would help.

3) Section 5.1 (state-based vs. edge-based) does not justify superiority. The taxonomy is informative, but it does not provide insight into why the multigraph instantiation should outperform MH, which is also edge-based. If the key benefit is avoiding unnecessary dependencies or reducing ad hoc representational overhead, please make that explicit and tie it to observed gains or to a measurable difference in model capacity/regularization.

4) Spurious structures discussion is too abstract (Section 5.2). The current argument asserts nondeficiency for the multigraph model and deficiency for MH but provides limited intuition. Please include at least one fully worked, minimal example where MH’s normalization sums over multiple distinct structure interpretations that share a subgraph (i.e., spurious structures), and then show how the multigraph factorization prevents this. A numerical toy example would make the claim concrete.

5) Why the model works better remains opaque. The paper reports improvements but does not convincingly connect them to the proposed design choices (separators, nondeficiency). For example, in GENIA the model’s recall is slightly lower on overlapping sentences, and in ACE the gains are clearer. Please discuss when the proposed modeling choices help or hurt, and whether the improvements are driven by better handling of specific overlap patterns (nested vs. crossing), sentence lengths, or entity types.

Questions to authors
- Can you explicitly enumerate MH’s limitations that motivated the multigraph design, beyond the spurious-structure issue? For example, are there particular overlap patterns that MH cannot uniquely encode or that lead to ambiguity during decoding?

- What concrete information do the entity separators encode that cannot be captured by T/I/O states plus local features? Please provide a compact example illustrating how separators disambiguate overlapping spans.

- Could you include a minimal, step-by-step example (with scores) that demonstrates spurious structures in MH’s normalization and contrasts it with your model’s calculation? This would greatly clarify Section 5.2.

- Do the improvements concentrate on nested or on crossing overlaps? A breakdown by overlap type and by entity type would help diagnose where separators and nondeficiency matter most.

- How sensitive are your gains to the number or design of separators? For example, if you collapsed some combinations (e.g., ES vs. C) or altered edge semantics, would performance degrade?

Overall recommendation
The paper tackles an important problem and shows promising empirical and theoretical advantages over MH. However, the motivation for the new representation, the role of separators, and the spurious-structure argument need clearer, example-driven exposition to justify why the model works better. With stronger explanations and illustrative cases, the contribution would be significantly clearer.
