Summary
This paper proposes a structured prediction model for recognizing overlapping named entities using a multigraph representation in which multiple edges between the same pair of nodes carry explicit semantics (“entity separators” such as E, C, S and their combinations). The model performs training and inference with an inside–outside style algorithm and Viterbi-like decoding, achieves the same O(nT) complexity as the mention hypergraph model, and is argued to be nondeficient (i.e., free of spurious structures in its partition function). Experiments on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA show modest F1 gains over prior work, and the authors provide some analysis of convergence and complexity.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A new edge-centric multigraph formulation for overlapping entity recognition that assigns semantics to multiple edges (entity separators), enabling a compact representation and inference of overlapping spans.
2) A theoretical analysis arguing the model is nondeficient compared to mention hypergraphs, avoiding spurious structures while retaining O(nT) inference.
3) An empirical evaluation on standard datasets with competitive results and small improvements, plus an observation of faster convergence in practice.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity of the representation: Encoding separator semantics directly on edges is a clean, general idea for modeling overlaps. The connection to the “edge-based paradigm” is well articulated and differentiates the approach from conventional state-based sequence models.
- Theoretical claim of nondeficiency: The comparison to mention hypergraphs and the argument that the partition function excludes spurious structures is important. If correct, this addresses a known weakness of a strong baseline while maintaining linear-time inference.
- Competitive empirical results: On ACE and GENIA, the model matches or modestly exceeds strong structured baselines (mention hypergraph, Finkel & Manning’s parser), suggesting the modeling change is not just cosmetic.
- Efficiency and convergence discussion: Same asymptotic complexity with empirical evidence of faster convergence per training run is practical and useful for adoption.
- Potential generality: The formalism could extend to other overlapping-structure problems (e.g., equation parsing), making the contribution broader than NER.

Weaknesses
- Motivation and use cases for nested entities are underdeveloped: The paper does not make a compelling case for why nested entities matter to downstream applications (e.g., relation/event extraction, entity linking, slot filling). The examples focus on containment (“Bank of China”/“China”) without showing concrete task-level benefits. The paper also does not separate nested from crossing cases in analysis; crossing mentions are arguably the more compelling challenge.
- Missing algorithmic details: The paper presents the separator set (E, C, S and combinations) but does not precisely specify the decision criteria that govern edge presence during training/inference, nor how ambiguous cases (e.g., CS vs C vs ES) are resolved algorithmically. The mapping from separators to spans during decoding, and how multiple edges between identical states are handled in dynamic programming, need clearer specification.
- Empirical claims vs effect size: Gains are generally small (often ~0.4–2 F1). The paper repeatedly says “outperforms,” but does not report statistical significance tests, absolute counts of additionally found entities, or a breakdown on overlapping vs non-overlapping sentences sufficient to assess practical utility. There is no focused analysis on crossing entities (precision/recall, which new cases are captured, which failures remain).
- Baselines and analysis breadth: While comparisons to mention hypergraph and Finkel & Manning (2009) are appropriate, the paper lacks simple but informative baselines (e.g., a high-coverage dictionary/regex span enumerator) to contextualize the gains on overlapping spans. Error analysis is light; there is little qualitative comparison of errors unique to the proposed model vs prior work, or ablations (e.g., without mention penalty, without particular separator types).
- Presentation and clarity issues: Several terminology and exposition issues hinder readability: “state” vs “node” is used interchangeably; the role of edges in hypergraphs is not always clear; Figure 2 is hard to parse in grayscale; the footnote about “missing edge” is under-specified; numerous minor writing issues and typos (examples below) need attention.

Questions to authors
- Motivation: Please provide concrete downstream scenarios where nested entities improve end-task performance (e.g., event extraction in ACE/GENIA, entity linking, clinical IE). Can you quantify impact in any such setting?
- Separators and decoding: Precisely define the mapping from separator labels to span operations. How is the CS edge chosen algorithmically at training/decoding time? How are multiple edges between the same states handled in the inside–outside sums and in max-product decoding?
- Nondeficiency: Can you formalize the nondeficiency claim with a concise proof sketch that every valid entity combination corresponds to a unique subgraph (no multiplicity), and that the partition function sums over exactly those structures?
- Crossing vs nested: Please report per-type (nested vs crossing) precision/recall/F1 and counts of additional entities recovered vs mention hypergraph. Which crossing patterns are newly captured, and which errors persist?
- Statistical testing and effect size: Are the improvements statistically significant (e.g., paired bootstrap on spans)? Can you report absolute numbers of additional true positives/false positives, particularly for overlapping spans?
- Ambiguity resolution: You mention resolving ambiguous outputs as nested rather than crossing. Why is that choice appropriate, and how does it affect evaluation on datasets that contain crossing mentions?

Additional comments (minor)
- Define “multigraph” on first mention. Clarify “state” vs “node,” and “edge” vs “hyperedge.”
- Fix phrasing such as “previously noted by … many previous,” “Solving this task,” “time linear in n,” “used in this experiments,” “pervious,” “published on their website,” “highlighted in bold,” “outperform(s)” for small deltas, and similar style issues.
- Avoid using references as nouns; prefer “Following Lu and Roth (2015) …”
- Ensure consistency of “denoting …” parenthetical style; place footnotes after punctuation.
- Figure 2 should be legible in grayscale; explain why some nodes/edges are grayed and how highlighted edges were selected.
- Explain how the “missing edge” in footnote 2 is determined. Clarify “child nodes” in a hypergraph.
- Specify whether BILOU/BIO claims and other small-number mentions use words (“five”) rather than digits.
- Provide details on the GENIA preprocessing choices, tokenization, and their impact. Standardize references capitalization.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and potentially useful formulation with solid complexity and competitive results. Strengthening motivation, tightening algorithmic details, and adding targeted analysis on crossing entities and effect size would substantially improve the paper.
