Summary:
The paper proposes a multigraph-based approach for detecting overlapping named entities (including nested and crossing entities). The method is presented clearly and includes some discussion of efficiency relative to prior work. Empirically, the approach provides modest improvements over selected baselines.

Strengths:
- Overlapping entities, especially crossing cases, are challenging and under-explored; addressing them is valuable.
- The model is described clearly overall, with attention to computational efficiency compared to a prior system.
- The results are competitive and show incremental gains; the paper is generally well written.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Motivation: The practical importance of nested entities is under-motivated. The introduction’s example (“China” within “Bank of China”) does not make clear why both should be detected. Please articulate concrete use cases and downstream benefits (e.g., relation extraction, coreference, slot filling) and distinguish the value of nested vs. crossing entities.
- Model specification: The decision criterion for including edges is underspecified. Lines 229–233 mention several options for the I^k_t nodes, but it is not clear which edges are actually instantiated and under what conditions. A precise definition of node/edge sets and construction rules is needed.
- Empirical significance: Report effect sizes and statistical significance. The gains appear small (e.g., ~0.2–2 F1 points). Clarify whether such differences are statistically significant and substantively meaningful to users.
- Comparative context: Include stronger baselines for context, such as a simple dictionary-based method that permits overlaps and, if feasible, results from a commercial NER system. This would help calibrate the practical value of the improvements.
- Error analysis and use-case insight: Provide analyses focused on crossing entities (arguably the most interesting subset). How many crossing mentions are recovered that prior methods miss? Which are still missed and why? Is the overall improvement driven primarily by nested or by crossing cases? A brief qualitative error analysis comparing your system to a strong prior model would strengthen the contribution.
- Results interpretation: Avoid overclaiming. Phrases like “outperform” seem strong for sub-point improvements; use more cautious language unless improvements are both statistically significant and practically meaningful.
- Reproducibility: Clarify hyperparameters, feature choices, and any heuristics used to construct the multigraph (e.g., thresholds, pruning). Provide code or pseudo-code for graph construction to eliminate ambiguity.

Overall evaluation:
I find the problem interesting and the approach promising, especially for crossing entities. However, the current draft underspecifies key modeling details, does not convincingly motivate nested entities, and offers limited analysis of crossing cases. The empirical gains are modest and their significance is not established. With stronger motivation, clearer graph construction details, more informative baselines, and a targeted error analysis (especially for crossing entities), the paper would be considerably stronger. My current recommendation is borderline, leaning toward rejection.
