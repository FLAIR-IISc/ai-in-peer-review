- Proposes a multigraph-based method for detecting overlapping (particularly crossing) entities and appears to work empirically.  
- Paper discusses efficiency relative to prior systems and is generally well written, though needs polishing.  
- Problem motivation is weak, especially for nested-entity scenarios (e.g., why detect “China” inside “Bank of China”).  
- Key methodological details are missing: criteria for including edges and which I^k_t node options are actually instantiated.  
- Empirical gains are modest; claiming to “outperform” prior work is overstated without effect size or practical significance analysis.  
- Lacks comparisons to simple baselines (e.g., dictionary lookup) and commercial systems (e.g., Google NLP) to contextualize results.  
- Insufficient focus on crossing entities in evaluation; need per-subset performance, counts of newly found crossing entities, and comparative error analysis.  
- Unclear whether gains come from handling crossing entities or just improved nested detection.  
- Request for more error analysis and concrete examples to illustrate advantages and failures, emphasizing crossing vs. nested cases.  
- Many clarity and presentation issues: define “multigraph” at first mention, fix awkward/ambiguous phrasing, and resolve inconsistent terminology (e.g., “state,” “child nodes,” “CS-edge”).  
- Figure and visualization problems: Figure 2 unclear (why a hypergraph, grayscale visibility, meaning of gray nodes/edges, why both entities should be detected).  
- Numerous algorithmic/implementation clarifications needed: how separator/CS-edge is chosen, how missing edges were determined, what “first order assumption” means, and edge selection procedure.  
- Statistical/reporting issues: spell out effect sizes, justify calling small F1 gains “outperforming,” clarify significance, and explain dataset statistics and low recall claims.  
- Copyediting and formatting errors throughout: typos, awkward citation usage, inconsistent capitalization in references, punctuation/footnote placement, and several grammatical slips.
