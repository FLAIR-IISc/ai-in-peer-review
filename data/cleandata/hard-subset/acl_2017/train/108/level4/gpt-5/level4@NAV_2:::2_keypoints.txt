- Paper proposes a multigraph-based model to recognize overlapping (including crossing and nested) entities in text.

Strengths
- Topic is compelling for handling crossing entities and deciding which mentions are present.
- Method appears to work as intended, producing modest empirical gains.
- Discussion of inference efficiency compared to prior work is appreciated.
- Writing is generally clear though some details need polish.

Weaknesses
- Motivation is underdeveloped, especially the justification for focusing on nested entities.
- Paper fails to make a strong case that nested recognition is important compared to crossing entities.
- Several technical details are underspecified (e.g., precise decision rule for including/excluding edges).
- Unclear which Ikt state options are actually instantiated in the model description.
- Empirical improvements over prior systems are small and may not justify claims of “outperforming.”
- Paper lacks contextualization of effect sizes and practical impact (e.g., what a two-point F1 gain means).
- Missing comparisons to simple dictionary-based methods and commercial NER baselines.
- Analysis does not focus enough on crossings: need per-crossing performance, which crossings were recovered/missed, and why.
- Comparative error analysis versus baselines is needed to strengthen claims.

General discussion
- Reviewer is interested in NER and crossing entities but finds the paper incremental and not fully convincing.
- Recommends deeper error analysis with concrete examples and a clear emphasis on crossing entities.
- Overall assessment is lukewarm with a slight lean toward rejection.

Minor remarks (writing, clarity, and presentation)
- Define “multigraph” on first mention for readers unfamiliar with graph terminology.
- Rephrase awkward sentences (e.g., “Previously noted by … many previous”) and clarify referents like “they.”
- Clarify which task is meant by phrases like “Solving this task.”
- Standardize formatting for Latin abbreviations (e.g., e.g., i.e.) and citation styles.
- Clarify why linear vs cubic time matters for typical sentence lengths and deployments.
- Define terms early: “spurious structures,” “regarded as a chunk,” “state” vs “node,” “child nodes.”
- Specify the decision/algorithmic selection for CS edges and how missing edges are determined (footnote issues).
- Improve Figure 2: explain why it’s a hypergraph, meaning of gray nodes/edges, and edge highlighting choices; justify identifying both short and long spans.
- Fix numerous grammatical/typographical issues (e.g., “pervious” → “previous,” awkward phrases, subject–verb agreement).
- Place footnotes after punctuation and avoid mid-sentence footnote placement.
- Explain experimental protocol details clearly (e.g., what recall refers to, which datasets/splits).
- Report statistical significance and effect sizes rather than loosely saying “significantly better”; include tests/p-values if claiming significance.
- Avoid overstating small F1 differences (e.g., ~0.4 points) without statistical support.
- Clean up table/figure captions and ensure bolding is accompanied by effect-size context.
- Standardize reference/title capitalization and include stable URLs for cited online resources.
