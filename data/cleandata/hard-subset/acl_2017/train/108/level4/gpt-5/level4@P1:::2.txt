Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a multigraph-based structured prediction model for recognizing overlapping named entities, including nested and crossing cases. Unlike prior graph or hypergraph approaches, the model assigns explicit semantics to multiple edges between the same pair of nodes (entity separators), and trains a log-linear model over edges with inference via an inside–outside-style algorithm. The authors argue the model is nondeficient (no spurious structures in the normalization), matches the linear-time complexity of the mention hypergraph approach, and shows modest empirical gains on ACE and GENIA.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A multigraph formulation with edge semantics (entity separators) for overlapping entities that purportedly avoids spurious structures and allows linear-time inference.
2) A theoretical analysis contrasting edge-based vs. state-based paradigms and arguing nondeficiency relative to mention hypergraphs.
3) An empirical comparison on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA showing small F1 improvements over prior systems and comparable runtime.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: Explicitly modeling crossing overlaps is valuable and has been underexplored compared to nested-only methods. The multigraph abstraction is interesting and generalizable.
- Clear attention to efficiency: The paper maintains O(nT) inference and compares complexity with prior work, which is practically relevant.
- Theoretical analysis: The discussion of edge- vs. state-based paradigms and the nondeficiency/spurious structures issue is useful and could inspire further work.
- Solid implementation and benchmarking: Comparisons against mention hypergraphs, semi-CRF, and linear-chain CRF baselines are informative, and the feature design follows prior art to enable fair comparisons.
- Writing quality: The paper is generally well written, and most technical ideas are understandable (though see missing details and minor edits below).

Weaknesses
- Motivation is weak for nested entities: The introduction gives examples but does not convincingly argue why detecting entities nested within others is practically important. The case for crossing entities feels stronger; the paper should better motivate nested recognition with concrete downstream use cases or error analyses.
- Missing algorithmic details: The decision procedure for which edges (entity separators) are present between positions is not clearly specified. In particular, the options listed for Ikt (lines 229–233) do not resolve how the model restricts or enumerates legal transitions, and how the CS/EC/ECS separator is algorithmically chosen. Clarifying constraints and edge inclusion criteria is essential for reproducibility.
- Empirical gains are small and claims are overstated: Improvements are mostly modest (often around 0.4–2 F1). “Outperforms” reads too strong without effect size analysis or significance testing. It remains unclear whether such gains matter for users (e.g., number of additional important entities recovered).
- Missing baselines and external context: A simple dictionary-based approach or a strong commercial system (e.g., Google Cloud NLP) would contextualize the gains, especially for nested/crossing spans. Even if exact comparisons are imperfect, an approximate contrast would help readers judge impact.
- Limited analysis of crossing entities: Given that crossing spans are arguably the most interesting subset, I would expect a focused evaluation: counts of crossing entities, precision/recall on crossing vs. nested vs. non-overlapping, qualitative errors, and whether improvements come from better crossing detection or mostly from nested/non-overlapping cases.
- Error analysis is thin: The paper would benefit from examples where the proposed model succeeds while baselines fail (especially for genuine crossings), cases it misses and why, and how nondeficiency might translate into recall gains in practice.

Questions to authors
- Motivation: What downstream tasks measurably benefit from nested entity detection? Can you provide concrete examples where recovering inner entities (e.g., China in Bank of China) improves end-task performance?
- Edge inclusion: Precisely how are entity separators determined between tokens during inference and training? What constraints ensure only valid combinations (S, E, C, ES, EC, CS, ECS, X) are instantiated? Please formalize the transition system.
- CS-edge selection: In Figure 2 and in the paragraph before Section 4.1, how is the CS separator chosen algorithmically for specific token pairs?
- Crossing-focused results: How many crossing entities exist per dataset? What are P/R/F1 specifically for crossing spans? Which new crossing mentions are detected compared to prior systems? Which are missed and why?
- Effect sizes and significance: Are the reported F1 gains statistically significant? How many additional unique entities are recovered (absolute counts), and are they important categories?
- Baselines: Can you add a dictionary-based baseline and, if feasible, a comparison to a commercial system to contextualize performance?
- Nondeficiency impact: Can you link the nondeficiency claim to measurable improvements (e.g., calibration, recall on overlaps), beyond the qualitative argument?
