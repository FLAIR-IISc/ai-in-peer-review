Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a multigraph-based, log-linear model for recognizing overlapping entities. The key idea is to assign explicit semantics to multiple edges between the same pair of nodes (“entity separators” capturing start/end/continue events), enabling a compact, edge-labeled representation of overlapping structures. Training and inference are carried out with inside–outside and max-product decoding, with O(nT) complexity. Empirically, the model outperforms a strong mention hypergraph (MH) baseline on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA, and also improves on CoNLL 2003 (non-overlapping) despite not modeling cross-type interactions.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A representational reformulation of overlapping mention extraction via an edge-labeled multigraph that eliminates spurious structures present in MH, yielding a nondeficient model with the same asymptotic complexity.
2) Empirical improvements over MH and conventional baselines on standard benchmarks, with analyses by sentence type (with/without overlaps) and training dynamics.
3) A conceptual framing of state-based vs. edge-based modeling, arguing for edge semantics as a useful design axis for overlapping structure prediction.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem with clear practical importance. Overlapping mentions occur frequently in ACE and GENIA; handling them within an efficient structured model is useful for downstream IE systems.
- Theoretical clarification and model property. The paper identifies and analyzes spurious structures in MH and argues the proposed multigraph is nondeficient. While the two models have similar dynamic programs, highlighting and removing deficiency is a meaningful contribution and plausibly explains the improved recall observed in basic (non F1-tuned) settings.
- Solid empirical evaluation and fair comparisons. The authors:
  • Use identical feature sets for MH and their model;
  • Evaluate on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA with standard splits, plus a non-overlapping CoNLL 2003 study;
  • Report precision/recall/F1, with and without F1 tuning via a mention penalty;
  • Analyze performance separately for sentences with and without overlaps, giving insight into where gains come from.
  The model shows consistent improvements over MH (e.g., +2.1 F1 on ACE2004 after F1 tuning) and matches/exceeds the Finkel & Manning parser on GENIA.
- Training behavior and practicality. The convergence plot indicates fewer LBFGS iterations to reach a good objective value (albeit with ~12% slower iterations), suggesting the nondeficient formulation may optimize more stably.
- Clear promise for broader applicability. The edge-based perspective and multigraph construction may generalize to other overlapping structure tasks (e.g., equation parsing).

Weaknesses
1) Insufficient motivation and intuition for “entity separators.” Section 4 lists the eight separator types but does not clearly show what information they encode beyond T/I/O or how they resolve ambiguities present in MH. A small, step-by-step worked example (one sentence, all separator labels, resulting edges, features fired) would greatly clarify the design.
2) Abstract discussion of spurious structures (Sec. 5.2). The explanation relies on inside scores and shared subgraphs but remains hard to follow. Although the supplement gives a toy example, the main text would benefit from a compact, self-contained example contrasting MH’s normalizer (with spurious alternatives) and the multigraph’s normalizer (without), including explicit path/edge scores to show different masses assigned.
3) Limited evidence that nondeficiency drives the gains. The empirical section hypothesizes improved recall stems from removing spurious structures, but no ablation isolates this factor. For instance, reporting calibration metrics, likelihoods, or recall changes when artificially introducing/removing spurious paths would make the causal link more convincing.
4) Expressivity and decoding ambiguity. The paper notes that ambiguous outputs are resolved “as nested rather than crossing” when recovering entities. It is not clear whether the model can, in principle and in decoding, produce crossing mentions, or whether the interpretation step disallows them. This affects the scope of claims (overlapping vs. nested only) and should be clarified and, if possible, quantified.
5) Novelty relative to MH may appear incremental. Much of the machinery (log-linear training, inside–outside, edge features, mention penalty) mirrors MH; the main change is the multigraph/separator representation and the resulting normalizer. Framing the contribution as a principled fix to a known deficiency (with empirical gains) helps, but stronger empirical and analytical evidence for why this fix matters would bolster novelty.
6) Missing implementation details and efficiency constants. The paper claims O(nT) complexity but does not quantify constants induced by eight separator edges per token gap per type. Reporting wall-clock times, memory, parameter counts, and beam/regularization sensitivity would improve practical transparency.

Questions to authors
- Please provide a concrete, end-to-end example showing how separators are assigned between tokens, which edges are added, and how entities are read off, contrasted against the MH representation for the same sentence.
- Can your model produce crossing mentions at decoding time? If not, please adjust claims and discuss implications. If yes, how are crossing outputs evaluated on datasets that contain them?
- Feature design: are templates shared across all separator types, or are there separator-specific templates/parameters? Is there any parameter tying across separators that share semantics (e.g., ES vs. C both connecting I→I)?
- How sensitive are results to the mention penalty tuning? Please report dev-set P/R/F1 curves or sensitivity plots.
- Please quantify constants: average training time per epoch and total convergence time for ACE/GENIA, peak memory, and number of parameters for MH vs. multigraph.
- Since the model factors by entity type, did you try cross-type features? Do they help on ACE?

Additional comments
- Section 3 should explicitly state MH’s issues up front (spurious structures, deficiency) to motivate the new formulation.
- Clarify notation and figures in Section 4 (e.g., Tt, Ikt, Okt; R and X; directed edges vs. the single root hyperedge). Ensure node/edge types are consistent across text and figures.
- The state-vs.-edge paradigm section (5.1) could be tightened and directly connected to concrete benefits (e.g., parameter efficiency, inference or optimization advantages).
- Several references contain placeholders (venues/pages/dates). Please fix bibliographic details.
- Minor style: fix capitalization/hyphenation (e.g., “multigraph-based”), and typos (e.g., “previous” misspelled as “pervious”).

Overall recommendation
This is a well-executed paper on an important task, with a clear representational contribution that removes a known deficiency in MH and yields consistent empirical gains. I lean positive, contingent on improving the clarity of the separator motivation, tightening the spurious-structure analysis in the main text, and clarifying decoding expressivity.
