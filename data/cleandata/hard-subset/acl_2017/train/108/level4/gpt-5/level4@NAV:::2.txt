Review- The manuscript proposes a multigraph-based method (allowing multiple edges between the same two nodes) for detecting potentially overlapping entities.

Strengths:
The problem can be quite compelling, particularly for crossing entities where one must decide which entity is actually referenced in text. The method appears to function, though the empirical gains are not “dramatic.” I appreciate the discussion of efficiency relative to a prior system. Overall, the paper is well written, but it still needs some polishing in places (see minor remarks below).

Weaknesses:
The problem setup is not convincingly motivated. For instance, why is it important to identify China as a separate entity inside Bank of China, as in the introductory example? I understand the case for crossing entities, but what is the practical scenario for nested entities? This should be better motivated to engage the reader. Regarding the approach, in my view some key details are missing: What criterion determines whether an edge is included? In lines 229–233, multiple options for the I^k_t nodes are listed, but it is never specified which edges should be instantiated.
Concerning the empirical evaluation, the results are modestly better than some prior work but not by a large margin. I would hesitate to describe such small gains as “outperforming,” as the paper does. What is the effect size? Does a two-point F1 increase matter to end users? What tangible difference does it make? How many “important” entities are newly discovered that previous methods missed? Also, what would a simple dictionary-based baseline achieve for overlapping cases? Similarly, how would a commercial system (e.g., Google’s NLP Cloud, which should detect and link entities) perform on these datasets? This would help situate the results relative to “commercial” systems.
In the result discussion, I would have preferred more focus on truly crossing entities. How does the system perform there? This subset seems more interesting than purely nested cases. How many additional crossing entities are found compared to before? Which were missed, and why? Are the gains driven by improved nested detection only, or also by better handling of crossing entities? A comparative error analysis against prior systems would strengthen this part.

General Discussion:
I am interested in NER and see value in recognizing crossing entities. However, why should one care about nested entities? The paper does not convincingly motivate this scenario, nor is it illuminated by the evaluation. A discussion of errors and advantages with concrete examples, emphasizing results on crossing entities versus other methods, would have been more persuasive. Overall, I remain lukewarm, with a slight lean toward rejection. It comes across as yet another attempt that does not foreground what I consider the central issue: crossing entities.
