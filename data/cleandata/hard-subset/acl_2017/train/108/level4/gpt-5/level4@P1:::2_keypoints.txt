- Proposes a multigraph-based structured prediction model that assigns explicit semantics to multiple edges (entity separators) to recognize overlapping, nested, and crossing named entities.
- Claims linear-time inference via an inside–outside-style algorithm and argues the model is nondeficient (no spurious structures) versus prior mention hypergraphs.
- Theoretical contribution contrasts edge-based vs. state-based paradigms and argues nondeficiency relative to mention hypergraphs.
- Empirical evaluation on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA shows modest F1 improvements and comparable runtime to prior systems.
- Addresses the underexplored problem of crossing overlaps and presents a generalizable multigraph abstraction.
- Emphasizes efficiency, maintaining O(nT) inference and comparing complexity with prior work.
- Provides a useful theoretical discussion on spurious structures that could inspire further research.
- Solid implementation and benchmarking with fair feature design and comparisons to mention hypergraphs, semi-CRF, and linear-chain CRF baselines.
- Generally well written and most technical ideas are understandable.
- Motivation for nested entity recognition is weak and needs concrete downstream use cases or error analyses.
- Missing algorithmic details on how entity separator edges are chosen and which transitions are legal, harming reproducibility.
- Empirical gains are small (often ~0.4–2 F1) and claims of “outperforms” are overstated without effect size or significance testing.
- Lacks baselines and external context (e.g., dictionary-based or commercial systems) to contextualize gains on nested/crossing spans.
- Limited focused analysis of crossing entities; missing counts and P/R/F1 for crossing vs. nested vs. non-overlapping spans.
- Error analysis is thin; needs examples of successes/failures, especially for genuine crossings, and links between nondeficiency and practical recall gains.
- Requests motivation evidence: what downstream tasks benefit from nested entity detection and examples showing inner-entity utility.
- Asks for precise formalization of edge inclusion: how separators are determined and what constraints ensure valid combinations.
- Wants algorithmic clarification on CS-edge selection (Figure 2 and related text).
- Requests dataset statistics and focused metrics for crossing entities and examples of new/missed crossing mentions relative to baselines.
- Seeks effect size/significance testing and absolute counts of additional entities recovered, with importance assessment by category.
- Suggests adding dictionary-based and, if feasible, commercial-system baselines for context.
- Asks for empirical linkage between nondeficiency and measurable improvements (e.g., calibration, recall on overlaps).
- Minor suggestions: define multigraph early, clarify “state” vs. “node,” explain “child nodes” in a hypergraph, and spell out small numbers in text.
- Writing/style edits: fix awkward phrasing, avoid italics for “e.g.,” clarify references used as nouns, and replace vague phrases throughout.
- Figures/notation: make Figure 2 interpretable in grayscale, explain gray highlighting and edge selection, and justify detecting both entities shown.
- Footnotes and punctuation: place footnotes after punctuation and justify claims made in footnotes (e.g., why an edge is “missing”).
- Technical clarifications: reference mention hypergraph specifics, clarify first-order assumption, explain why nondeficiency might increase recall, and fix wording about convergence.
- Results reporting: report effect sizes and statistical significance and temper claims where F1 differences are very small.
- References: harmonize title casing and citation formatting across the paper.
