**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper introduces a novel multigraph-based model for named entity recognition (NER), specifically designed to handle overlapping entities. The core idea is to model the problem using states that indicate whether a token is inside or outside an entity of a certain type, and connecting these states with a set of eight distinct, semantically meaningful edges called "entity separators" (e.g., Start, End, Continue, and their combinations). This formulation naturally represents complex overlapping structures. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and intuitive multigraph-based model for overlapping NER. The use of explicit "entity separator" edges is an elegant way to factor the problem, allowing multiple entity decisions to be modeled at each token boundary.
2.  A significant theoretical analysis that differentiates the proposed model from a key state-of-the-art baseline (Lu and Roth, 2015). The authors demonstrate that their model is "nondeficient," meaning its probabilistic objective does not suffer from summing over "spurious structures" that exist in the baseline's normalization term.
3.  A thorough empirical evaluation on three standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA) with overlapping entities, plus one non-overlapping dataset (CoNLL2003). The results show consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines, including a reimplementation of the model from Lu and Roth (2015) using an identical feature set.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Model Formulation:** The proposed model is well-conceived and clearly presented. The concept of using "entity separators" as typed edges in a multigraph is a highly intuitive way to decompose the complex task of identifying overlapping spans. It provides a clean alternative to more complex formalisms like constituency parsing or hypergraphs. The authors' framing of this as an "edge-based paradigm" is an insightful conceptual contribution that helps position the work in the broader context of structured prediction.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Justification:** A major strength is the analysis of the "spurious structures" issue in the mention hypergraph model. The argument that the baseline's normalization term `Z(x)` overcounts by summing over multiple interpretations of the same subgraph is convincing and points to a fundamental flaw in its probabilistic formulation. By showing that their model is "nondeficient" and correctly computes the partition function, the authors provide a strong theoretical motivation for their approach, moving beyond a purely empirical "bake-off".
3.  **Comprehensive and Fair Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are rigorous and well-designed. The authors compare against the most relevant state-of-the-art model and ensure a fair comparison by reimplementing the baseline and using the exact same feature sets for both models. The performance gains are consistent across multiple datasets with varying characteristics. The additional analysis on sentences with and without overlapping entities, as well as on a purely non-overlapping dataset (CoNLL2003), provides valuable insights into the model's behavior and robustness.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The model is explained clearly with helpful figures (Fig. 2, 4) that effectively illustrate the core ideas and differences from the baseline. The logical flow from model description to theoretical analysis to empirical results is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The link between the "nondeficient" property and empirical gains is not conclusively established.** The authors hypothesize that their model's higher recall stems from being more "confident" due to its nondeficient nature. While this is a compelling narrative, it remains speculative. The performance gains could also be attributed to other factors, such as the model's specific factorization via the 8 separators providing a better inductive bias or a smoother optimization landscape (as suggested by the faster convergence in Figure 7). The paper would be stronger if it could provide more direct evidence linking the theoretical advantage to the empirical improvements beyond speculation.
2.  **Unsubstantiated claims about the "state-based" equivalent.** In Section 5.1, the authors rightly acknowledge that their model could be reformulated as a state-based sequence model where the "separators" are labels on the transitions between words. They dismiss this alternative by claiming it would introduce "unnecessary dependencies" and be "less concise," but this claim is not backed by evidence. A more detailed discussion of what these dependencies are and why they are problematic would be necessary to establish the superiority of the "edge-based paradigm" as a fundamentally different and better approach, rather than just a different but equivalent representational choice.
3.  **Lack of clarity on handling crossing vs. nested entities during decoding.** Footnote 3 mentions a crucial implementation detail: "we resolved ambiguous structures by considering them as nested entities instead of crossing entities." This raises important questions. The paper's theoretical analysis of spurious structures highlights the ambiguity between crossing and nested entities as a key problem in the baseline model. If the proposed model resolves this ambiguity by a fixed heuristic at decoding time, it is unclear if it is truly learning to distinguish these cases. This policy could potentially bias the model and limit its ability to correctly identify genuine crossing entities. More details on how often such ambiguities arise and the impact of this decoding strategy are needed.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the decoding heuristic in footnote 3: Could you elaborate on how "ambiguous structures" are defined and detected? How frequently do they occur in your experiments? Does this heuristic of defaulting to a nested interpretation affect the model's performance on datasets that may contain true crossing entities?
2.  Regarding the state-based equivalent of your model: Could you provide a concrete example of an "unnecessary dependency" that a first-order sequence model over separator-states would model, but which your multigraph formulation avoids? Does your graph structure implicitly enforce certain hard constraints on adjacent separators?
3.  In Table 4, your model underperforms the MH baseline on the subset of GENIA sentences containing overlapping entities. You suggest this is due to sparse training data for overlap in GENIA. Could this result also be related to the nature of entities in that corpus (e.g., the discontiguous entities mentioned in Appendix D) interacting differently with your model's factorization compared to the more flexible hypergraph structure?
