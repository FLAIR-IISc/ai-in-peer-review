**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping entities in text. The model uses multigraphs to represent entities and their combinations, allowing for the recognition of overlapping entities. The authors claim that their model outperforms previous models on standard datasets containing overlapping and non-overlapping entities. The main contributions of the paper are:

1. A novel multigraph-based model for predicting overlapping entities.
2. Empirical results showing that the model achieves higher F1-scores compared to previous models on multiple datasets.
3. Theoretical analysis showing that the model does not present the spurious structures issue in its inference procedure and is therefore non-deficient.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novel approach**: The paper proposes a new approach to recognizing overlapping entities using multigraphs, which is a significant departure from traditional methods that use simple graphs or hypergraphs.
2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper presents strong empirical results on standard datasets, showing that the proposed model outperforms previous models in terms of F1-score.
3. **Theoretical analysis**: The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the model, showing that it does not suffer from the spurious structures issue that affects previous models.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited comparison**: The paper only compares the proposed model with a few previous models, and it would be useful to see a more comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models.
2. **Lack of interpretability**: The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the multigraph-based model works, making it difficult to understand the underlying mechanisms.
3. **Overfitting**: The paper does not provide any analysis of overfitting, which is a common issue in machine learning models.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the multigraph-based model works, including how the entity separators are used to represent overlapping entities?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting in the proposed model?
3. Can you provide a more comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models, including those that use different approaches to recognizing overlapping entities?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed model and its empirical results. However, there are some minor issues with formatting and grammar that need to be addressed. Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of named entity recognition and is a strong candidate for publication.
