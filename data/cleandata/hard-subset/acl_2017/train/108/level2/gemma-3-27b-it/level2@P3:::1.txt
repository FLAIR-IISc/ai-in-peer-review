The paper proposes a multigraph-based model for overlapping entity recognition, arguing it addresses limitations of existing approaches like hypergraphs. The primary contributions are: 1) a novel multigraph representation for overlapping structures, 2) empirical demonstration of improved F1-scores on standard datasets, and 3) a theoretical analysis showing the model avoids a “spurious structures” issue present in a baseline model while maintaining comparable inference complexity.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Multigraph Representation:** The use of multigraphs to explicitly represent different edge semantics for overlapping entities is a potentially valuable contribution. It offers a way to move beyond limitations of simple or hypergraphs where representing overlapping relationships can be awkward or inefficient. The distinction between different edge types (entity separators) and their assigned semantics is well articulated.
2. **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent performance improvements over the compared mention hypergraph baseline on the ACE and GENIA datasets. The results are clearly presented in tables, and the inclusion of results with and without hyperparameter tuning provides a good assessment of model performance. The analysis demonstrating the benefit on sentences *containing* overlapping entities is also useful.
3. **Theoretical Analysis of Spurious Structures:** The identification and explanation of the “spurious structures” issue in the mention hypergraph model is insightful. The argument that the proposed model avoids this problem is a strong theoretical justification for the proposed approach and is well explained.  The detailed explanation in Appendix A further clarifies this point.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The figures (particularly Figures 2 and 4) effectively illustrate the multigraph representation and its components.  The connection to prior work is adequately established.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Baselines:** While the comparison to the mention hypergraph is strong, the set of baselines is relatively limited. Comparing against a standard linear-chain CRF (as done) is helpful, but including other state-of-the-art NER models (even those not explicitly designed for overlap) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
2. **Feature Set Details:** The description of the features used is somewhat brief. While the paper refers to features used in prior work, a more detailed explanation of the specific features employed in *this* work would improve reproducibility and understanding. The feature engineering aspect feels slightly underspecified.
3. **Impact of Tokenization:** The preprocessing details in Appendix D, specifically regarding handling hyphenated words in GENIA, suggest a potential issue with the data.  Acknowledging how this preprocessing might affect results (and potentially comparing against a baseline without this preprocessing) would strengthen the analysis.
4. **Generalizability:** While the empirical results are positive, the improvement is not dramatic. It remains unclear how well this model would generalize to datasets with significantly different characteristics (e.g., different entity types, domain, or levels of overlap). The discussion of this limitation would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed description of the features used in your model, including specific examples of how they are represented?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the choice of hyperparameters (other than the regularization parameter)? Were any other hyperparameters tuned, and if so, what values were used?
3. Given the preprocessing step involving splitting hyphenated words in the GENIA dataset, did you consider evaluating the model on the original, un-preprocessed data to assess the impact of this step?
4. In Section 5.2 you describe a link between the edge-based and state-based paradigms. Could you elaborate on the practical implications of this relationship for future model development?



**Additional Comments:**

- The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational cost associated with the multigraph representation and inference, particularly in comparison to simpler models.
- The authors should consider making the code and datasets publicly available to further facilitate reproducibility and encourage future research.
- It would be interesting to see an ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., different edge types).
