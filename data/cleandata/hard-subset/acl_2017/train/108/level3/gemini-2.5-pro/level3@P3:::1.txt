### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the important problem of overlapping named entity recognition (NER), where entities can be nested or crossing. The authors propose a novel multigraph-based model that represents entities and their relationships through a set of eight "entity separators" (e.g., Start, End, Continue) defined on the edges between word positions. This approach is framed as an "edge-based" paradigm, in contrast to traditional "state-based" models like CRFs. The authors' primary theoretical claim is that their model is "non-deficient" because it avoids the "spurious structures" issue present in the state-of-the-art Mention Hypergraph (MH) model, where a single subgraph can ambiguously represent multiple distinct entity configurations. Empirically, the proposed model achieves new state-of-the-art results on several standard benchmarks, including ACE2004, ACE2005, and GENIA, outperforming the MH baseline.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **A novel and effective model for overlapping NER that achieves significant empirical improvements** over a strong state-of-the-art baseline on multiple standard datasets. The consistent gains in F1-score are the paper's most significant contribution.
2.  **A theoretical analysis that identifies a potential weakness in the Mention Hypergraph model**, termed the "spurious structures" issue. This provides a compelling motivation for the proposed multigraph model and offers a new perspective for analyzing structured prediction models of this type.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a significant and practical NLP problem.** Standard sequence labeling approaches to NER cannot handle overlapping entities, yet such structures are prevalent in many important domains like biomedical text (GENIA) and news (ACE). This work makes a solid contribution to a well-established and challenging task.

2.  **Strong and convincing empirical results.** The proposed model demonstrates consistent and significant performance gains over strong baselines across three different datasets. For instance, the F1-score improvement of 2.1 points on ACE2004 over the Mention Hypergraph (MH) model is impressive. The authors also provide a useful breakdown of performance on sentences with and without overlapping entities, further strengthening their empirical case.

3.  **Novel conceptualization of the problem.** The idea of using explicit semantics on multiple edges between nodes (the "entity separators") is an interesting departure from typical graphical models. The framing of this as an "edge-based" paradigm and the identification of the "spurious structures" issue in the MH model provide a valuable, if not fully proven, theoretical insight that could inspire future work in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The motivation for the new model is poorly structured.** The paper introduces the Mention Hypergraph (MH) model in Section 3 but does not critique it or identify any specific shortcomings. The core motivation for the multigraph model—the "spurious structures" issue—is not discussed until Section 5.2. This leaves the reader wondering throughout Section 4 why the new, more complex model is necessary. The paper would be much stronger if the limitations of the MH model were clearly articulated upfront to motivate the design choices of the proposed multigraph model.

2.  **The role and novelty of the "entity separators" are unclear.** In Section 4, the paper introduces a set of 8 separators (ECS, EC, CS, etc.) that form the core of the model. However, the rationale for this specific set is not well-justified. It is not clear what unique modeling capacity these separators provide that could not be achieved by a more conventional, state-based sequence model that tags the transitions *between* words. The authors briefly dismiss this alternative in Section 5.1 as being "less intuitive" and leading to "unnecessary dependencies," but this claim is not substantiated. Without a clearer justification or an ablation study, the separators feel somewhat arbitrary.

3.  **The key theoretical claim about "spurious structures" is underdeveloped and poorly illustrated.** Section 5.2 argues that the MH model is "deficient" because one of its subgraphs can correspond to multiple distinct entity interpretations (e.g., nested vs. crossing), which corrupts the partition function. This is the paper's most important theoretical argument, yet it is explained in a highly abstract manner without a single concrete example or diagram in the main text. To make this point compelling, the authors needed to walk the reader through a simple case, showing exactly how a specific MH subgraph is ambiguous and how their multigraph representation resolves this ambiguity. While an example is provided in the appendix, this is too critical to the core argument to be relegated from the main paper.

4.  **The connection between the theoretical analysis and the empirical gains is speculative.** The paper suggests that its empirical improvements (particularly higher recall) are a result of being a "non-deficient" model that is "more confident in its predictions." However, this link is never proven. The performance gains could equally stem from other factors, such as the specific parameterization enabled by the 8 separators, which might allow for a more expressive feature representation than the MH model. The analysis fails to disentangle these potential causes, leaving the central "why" of the paper's success unanswered.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your core theoretical argument rests on the "spurious structures" issue in the Mention Hypergraph model. Could you provide a simplified, concrete example with a diagram in the main text to illustrate how a single MH subgraph can ambiguously represent both crossing and nested entities, and show how your multigraph model resolves this?
2.  The set of 8 "entity separators" is central to your model's design. Could you provide more intuition for why this specific set is necessary? Have you considered an ablation study to measure the impact of removing some of the more complex separators (e.g., ECS, EC, CS)?
3.  You speculate that the model's "non-deficient" nature is the cause of its improved recall. Can you provide a more direct analysis to support this claim? For example, does an error analysis show that the MH model is more prone to confusing nested and crossing structures than your model?
