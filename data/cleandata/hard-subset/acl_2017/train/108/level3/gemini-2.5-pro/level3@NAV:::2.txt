**Review of "A Multigraph-based Model for Overlapping Entity Recognition"**

**Summary & Contributions**

This submission introduces a novel multigraph-based model for the task of overlapping named entity recognition, a problem encompassing both nested and crossing entities. The authors posit that their approach is more efficient and robust than prior hypergraph models by avoiding a "spurious structures" issue during inference. As I see them, the primary contributions are: (1) Focusing on the important and under-explored problem of overlapping entities, particularly the more challenging case of crossing entities. (2) Proposing a novel multigraph-based structured prediction framework that achieves modest empirical improvements over a state-of-the-art baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  The problem addressed is both significant and relevant. The recognition of overlapping entities is a known limitation of standard NER models, and the paper's focus on this challenging area, especially crossing entities, is a valuable contribution to the field.
2.  The proposed multigraph model is a novel technical contribution. It is empirically demonstrated to achieve consistent, albeit small, F1-score improvements over a strong hypergraph-based baseline across several benchmark datasets, suggesting the approach is sound.
3.  The paper provides a useful theoretical analysis comparing the proposed model to a key predecessor. The discussion of the "spurious structures" issue is insightful and effectively demonstrates a theoretical advantage of their formulation, which is further supported by faster training convergence.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The primary weakness is the significant disconnect between the paper's motivation and its empirical evaluation. While crossing entities are highlighted as a key challenge, the experiments and analysis do not sufficiently demonstrate that the model specifically improves their detection. It remains unclear whether the reported gains are attributable to this difficult sub-task or simply to identifying nested entities (e.g., "China" within "Bank of China"), a problem for which the paper provides weak motivation and limited practical justification.
2.  The empirical improvements are marginal and overstated. Gains of 1-2 F1 points are presented as significant outperformance without any discussion of statistical significance or effect size. It is difficult to assess whether these small gains have any practical impact. The evaluation is further weakened by the absence of important baselines, such as simple dictionary-based methods or widely-used commercial systems, which would help contextualize the results.
3.  The paper suffers from a lack of technical clarity that hinders reproducibility. Key algorithmic details—such as the decision criterion for including edges in the multigraph—are missing. The overall presentation requires substantial polishing; several terms are used without definition, figures are difficult to interpret (especially in black-and-white), and numerous grammatical and stylistic errors detract from the paper's clarity.
