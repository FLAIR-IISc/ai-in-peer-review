Summary: Proposes a multigraph edge-semantics structured prediction model for overlapping (nested/crossing) NER with log-linear training and max-product decoding, claiming modest F1 gains and nondeficiency vs. mention hypergraphs.

Main contributions:
- Multigraph-based edge-semantics formulation that preserves linear-time inference as an alternative to hypergraph models.
- Empirical evidence of small F1 improvements and training-efficiency benefits over mention hypergraphs on ACE/GGENIA.
- Theoretical claim that the model avoids spurious structures present in mention hypergraphs.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important and under-supported problem (overlapping/crossing entities) in NER.
- Shows consistent modest F1 improvements on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA, often via higher recall.
- Maintains O(nT) inference complexity and may converge in fewer optimization iterations.
- Provides clearer edge semantics and a nondeficiency argument that is conceptually clean.
- Generally well-written with useful discussion of state vs. edge paradigms.

Weaknesses:
- Weak motivation and unclear practical importance for nested vs. crossing entities; decoding defaults to nested which undermines crossing claims.
- Missing algorithmic details about which separator edges are used, node variants active in inference, edge-combination constraints, and exact decoding/entity reconstruction rules.
- Empirical gains are small (0.4–2.1 F1) and claims of “outperforms/significant” lack statistical testing and effect-size discussion.
- No analysis of practical impact (how many important mentions recovered, downstream task impact).
- Lacks contextual baselines (simple heuristics or commercial NER APIs) to contextualize absolute performance.
- Experiments and error analysis do not isolate crossing entities, so it’s unclear whether gains address crossings or just nested cases.
- Limited qualitative error analysis and absence of side-by-side examples with baselines.

Questions to authors (rebuttal requests):
- Are all eight separator edges enabled at every token gap/type or are there constraints/pruning heuristics?
- Which node variants (Ikt, Okt, Tt) and Tt connections are active during inference at each position?
- What are the exact MPE decoding and entity reconstruction rules, especially when multiple separators map to the same state pair?
- How many crossing mentions exist in ACE/GENIA tests, and can you report results on crossing-only subsets with examples?
- Provide statistical significance tests (bootstrap/randomization) and confidence intervals for claimed improvements.
- Quantify practical impact: additional correct entities per 1k tokens and their importance by type/length/frequency; any downstream evaluation?
- Provide end-to-end wall-clock training/decoding times and memory usage vs. mention hypergraphs to confirm efficiency claims.
- Include simple dictionary/heuristic and commercial NER baselines for context; report their performance on non-overlapping subsets.
- Which feature templates are tied to which separators and any ablation results showing which separators/features drive gains?

Additional comments / minor issues:
- Define “multigraph” at first mention and cite a graph-theory source.
- Clarify and standardize terminology/notation: “spurious structures,” “state” vs. “node,” separator semantics, and edge selection labels.
- Improve figures (Fig.2 unclear in grayscale); label edges and node types directly and explain highlighted elements.
- Fix typos/grammar, standardize reference formatting, and ensure consistent punctuation around equations and footnotes.
- Report exact effect sizes, precision/recall breakdowns per dataset and sentence type, all hyperparameters, stopping criteria, and random seeds.

Overall recommendation:
- Lean reject: interesting and promising modeling idea but modest empirical gains, insufficient algorithmic clarity, limited crossing-focused evaluation, and inadequate practical impact analysis.
