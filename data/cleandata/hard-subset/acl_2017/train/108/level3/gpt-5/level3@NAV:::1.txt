Summary
The paper tackles overlapping entity recognition by replacing hypergraph structures with a multigraph whose edges encode “entity separators.” The model retains linear-time inference, claims to be nondeficient (no spurious structures in the partition function), and empirically improves F1 over strong baselines on ACE04/05 and GENIA.

Main contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
1) Strong empirical improvements on standard overlapping-NER benchmarks with careful, feature-matched comparisons to Mention Hypergraphs (MH) and other baselines.
2) A multigraph/edge-separator formulation with linear-time training/inference and an argument that it avoids spurious structures present in MH.
3) A generalizable edge-centric modeling perspective for overlapping structures.

Strengths
- Empirical significance: Consistent F1 gains over MH and other baselines across datasets; additional analyses on overlapping vs. non-overlapping subsets and CoNLL suggest broad utility.
- Methodological clarity: Training/inference are presented cleanly; complexity matches MH; experiments control for features and tuning, improving fairness of comparisons.
- Theoretical angle: The nondeficiency argument is an interesting perspective on MH’s partition function and could matter for confidence/recall, aligning with the paper’s recall gains.
- Practicality: The model appears simpler to implement and tune than pipelines or parsers for nested entities; promised code release aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient motivation vs. MH (Section 3): The paper does not concretely enumerate MH’s specific failure modes that the multigraph fixes; examples are needed.
- Separator design (Section 4): Unclear why eight separators are necessary/sufficient and what they add beyond T/I/O-style state semantics; no ablations on separator set or tying.
- Analysis gaps (Sections 5.1/5.2): The superiority argument is largely conceptual; the spurious-structure discussion is abstract and lacks sentence-level, side-by-side examples quantifying prevalence and impact on learning/inference.
- Missing ablations: No studies on features vs. structure, mention penalty, decoding policies, or turning nondeficiency on/off to link theory to gains.
- Limited novelty: Framed as a variant of MH with edge semantics; the extent of conceptual novelty over prior edge-centric MH is modest absent deeper analysis.

Questions to authors
- Please enumerate concrete MH deficiencies on real sentences that your model resolves; provide minimal counterexamples.
- Quantify how often spurious structures arise in MH on your data and correlate with observed error types.
- Provide ablations: reduced separator sets; tying edges; feature-only vs. structure gains; effect of mention penalty.
- Clarify decoding disambiguation (nested vs. crossing) and its impact on metrics.
- Report runtime and memory vs. MH in wall-clock terms and per-iteration costs.
