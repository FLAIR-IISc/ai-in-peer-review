- Summary: Proposes a multigraph-based structured model using “entity separators” with log-linear inside–outside inference, claiming O(nT) complexity and nondeficiency, and shows empirical gains on ACE2004/2005, GENIA, and CoNLL03.
- Contribution — empirical: Consistent improvements over mention hypergraphs (MH) and strong non-overlapping baselines with careful feature parity and tuning.
- Contribution — formalization: Introduces a multigraph representation assigning semantics to multiple edges (separators) enabling compact encoding of overlapping entities and Viterbi-style decoding.
- Contribution — analysis: Argues the model is nondeficient (avoids spurious structures) and reports faster convergence in iterations.
- Strength: Strong empirical results across datasets, with useful split analysis showing gains on both overlapping and non-overlapping sentences.
- Strength: Clear formalization and inference description, preserving O(nT) complexity and using identical features for fair comparison.
- Strength: Addresses a practically important problem (overlapping entities) relevant to ACE/GENIA and biomedical domains, offering a compact, trainable alternative.
- Strength: Raises the spurious-structure issue and provides convergence observations suggesting cleaner gradients.
- Weakness: Lacks concrete motivation contrasting MH’s practical failure modes and frequency of those failures in real data.
- Weakness: The necessity and minimality of the eight separator types are not justified or explicitly mapped to MH edge combinations.
- Weakness: Paradigm claim (state vs. edge) is conceptual without empirical ablations demonstrating the edge-based advantage.
- Weakness: Spurious-structure analysis is abstract and relegated to the supplement; needs a self-contained toy example and quantification in the main text.
- Weakness: Mechanism of improvement is unclear; no fine-grained error analysis by span length, overlap depth, boundaries, or separator usage.
- Weakness: Limited baseline breadth—more comparisons or discussion of other overlapping-entity or span/neural models are needed.
- Question: Provide concrete MH failure examples from development and their frequency across datasets.
- Question: Explain what the 8 separators express that a state-based gap-labeling CRF cannot, and include ablations collapsing separators.
- Question: Move a worked toy example showing MH overcounting into the main text and quantify prevalence of spurious subgraphs in datasets.
- Question: How often does enforced nested decoding affect results per dataset, and does it bias outputs relative to annotations?
- Question: Report wall-clock training time, memory usage vs. MH, and per-sentence inference time.
- Additional comment: Writing is generally good but has minor notation inconsistencies and figure/legend issues.
- Additional comment: Reproducibility is strong (features/hyperparams); recommend releasing code and tokenization scripts.
- Additional comment: Request statistical significance testing on main results.
- Additional comment: A qualitative error analysis showing typical corrected errors would clarify model behavior.
- Overall: Solid and useful empirical contribution but would be stronger with clearer motivation against MH, concrete nondeficiency demonstrations, and ablations validating separator design.
