Summary
The paper tackles overlapping entity recognition by introducing a multigraph-based structured model that assigns explicit semantics to multiple edges between the same node pair (“entity separators”). It follows a log-linear formulation with inside–outside inference, claims the same O(nT) complexity as mention hypergraphs (MH; Lu & Roth, 2015), and argues it is nondeficient (free of spurious structures). Experiments on ACE2004/2005, GENIA, and CoNLL03 report consistent gains over MH and other non-overlapping baselines. The paper is generally clear and well organized, with minor presentation issues.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical advance: Consistent improvements over MH and strong non-overlapping baselines on multiple benchmarks, with careful feature parity and tuning.
2) Modeling formalization: A multigraph representation with edge-semantics (“entity separators”) enabling compact encoding of overlapping structures and Viterbi-style decoding with inside–outside.
3) Analysis claim: Argument that the model is nondeficient (no spurious structures) unlike MH, plus observations on training convergence.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across datasets: The approach beats MH on ACE04/05 and GENIA, and also improves on CoNLL03 despite having no overlapping entities. The split analysis (sentences with vs. without overlap) is useful, and suggests the model helps both cases, with clearest gains on ACE where overlap is frequent.
- Clear formalization and inference story: The mapping from separators to edges, dynamic programming (inside/outside), and preservation of O(nT) are described coherently. Using identical feature sets for fair comparisons is appreciated.
- Useful problem with real impact: Overlapping entities are common in ACE/GENIA and in clinical/biomedical domains. A compact, trainable alternative to parsing-based solutions is valuable.
- Nondeficiency claim and convergence observation: The spurious-structure discussion, while needing more concreteness, motivates why gradients may be cleaner. The convergence figure indicates fewer iterations to reach good optima (albeit with slightly slower iterations).

Weaknesses
- Missing, concrete motivation vs. MH (Section 3): The paper does not spell out the precise practical shortcomings of MH that motivate the new model. Which predictions are mis-scored or invalid in MH? How often do those cases arise in real data? Without specific failure modes, the leap to a new formalism feels under-motivated.
- Separators are not justified beyond T/I/O (Section 4): It is unclear why introducing 8 separator types is necessary and what they capture that cannot be represented by a richer set of states (e.g., gap labeling) or straightforward edge feature augmentation in MH. A table mapping each separator to MH edge combinations exists implicitly; making it explicit, and arguing minimality/necessity, would help.
- State vs. edge paradigm (Section 5.1) is conceptual but not evidential: The section argues paradigms, but does not demonstrate empirically why the proposed edge-based instantiation is superior. A direct ablation—e.g., a linear-chain “gap-labeling” CRF where separators are states; a variant that collapses some separators (ES vs. C); or a single-edge variant—would make the point concrete.
- Spurious structures analysis is too abstract (Section 5.2): The current discussion is hard to verify and connect to practice. The example is relegated to the supplement; the main text should contain a small, self-contained toy sentence demonstrating (1) a subgraph shared by multiple distinct interpretations in MH, (2) the exact terms that inflate Z(x), (3) why the numerator never includes those variants, and (4) how the proposed model avoids this. Quantifying the prevalence of such cases in ACE/GENIA and correlating them with recall gains would substantiate the nondeficiency claim.
- Why and how the model wins remains unclear: While gains are solid, the mechanism is not demonstrated. For instance, does the model improve recall on long, nested spans, or precision on boundary decisions with adjacent entities? A fine-grained error analysis (by entity length, overlap depth, boundary types, separator usage) and ablations on separator sets would make the “why” convincing.
- Baseline breadth: Beyond MH and F&M’09 (GENIA), there is limited comparison to other overlapping-entity methods. Even if exact contemporaries are few, discussing why neural sequence or span models from the same period are inapplicable (or including a simple neural span baseline) would strengthen the positioning.

Questions to authors
- Section 3: Please enumerate concrete MH failure cases you observed in development (with examples) that the multigraph fixes. How often do they occur across datasets?
- Section 4: What can the 8 separators express that a state-based gap-labeling CRF with separator-as-state cannot, under similar first-order assumptions? Could you include an ablation collapsing some separators (e.g., ES with C), and report the impact on F1 and speed?
- Section 5.2: Can you move a fully worked toy example into the main text showing the exact overcounting in MH’s Z(x) (with edge weights) and the absence of spurious terms in your model? Also, quantify how frequently spurious-structure-amenable subgraphs appear in ACE/GENIA and whether they correlate with the observed recall boost.
- Decoding and ambiguity: You resolve ambiguous structures as nested, not crossing. How many cases does this affect per dataset? Does enforcing nestedness at decode time bias results compared to dataset annotations?
- Efficiency: Please report wall-clock training time and memory vs. MH (not just iterations), and per-sentence inference time on test.

Additional comments
- Writing/clarity: The paper is generally well-written. Minor issues: inconsistent notation (e.g., Tkt vs. Tt), and figures could include a legend explicitly mapping separators to edges. Some references have formatting glitches.
- Reproducibility: Good detail on features and hyperparameters. Consider releasing code upon camera-ready, and including scripts for tokenization quirks in GENIA.
- Statistical significance: Please add significance testing on main tables.
- Error analysis: A small qualitative section illustrating typical corrected errors (with separators invoked) would substantially improve clarity about the model’s behavior.

Overall
This is a useful contribution with solid empirical gains on an important problem. The paper would be considerably stronger with clearer motivation vis-à-vis MH, concrete demonstrations of nondeficiency and its practical consequences, and ablations validating the necessity of the separator design.
