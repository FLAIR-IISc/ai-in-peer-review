Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a structured prediction model for overlapping entity recognition based on multigraphs. The key idea is to assign explicit semantics to multiple edges between the same pair of nodes (entity separators), enabling representation and scoring of overlapping (including crossing) entities. The model is trained with a log-linear objective and uses inside–outside style inference with overall O(nT) complexity. Experiments on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA show small but consistent improvements over the mention hypergraph baseline and competitive results against prior tree/semi-CRF models.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of importance:
1) A concrete multigraph-based formalism for overlapping entity recognition that is nondeficient (avoids spurious structures) while retaining O(nT) inference.
2) An empirical study across three standard datasets demonstrating small F1 gains over a strong mention-hypergraph baseline and competitive performance versus prior parsers.
3) A clear discussion of the “edge-based paradigm” and its relationship to state-based models, which may inform future modeling of overlapping structures.

Strengths
- Problem relevance and scope: Overlapping entities (nested and crossing) are common in ACE and GENIA; a model that natively supports them is practically valuable. The paper targets both nested and crossing cases, unlike tree parsers that cannot handle crossing spans.
- Sound formalization and complexity: The multigraph construction with typed “entity separators” is intuitive and compact. The inside–outside style inference in O(nT) matches the best prior complexity while addressing the spurious-structure deficiency documented for mention hypergraphs. This is a substantive modeling clarification.
- Empirical effectiveness across datasets: The model yields consistent improvements over the mention hypergraph baseline: +2.1 F1 (ACE2004), +1.3 (ACE2005), and ≈+0.4 (GENIA). The analysis separating sentences with and without overlaps is helpful, and results on CoNLL (non-overlapping) indicate the approach remains competitive even when overlaps are absent.
- Clarity of the paradigm discussion: The state-based vs. edge-based framing is useful. The examples showing how edges encode role combinations highlight why multigraphs can be more concise than ad-hoc state expansions.
- Implementation details and training behavior: Using identical feature sets for fair comparisons and reporting convergence behavior (fewer L-BFGS iterations albeit slightly slower per-iteration) make the empirical section more credible.

Weaknesses
- Motivation and impact underdeveloped: The paper gives limited motivation for why recognizing nested/crossing entities matters downstream. Examples like “China” in “Bank of China” are common in the literature but the paper does not quantify how many practically important mentions are recovered only because overlaps are modeled, nor any downstream benefit (e.g., relation extraction, coreference).
- Missing/ambiguous algorithmic details: Several core details are underspecified, making reproduction difficult. For example: the exact decision criterion for choosing among multiple parallel edges (entity separators) between I/O states; how constraints ensure globally consistent separator sequences; how ties and ambiguous subgraphs are decoded; and precise definitions of node variants (Ikt, Okt, Tt) and their connectivity for all separator types.
- Modest effect sizes and lack of statistical testing: Improvements are small (often ≤2 F1). Claims of “outperforming previous models” should be tempered by effect-size discussion and significance tests. The paper notes recall/precision tradeoffs (e.g., GENIA overlapping subset), but does not analyze whether improvements are practically meaningful.
- Insufficient analysis of crossing vs. nested: Although the model supports crossing entities, experiments do not report separate metrics for nested vs. crossing, nor error breakdowns showing where gains arise. It remains unclear whether the improvements mainly stem from nested cases already handled reasonably by prior work.
- Baseline context is narrow: Comparisons are limited to a mention hypergraph, a linear-chain CRF, and a tree parser. Including simpler dictionary/heuristic systems and widely used toolkits (e.g., commercial APIs) would better contextualize practical gains, or at least a strong neural non-overlapping baseline plus a simple overlap-merging heuristic.
- Limited qualitative error analysis: The paper would benefit from concrete examples where the multigraph model succeeds and the baselines fail (and vice versa), especially for crossing spans and difficult biomedical cases, to illuminate what the multigraph edges capture in practice.

Questions to authors
- Can you report separate precision/recall/F1 for nested-only and crossing-only entities, and the proportion of crossing instances per dataset? Where do your gains come from?
- What constraints govern valid sequences of entity separators across a sentence? Are there illegal edge combinations and how are they prevented during inference/decoding?
- How exactly is decoding ambiguity resolved? You mention interpreting ambiguous structures as nested rather than crossing—does this bias results, and how sensitive are scores to that choice?
- Did you run statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap or randomization) on the F1 differences? If so, please report them; if not, can you provide them in the rebuttal?
- Can you quantify practical impact: e.g., count of “new” correct overlapping mentions found vs. baselines, and error categories reduced?

Additional comments (presentation and minor issues)
- Define “multigraph” on first use and explain why multiple edges with distinct semantics are beneficial here.
- Clarify terminology: use “node” consistently (avoid mixing “state”/“node”); define “spurious structures” more concretely with a small running example early in the main text.
- Figures: Ensure grayscale-friendly rendering; explain edge colors/line styles and node shading in captions; motivate the example’s purpose.
- Math and notation: Standardize punctuation around equations; ensure symbols are introduced before use; specify the partition function and inside/outside recurrences precisely for all node/edge types.
- References and writing: Fix typos/grammar; avoid using references as nouns; standardize capitalization and formatting across the bibliography; ensure footnotes and URLs are properly placed.
- Experimental reporting: Replace “significant”/“better” with exact effect sizes; report per-dataset precision/recall tradeoffs prominently; state mention penalty tuning protocol; release code and data splits to aid reproducibility.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important setting with a conceptually clean multigraph formalism and presents small but consistent empirical gains and a useful analysis of deficiency. However, the practical motivation, separation of nested vs. crossing benefits, and several core algorithmic details are underdeveloped. I am lukewarm: with stronger analysis (especially for crossing entities), clearer specifications, and tempered claims around effect sizes, this could be a solid contribution.
