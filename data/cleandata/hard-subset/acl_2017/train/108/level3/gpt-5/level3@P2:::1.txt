Summary and contributions
This paper addresses recognition of overlapping entities by introducing a multigraph-based structured prediction model that assigns explicit semantics to multiple edges (entity separators) between the same pair of nodes. The model targets the limitations of sequence models for overlapping spans and compares primarily to the mention hypergraph (MH) approach.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A multigraph-based structured model with typed edges (entity separators) for overlapping entity recognition, with inside–outside inference and Viterbi-style decoding in O(nT). The framing clarifies an edge-based paradigm distinct from conventional state-based approaches.
2) Empirical improvements over strong baselines (including MH) on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA, plus competitive results on CoNLL 2003, suggesting the approach is robust even when overlaps are absent.
3) A theoretical argument that the model is nondeficient (avoids spurious structures) in contrast to MH, potentially explaining better calibration/recall.

Strengths
- Significance and scope: Overlapping entities are common in ACE and GENIA, yet still under-served by standard sequence models. The paper tackles a meaningful pain point and positions the formulation as a general tool for overlapping structures.
- Solid empirical results across datasets: The model consistently matches or outperforms MH; the largest F1 gains (~+2.1 on ACE2004) are meaningful on standard benchmarks. The evaluation includes both overlapping and non-overlapping regimes, including a non-overlapping dataset (CoNLL 2003) where the model remains competitive or better.
- Methodological care in features and tuning: The authors aim for feature parity with prior work per dataset, tune regularization and mention penalty on dev, and report both raw and dev-optimized results, which helps interpretability of gains.
- Complexity and training behavior: The algorithm preserves the desirable O(nT) complexity of MH and is reported to converge in fewer iterations (albeit with ~12% slower iterations). This is a practical advantage in training efficiency and suggests favorable optimization properties.
- Conceptual clarity on edge-based modeling: The state-vs-edge paradigm discussion is useful and can inform future work on structured prediction beyond NER.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient motivation vs MH: Section 3 does not clearly articulate concrete failure modes of MH that the multigraph fixes beyond the general spurious-structure argument. Where does MH’s deficiency empirically hurt (precision vs recall, certain span lengths, nested vs crossing)? A targeted error analysis comparing MH and the multigraph on overlapping cases is missing.
- Unclear necessity and benefit of “separators”: Section 4 introduces 8 entity separators, but it is not demonstrated why separators are needed beyond T/I/O states, nor which separators contribute most. An ablation that merges or removes separator types (e.g., collapsing ES and C) would clarify whether the explicit edge semantics are the source of gains versus feature engineering or tuning.
- Spurious structures argument too abstract and under-validated: Section 5.2 sketches the issue; Appendix A helps, but the main paper should include a concrete, minimal example and a clear definition/proof of nondeficiency for the proposed model. More importantly, it should be shown that eliminating spurious structures materially improves learning (e.g., synthetic experiments where MH and the multigraph differ only in deficiency; calibration or likelihood analyses; correlation between nondeficiency and recall improvements).
- Ambiguity about overlapping types and decoding: The paper claims support for overlapping structures in general, but decoding “resolves ambiguous structures as nested entities instead of crossing entities.” Does the model truly support crossing spans, or are they forced to be nested at decode time? This needs to be clarified and, if nesting-only is enforced, discussed as a limitation with an assessment of prevalence/impact.
- Reporting gaps and fairness concerns: Statistical significance of improvements is not reported; GENIA preprocessing differs from some prior setups and may affect comparability; mention penalties are tuned for F1—please confirm identical tuning for all systems and discuss sensitivity. The constant factors in O(nT) (memory/time per token per type) for 8 separator edges should be quantified with wall-clock and memory benchmarks.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Please provide a crisp, minimal working example in the main paper that demonstrates spurious structures in MH and shows how your multigraph avoids them, including explicit inside/outside computations and resulting normalization terms.
- Do you truly allow crossing entities at inference and decoding? If not, what fraction of gold crossing spans exists in ACE/GENIA, and how would this affect performance? If yes, why choose to resolve ambiguities as nested—does this bias results?
- What empirical evidence supports that nondeficiency, rather than other differences (e.g., feature interactions induced by separators), explains the observed gains? Can you provide a synthetic study or ablation isolating this factor?
- Please add ablations on separator design: (a) fewer separator types; (b) collapsing to a state-based variant that labels gaps with ES/CS/etc.; (c) removing the mention penalty. How do these affect F1 and recall on overlapping spans?
- Provide statistical significance tests for the main tables and wall-clock/memory comparisons vs MH on ACE2004/2005.
- Are interactions between entity types modeled at all? If not, do you anticipate further gains from modest cross-type features consistent with your multigraph? Any preliminary evidence?

Additional comments
- Section 3 should clearly state the specific limitations of MH that motivate your design (beyond complexity parity), ideally linked to the later analysis and error breakdowns.
- Section 4 would benefit from a step-by-step walk-through on a short sentence contrasting MH edges vs your separators and showing how the same spans are represented and scored.
- Please clean up references (formatting inconsistencies, stray question marks/escapes) and ensure all datasets/splits are precisely specified; clarify GENIA tokenization differences and their impact.
- Minor: make figures self-contained (legends for separators; consistent node/edge notation); add a table mapping separator types to span events to improve readability.

Overall, this is a well-written paper tackling an important problem with a pragmatic, effective model and strong results. The main reservations concern clarity of motivation and analysis versus MH and the need for targeted ablations and concrete evidence linking nondeficiency to empirical gains. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper.
