Summary
This paper proposes a structured prediction model for overlapping entity recognition based on a multigraph representation with edge semantics (“entity separators”), arguing it avoids spurious structures found in mention hypergraphs while retaining O(nT) complexity. Experiments on ACE2004/2005 and GENIA report modest F1 gains over mention hypergraphs and comparable/better performance than non-overlap baselines, along with some training-efficiency observations.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
- A conceptual formulation of overlapping entity recognition in an edge-based multigraph with explicit separator semantics and a nondeficient normalization (primary).
- An empirical comparison showing small but consistent improvements over mention hypergraphs and some training-efficiency advantages (secondary).
- A discussion contrasting state-based vs edge-based paradigms that may clarify modeling trade-offs (tertiary).

Strengths
- Tackles an important and underexplored problem: detecting overlapping/crossing entities is relevant for ACE/GENIA and beyond.
- The multigraph technique is sensible and empirically competitive; gains of up to ~2 F1 are observed on ACE, and performance is at least on par with strong baselines on GENIA.
- The paper provides a useful analysis of spurious structures, positioning the proposed model as nondeficient while matching O(nT) complexity.
- Some evidence of efficiency improvements (fewer L-BFGS iterations to converge) compared to mention hypergraphs, with transparent acknowledgment of per-iteration overhead.
- Generally clear exposition of the edge-based paradigm and feature design alignment with baselines.

Weaknesses
- Weak motivation for nested entities and minimal discussion of practical utility; the paper does not quantify how many “new” or important entities are recovered or whether small F1 gains matter for users.
- Crossing entities are not convincingly emphasized: decoding resolves ambiguities as nested rather than crossing, and experiments/error analysis do not isolate crossing-specific improvements; it remains unclear if gains stem primarily from nested detection.
- Key algorithmic details are missing: precise edge inclusion criteria, which Ikt variants/states are active, how CS/EC/ECS edges are selected/scored, and how ambiguous structures are disambiguated at decode time.
- Empirical gains are small; claims of “outperformed” should be tempered with effect-size reporting, confidence intervals/significance tests, and per-type analyses.
- Missing baselines that contextualize impact (dictionary-based, gazetteer, and commercial NLP systems). No ablations on features or separator inventory.
- Presentation issues: “multigraph” is not defined on first use; figures (e.g., Fig. 2) are unclear in grayscale; several terms/notations (“spurious structures,” “state” vs “node,” CS-edge) are under-specified; many style/grammar and reference-formatting inconsistencies.

Questions to authors (optional)
- How many crossing entities are in each test set, and what are precision/recall/F1 specifically for crossing vs nested vs non-overlapping mentions?
- Does resolving ambiguous structures as nested suppress crossing detection? Can you report results with an alternative policy that favors crossing?
- What is the exact decision criterion for including ES/CS/EC/ECS edges between token positions? Are any separators pruned heuristically?
- Which Ikt variants/states are instantiated, and how are transitions restricted? Please provide full dynamic-programming recurrences.
- Please report effect sizes with CIs and statistical tests; include per-entity-type metrics and calibration/error breakdowns.
- Can you compare against simple gazetteer/dictionary baselines and a commercial system to quantify practical headroom?
- Provide concrete examples where the multigraph succeeds/fails versus mention hypergraph, especially for crossing cases.

Overall, I am lukewarm and lean toward rejection unless the crossing-entity contribution is empirically substantiated and the missing details/analyses are addressed.
