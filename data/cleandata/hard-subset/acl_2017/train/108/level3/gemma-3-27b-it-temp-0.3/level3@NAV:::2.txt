## Review of "A Multigraph-based Model for Overlapping Entity Recognition"

This paper proposes a multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping named entities, addressing a known challenge in NER. The core idea of using multigraphs to represent overlapping structures is interesting and potentially valuable, and the empirical results demonstrate some improvement over existing methods, particularly the mention hypergraph baseline. The paper also correctly identifies and discusses a potential advantage regarding “spurious structures” in comparison to the baseline.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the exploration of multigraphs as a representational tool for overlapping entities, and the demonstration that this approach can yield modest gains in performance. The analysis of spurious structures is a secondary, but valuable, contribution. 

However, despite these strengths, I remain lukewarm about this submission and lean towards rejection. The gains reported are small, and the paper doesn’t convincingly establish the practical significance of these improvements. 

My main concerns are as follows:

1. **Limited Impact & Overstated Claims:** The F1 gains (often a few points) are presented as “outperforming” previous models without sufficient discussion of effect size or statistical significance. The paper lacks analysis of *why* these gains matter – how many previously undetected entities are now identified, and does this translate to a meaningful improvement for end-users?

2. **Insufficient Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is lacking in several key areas. There are no comparisons to simple dictionary-based methods or commercial NER systems (e.g., Google NLP), making it difficult to contextualize the results. Furthermore, the experiments don’t sufficiently focus on *crossing* entities – it’s unclear whether the gains are primarily due to improved nested entity detection or genuine progress on the more challenging crossing entity problem. A detailed error analysis with concrete examples of successes and failures is also missing.

3. **Algorithmic Clarity:** Key algorithmic details are missing. The criteria for including edges in the multigraph are not clearly defined, and the specific variants of the I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub> nodes used are not explained. This lack of detail hinders reproducibility and understanding.

4. **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper suffers from numerous minor issues including unclear terminology ("spurious structures," "state" vs. "node"), poorly explained figures (Fig. 2 is particularly difficult to interpret in grayscale), and inconsistencies in notation and referencing. The term "multigraph" itself isn’t defined upon first use.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, including specific examples of cases where your model succeeds and fails, and how this compares to the baseline?
2.  What is the computational overhead of using multigraphs compared to hypergraphs, beyond the reported iteration count?
3.  Could you clarify the specific criteria used to determine which edges are included in the multigraph, and the specific I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub> node variants employed?
4.  How do the reported F1 gains translate into a measurable improvement in a downstream application that relies on accurate entity recognition?



**Additional Comments:**

Please address all minor issues related to terminology, notation, figure clarity, and writing style. A more thorough comparison to existing systems, including commercial options, is highly recommended.
