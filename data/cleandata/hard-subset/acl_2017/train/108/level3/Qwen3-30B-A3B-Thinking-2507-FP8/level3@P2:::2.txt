

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping entities in text, as opposed to the simple graphs or hypergraphs commonly used in structured prediction. The authors claim their model resolves "spurious structures" in a previous state-of-the-art hypergraph model while maintaining the same inference time complexity. The main contributions I see are:

1. The theoretical analysis showing their model is "non-deficient" (does not contain spurious structures) compared to the previous hypergraph model, which is a significant conceptual contribution to structured prediction.

2. The empirical demonstration that their model achieves modest but consistent improvements over the hypergraph baseline across multiple standard datasets (ACE2004, ACE2005, GENIA).

3. The introduction of an edge-based paradigm for modeling entity recognition, where explicit semantics are assigned to edges connecting the same pair of nodes, which represents a novel perspective compared to the conventional state-based paradigm.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical insight on spurious structures**: The paper provides a clear and valuable theoretical analysis of "spurious structures" in the previous hypergraph model (Lu and Roth, 2015), which is a significant contribution to the understanding of structured prediction models. The authors demonstrate that their model avoids this deficiency while maintaining the same inference complexity, which is an important theoretical contribution.

2. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation**: The authors conduct experiments across three standard datasets with proper splits and comparison to multiple baselines, including a strong hypergraph baseline. The consistent performance improvements (though modest) across datasets lend credibility to their approach.

3. **Novel paradigm shift**: The paper successfully introduces and articulates the distinction between state-based and edge-based paradigms for structured prediction. This conceptual contribution has potential to inspire future work in structured prediction for overlapping structures.

## Weaknesses
1. **Weak motivation for the problem**: The paper fails to convincingly explain why overlapping entities (particularly crossing entities) are important in real-world applications. The example of "China" inside "Bank of China" is insufficient to justify the significant effort required to address this problem. Without strong motivation, the paper's contribution appears incremental rather than impactful.

2. **Modest empirical gains without proper contextualization**: The improvements over the hypergraph baseline are typically only 1-2 F1 points (e.g., 2.1 points on ACE2004). The paper overstates these gains as "outperforming previous models" without providing adequate context about whether these small improvements are meaningful in practical applications.

3. **Insufficient analysis of crossing entities**: The paper claims to handle both nested and crossing entities but provides little evidence that the improvements come from better handling of crossing entities (the more challenging case). The experiments don't include detailed error analysis to distinguish between improvements in nested vs. crossing entity recognition.

4. **Missing algorithmic details**: Key aspects of the model are unclear, including how the model decides which edges to include and which variants of the I^k_t nodes are used. The paper lacks sufficient detail to fully understand or replicate the model.

5. **Lack of meaningful baselines**: The paper doesn't compare against simpler approaches (e.g., dictionary-based methods) or commercial NLP systems that could help contextualize the significance of the reported improvements.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more concrete examples of real-world applications where recognizing overlapping entities (particularly crossing entities) is critical, and how your model would improve performance in those specific scenarios?

2. The paper mentions that your model achieves better results on non-overlapping entities as well. Could you provide more detailed analysis on this finding and explain why the model might perform better on non-overlapping entities despite not being explicitly designed for them?

3. How does your model specifically handle crossing entities versus nested entities? Could you provide examples from your experiments showing improvements for crossing entities specifically?

## Additional Comments
The paper is generally well-written but requires significant polishing in terms of grammar, terminology, and figure clarity. The term "multigraph" should be defined on first use. The figures (especially Figure 2) are unclear in black-and-white, and the example's goals are not sufficiently justified. There are numerous minor presentation issues, including inconsistent reference formatting and unclear notations (e.g., "spurious structures," "state" vs. "node").

While the theoretical contribution regarding spurious structures is valuable, the paper's overstatement of the empirical significance combined with weak motivation and insufficient analysis of crossing entities makes it difficult to justify the paper's contribution as a major advance in the field. The modest gains don't clearly demonstrate that the problem is both important and well-solved by the proposed approach.
