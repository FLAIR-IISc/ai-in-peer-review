This paper proposes a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping entities, a problem that is both interesting and relevant. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments on standard datasets, achieving higher F1-scores compared to previous models. The paper is generally well-written, although it requires polishing in several details.

The proposed multigraph technique appears to work empirically, although the gains are not large. The authors also discuss efficiency improvements compared to a previous system, which is a notable contribution. The paper's discussion on the differences between the proposed model and previous models is informative, and the analysis on the implications of these differences is insightful.

However, there are several weaknesses in the paper. The motivation for detecting nested entities is weak and under-explained. Key algorithmic details are missing, such as the decision criterion for including edges and which I^k_t node variants are used. The empirical improvements are small, often only a few F1 points, and the claim of "outperforming" previous models is overstated without a discussion of effect size.

Furthermore, the paper lacks analysis of the practical impact of the proposed model, such as how many important new entities are found and whether small F1 gains matter to users. The authors also fail to provide baseline comparisons to simple dictionary-based methods and commercial systems, which would help to contextualize the results.

The paper's focus on crossing entities in experiments and error analysis is insufficient, making it unclear whether the gains come from nested detection only or also improved crossing detection. Additionally, the paper would benefit from detailed error comparisons with prior systems and concrete examples of successes and failures.

The reviewer remains lukewarm and leans toward rejection because the paper does not convincingly emphasize the important problem of crossing entities.

Minor issues with the paper include the need to define "multigraph" on first use for readers unfamiliar with the term. There are numerous wording, grammar, and stylistic issues, including typos, awkward phrases, and punctuation errors. Several unclear terms and notations need clarification, such as "spurious structures," "state" vs. "node," and CS-edge selection.

The figures, especially Figure 2, are unclear in black-and-white, and the use of colors/gray nodes and highlighted edges is not explained. The example's goals are not justified, and there are many presentation issues in math and text, including commas/periods, equation punctuation, and inconsistent reference formatting.

The reference usage and capitalization in the bibliography are inconsistent, and the authors should avoid using references as nouns. Experimental details and reporting could be improved, including what "significant" and "better" mean, exact effect sizes, and specific recall/precision issues on datasets.
