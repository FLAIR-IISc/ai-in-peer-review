**Paper Summary**

The paper proposes a novel multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping entities in text. The model uses a multigraph representation to capture the complex relationships between entities and their combinations. The authors claim that their model outperforms previous state-of-the-art models on standard datasets and demonstrate its effectiveness in recognizing overlapping entities.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a novel multigraph-based model for predicting overlapping entities.
2. They empirically show that their model achieves higher F1-scores compared to previous models on multiple datasets.
3. They theoretically analyze the differences between their model and previous models, highlighting the advantages of their approach.

**Strengths**

1. The problem of detecting overlapping entities is interesting and relevant, and the authors' approach to addressing it is novel and effective.
2. The paper discusses efficiency improvements compared to a previous system, which is a significant contribution.
3. The paper is generally well-written, although it needs polishing in several details.

**Weaknesses**

1. The motivation for detecting nested entities is weak and under-explained, which makes it difficult to understand the practical impact of the authors' approach.
2. Key algorithmic details are missing, such as the decision criterion for including edges and which I^k_t node variants are used.
3. The empirical improvements are small, often only a few F1 points, which raises questions about the significance of the results.
4. The paper lacks analysis of practical impact, such as how many important new entities are found and whether small F1 gains matter to users.
5. The paper would benefit from detailed error comparisons with prior systems and concrete examples of successes/failures.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the decision criterion for including edges in the multigraph representation?
2. How do you select which I^k_t node variants to use in the model?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the practical impact of your approach, such as how many important new entities are found and whether small F1 gains matter to users?
4. Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the specific recall/precision issues on datasets?

**Minor Comments**

1. Define "multigraph" on first use for readers unfamiliar with the term.
2. Numerous wording, grammar, and stylistic issues (typos, awkward phrases, punctuation) need to be addressed.
3. Several unclear terms and notations (e.g., "spurious structures," "state" vs. "node," CS-edge selection) need clarification.
4. Figures (especially Fig.2) are unclear in black-and-white, and the use of colors/gray nodes and highlighted edges is not explained.
5. Many presentation issues in math and text (commas/periods, equation punctuation, inconsistent reference formatting, footnote placement) need to be addressed.
6. Inconsistent reference usage and capitalization in bibliography; avoid using references as nouns.
7. Clarify experimental details and reporting (what "significant"/"better" means, exact effect sizes, specific recall/precision issues on datasets).
