## Review of “A Multigraph-based Model for Overlapping Entity Recognition”

This paper proposes a multigraph-based model for recognizing overlapping entities, addressing a known challenge in Named Entity Recognition (NER). The core idea of using multigraphs to explicitly represent semantic relationships between entities is interesting and potentially valuable. The authors demonstrate empirical improvements over a baseline mention hypergraph model on standard datasets, and highlight efficiency gains. The paper is generally well-written, although it requires polishing in several details.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the exploration of multigraphs as a representational framework for overlapping entities. While the gains are modest, the introduction of this approach is a worthwhile contribution to the field. The analysis comparing the proposed model to the mention hypergraph, particularly regarding “spurious structures”, is also a notable strength. Finally, the report of efficiency improvements, even if incremental, adds to the overall value of the work. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the motivation for detecting *nested* entities (e.g., “China” within “Bank of China”) feels underdeveloped. The paper doesn’t convincingly argue why accurately identifying these nested structures is critically important. Secondly, key algorithmic details are missing. Specifically, the criteria for including edges in the multigraph and which variations of the I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub> nodes are used are not clearly defined. This lack of specificity hinders reproducibility and understanding. 

The empirical improvements reported are small (often a few F1 points), and the authors' claim of “outperforming” previous models feels overstated without a thorough discussion of effect sizes and statistical significance.  Furthermore, there is a concerning lack of analysis regarding practical impact; the paper doesn't discuss how many new, important entities are identified due to the proposed model or whether these small F1 gains translate to meaningful improvements for end-users. Crucially, the paper lacks baseline comparisons to simpler, dictionary-based methods or commercially available NLP systems (e.g., Google NLP’s NER), which would contextualize the reported results. 

The experiments focus insufficiently on crossing entities, making it unclear whether the observed gains stem from improved nested entity detection or genuinely better handling of crossing entities. A detailed error analysis, with concrete examples of successes and failures, is needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more compelling justification for the importance of accurately detecting *nested* entities specifically?
2. Please clarify the edge inclusion criteria for the multigraph – what features are used to determine which edges are added?
3. Which specific variants of the I<sup>k</sup><sub>t</sub> nodes are utilized in your model? 
4. What is the statistical significance of the reported F1 improvements?
5. Could you provide examples of cases where your model successfully identifies overlapping entities that previous models missed? And conversely, examples where it fails?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper requires thorough editing to address numerous minor issues with wording, grammar, and notation. Figures, particularly Fig. 2, are unclear in black and white and need improved labeling. Definitions of terms like “spurious structures” and the distinction between “state” and “node” should be clarified. The reference list contains inconsistencies.



Overall, while the paper presents an interesting idea, the lack of detail, modest gains, and insufficient analysis lead me to lean towards **rejection**. The authors need to address the outlined weaknesses to convincingly demonstrate the value of their proposed approach.
