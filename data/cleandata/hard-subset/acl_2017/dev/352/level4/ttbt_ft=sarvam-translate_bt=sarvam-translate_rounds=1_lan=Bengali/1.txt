This paper introduces new configurations and training objectives for neural sequence models in multi-tasking settings. As the authors well describe, multi-tasking settings are important because some tasks involve sharing information, and in some situations, learning many tasks can improve overall performance. The methods section is relatively clear and logical, and I liked where it ended, although it could have been organized a little better. The organization I perceived after reading is that there are two problems: 1) shared features end up in the private feature space, and 2) private features end up in the shared space. There is a new approach for each problem. The initial organization will further consolidate those approaches. However, they introduce a method that keeps task-specific features away from the shared representation (universal loss) and another that keeps shared features away from the task-specific representation (orthogonality constraints). My only point of confusion is the universal system.

After the LSTM output, there is another layer, D(s^kT, \thetaD), which depends on the U and b parameters. This output is considered a probability distribution that is compared to the actual output. This means that it can only possibly learn U and b, which effectively mask the task-specific information from the LSTM output, and it does not seem to guarantee the removal of task-specific information.

Before reading the evaluation section, I wrote that I expected the tests to look like this, and it mostly did. It is an interesting idea and many more experiments can be imagined, but I think there are key points here to show the validity of their approach. It would be helpful to have the best results on these tasks.

My primary concern with this paper is the lack of deep motivation for the approach. I think it is easy to understand that there will be problems due to conflicts in the feature space in a fully shared model. The extension to partially shared features seems to be a response to that problem - one would expect that useful shared information is in the shared latent space, and each task-specific space would learn the features for that space. Maybe it works and maybe it doesn't, but the logic is clear to me. Conversely, the authors seem to have started from the assumption that this "shared-private" model has this problem. I was hoping that the flow of logic would be 1) fully shared must have this problem; 2) shared-private seems to solve it; 3) in reality, shared-private does not completely solve this problem due to a, b, c; 4) we introduce a method that more effectively constrains the spaces.

Table 4 helped me partially understand what is going wrong with shared-private and what your methods do; some words usually carry one or the other meaning, and that general tendency can probably take them to the shared feature space. This simple explanation, an example, and a more logical flow of arguments would help the introduction and make it a truly beautiful readable paper.

Finally, I think this research is related to some other unsolicited MTL work [1], which does deep hierarchical MTL - POS tagging supervised at the lower level, chunking at the next level, CCG tagging at the higher level, etc. They then discuss some qualities at the end that make MTL possible and conclude that MTL only works "when the tasks are sufficiently similar." The ASP-MTL paper reminded me of this previous work because this model can probably learn what is sufficiently similar - that is, if two tasks are not sufficiently similar, the shared model will learn nothing, and it will revert to learning two independent systems, a shared-private model compared to the baseline that can overfit and perform poorly.
