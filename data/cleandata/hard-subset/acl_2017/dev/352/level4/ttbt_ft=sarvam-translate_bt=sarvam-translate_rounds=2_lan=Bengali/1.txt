This paper presents new configurations and training objectives for neural sequence models in multi-tasking settings. As the authors describe well, multi-tasking settings are important because some tasks share information, and in some situations, learning many tasks can improve overall performance. The methods section is relatively clear and logical, and I liked where I ended, although it could have been a little better organized. The organization I perceived after reading is that there are two problems: 1) shared features end up in the private feature space, and 2) private features end up in the shared space. There is a new approach for each problem. The initial organization will further consolidate those approaches. However, they introduce a method that keeps task-specific features away from the shared representation (universal loss), and another that keeps shared features away from the task-specific representation (orthogonality constraint). The only point of confusion for me is the universal system.

After the LSTM output, there is another layer, D (SKT, Theta D), which depends on the U and B parameters. This output is considered a probability distribution compared to the actual output. This means it can only possibly learn U and B, effectively masking the task-specific information from the LSTM output, and it doesn't seem to guarantee the removal of the task-specific information.

Before reading the evaluation section, I wrote that I expected the tests to look like this, and it mostly did. It's an interesting idea, and many more tests could be imagined, but I think the main points are here to show the validity of their methods. It would be helpful to have the best results in these tasks.

My primary concern with this paper is the lack of deep motivation for the methods. I think it's easy to understand that there will be problems due to conflict in the feature space in a fully shared model. The extension to partially shared features seems to be a response to that problem - one would expect that useful shared information is in the shared latent space, and each task-specific space would learn the features of that space. Maybe it works, and maybe it doesn't, but the logic is clear to me. Conversely, the authors seem to have started from the assumption that this problem exists in this "shared-private" model. I expected the flow of logic to be 1) fully shared must have this problem; 2) shared-private seems to solve it; 3) in reality, shared-private does not fully solve this problem due to A, B, C; 4) we introduce a method that more effectively limits the spaces.

Table 4 helped me partially understand what is going wrong with shared-private and what your methods do; some words usually carry one or the other meaning, and that general tendency can probably lead them to the shared feature space. This simple explanation, an example, and a more logical flow of logic would help the introduction and make it a truly beautiful readable paper.

Finally, I think this research is related to some other unwanted MTL tasks [1], which do deep hierarchical MTL - supervised pass tagging at the lower level, checking at the next level, CCG tagging at the higher level, etc. They then discuss some of the qualities of the latter that make MTL possible and conclude that MTL only works "when the tasks are similar enough." The ASP- MTL paper reminded me of this previous work because this model can probably learn what is similar enough - that is, if two tasks are not similar enough, the shared model will not learn anything, and it will revert to learning two independent systems, a shared-private model compared to the baseline that can overfit and perform poorly.
