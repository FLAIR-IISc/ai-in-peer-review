This paper describes the concept of phrase representation learning in RNN-based language models and neural machine translation, and their advantages.

- Strengths:
The idea of including phrase information is appealing.
- Weaknesses:
- The description is difficult to follow. It would be helpful to understand proof-reading by an English native speaker.
- The method has several weaknesses in its evaluation.
- General Discussion
- In equations 1 and 2, the authors mention a phrase representation that gives a word embedding vector of a specific length. But this was not used in the model. The representation is based on an RNN. What is the proposal of this description?
- Why are you using GRU for the pyramid and LSTM for the sequential part? Is the combination of two architectures beneficial for you?
- What is the simplified version of GRU? Why is it working well? How does it work on large datasets?
- What is the difference between RNNsearch (Groundhog) and RNNsearch (Baseline) in Table 4?
- What is the purpose behind using only the last phrase, and for example, what is the purpose behind not using the starting phrase?
- Did you only use the pyramid encoder? How does it work? It would be a fairer comparison because it usually helps to make the model more complex.
- Why did you run RNNsearch several times, but PBNM only once?
- Section 5.2: What is the purpose of this section?
