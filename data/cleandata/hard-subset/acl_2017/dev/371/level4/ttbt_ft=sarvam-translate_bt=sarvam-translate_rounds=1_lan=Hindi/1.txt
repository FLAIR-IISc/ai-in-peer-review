This paper describes an idea for learning phrase representation and making it convenient for use in RNN-based language models and neural machine translation.

- Strengths:
The idea of incorporating phrase information into the task is interesting.
- Weaknesses:
- The description is difficult to understand. Proof-reading by an English-speaking speaker would help with comprehension.
- There are several weaknesses in the evaluation of the approach.
- General Discussion
- In equations 1 and 2, the authors mention that phrase representation gives a word embedding vector of a certain length. But this is not used in the model. The representation is generated based on an RNN. What is the proposal for this description?
- Why are you using GRU for the pyramid and LSTM for the sequential part? Is the combination of the two architectures the reason for your improvements?
- What is the simplified version of GRU? Why is it performing better? How is it performing on large data sets?
- What is the difference between RNNsearch (Groundhog) and RNNsearch (Baseline) in Table 4?
- What is the motivation for using only the final phrases and not using the starting phrases, for example?
- Did you only use the pyramid encoder? How is it performing? This would be a more fair comparison because it generally helps to make the model more complex.
- Why did you run RNNsearch multiple times, but PBNMT only once?
- Section 5.2: What is the purpose of this section?
