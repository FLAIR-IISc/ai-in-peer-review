- Introduces pRNN to incorporate task-specific nested phrasal information into RNN-based language models and NMT without human-labeled data.  
- Proposes an RNN pyramid architecture that generates fixed-length vector representations for phrases of arbitrary lengths.  
- Reports strong empirical gains: >10 perplexity points over a strong LSTM baseline and better NMT performance than PBSMT and RNNsearch.  
- Strength: Novel incorporation of phrasal information avoiding external resources like labeled data or alignments.  
- Strength: Pyramid enables parallel RNN sequences as an alternative to stacking deeper RNN layers.  
- Strength: Demonstrates substantial empirical improvements on both language modeling and NMT tasks.  
- Weakness: Paper clarity is poor and needs thorough proofreading to improve readability.  
- Weakness: Evaluation methodology concerns — inconsistent number of runs for baselines and no standalone evaluation of the pyramid encoder.  
- Weakness: Unclear design choices — no justification for using GRU in the pyramid and LSTM elsewhere, and insufficient analysis of the simplified GRU on larger datasets.  
- Weakness: Ambiguities in results — unclear distinction between RNNsearch (groundhog) and RNNsearch (baseline) in Table 4, and unexplained focus on ending phrases only.  
- Weakness: Section 5.2 (Deep Memory Network) is unclear and appears not to contribute to the paper’s core findings.  
- Question: Why choose GRU for the pyramid encoder and LSTM for the sequential part — any empirical/theoretical justification?  
- Question: What is the purpose of the fixed-length phrase representation in Equations 1–2 if it’s not directly used?  
- Question: Why were only ending phrases considered — would starting phrases offer complementary information?  
- Question: Please clarify the difference between RNNsearch (groundhog) and RNNsearch (baseline) in Table 4.  
- Question: How does the simplified GRU behave on larger datasets and why does it outperform the standard GRU?  
- Recommendation: Major revisions required to address writing, evaluation methodology, and design clarity before acceptance.
