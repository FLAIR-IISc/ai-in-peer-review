- Introduces UDP: a training-free, rule-based unsupervised UD parser using head rules, two-step decoding, and personalized PageRank to rank clausal heads.  
- Combines PageRank for predicate salience with linguistically-motivated rules and UD constraints to form an effective, simple parsing algorithm.  
- Demonstrates strong cross-lingual performance across 32 languages and superior robustness to domain shifts versus a multi-source delexicalized parser.  
- Simplicity and strong empirical results: competitive average UAS (≈6.4% gap) with a supervised delexicalized baseline across 32 languages.  
- Thorough, rigorous evaluation spanning 32 languages with gold/predicted POS analyses and error-propagation experiments.  
- Detailed analysis showing much lower cross-domain variance and greater stability than data-driven transfer models.  
- Model is well-motivated and interpretable, with design choices tied to UD principles (e.g., function words as leaves, PageRank for prominence).  
- Relies on hand-tuned head rules and fixed function-word attachment directions developed on English, risking English-centric bias.  
- Limited baseline scope: lacks direct comparisons to classic unsupervised grammar-induction models (e.g., DMV) on the UD dataset.  
- Main-predicate heuristic (first verb/content word) is oversimplified and may fail for flexible word-order languages or complex clauses.  
- Question: How much English-specific bias do the hand-crafted rules introduce, and can rules/directions be induced from unlabeled target-language data?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the PageRank personalization heuristic, especially for multi-clause or verb-initial languages?  
- Question: Why was the NOUN → VERB rule excluded during development, and what were the performance trade-offs when it was included?
