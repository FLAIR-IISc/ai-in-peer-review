### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The system operates without any training data, relying instead on a small set of hand-crafted, POS-based head-selection rules derived from the UD formalism. The core of the method involves constructing a dependency graph based on these rules and then using a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank content words, giving preferential weight to the likely main predicate of the sentence. A two-step decoding algorithm then constructs the final dependency tree, first attaching content words based on their PageRank score and then attaching function words to ensure they remain leaf nodes, a key constraint in UD. The parser's performance is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks, comparing it against a multi-source delexicalized transfer system under gold, predicted, and naive POS tag settings. A key finding is that the proposed system is not only competitive but also significantly more robust to domain shifts than the data-driven baseline.

### Contributions
1.  **An extensive cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation of a simple unsupervised parser.** The paper's most significant contribution is its thorough empirical analysis. The UDP system is tested on 32 languages in various settings (gold POS, predicted POS), providing a wide-ranging benchmark. More importantly, the detailed cross-domain analysis in Section 6.3 provides strong evidence that this simple, rule-based system is more stable across different text genres than a competitive, data-driven delexicalized transfer parser. This finding is valuable for the community, highlighting the trade-offs between data-driven and knowledge-based approaches, particularly in out-of-domain scenarios.
2.  **A strong, transparent, and reproducible unsupervised baseline for UD parsing.** The proposed UDP system, while building on prior work, is adapted specifically for the constraints of Universal Dependencies. Its performance is surprisingly competitive for a system with no training, achieving UAS scores that are, on average, only 6.4 points lower than a supervised multi-source delexicalized parser on gold POS tags. Its simplicity, reliance on a few clear rules, and lack of training data make it an excellent and easy-to-implement baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.

### Strengths
1.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength. Evaluating on 32 languages provides a broad picture of the system's applicability. The inclusion of experiments with predicted POS tags (UDPP) offers a realistic assessment of the parser's performance in a practical pipeline. The cross-domain evaluation (Tables 6 and 7) is particularly compelling; the analysis of standard deviation across domains clearly demonstrates that UDP is substantially more robust to domain shift than the MSD baseline. This is a significant and well-supported claim.
2.  **Strong Performance for an Unsupervised Method:** Despite its simplicity, the UDP parser achieves impressive results. It significantly outperforms a standard adjacency baseline and is remarkably close to the performance of a strong, supervised delexicalized transfer system (MSD). The fact that it even surpasses the MSD system on Hindi (with gold POS) underscores its effectiveness. This level of performance makes it a valuable tool and a non-trivial baseline for the field.
3.  **Methodological Transparency and Simplicity:** The proposed method is straightforward and linguistically motivated. It directly encodes core principles of the UD formalism (e.g., function words as dependents) into a simple decoding algorithm. The use of a small set of explicit rules (Table 1) and a well-understood algorithm like PageRank makes the system's decisions interpretable, which is a notable advantage over more complex, "black box" models.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core methodology is a direct extension of prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012b), which also employed PageRank over a graph constructed from POS-based rules for unsupervised parsing. The primary novelties presented are the adaptation to UD-specific constraints, the use of *personalized* PageRank to up-weight the main predicate, and the two-step decoding process. While effective, these represent incremental improvements rather than a significant conceptual leap.
2.  **Underdeveloped Justification for Key Parameters:** The main methodological contribution—the personalization of PageRank—feels somewhat heuristic. The system identifies the main predicate with a simple rule ("the first verb or the first content word") and assigns it a personalization weight of 5. This weight seems arbitrary and lacks a thorough justification or sensitivity analysis. While the authors state it was tuned on the English dev set, the paper would be stronger if it explored this parameter's impact more deeply or considered more principled ways to identify and weight the sentence's predicate.
3.  **Incomplete Reporting in the Experimental Analysis:** In the predicted POS evaluation scenario (Section 5.3), the authors calculate an error propagation rate from POS tagging to parsing. However, they do not report the actual POS tagging accuracies for the TnT tagger on each language's test set. Including these scores would be crucial for a full understanding of the results, allowing readers to directly correlate the quality of the input POS tags with the final parsing accuracy and better interpret the claimed robustness of the parser to tagging errors.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate appears to be a critical parameter. Could you provide more insight into how this value was chosen? How sensitive is the final parsing performance to this specific value? Furthermore, have you experimented with more sophisticated heuristics for identifying the main predicate, especially for verb-final languages?
2.  Could you clarify the conceptual distinction from Søgaard (2012b) in more detail? While the adaptation to UD and the use of personalization are clear, the fundamental approach of using PageRank on a POS-based dependency graph seems very similar. Highlighting the key differences that lead to the strong performance would strengthen the paper's claims of novelty.
3.  For the final version of the paper, would it be possible to include the per-language POS tagging accuracies achieved by the TnT tagger in the UDPP setting? This would provide valuable context for your error propagation analysis and allow for a more nuanced interpretation of the results.

### Additional Comments
- The extensive use of tables to present results is thorough, but some of the key findings, particularly the cross-domain analysis in Table 6 (standard deviations), could be more effectively communicated with a bar chart or other visualization to immediately highlight the difference in stability between UDP and MSD.
- Minor typo in the PDF: In Section 3.1, the first sentence of the last paragraph reads "The the main idea...". This should be "The main idea...".
