This paper presents a novel deterministic algorithm for dependency parsing and evaluates its performance across a range of languages. The core of the algorithm is a set of hand-crafted, POS-based head rules that define a graph of permitted dependencies. A biased PageRank algorithm ranks the words within this graph. The final parse is constructed by iterating through the ranked words and attaching each to its closest permissible head, subject to constraints that maintain a tree structure and enforce a consistent head directionality.

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and presents an interesting approach to unsupervised dependency parsing. The evaluation across a wide variety of languages is a commendable strength. However, the proposed method appears to share significant similarities with Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training," and the paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of its novel contributions in relation to this prior work.

Despite this, the work is a valuable exploration of rule-based, unsupervised parsing. Below are several questions and suggestions for improvement.

**Major Points**

1.  **Analysis of Head Rules:** The head rules presented in Table 1 are central to the entire system. The paper would benefit significantly from a more in-depth analysis of these rules and their interaction with the corpora. For example, Section 3.1 mentions that the graph defined by these rules is not always connected. It would be very informative to quantify this. For each language, how frequently does this disconnection occur? When the graph is disconnected, what is the typical size and number of the resulting components? This analysis would provide a clearer picture of the rules' coverage and inherent limitations.

2.  **Oracle Analysis of Constraints:** The algorithm's performance is limited by several hard constraints: the head rules, directionality, and the distance-based attachment preference. Each of these imposes a ceiling on the maximum achievable accuracy. To better understand the system's error sources and to guide future research, an oracle analysis would be invaluable. For instance, what would the upper-bound accuracy be if we consider only the head rules (i.e., the best possible parse that conforms to the rules)? What is the ceiling when also adding the directionality constraint? Such an analysis would help isolate which components of the system are the most significant bottlenecks and where efforts for improvement could be most fruitfully applied.

3.  **Choice of Data for Head Direction:** The method for determining head directionality relies on a small sample from the test set. While the authors correctly note that this decision converges rapidly (within 10-15 sentences), using test data for any form of parameter tuning or system configuration is unconventional and methodologically questionable. Could the authors clarify the rationale for this choice over using a standard development set or a small portion of the provided training data?

4.  **Tie-Breaking in PageRank:** The paper does not specify the tie-breaking strategy for words that receive identical PageRank scores. Are ties resolved randomly, by linear sentence order, or by some other heuristic? It would be useful to know how often such ties occur in practice and whether the choice of tie-breaking mechanism has a noticeable impact on parsing accuracy.

**Minor Suggestions**

*   In Section 4.1, the text reads "we obtain rank". This should be corrected to "we obtain **the** rank".
*   In Section 6.1, the text reads "isolate the of both". This appears to be missing a word, likely "isolate the **contribution** of both".
*   The column ordering in Table 5 and Table 7 is inconsistent. The arrangement in Table 7 is clearer and should be used for both tables to improve readability.
