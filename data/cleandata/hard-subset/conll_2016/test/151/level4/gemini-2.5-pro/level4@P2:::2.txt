### Review

**Summary**
This paper introduces UDP, a novel unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The system operates without any training data, relying instead on a small, predefined set of linguistically-motivated head-selection rules derived from UD principles. The core of the method involves two key stages. First, it uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank the "content words" (nouns, verbs, etc.) in a sentence, with a simple heuristic to identify and boost the score of the likely main predicate. Second, it employs a two-step decoding algorithm that first builds a skeleton of the parse tree using the ranked content words and then attaches "function words" (determiners, adpositions, etc.) as leaves, thereby respecting a core constraint of the UD formalism. The authors evaluate their system on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks, comparing it against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parsing system. The results demonstrate that UDP is competitive, particularly in its robustness to domain shift and performance degradation from predicted (vs. gold) POS tags.

**Contributions**
1.  **A Complete, Training-Free UD Parser:** The primary contribution is the design and implementation of a coherent, end-to-end unsupervised dependency parser specifically tailored to the constraints and principles of the Universal Dependencies framework. By combining a personalized PageRank for predicate identification with a two-step decoding process that enforces the content-function word distinction, the authors present a simple yet effective system that serves as a strong, linguistically-informed baseline for cross-lingual parsing.

2.  **Extensive Empirical Analysis of Robustness:** A significant contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed parser. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness across 32 languages and, more importantly, provide a detailed analysis of its robustness. By testing on both gold and predicted part-of-speech tags and conducting a cross-domain evaluation on several languages, they show that their rule-based system is more stable and less sensitive to domain shifts than a competitive data-driven delexicalized transfer system.

**Strengths**
1.  **Elegant and Principled Approach:** The method is commendably simple and elegant. Instead of relying on complex statistical models, it directly exploits the design principles of the Universal Dependencies schema (e.g., function words as dependents of content words). The use of personalized PageRank as a proxy for identifying syntactic heads is intuitive and well-motivated. This approach represents a valuable and fresh perspective in a field dominated by data-driven transfer learning methods.

2.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough and convincing. Evaluating on 32 languages demonstrates the wide applicability of the method. The inclusion of results with predicted POS tags provides a realistic assessment of the parser's utility in a practical pipeline, and the finding that it suffers less from error propagation than the supervised baseline is a strong result. The cross-domain analysis is particularly valuable, offering compelling evidence that the parser's generalized rules make it more robust to domain shifts than a data-driven system potentially overfit to training data characteristics.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the motivation for their design choices, detail the algorithm with pseudocode (Figure 1), and provide a concrete run-through example (Section 4). This high level of detail, combined with the authors' commitment to releasing the code and datasets, ensures that the work is highly reproducible and can serve as a solid foundation for future research in unsupervised and rule-based parsing.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Heuristics Feel Ad-hoc and Under-Justified:** Several key components of the parser rely on heuristics that seem somewhat arbitrary and could be potential points of failure.
    *   **Predicate Identification:** The PageRank personalization gives a 5x weight to "the first verb or the first content word." This is a strong, language-specific bias that favors SVO/VSO languages and may be suboptimal for the many verb-final languages in the test set. While the authors claim this works even for Hindi, the generality of this heuristic is questionable.
    *   **Head Rule Selection:** The paper states the head rules in Table 1 were selected to "maximize precision on the development set" (English). This process is not detailed. It is unclear whether this was a manual, intuition-driven process or a more systematic search. Furthermore, the decision to exclude a `NOUN -> VERB` rule because it makes relations "less conclusive" feels subjective and warrants a more empirical justification, such as an ablation study.

2.  **Limited Scope of Head Rules and Attachment Strategy:** The set of head rules is very small and does not cover many syntactic phenomena, such as relative clauses or coordination. The back-off strategy (dropping constraints to assign the closest head) ensures a tree is always formed, but this may lead to arbitrary and linguistically unmotivated attachments when the rules do not apply. This limits the grammatical coverage of the parser.

3.  **"Training-Free" Claim Is Slightly Overstated:** While the system does not undergo training in the conventional machine learning sense, key parameters were tuned on the English development set. This includes the entire set of head rules (Table 1) and the attachment direction for several function words (Section 3.2). This constitutes a form of manual feature engineering and light supervision on a specific language's development data. While the paper is transparent about this, the "without training" framing could be tempered to more accurately reflect this development process. The system's strong performance on languages typologically distant from English suggests this is not a fatal flaw, but it is a noteworthy aspect of the methodology.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you elaborate on the process for selecting the head rules in Table 1? Was an ablation study performed on the English development set to measure the impact of each rule, and could you share those results? For instance, what is the performance impact of excluding the `NOUN -> VERB` rule?

2.  The main predicate identification for PageRank personalization uses a "first verb" heuristic, which seems biased toward verb-initial/medial languages. Have you explored alternative, more typologically neutral heuristics for identifying the sentence root (e.g., using word frequency, or a different structural property of the graph)?

3.  The attachment direction for function words like AUX and DET was fixed based on English development data. However, the direction for ADP was successfully inferred from the test data. Have you considered applying a similar data-driven inference method for other function words to make the parser even more language-agnostic?
