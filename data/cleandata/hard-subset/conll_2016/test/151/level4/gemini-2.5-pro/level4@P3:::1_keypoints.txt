- Summary: UDP is a fully unsupervised, rule-based UD parser that builds a POS-rule graph, uses personalized PageRank to rank content words (biasing the main verb as root), and decodes deterministically enforcing function words as leaves.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a simple, training-free dependency parser that attains performance close to multi-source delexicalized transfer baselines.

- Contribution 2: Provides an extensive cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation on 32 treebanks showing strong robustness to domain shift.

- Contribution 3: Adapts PageRank with personalization for root identification and introduces a UD-compliant two-step decoder enforcing function-word leaf constraints.

- Strength 1: Method is transparent, easy to implement, and achieves competitive UAS (≈6.4% below a strong baseline on gold POS).

- Strength 2: Experimental setup is thorough; cross-domain results show notably lower variance than the data-driven baseline (std 2.5 vs 4.5 on gold POS).

- Strength 3: Design choices are well-motivated by UD principles (content vs function distinction, linguistically-plausible head rules).

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty over Søgaard (2012b); main innovations (personalized PageRank, two-step decoding) need clearer quantification against prior work.

- Weakness 2: Hand-crafted head rules lack analysis (coverage across languages, frequency of disconnected graphs); no oracle/upper-bound experiments.

- Weakness 3: Adposition direction is estimated from test sentences, a questionable use of test data that blurs unsupervised claims.

- Question 1: What is the performance impact of personalized PageRank and the two-step decoder versus the original non-personalized/single-step methods (ablation)?

- Question 2: What percentage of gold dependencies are licensed by the head rules (coverage/oracle upper bound)?

- Question 3: Why estimate adposition direction from test data, and how do results change using a dev set or typological priors?

- Question 4: How are ties in PageRank scores broken, how often do they occur, and how much do they affect final parsing accuracy?
