### Summary

This paper presents UDP, a deterministic, unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies. The core of the algorithm is a small, fixed set of head-dependent rules based on POS tags. The parser first builds a graph based on these permitted dependencies and uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank the content words in a sentence. A two-step decoding process then constructs the final dependency tree by iterating through the ranked words and attaching each one to the closest available head that satisfies the predefined rules and a directionality constraint.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A simple and effective unsupervised, rule-based dependency parser tailored specifically for the constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism.
2.  An extensive empirical evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domains, demonstrating that the parser is competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer system.
3.  A detailed analysis showing that the proposed rule-based system is significantly more robust to domain shift than its data-driven counterpart.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Clarity:** The proposed UDP algorithm is simple, intuitive, and requires no training data, making it highly accessible. The paper presents the method very clearly, with a helpful step-by-step example in Section 4 that makes the entire process easy to follow and reproduce.

2.  **Extensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation across 32 languages from the UD treebanks. The analysis is comprehensive, including comparisons against a competitive delexicalized transfer baseline under both gold and predicted POS tag settings. This wide-ranging experiment provides a solid empirical grounding for the paper's claims.

3.  **Robustness Analysis:** A significant strength is the detailed analysis of the parser's robustness to domain shift (Section 6.3). The experiments on curated domain splits provide convincing evidence that this rule-based approach is more stable across domains than the data-driven baseline. This is an important finding with practical implications, as real-world applications often involve parsing out-of-domain text.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The proposed method appears to be an incremental adaptation of existing work, particularly Søgaard (2012b), "Unsupervised dependency parsing without training." While the paper acknowledges this prior work, the main contributions—using personalized PageRank and a two-step decoding process adapted for UD constraints—feel like engineering refinements rather than a fundamental conceptual advance. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly situated its contributions against this highly similar prior work and better articulated its novel insights.

2.  **Use of Test Data for Parameter Estimation:** The method for determining adposition head direction is estimated on the fly from the test data (Section 3.2). Although the authors argue that this converges quickly (10-15 sentences) and thus has minimal impact, using test data to set any model parameter is methodologically questionable. This choice conflates testing and development, even if the effect is small.

3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of Constraints:** The parser's performance is fundamentally limited by its constituent constraints (the head rules, directionality heuristics, and distance metric). The paper lacks an analysis of the performance ceiling imposed by each of these components. Including oracle experiments (e.g., performance with perfect head rules or perfect directionality) would provide valuable insight into which components are the primary sources of error. Such an analysis would be very helpful for guiding future work and understanding the parser's limitations.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The head rules in Table 1 are central to the method. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of their properties and coverage on the evaluation corpora? For example, you mention that the rules do not always yield a connected graph (Section 3.1). How frequently does this occur in practice, and what is the typical size of the resulting graph components?

2.  How does the algorithm handle tie-breaking for words that receive the same PageRank score? Does this scenario occur frequently in your experiments, and does the choice of tie-breaking strategy have a noticeable impact on final parsing performance?
