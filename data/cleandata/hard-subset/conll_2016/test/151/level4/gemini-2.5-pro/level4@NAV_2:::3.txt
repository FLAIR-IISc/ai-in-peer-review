**Summary and Contributions**
This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm designed for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method operates without any training data, relying instead on a predefined set of POS-based head-dependency rules and the PageRank algorithm. A key aspect is the use of personalized PageRank to give higher importance to the sentence's main predicate, which helps in structuring the parse tree. The decoding process is performed in two steps: first, content words are attached based on their PageRank score, and second, function words are attached as dependents to the already-placed content words, ensuring they remain leaves as per UD guidelines.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A simple yet competitive unsupervised parser.** The primary contribution is a completely unsupervised method that leverages the linguistic constraints of the UD formalism to achieve parsing scores that are remarkably close to a strong, supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer system across a wide array of 32 languages.
2.  **Demonstrated robustness to domain shift.** The paper includes a thorough cross-domain evaluation, providing strong evidence that the rule-based nature of UDP makes it significantly more stable across different text domains compared to the data-driven transfer baseline.
3.  **An effective application of personalized PageRank for predicate identification.** While the underlying PageRank parsing idea is not new, the specific use of personalization to bias the graph ranking towards the main predicate is a simple and effective modification that improves performance.

**Strengths**
1.  **Impressive Performance for an Unsupervised System:** The proposed method is surprisingly effective, achieving an average UAS that is only 6.4% below a competitive supervised delexicalized transfer parser on gold POS tags. The fact that it can even outperform the baseline on Hindi demonstrates the power of exploiting the formalism's structural constraints without lexical information.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive. The evaluation across 32 languages, under three different POS tagging conditions (gold, predicted, and naive), provides a clear picture of the system's capabilities. The cross-domain analysis is particularly strong, showing with quantitative evidence (lower standard deviation in Table 6) that UDP is more robust to domain changes than its data-driven counterpart.
3.  **Simplicity and Language Independence:** The method is elegant and does not require any annotated training data, making it applicable to any language with a POS tagger. The on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality from test data is a clever feature that further enhances its adaptability without requiring language-specific, pre-configured parameters.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The work is heavily based on previous research, especially Søgaard (2012b), which also employed PageRank for unsupervised dependency parsing. The core contributions—adapting rules to UD and using personalization—while effective, feel more like incremental improvements upon an existing framework rather than a fundamentally new approach.
2.  **Under-explored and Arbitrary Personalization:** The personalization of PageRank, a key element of the method, is applied in a very limited way. It is used solely to up-weight a single, heuristically-identified main predicate with a weight of '5', a value that appears arbitrary and is only justified by tuning on English development data. The paper would be stronger if it explored the impact of this hyperparameter more thoroughly or proposed a more principled method for setting the personalization weights.
3.  **Clarity of Results Presentation:** While the tables are detailed, their density makes it difficult to quickly grasp key trends and comparisons. The inclusion of charts or graphs, for instance to visualize the performance drop with predicted POS tags across languages or the variance across domains, would have significantly improved the readability and impact of the results sections.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The performance of the system in the predicted POS setting (UDPP) is a key result. To better understand the relationship between tagger and parser performance, could you report the POS tagging accuracies achieved by the TnT tagger for each language? This would help clarify whether the parser's performance degradation is strongly correlated with the difficulty of POS tagging for a given language.
