### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser designed for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method is entirely rule-based, requiring no training data. It operates by first using a set of POS-based dependency rules to construct a graph over the words in a sentence. It then employs a personalized PageRank algorithm to score the prominence of content words, biasing the ranking towards the likely main predicate. Finally, a two-step decoding algorithm assembles the dependency tree, first connecting content words based on their PageRank scores and then attaching function words as leaves.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A simple, training-free UD parser:** The paper presents a complete, unsupervised parsing system that leverages the specific constraints of the UD formalism (e.g., function words as dependents) to achieve competitive results without any training data.
2.  **Extensive cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation:** The system is rigorously evaluated on 32 languages from the UD treebanks and its robustness is tested across different domains for a subset of these languages. This analysis demonstrates that the unsupervised approach is more stable across domain shifts than a supervised, delexicalized transfer-based system.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Robustness:** A key strength of this work is its simple and elegant approach. The parser relies on a small, interpretable set of rules and a well-understood algorithm (PageRank), making it easy to analyze. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that this simplicity leads to greater robustness against domain shift when compared to a data-driven transfer learning system (MSD), as shown by the lower standard deviation in performance across domains (Table 6).
2.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation is exceptionally thorough. Testing the system on 32 languages under different POS-tagging conditions (gold, predicted, and naive) provides a comprehensive picture of its performance and limitations. The detailed cross-domain analysis for several languages (Tables 6 and 7) is a valuable contribution that highlights a significant advantage of this rule-based approach.
3.  **Competitive Performance for an Unsupervised System:** Despite its training-free nature, the parser achieves surprisingly strong performance. On average, it lags behind the multi-source delexicalized parser by only 6.4 UAS points in the gold POS setting (UDPG vs. MSDG) and even outperforms it on one language (Hindi). This makes it a strong and viable baseline for unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The primary weakness of this submission is its limited novelty in light of prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012b), which also used PageRank and POS-based rules for unsupervised dependency parsing. The core contributions presented here—adapting the rules to UD, using personalization for the main predicate, and implementing a two-step decoding to enforce leaf status for function words—represent an effective and well-executed incremental adaptation rather than a fundamentally new approach to the problem.
2.  **Underdeveloped Use of Personalization:** The use of personalized PageRank is a promising idea, but its implementation in the paper feels superficial. The personalization is limited to applying a hard-coded weight of 5 to a single node identified by a simple heuristic (the first verb or content word). The choice of this weight appears arbitrary, and the potential for a more sophisticated, data-driven, or linguistically motivated personalization scheme is not explored.
3.  **Clarity of Results Presentation:** While the results are comprehensive, their presentation could be significantly improved. The heavy reliance on large tables (e.g., Table 5 and 7) makes it difficult for the reader to quickly grasp key trends, comparisons, and outliers. The inclusion of graphs or charts (e.g., bar charts comparing UDPG and MSDG per language, or a scatter plot of parsing degradation vs. POS tagging degradation) would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and impact.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In Section 5.3, you analyze the error propagation from POS tagging to parsing, noting that your system is less sensitive to POS errors than the MSD baseline. To better contextualize these findings, could you please report the actual POS tagging accuracies achieved by the TnT tagger for each language in the UDPP setting? This would allow for a more direct analysis of the correlation between tagging and parsing performance degradation.
