This paper presents a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and evaluates its performance across a range of languages. At its core, the algorithm uses a set of rules based on POS tags to define permitted dependencies. The process begins by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank algorithm on a graph where edges represent these permitted dependencies. The algorithm then iterates through the words in ranked order, linking each to the closest available head that preserves the tree structure while satisfying the head rules and a directionality constraint.

Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly presented. However, its primary contribution appears to be an incremental improvement over Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training".

I have a few questions and suggestions for improvement:

*   **Head Rules (Table 1):** An analysis of the proposed head rules in the context of the corpus data would be a valuable addition. For example, Section 3.1 mentions that the rules do not always produce a connected graph, but the paper does not quantify how frequently this occurs or the typical size of the resulting components. Such an analysis would provide deeper insight into the behavior of the rules.

*   **Head Direction:** The decision to determine head direction using the test set, rather than a development set, is surprising. While this may not be a major issue given how quickly the decision converges (within 10-15 sentences), it is an unorthodox choice that deviates from standard practice.

*   **Tie-Breaking:** The paper does not specify the tie-breaking mechanism for words with identical PageRank scores. It would be useful to clarify how ties are handled and to understand whether this choice significantly impacts performance, or if ties are rare enough that their resolution is negligible.

*   **Oracle Analysis:** The various constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) necessarily impose an upper bound on the system's potential performance. It would be highly informative to include oracle results for each constraint. This would quantify the extent to which each component limits the maximum achievable score and would be particularly helpful for guiding future work by pinpointing the most promising areas for improvement.

*   The column ordering in Tables 5 and 7 is inconsistent. I found the arrangement in Table 7 to be clearer.
