### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces UDP, a deterministic, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies. The method is training-free and relies on a small, pre-defined set of part-of-speech (POS) based head-selection rules. The core of the algorithm involves two main steps. First, it constructs a graph where edges represent potential dependencies licensed by the rules and uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank the content words in a sentence, biasing the rank towards a likely predicate. Second, it employs a two-step decoding process: it first builds a syntactic backbone by attaching content words to the tree in their rank order, and then attaches all function words as leaves. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages from the Universal Dependencies treebanks and compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer system.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **An extensive empirical evaluation and robustness analysis of a simple, rule-based unsupervised parser.** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across 32 languages with both gold and predicted POS tags. The cross-domain analysis is particularly valuable, demonstrating that this simple, linguistically-motivated system is significantly more robust to domain shift than a competitive data-driven transfer parser.
2.  **A simple and effective unsupervised parsing method tailored for the Universal Dependencies formalism.** While building on prior work, the paper presents a clear, self-contained algorithm that effectively leverages key UD principles, such as the constraint that function words must be dependents (leaves). The specific combination of personalized PageRank and two-step decoding proves to be a competitive approach for a system that requires no training data.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The paper's strongest aspect is its comprehensive evaluation. Testing on 32 languages provides a broad cross-linguistic perspective. More importantly, the inclusion of experiments with predicted POS tags and the detailed cross-domain analysis (Tables 6 and 7) offer significant insights. The finding that UDP is more stable across domains and less sensitive to POS tagging errors than the multi-source delexicalized system is a key result and a strong argument for the value of such a linguistically-informed, parameter-light approach.
2.  **Simplicity and Reproducibility:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. It is based on a small set of interpretable rules and a well-understood algorithm (PageRank). This makes the system easy to understand, analyze, and reimplement, which is a valuable quality for a baseline model in future research on unsupervised or cross-lingual parsing.
3.  **Strong Performance for a Training-Free System:** UDP achieves remarkably competitive performance for an unsupervised system. It significantly outperforms a simple adjacency baseline and closes a substantial portion of the gap to a supervised, multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), trailing by only 6.4% UAS on average with gold POS tags. The fact that it even surpasses the MSD system on Hindi demonstrates the effectiveness of its approach.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core idea of using PageRank over a graph derived from hand-crafted dependency rules is not new and builds directly on Søgaard (2012b). While the authors acknowledge this, the paper's contributions—personalizing PageRank and using a two-step decoding process to enforce UD constraints—feel more like incremental refinements than a fundamentally new approach. The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly delineated and quantified the performance gains attributable to its specific modifications over the prior work.
2.  **Use of Test Data for Parameter Estimation:** The system estimates adposition head directionality (pre- or postposition) by analyzing bigram frequencies directly on the test data. While the authors state this converges quickly (10-15 sentences), this practice violates the standard principle of keeping the test set entirely unseen during any part of the system's configuration. This is a methodological flaw that clouds the purity of the evaluation, even if the practical impact on scores is likely small.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of the System's Components:** The paper presents the final system and its results but offers limited insight into the behavior and contribution of its core components. For instance, the head rules in Table 1 are central, but their coverage across the 32 languages is not analyzed, nor is the frequency with which the back-off decoding steps are required. An oracle study showing the performance ceiling imposed by the hard-coded rules and directionality constraints would have been highly informative, helping to identify which components are the main sources of error and providing a clearer roadmap for future improvements.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The decoding algorithm depends on a strict ordering of content words provided by PageRank. How are ties in PageRank scores handled? Does the system use a secondary sorting key (e.g., sentence order), and have you measured whether ties are frequent enough to significantly impact performance?
2.  Could you elaborate on the decision to estimate adposition directionality from the test set? To maintain a stricter separation of data, would it be feasible to estimate this parameter from a small, language-agnostic development set or the first few sentences of each language's training data, without undermining the "training-free" claim?
3.  The head rules in Table 1 appear to have been developed using the English treebank. Could you provide more insight into this development process? Furthermore, have you analyzed how well these rules generalize by, for example, measuring their coverage or the error rates associated with each rule across the diverse set of 32 languages?
