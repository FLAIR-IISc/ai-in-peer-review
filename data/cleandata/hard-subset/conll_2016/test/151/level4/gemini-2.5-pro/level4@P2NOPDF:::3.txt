This paper proposes a dependency parsing method for Universal Dependency treebanks that relies exclusively on Part-of-Speech (POS) tags. The core of the approach is a modified PageRank algorithm, personalized to assign greater importance to key content words (such as the main predicate) over function words.

The central idea is intriguing, but its contribution appears to be incremental, bearing a strong resemblance to the work of Søgaard (2012). The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the distinctions between the two approaches to better highlight its novel contributions.

A promising aspect of the work is the personalization factor that gives additional weight to the main predicate. However, this concept feels underdeveloped. Based on the description, the personalization is applied only to the main predicate, and the choice of a weight of 5 seems arbitrary and lacks empirical justification. The work would be significantly strengthened by a more systematic exploration of this mechanism. For instance, the authors could investigate applying personalization to other content words or conduct an ablation study to determine an optimal weighting scheme.

Regarding the evaluation and analysis, the results are presented entirely in tables. While these tables are detailed, they make it difficult for the reader to discern overarching trends and key takeaways at a glance. The inclusion of charts or other visualizations would greatly enhance the clarity and impact of the analysis.

Furthermore, the analysis would be more complete if it reported the POS tagging accuracy when using predicted tags. This would enable a direct examination of the correlation between tagging errors and the corresponding degradation in parsing performance. An error analysis of this nature is crucial for understanding the practical limitations of a model that relies solely on an upstream POS tagger.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting direction, its incremental contribution over prior work is limited. The novel components are not fully explored, and the presentation of the results could be improved for better clarity. With a more robust justification of its novel aspects and a deeper analysis, the paper could represent a more significant contribution to the field.
