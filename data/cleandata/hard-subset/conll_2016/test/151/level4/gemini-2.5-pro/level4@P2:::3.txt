### Summary

This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The system operates without any training data, relying on a small set of hand-crafted, linguistically-motivated head-dependent rules based on Part-of-Speech (POS) tags. It employs a two-step decoding process to distinguish between content and function words, ensuring function words are treated as leaves in line with UD principles. A key component of the method is the use of personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate and rank content words for attachment, which guides the decoding process. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD treebanks and is compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer system, with additional analyses on robustness to POS tagging errors and domain shift.

### Contributions

1.  The primary contribution is a simple, transparent, and completely unsupervised rule-based parsing system for Universal Dependencies. It serves as a strong, linguistically-motivated baseline that, unlike many data-driven methods, requires no training data. Its performance is shown to be competitive with a standard delexicalized transfer parsing system.

2.  A second significant contribution is the extensive empirical evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domains. The analysis of the system's robustness to domain shift and noisy POS tags provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between rule-based and delexicalized transfer-based approaches, particularly highlighting the stability of the proposed method.

### Strengths

1.  **Robustness Across Domains:** The most compelling argument for this work is the demonstrated robustness of the parser to domain shift. As shown in Table 6, the UDP system exhibits a considerably lower average standard deviation in performance across different domains (e.g., 2.5 for UDPG vs. 4.5 for MSDG) compared to the multi-source delexicalized (MSD) baseline. This provides strong evidence for the hypothesis that a system based on general linguistic principles is less susceptible to the sampling biases of training data, making it a reliable tool for parsing out-of-domain text.

2.  **Competitive Performance without Training:** The parser's performance is surprisingly strong for a fully unsupervised system. On average, across 32 languages, it performs only 6.4 UAS points below a supervised, multi-source delexicalized transfer system (Table 5). The fact that it even outperforms the MSD system on Hindi highlights its effectiveness and potential as a strong baseline for cross-lingual parsing research, especially in low-resource scenarios where no training data is available.

3.  **Simplicity and Interpretability:** The method's simplicity is a key strength. The parser is built on a small, explicit set of rules (Table 1) and a transparent decoding algorithm. This makes its behavior easy to understand, analyze, and potentially extend, which is a significant advantage over more complex, black-box neural or statistical models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The primary weakness is the limited novelty of the proposed method over prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012b). The core idea of using PageRank on a graph derived from dependency rules to score words for unsupervised parsing was introduced in that work. The main extensions presented here—personalization for the main predicate and a two-step decoding for function words—feel like incremental improvements rather than a fundamentally new approach.

2.  **Superficial Use of Personalization:** The personalization of PageRank, a key claimed contribution, is applied in a limited and somewhat arbitrary manner. It is used only to up-weight the heuristically identified main predicate by a fixed factor of 5. The choice of this value is not well-justified beyond tuning on the English development set. A more sophisticated use of personalization, perhaps incorporating other linguistic priors or a more principled method for determining weights, would have strengthened this contribution significantly.

3.  **Clarity of Results Presentation:** While the paper presents a wealth of experimental results, their presentation in large tables makes it difficult to discern key trends and takeaways. For instance, Tables 5 and 7 are dense with numbers, obscuring comparisons. The analysis would have been greatly enhanced by visualizations, such as plots showing the relationship between POS tagging accuracy and parsing performance per language, or bar charts comparing the standard deviation in performance across domains for different systems. This would make the paper's strong empirical findings more accessible and impactful.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The personalization factor for the main predicate is set to 5. Could you elaborate on how this value was chosen? How sensitive is the parser's performance to this hyperparameter? For instance, what is the performance with values like 2 or 10, or with no personalization at all?

2.  Section 5.3 mentions an average TnT POS tagging accuracy of 94.1%. However, performance likely varies significantly across the 32 languages. Could you provide a per-language breakdown of POS tagging accuracy for the UDPP setting? This would allow for a more detailed analysis of the correlation between POS tagging errors and parsing performance degradation across different languages and language families.
