This paper proposes a method for dependency parsing of Universal Dependency treebanks that relies exclusively on Part-of-Speech (POS) tags. The core of the approach is a modified PageRank algorithm, which prioritizes syntactically significant words by down-weighting stop words.

The central idea is intriguing, but its novelty is limited as it builds very closely on the methodology presented by Søgaard (2012). The main extension appears to be the introduction of a personalization factor in the PageRank algorithm to assign more weight to the main predicate. While this is a promising addition, its implementation feels underdeveloped. As described, the personalization is solely applied to the main predicate, and the choice of a weight of 5 seems arbitrary. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a justification for this specific value, ideally supported by an ablation study or empirical tuning. A more thorough exploration of this personalization mechanism, such as applying it to other key syntactic elements, could have constituted a more substantial contribution.

Regarding the evaluation and analysis, the presentation could be improved. While the paper provides detailed tables of results, the key trends and takeaways are often difficult to discern from the raw numbers alone. The inclusion of charts or graphs to visualize the results across different languages or phenomena would greatly enhance readability and help to highlight the main findings more effectively.

Furthermore, a valuable addition to the analysis would have been a report on the POS tagging accuracy in the prediction setting. This would allow for a direct examination of the correlation between POS tagging errors and the resulting degradation in parsing performance. Such an analysis is crucial for understanding error propagation in the proposed pipeline and would likely confirm the expected relationship between the performance of these two stages.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting application and a minor extension of a previously established technique. However, the contribution over the work of Søgaard (2012) is incremental. While the paper is generally well-written, the limited novelty and the lack of in-depth exploration of its new components are notable weaknesses.
