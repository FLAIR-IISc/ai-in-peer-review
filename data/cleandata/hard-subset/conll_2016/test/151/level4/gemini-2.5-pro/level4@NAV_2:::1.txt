### Summary of the paper and contributions
This paper presents UDP, a deterministic, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that is entirely rule-based and requires no training data. The method operates by first building a graph of potential dependencies from a small set of POS-based head rules. It then employs a personalized PageRank algorithm to score and rank content words, giving priority to likely sentence predicates. A subsequent two-step decoding algorithm attaches words to their closest valid heads, processing content words first, followed by function words. This design explicitly enforces the Universal Dependencies constraint that function words should be leaf nodes in the final tree.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1. The development of a simple yet effective unsupervised parsing algorithm that combines personalized PageRank with a two-step decoding process to enforce key UD principles. The resulting system achieves performance that is highly competitive with a strong, multi-source delexicalized transfer parsing baseline.
2. A comprehensive empirical evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domains. The analysis provides convincing evidence that this rule-based approach is significantly more robust to domain shifts and less sensitive to POS tagging errors than its data-driven counterpart, highlighting its utility in low-resource and cross-domain settings.

### Strengths
1. **Effective Simplicity and Strong Performance:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity, relying on a small, linguistically-motivated rule set and the well-established PageRank algorithm without the need for any training data. Despite this simplicity, it achieves remarkably strong performance across a diverse set of languages, closing a significant portion of the performance gap to a supervised delexicalized transfer system. This establishes it as a very strong and accessible baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.
2. **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** A key strength of the paper lies in its thorough investigation of the parser's robustness. The cross-domain experiments in Section 6.3 provide clear quantitative evidence (Table 6) that UDP is substantially more stable across different text genres than the data-driven MSD parser. The consistently lower standard deviation in its performance demonstrates a reduced sensitivity to data sampling bias, which is a critical advantage for practical, real-world applications where domain mismatch is common.
3. **Thorough and Insightful Evaluation:** The paper is supported by an extensive and well-executed evaluation on 32 languages. The authors analyze performance not only with gold POS tags but also with predicted tags and a naive content/function tagging scheme, providing a realistic and multi-faceted assessment of the system's capabilities. Furthermore, the ablation study isolating the contribution of PageRank and the breakdown of performance by POS tag add significant depth and value to the analysis.

### Weaknesses
1. **Incremental Novelty Over Prior Work:** While the system is well-engineered and thoroughly evaluated, its core ideas build very closely on previous research, particularly Søgaard (2012b). The main novelties appear to be the use of PageRank personalization to better identify the main predicate and the two-step decoding process to enforce UD's function-word-as-leaf constraint. These are valuable adaptations for parsing UD-style dependencies, but they represent incremental improvements rather than a fundamentally new paradigm for unsupervised parsing.
2. **Lack of Analysis on Constraint-Imposed Performance Ceilings:** The system's accuracy is fundamentally limited by its three main components: the fixed head rules (Table 1), the head directionality heuristics, and the closest-attachment decoding strategy. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an oracle study that quantifies the upper bound on performance imposed by these constraints. For instance, what UAS could be achieved if an oracle selected the correct head from the set of candidates permitted by the head rules? Such an analysis would provide crucial insight into the primary sources of error and be invaluable for guiding future improvements to this line of work.
3. **Unconventional Use of Test Data for Parameter Estimation:** The method estimates adposition directionality on-the-fly from the test data itself. Although the authors argue this converges quickly, using the test set to tune any aspect of a model is an unconventional practice that complicates the interpretation of the results. It would be preferable to use a standard development set or to demonstrate that a single setting derived from one language's development data (e.g., English) generalizes sufficiently well across languages.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the head rules in Table 1: you mention that they do not always yield a connected graph for the PageRank algorithm. Could you provide some statistics on how frequently this occurs across the languages tested, and what the typical size of the resulting graph components is?
2.  Could you clarify the motivation for estimating adposition directionality from the test data rather than using a universal development set? Would using a single, fixed setting derived from the English development data (as was done for other function words) significantly degrade performance across the 32 languages?
3.  How does the algorithm handle ties in PageRank scores when ranking content words? Is this a frequent occurrence in your experiments, and does the tie-breaking method have a noticeable impact on final parsing accuracy?
4.  To better understand the system's inherent limitations (as mentioned in Weakness #2), would it be possible to perform an oracle experiment? For example, what is the upper-bound UAS if your system is constrained by the head rules in Table 1 but an oracle chooses the correct head among the valid candidates? This would help distinguish errors caused by the rule set's limitations versus those from the closest-attachment heuristic.
