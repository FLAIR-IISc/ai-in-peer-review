### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a novel unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The approach is rule-based, requires no training data, and relies on a small set of UD-compliant head rules, a two-step decoding algorithm, and personalized PageRank to identify and rank clausal heads. The system's effectiveness is demonstrated across 32 languages, where it is shown to be competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. A key finding is the system's superior robustness to domain shifts compared to its data-driven counterpart.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **An effective and simple unsupervised parsing algorithm.** The proposed combination of personalized PageRank for predicate salience, a small set of linguistically-motivated rules, and a two-step decoding process that enforces UD constraints (e.g., function words as leaves) is a novel and elegant approach. Its strong performance relative to a supervised transfer system across a wide range of languages highlights its practical value as a tool and a strong baseline.
2.  **A convincing demonstration of robustness to domain variation.** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation showing that this formalism-driven parser is significantly more stable across different domains and genres than a standard data-driven transfer model. This is a critical finding, underscoring the benefits of linguistically-grounded models in real-world, multi-domain scenarios.
3.  **A strong, accessible baseline for low-resource UD parsing.** By being training-free and language-agnostic (apart from POS tags), the parser serves as an excellent and hard-to-beat baseline for future research in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing, particularly for languages with no available treebanks.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Strong Performance:** The proposed method is remarkably simple and requires no training, yet it achieves impressive performance that is competitive with a strong, supervised multi-source delexicalized parser. An average UAS difference of just 6.4% across 32 languages (Table 5, UDPG vs. MSDG) is an excellent result for a fully unsupervised system. This makes the approach highly practical, especially in low-resource settings.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough and convincing. The evaluation spans a diverse set of 32 languages and considers realistic scenarios with both gold and predicted POS tags. The error propagation analysis (Section 5.3) provides valuable insight, showing UDP is less sensitive to POS errors than the baseline.

3.  **Excellent Analysis of Cross-Domain Robustness:** The paper's most significant strength is its detailed investigation of cross-domain performance (Section 6.3, Tables 6 and 7). The authors clearly demonstrate that their rule-based system exhibits much lower variance in performance across domains compared to the data-driven model. This provides strong evidence for the hypothesis that parsers based on general linguistic principles are more robust than those sensitive to the statistical biases of training data.

4.  **Well-Motivated and Interpretable Model:** The design of the parser is well-motivated by the principles of the Universal Dependencies formalism. Key architectural choices, such as the two-step decoding to ensure function words are leaves and the use of PageRank to identify prominent words, are intuitive and directly tied to linguistic principles. This makes the model's behavior highly interpretable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on Hand-Tuned Rules and Parameters:** The system's core is built on a set of hand-crafted head rules (Table 1) and fixed attachment directions for several function word classes, which were developed using the English dev set. This raises concerns about a potential English-centric bias. The paper notes that the fixed right-attachment for determiners is incorrect for Basque, highlighting the limitation of this approach. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the sensitivity to these rules or an exploration of methods to learn them from unlabeled data.

2.  **Limited Scope of Baselines in Unsupervised Parsing:** The primary comparison is against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. While this is a strong and relevant baseline for cross-lingual parsing, the paper omits a direct comparison with other well-known unsupervised dependency parsing models, such as Dependency Model with Valence (DMV). While the authors cite prior work showing a similar system outperforming DMV, a direct comparison on the large and diverse UD dataset would be more conclusive for situating this work within the broader field of grammar induction.

3.  **Oversimplified Main Predicate Heuristic:** The heuristic for identifying the main predicate (first verb or first content word), which seeds the personalized PageRank, is quite simple. While seemingly effective on average, this strong prior may be fragile for languages with more flexible word order (e.g., VSO/VOS) or in sentences with multiple or complex clauses where the main predicate is not the first verb. A more detailed analysis of failure cases related to root attachment would be beneficial.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The head rules in Table 1 and the fixed attachment directions for function words (e.g., AUX, DET) were developed using English data. Could you comment on the potential English-specific bias introduced by this process? Have you experimented with methods to induce these rules or directions from unlabeled data for a target language, similar to your on-the-fly estimation for adpositions?

2.  The heuristic for personalizing PageRank (boosting the first verb/content word) is a crucial component of your model. How sensitive is the overall performance to this heuristic? For example, how does the model perform on sentences with multiple clauses or in verb-initial languages where the main predicate might not be the first verb encountered?

3.  You mention excluding the NOUN → VERB rule during development to make the hierarchical relation between verbs and nouns "less conclusive," which prevents the model from handling relative clauses. Could you please elaborate on this design choice? What was the performance impact (positive or negative) on other dependency types when this rule was included during your development experiments?
