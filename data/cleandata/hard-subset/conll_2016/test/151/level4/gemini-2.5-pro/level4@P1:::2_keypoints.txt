- Paper introduces UDP: a training-free unsupervised UD parser using a small set of POS-based head-dependent rules, personalized PageRank to find predicates, and a deterministic two-step decoder; evaluated on 32 languages with strong robustness.

- Contribution: A simple, effective unsupervised parser that rivals supervised multi-source baselines and serves as a practical low-resource/cross-lingual baseline.

- Contribution: Novel use of personalized PageRank to identify predicates combined with a decoder that encodes UD priors (content words attached first, function words as leaves).

- Strength: Simplicity and effectiveness — competitive UAS (57.5% gold POS) close to supervised baselines and even outperforming them on some languages (e.g., Hindi).

- Strength: Excellent robustness — small degradation from gold to predicted POS and greater stability across domains compared to supervised baselines.

- Strength: Clear presentation and thorough analysis, including examples, ablations isolating PageRank, and POS-wise breakdowns.

- Strength: Truly training-free approach that requires no corpora for parameter estimation, offering a linguistically-motivated baseline for low-resource work.

- Weakness: Reliance on a fixed set of hand-crafted rules (derived from English dev set) may introduce language bias and limits handling of constructions like relative clauses.

- Weakness: Practical dependency on accurate supervised POS taggers — performance drops substantially without a good tagger, limiting use in truly low-resource languages.

- Weakness: Simplified treatment of complex phenomena (e.g., punctuation, coordination) leads to very low UAS on punctuation and conjunctions, reducing utility for fine-grained syntax tasks.

- Questions: How were the Table 1 rules validated for cross-linguistic applicability and could rules be derived automatically from UD guidelines?; why does the simple PageRank personalization (first verb/content word) work so robustly and were alternative, order-agnostic strategies tried?
