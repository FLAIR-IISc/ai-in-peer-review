### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces UDP, a simple and entirely unsupervised dependency parser designed for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data. Its core logic relies on a small, fixed set of linguistically-motivated dependency rules based on Part-of-Speech (POS) tags. To parse a sentence, the algorithm first constructs a graph where edges represent potential dependencies licensed by these rules. It then uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank the content words, biasing the main verb to be the root. Finally, a deterministic two-step decoding process attaches words to their heads based on this ranking, proximity, and directionality constraints, ensuring that function words are always leaf nodes, in line with UD principles. The authors perform an extensive evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domains, comparing UDP against a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline.

### Contributions

1.  **A Simple, Effective Unsupervised Baseline for UD Parsing:** The primary contribution is a straightforward, rule-based parsing algorithm that is easy to understand and implement. Despite its simplicity and lack of training, it serves as a strong baseline, achieving performance remarkably close to a supervised delexicalized transfer system across a wide range of languages.
2.  **Extensive Cross-Lingual and Cross-Domain Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough empirical analysis on 32 UD treebanks. A key finding is that the proposed rule-based system is significantly more robust to domain shift than the data-driven transfer parser, providing valuable evidence for the stability of such approaches.
3.  **Novel Application of PageRank and a UD-Compliant Decoder:** The paper adapts prior work on PageRank-based parsing by introducing personalization to better identify the main predicate. Furthermore, its two-step decoding algorithm is novel in its explicit enforcement of the UD constraint that function words must be leaves, which contributes to its effectiveness.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Strong Performance:** The proposed UDP system is elegant in its simplicity. It relies on a handful of POS-based rules and a deterministic decoding algorithm, making it highly transparent and reproducible. Despite this, it achieves impressive results, with an average Unlabeled Attachment Score (UAS) only 6.4% below a competitive multi-source delexicalized transfer system on gold POS tags. Its strong performance makes it an excellent and valuable baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive and provides convincing evidence for the system's utility. The evaluation across 32 languages demonstrates broad applicability. More importantly, the cross-domain analysis in Section 6.3 is a major strength. The finding that UDP exhibits a much lower variance in performance across domains than the data-driven baseline (average standard deviation of 2.5 vs. 4.5 for gold POS) is a significant result that underscores the robustness of linguistically-informed, rule-based systems.
3.  **Method is Well-Grounded in UD Principles:** The parser's design choices are well-motivated by the UD formalism itself. The distinction between content and function words, the enforcement of function words as leaves through two-step decoding, and the use of linguistically-plausible head rules (e.g., adverbs modifying verbs) align directly with the goals of UD. This principled design likely contributes to its cross-lingual stability.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty Over Prior Work:** The paper builds directly on Søgaard (2012b), which also proposed an unsupervised dependency parser using PageRank on a graph built from head-selection rules. While the authors acknowledge this, the exact contribution over this prior work could be clearer. The primary novelties appear to be the personalization of PageRank and the two-step decoding tailored for UD constraints. The paper would be stronger if it included a direct comparison or an ablation study to quantify the performance gains from these specific modifications over the original Søgaard (2012b) approach.
2.  **Lack of Analysis of the Hand-Crafted Rules:** The performance of the system is critically dependent on the head rules presented in Table 1. However, these rules are presented without extensive justification, beyond being tuned on the English development set. An analysis of these rules would significantly strengthen the paper. For instance, what is their coverage across the 32 languages? How frequently do they result in a disconnected graph (as mentioned in Section 3.1)? An oracle experiment showing the upper-bound performance achievable with these rules would also be highly informative and could help guide future work by indicating whether the main source of error is the rules themselves or the decoding algorithm.
3.  **Use of Test Data for Parameter Estimation:** The adposition head direction (pre- or post-positional) is estimated on-the-fly from the first 10-15 sentences of the test data. While this is a minor parameter and converges quickly, using the test set for any form of parameter tuning is questionable practice. This choice blurs the line between a purely unsupervised system and one that peeks at the test data. The authors should justify this decision more thoroughly or, preferably, show results using a fixed parameter derived from a small development set or from external typological information.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you further clarify the novelty of your method compared to Søgaard (2012b)? Specifically, what is the performance impact of using *personalized* PageRank and the two-step decoding mechanism compared to the original approach? An ablation that reverts to a non-personalized PageRank or a single-step decoder would be very insightful.
2.  The head rules in Table 1 are fundamental to your parser. Could you provide a brief analysis of their coverage? For example, what percentage of gold-standard dependencies in the test sets are licensed by these rules? This would help quantify the theoretical performance ceiling of your approach.
3.  Could you justify the choice to estimate adposition directionality from the test set? What is the impact on performance if you instead use a held-out development set or fix the direction based on known language typology for each language?
4.  In the PageRank scoring, how are ties between words with identical scores handled? Is this a frequent occurrence, and does the tie-breaking strategy have a noticeable impact on final parsing accuracy?
