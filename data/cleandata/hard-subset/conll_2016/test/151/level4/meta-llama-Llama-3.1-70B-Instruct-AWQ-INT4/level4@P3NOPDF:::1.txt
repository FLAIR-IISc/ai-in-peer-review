This paper presents a novel deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and evaluates its performance across a range of languages. The algorithm's core component is a set of rules that define permitted dependencies based on part-of-speech (POS) tags. It begins by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank over a graph with edges defined by the permitted dependencies. The algorithm then iterates through the ranking, linking each word to the closest word that maintains a tree structure and adheres to the head rules and a directionality constraint.

Overall, the paper is well-written and engaging, although it bears some resemblance to Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training". Despite this similarity, the paper offers a fresh perspective on dependency parsing and is worth reading.

There are several aspects of the paper that warrant further discussion and analysis. Firstly, the head rules presented in Table 1 could benefit from a more in-depth examination in relation to the corpus. For instance, in section 3.1, the authors mention that these rules do not always yield a connected graph, but they do not provide information on the frequency of this occurrence or the typical size of the resulting components. This additional context would be valuable in understanding the algorithm's behavior.

Furthermore, it is surprising that the authors chose to determine head direction using the test data rather than the training or development data. Although the decision converges quickly (within 10-15 sentences), this choice seems counterintuitive. The authors may want to consider justifying this decision or exploring alternative approaches in future work.

Another point that requires clarification is the tie-breaking mechanism for words with the same PageRank score. The authors do not specify how ties are resolved, and it is unclear whether this affects the algorithm's performance significantly. If ties are rare, this may not be a major concern, but it would be reassuring to have confirmation of this.

The various constraints employed in the algorithm, including head rules, directionality, and distance, inevitably impose upper bounds on the system's performance. To provide a more comprehensive understanding of these constraints' impact, it would be helpful to include oracle results for each constraint. This would enable the authors to demonstrate how much each constraint affects the maximum possible score, guiding future research on potential modifications to the system.

On a minor note, there are a few typographical errors and inconsistencies in the paper. For example, in section 4.1, the phrase "we obtain [the] rank" contains an unnecessary definite article. Additionally, the column orders in Table 5 and Table 7 differ, with the arrangement in Table 7 being clearer. Finally, in section 6.1, the phrase "isolate the [contribution] of both" contains an unnecessary definite article.

In conclusion, the paper presents an innovative approach to dependency parsing and is well-written. However, addressing the above points would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's behavior and potential limitations.
