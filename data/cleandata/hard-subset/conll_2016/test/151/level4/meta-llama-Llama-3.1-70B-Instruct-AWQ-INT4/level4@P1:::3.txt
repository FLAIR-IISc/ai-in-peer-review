This paper proposes a novel approach to parsing Universal Dependency (UD) trees without training, relying solely on part-of-speech (POS) constraints and a modified version of PageRank. The idea is intriguing, although it bears some resemblance to Søgaard (2012)'s work. The personalization factor, which gives more weight to the main predicate, is a nice touch, but its potential could be further explored. Currently, the personalization seems to be limited to the main predicate, and the weight of 5 appears to be an arbitrary choice.

The evaluation and detailed analyses are thorough, but the use of charts would have facilitated a clearer understanding of the results, as the tables can be overwhelming at times. Additionally, it would have been beneficial to include the scores of the POS tagging in prediction mode to assess whether the degradation in parsing performance is heavily correlated with the degradation in tagging performance, as one would expect.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach, but the increment over Søgaard (2012)'s work is relatively small. Some minor issues were noted, including a grammatical error ("The the main idea" should be "The main idea").

**Summary of the paper:**
The paper presents an unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) based on PageRank and a small set of specific dependency head rules. The parser requires no training and is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system. The authors evaluate their system on 32 languages and three setups, depending on the reliability of available POS tags, and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system.

**Main contributions:**
1. An unsupervised parsing algorithm for UD that relies solely on POS constraints and a modified version of PageRank.
2. A small set of specific dependency head rules that are adapted to UD-compliant POS relations.
3. The use of personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification.

**Strengths:**
1. The parser is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system, despite requiring no training.
2. The system is robust to domain change across languages.
3. The use of personalized PageRank is a nice touch, although its potential could be further explored.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The increment over Søgaard (2012)'s work is relatively small.
2. The personalization factor seems to be limited to the main predicate, and the weight of 5 appears to be an arbitrary choice.
3. The evaluation could benefit from the use of charts to facilitate a clearer understanding of the results.

**Questions to authors:**
1. Could you provide more insight into the choice of the weight 5 for the personalization factor, and whether other values were explored?
2. How do you plan to extend the parser to handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names?
3. Would it be possible to include the scores of the POS tagging in prediction mode to assess the correlation between parsing and tagging performance?
