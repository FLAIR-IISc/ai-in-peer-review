This paper presents a novel deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and evaluates its performance across a range of languages. The algorithm's core is a set of rules defining permitted dependencies based on POS tags. It starts by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank over a graph with edges defined by the permitted dependencies. Stepping through the ranking, each word is linked to the closest word that will maintain a tree and is permitted by the head rules and a directionality constraint.

Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly presented, although it appears to differ only slightly from Søgaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training". The authors' contributions to the existing work on rule-aided unsupervised dependency parsing are not entirely clear, and a more detailed comparison with Søgaard's work would be beneficial.

The paper's strengths include its ability to provide a competitive system for all Universal Dependencies (UD) languages without requiring training data. The use of personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification is a notable aspect of the algorithm. Additionally, the system's robustness to domain change across languages is a significant advantage.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas for improvement. The head rules (Table 1) could be analyzed in more depth, including their frequency of application and the typical size of the components they produce. It is also unclear how the decision to estimate head direction using test data rather than training or development data was made, although the authors note that this decision does not significantly impact performance.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the constraints used in the algorithm and their impact on performance. Including oracle results for each constraint would provide valuable insights into the maximum possible score and guide future work on modifying the system.

Some minor suggestions for improvement include:

* In Section 4.1, the sentence "we obtain [the] rank" should be rephrased for clarity.
* The columns in Table 5 and Table 7 are in different orders. The arrangement in Table 7 is clearer and should be used consistently throughout the paper.
* In Section 6.1, the phrase "isolate the [contribution] of both" should be rephrased for clarity.

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the head rules (Table 1) and their application in the algorithm?
* How did you decide to estimate head direction using test data rather than training or development data?
* Can you include oracle results for each constraint used in the algorithm to provide insights into the maximum possible score?
* How does tie-breaking for words with the same PageRank score work, and does it impact performance significantly?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and competitive dependency parsing algorithm. With some revisions to address the weaknesses and areas for improvement mentioned above, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
