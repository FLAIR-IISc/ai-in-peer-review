This paper proposes an unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages PageRank and a small set of specific dependency head rules. The algorithm, referred to as UDP, does not require training data and is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. Developing an unsupervised parsing algorithm that adapts dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations.
2. Incorporating the UD restriction of function words being leaves into the parsing algorithm.
3. Utilizing personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification.
4. Estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data, making the system completely free of language-specific parameters.

Strengths:
1. The proposed algorithm is competitive with a state-of-the-art delexicalized transfer parser, achieving a high unlabeled attachment score (UAS) across 32 languages.
2. UDP is robust to domain change across languages, outperforming the delexicalized transfer parser in some cases.
3. The algorithm is unsupervised, eliminating the need for training data, which is a significant advantage in low-resource settings.
4. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their system, including comparisons with a baseline and a state-of-the-art parser, as well as an analysis of the algorithm's performance across different parts of speech.

Weaknesses:
1. The algorithm relies heavily on the quality of the POS tags, which may not always be accurate, especially in low-resource languages.
2. The system's performance may degrade when faced with out-of-domain data or languages with unique grammatical structures.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the algorithm's time complexity, which may be an important consideration for large-scale parsing tasks.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of POS tag quality, particularly in low-resource languages?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the algorithm's performance on languages with unique grammatical structures, such as those with non-linear word order?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the algorithm to handle more complex linguistic phenomena, such as multiword expressions and coordination?
