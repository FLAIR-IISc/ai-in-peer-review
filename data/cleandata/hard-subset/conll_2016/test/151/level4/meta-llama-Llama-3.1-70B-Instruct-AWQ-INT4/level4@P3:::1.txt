**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies on a small set of specific dependency head rules and PageRank. The parser requires no training data and is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages and three setups, depending on the reliability of available POS tags, and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system. The results show that UDP is a solid and competitive system for all UD languages, with increased stability to domain change across languages.

**Main Contributions**

1. A novel unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm that adapts dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations.
2. The incorporation of UD restrictions on function words being leaves and the use of personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification.
3. The estimation of adposition attachment direction directly on test data, making the parser completely free of language-specific parameters.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a clear and well-structured description of the UDP algorithm and its components.
2. The evaluation of UDP on a large set of languages and setups provides a comprehensive understanding of its performance and robustness.
3. The comparison to a supervised cross-lingual system (MSD) demonstrates the competitiveness of UDP.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper does not provide an in-depth analysis of the head rules in relation to the corpus, which could help understand their impact on the parser's performance.
2. The choice of using test data to determine head direction is surprising and could be further justified.
3. The paper does not discuss the impact of tie-breaking for words with the same PageRank score on the parser's performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more information on the frequency and size of disconnected graphs resulting from the head rules?
2. How does the decision to use test data to determine head direction impact the parser's performance, and are there any plans to explore alternative methods?
3. Can you include oracle results for each constraint to show their impact on the maximum possible score and guide future work?

**Additional Comments**

1. Minor errors in grammar and formatting were found throughout the paper (e.g., "we obtain [the] rank" in Section 4.1).
2. The column order in Table 5 and Table 7 is different; the arrangement in Table 7 is clearer.
3. In Section 6.1, "isolate the [contribution] of both" should be rephrased for clarity.
