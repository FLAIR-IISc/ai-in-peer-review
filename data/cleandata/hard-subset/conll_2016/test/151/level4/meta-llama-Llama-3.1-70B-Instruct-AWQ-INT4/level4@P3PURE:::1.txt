This paper presents a novel deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and evaluates its performance across a range of languages. The algorithm's core is a set of rules defining permitted dependencies based on POS tags. It begins by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank over a graph with edges defined by the permitted dependencies. Stepping through the ranking, each word is linked to the closest word that maintains a tree and is permitted by the head rules and a directionality constraint.

Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly presented, although it appears to differ only slightly from Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training". To further strengthen the paper, I have a few questions and suggestions.

Firstly, it would be beneficial to provide some analysis of the Head Rules (Table 1) in relation to the corpus. For instance, in section 3.1, the paper mentions that these rules do not always lead to a connected graph, but it does not discuss the frequency of this occurrence or the typical size of the resulting components.

I was also surprised to find that the head direction was chosen using the test data rather than the training or development data. Although the decision converges quickly (within 10-15 sentences), this choice seems counterintuitive. Nevertheless, its impact is likely minor given the rapid convergence.

Another point of clarification is needed regarding tie-breaking for words with the same PageRank score. How does the algorithm handle such cases, and does it significantly impact performance? Alternatively, are ties rare enough that their effect is negligible?

The various types of constraints (head rules, directionality, and distance) impose upper bounds on the possible performance of the system. To provide a clearer understanding of these limitations, it would be informative to include oracle results for each constraint, illustrating the extent to which they affect the maximum possible score. This information would be particularly valuable for guiding future work, as it would help identify areas where modifications to the system could be most fruitful.

On a minor note, there are a few formatting issues and typos that should be addressed. In section 4.1, the phrase "we obtain [the] rank" contains an unnecessary definite article. Additionally, the columns in Table 5 and Table 7 are in different orders; I found the arrangement in Table 7 to be clearer. Finally, in section 6.1, the phrase "isolate the [contribution] of both" contains an unnecessary definite article.
