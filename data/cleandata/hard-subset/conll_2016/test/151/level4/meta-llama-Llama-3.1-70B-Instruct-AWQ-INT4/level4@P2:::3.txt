This paper presents an unsupervised dependency parsing approach for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies solely on part-of-speech (POS) constraints. The method, called UDP, adapts dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, incorporates the UD restriction of function words being leaves, uses personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, and estimates adposition attachment direction directly on test data. The approach is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system and robust to domain change across languages.

**Contributions:**

1. The development of an unsupervised dependency parsing approach that leverages the constraints of the UD formalism to achieve competitive performance without requiring training data.
2. The adaptation of dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, which enables the parser to effectively handle the nuances of different languages.
3. The use of personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, which is a crucial aspect of dependency parsing.

**Strengths:**

1. The parser's ability to achieve competitive performance without requiring training data, making it a valuable resource for low-resource languages.
2. The approach's robustness to domain change across languages, which is essential for real-world applications where domain shift is common.
3. The parser's ability to handle the nuances of different languages by adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The incremental contribution of this work over Søgaard (2012) is relatively small, as the core idea of using PageRank for dependency parsing is not new.
2. The personalization factor, which gives more weight to the main predicate, seems arbitrary and could be explored further to improve its effectiveness.
3. The evaluation and analysis could benefit from additional visualizations, such as charts, to facilitate easier interpretation of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more insight into the choice of the personalization factor (weight of 5) for the main predicate, and whether this value was optimized or chosen arbitrarily?
2. How do you plan to address the limitations of the current approach, such as handling multi-word expressions, coordination, and proper names?
3. Can you provide the scores of the POS tagging in prediction mode to assess the correlation between POS tagging performance and parsing performance degradation?
