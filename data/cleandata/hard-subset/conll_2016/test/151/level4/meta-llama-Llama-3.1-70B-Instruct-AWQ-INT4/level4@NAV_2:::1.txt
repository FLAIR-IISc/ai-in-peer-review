**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) called UDP. The algorithm relies on PageRank and a small set of specific dependency head rules, requiring no training data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages and compare its performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system.

**Main Contributions**

1. A linguistically sound unsupervised alternative to cross-lingual UD parsing that is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system.
2. A robust parser that is distinctly robust to domain change across languages.
3. A novel approach to estimating adposition direction directly from test data.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and clearly explained approach to unsupervised dependency parsing.
2. The evaluation is thorough, covering 32 languages and comparing UDP to a strong baseline.
3. The results show that UDP is competitive with a supervised transfer approach and more robust to domain change.

**Weaknesses**

1. The algorithm's reliance on a small set of hand-crafted rules may limit its generalizability to other languages or domains.
2. The use of PageRank may not be the most effective way to score content words, and alternative approaches could be explored.
3. The evaluation could be strengthened by including more detailed analysis of the parser's performance on specific linguistic phenomena.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How did you select the specific dependency head rules used in the algorithm, and what was the process for determining their effectiveness?
2. Can you provide more detail on how the adposition direction is estimated from test data, and how this approach compares to using training or development data?
3. How do you handle tie-breaking for words with the same PageRank score, and does this impact performance significantly?
4. Have you considered exploring alternative approaches to scoring content words, such as using machine learning models or other graph-based methods?
5. Can you provide more detailed analysis of the parser's performance on specific linguistic phenomena, such as verb final languages or languages with complex morphological systems?
