This paper presents a novel deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and evaluates its performance across multiple languages. 
At its core, the algorithm relies on a set of rules that define permissible dependencies based on part-of-speech (POS) tags.
The algorithm initiates by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank over a graph, with edges defined by the permitted dependencies.
It then iterates through the ranking, linking each word to the closest word that maintains a tree structure and adheres to the head rules and directionality constraints.
Overall, the paper is well-structured and engaging, although it appears to share similarities with Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training".
Several questions and suggestions arose during the review:
The head rules (Table 1) would benefit from a more in-depth analysis in relation to the corpus.
For instance, in section 3.1, it is mentioned that these rules do not always yield a connected graph, but the frequency of this occurrence and the typical size of the resulting components are not discussed.
The decision to use test data for determining head direction, rather than training or development data, was unexpected.
Although the decision converges rapidly (within 10-15 sentences), this choice seems unusual.
It would be helpful to clarify how tie-breaking is handled for words with identical PageRank scores and assess its impact on performance.
The various constraints (head rules, directionality, and distance) impose upper bounds on the system's potential performance.
Including oracle results for each constraint would provide valuable insights into their impact on the maximum achievable score, guiding future research on potential system modifications.
Minor suggestions include:
- In section 4.1, rephrase "we obtain [the] rank" for clarity.
- Standardize the column order in Table 5 and Table 7, as the arrangement in Table 7 is more intuitive.
- In section 6.1, rephrase "isolate the [contribution] of both" for improved readability.
