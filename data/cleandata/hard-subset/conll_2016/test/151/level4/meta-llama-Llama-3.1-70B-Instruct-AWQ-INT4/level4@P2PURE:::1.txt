This paper presents a novel deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and evaluates its performance across a range of languages. The algorithm's core component is a set of rules that define permitted dependencies based on part-of-speech (POS) tags. The parsing process begins by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank algorithm over a graph, where edges are defined by the permitted dependencies. The algorithm then iterates through the ranking, linking each word to the closest word that maintains a tree structure and adheres to the head rules and a directionality constraint.

Overall, the paper is well-written and engaging, although its contribution seems to be a minor extension of the work presented in Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training".

I have several questions and suggestions for improvement:

Regarding the head rules (Table 1), it would be beneficial to include an analysis of these rules in relation to the corpus. For instance, while Section 3.1 mentions that the head rules do not always yield a connected graph, it would be helpful to provide information on the frequency of this occurrence and the typical size of the resulting components.

I was surprised to find that the head direction was determined using the test data rather than the training or development data. Although the decision converges quickly (within 10-15 sentences), this choice seems unconventional. Could the authors provide insight into the reasoning behind this decision?

Additionally, I would like to know how the algorithm handles tie-breaking for words with the same PageRank score. Does this occur frequently enough to impact performance significantly, or are ties rare enough to be negligible?

The various constraints employed by the system, including head rules, directionality, and distance, will inevitably impose upper bounds on its performance. To provide a clearer understanding of the impact of these constraints, it would be informative to include oracle results for each constraint, demonstrating how they affect the maximum possible score. This information would be particularly useful for guiding future work on modifying and improving this system.

Minor suggestions:

* In Section 4.1, the phrase "we obtain [the] rank" could be rephrased for clarity.
* The column orders in Table 5 and Table 7 are inconsistent. I found the arrangement in Table 7 to be clearer and more intuitive.
* In Section 6.1, the phrase "isolate the [contribution] of both" could be rephrased for improved clarity.
