The authors present an unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The approach leverages PageRank to assess word relevance and incorporates a set of dependency head rules. 

The core contributions of this work, as I see them, are threefold. First, the authors demonstrate a viable approach to cross-lingual UD parsing *without* relying on training data, a significant step forward for low-resource languages. Second, the system achieves competitive performance compared to a delexicalized transfer system, showcasing the effectiveness of combining linguistically motivated rules with a graph-based ranking algorithm. Finally, the method exhibits robustness to domain change, a critical advantage over supervised approaches which can be susceptible to overfitting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves surprisingly strong performance, nearing the accuracy of a delexicalized transfer system across a wide range of languages (32 in total). This is a substantial result for an entirely unsupervised method and demonstrates the potential of leveraging the constraints inherent in the Universal Dependencies framework. The results in Table 5 clearly illustrate this point, particularly the consistent performance across languages.

2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The evaluation on multiple domains (Table 6 and 7) demonstrates a key strength of this approach. The lower standard deviation in performance across domains compared to the supervised baseline suggests that the rule-based nature of UDP makes it less susceptible to overfitting on specific datasets. This is particularly important for real-world applications where data distributions can vary significantly.

3. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a comprehensive explanation of the methodology. The rationale behind each design choice, from the PageRank personalization to the head direction heuristics, is clearly articulated. This allows for easy understanding and replication of the work. The inclusion of the example parse in Section 4 further enhances clarity.

4. **Sound Linguistic Motivation:** The authors ground their approach in established linguistic principles, particularly the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies framework.  The explicit use of POS constraints and the handling of function words as leaves demonstrate a thoughtful consideration of linguistic structure. This contrasts with purely data-driven approaches that may learn spurious correlations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Expressiveness:** While robust, the system’s reliance on a fixed set of rules limits its ability to capture more complex syntactic phenomena. The exclusion of the NOUN → VERB dependency rule, as noted by the authors, is one example of this limitation. This could impact performance on languages or datasets with richer syntactic structures.

2. **Sensitivity to POS Tagging Accuracy:** Although the method demonstrates greater robustness to POS errors than the supervised baseline, performance still degrades with predicted POS tags (UDPP). The quality of the initial POS tags remains a crucial factor, and inaccuracies can propagate through the parsing process. While the authors address this, further investigation into mitigating this sensitivity would be valuable.

3. **Heuristic-Based Head Direction:** The method for determining head direction, based on adposition frequency, is a reasonable heuristic but might not generalize well to all languages.  The observed variability in performance across languages (particularly for languages like Basque, noted in the discussion) suggests that this aspect could benefit from more sophisticated treatment.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions excluding the NOUN → VERB dependency rule. Could the authors elaborate on experiments performed with this rule included, and the specific issues encountered that led to its exclusion?
2.  Given the sensitivity to POS tagging accuracy, have the authors considered integrating a feedback loop where POS tags are refined based on the dependency parse? 
3.  The personalization of PageRank focuses on identifying the main predicate. Have the authors explored alternative personalization strategies, such as weighting other content words based on their frequency or semantic role?
