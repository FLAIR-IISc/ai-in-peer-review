This paper presents a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and analyzes its performance across multiple languages. The algorithm’s core is a set of rules that define permissible dependency relations based on part-of-speech (POS) tags. It operates by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank algorithm applied to a graph where edges represent these permitted dependencies. Iteratively, words are linked to the nearest suitable head – one that maintains a tree structure and adheres to the defined head rules and a directionality constraint. 

Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly explains a potentially valuable approach. However, the contribution feels incremental, exhibiting considerable similarity to Sogaard (2012)’s work on unsupervised dependency parsing. While this is not necessarily a weakness, explicitly outlining the differences and improvements over Sogaard (2012) would strengthen the paper’s impact.

I have several questions and suggestions that could further improve the work.

Regarding the head rules (Table 1), a more detailed analysis of their interaction with the corpora used would be beneficial. Section 3.1 notes that the rules do not always produce a connected graph; however, quantifying the frequency of this occurrence and the typical size of the resulting disconnected components would provide valuable insight. Understanding how often and to what extent the rules lead to incomplete parses is crucial for evaluating their effectiveness.

I found the choice to determine head direction using test data rather than dedicated training or development data somewhat surprising. While the rapid convergence of the decision process (10-15 sentences) mitigates this concern, the rationale behind this design choice warrants further explanation. 

The paper does not detail how ties are broken when words share identical PageRank scores. Given the potential for such ties, clarifying the tie-breaking mechanism and assessing its impact on performance—or confirming that ties are sufficiently rare to be inconsequential—would be helpful.

The imposed constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) inherently limit the algorithm’s potential performance. To better understand these limitations and guide future development, I recommend including oracle results for each constraint. Specifically, reporting performance metrics achievable when each constraint is relaxed individually would illuminate the extent to which each constraint restricts the system’s maximum possible score. This analysis would be particularly valuable in identifying promising avenues for future work, such as refining the head rules or relaxing the directionality constraint.

Finally, I have a few minor suggestions for improvement:

*   Section 4.1, “we obtain [the] rank” – adding “the” improves readability.
*   Tables 5 and 7 present columns in differing orders. I found the arrangement in Table 7 to be clearer and suggest consistency across both tables.
*   Section 6.1, “isolate the [contribution] of both” – replacing “both” with “individual” or a similar term would improve clarity.
