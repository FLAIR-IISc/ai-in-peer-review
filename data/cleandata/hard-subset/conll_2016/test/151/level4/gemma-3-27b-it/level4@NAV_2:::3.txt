This paper presents an unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) based on PageRank and dependency head rules, requiring no training data. The core idea – leveraging POS constraints and prioritizing content words via PageRank – is interesting, though closely related to previous work, particularly Søgaard (2012). The personalization of PageRank to emphasize the main predicate is a valuable addition, though the choice of a weight of 5 appears somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from further investigation.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are: 1) a robust, unsupervised parsing approach competitive with delexicalized transfer systems on a wide range of UD languages; 2) a demonstration of increased stability to domain change compared to supervised transfer learning; and 3) an effective integration of UD constraints – specifically function word placement – into an unsupervised parsing framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves surprisingly strong results, rivaling a delexicalized transfer system (MSD) across 32 languages. This is a significant accomplishment for an unsupervised approach, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed method. The results in Table 5 demonstrate this consistently.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The evaluation across different domains (Table 6) reveals that the UDP parser exhibits greater stability than the MSD system, indicating a reduced sensitivity to variations in text genre. This is a valuable attribute for real-world applications where domain adaptation can be challenging.
3. **Effective Use of UD Constraints:** The parser effectively exploits the constraints inherent in the UD formalism, such as the treatment of function words, to guide the parsing process. This reliance on linguistic principles rather than solely data-driven learning contributes to its robustness and generalizability.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The thorough evaluation across multiple languages, POS setups (gold vs. predicted), and domains provides strong evidence for the effectiveness and generalizability of the proposed approach. The inclusion of both standard metrics and ablation studies (e.g., removing PageRank) enhances the credibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the method is effective, the novelty compared to existing work, particularly Søgaard (2012), is limited. The personalization of PageRank is a worthwhile extension, but further exploration of this mechanism (e.g., dynamic weighting or alternative personalization strategies) could strengthen the contribution.
2. **Arbitrary Parameter Choice:** The weight of 5 assigned to the main predicate in the PageRank personalization step appears arbitrary and lacks justification. A more systematic investigation of this parameter, potentially through experimentation or theoretical analysis, would be beneficial.
3. **Presentation of Results:** While the tables are informative, the dense presentation of numerical results can be challenging to interpret. Incorporating visualizations (e.g., charts or graphs) could enhance the clarity and impact of the findings.
4. **Correlation Analysis:** The paper lacks an analysis of the correlation between POS tagging accuracy and parsing performance in the predicted POS setup. Reporting the POS tagging scores would allow for a more nuanced understanding of the error propagation and the relative contributions of each component.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed rationale for the choice of a weight of 5 for the main predicate in the PageRank personalization step? Did you explore alternative weighting schemes?
2.  What is the computational complexity of the parsing algorithm, particularly concerning the PageRank calculation for long sentences?
3.  The paper mentions excluding Japanese due to the lack of word forms. Have you considered alternative approaches to handle languages with different data representations?
