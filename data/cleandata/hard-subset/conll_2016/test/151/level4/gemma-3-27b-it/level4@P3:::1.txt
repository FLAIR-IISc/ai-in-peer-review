This paper presents a novel, deterministic dependency parsing algorithm, UDP, designed for Universal Dependencies, and analyzes its performance across a diverse set of languages. The core of the approach leverages a set of linguistically motivated dependency head rules combined with a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank words and guide dependency construction. The system operates without any training data, making it a compelling alternative to supervised and transfer learning methods.

The primary contribution of this work is a competitive, unsupervised dependency parser that demonstrates robustness across languages and domains. While building upon prior work in rule-aided unsupervised parsing (e.g., Søgaard, 2012), the authors effectively adapt dependency head rules to the UD framework, incorporating UD-specific constraints and utilizing personalized PageRank for improved predicate identification. The method’s ability to operate without language-specific parameters and estimate adposition attachment direction directly on test data is also a significant aspect of the contribution.

A secondary, valuable contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation of the system across 32 languages and multiple domains. The detailed experimental setup, including comparisons to a strong delexicalized transfer baseline, provides a thorough assessment of UDP's performance and robustness. The analysis of performance degradation under varying POS tag quality and across domains offers valuable insights into the system’s strengths and limitations.

Finally, the paper provides a clear and detailed description of the algorithm, facilitating reproducibility and further research. The presentation is well-organized and the experimental results are presented in a clear and accessible manner.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** UDP achieves remarkably competitive results, approaching the performance of a supervised delexicalized transfer parser (MSDG) on many languages, particularly considering its unsupervised nature. As demonstrated in Table 5, UDPG often outperforms MSDG, demonstrating the effectiveness of the rule-based approach and the benefits of leveraging UD’s constraints. This is a significant achievement for an unsupervised parsing system.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The experimental results demonstrate UDP’s superior robustness to domain changes compared to the delexicalized transfer baseline (MSD). Table 6 clearly illustrates that UDP exhibits lower variance in performance across different domains, suggesting that the system is less susceptible to biases introduced by specific datasets. This robustness is a key advantage for applications involving diverse text sources.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The extensive evaluation across 32 languages, combined with analysis of POS tag quality and domain sensitivity, provides a compelling and rigorous assessment of the system. The inclusion of both gold-standard and predicted POS evaluations allows for a realistic assessment of the parser’s practical applicability.  The detailed breakdown by POS tag in Section 6.2 provides valuable insights into the system’s behavior and potential areas for improvement.
4. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the algorithm, including the head rules (Table 1), PageRank setup, and decoding process (Fig. 1). This level of detail is crucial for reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the individual components – PageRank, rule-based parsing, and UD constraints – are not entirely novel. The paper needs to more strongly articulate the specific innovations that differentiate UDP from existing approaches, especially Søgaard (2012). A clearer positioning within the broader landscape of unsupervised parsing would strengthen the contribution.
2. **Dependency Rule Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the head rules (Table 1). While the rules are crucial to the algorithm’s performance, there is limited discussion regarding their origin, justification, or potential impact on different languages.  Providing statistics on the frequency of rule application or analyzing errors related to specific rules would enhance the understanding of the system’s behavior. As the authors mention, the lack of connected graphs isn’t quantified, which limits understanding of the rules’ coverage.
3. **Tie-Breaking Mechanism:** The paper does not explicitly describe how ties are broken when multiple words have the same PageRank score. The impact of this tie-breaking mechanism on performance is unclear. Clarifying this aspect of the algorithm would improve transparency and reproducibility.
4. **Oracle Performance Bounds:** The authors rightly point out that the constraints imposed by the head rules and other factors will limit the system's potential performance. However, the paper does not provide oracle results to quantify these limitations. Including oracle scores for each constraint (e.g., an oracle using only the head rules) would provide a valuable benchmark and guide future development efforts.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more details on the selection and justification of the head rules presented in Table 1? What criteria were used to determine which dependencies to include, and how were conflicts resolved?
2. Could you elaborate on the tie-breaking mechanism used when multiple words have the same PageRank score? Does the choice of tie-breaking strategy significantly impact performance?
3. Would it be possible to provide oracle results for each of the constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) to quantify their impact on potential performance?
4. In Section 6.3, you mention analyzing the impact of the initial PageRank personalization. Could you provide more details on that analysis? What specific findings motivated the 5x weight given to the estimated main predicate?
