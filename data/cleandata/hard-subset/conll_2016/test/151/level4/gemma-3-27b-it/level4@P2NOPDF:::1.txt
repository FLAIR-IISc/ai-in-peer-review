This paper introduces a novel, deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and evaluates its performance across multiple languages. The algorithm relies on a rule-based system defining permissible dependencies based on part-of-speech (POS) tags. It initiates parsing by ranking words using a modified PageRank algorithm applied to a graph where edges represent these permitted dependencies. The algorithm then iteratively links each word to its nearest permissible head, ensuring the construction of a tree structure while adhering to defined head rules and directionality constraints. 

The paper is well-written and presents an interesting approach to dependency parsing. However, the method appears to represent an incremental improvement over Sogaard’s (2012) work on unsupervised dependency parsing.

I have several questions and suggestions that could strengthen the paper.

Regarding the head rules (Table 1), a more detailed analysis of their relation to the corpora used would be valuable. While Section 3.1 notes that the rules occasionally result in disconnected graphs, the frequency of this occurrence and the typical size of the resulting components are not quantified. Providing this information would enhance understanding of the rule set’s limitations.

I also found the choice to determine head direction using the test set, rather than dedicated training or development data, somewhat unusual. While the rapid convergence of the decision-making process (10-15 sentences) mitigates this concern, the rationale behind this design choice deserves further explanation.

Clarification on the tie-breaking mechanism for words with identical PageRank scores would be appreciated. Determining whether ties are infrequent or significantly impact performance is important for a complete understanding of the algorithm.

The imposed constraints—head rules, directionality, and distance limitations—inherently establish upper bounds on the system’s potential performance. Including oracle results for each constraint, demonstrating the performance reduction caused by each, would be highly informative. This would particularly aid in directing future development efforts by pinpointing areas where modifications could yield the greatest improvements. Specifically, understanding which constraints are most restrictive would be beneficial.

Finally, I have a few minor comments:

*   In Section 4.1, “we obtain [the] rank” should be revised.
*   The column order in Table 5 and Table 7 differs. I found the arrangement in Table 7 to be more intuitive and suggest adopting it consistently.
*   In Section 6.1, “isolate the [contribution] of both” should be rephrased for clarity.
