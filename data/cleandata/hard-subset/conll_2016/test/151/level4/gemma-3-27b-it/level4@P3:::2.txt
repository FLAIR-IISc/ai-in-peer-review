This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages PageRank and a set of dependency head rules, requiring no training data. The core idea is to exploit the constraints inherent in the UD formalism to build a robust and cross-lingually adaptable parser.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is a practical demonstration of the feasibility of competitive unsupervised parsing *within* the constraints of a well-defined linguistic framework (UD). While unsupervised parsing is not new, achieving performance comparable to supervised transfer learning, and exhibiting robustness to domain shift, represents a significant advance. A secondary contribution lies in the effective combination of PageRank personalization – prioritizing likely predicate identification – with linguistically motivated head rules. Finally, the thorough evaluation across a substantial number of languages (32) and under varying POS tag reliability conditions provides a valuable resource and benchmark for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores, often surpassing a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, particularly when considering robustness to domain shift. This is evidenced by the results presented in Table 5 and Table 6, demonstrating that UDP maintains a comparatively stable performance across different genres and domains. The consistent performance across diverse languages (Table 5) further highlights the system’s generalizability.
2. **Novel Integration of Linguistic Constraints and Graph-Based Ranking:** The paper effectively integrates the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies framework – specifically, the treatment of function words – with a graph-based ranking algorithm (PageRank).  The use of personalization within PageRank to prioritize content words is a clever approach that demonstrably improves parsing accuracy. This combination is well-explained and justified.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors consider multiple scenarios (gold POS tags, predicted POS tags, naive POS tags), as well as domain adaptation, providing a nuanced understanding of the parser’s strengths and weaknesses.  The inclusion of both in-house and publicly available datasets (Table 6) strengthens the validity of the findings.
4. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The results consistently show that UDP is more resilient to domain changes compared to the supervised baseline (MSD), as highlighted in Table 6. This is a significant advantage for practical applications where the parser may encounter diverse text types. The detailed analysis of performance across various domains provides valuable insights.
5. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology. The two-step decoding algorithm (Fig. 1) and the PageRank setup are described in a precise and accessible manner. The inclusion of an example run (Section 4) is particularly helpful for understanding the system’s operation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Handling of Complex Structures:** The parser's reliance on relatively simple head rules might limit its ability to accurately parse sentences with complex syntactic structures, such as those involving coordination or non-projective dependencies. While acknowledged in the discussion, the extent of this limitation warrants further investigation.
2. **Sensitivity to POS Tagging Errors:** While the parser demonstrates robustness compared to the supervised baseline, performance still degrades significantly when using predicted POS tags (Table 5).  This highlights the importance of high-quality POS tagging for optimal performance and suggests a potential area for improvement through integration with more sophisticated POS taggers or error correction mechanisms.
3. **Lack of Edge Labeling:** The parser currently only focuses on unlabeled attachment score. Adding the capacity to predict dependency relations (edge labels) would significantly enhance its functionality and applicability. This is acknowledged as a future direction, but its omission limits the current scope of the work.
4. **Rule Development Reliance on English:** Although the head rules are intended to be generalizable, their initial development and tuning were performed on English data. It’s unclear to what extent this introduces a bias that might affect performance on languages with significantly different syntactic characteristics.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis identifying specific syntactic structures or linguistic phenomena that UDP consistently struggles with? 
2.  How sensitive is the system to the choice of teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm, and how was the value of 0.05 determined?
3.  What are the computational costs associated with running UDP compared to the delexicalized transfer parser, particularly in terms of processing time and memory usage?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper’s extensive referencing is commendable.
*   The availability of the parser and annotated datasets is a valuable contribution to the research community.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough and provides a good context for the presented research.
