This paper describes a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and analyzes its behavior across a range of languages. The core of the algorithm is a set of rules defining permitted dependencies based on POS tags. The algorithm starts by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank over a graph with edges defined by the permitted dependencies. Stepping through the ranking, each word is linked to the closest word that will maintain a tree and is permitted by the head rules and a directionality constraint. Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly presented, though it builds upon existing work such as Søgaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training."

The primary contribution of this work is a competitive unsupervised dependency parsing approach that leverages the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework, achieving results comparable to supervised transfer learning methods. A second, noteworthy contribution lies in the novel combination of personalized PageRank with a two-step decoding process designed to explicitly handle content and function words according to UD guidelines. Finally, the robustness of the parser to domain shift, demonstrated through cross-domain evaluation, is also a significant aspect of this submission.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves remarkably competitive performance, approaching the accuracy of a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) on many languages, particularly considering it requires no training data. The results in Table 5 clearly demonstrate this, with UDPG frequently matching or exceeding MSDG, and maintaining a small performance gap when using predicted POS tags (UDPP). This is a strong indicator of the method’s efficacy and its ability to generalize across languages.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The extensive cross-domain evaluation (Table 6 and Table 7) reveals that UDP is more resilient to changes in domain compared to the supervised MSD baseline. The consistently lower standard deviation in UAS scores across different domains for UDP suggests a greater stability and less susceptibility to domain-specific biases. This is a critical advantage for real-world applications where the test data distribution may differ from the training data.
3. **Principled Approach Grounded in UD:** The parser's design is firmly rooted in the principles of the Universal Dependencies framework, explicitly exploiting the constraints and conventions of UD to guide the parsing process. This approach, rather than relying on large amounts of data, contributes to the method’s robustness and generalizability. The use of UD-compliant POS relations and the treatment of function words as leaves are particularly effective.
4. **Efficient and Deterministic:** The algorithm is deterministic and relatively efficient, avoiding the complexities of probabilistic models and training procedures. The two-step decoding algorithm, coupled with the PageRank-based ranking, allows for a straightforward and predictable parsing process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the individual components – PageRank, rule-based parsing, and UD constraints – are not entirely novel. The core contribution lies in the integration and application of these components within the UD framework. A more detailed comparison with existing work, especially Søgaard (2012), highlighting the specific improvements and advantages of this approach, would strengthen the paper.
2. **Dependency on POS Tagging Accuracy:** Despite being more robust than MSD to POS errors, the parser's performance is still significantly affected by the quality of the input POS tags. While the authors acknowledge this and evaluate with predicted POS tags, the performance drop is substantial. Exploring methods to mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors further, or incorporating POS tagging directly into the parsing process, could be beneficial.
3. **Head Rule Limitations:** The current set of head rules (Table 1) appears somewhat limited and may not fully capture the complexities of dependency structures across all languages. The parser's inability to handle certain constructions, such as relative clauses (mentioned in Section 6.2), is a consequence of these limitations. A more comprehensive and adaptable set of head rules could improve parsing accuracy.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the authors provide some error analysis in Section 6, a more in-depth investigation of the types of errors made by the parser would be valuable. Identifying common error patterns and their underlying causes could inform future improvements to the algorithm and head rules.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with Søgaard (2012), specifically outlining the key differences and advantages of your approach?
2. How does the tie-breaking mechanism for words with equal PageRank scores function, and what impact does it have on performance?
3. The paper mentions that the head rules were developed using English data. Have you considered adapting the head rules for individual languages to improve performance, and if so, what were the results?
4. Could you provide examples of the most frequent errors made by the parser and discuss the potential reasons behind them?
