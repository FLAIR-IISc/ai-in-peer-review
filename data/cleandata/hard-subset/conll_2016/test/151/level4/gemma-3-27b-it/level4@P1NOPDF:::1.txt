This paper describes a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and analyzes its behavior across a range of languages. The core of the algorithm is a set of rules defining permitted dependencies based on part-of-speech (POS) tags. The algorithm begins by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank algorithm over a graph where edges are defined by these permitted dependencies. Iterating through the ranking, each word is linked to the closest word that maintains a tree structure and is permitted by the head rules and a directionality constraint.

Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly presented, although it appears to differ only slightly from Sogaard (2012), “Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training.”

I have a few questions and suggestions for improvement:

**Head Rules (Table 1)** – It would be beneficial to include some analysis of these rules in relation to the corpora used. For instance, section 3.1 mentions that the rules do not always result in a connected graph, but it doesn’t quantify how frequently this occurs, or the typical size of the resulting connected components.

I was also surprised that head direction was determined using the test data, rather than training or development data. While the rapid convergence of the decision (10-15 sentences) mitigates this as a major issue, it remains an unexpected choice. 

Could the paper elaborate on how ties are broken for words with identical PageRank scores? Does this tie-breaking mechanism significantly impact performance, or are ties infrequent enough to be negligible?

The various constraints employed – head rules, directionality, and distance – will inherently impose upper bounds on the system’s potential performance. Including oracle results for each constraint would be informative, demonstrating the extent to which they limit the maximum achievable score. This would be particularly useful for guiding future work and identifying areas where modifications to the system might yield the greatest improvements.
