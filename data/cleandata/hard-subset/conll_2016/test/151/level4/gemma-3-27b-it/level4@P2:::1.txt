This paper presents a novel, deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and evaluates its performance across numerous languages. The algorithm centers around a set of POS-tag based dependency rules, utilizing a biased PageRank to rank words within a graph defined by these permitted dependencies. A decoding step then iteratively connects words to their closest permissible head, ensuring a tree structure. Overall, the work is well-written and presents an interesting approach, though its novelty relative to prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012), appears incremental.

The primary contribution of this work is a competitive unsupervised dependency parsing approach that leverages the constraints inherent in the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. While unsupervised parsing is an established field, this work demonstrates a robust and surprisingly effective system requiring no training data, achieving results comparable to supervised transfer learning methods. A second contribution lies in the system’s stability; it exhibits greater robustness to domain shift than a comparable supervised baseline, a crucial advantage for real-world applications. Finally, the practical demonstration of estimating adposition direction directly on test data—eliminating the need for language-specific parameters—represents a valuable methodological contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores (averaging 55.3% across languages) without relying on any training data. This is demonstrated by the comparison to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system (MSD), where UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi and shows comparable performance across most languages. The reported results in Table 5 are compelling, especially given the lack of supervision.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The results presented in Table 6 and the discussion in Section 6.3 strongly support the claim that UDP is more robust to changes in domain than the supervised MSD baseline. This is evidenced by the consistently lower standard deviation in UAS scores across different domains for UDP. This robustness is a significant practical advantage.
3. **Effective Use of UD Constraints:** The design of UDP intrinsically incorporates the constraints of the Universal Dependencies framework, leading to a linguistically sound and generalizable approach. By leveraging the UD formalism’s restrictions on function words and employing explicit head rules, the system avoids many of the pitfalls of purely data-driven approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the results are strong, the paper does not fully articulate the differences between this work and Søgaard (2012). A more detailed comparison, highlighting the specific innovations and improvements offered by UDP, would strengthen the paper. The claim that UDP is “nearly as well as a delexicalized transfer parser” requires a more nuanced discussion of the limitations and trade-offs.
2. **Limited Analysis of Head Rules:** The head rules (Table 1) are a core component of the system, yet the paper lacks a detailed analysis of their impact and coverage. Providing statistics on how frequently the rules lead to disconnected graphs (as mentioned in Section 3.1) and the typical size of these components would provide valuable insight.
3. **Justification for Test Data-Driven Head Direction:** The decision to estimate adposition direction on the test data, while efficient, feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the convergence is fast, a stronger justification for this approach—perhaps exploring alternative methods or analyzing the potential for bias—would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison to Søgaard (2012), specifically outlining the key innovations and improvements in UDP?
2. What is the frequency with which the head rules in Table 1 lead to disconnected graphs, and what is the typical size of those disconnected components?
3. How does the system handle ties when multiple words have the same PageRank score? Does this impact performance, and if so, how is it mitigated?
4.  Would it be possible to report oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to understand their impact on maximum achievable performance, and guide future system development?

**Additional Comments:**

* In 4.1, change "we obtain [the] rank" to "we obtain the rank."
* Table 5 and Table 7 currently have different column orders. Consider standardizing the order for consistency (Table 7’s arrangement seems clearer).
* In 6.1, change "isolate the [contribution] of both" to "isolate the contribution of both."
* It would be helpful to include a discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm.
