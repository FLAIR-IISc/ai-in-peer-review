## Review: Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) leveraging POS constraints and a personalized PageRank algorithm. The core idea – to build a parser relying solely on POS tags and a modified PageRank – is interesting, although closely related to prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012). The personalization of PageRank to prioritize likely predicate identification is a positive contribution, though the fixed weight of 5 applied to the main predicate appears empirically determined without substantial justification.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A competitive unsupervised parsing approach for UD:** The paper demonstrates a parser that achieves competitive UAS scores across a wide range of languages *without* any training data. This is a significant result given the challenges of unsupervised parsing and the ambition of creating truly cross-lingual parsers.
2. **Effective adaptation of PageRank for dependency parsing:** The use of personalized PageRank, specifically weighting the likely predicate, demonstrably improves parsing performance. This adaptation shows a pragmatic way to inject linguistic knowledge into an otherwise purely statistical method.
3. **Robustness to domain change:** The evaluation presented suggests UDP exhibits greater robustness to domain shifts compared to a supervised, multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. This is a valuable property for practical applications where test data domain may not match training data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Broad Language Coverage:** Evaluating the parser on 32 languages is a substantial undertaking and provides strong evidence for the generality of the approach. This comprehensive evaluation is a major strength.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves UAS scores that are remarkably close to a strong supervised baseline (multi-source delexicalized parsing) on average, demonstrating its effectiveness. The fact that UDP *outperforms* the supervised baseline on some languages (e.g., Hindi) is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Robustness Analysis:** The analysis of performance across different domains demonstrates the robustness of the system, a crucial characteristic for real-world deployment. The quantitative data supporting this claim (Table 6 & 7) is compelling.
4. **Clear Presentation of Method:** The paper clearly explains the core methodology, including the dependency rules and the two-step decoding algorithm. The inclusion of Figure 1 illustrating the decoding algorithm is helpful.
5. **Availability of Resources:** The availability of both the parser and the annotated datasets used for evaluation is a positive contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the paper builds on existing work, the contributions feel incremental rather than transformative. The personalization of PageRank, while effective, could benefit from more detailed justification for the chosen weight (5x). A more thorough exploration of alternative personalization strategies would strengthen the work.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper touches upon error analysis, a more in-depth investigation into the types of errors made by the parser would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the parser struggles would guide future improvements. The discussion of PUNCT and CONJ errors is a good start, but could be expanded.
3. **Lack of POS Tagging Performance Details:** The paper reports results with predicted POS tags, but does not provide the POS tagging accuracy scores themselves. Reporting these scores would allow for a more direct assessment of the correlation between tagging errors and parsing performance degradation, as suggested by the authors.
4. **Table Presentation:** Some tables (e.g., Table 5) are dense and difficult to quickly interpret. Visual aids, such as highlighting significant results or providing summary statistics, could improve readability.
5. **Arbitrary Rule Selection:** The selection of head rules seems somewhat ad-hoc, based on development set performance. A more systematic approach to rule selection, potentially informed by linguistic principles or cross-lingual analysis, would be desirable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide the POS tagging accuracy scores for the predicted POS experiments?
2.  What motivated the choice of 5x as the weight for the main predicate in the PageRank personalization? Was this value determined through experimentation, and if so, what range of values was explored?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of parsing errors made by the system?
4.  How sensitive is the system’s performance to the teleport probability used in the PageRank algorithm?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Line 207: Correct "The the main idea" to "The main idea".
*   It would be interesting to see a comparison to other unsupervised parsing methods beyond Søgaard (2012), particularly more recent approaches.
*   A discussion of the limitations of the UD formalism itself, and how those limitations might impact the parser's performance, would be valuable.
