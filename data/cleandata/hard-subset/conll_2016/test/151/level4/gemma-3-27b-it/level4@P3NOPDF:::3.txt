This paper proposes a method for dependency parsing, specifically targeting Universal Dependency Treebanks, that leverages only Part-of-Speech (POS) tags and a modified PageRank algorithm. The core idea – weighting graph nodes to prioritize meaningful words over stop words – is interesting, though closely related to previous work by Søgaard (2012). The personalization factor, designed to emphasize the main predicate, is a valuable addition, but its implementation feels somewhat limited. 

Specifically, the personalization appears to be solely focused on the head predicate, and the assigned weight of 5 lacks clear justification. Exploring a more nuanced approach to personalization, potentially incorporating multiple predicate-related features or dynamically adjusting weights, could yield stronger results. A discussion of the rationale behind choosing a fixed weight of 5 would strengthen the contribution.

The evaluation section presents results effectively, but the inclusion of visual aids would improve clarity. While the tables are comprehensive, charts illustrating key performance differences would help readers quickly grasp the significance of the findings. For example, visualizing the performance gains (or lack thereof) across different languages or dependency relations would be particularly insightful.

Furthermore, analyzing the performance of the POS tagging component in a predictive setting would be beneficial. Determining whether a decline in parsing performance correlates with a decrease in POS tagging accuracy – as one would expect given the method’s reliance on POS tags – would provide valuable insight into the system’s behavior and potential bottlenecks. Reporting the POS tagging scores, particularly UAS and LAS, when used for parsing would allow for a more complete understanding of the interplay between these two components.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting approach to dependency parsing. However, the incremental improvement over Søgaard (2012) appears modest. The potential for a more sophisticated personalization strategy and the absence of visual aids in the evaluation section detract slightly from the work’s overall impact.
