This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies that leverages POS constraints and a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify main predicates. The core idea – achieving dependency parsing without training data – is compelling, though conceptually related to prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012). The personalization of PageRank, weighting the likely main predicate more heavily, is a valuable addition, though the chosen weight of 5 appears empirically determined without extensive justification.

The paper’s main contributions, as I see them, are: 1) a demonstration of competitive unsupervised parsing performance across a broad range of languages using a relatively simple rule-based approach grounded in the Universal Dependencies framework; 2) the incorporation of personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, enhancing parsing accuracy; and 3) a robust approach to cross-domain parsing, exhibiting greater stability than a delexicalized transfer system.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Cross-Lingual Performance:** The parser achieves surprisingly strong results on 32 languages without any language-specific training data. This is a significant accomplishment, particularly considering the challenges of cross-lingual parsing. The competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer system (MSD) demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The experimental results clearly show that UDP is more resilient to variations in domain (e.g., news, legal, weblogs) compared to the supervised MSD baseline. This is a crucial advantage in real-world applications where data distributions can vary substantially. The lower standard deviation in performance across domains for UDP reinforces this point.
3. **Principled Approach Leveraging UD:**  The system's design explicitly incorporates the constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism. This constraint-driven approach allows for a linguistically motivated parser that doesn't rely on large amounts of data or complex machine learning models. This makes the approach interpretable and generalizable.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, employing multiple experimental setups (gold POS, predicted POS) and considering a substantial number of languages.  The comparison with a state-of-the-art transfer learning baseline provides a valuable point of reference.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the approach is effective, the novelty over Søgaard (2012) appears limited. The personalization of PageRank is a worthwhile addition, but further exploration of the weighting scheme—and justification for the chosen value of 5—would strengthen the contribution.
2. **Lack of Visualizations:** The paper relies heavily on tables to present the results. The inclusion of visualizations, such as charts illustrating the performance differences across languages or domains, would improve clarity and facilitate understanding.
3. **Missing POS Tagging Performance:** Reporting the POS tagging accuracy for the predicted POS setup is essential. Correlating the degradation in parsing performance with the decrease in POS tagging accuracy would provide valuable insights into the error propagation within the system. This would also help establish the limits of the approach.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper touches on some error sources, a more detailed error analysis—identifying common error patterns and their causes—would be beneficial. This could guide future improvements to the system.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the rationale behind choosing a personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate in the PageRank algorithm? Were other values explored, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Can you provide the POS tagging accuracy scores for the predicted POS setup, broken down by language if possible?
3.  What types of errors does UDP most frequently make? Can you provide examples and discuss potential strategies for addressing these errors?
4.  Could you comment on the computational complexity of the system, especially concerning the PageRank calculation? Is it scalable to very large datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as its inability to handle complex syntactic structures or languages with significantly different grammatical properties than those in the training data.
