

This paper proposes a dependency parsing method for universal dependency treebanks that relies solely on part-of-speech (POS) tags, employing a modified PageRank algorithm to prioritize meaningful words (e.g., main predicates) over stop words. While the core idea is conceptually interesting, the contribution appears modest in relation to existing work. The approach bears significant resemblance to Søgaard (2012), which similarly leveraged POS tags and personalized PageRank for parsing. The personalization strategy—assigning a weight of 5 to the main predicate—is a thoughtful touch but lacks justification. The authors do not explore whether alternative weights or more nuanced personalization criteria (e.g., incorporating syntactic roles beyond the predicate) could yield greater gains, leaving the choice of 5 as an arbitrary heuristic. A more systematic exploration of this parameter would have strengthened the contribution.

The evaluation section presents parsing results but would benefit from clearer presentation. The dense tables make it difficult to extract key insights, and supplementary visualizations (e.g., bar charts comparing parsing accuracy across categories) would significantly improve interpretability. For instance, showing how performance varies by syntactic relation or word type would help contextualize the results beyond raw accuracy metrics.

A critical omission is the lack of POS tagging performance data in the parsing pipeline. Since the method relies exclusively on POS tags, it is essential to report tagging accuracy under the same conditions as parsing. Without this, it remains unclear whether observed parsing degradation stems from tagging errors or inherent limitations of the parsing approach. The authors should explicitly correlate parsing and tagging performance to validate their assumption that parsing issues are primarily driven by POS inaccuracies.

The paper’s incremental nature is its most significant limitation. While the modified PageRank framework is well-motivated, the enhancements over Søgaard (2012) are marginal. The authors do not convincingly demonstrate that their personalization strategy—restricted to the main predicate—provides a substantial advantage over simpler heuristics or the baseline method. A comparative analysis with more recent POS-based parsers (e.g., those using neural architectures) would have better positioned the work within current literature. The absence of such a comparison weakens the claim of novelty.

Minor issues require attention:  
- Line 207: “The the main idea” → “The main idea.”  
- The term “meaningful words” is vague; clarifying which lexical categories or features define them would improve precision.  
- The paper states that stop words are “ignored” in the ranking, but does not specify how this is implemented (e.g., via exclusion from the graph or weight suppression).  

In summary, the paper presents a plausible parsing approach grounded in POS tags and personalized PageRank. However, the technical novelty is limited, the justification for key design choices (e.g., the weight of 5) is underdeveloped, and the evaluation lacks depth in both presentation and analysis. While the work is technically sound, the contribution falls short of the significant advancement expected for a CONLL venue. The authors should address the above points—particularly by justifying the personalization strategy, providing clearer evaluation visuals, and reporting POS tagging performance—to strengthen their case for novelty. As currently presented, the incremental contribution is modest, and the paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison with Søgaard (2012) and contemporary methods.
