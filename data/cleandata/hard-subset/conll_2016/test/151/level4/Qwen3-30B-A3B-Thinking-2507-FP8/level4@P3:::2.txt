

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The authors leverage UD's linguistic constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) and combine them with personalized PageRank for content word ranking. The key contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A linguistically sound unsupervised parsing approach that achieves competitive performance (within 6.4% of a delexicalized transfer system) across 32 languages without any training data.
2. A robust system that demonstrates significantly greater resilience to domain shifts than data-driven approaches, with substantially lower standard deviations across different text domains (as shown in Table 6).
3. A novel application of personalized PageRank with two-step decoding that enforces UD's leaf constraint for function words, resulting in well-formed dependency trees without requiring language-specific parameters.

## Strengths

1. The paper makes a substantial contribution to unsupervised parsing by demonstrating that a linguistically constrained rule-based approach can achieve performance comparable to supervised transfer methods. The results in Table 5 show UDPG (gold POS) outperforming the rule-based baseline by 6.4% average UAS and being only 6.4% behind MSDG (the supervised baseline) on average. This is particularly impressive given that UDP requires no training data, and the system even outperforms the supervised baseline on Hindi. The authors provide compelling evidence that UD's linguistic constraints can be leveraged effectively for unsupervised parsing.

2. The robustness analysis across domains (Table 6 and Table 7) represents a significant contribution to the field. UDP consistently demonstrates much lower standard deviations across different text domains compared to the supervised baseline (MSD), confirming earlier findings that rule-based parsers are less sensitive to domain shifts. This is a crucial practical advantage for real-world applications where domain shifts are common, and it directly addresses a key limitation of data-driven approaches.

3. The methodology is elegantly designed to leverage UD's constraints. The two-step decoding (Sec. 3.3) to enforce function words as leaves, the adposition direction estimation from test data (Sec. 3.2), and the personalized PageRank for main predicate identification (Sec. 3.1) are all well-motivated solutions that require minimal parameter tuning. The paper's clear explanation of how these components interact (Fig. 1 and Sec. 4) demonstrates careful design and thorough evaluation.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper does not adequately address how UD's varying compliance across languages impacts parsing performance. While the authors note UD treebanks have different compliance levels (Sec. 6.2), they do not systematically analyze how this affects UDP's performance. For example, it's unclear whether languages with higher UD compliance (like Italian) perform better than those with lower compliance (like Tamil), which would be important for understanding the limits of the approach.

2. The evaluation of POS error propagation (Sec. 5.3) is somewhat superficial. While the authors report a 2.2% performance drop when using predicted POS tags compared to gold POS, they do not provide a detailed error analysis of how POS errors propagate to dependency errors. A more thorough investigation of error patterns would strengthen the claim that UDP is more robust to POS errors than the supervised baseline (MSD).

3. The limitations of the approach for specific linguistic phenomena are not sufficiently addressed. The paper notes that the parser cannot handle relative clauses due to excluding the NOUN→VERB head rule (Sec. 6.2), but it does not quantify how this limitation affects overall performance or provide concrete examples of where this leads to significant errors. This is particularly relevant given the importance of relative clauses in many languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that UDP outperforms the supervised baseline on Hindi. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, given that Hindi has a verb-final structure that might make the PR personalization strategy (which biases toward the first verb as main predicate) less effective?

2. The authors note that POS accuracy for TnT is 94.1% on average. Could you provide more details about the languages where POS accuracy was particularly low and how this specifically affected dependency parsing performance?

3. The paper mentions that the parser does not handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names. Could you elaborate on how these limitations might impact real-world applications, and what would be the most straightforward way to extend the current approach to handle these phenomena?
