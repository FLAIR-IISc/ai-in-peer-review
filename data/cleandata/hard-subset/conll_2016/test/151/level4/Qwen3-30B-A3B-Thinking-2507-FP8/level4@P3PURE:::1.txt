

This paper introduces a novel deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and analyzes its behavior across multiple languages. The core of the algorithm consists of rules defining permitted dependencies based on part-of-speech (POS) tags. It begins by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank computation over a graph where edges represent permitted dependencies. The algorithm then iteratively processes this ranking, linking each word to the closest preceding word that maintains a tree structure while adhering to the head rules and a directionality constraint.  

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents an interesting approach. However, it appears to differ only marginally from Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training," raising questions about its novelty.  

**Major Concerns:**  
- **Head Rules (Table 1):** An analysis of these rules’ applicability to the corpus would strengthen the work. Section 3.1 notes that the rules do not always yield a connected graph, but the paper does not quantify how frequently this occurs or characterize the typical size of disconnected components.  
- **Head Directionality:** The choice to determine head direction using test data (rather than training or development data) is surprising, given the rapid convergence of this decision (within 10–15 sentences). While this is not a critical issue, it warrants justification.  
- **Tie-Breaking:** The mechanism for resolving ties when words share identical PageRank scores is unclear. Does this tie-breaking significantly impact performance, or are ties sufficiently rare to be negligible?  
- **Constraint Analysis:** The head rules, directionality, and distance constraints impose upper bounds on performance. Including oracle results for each constraint would clarify how much each constraint reduces the maximum achievable score. Such results would be particularly valuable for guiding future improvements to the system.

- Tables 5 and 7: Columns are presented in different orders; Table 7’s arrangement is clearer.
