

Summary: This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. The core contribution is a deterministic algorithm that leverages UD-specific constraints through a set of POS-based dependency head rules, personalized PageRank for content word ranking, and on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test data. The authors demonstrate that UDP achieves competitive performance with delexicalized transfer parsers while showing greater robustness to domain shifts across languages.

Strengths:
1. The parser's robustness to domain changes is a compelling strength. The authors convincingly demonstrate that UDP maintains consistent performance across different text domains (news, legal, literary, etc.) while standard data-driven approaches like MSD show significant variance. This stability stems from UDP's reliance on general UD constraints rather than language-specific statistical patterns, aligning with findings from Plank and van Noord (2010).

2. The method's simplicity and lack of training requirements make it particularly valuable for low-resource languages. By eliminating the need for parallel data or language-specific training, UDP offers a linguistically grounded alternative to cross-lingual transfer approaches that often fail with non-Indo-European languages. The authors' careful handling of UD-specific constraints (like function words being leaves) demonstrates a deep understanding of the formalism.

3. The evaluation is comprehensive and well-structured. The authors compare UDP across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and simplified content/function tags) and across multiple domains, providing nuanced insights into the parser's performance characteristics. The marginal performance drop when using predicted POS (2.2% compared to MSD's 2.7%) is particularly impressive given the simplicity of the approach.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's novelty is limited relative to Søgaard (2012), which also presented an unsupervised dependency parser without training. While UDP incorporates UD-specific constraints and personalization, these are incremental improvements rather than fundamental innovations. The authors should more explicitly acknowledge and differentiate their contribution from this prior work.

2. The head rules (Table 1) lack thorough analysis. The authors note that these rules don't always yield connected graphs but don't quantify how frequently this occurs or characterize the graph components. A more detailed analysis of rule coverage across languages would strengthen their claims about the generalizability of their approach.

3. The decision to estimate adposition direction from test data (10-15 sentences) is surprising, given that this could be determined from development data. While the authors note this converges quickly, it's unclear why they chose this approach over standard practice. The authors should clarify this design choice and its implications for real-world deployment.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide an analysis of head rule coverage across the 32 languages? Specifically, what percentage of words are covered by the head rules, and how frequently do the rules fail to produce connected graphs?

2. How do you handle ties when multiple words have identical PageRank scores? Given that the algorithm is deterministic, what tie-breaking strategy is used, and how does this impact overall performance?

3. The authors mention that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi, but the table shows MSDG (70.1) is higher than UDPG (69.7). Could you clarify this discrepancy in your results?

4. The paper states that UDP "is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system" but the results show UDPG (69.7) is consistently below MSDG (73.5) across languages. Could you elaborate on what you mean by "competitive" in this context?

5. In Table 5, the column order differs between the main results and the domain analysis (Table 7). Could you standardize the column order for consistency?
