

Summary and Contributions: The paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training and relies solely on UD's explicit constraints. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) a linguistically sound approach that exploits UD's formalism (single-rooted trees, function words as dependents of content words) rather than relying on data-driven methods; (2) a personalized PageRank method for improved main predicate identification; (3) a technique for estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data without language-specific parameters; and (4) a two-step decoding algorithm that treats content and function words differently according to UD's formalism.

Strengths: 
1. The system achieves competitive performance with a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) across 32 languages, with UDPG (gold POS) matching MSDG (gold POS) on average (63.9% vs. 61.2% UAS), even outperforming it on Hindi (68.1% vs. 65.7% UAS). This demonstrates that a simple rule-based approach can match more complex data-driven methods without requiring any training data.

2. The parser's robustness to domain shifts is exceptionally well-documented. UDP consistently shows lower standard deviations across domains (e.g., 2.5 UAS vs. 4.2 for MSDP in Table 6), confirming that rule-based approaches are less sensitive to sampling bias. This is particularly significant as it addresses a major limitation of data-driven methods in cross-lingual settings.

3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive type-constrained), demonstrating that UDP's error propagation from POS to dependency errors (0.37) is substantially lower than MSD's (0.46). This shows UDP's practical viability even when used with imperfect POS taggers.

4. The paper's methodological clarity is exemplary. The personalized PageRank approach (Section 3.1) and two-step decoding algorithm (Figure 1) are explained with sufficient detail to enable replication, while the careful analysis of UD constraints (Section 2) demonstrates a deep understanding of the formalism's linguistic value.

Weaknesses:
1. The performance gap between UDPG and MSDG is marginal (63.9% vs. 61.2% UAS), and the authors' claim that UDP "compares favorably" to MSD is somewhat overstated. The paper should more precisely qualify this comparison, noting that while UDP matches MSD on average, it does not consistently outperform it across all languages.

2. The paper doesn't adequately address how the system handles languages with significant UD formalism deviations. For instance, the analysis in Section 6.2 notes that "not all v1.2 treebanks have the same level of formalism compliance," yet the evaluation treats all treebanks as equally compliant. The authors should discuss how their method would perform on languages where UD conventions are inconsistently applied.

3. The evaluation of UDP's performance on languages with predicted POS tags (UDPP) shows a 2.2% drop compared to MSD (Table 5), but the authors don't sufficiently explore why this occurs. Given that UDP's error propagation rate (0.37) is lower than MSD's (0.46), this suggests the POS tagger's performance might be the limiting factor rather than the parser itself. A more detailed error analysis would strengthen this claim.

4. The paper doesn't address computational complexity, which is relevant for practical applications. While the authors note that "decoding runs in less than O(n²)," they don't discuss how this compares to existing parsers, particularly in terms of wall-clock time for real-world processing.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper mentions that "not all v1.2 treebanks have the same level of formalism compliance" (Section 6.2), yet the evaluation treats all treebanks equally. How would the parser's performance differ if evaluated only on treebanks with high formalism compliance?

2. The authors claim UDP "is distinctly robust to domain change across languages" (Abstract), but the cross-domain evaluation (Table 7) shows significant variation in performance across domains. How would you explain the cases where UDP underperforms MSD in certain domains (e.g., Bulgarian legal documents)?

3. The paper states that UDP "is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system" (Abstract), but the performance difference is marginal. Could you provide more nuanced analysis of where UDP excels relative to MSD beyond the Hindi case?
