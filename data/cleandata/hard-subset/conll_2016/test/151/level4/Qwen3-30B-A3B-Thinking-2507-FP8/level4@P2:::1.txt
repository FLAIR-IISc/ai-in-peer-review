

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The system leverages UD's linguistic constraints to build a deterministic parsing algorithm using personalized PageRank over a graph defined by dependency head rules. The core contributions, ordered by significance, are: (1) a linguistically sound unsupervised approach that fully exploits UD's formalism constraints, (2) the use of personalized PageRank for improved main predicate identification, and (3) the ability to estimate adposition direction directly from test data without language-specific parameters.

Strengths:
1. UDP achieves competitive performance against supervised delexicalized transfer systems, with an average UAS difference of only 6.4% across 32 languages. Notably, it outperforms the multi-source delexicalized parser on Hindi, demonstrating the effectiveness of the unsupervised approach. This is particularly valuable for low-resource languages where training data is scarce.

2. The system demonstrates exceptional robustness to domain changes, with significantly lower standard deviations across domains compared to data-driven baselines. This confirms earlier findings about rule-based parsers being more stable to domain shifts, providing a practical advantage for real-world applications where domain mismatch is common.

3. The parser's simplicity and minimal resource requirements are significant strengths. By relying solely on POS tags and UD constraints, the system avoids the complex data dependencies and language-specific parameter tuning required by transfer learning approaches, making it more accessible for deployment in resource-constrained settings.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the approach is limited, as UDP builds directly on Søgaard (2012) with only incremental modifications (personalized PageRank and UD-specific adaptations). The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how these modifications overcome the limitations of prior work, making it difficult to justify as a significant advance beyond existing unsupervised parsing methods.

2. The evaluation lacks critical analysis of the head rules' coverage and their impact on performance. While the paper mentions that the rules don't always yield connected graphs, it provides no data on how frequently this occurs or the size of disconnected components. This omission makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the approach across languages.

3. The decision to estimate adposition direction from test data rather than development data is surprising and not adequately justified. Given that the directionality can be accurately determined with just 10-15 sentences, using development data would have been more standard practice and would have provided a more direct comparison to prior work.

Questions to Authors:
1. What is the frequency of disconnected graphs when building the dependency graph from the head rules, and how large are the disconnected components when they occur? This would help assess the reliability of the graph construction approach.

2. How are ties resolved when words have identical PageRank scores, and what impact does this tie-breaking mechanism have on overall performance? Given the deterministic nature of the parser, this detail is crucial for reproducibility.

3. Could the authors provide oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to demonstrate the theoretical upper bound of performance achievable with this approach? This would be invaluable for guiding future work on constraint-based parsing.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, with a logical structure that effectively communicates the method and results. However, the limited novelty and insufficient justification of key methodological choices weaken the contribution. While the results are impressive for an unsupervised approach, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to prior work and a deeper analysis of the constraints that limit performance. The evaluation across domains is particularly valuable and well-executed, but the lack of detailed analysis of the head rules and the unexpected test-data direction estimation are significant drawbacks.
