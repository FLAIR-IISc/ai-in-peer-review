- Summary: Proposes UDP, an unsupervised UD parser using only POS constraints and personalized PageRank to rank content words, followed by two-step decoding for heads.

- Contribution 1: Introduces personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification.

- Contribution 2: Adapts dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations to operate within the UD formalism.

- Contribution 3: Estimates adposition attachment direction directly on test data, removing language-specific parameters.

- Strength 1: Competitive performance on 32 languages, with average UAS only 6.4% lower than a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

- Strength 2: Superior robustness to domain changes, showing much lower performance variance across text domains than supervised transfer parsers.

- Strength 3: Requires no training data and is linguistically principled, making it useful for truly low-resource languages.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty due to close similarity with Søgaard (2012).

- Weakness 2: Choice to estimate head direction on test data is surprising and not well justified despite fast convergence.

- Weakness 3: Missing analysis of how often head rules produce disconnected graphs and the typical sizes of components.

- Weakness 4: No details on tie-breaking for identical PageRank scores or analysis of its performance impact.

- Weakness 5: Lacks oracle results for individual constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) to quantify their limiting effects.

- Question 1: Please analyze how frequently head rules lead to disconnected graphs and component size distribution on test data.

- Question 2: How are ties in PageRank scores broken, and does the tie-breaking strategy significantly affect parsing performance?

- Question 3: Can you provide oracle results per constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to show each constraint's ceiling?

- Question 4: Why estimate head direction using test data rather than development data, and how would using development data affect performance?
