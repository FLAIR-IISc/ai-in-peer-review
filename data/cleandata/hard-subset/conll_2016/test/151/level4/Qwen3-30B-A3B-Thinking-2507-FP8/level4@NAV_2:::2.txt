

Summary: This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. The system leverages UD-specific constraints (function words as leaves, head-dependent rules) and personalized PageRank for main predicate identification, estimating adposition direction directly from test data. It achieves competitive results against supervised delexicalized transfer systems while demonstrating superior robustness to domain shifts.

Main Contributions:
1. A linguistically principled unsupervised parsing approach that fully exploits UD's formal constraints (function words as leaves, head rules) without language-specific parameters.
2. A robust method for estimating adposition direction from minimal test data (10-15 sentences), eliminating the need for pre-defined language-specific rules.
3. Competitive performance against supervised cross-lingual parsers (within 6.4% UAS of MSDG baseline) with significantly lower sensitivity to domain shifts.

Strengths:
1. The parser achieves state-of-the-art unsupervised performance (63.9 UAS on gold POS), outperforming the delexicalized transfer baseline (MSDG) on 12 languages and matching it within 6.4% average UAS difference. Notably, it surpasses MSDG on Hindi (67.9 vs. 65.7 UAS), demonstrating strong cross-lingual applicability without training data.
2. UDP exhibits exceptional robustness to domain shifts (Table 6), with a 3.7× lower standard deviation across domains than MSD (2.5 vs. 9.2), confirming its stability as a rule-based system versus data-driven baselines.
3. The method provides a linguistically sound alternative to transfer-based approaches by strictly adhering to UD constraints (e.g., function words as leaves), avoiding the domain adaptation pitfalls of supervised methods (e.g., Klein and Manning's German noun head bias).
4. The personalization of PageRank (5× weight to main predicate) significantly improves content word ranking (9 UAS points over non-personalized baseline), directly addressing the core challenge of main predicate identification in unsupervised parsing.

Weaknesses:
1. The parser's performance degrades substantially under naive POS tagging (UDPN: 29.9 UAS vs. UDPG: 63.9 UAS), indicating high sensitivity to POS quality. While the authors note 94.1% TnT accuracy, this remains a critical dependency for real-world deployment.
2. The adposition direction estimation requires 10-15 sentences of test data to converge, limiting applicability to very short texts. The paper lacks analysis of how this affects parsing in low-resource domains.
3. The evaluation excludes Japanese (due to missing word forms) and omits error analysis for problematic POS tags (e.g., PUNCT UAS = 21%), leaving uncertainty about the parser's behavior in edge cases.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would the parser perform on languages with highly divergent word order (e.g., SOV languages like Japanese) where adposition direction estimation might be particularly error-prone?
2. Could the adposition direction estimation be refined using UD treebank statistics (e.g., aggregated across languages) to reduce the required test data from 10-15 sentences?
3. The paper notes that UDP fails to handle relative clauses due to excluding NOUN→VERB rules. Could this be addressed by dynamically inferring such relations from UD's dependency hierarchy?
