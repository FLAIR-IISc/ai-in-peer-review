

This paper introduces a deterministic dependency parsing algorithm grounded in POS-tag-based head rules and analyzes its cross-lingual performance. The core approach employs a modified PageRank to rank words, followed by iterative construction of a tree structure where each word connects to the nearest permissible head (satisfying head rules and directionality constraints). The presentation is clear, and the technical contribution is well-motivated. However, the method appears marginally distinct from Sogaard (2012), raising questions about novelty relative to existing unsupervised parsing work. Below, I outline key concerns and suggestions for improvement.

**Major Concerns**  
1.  **Head Rule Analysis**: The head rules (Table 1) lack contextualization against the target corpus. While Section 3.1 notes that these rules sometimes yield disconnected graphs, the paper omits critical quantitative details: How frequently do disconnected graphs occur? What are the typical sizes of disconnected components? A brief statistical analysis would strengthen the rationale for the chosen rules and clarify their practical limitations. Without this, the rules’ effectiveness remains under-evaluated.

2.  **Head Direction Determination**: The paper determines head direction using test data (Section 4.1), which is unconventional for unsupervised parsing. Although the direction decision converges rapidly (within 10–15 sentences), this choice risks introducing test-data leakage and undermines the unsupervised claim. The authors should justify this choice or demonstrate that using development data yields comparable results. If the convergence speed truly mitigates the issue, this should be explicitly stated.

3.  **PageRank Tie-Breaking**: The handling of words with identical PageRank scores is unspecified. How are ties resolved (e.g., lexicographical order, random selection)? Crucially, the authors should address whether this tie-breaking impacts parsing performance. If ties are rare (e.g., <1% of words), this is negligible; if common, it could significantly affect results. A brief empirical note on tie frequency and its performance impact is essential.

4.  **Constraint Analysis via Oracle Results**: The paper imposes three key constraints (head rules, directionality, distance), each limiting the theoretical maximum performance. The absence of oracle results for each constraint makes it impossible to gauge their individual impact on achievable accuracy. For instance, *what is the highest possible F1 score if only head rules were relaxed?* Including such oracle bounds would clarify the system’s ceiling and guide future improvements (e.g., "Constraint X is the primary bottleneck"). This is particularly valuable for the community’s methodological development.

**Minor Issues**  
- Section 4.1: "we obtain [the] rank" → remove "[the]" for grammatical correctness.  
- Tables 5 and 7: Columns are ordered inconsistently. Table 7’s arrangement (e.g., ordering by language) is clearer; Tables 5 and 7 should adopt a uniform structure.  
- Section 6.1: "isolate the [contribution] of both" → remove "[contribution]" for clarity.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The work presents a coherent, deterministic parsing framework with strong clarity in exposition. Its primary contribution lies in the systematic application of POS-based constraints, but its incremental nature relative to Sogaard (2012) warrants stronger justification. Addressing the head rule analysis, directionality choice, tie-breaking mechanism, and constraint oracles would substantially elevate the paper’s technical rigor and utility. Without these clarifications, the paper’s novelty and the scalability of its approach remain ambiguous. The minor formatting points are easily resolved but should be corrected.  

**Recommendation**  
**Weak Accept** (conditional on addressing the major concerns). The paper’s core idea is sound, but the reviewers require evidence that the method’s advantages over Sogaard (2012) are non-trivial, particularly through the proposed constraint analysis and empirical validation of key assumptions. The minor issues do not detract from the paper’s validity but should be resolved for publication.
