

This paper presents a novel deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and examines its performance across multiple languages. The algorithm's foundation consists of rules that specify permissible dependencies using part-of-speech (POS) tags. It initiates by ranking words via a subtly biased PageRank on a graph where edges represent permitted dependencies. Subsequently, it constructs the parse tree by sequentially linking each word to the nearest preceding word that preserves tree structure, adheres to the head rules, and satisfies directionality constraints. Overall, the paper is well-presented and engaging, though it appears to differ only marginally from Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training." I offer the following questions and suggestions:  

Head Rules (Table 1) – An analysis linking these rules to the corpus would be valuable. Specifically, Section 3.1 notes that the rules do not always yield a connected graph, but omits details on how frequently this occurs or the typical size of disconnected components.  
The selection of head direction using test data rather than training or development data was unexpected. Given the rapid convergence of this decision (within 10–15 sentences), this is not a critical issue, yet remains a surprising design choice.  
How are ties resolved for words sharing identical PageRank scores? Does this tie-breaking significantly affect performance, or are such ties sufficiently rare to be negligible?  
The constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) impose upper bounds on system performance. Including oracle results for each constraint would clarify how much each constraint limits the maximum achievable score, which would be particularly useful for directing future improvements.
