

Summary: This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training and relies solely on POS constraints and a small set of dependency head rules. The authors claim their system is competitive with delexicalized transfer parsers while being more robust to domain shifts across languages.

Contributions (as I see them):
1. The use of personalized PageRank to identify main predicates (primary contribution)
2. The adaptation of dependency head rules to UD's specific formalism constraints (secondary contribution)
3. The approach of estimating adposition direction directly from test data, making the system language-agnostic (tertiary contribution)

Strengths:
1. UDP achieves competitive results with supervised transfer systems without any training data (UDPG averages 63.9 UAS vs. MSDG's 61.2 UAS on gold POS, with UDPG outperforming MSDG on Hindi). This is a significant achievement for an unsupervised approach, especially given the challenging nature of cross-lingual parsing.

2. The parser demonstrates superior robustness to domain shifts across languages, evidenced by consistently lower standard deviations across domains compared to the supervised MSD baseline (Table 6). This confirms the hypothesis that rule-based approaches can be more stable to domain shifts than data-driven methods, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

3. The parser's performance degradation with predicted POS tags is minimal (only 2.2% drop compared to MSD's 2.7% drop), indicating UDP is less sensitive to POS errors than data-driven approaches. This is particularly important for real-world applications where gold POS tags are unavailable.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the approach is limited. The core methodology (PageRank for ranking content words) builds directly on Søgaard (2012b), and the personalization factor (weight of 5 for the main predicate) appears arbitrary without sufficient justification or ablation studies to demonstrate its optimality. The paper doesn't clearly explain why 5 is the optimal value, which weakens the contribution claim.

2. The paper claims UDP is "distinctly robust to domain change" but doesn't provide sufficient analysis to support this claim. While Table 6 shows lower standard deviations across domains, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis to explain why this robustness occurs or how the parser handles specific domain challenges (e.g., why Bulgarian bulletin is particularly difficult).

3. The evaluation lacks transparency regarding POS tagging performance. The paper reports an average POS accuracy of 94.1% for TnT but doesn't provide language-specific tagging scores. Without these, it's difficult to assess whether the parsing performance degradation is directly correlated with POS errors (as expected), making it hard to evaluate the true robustness of the parser.

Questions to Authors:
1. The personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate appears arbitrary. Could you provide more justification for this specific value, or show ablation results for different weight values (e.g., 3, 5, 7) to demonstrate its optimality?

2. The paper claims UDP is more robust to domain shifts but doesn't explain why. Could you provide a detailed error analysis comparing the parsing errors across different domains (e.g., legal vs. news) to identify patterns that explain this robustness?

3. The paper reports that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (Table 5), but doesn't provide a language-specific error analysis for this case. Could you elaborate on why UDP performs better on Hindi compared to other languages and how this relates to the UD formalism?

Minor issues:
- Line 207: "The the main idea" should be "The main idea"
- The paper would benefit from including more visualizations (e.g., error rate per POS tag) to complement the tables and make the results more accessible.

Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to unsupervised parsing, demonstrating that a rule-based approach can achieve competitive results with supervised methods while being more robust to domain shifts. However, the incremental nature of the work, particularly the limited novelty compared to Søgaard (2012b), is a significant limitation. With more thorough justification of the personalization factor and deeper domain analysis, this paper could be significantly strengthened.
