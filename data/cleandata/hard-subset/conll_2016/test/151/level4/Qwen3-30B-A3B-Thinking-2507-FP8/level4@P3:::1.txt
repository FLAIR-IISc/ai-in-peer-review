

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. A linguistically sound rule-based dependency parsing approach that achieves competitive performance with supervised transfer systems across 32 languages, demonstrating that simple rule-based methods can rival data-driven approaches for UD parsing.

2. A robust parser that shows significantly better cross-domain stability than supervised transfer systems, with lower standard deviations across different text domains (e.g., 1.8 UAS standard deviation for UDP vs. 4.5 for MSD).

3. A novel method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data, eliminating the need for gold-standard directionality information while maintaining high accuracy (75% ADP UAS).

**Strengths**

1. The paper's most compelling contribution is the robust performance across languages and domains. UDPG (gold POS) achieves 63.9% UAS on average, remarkably close to the supervised MSDG baseline (69.7% UAS), with only a 6.4% average gap. Notably, UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (66.9% vs. 65.7% UAS), demonstrating that unsupervised methods can sometimes surpass transfer-based approaches.

2. The cross-domain robustness is a significant practical contribution. As shown in Table 6, UDP has substantially lower standard deviations across domains (1.8 vs. 4.5 for MSD), confirming that rule-based approaches are less susceptible to sampling bias than data-driven methods. This is particularly valuable for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

3. The method for estimating adposition direction from test data is elegant and practical. By analyzing just 10-15 sentences to determine whether a language uses prepositions or postpositions, the system achieves 75% ADP UAS accuracy. This eliminates a major dependency on gold-standard directionality information, making the parser more applicable to truly low-resource scenarios.

**Weaknesses**

1. The novelty of the approach is limited. UDP is a straightforward extension of Søgaard (2012), with only minor modifications (personalized PageRank and two-step decoding to accommodate UD constraints). The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how these modifications substantially improve upon the existing work. Without a more thorough comparison to Søgaard (2012), the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

2. The paper lacks sufficient analysis of the head rules (Table 1). The authors note that the rules don't always yield connected graphs but don't quantify how frequently this occurs or how large the disconnected components typically are. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the reliability of the rule-based approach.

3. The choice to estimate head direction from test data rather than development data is surprising and not well justified. While the authors note that directionality converges quickly (10-15 sentences), they don't explain why this approach was chosen over the more conventional development data method. This decision point needs stronger justification.

4. The paper omits oracle analyses for the various constraints (head rules, directionality, distance), which would provide valuable insight into the theoretical performance limits of the system. Such analyses would help identify where future work could make the most significant improvements.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Please provide statistics on how frequently the head rules lead to disconnected graphs, and characterize the size of disconnected components when they occur.

2. Why was head direction estimated from test data rather than development data? What would be the performance impact if directionality were estimated from development data instead?

3. How does the tie-breaking mechanism work for words with identical PageRank scores, and does this mechanism significantly impact performance?

4. Could you provide oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to illustrate the theoretical upper bounds of performance? This would help clarify the limitations of the current approach.

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, with a strong evaluation across multiple languages and domains. The inclusion of the in-house annotated test sets for domain analysis (Bible, magazine, questions) is particularly valuable for understanding cross-domain behavior. The methodological clarity is excellent, and the code availability will facilitate reproducibility.

The main limitation is the insufficient differentiation from prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012). The authors should more clearly articulate how their personalized PageRank and two-step decoding specifically address the limitations of the prior work, and provide a more detailed comparison with the baseline system. A more thorough analysis of the head rules and their limitations would also strengthen the paper.
