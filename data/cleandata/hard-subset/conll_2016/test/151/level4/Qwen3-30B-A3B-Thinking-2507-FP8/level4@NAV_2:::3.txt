

Summary: The paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that relies solely on POS tags and a modified PageRank algorithm. As I see them, the main contributions are: 1) adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, 2) incorporating the UD restriction of function words being leaves, and 3) demonstrating robustness to domain changes compared to delexicalized transfer systems.

Strengths: The parser achieves competitive performance with delexicalized transfer systems (MSD) across 32 languages, with only a 6.4% average gap in UAS and outperforming MSD on Hindi. The system's robustness to domain shifts is significant, with UDP showing consistently lower standard deviation across domains (0.7-1.0) compared to MSD (1.5-2.5), confirming that rule-based parsers are more stable to domain shifts. The evaluation across different POS scenarios provides valuable insights into error propagation, showing UDP has a lower error propagation rate (0.37) compared to MSD (0.46).

Weaknesses: The core approach is very similar to Søgaard (2012b), with the main novelty being adaptation to UD formalism. The personalization weight (5) for the main predicate lacks justification and appears arbitrary. The paper lacks visualizations or charts to better illustrate key findings, making it difficult to grasp insights from the tabular data. The evaluation of UD formalism compliance is superficial, with no detailed analysis of how the parser handles specific UD challenges like relative clauses or coordination.

Questions to Authors: Could you provide more justification for the choice of personalization weight (5) for the main predicate, including sensitivity analysis with alternative weights? Could you include visualizations of parsing performance across domains to better illustrate the robustness claim? Could you provide POS tagging accuracy scores for the predicted POS setup to better understand the relationship between POS errors and parsing errors?
