- Paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised UD parser that requires no training data and uses only POS constraints.  
- UDP uses personalized PageRank to identify main predicates, UD-compliant head rules, and estimates adposition direction from test data.  
- Evaluation covers 32 languages, three POS scenarios, and comparisons to rule-based baselines and a multi-source delexicalized transfer system.  
- Contribution: a fully unsupervised UD parser achieving competitive performance with supervised transfer systems.  
- Contribution: method to estimate adposition attachment direction directly from test data, removing language-specific parameters.  
- Contribution: personalized PageRank that amplifies estimated main predicate weight (×5) to improve content word attachment.  
- Strength: competitive performance (average UAS 63.9%) comparable to a supervised multi-source delexicalized parser and outperforming it on some languages (e.g., Hindi).  
- Strength: greater robustness to domain shifts, showing lower standard deviation across domains than supervised baselines.  
- Strength: resource-efficient and suitable for low-resource languages, with smaller performance drop under predicted POS tags than supervised baselines.  
- Weakness: insufficient differentiation from prior PageRank-based work (Søgaard 2012) and lack of justification for the personalization weight of 5.  
- Weakness: inadequate error analysis explaining varying performance across languages (e.g., small gap for Bulgarian vs large for Estonian).  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of how POS tagging errors affect parsing performance and lack of correlation between POS accuracy and parsing.  
- Weakness: limited linguistic validation and lack of analysis/examples for language-specific phenomena (e.g., verb-final languages).  
- Question: request justification and sensitivity analysis for the personalization weight of 5.  
- Question: how does UDP handle languages/treebanks with low UD formalism compliance (e.g., Hindi)?  
- Question: request broader domain-robustness analysis across more languages beyond Bulgarian, Croatian, and Italian.  
- Additional comment: paper would benefit from more visualizations (error-analysis plots, domain trends) and clearer differentiation from prior work, though it is well-written and technically sound.
