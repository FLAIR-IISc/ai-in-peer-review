

Summary: This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. Instead, it leverages UD's explicit constraints on part-of-speech (POS) relations and dependency structures, using personalized PageRank to identify main predicates and a small set of UD-compliant head rules. The system estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data, making it completely free of language-specific parameters.

Main Contributions (ordered by strength):
1. A linguistically grounded unsupervised parsing approach that fully exploits UD's formal constraints rather than relying on data-driven transfer methods.
2. A novel application of personalized PageRank to identify main predicates without training data, improving main predicate identification.
3. A completely parameter-free system that estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a compelling case for unsupervised parsing as a viable alternative to transfer-based approaches. UDP achieves competitive performance with multi-source delexicalized transfer systems (MSD), with an average UAS difference of only 6.4% (UDPG vs. MSDG), and even outperforms MSD on Hindi (55.3 vs. 54.8 UAS). This is significant because it demonstrates that unsupervised parsing can match supervised transfer methods without requiring any language-specific training data.

2. The evidence for UDP's robustness to domain shifts is particularly strong. The authors show that UDP maintains consistent performance across different domains (news, legal, literature) with a much lower standard deviation (1.8-2.5) compared to MSD (4.5-4.2). This confirms earlier findings that rule-based parsers are less sensitive to domain shifts, which is increasingly important as NLP systems face diverse real-world applications.

3. The method's simplicity and effectiveness are impressive. By leveraging UD's formal constraints and using a minimal set of head rules, the authors achieve competitive performance without any training data. The system is also computationally efficient (O(n×|C|) for decoding), and the authors provide a clear, step-by-step explanation of the personalized PageRank approach and two-step decoding algorithm, making the method highly reproducible.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation is limited to 32 languages, excluding Japanese due to lack of word forms. While understandable given UD treebank constraints, this limitation means the approach's performance on languages with highly complex morphology or different word orders (e.g., agglutinative languages) remains unexplored, which would be valuable for a more comprehensive assessment.

2. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of the head rules, particularly regarding relative clauses. The authors exclude the NOUN → VERB head rule during development because it "makes the hierarchical relation between verbs and nouns less conclusive," but they don't provide a thorough analysis of how often this limitation affects overall performance or how many relative clauses are present in UD treebanks. This omission weakens the claim that the approach is "linguistically sound."

3. While the paper demonstrates UDP's lower error propagation rate (0.37 vs. MSD's 0.46) when using predicted POS tags, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of where these errors occur. A more thorough examination of error patterns would strengthen the argument for UDP's robustness to POS errors.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper shows UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (55.3 vs. 54.8 UAS), but this difference is marginal. Could the authors provide a statistical significance analysis of this difference, and explain why UDP performs better on this specific language?

2. The authors state that UDP cannot infer relative clauses due to excluding the NOUN → VERB head rule. Could they provide an analysis of how many relative clauses are present in UD treebanks and how this exclusion affects the overall performance metrics?

3. The domain evaluation is limited to Bulgarian, Croatian, Italian, and English. Could the authors explain why these specific languages were chosen for the domain analysis, and whether they plan to expand this evaluation to more languages in future work?
