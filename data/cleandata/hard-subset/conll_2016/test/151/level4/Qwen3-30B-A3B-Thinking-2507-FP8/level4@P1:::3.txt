

Summary: This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data and relies solely on POS constraints and a small set of dependency head rules. The main contributions are: (1) adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, (2) incorporating UD's restriction that function words must be leaves, and (3) using personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification. The authors demonstrate competitive performance against delexicalized transfer systems while being more robust to domain shifts.

Strengths:
1. The parser's robustness to domain shifts is a significant contribution. The authors demonstrate that UDP maintains consistent performance across different text domains (e.g., news, legal, literary) while multi-source delexicalized parsers show substantially higher variance. This stability stems from UDP's design that explicitly follows UD formalism constraints rather than learning from data, making it less susceptible to sampling biases.

2. The evaluation across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive type-constrained) provides valuable insights into the parser's resilience to POS errors. The authors show that UDP's error propagation rate (0.37) is substantially lower than the supervised baseline (0.46), demonstrating that their approach remains effective even when POS tagging degrades.

3. The parser's simplicity and lack of language-specific parameters is noteworthy. By estimating adposition direction directly from test data and using a universal set of head rules, UDP avoids the complex data requirements of transfer-based approaches while maintaining competitive performance across 32 languages.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the approach is limited compared to Søgaard (2012), which also used PageRank for unsupervised parsing. While UDP makes two key improvements (personalized PageRank and two-step decoding for UD constraints), the overall methodological framework remains very similar, with the most significant difference being the adaptation to UD formalism constraints rather than a substantial algorithmic innovation.

2. The personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate appears arbitrary without sufficient justification. The authors do not provide a systematic analysis of how this parameter was tuned or whether alternative weights would yield better results. This lack of exploration weakens the claim that the personalization strategy is optimal.

3. The evaluation relies heavily on tables rather than visualizations, making it difficult to quickly grasp key insights. For instance, the domain-shift results (Table 7) would benefit from a visualization showing the standard deviation across domains for each system, which would more clearly demonstrate UDP's superior stability.

4. The authors do not provide sufficient analysis of how POS tagging accuracy directly impacts parsing performance. While they report an error propagation rate, they fail to present the actual POS tagging scores for the predicted POS setup, making it difficult to determine whether the parsing degradation is directly proportional to tagging errors.

5. The paper claims to outperform a delexicalized transfer system on Hindi (Table 5), but this result is not thoroughly analyzed. The authors do not explain why UDP performs better on this specific language, which would help determine whether this is a general advantage or an anomaly.

Questions to Authors:
1. How was the personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate determined? Could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with different weight values?

2. Could you include the POS tagging accuracy scores for the predicted POS setup (UDPP) in your results? This would help clarify whether the parsing performance degradation is directly correlated with POS errors.

3. The paper mentions that UDP outperforms the delexicalized transfer parser on Hindi. Could you provide more detailed analysis of why this language is an exception to the general trend where the supervised baseline performs better?

4. In Table 6, the standard deviation for UDP is consistently lower than for MSD across domains. Could you elaborate on why this stability occurs, and whether it's a general property of rule-based systems or specific to UDP's design?
