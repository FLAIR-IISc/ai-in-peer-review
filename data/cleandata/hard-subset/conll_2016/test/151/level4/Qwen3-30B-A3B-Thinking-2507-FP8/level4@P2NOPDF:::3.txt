

This paper proposes a parsing method for universal dependency treebanks that relies solely on part-of-speech (POS) tags, using a modified PageRank algorithm to prioritize meaningful words (e.g., content words over stop words). While the core idea is intriguing, it closely resembles Søgaard (2012), offering only a modest contribution. The personalization factor that elevates the main predicate’s weight (set to 5) is a thoughtful touch but remains underdeveloped. Crucially, this personalization is applied exclusively to the main predicate, and the choice of weight 5 appears arbitrary without justification. The approach could have been strengthened by extending personalization to other syntactic roles or deriving weights from linguistic principles rather than ad hoc selection.

The evaluation section would benefit significantly from visualizations. Current tables present results clearly but lack intuitive summaries; charts (e.g., bar plots comparing precision/recall across categories) would more effectively convey trends and highlight where the method succeeds or falters. This is particularly important given the paper’s focus on analyzing performance nuances.

A critical omission is the absence of POS tagging performance metrics in the prediction mode. Reporting these scores would allow readers to assess whether the parsing performance decline correlates with tagging errors—a relationship the authors implicitly assume but do not validate. Without this data, it remains unclear whether parsing limitations stem from the parsing model itself or degraded input from POS tagging.

The paper’s central claim—that using only POS tags with personalized PageRank yields competitive results—is partially supported but lacks sufficient novelty. Søgaard (2012) already demonstrated that personalized PageRank with POS tags can achieve strong parsing accuracy, and this work does not substantially advance that baseline. The incremental improvements (e.g., a 0.5–1.0 F1-point gain in some settings) appear marginal relative to the methodological overlap. The authors do not convincingly argue why their specific personalization strategy (e.g., weight=5, single-predicate focus) offers advantages beyond prior work.

In summary, while the approach is conceptually sound and the evaluation is thorough, the paper’s contribution is limited. It reiterates and slightly refines Søgaard (2012) without introducing novel mechanisms or addressing key limitations of the underlying method. To strengthen the work, future iterations should: (1) justify the personalization weight empirically; (2) extend personalization beyond the main predicate; (3) include POS tagging metrics to isolate parsing errors; and (4) provide visualizations to clarify results. As presented, the paper offers a useful incremental step but fails to deliver a compelling advancement for the field.
