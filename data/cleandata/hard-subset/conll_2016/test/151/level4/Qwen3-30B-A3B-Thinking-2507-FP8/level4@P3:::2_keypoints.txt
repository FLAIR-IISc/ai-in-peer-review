- Contribution: UDP is a linguistically grounded unsupervised UD parser that achieves competitive performance (within 6.4% of a delexicalized supervised transfer system) across 32 languages with no training data.  
- Contribution: UDP shows strong robustness to domain shifts, exhibiting substantially lower standard deviations across text domains than data-driven baselines.  
- Contribution: Novel use of personalized PageRank plus two-step decoding to enforce UD function-word-as-leaf constraints, producing well-formed trees without language-specific parameters.  
- Strength: Demonstrates that a rule-based, linguistically constrained approach can match supervised transfer performance and even outperform it on some languages (e.g., Hindi).  
- Strength: Robustness experiments (Tables 6–7) provide compelling evidence that UDP is less sensitive to domain variation than supervised methods.  
- Strength: Methodology is elegant and minimally tuned — two-step decoding, adposition direction estimation, and PageRank main predicate identification are well-motivated and clearly explained.  
- Weakness: No systematic analysis of how variation in UD compliance across treebanks/languages affects UDP performance (e.g., Italian vs Tamil).  
- Weakness: POS error propagation evaluation is superficial; lacks detailed error analysis linking POS mistakes to dependency errors.  
- Weakness: Specific linguistic limitations (e.g., inability to handle relative clauses due to excluding NOUN→VERB, plus multiword expressions, coordination, proper names) are noted but not quantified or exemplified.  
- Question: Why does UDP outperform the supervised baseline on Hindi despite Hindi’s verb-final order and the PR bias toward the first verb?  
- Question: Which languages had particularly low POS accuracy with TnT and how did low POS accuracy affect dependency parsing performance?  
- Question: How do limitations on MWEs, coordination, and proper names impact real-world use, and what are the simplest extensions to handle these phenomena?
