

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data and relies solely on POS constraints imposed by the UD formalism. The system uses a personalized PageRank approach to identify main predicates, incorporates UD-compliant dependency head rules, and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate UDP across 32 languages in three POS scenarios and compare it to both rule-based baselines and a multi-source delexicalized transfer system.

## Main Contributions
1. A completely unsupervised dependency parser for UD that achieves competitive performance with supervised transfer systems without requiring any training data.
2. A method for estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data, eliminating the need for language-specific parameters.
3. A personalized PageRank approach that improves main predicate identification by giving 5x more weight to the estimated main predicate, resulting in significantly better content word attachment than baseline approaches.

## Strengths
1. **Competitive Performance**: UDP achieves remarkable results, with an average UAS of 63.9% (UDPG) compared to 63.9% for the supervised multi-source delexicalized parser (MSDG), differing by only 6.4% on average. Notably, UDP outperforms MSDG on Hindi (69.7% vs. 68.1%), demonstrating the approach's viability for cross-lingual parsing.

2. **Domain Robustness**: The paper provides compelling evidence that UDP is significantly more robust to domain shifts than supervised systems. As shown in Table 6, UDP exhibits substantially lower standard deviations across domains (2.5 vs. 4.2 for MSDP), confirming that rule-based approaches can be more stable to domain variation than data-driven methods.

3. **Resource Efficiency**: By requiring no training data and operating solely on POS tags, UDP is uniquely positioned for truly low-resource languages where training data is unavailable. The system's performance degradation under predicted POS (UDPP) is significantly less than that of the supervised baseline (2.2% vs. 2.7% drop), demonstrating its resilience to POS errors.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Clear Differentiation from Prior Work**: The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from Søgaard (2012), which also used PageRank for unsupervised parsing. While the authors mention "personalized PageRank" and "two-step decoding," they fail to provide convincing evidence that these modifications significantly advance the state of the art. The personalization weight of 5 appears arbitrary without justification.

2. **Inadequate Error Analysis**: The paper lacks a thorough analysis of why UDP outperforms the baseline in some languages but not others. For instance, the performance gap between UDPG and MSDG varies significantly (e.g., 2.7% for Bulgarian but 15.1% for Estonian), yet the authors provide no linguistic explanation for these differences.

3. **Insufficient POS Tagging Analysis**: While the paper reports that UDP's performance degrades only marginally with predicted POS (2.2% drop), it does not provide sufficient detail on how POS tagging errors affect parsing performance. The authors could have shown how parsing performance correlates with POS accuracy across languages to better understand error propagation.

4. **Limited Linguistic Validation**: The claim that UDP offers "linguistically sound" parsing lacks substantive linguistic analysis. The paper does not demonstrate how the parser handles language-specific phenomena (e.g., verb-final languages) or provide examples of linguistically correct parses compared to incorrect ones.

## Questions to Authors
1. The personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate appears arbitrary. Could you provide a more principled justification for this value, and how sensitive is the system to this parameter choice?

2. The paper shows UDP outperforms MSDG on Hindi (69.7% vs. 68.1%), but the Hindi treebank has lower UD compliance (34.6% UAS for the baseline). How does the system handle languages with lower UD formalism compliance?

3. The paper claims UDP is "distinctly robust to domain change across languages," but the domain analysis is limited to Bulgarian, Croatian, and Italian. Could you provide more detailed analysis across additional languages to support this claim?

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from more visualizations to complement the tables (e.g., error analysis plots, domain performance trends). The draft review mentions "some charts would have been beneficial," and I agree with this assessment. The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the contribution claims could be strengthened by more clearly differentiating from prior work and providing deeper linguistic insights.
