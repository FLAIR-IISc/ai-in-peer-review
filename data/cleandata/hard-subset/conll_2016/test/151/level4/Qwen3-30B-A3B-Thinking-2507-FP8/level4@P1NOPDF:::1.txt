

This paper presents a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and analyzes its behavior across multiple languages. The core innovation lies in a set of head rules that define permitted dependencies based on part-of-speech (POS) tags. The algorithm begins by ranking words using a slightly biased variant of PageRank on a graph where edges represent permitted dependency relations. It then iterates through the ranked list, linking each word to the nearest preceding word that maintains a tree structure while satisfying both the head rules and a directionality constraint.  

The paper is well-written and presents an interesting approach, though its novelty appears limited compared to Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training." Below are specific suggestions and questions to strengthen the work:  

**Head Rules (Table 1):**  
The paper notes that the head rules sometimes fail to produce connected graphs (Section 3.1), but provides no quantitative analysis of this issue. It would be valuable to include metrics such as:  
- The frequency of disconnected graphs across languages/corpora.  
- Typical sizes of disconnected components when they occur.  
This would clarify the practical impact of the rules.  

**Head Directionality:**  
The choice to determine head directionality using test data—rather than training or development data—is surprising. While the decision converges rapidly (within 10–15 sentences), making this a minor concern, it warrants justification. Why not leverage development data for this hyperparameter, as is standard practice?  

**Tie-Breaking Mechanism:**  
How are ties resolved when multiple words share identical PageRank scores? Does this tie-breaking strategy significantly affect parsing performance, or are such ties rare enough to be negligible? Clarifying this would improve reproducibility.  

**Constraint Impact Analysis:**  
The head rules, directionality constraint, and distance limitations collectively impose upper bounds on system performance. To guide future improvements, it would be highly informative to include oracle results for each constraint. For example:  
- What is the maximum possible accuracy if *only* the head rules are relaxed?  
- How much does each constraint individually reduce the performance ceiling?  
This would help prioritize where to focus future work to achieve meaningful gains.  

**Minor Corrections:**  
- Section 4.1: "we obtain [the] rank" → remove "[the]" for conciseness.  
- Tables 5 and 7: Table 7’s column order is clearer than Table 5’s; consider standardizing the order.  
- Section 6.1: "isolate the [contribution] of both" → remove "[contribution]" for grammatical flow.  

Overall, the approach is promising but requires deeper empirical analysis to demonstrate its distinct contribution over prior work. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
