

This paper introduces a novel deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and examines its behavior across multiple languages. The algorithm's core consists of rules specifying permitted dependencies based on part-of-speech (POS) tags. It begins by ranking words via a slightly biased PageRank over a graph defined by these permitted dependencies. The algorithm then processes words in ranked order, linking each to the closest preceding word that maintains a valid tree structure, adheres to the head rules, and satisfies a directionality constraint.  

Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly presented, though it appears only marginally distinct from Sogaard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training."  

I have the following questions and suggestions:  
- **Head Rules (Table 1):** An analysis of these rules' behavior relative to the corpus would be valuable. For instance, Section 3.1 notes that the rules do not always yield a connected graph, but does not quantify how frequently this occurs or describe the typical size of disconnected components.  
- **Head Directionality:** It is unexpected that head direction was determined using test data rather than training or development data. While the decision converges rapidly (within 10–15 sentences), this choice warrants justification.  
- **PageRank Tie-Breaking:** How are ties resolved when multiple words share identical PageRank scores? Does this impact performance significantly, or are ties sufficiently rare to be negligible?  
- **Constraint Analysis:** The constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) impose upper bounds on system performance. Including oracle results for each constraint would clarify how much each degrades the maximum achievable score, aiding future work in identifying optimization targets.
