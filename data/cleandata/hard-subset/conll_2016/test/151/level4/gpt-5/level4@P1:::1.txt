Summary
This paper presents UDP, a deterministic, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies. The method uses a small set of POS-based head rules to construct a graph, runs a slightly personalized PageRank to rank content words (favoring the estimated main predicate), and then performs two-step decoding: first attaching content words (enforcing a single root), then attaching function words (enforcing leaf status) with directionality constraints and a distance-based tie-breaker. The system requires no training and estimates adposition attachment direction on the fly from test data. Experiments on 32 UD languages show that UDP is competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer and more robust to domain shift.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, training-free UD parser that is competitive with delexicalized transfer and shows strong cross-domain robustness across many languages.
2) An effective adaptation of PageRank with personalization to UD parsing, combined with two-step decoding that enforces UD function-word leaf constraints.
3) A lightweight, test-time heuristic for estimating adposition direction that avoids language-specific parameters.

Strengths
- Broad, careful evaluation: Results on 32 languages with gold and predicted POS, plus cross-domain analyses, make a compelling case for the approach’s generality and stability. UDP is within 6.4 UAS points of MSD on average (gold POS) and even surpasses it on Hindi.
- Robust to POS errors and domain shift: The performance drop from gold to predicted POS is relatively small (2.2% vs. 2.7% for MSD), and the reported error propagation rate is lower than MSD’s (0.37 vs. 0.46). The cross-domain standard deviations are consistently lower than MSD’s, supporting the claim of greater robustness.
- Clear ablation for PageRank: The comparison to UDP without PR (and to the baseline) shows a sizable contribution of PageRank to content-word attachment quality (~9 UAS points), with two-step decoding also helping over the baseline (~4 UAS points).
- Methodological clarity and determinism: The algorithm is easy to reproduce, does not rely on parallel data or cross-lingual resources, and enforces UD constraints (single root, function-word leaves) by design.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The approach appears to differ only modestly from Søgaard (2012), mainly via personalization in PageRank and the explicit two-step decoding tailored to UD constraints. The paper could better articulate what is fundamentally new beyond these refinements.
- Insufficient analysis of the head rules: The rules do not always yield connected graphs, but the paper does not quantify how often this occurs or the typical component sizes across languages/domains. Given that these rules are central, more corpus-level analysis would strengthen the claims.
- Test-time estimation of head direction: Choosing adposition direction from the test set (albeit with fast convergence after 10–15 sentences) is surprising. While not a major issue, it blurs the line between purely unsupervised inference and using test data to set a parameter; reporting performance when direction is fixed from dev/training would clarify the trade-offs.
- Unspecified tie-breaking for PageRank ties: The algorithm depends on the rank order of content words. The paper does not explain how ties in PageRank scores are resolved or whether ties occur often enough to affect outcomes, especially in short sentences or sparse graphs.
- Lack of oracle upper bounds per constraint: Head rules, directionality, and distance constraints jointly cap the attainable UAS. Oracle analyses isolating each constraint (e.g., allowing gold directionality, or gold-licensed heads, or relaxed distance) would identify the main bottlenecks and guide future extensions.

Questions to the authors
- Head-rule graph connectivity: How often are PR graphs disconnected across languages and domains, and what are the typical component size distributions? Does disconnection correlate with performance drops?
- Test-time directionality: Why was adposition direction chosen from the test set rather than dev/training? How do results change if direction is set from a small held-out set? How sensitive is performance to the number of sentences used for estimation?
- PageRank tie-breaking: How are ties handled in practice? Do ties occur frequently, and do different tie-break strategies measurably impact UAS?
- Oracle constraints: Can you report upper bounds by relaxing one constraint at a time (e.g., oracle directionality; oracle head-rule licensing; oracle ordering of content words) to quantify where most errors arise?
