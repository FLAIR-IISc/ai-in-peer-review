Summary
The paper proposes UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that relies only on POS tags and a small set of UD-compliant head rules. It uses personalized PageRank to prioritize content words, especially the main predicate, and estimates adposition attachment direction from surface bigrams on test data. The system is evaluated on 32 languages with gold and predicted POS, and compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline. The authors also analyze robustness to domain shifts.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, training-free UD parser that combines UD-conformant head rules with a two-phase decoding procedure ensuring content–function word separation and leafness of function words.
- A personalized PageRank scheme to rank content words and improve root selection, yielding competitive performance relative to delexicalized transfer.
- A lightweight, test-time estimation of adposition attachment direction from ADP–NOMINAL bigrams, removing language-specific parameters and improving cross-lingual applicability.

Strengths
- Simplicity and generality: The method requires no training data, uses only POS tags, and avoids language-specific tuning (except a small rule set tuned on English). This makes it widely applicable, especially for low-resource settings.
- Competitive cross-lingual performance: With gold POS, UDP is close to a strong delexicalized transfer baseline and even outperforms it on Hindi. The drop with predicted POS is modest, suggesting robustness to tagging noise.
- Domain robustness: The cross-domain evaluation indicates lower variance across domains than the supervised delexicalized system. This supports the claim that the rule-based/principled approach better resists domain shift.
- Clear ablations: The comparison to a no-PageRank variant and a purely rule-based baseline shows the benefit of personalization and the two-step decoding (notably, ~9 UAS improvement attributed to PageRank).
- Practicality: Estimating head-directionality for adpositions directly from test data is an elegant, data-light solution that converges quickly and works across languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to Søgaard (2012): The approach is conceptually close to prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing with PageRank. The main addition—personalization toward the main predicate—is useful but feels incremental.
- Personalization design appears ad hoc: The weight of 5 for the main predicate and the choice to personalize only a single word (the first verb or first content word) seem arbitrary. There is no sensitivity analysis, alternative weighting scheme, or broader use of personalization (e.g., edge-level scoring or multiple candidates).
- Rule set coverage and structural limitations: The fixed head rules preclude certain constructions (e.g., relative clauses due to excluding NOUN→VERB), and coordination/punctuation remain weak. While acknowledged, these gaps limit applicability and ceiling performance.
- Potential development bias: Although results generalize well, the rule set and some parameters were tuned on English, which could bias decisions (e.g., attachment direction for non-ADP function words). Data-driven estimation (as done for ADP) could be extended to other function words.
- Presentation: The heavy reliance on large tables makes it hard to digest trends. Visual summaries (e.g., bar charts for per-language gains, variance across domains, and ablation effects) would aid readability. Also, POS tagging accuracies in the predicted-POS setting are not reported per language, limiting the reader’s ability to assess error propagation and correlations.

Questions to authors
- Personalization and parameters: How sensitive are results to the personalization weight (5) and the teleport probability (0.05)? Did you try personalizing a small set of likely predicates (e.g., all verbs) or learning the weight on development data?
- Predicate identification: In languages with verb-final order or copula-drop, how often does “first verb or first content word” fail, and what are the observable impacts? Could a simple heuristic (e.g., verb density or morphological cues) improve predicate selection?
- Function-word directionality: You estimate directionality for ADP. Did you experiment with estimating directionality for DET, AUX, CONJ/SCONJ as well? If not, why?
- POS-tagging impact: Please report per-language POS tagging accuracy in the predicted scenario and the correlation between POS accuracy and UAS. How consistent is the reported error-propagation measure across languages?
- Punctuation and coordination: Have you tried simple post-processing heuristics for punctuation and coordination (e.g., attach punctuation to nearest content head, or head-final coordination rules)? If so, what was the effect?

Additional comments
- Reporting: Consider adding figures summarizing (i) average performance vs. baselines, (ii) variance across domains, and (iii) ablation results.
- Reproducibility: Clarify code and data availability (including the in-house annotated domain datasets) and provide scripts to reproduce the bigram-based directionality estimation.
