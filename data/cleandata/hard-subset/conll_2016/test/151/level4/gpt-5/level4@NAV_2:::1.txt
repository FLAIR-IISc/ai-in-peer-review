This paper introduces a deterministic dependency parsing method and examines its behavior across multiple languages. The approach is driven by a compact set of POS-based head rules that define allowable dependencies. The procedure begins by ranking tokens with a lightly personalized PageRank over a graph constructed from those permissible relations. Iterating over that ranking, each token is attached to the nearest head that preserves a tree and satisfies both the head-rule and directionality constraints. The paper is engaging and clearly written, though the differences from Sogaard (2012), “Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training,” seem relatively incremental.

I have a few questions and suggestions:
Head Rules (Table 1) – It would be useful to include empirical analysis of how these rules interact with the data. For instance, Section 3.1 notes that the resulting graphs can be disconnected, but there is no quantification of how often this occurs or typical component sizes.
Choosing head direction on the test set rather than development or training data is unexpected. Given that the estimate stabilizes quickly (10–15 sentences), this may not be critical, but the rationale for this choice would be useful.
How are ties in PageRank handled? Do tie-breaking choices materially affect results, or are ties sufficiently rare to be negligible?
Each constraint type (head rules, directionality, proximity) implicitly caps achievable accuracy. Providing oracle-style results for each constraint (e.g., allowing a perfect choice under one constraint while keeping others fixed) would clarify which limitations are most constraining and guide future extensions.
