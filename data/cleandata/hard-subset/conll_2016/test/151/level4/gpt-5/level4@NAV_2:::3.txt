Summary of the paper
The paper proposes UDP, a training-free Universal Dependencies (UD) parser that relies solely on POS tags and a small set of UD-compliant head rules. It uses a personalized PageRank variant to prioritize likely heads among content words, with additional constraints to ensure function words attach as leaves. The system is positioned as an unsupervised alternative to delexicalized transfer, with evaluations across many languages and POS-tagging scenarios, and an exploration of domain robustness.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, UD-aware, rule-based parser that requires no training data and uses only POS-driven constraints aligned with UD (e.g., function words as dependents).
2) A modest extension of prior PageRank-based unsupervised parsing (e.g., Søgaard, 2012) via personalization that biases the main predicate; the chosen scheme gives extra weight to the first verb (or first content word).
3) A broad empirical study across languages and POS conditions, with indications of robustness to domain shift; however, the methodological increment over Søgaard (2012) appears limited.

Strengths
- Clear, training-free baseline: The approach is conceptually straightforward, language-agnostic, and does not rely on parallel data or model transfer, making it a practical unsupervised baseline for UD parsing.
- Sensible use of UD constraints: Enforcing function words as leaves and using POS-only head rules is linguistically grounded and aligns well with the UD formalism.
- Personalized PageRank is a reasonable addition: Biasing the ranking toward the main predicate is a neat idea that plausibly improves root selection without supervision.
- Broad empirical coverage: The paper reports results across many languages and POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and a coarse content/function setup) and discusses domain robustness, helping position the method relative to delexicalized transfer.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to Søgaard (2012): The core idea—PageRank-based ranking plus rule-based decoding—is very close to earlier work; the personalization component, while useful, feels incremental.
- Narrow personalization design with unclear tuning: Personalization appears to target only the main predicate, and the weight (5×) seems ad hoc; no sensitivity analysis or justification is provided.
- Presentation could be clearer: Heavy reliance on tables makes it hard to digest key patterns; figures/plots summarizing cross-language and cross-domain trends would improve readability.
- Missing POS accuracy context: In the predicted-POS setting, the paper does not report POS tagging accuracies per language or analyze how POS errors correlate with parsing degradation, which is important for interpreting the drops in UAS.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- How was the 5× personalization weight selected? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis across values and languages, and how does UAS vary with this parameter?
- Why restrict personalization to the presumed main predicate? Did you consider broader schemes (e.g., weighting all verbs or using heuristics over content-word neighborhoods), and how did they perform?
- Please report POS tagging accuracies (per language) used in the predicted-POS condition and, if possible, quantify the correlation between tagging errors and UAS drops.
