Summary
The paper proposes a lightweight dependency parsing approach for Universal Dependencies that relies solely on POS tags. It frames parsing as ranking potential heads via a modified, personalized PageRank, assigning higher prior weight to “meaningful” tokens and especially to the sentence’s main predicate. The idea is appealing for its simplicity and potential cross-lingual applicability when lexical resources are scarce.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for a POS-only parser as a robust baseline with minimal supervision and lexicon dependence.
- Recasting dependency parsing with personalized PageRank is elegant and easy to implement.
- The use of a predicate-centered prior is intuitive and linguistically motivated.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited novelty relative to Søgaard (2012). The approach feels like a modest extension; the personalization mechanism primarily targets the main predicate, without deeper exploration of richer priors or learned weights.
- The predicate personalization weight (set to 5) appears ad hoc. There is no justification, sensitivity analysis, or principled tuning procedure provided.
- The paper would benefit from a clearer description of how the “main predicate” is identified using only POS information, especially in languages with auxiliary chains or non-finite predicates.
- Evaluation presentation is hard to digest: large tables without visual summaries obscure key trends and error patterns.
- The connection between POS tagging quality and parsing quality is not quantified. Without reporting predicted POS accuracy and correlating it with parsing metrics, it is difficult to attribute performance drops.
- It is unclear how “meaningful words” vs. “stop words” are operationalized using only POS tags (e.g., which UPOS tags are up/down-weighted, whether punctuation is excluded, and whether the same scheme is used across languages).
- Reproducibility details on PageRank hyperparameters (damping factor, convergence criteria, initialization) and graph construction are insufficient for faithful replication.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen the contribution beyond Søgaard (2012):
  - Explore richer personalization schemes: distribute prior mass across multiple verbal heads/auxiliaries, weight content vs. function POS tags systematically, or use IDF-like tag frequencies. Consider learning tag-specific priors from development data.
  - Provide ablations: no personalization, predicate-only personalization, content-word personalization, and combinations thereof.
  - Include sensitivity analyses for the predicate weight (e.g., 1–10), damping factor, and any edge-weighting parameters. Report validation curves to justify the chosen values.
- Clarify methodology:
  - Specify how the main predicate is detected with POS-only inputs, including fallback behavior for sentences without a clear finite verb.
  - Define exactly how stop/function words are down-weighted using UPOS; list which tags are affected and whether the scheme is language-specific or universal.
  - Describe graph topology: what constitutes candidate head–dependent edges, directionality, and any distance or positional biases.
- Improve evaluation and analysis:
  - Report results with both gold and predicted POS, include the tagger’s accuracy, and analyze correlation between POS accuracy and UAS/LAS (e.g., per-sentence bins or scatter plots).
  - Add plots to complement tables: UAS by sentence length, by POS-tagging accuracy bins, and by dependency relation groupings (core vs. adjunct).
  - Provide an error analysis highlighting recurring failure modes (e.g., attaching auxiliaries, prepositions, coordination).
  - If applicable, discuss cross-lingual trends and whether the same priors work uniformly across typologically diverse languages.
  - Compare to stronger or more recent POS-only baselines (including Søgaard, but also other parsers with restricted feature sets), and include an oracle that knows the root to bound performance.
- Reproducibility:
  - Include precise hyperparameter settings, convergence thresholds, and random seeds, and release code or pseudocode detailed enough to reproduce results.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a neat, resource-light parsing approach that could serve as a strong baseline in low-resource or cross-lingual settings. However, the methodological increment over Søgaard (2012) is modest, and several design decisions (notably the predicate weight) lack empirical justification. Strengthening the experimental analysis—especially with ablations, sensitivity studies, and clearer visualization—and clarifying how the personalization prior is constructed and applied would substantially improve the paper’s impact and clarity.
