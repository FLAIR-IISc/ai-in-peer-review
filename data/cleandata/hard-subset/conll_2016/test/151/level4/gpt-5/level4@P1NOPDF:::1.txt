This paper presents a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and analyzes its behavior across multiple languages. The core idea is a set of POS tag–based head rules that define permitted dependencies. The algorithm begins by computing a slightly biased PageRank over a graph whose edges are given by these permitted dependencies, producing a word ranking. Iterating through this ranking, each word is attached to the closest candidate that preserves a valid tree and is permitted by the head rules and a fixed head-direction constraint.

Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly written, but it seems to differ only slightly from Sogaard (2012), “Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training.”

Questions and suggestions:

- Head rules (Table 1): It would be useful to provide an empirical analysis of these rules relative to the corpus. For example, Section 3.1 notes that they do not always produce a connected graph. How often does this occur, and what are the typical sizes of the resulting components? Quantifying this would help readers understand where the rules succeed or fail across languages and tagsets.

- Head-direction selection: I was surprised that head direction is chosen using the test data rather than training or development data. Given how quickly the decision appears to converge (10–15 sentences), this may not be a major issue, but it is still an unexpected choice. Please justify this decision and clarify whether it could introduce any bias or information leakage. If a dev set is unavailable, consider reporting an alternative selection protocol to demonstrate robustness.

- Tie-breaking in PageRank: How are ties handled for tokens with identical PageRank scores? Does the tie-breaking scheme have a measurable impact on performance, or are ties rare enough to be negligible? If possible, please report sensitivity to different tie-breaking strategies (e.g., left-to-right, right-to-left, random with fixed seed).

- Constraint-induced upper bounds: The combination of constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) imposes upper bounds on achievable performance. It would be informative to include oracle analyses for each constraint individually and in combination, to quantify how much each constraint reduces the maximum attainable score. Such analyses would help identify which constraints are most restrictive and guide future work on where to relax or refine the system.

Minor comments:
- 4.1: “we obtain [the] rank”
- Tables 5 and 7 present columns in different orders; the Table 7 arrangement is clearer.
- 6.1: “isolate the [contribution] of both”
