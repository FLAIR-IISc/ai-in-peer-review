Summary
The paper presents UDP, a deterministic, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). UDP combines a small set of POS-based head rules with a personalized PageRank (PR) over a rule-induced graph to rank content words and identify the main predicate. Decoding proceeds in two phases: first attaching content words (guided by PR) and then attaching function words to enforce leafness, with simple directionality constraints. Adposition attachment direction is estimated on the fly from ADP–NOMINAL bigrams. The system is evaluated on 32 UD languages under three POS settings (gold, predicted, and a coarse CONTENT/FUNCTION proxy) and is compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (MSD). UDP achieves competitive UAS and exhibits lower variance under domain shift than MSD.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, language-agnostic UD parser that operationalizes UD constraints via two-step decoding and POS-based head rules, achieving competitive UAS without any training data.
- The use of personalized PageRank to prioritize main predicate identification and guide attachment order, yielding notable gains over rule-only baselines.
- An extensive multilingual and cross-domain evaluation showing that a rule-based, training-free approach can rival delexicalized transfer and be more robust to domain shifts.

Strengths
- Strong multilingual baseline with minimal supervision: With gold POS, UDP is on average 6.4 UAS points below MSD and surpasses it on Hindi; with predicted POS, UDP’s performance drops only 2.2 points vs. 2.7 for MSD, suggesting lower error propagation from POS. These results make UDP a compelling unsupervised baseline across 32 languages.
- Cross-domain robustness: The paper reports consistently lower standard deviation across domains for UDP than MSD (both with gold and predicted POS), and UDP outperforms MSD in several hard Italian domains. This supports the claim that the method’s reliance on general UD constraints reduces sensitivity to sampling bias.
- Clear algorithmic design with useful ablations: The two-step decoding cleanly enforces UD leafness for function words. The ablation shows that disabling PR reduces UAS by ~9 points and that decoding (without PR) still contributes ~4 points over a strong rule baseline, quantifying the value of each component.
- Practicality and reproducibility: The approach requires only POS tags; head direction for adpositions is estimated from a handful of sentences; the algorithm is deterministic for a given POS sequence; and both parser and additional datasets are stated to be available, facilitating reuse.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to Søgaard (2012): The core ideas (PR-based ranking, rule-guided decoding without training) are close to prior work. Personalized PR and UD-driven two-step decoding are sensible but modest extensions. The paper would benefit from deeper methodological innovation or stronger analytical insights that clearly separate it from prior art.
- Estimating ADP direction on test data: Determining head direction from the test set conflates modeling and evaluation and risks data leakage, even if it converges with 10–15 sentences. A development-only or strictly unsupervised online estimate (without peeking at the evaluation pool) would be cleaner; the paper should quantify the effect of moving this estimation off test data.
- Limited analysis of head rules and graph properties: The paper notes that rule-induced graphs may be disconnected but does not report how often this happens or component sizes across languages. Coverage and violation rates for each rule, and their contribution to errors, are not analyzed. This makes it hard to assess where rules help or hurt.
- Missing oracle and upper-bound analyses: Because decoding is constrained by head rules, directionality, and distance, there are clear upper bounds. Oracle experiments (e.g., perfect head rules, perfect directionality, perfect PR ranking) would identify which constraints most limit performance and guide future improvements (e.g., handling of relative clauses given the exclusion of NOUN→VERB).
- Evaluation scope and detail: Results are unlabeled UAS; there is limited discussion of labeled performance, non-projectivity rates, and specific structures known to be hard under UD (coordination, punctuation, MWE, proper names). The paper acknowledges poor PUNCT and some DET behavior (e.g., Basque) but provides little quantitative breakdown by POS across languages.

Questions to authors
- How often are rule-induced graphs disconnected before applying teleportation? Please report the proportion of sentences with disconnected graphs and the distribution of component sizes per language.
- How exactly are PR ties and distance ties broken, and how frequent are they? Do tie-breaking choices affect determinism across runs or scripts? Any measurable impact on UAS?
- How is ADP direction estimated in practice—once per language on the entire test set, or online per document? Please provide results when the direction is estimated using only training/development data (or a small held-out set) to avoid using test data.
- Can you report oracle upper bounds per constraint (rules, directionality, PR ordering, distance) and combinations thereof? Which constraint is the main bottleneck?
- What is the rate of non-projective attachments produced by the one-pass decoder? Does this vary by language, and does it correlate with errors?
- You set directionality for AUX/DET/SCONJ on English development data. Have you tried estimating these directions per language (analogous to ADP)? If so, how much does UAS change, and does it fix the Basque DET issue?
