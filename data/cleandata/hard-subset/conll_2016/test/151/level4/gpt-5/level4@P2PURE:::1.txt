This paper introduces a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and analyzes its behavior across multiple languages. The core of the method is a set of POS tag–based rules that define which dependencies are permitted. The algorithm first constructs a graph with edges given by these permitted dependencies and ranks words using a slightly biased PageRank. Proceeding through this ranking, each word is attached to the nearest candidate that preserves a tree and satisfies both the head rules and a directionality constraint. Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly presented, but it appears to differ only slightly from Sogaard (2012), “Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training.”

I have a few questions and suggestions:
Head rules (Table 1) — It would be helpful to analyze these rules in relation to the corpus. For instance, Section 3.1 notes that they do not always produce a connected graph, but the frequency of disconnection and typical component sizes are not reported.

I was surprised that head direction was selected using test data rather than training or development data. Given how quickly the decision converges (10–15 sentences), this is likely not a major issue, but it is an unexpected choice.

How are ties handled for words with identical PageRank scores? Does the tie-breaking strategy significantly affect performance, or are ties rare enough to be negligible?

The various constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) impose upper bounds on the system’s achievable performance. It would be informative to include oracle results for each constraint to quantify how much each one reduces the maximum possible score. This would help guide future work toward the most impactful modifications.
