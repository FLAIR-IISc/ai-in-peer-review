Summary
This paper presents UDP, a deterministic, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method relies on a small set of POS-based head rules, a two-step decoding that first attaches content words and then function words (enforcing UD’s leaf constraint for function words), and a personalized PageRank (PR) over a rule-induced graph to prioritize likely heads. Adposition attachment direction is estimated on the fly from test data using simple bigram statistics. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages under gold and predicted POS, and compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer system. UDP is competitive with delexicalized transfer on UAS and appears more robust to domain shift.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, language-agnostic, training-free UD parser combining POS-based head rules with personalized PR to identify likely roots and head attachment order.
- A two-step decoding procedure that enforces UD’s constraint that function words are dependents (leaves), plus a practical, data-driven heuristic for adposition direction estimated at test time.
- A broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation showing surprising robustness relative to a delexicalized supervised baseline.

Strengths
- Simplicity and clarity: The approach is conceptually straightforward, easy to reproduce, and clearly described. The deterministic decoding given a POS sequence is appealing and facilitates analysis.
- Competitive performance without training: Despite its simplicity, UDP achieves UAS close to a strong delexicalized baseline across many languages, and even outperforms it on Hindi. The degradation under predicted POS is modest relative to the supervised transfer system.
- Cross-domain robustness: The cross-genre evaluation indicates lower variance across domains compared to the delexicalized baseline, consistent with the intuition that a rule-based, low-parameter system is less prone to domain overfitting.
- Personalized PR is effective: The PR-based ranking (with personalization toward the first verb/content word) yields meaningful improvements over rule-only variants and accelerates convergence, according to the reported ablations.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to Søgaard (2012): The overall design—rule-induced graphs and PR-based ordering for unsupervised parsing—closely resembles prior work. The main differences (personalization, strict UD leaf constraints, and test-time estimation of ADP direction) are useful but incremental.
- Insufficient analysis of head rules: The paper does not quantify how often the rule-induced graphs are disconnected, nor the typical component sizes. More diagnostics on coverage, error patterns per rule, and cross-lingual variation would strengthen the claims.
- Use of test data for directionality: Estimating adposition direction from the test set—even if it converges quickly—blurs the line between pure test-time inference and tuning. A comparison against development-set estimation (and extension to other function words) would clarify the trade-offs.
- Missing oracle upper bounds: With multiple constraints (rules, direction, distance, PR order), it would be informative to report oracle attachments for each constraint in isolation to identify the main performance bottlenecks and guide future improvements.
- Limited syntactic coverage: Explicitly excluding NOUN→VERB heads prevents recovering relative clauses; coordination and punctuation are also weak spots. Discussing how often these cases arise and their contribution to overall errors would help contextualize results.

Questions to authors
- Rule-induced graphs: How often are sentence graphs disconnected under your head rules, and what are the typical sizes of connected components across languages? Does disconnection correlate with performance?
- Tie-breaking in PR: How are ties in PR scores resolved (e.g., by token order, index, or degree)? How frequent are ties, and how sensitive is UAS to the tie-breaking strategy?
- Personalization and PR settings: How sensitive are results to the personalization weight (currently 5×) and teleport probability (0.05)? Did you test alternatives or language-specific defaults?
- Directionality estimation: Why prefer estimating ADP direction on test rather than on development data? Have you tried extending this estimation to other function words (DET, AUX, SCONJ), especially for languages like Basque where DET direction differs?
- Constraint oracles: Can you report oracle results for (i) perfect PR ranking, (ii) perfect directionality, and (iii) perfect rule licensing, to isolate each constraint’s ceiling?
- Determinism and reproducibility: Given determinism with respect to POS tags, are there any remaining sources of nondeterminism (e.g., PR numeric precision or tie-breaking)? Will you release code and exact settings?

Minor comments
- Section 4.1: “we obtain the rank” (add the article).
- Section 6.1: “isolate the contribution of both” (typo).
- Tables: Table 5 and Table 7 use different column orders; consider adopting Table 7’s clearer arrangement consistently.
- Typos/wording: “assess” (not “asses”); check “presidential” vs “presidental”; standardize “Søgaard” spelling; “PageRank” capitalization; “adposition.”
- Clarify in text that decoding is deterministic given a POS sequence and directionality settings.
- Cite exactly how many languages were excluded (e.g., Japanese due to missing word forms) at first mention, for transparency.
- Consider adding a brief note on computational cost: PR dominates runtime—what are typical sentence-level runtimes?

Overall evaluation
A well-executed, clear, and surprisingly strong unsupervised baseline tailored to UD. While the methodological novelty is limited, the breadth of evaluation and the cross-domain robustness make it a valuable contribution and a useful reference point for future UD parsing work. I recommend acceptance provided the authors expand the analysis of rule behavior, justify test-time direction estimation, and add oracle/ablation diagnostics to pinpoint where the main gains and ceilings lie.
