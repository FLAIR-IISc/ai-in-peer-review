Summary
This paper proposes UDP, a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). UDP builds on a small set of UD-aligned head rules, enforces function words as leaves via a two-step decoding procedure, and uses personalized PageRank to prioritize likely main predicates and guide attachment. Adposition attachment direction (pre vs. postposition) is estimated directly from test data using simple bigram statistics. The method is evaluated on 32 languages under three POS-tagging regimes and compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). UDP is shown to be competitive with MSD, less sensitive to POS errors, and more robust to domain shifts.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, training-free UD parser that operationalizes key UD constraints (single root; function words as dependents) with a two-step decoding strategy and a small, language-agnostic rule set.
2) Personalized PageRank for content-word prioritization, improving root and head selection without supervision, supported by an ablation (UDPNoPR) showing a sizable UAS gain.
3) A practical, on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test data, avoiding language-specific parameters and contributing to cross-lingual applicability.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual and cross-domain robustness with zero training: UDP attains UAS close to a multi-source delexicalized baseline across 32 languages (even surpassing MSD on Hindi), while being less sensitive to domain shifts (lower per-domain standard deviation) and to POS errors (lower error propagation). This makes UDP an attractive baseline for low-resource or out-of-domain scenarios.
- Effective use of UD formalism: The two-step decoding that first attaches content words and then function words enforces UD’s leaf status for function words and single-root constraint. The approach is linguistically motivated and easy to reason about.
- Sensible parameter-free adaptation: Estimating ADP attachment direction from test bigrams (converging quickly with 10–15 sentences) removes language-specific tuning and worked well empirically (high ADP UAS), supporting UDP’s claim of being training-free and portable.
- Comprehensive evaluation and useful ablations: The experiments cover gold and predicted POS, an extreme content/function tag scenario, a broad set of languages, and domain splits. The ablation indicates PageRank accounts for ~9 UAS points over the non-PR version, clarifying where gains come from.
- Clarity of algorithm and efficiency: The decoding algorithm is simple, deterministic given POS, and near-quadratic in practice, with PageRank as the main computational cost. This simplicity favors reproducibility and deployment.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The approach is conceptually close to Søgaard (2012), which also used PageRank and rules for unsupervised dependency parsing. The main additions—personalization toward the main predicate, two-step decoding to enforce function-word leaves, and ADP-direction estimation—are incremental rather than transformative. The paper would benefit from a deeper comparison to Søgaard (e.g., side-by-side ablations) and discussion of what new capabilities UDP unlocks.
- Heuristic choices lack sensitivity analysis: The fixed personalization weight (5x) and teleport probability (0.05) are tuned on English and used universally, but the paper does not explore their sensitivity across languages or scripts, nor justify the specific values beyond convergence needs. Similarly, the “first verb” heuristic for personalization may be fragile for languages/genres with non-verbal predicates or verb-final structures.
- Rule coverage and construction: The head rules were selected on English dev data, which risks encoding English-specific biases. Known limitations are acknowledged (e.g., no NOUN→VERB edges prevents modeling relative clauses; low performance on coordination and punctuation), but the paper does not attempt to mitigate them or quantify their contribution to the error budget. Labeling is not considered.
- Evaluation gaps: While the predicted-POS scenario is informative, per-language POS accuracies (from TnT) and their correlation with UAS degradation are not provided, making it harder to interpret robustness claims. Statistical significance testing is absent. Some analyses are hard to parse from large tables; visualization would help.
- Practicality in truly low-resource settings: The predicted POS setup relies on in-language supervised taggers (TnT trained on UD training sets). This is reasonable for UD but limits claims about “unsupervised” applicability in settings without target-language POS data. The extreme CF-tag scenario is informative but too crude to stand in for realistic low-resource tagging.

Questions to Authors
- Please report per-language POS-tagging accuracies for the predicted-POS condition and quantify the correlation between POS accuracy and UAS drop. How was the error-propagation metric computed per language?
- How sensitive is performance to the personalization weight and teleport probability? Have you tried learning the personalization distribution over multiple candidate roots (e.g., verbs and nominals) instead of a fixed 5x on the first verb?
- Can the directionality constraints for other function words (beyond ADP) be estimated from data as you do for ADP? Would that improve DET in Basque and other outliers?
- Could you provide a breakdown of errors attributable to the rule set’s known limitations (e.g., absence of NOUN→VERB for relative clauses, coordination handling, punctuation)? How much headroom remains if these are addressed?
- In languages with mixed adposition behavior or many multiword adpositions, how stable is the bigram-based ADP-direction estimate? A learning curve or per-language analysis would help.

Additional Comments
- Presentation: Some graphical summaries (e.g., per-language scatter plots of UDP vs. MSD, domain-wise variance plots, ablation deltas) would improve readability.
- Minor typo: l. 207: “The the main idea” → “The main idea.”
- Reproducibility: Please ensure code, scripts for domain splits, and the in-house annotated datasets are clearly linked and versioned.
