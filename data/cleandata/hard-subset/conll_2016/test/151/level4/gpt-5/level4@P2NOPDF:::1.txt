This paper presents a deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and evaluates its behavior across multiple languages. The core approach is a rule set that licenses dependencies based on POS tags. The method constructs a graph from these permitted arcs, computes a slightly biased PageRank to rank tokens, and then, in that order, attaches each word to the nearest candidate that preserves tree well-formedness and satisfies both the head-selection rules and a directionality constraint.

Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly written, but the novelty appears limited relative to Sogaard (2012), “Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training.”

Questions and suggestions:
- Head rules (Table 1): Please provide corpus-level analysis of these rules. In Section 3.1 you note that they can yield a disconnected graph; how often does this occur, and what are the typical component sizes?
- Head direction: It is surprising that head direction is selected on the test data rather than on training or development data. Given that the choice stabilizes within 10–15 sentences, this may not be a major issue, but the decision procedure should be justified and, ideally, moved off the test set. If kept, please discuss potential leakage and its impact.
- PageRank ties: How are ties in PageRank scores broken? Do tie-breaking choices affect performance measurably, or are ties sufficiently rare to be negligible?
- Constraint-induced upper bounds: The combination of head rules, directionality, and distance constraints imposes ceilings on achievable accuracy. It would be very informative to report oracle results isolating each constraint’s effect—e.g., oracle with only head rules, then adding directionality, then distance—to quantify how much each constraint reduces the maximum possible score. This would help guide future modifications by identifying the most limiting components.
