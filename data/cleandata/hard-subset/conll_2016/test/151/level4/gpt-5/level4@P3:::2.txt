Summary and contributions
This paper proposes UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. UDP combines a small set of UD-compliant head rules with personalized PageRank to rank content words and a two-step decoding algorithm that enforces UD constraints (single-root, function words as leaves). A language-agnostic heuristic estimates adposition directionality directly from the input. The system is evaluated on 32 languages under gold and predicted POS, and on multiple domains, against a competitive multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, training-free UD parser that is competitive with a strong cross-lingual delexicalized baseline across many languages, and demonstrably more robust to domain shift and POS noise.
2) A principled use of personalized PageRank and two-step decoding tailored to UD constraints, which substantially improves root selection and content-word attachment without supervision.
3) A language-agnostic mechanism for estimating adposition attachment direction from test data, removing language-specific parameters.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual performance without training: UDP achieves UAS close to a multi-source delexicalized parser (average gap ~6.4% with gold POS) and even outperforms it on Hindi. This is notable given it uses no parallel data, no target-side training, and only POS tags and generic rules. The ablation indicates PageRank contributes ~9 UAS points over a rules-only variant.

- Robustness to domain shift: The cross-domain analyses (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian) show substantially lower performance variance for UDP than for the delexicalized baseline, under both gold and predicted POS. This supports the claim that rule-based, UD-driven design yields stable behavior across genres compared to data-driven transfer susceptible to sampling bias.

- Resilience to POS degradation: Under predicted POS, UDP’s performance drops only marginally (~2.2%), with lower error propagation from POS to dependencies (0.37) than the delex baseline (0.46). This is valuable in realistic low-resource scenarios where POS accuracy is imperfect.

- Clear, linguistically grounded design: The parser explicitly encodes UD principles (function words as dependents, single-root constraint) and separates content and function words during decoding. The simple, interpretable head rules and the adposition direction heuristic are easy to audit and adapt.

- Transparent methodology and analysis: The paper includes detailed algorithmic description, complexity considerations (decoding O(n×|C|), main cost in PageRank), illustrative walk-through, thorough language- and POS-wise results, and ablations (PageRank vs. rules-only) that isolate contributions.

Weaknesses
- Mild target-side adaptation on test data: Estimating adposition direction from the test set blurs the line between purely unsupervised parsing and light target adaptation. While the heuristic converges quickly and is language-agnostic, using the test text to estimate parameters could be seen as using test-side signals at inference time. Clarifying its practical deployment (e.g., per-document online estimation) would help.

- Limited coverage of syntactic phenomena: The fixed rules exclude certain attachments (e.g., NOUN→VERB to capture relative clauses), and the system has difficulty with coordination and punctuation (low UAS for PUNCT and CONJ). This limits ceiling performance and may systematically affect languages or domains where these phenomena are frequent.

- Dependence on UD POS quality and conventions: Although UDP is relatively robust to predicted POS, its behavior relies on consistent UD POS distinctions (e.g., AUX vs. VERB, DET vs. PRON), which vary across UD v1.2 treebanks. The fixed function-word attachment directions tuned on English may be suboptimal for some languages (e.g., Basque DET).

- Evaluation scope: Results are reported only for unlabeled attachment. Given the rule-based design and UD constraints, partial labeling (or analysis of relation-specific accuracy) could further substantiate the utility of the approach. Comparisons largely focus on a single supervised baseline; including additional unsupervised/cross-lingual baselines would strengthen claims.

- Personalization heuristic may bias root selection: The PageRank personalization assumes the first verb (or first content word) is the main predicate. While effective overall and even in verb-final languages, the paper notes root accuracy variability (e.g., very low for Arabic, Tamil). Additional analysis of failure modes and alternatives could be informative.

Questions to the authors
- Test-time estimation of adposition direction: Is this computed per language, per document, or per sentence batch? How do you avoid look-ahead when parsing streaming text? Would estimating from development data or a small unlabeled sample materially change results?

- Sensitivity to PageRank parameters: How sensitive is performance to teleport probability and the 5× personalization weight? Did you test language-specific alternatives (e.g., verb-final heuristics) and does personalization ever harm performance?

- Rule set portability: You tuned rule inclusion and function-word directions on English. How robust are these choices across typologically diverse languages? Could you estimate directions for DET/AUX/SCONJ from test data, akin to ADP, and would that help Basque and similar cases?

- Phenomena coverage: Do you have empirical estimates of errors attributable to coordination, MWEs, and relative clauses? Among the proposed future extensions, which yields the largest gains?

- Runtime and scalability: What are average runtimes per sentence and per language, and how does PageRank cost scale with sentence length and graph sparsity? Any practical constraints for large-scale processing?

Overall recommendation
This is a clear, well-motivated paper that delivers a simple and effective unsupervised baseline for UD parsing. The method is competitive with delexicalized transfer across many languages, more robust to domain shifts, and elegantly exploits UD constraints. Despite limitations in coverage and the test-time ADP direction heuristic, the contribution is solid and timely. I recommend acceptance.
