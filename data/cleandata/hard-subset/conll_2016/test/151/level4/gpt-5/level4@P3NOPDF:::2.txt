This paper proposes an unsupervised approach to Universal Dependencies parsing that requires no training. The method ranks words using PageRank over a graph constructed from sentences and applies a small set of deterministic rules to produce dependency structures. The paper is clearly written, detailed, and provides sound intuition for the priors and constraints incorporated into the model. The contribution is relevant to unsupervised parsing and highlights promising directions for low-resource and training-free settings.

Strengths
- Clear and accessible presentation. The paper motivates the design choices, explains the role of prior information, and situates the approach within the broader UD framework.
- Simplicity and practicality. A training-free method with a compact ruleset is appealing as a strong, reproducible baseline, especially for new languages or domains lacking annotated data.
- Conceptual novelty. Leveraging PageRank as a central scoring mechanism, combined with minimal rule-based constraints, is an elegant alternative to parameterized unsupervised models.
- Potential impact. The approach could facilitate rapid bootstrapping of annotations, provide initialization for weakly supervised systems, or serve as a fallback parser in low-resource pipelines.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on hand-crafted rules. Although few, these rules may encode language- or annotation-specific biases that could limit generalization across UD languages and treebanks. More discussion on language-agnostic design and any language-specific exceptions would be helpful.
- Scope and robustness of evaluation. The paper would benefit from:
  - Broad multilingual evaluation across diverse UD languages (head-initial/head-final, morphologically rich, nonprojective).
  - Strong baselines, including standard unsupervised parsers and simple heuristics (e.g., right-/left-branching), as well as delexicalized supervised parsers.
  - Clear metrics and settings (UAS/LAS, with/without punctuation), significance testing, and per-language breakdowns.
- Ablation and sensitivity analysis. Given the hybrid nature of the method, it is important to quantify:
  - The contribution of PageRank vs. each rule (and any fallbacks).
  - Sensitivity to graph construction choices, damping factor, convergence criteria, and edge definitions or weights.
  - The effect of different sources of prior information (e.g., POS tags, morphological cues) if used.
- Clarification of “unsupervised.” While the method does not use annotated data for training, the rules may implicitly encode knowledge derived from UD guidelines or linguistic conventions. A brief reflection on what form of supervision is being avoided and what prior knowledge is being injected would strengthen the claims.
- Structural properties. It is not clear how the approach enforces well-formed trees (single head, acyclicity, connectivity) or handles nonprojective dependencies. A precise description of constraints and tie-breaking strategies would improve clarity.
- Reproducibility and resource release. To maximize impact, releasing code, rule definitions, and configuration files would be valuable. If code is not available, a step-by-step algorithmic description and full rule list (perhaps in an appendix) are essential.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand the empirical study to include more languages and domains, report per-language results, and conduct thorough ablations. Include strong baselines and significance tests.
- Provide detailed algorithmic specifications:
  - How the graph is built (nodes, edges, directionality, weighting).
  - PageRank parameters and stopping criteria.
  - How heads are selected and labels assigned; how tree constraints are enforced.
- Analyze error patterns by dependency relation, sentence length, and punctuation handling; discuss typical failure modes and how rules interact with PageRank scores.
- Discuss computational complexity and runtime, including scalability to long sentences and large corpora.
- Position the work relative to prior unsupervised parsing and graph-ranking approaches, clarifying what is novel about the integration and what limitations remain.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written and thoughtfully motivated paper that proposes a simple and potentially impactful unsupervised UD parsing approach grounded in PageRank and minimal deterministic rules. With a more comprehensive evaluation, stronger baseline comparisons, and detailed ablations, the contribution could serve as a solid benchmark and inspire follow-up research on hybrid unsupervised parsing. I view the work as a meaningful contribution to the area; addressing the concerns above would significantly strengthen the paper’s empirical and methodological rigor.
