Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies solely on POS tags and a small set of head rules. It uses a personalized PageRank variant to prioritize content words, with extra weight assigned to the estimated main predicate. The system requires no training and is evaluated against delexicalized transfer baselines across many languages and domains.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, training-free UD parser that combines POS-based head rules with a lightweight personalized PageRank signal to identify the main predicate and guide attachment.
2) An empirical comparison to delexicalized transfer, including multilingual evaluation and domain-shift analysis.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and attractive: The approach is easy to implement, training-free, and exploits UD’s POS-level constraints. Using PageRank to prioritize content words is a sensible idea in this setting.
- Personalization insight: Adding a personalization factor for the main predicate is a neat twist on prior PageRank-based parsing work.
- Broad evaluation: The paper provides extensive multilingual experiments and domain analyses, which is valuable for assessing robustness.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty over Søgaard (2012): The approach feels very close to prior rule-aided, PageRank-based unsupervised parsing. The main technical addition—personalization focused on the main predicate—seems incremental. As presented, the method does not clearly “take the next step” beyond Søgaard (2012).
- Underutilized personalization: As far as I can tell, personalization is used only to weight the main predicate, and the chosen weight (5) appears arbitrary. There is little justification or exploration of this choice, and no analysis of sensitivity to this hyperparameter.
- Presentation of results: The evaluation relies heavily on tables. Some key findings would be easier to absorb with charts (e.g., per-language or per-domain variance plots, or error-propagation visuals).
- Missing POS-tagging details: For the predicted POS setup, it would be important to report POS tagging accuracy and to analyze how much of the parsing degradation is explained by tagging errors (e.g., correlation or an error-propagation estimate). This would clarify whether the observed drop is primarily due to POS quality, as one would expect.

Questions to authors
- Personalization:
  - Why is the main predicate weighted by exactly 5? Did you try other values or tuning strategies, and how sensitive are the results to this choice?
  - Did you consider extending personalization beyond the main predicate (e.g., to other content words or to edge-level scoring), or incorporating a more principled prior?
- Predicted POS:
  - Please report POS tagging accuracy per language in the predicted-POS setup.
  - Can you quantify the correlation between POS accuracy and UAS, or provide an error-propagation analysis, to substantiate the impact of tagging errors on parsing performance?
- Analysis and presentation:
  - Could you add charts to highlight cross-language and cross-domain variance, and to visualize the contribution of personalization versus no-personalization baselines?

Overall assessment
The paper is interesting and the idea of using personalized PageRank in a POS-only, rule-based UD parser is appealing. However, the increment over Søgaard (2012) appears modest, with personalization applied in a limited way and with an apparently arbitrary weight. Strengthening the justification for personalization, expanding its use, and clarifying the effect of POS accuracy on parsing would improve the contribution and its clarity.
