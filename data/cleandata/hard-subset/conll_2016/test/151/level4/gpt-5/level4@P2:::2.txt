Summary
This paper presents UDP, a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that combines a small set of POS-based head rules with personalized PageRank to rank content words and identify the sentence’s main predicate. Decoding proceeds in two steps: content words are attached first according to PageRank order and rule constraints, then function words are attached with directionality heuristics that enforce UD’s leaf constraint for function words. Adposition attachment direction is estimated directly on test data via a simple bigram heuristic. Evaluations on 32 languages (UD v1.2) under gold POS, predicted POS, and a coarse content/function tag setting show that UDP is competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline and more robust to domain shifts.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, training-free UD parsing baseline that achieves results close to a strong delexicalized transfer system across many languages and domains.
2) A practical use of personalized PageRank plus a two-step decoding scheme to enforce UD’s function-word-as-leaf constraint and improve root and head selection without supervision.
3) A lightweight, test-time estimation of adposition directionality that removes language-specific parameters.

Strengths
- Clear, principled design: The method ties directly to UD’s constraints (single root, function words as dependents) and explains how PageRank personalization and rule-based decoding interact. The algorithm is easy to reproduce and understand.
- Broad and thorough evaluation: Results cover 32 languages, three POS setups, and multiple domains, including in-house annotated splits. The analysis includes error propagation from POS to dependencies and ablations (e.g., UDP vs. UDPNoPR), quantifying PageRank’s contribution (~9 UAS on average) and the decoding step (~4 UAS over the baseline).
- Competitive performance with robustness: UDP is on average within 6–7 UAS of the multi-source delexicalized model with gold POS, outperforms it on Hindi, and degrades less when moving from gold to predicted POS. Cross-domain variance is consistently lower than the delexicalized baseline, supporting claims of stability.
- Minimal language-specific tuning: Apart from English-based development of rule choices and function-word directionality (except ADP), the approach avoids per-language parameters; ADP direction is inferred from test text.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on POS quality: Although the parsing algorithm is training-free, it relies on POS tags, and performance drops substantially under the content/function-only setup. The “unsupervised” claim is therefore contingent on having reliable POS (often supervised). A comparison using cross-lingual projected POS tags could better reflect low-resource scenarios.
- Test-time estimation on target data: Inferring adposition direction from the test set may be seen as peeking at the target domain. While unsupervised, it assumes access to sufficient target text and may be brittle in very small or skewed test sets. Reporting sensitivity to sample size and domain skew would strengthen the claim.
- Limited rule coverage and linguistic phenomena: Excluding NOUN→VERB heads prevents modeling relative clauses; performance on PUNCT and CONJ is low and variable; DET directionality differs in Basque. Some of these could potentially be inferred per language at test time (as with ADP), but are currently fixed from English development.
- Scope of baselines: The comparison focuses on a delexicalized transfer parser. Additional baselines (e.g., projection-based parsers, recent unsupervised or typology-informed systems) and neutralized-direction metrics could better position the method relative to broader prior work.
- Reporting details: It would be helpful to quantify how often decoding falls back to dropping rule/direction constraints to ensure a tree, the rate of non-projective attachments, statistical significance across languages, and runtime breakdown (PageRank vs. decoding).

Questions to the authors
- How sensitive are results to the PageRank teleport parameter and the 5× personalization weight? Did you explore language-specific or sentence-length-dependent settings?
- What is the minimum number of sentences needed to reliably estimate adposition direction, and how robust is the heuristic under distribution shifts (e.g., domains with atypical ADP usage)?
- Could the directionality of DET/AUX/SCONJ be estimated per language at test time as you do for ADP, and would that close gaps like Basque DET attachments?
- How often does the algorithm back off by dropping rule/direction constraints, and how does this correlate with errors? What fraction of produced trees are non-projective?
- Do you have per-family analyses (e.g., SOV vs. SVO, case-rich vs. analytic) to understand when PageRank personalization and the two-step decoding help or hurt?

Overall assessment
The paper is well written, methodologically transparent, and provides a strong, interpretable training-free baseline for UD parsing. The empirical coverage and robustness findings are compelling. I view this as a substantive contribution to unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing, with clear avenues for extension (learning more directionality at test time, handling coordination and MWEs, edge scoring via PageRank on reified graphs). I recommend acceptance.
