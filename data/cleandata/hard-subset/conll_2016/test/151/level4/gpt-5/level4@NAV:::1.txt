This paper presents a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and examines its behavior across several languages. The method centers on a set of POS-tag-based rules that specify allowed dependencies. It begins by ranking tokens using a slightly biased PageRank on a graph whose edges correspond to the permitted dependencies. Proceeding through the ranking, each token attaches to the nearest token that preserves a tree structure and is allowed by the head rules and a directionality constraint. Overall, the paper is interesting and clearly written, though it seems to differ only modestly from Sogaard (2012), “Unsupervised Dependency Parsing without Training.”

I have a few questions and suggestions:
Head Rules (Table 1) - It would be helpful to analyze these rules in relation to the corpus. For example, Section 3.1 notes that they do not always produce a connected graph, but there is no information on how often this occurs or the typical sizes of the components.
I was surprised that head direction was chosen using the test data rather than training or development data. Given how quickly the decision converges (10–15 sentences), this is not a major issue, but it is an unexpected choice.
How are ties handled for tokens with identical PageRank scores? Does this materially affect performance, or are ties rare enough to be negligible?
The various constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) impose upper bounds on achievable performance. It would be informative to include oracle results for each constraint to quantify how much each reduces the maximum possible score. This would be especially useful for guiding future work on where to modify the system.
