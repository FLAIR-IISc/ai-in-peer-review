This paper describes a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm and analyzes its performance in various languages.

The algorithm's core is a set of rules defining the dependencies allowed based on POS tags.

The algorithm starts by ranking words using a slightly biased PageRank on a graph with edges defined by the allowed dependencies.

Moving forward through the ranking, each word is linked to the nearest word that will maintain a tree and is allowed by the head rules and a directional constraint.

Overall, this paper is interesting and clearly presented, although it seems to be slightly different from Sogard (2012), "Unsupervised Dependency Parsing Without Training."

I have some questions and suggestions:

Head rules (Table 1) - It would be good to have some analysis of these rules in relation to the corpus.

For example, Section 3.1 mentions the fact that they do not always lead to a connected graph, but it does not mention how often this happens, or how large the components are typically.

I was surprised that the head direction was chosen using test data rather than training or development data.

Given how quickly the decision converges (10-15 sentences), this is not a major problem, but a surprising choice.

How does tie-breaking work for words with the same PageRank score?

Does it significantly affect performance, or are ties so rare that it has no effect?

The variety of constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) will bring upper limits on the system's potential performance.

It would be informative to include the Oracle results for each constraint, to show how much they damage the maximum possible score.

This will be particularly helpful for guiding future work on where to try to modify this system.
