### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces UDP, a rule-based, unsupervised dependency parser designed for the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The method requires no training data in the conventional sense. Instead, it relies on a small, hand-crafted set of head-attachment rules based on UD POS tags, a two-step decoding algorithm to enforce that function words are leaves, and a personalized PageRank algorithm to determine the attachment order of content words. The system is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks, where it is compared against a rule-based baseline and a supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. The evaluation also includes an analysis of robustness to POS tag errors and domain shift.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An Extensive Empirical Study on Rule-Based Parsing for UD:** The primary contribution is the comprehensive evaluation of a simple, knowledge-based parser across a wide array of 32 languages and multiple domains. The analysis of the system's robustness to domain shift and non-gold POS tags provides valuable insights into the practical trade-offs between rule-based and data-driven approaches in a cross-lingual setting.
2.  **A Strong, Training-Free Baseline System:** The paper presents a well-engineered and surprisingly effective baseline for unsupervised UD parsing. While its components are based on existing ideas, their specific adaptation and combination for the UD framework result in a strong, transparent, and easily reproducible system that can serve as a valuable point of reference for future research in low-resource and unsupervised parsing.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Wide-Ranging Evaluation:** The authors are to be commended for the scale of their experiments. Evaluating on 32 languages provides strong evidence for the general applicability of their approach within the UD framework. The inclusion of experiments with both gold and predicted POS tags offers a realistic assessment of the system's utility.
2.  **Compelling Robustness Analysis:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a significant strength. The finding that UDP exhibits lower variance in performance across domains compared to the data-driven MSD system (Table 6) provides strong, empirical support for the long-held belief that knowledge-based systems can be more stable. This is a crucial practical consideration for real-world applications.
3.  **Methodological Simplicity and Transparency:** The proposed parser is conceptually straightforward, computationally inexpensive, and fully deterministic. The core logic is encoded in a small set of explicit rules (Table 1) and a clear algorithm (Figure 1). This transparency is a welcome departure from the opacity of many contemporary neural models and makes the system's behavior easy to analyze and understand.
4.  **Competitive Performance for an Unsupervised Method:** For a system that eschews conventional training, the results are quite strong. The fact that UDP performs within an average of 6.4% UAS of a competitive multi-source delexicalized transfer system (MSDG) is a noteworthy achievement and demonstrates the power of leveraging the explicit constraints of a consistent annotation formalism like UD.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The approach is fundamentally a carefully engineered collection of existing ideas and heuristics. Rule-based parsing, leveraging linguistic formalism constraints, and using graph centrality measures like PageRank to identify important words are all established concepts. The novelty is in the synthesis, but the work does not introduce a new parsing paradigm or learning algorithm. The system's success feels more like a testament to the consistency of the UD framework than a significant methodological advance in parsing itself.
2.  **Overstatement of "Training-Free" Status:** The authors claim the parser requires "no training," but this is not strictly accurate. The head attachment rules were selected by "maximizing precision on the development set" (English). Key parameters, including the PageRank teleport probability, personalization weight, and the attachment direction for most function words, were also tuned on the English development data. This constitutes a form of manual feature engineering and parameter tuning on a development set, which is a form of training, albeit not gradient-based. This should be stated more transparently.
3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Other Unsupervised Parsers:** The paper's primary comparison point is a *supervised* delexicalized transfer model. While this is a relevant baseline for the cross-lingual setting, the work positions itself within the tradition of unsupervised dependency parsing and grammar induction. As such, the complete omission of empirical comparisons to established unsupervised models like the Dependency Model with Valence (DMV) is a significant weakness. Citing a 2012 paper to dismiss an entire class of models is insufficient; a direct comparison, even on a few languages, would be necessary for a rigorous assessment of the state of the art.
4.  **Brittleness and Limited Expressivity of the Rule Set:** The parser's performance is fundamentally capped by its small, fixed rule set. The authors explicitly note that the system cannot handle relative clauses because the requisite `NOUN → VERB` rule was excluded during development. This highlights the inherent brittleness of the approach: it is incapable of learning or representing any syntactic phenomena not explicitly encoded in its hand-crafted rules. This makes it less a general-purpose grammar induction system and more an engine for producing structures that conform to a predefined template.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The personalization of PageRank, which gives a 5x weight to the likely predicate, appears crucial. Could you provide a sensitivity analysis for this hyperparameter? How does performance vary with different weights, and what was the rationale for choosing 5 over other values during development?
2.  The head attachment rules in Table 1 form the core of your parser. You state they were tuned on the English dev set. Could you elaborate on this process? Specifically, how many potential rules were considered and subsequently rejected? How can you be confident that this rule set is not overly biased towards English syntactic patterns, despite the cross-lingual aims of UD?
3.  The on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test set bigrams is a clever heuristic. Have you investigated its stability on small test sets or in languages that might not have a single dominant adpositional strategy? How many sentences are truly required for this estimation to become reliable across different languages?
