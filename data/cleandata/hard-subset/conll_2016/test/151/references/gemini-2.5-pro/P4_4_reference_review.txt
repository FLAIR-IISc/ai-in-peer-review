### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents UDP, a dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that operates without any training data. The method is rule-based, leveraging a small, fixed set of head-dependent attachment rules based on UD POS tags. The core of the system is a two-step decoding algorithm. In the first step, content words (nouns, verbs, etc.) are ranked using a personalized PageRank algorithm, which prioritizes likely sentence predicates. These words are then attached sequentially to form the syntactic backbone of the sentence, with each word attaching to the closest available head licensed by the rules. In the second step, function words (adpositions, determiners, etc.) are attached to the existing content words, ensuring they are treated as leaf nodes, a key constraint of the UD formalism. The system is designed to be language-agnostic, with language-specific properties like adposition directionality being estimated on-the-fly from the test data itself.

The authors conduct an extensive evaluation on 32 UD treebanks across three POS-tagging scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive). They compare their system against a simple rule-based baseline and a strong, supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). A key part of the evaluation is a detailed analysis of the parser's robustness to domain shift on a subset of languages. The results show that UDP is a competitive system, performing close to the supervised MSD parser and demonstrating superior robustness to both POS tagging errors and domain variation.

### Contributions
1.  **A comprehensive empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of a simple, training-free parser as a strong and robust baseline for UD parsing.** The paper's primary contribution is its extensive evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domains. The results convincingly show that a system based on a few core linguistic principles from the UD formalism can achieve competitive performance. The cross-domain evaluation, in particular, provides valuable evidence that such a system is less sensitive to domain shift than a data-driven transfer model, making it a highly reliable baseline for low-resource or out-of-domain parsing scenarios.
2.  **A novel combination of a two-step decoding algorithm and personalized PageRank for unsupervised parsing.** While building on prior work in rule-aided parsing, the paper introduces a novel and effective method. The two-step decoding algorithm is a direct and clever operationalization of the UD principle that distinguishes content and function words. The use of *personalized* PageRank to bootstrap the identification of the main predicate is a simple but impactful refinement that improves the quality of the syntactic backbone produced in the first decoding step.
3.  **The development of a nearly parameter-free, language-agnostic parsing system.** The parser relies on a minimal set of universal rules and heuristics. A notable feature is its ability to estimate a key typological parameter—adposition head direction—directly from the test data using a simple bigram frequency count. This makes the system easily applicable to any language with POS-tagged text without requiring language-specific knowledge or parameter tuning.

### Strengths
1.  **Extensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The parser is evaluated on a diverse set of 32 languages, which provides strong support for the universality of its underlying principles. The inclusion of experiments with both gold and predicted POS tags offers a realistic assessment of its performance. The most compelling aspect of the evaluation is the detailed cross-domain analysis (Section 6.3). The finding that UDP exhibits lower variance in performance across domains than the supervised MSD parser (average standard deviation of 2.5 vs. 4.5 on gold POS) is a significant result, confirming that linguistically-motivated systems can offer greater stability than data-driven ones.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Clarity:** The proposed method is straightforward, well-motivated, and clearly described. The components—the PageRank setup, the head rules (Table 1), and the decoding algorithm (Figure 1)—are easy to understand and implement. This clarity and simplicity make UDP an excellent candidate for a baseline in future research on unsupervised, cross-lingual, or low-resource parsing, as it is highly reproducible.
3.  **Strong Performance for a Training-Free System:** Despite its lack of training, UDP achieves an average Unlabeled Attachment Score (UAS) that is only 6.4 points lower than a competitive multi-source delexicalized transfer parser on gold POS tags. It even outperforms this strong supervised baseline on one language (Hindi). Furthermore, the analysis of error propagation shows that UDP is less sensitive to POS tagging errors than the MSD parser, which is a crucial advantage in realistic, low-resource settings where high-quality taggers are unavailable.
4.  **Effective Use of Formalism Constraints:** The system's design is deeply integrated with the principles of the Universal Dependencies formalism. The strict separation of content and function words in the two-step decoding process directly enforces the UD constraint that function words should be dependents of content words. This demonstrates how exploiting the structural constraints of a linguistic formalism can effectively compensate for the absence of training data.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** The core ideas of using POS-based head-attachment rules and employing graph centrality algorithms like PageRank for syntactic prominence are not new and build heavily on prior work, such as Søgaard (2012b). The paper's novelty lies in the specific combination and refinement of these components (e.g., personalized PageRank, two-step decoding). While the resulting system is effective, the conceptual contribution could be viewed as an incremental improvement over existing rule-aided parsing techniques rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.
2.  **Dependence on English Development Data for Rule Engineering:** The paper states that the head rules in Table 1 and the fixed attachment directions for several function word classes (e.g., AUX, DET) were determined using the English development set. This creates a potential conflict with the claim of being a "training-free" system and raises concerns about an English-centric bias. While the strong performance across 32 languages mitigates this concern to some extent, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the rule development process. For example, it is unclear which rules were tested and discarded, or how performance might differ if a typologically different language had been used for development.
3.  **Lack of Clarity on Tie-Breaking and Back-off Impact:** The decoding algorithm (Figure 1) selects heads by minimizing linear distance. The paper does not specify how ties are handled when a dependent is equidistant from two or more valid heads, a detail important for full reproducibility. More critically, the algorithm includes a back-off strategy (dropping first the head-rule constraint, then the directionality constraint) to ensure a tree is always formed. However, the impact of this mechanism is not analyzed. An analysis of how often this back-off is triggered across different languages would provide valuable insight into the coverage of the primary rules and the overall robustness of the decoding strategy.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the development process for the head rules (Table 1) and fixed function-word directions using the English dev set? Specifically, could you discuss whether alternative rule sets were considered and how the final set was chosen? Does this process introduce a potential bias towards SVO languages, and have you considered an alternative development process, for example, by deriving rules from the official UD documentation or using a non-English language?
2.  The decoding algorithm relies on finding the closest potential head. How are ties broken when a dependent is equidistant from multiple valid heads? Additionally, could you provide some analysis on the frequency of the back-off mechanism being triggered during decoding? Understanding how often the system has to fall back on proximity alone would help clarify the effective coverage of the syntactic rules.
