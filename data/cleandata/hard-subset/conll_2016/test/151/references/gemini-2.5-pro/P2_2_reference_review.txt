### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a rule-based dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The system combines a small set of hand-crafted, POS-based head-selection rules with a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify the main predicate and rank content words for attachment. A key feature is a two-step decoding process that first builds a skeleton of content words and then attaches function words as leaves, directly enforcing a core constraint of the UD formalism. The system also dynamically estimates adposition directionality (pre- vs. post-positional) from the test set.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive cross-lingual and cross-domain empirical evaluation.** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of a simple, linguistically-motivated parser across an impressive 32 languages. More importantly, the detailed cross-domain analysis provides strong evidence that this rule-based system is significantly more robust to domain shift than a competitive data-driven transfer learning baseline. This is a valuable finding for the community, especially for parsing in real-world, multi-domain scenarios.
2.  **An elegant and effective unsupervised parsing method.** While the individual components are not entirely new, their combination and adaptation to the UD formalism is novel and effective. The two-step decoding algorithm is a simple and clever way to enforce the function-word-as-leaf constraint, and the use of personalized PageRank to anchor the parse on a likely predicate is a well-motivated heuristic. The resulting system serves as a very strong and difficult-to-beat baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.

### Strengths

1.  **Extensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The scale of the evaluation is a major strength. Testing on 32 languages from UD 1.2 provides a robust measure of the method's cross-lingual applicability. The comparison against a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) is ambitious and provides a clear benchmark against a strong, standard transfer-learning approach. The results, showing UDPG is only 6.4 UAS points behind MSDG on average, are impressive for a system without training.
2.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a standout feature of the paper. By curating domain-specific splits and performing a detailed analysis, the authors convincingly show that their system (UDP) has a much lower variance in performance across domains than the data-driven MSD parser (e.g., average std. dev. of 2.5 vs 4.5 for gold POS). This confirms earlier findings about the stability of rule-based systems and provides strong quantitative evidence for it in a modern, large-scale setting.
3.  **Methodological Simplicity and Interpretability:** The proposed parser is straightforward and its components are well-motivated by linguistic principles and the UD specifications. The rules are explicit, and the decoding algorithm is easy to follow. This transparency makes the system's behavior predictable and its errors (e.g., inability to parse relative clauses) easy to diagnose, which is a significant advantage over more opaque, data-driven models.
4.  **Thorough Analysis:** The paper includes excellent analysis to support its claims. The ablation study in Section 6.1 clearly isolates and quantifies the contribution of the PageRank component (+9 UAS points), strengthening the paper's claims about its method. The breakdown by POS and the discussion of root attachment accuracy are honest and insightful.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The "Without Training" Claim is Overstated:** The paper's central claim of being "training-free" is not entirely accurate. The system relies on several components that were tuned or specified using the English development set. This includes the core head-dependency rules in Table 1, the PageRank teleport probability, and, most critically, the fixed attachment directions for function words like AUX, DET, and SCONJ (Section 3.2). These fixed directions represent an English-centric bias hard-coded into the system, which may not hold for all languages. While the system does not train on large labeled treebanks in a conventional sense, this reliance on development data for crucial design choices is a form of supervision that should be more explicitly acknowledged.
2.  **Limited and Rigid Rule Set:** The parser's strength—simplicity—is also a weakness. The small, fixed set of head rules is unable to capture many common syntactic phenomena. The authors rightly note that their rules prevent the system from parsing relative clauses (as NOUN cannot be a head of VERB). This rigidity places a hard ceiling on the parser's potential performance and means it will systematically fail on valid constructions not covered by its grammar. While effective as a baseline, this limits its applicability for generating high-quality parses.
3.  **Deterministic Nature based on POS Sequences:** As noted in Section 4.2, "Any 10-token sentence with the POS labels shown in Table 2 would yield the same dependency tree." This highlights a limitation: the parser is completely insensitive to lexical information, word order (beyond linear proximity for tie-breaking), and other contextual clues. While this is a feature of delexicalized approaches, it means the parser cannot distinguish between structurally ambiguous sentences that have the same POS sequence, a common challenge in parsing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The fixed head directions for function words (e.g., AUX, DET are right-attaching) were determined on English data and seem to introduce a language-specific bias. You demonstrate a clever on-the-fly estimation method for adposition directionality. Could a similar data-driven heuristic be applied to other function words to make the parser more genuinely language-agnostic and less reliant on English-derived rules?
2.  The hand-crafted rule set is a core component but also a key limitation. Have you considered any methods for semi-automatically expanding this rule set, perhaps by using typological databases (like WALS) or by inducing rules from unlabeled data, to improve coverage without requiring manually annotated treebanks?
3.  How sensitive is the overall performance to the specific rules chosen in Table 1? For instance, what is the impact on non-English languages if the rules had been developed using a typologically different language, such as Hindi or Basque, instead of English? This would help clarify the extent of the English-based development bias.
