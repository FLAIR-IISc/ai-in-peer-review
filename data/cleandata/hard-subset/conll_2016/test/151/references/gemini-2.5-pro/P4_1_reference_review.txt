### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised, rule-based dependency parser designed for the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The system requires no training data. Its core is a small set of POS-based head-attachment rules that are consistent with UD principles. The parsing process involves two main steps: first, it uses Personalized PageRank to rank "content words", giving a higher initial weight to the likely main predicate. Second, a two-step decoding algorithm attaches content words based on this ranking to form a syntactic backbone, and then attaches "function words" as leaves to this structure. This explicitly enforces the UD constraint that function words are dependents of content words. Head direction for adpositions is estimated dynamically from the test data itself, while other function word directions are fixed. The method is evaluated on 32 languages across three POS-tagging scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive) and on several domains for a subset of languages. Results show it is a competitive unsupervised system that demonstrates greater robustness to POS errors and domain shift than a supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

In my assessment, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation of a simple, training-free UD parser, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.** The primary contribution is not the novelty of the individual components but their effective integration and the extensive evaluation across 32 languages. The analysis of the parser's resilience to both POS tagging errors and domain shifts provides a valuable benchmark and demonstrates the practical utility of such a system as a strong baseline, particularly in low-resource or out-of-domain settings.
2.  **An effective, unsupervised parsing algorithm that explicitly encodes core principles of the Universal Dependencies formalism.** The system is designed around UD conventions, most notably the constraint that function words should be leaves. The two-step decoding algorithm is a simple and clear mechanism for enforcing this constraint, which contrasts with data-driven models that must learn such structural properties implicitly.
3.  **A novel application of Personalized PageRank for identifying the main predicate in an unsupervised parser.** While PageRank has been used in prior work for scoring word importance, the use of personalization to bias the ranking towards the sentence's likely main predicate (the first verb or content word) is a simple but effective heuristic that demonstrably improves parsing accuracy.

### Strengths

1.  **Extensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper's evaluation is a key strength. Testing on 32 languages from UD provides broad coverage. Crucially, the authors evaluate not just on gold POS tags but also on predicted tags and a naive content/function tagset, offering a realistic assessment of the system's performance under practical conditions. The cross-domain evaluation (Tables 6 and 7) is particularly compelling, providing strong quantitative evidence (lower standard deviation of UAS scores) that the rule-based system is more stable across domains than the supervised transfer baseline. This is a significant and valuable finding.
2.  **Clarity and Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is straightforward, well-motivated, and clearly described. Each component—the head rules, the PageRank ranking, and the two-step decoding—is easy to understand and implement. This simplicity makes the system an excellent and reproducible baseline for future work on unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing. The on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality is a clever feature that enhances its language independence.
3.  **Strong Performance for a Training-Free System:** For a system that uses no training data, the results are highly competitive. The average performance (UDPG) is only 6.4 UAS points below a strong supervised multi-source delexicalized system (MSDG), and it even outperforms this baseline on Hindi. This shows that a small set of well-chosen, formalism-aware rules can approximate the performance of more complex data-driven methods.
4.  **Insightful Analysis and Ablation:** The paper provides a thorough analysis of its results. The error propagation calculation in Section 5.3 quantifies the system's relative robustness to POS errors compared to the baseline. Furthermore, the ablation study in Section 6.1 effectively isolates and quantifies the contributions of the PageRank component (an improvement of ~9 UAS points) and the two-step decoding logic, justifying the key design decisions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** The core techniques employed are not new. The work is an extension of previous research on rule-aided unsupervised parsing, particularly Søgaard (2012b). The main novelties are the adaptation of the rules to UD, the personalization of PageRank, and the two-step decoding process. While the combination is effective, the paper could better contextualize its contributions against the broader history of rule-based parsing to more sharply define its conceptual advance.
2.  **Reliance on English Development Data for Parameter Tuning:** The paper states that several key parameters were tuned on the English development set. This includes the PageRank teleport probability, the specific set of head rules in Table 1, and the fixed attachment directions for all function words except adpositions (e.g., AUX and DET are always right-attaching). This reliance on English potentially compromises the claim of being a truly language-agnostic system and may introduce an English-centric bias. The paper would be stronger if it included an analysis of the system's sensitivity to these hard-coded choices or discussed methods for estimating them without a development set.
3.  **Potential for Overly Simplistic Rules:** The set of head rules is minimal, which is a design choice for generality. However, as the authors note, this prevents the model from capturing important syntactic phenomena like relative clauses (due to the exclusion of a NOUN → VERB rule). This simplification likely accounts for a significant portion of the performance gap with the supervised system. While a complete grammar is not the goal, a discussion of the trade-offs made in rule selection and the challenges in expanding the rule set in a cross-linguistically valid manner would be beneficial. The very low UAS for PUNCT (21%) and CONJ (38%) further indicates that key structural challenges remain unaddressed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The PageRank personalization heuristic, which boosts the "first verb or the first content word," appears successful even in verb-final languages. Could you elaborate on why this simple, linear-order-based heuristic is effective across typologically diverse languages? Did you experiment with alternative, more structurally-aware heuristics for identifying the main predicate?
2.  The fixed attachment directions for function words (e.g., DET, AUX) were tuned on English. How does performance change for other languages if these directions are altered (e.g., allowing left-attachment for DET)? An analysis of this sensitivity would help clarify how critical these English-tuned parameters are to the overall results.
3.  You mention that the NOUN → VERB head rule was excluded during development because it made verb-noun relations "less conclusive." Could you quantify the performance impact of including this rule, for example on the English development set? Understanding the specific trade-off (e.g., gains in relative clause attachment vs. losses elsewhere) would provide valuable insight into the challenges of designing a general-purpose rule set.
