### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The system requires no training data and instead relies on a small set of universal, linguistically-motivated head-attachment rules derived from the UD formalism itself. The core of the method involves using a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank content words (biasing towards the main predicate) and a two-step decoding process that first builds a skeleton of content words and then attaches function words as leaves. The system is evaluated extensively on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks under gold and predicted POS tag settings, and includes a detailed analysis of its robustness to domain shift.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A strong, simple, and transparent unsupervised baseline for UD parsing.** The paper demonstrates that by systematically exploiting the explicit constraints of the UD formalism (e.g., function words are dependents), one can build a parser that is surprisingly effective. Its performance, while not state-of-the-art, is remarkably close to a competitive multi-source delexicalized transfer system, making it an excellent and easy-to-reimplement benchmark for future work in cross-lingual and unsupervised parsing.

2.  **An extensive and insightful cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation.** The evaluation across 32 languages is thorough. More importantly, the analysis of the parser's robustness to POS tagging errors and, especially, to domain changes provides significant insights. The finding that this rule-based system is more stable across domains than its data-driven counterpart is a valuable empirical result.

3.  **A novel and well-motivated combination of existing techniques for the UD formalism.** The paper cleverly adapts known methods to the specific constraints of UD. The use of *personalized* PageRank to boost the score of the likely sentence root and the two-step decoding algorithm to enforce the leaf-status of function words are elegant design choices that directly map UD principles into a parsing algorithm.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Effectiveness Despite Simplicity:** The system's architecture is commendably simple, relying on a handful of POS-based rules (Table 1) and a PageRank-based ranking. Despite this, it achieves an average UAS of 57.5 on gold POS tags across 32 languages, which is only 6.4 points behind a strong supervised transfer baseline (MSDG). This is an impressive result for a system without any training, demonstrating the power of leveraging the inherent structure of the annotation scheme.

2.  **Thorough and Realistic Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their system not only on gold POS tags but also on predicted tags, which is a more realistic scenario for low-resource languages. The error propagation analysis (Sec 5.3) is insightful, showing that UDP is less sensitive to POS errors than the supervised transfer system. This suggests that the system's simple, high-level rules are less brittle than a delexicalized model that might overfit to POS sequence statistics.

3.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation (Sec 6.3, Tables 6 & 7) is a major strength of the paper. The analysis clearly shows that UDP exhibits lower variance (i.e., higher stability) across different domains compared to the MSD baseline. This empirically validates the hypothesis that parsers based on general linguistic principles are less susceptible to the sampling biases of training data, a finding of significant practical importance.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, and the proposed method is described with excellent clarity. The inclusion of pseudocode for the decoding algorithm (Fig. 1) and a concrete run-through example (Sec. 4) makes the system's mechanics easy to follow and reproduce. This transparency is a significant scientific contribution in itself.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The "No Training" Claim is Slightly Misleading:** While the parser does not use training data in the traditional sense, its core rules (Table 1) and some fixed-directionality assumptions (e.g., for AUX, DET) were manually developed and tuned using the English UD development set. The authors are transparent about this, but it constitutes a form of expert knowledge engineering and indirect supervision on one specific language. This weakens the claim of being a completely parameter-free, language-agnostic method, as the rules may be subtly biased towards English-like structures.

2.  **Limited Scope of Linguistic Phenomena:** The rule set is minimal and, as the authors acknowledge, cannot handle several complex syntactic phenomena. The inability to model relative clauses (by disallowing NOUN → VERB attachments) is a significant limitation. The extremely low UAS scores for punctuation (21%) and conjunctions (38%) further highlight that the system struggles with coordination and other structures not covered by the simple head-dependent rules. While effective as a baseline, its ceiling for overall parsing quality is inherently low.

3.  **Performance Gap Remains Significant:** Although the system is praised for being competitive, it is still outperformed by the supervised delexicalized parser on 31 out of 32 languages on gold POS tags, often by a substantial margin (e.g., >10 UAS points for Bulgarian, Croatian, Danish, French). This suggests that while leveraging formalism constraints is powerful, statistical patterns learned from even delexicalized data provide a crucial advantage that this system cannot capture. The paper convincingly establishes UDP as a strong *unsupervised* baseline, but it does not challenge the overall superiority of supervised transfer methods.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the development on the English data: Could you elaborate on the process used to derive the head rules in Table 1? Was this a purely manual, intuition-driven process, or was there some form of automated search or rule-mining involved? How confident are you that a different development language (e.g., a verb-final one) would have produced the same set of universal rules?

2.  The personalization factor for PageRank was set to 5 for the main predicate. How sensitive is the system's performance to this specific value? What is the performance impact of removing personalization entirely (i.e., using a uniform personalization vector)?

3.  The multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) was trained on 20k sentences sampled from the UD training sets. Since UD treebanks vary greatly in size, could this fixed sample size disadvantage the MSD baseline, especially for language families that are well-represented in UD? Have you experimented with using all available training data (excluding the target language) to see if the performance gap between MSD and UDP widens?
