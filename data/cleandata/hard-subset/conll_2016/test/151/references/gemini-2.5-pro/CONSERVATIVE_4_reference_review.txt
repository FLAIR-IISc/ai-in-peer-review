### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The method is rule-based, leveraging a small set of hand-crafted head-selection rules derived from UD principles. Its core mechanism involves a two-step decoding process: first, it uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to determine an attachment order for content words; second, it attaches function words, ensuring they remain leaf nodes as per UD guidelines. The system is evaluated on 32 languages from UD v1.2 against a rule-based baseline and a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). The authors claim the system is competitive with the supervised transfer baseline and more robust to domain shifts and POS tagging errors.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Simple and Reproducible Unsupervised Baseline:** The paper provides a clearly described, deterministic, and simple parser that can serve as a valuable and strong baseline for future work in cross-lingual and unsupervised parsing. Its performance, while not state-of-the-art, is surprisingly effective given its simplicity.

2.  **Thorough Cross-Domain and Robustness Analysis:** The most significant contribution is the empirical demonstration in Section 6.3 that this simple, rule-based system exhibits greater stability across different domains compared to a data-driven transfer model. The analysis, supported by lower standard deviations in performance, provides solid evidence for the robustness of non-parametric approaches in domain adaptation scenarios.

### Strengths

1.  **Extensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors are to be commended for the breadth of their evaluation. Testing on 32 languages provides a comprehensive view of the method's cross-lingual applicability. The comparison against a strong, established baseline (multi-source delexicalized transfer) is appropriate and allows for a realistic assessment of the system's capabilities. Furthermore, evaluating under gold POS, predicted POS, and a naive POS setting provides a clear picture of the system's sensitivity to input quality.

2.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain experiments in Section 6.3 are a key strength. The finding that UDP has a lower variance in performance across domains than the MSD baseline (average standard deviation of 2.5 vs. 4.5 on gold POS) is a compelling result. This empirically supports the long-held hypothesis that less parameterized, rule-based systems can be more robust than data-driven models, which may overfit to source data characteristics.

3.  **Methodological Transparency:** The proposed method is straightforward and easy to understand. The small set of rules, the PageRank heuristic, and the two-step decoding algorithm are all clearly explained. This transparency makes the system's behavior predictable and its errors analyzable, which is a valuable characteristic for a baseline model. The authors' plan to release the code further enhances its value to the research community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty and Ad-hoc Design Choices:** The core components of the system are largely adaptations of existing ideas. The use of head-attachment rules and PageRank for unsupervised parsing has been explored before (e.g., Søgaard, 2012b). The primary novelties claimed are the "personalization" of PageRank and the two-step decoding. However, these are implemented as simple, and at times arbitrary, heuristics. For example, the 5x weight for the "first verb" as the main predicate seems tailored for SVO languages and its effectiveness across typologically diverse languages is not sufficiently justified.

2.  **Overstated Performance Claims and Misleading Framing:** The paper claims its performance is "remarkably close" to the supervised MSDG system, but an average UAS gap of 6.4 points is significant in dependency parsing. On 31 out of 32 languages, the supervised transfer system performs better, often by a large margin (e.g., >10 UAS on Bulgarian, Croatian, Danish, etc.). While the results are impressive for a zero-resource system, framing it as nearly competitive with a strong supervised baseline is an overstatement. The system's primary value is as a robust baseline, not as a direct competitor in standard settings.

3.  **Generalization Issues and Hidden Language-Specific Tuning:** The claim that the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters" is questionable.
    *   Several function word attachment directions (AUX, DET, SCONJ, etc.) were fixed based on development on the *English* treebank (Section 3.2). This constitutes a clear English-centric bias. It is unlikely that these fixed directions are optimal for all 32 languages, which exhibit significant word order variation.
    *   The head rules in Table 1 were also selected based on the English development set. For instance, the exclusion of a `NOUN -> VERB` rule (to handle relative clauses) because it made relations "less conclusive" on English is a strong, language-specific heuristic that cripples the parser's ability to handle a fundamental syntactic construction across many languages.
    *   Estimating adposition direction on the test data (Section 3.2) is a minor form of data peeking. While the authors argue it requires few sentences, this is still a departure from a truly "zero-resource" setup and relies on the test set being large and representative enough for this heuristic to be stable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more justification for the fixed attachment directions for function words like AUX, DET, and CONJ? Given the typological diversity of the 32 languages tested, it seems improbable that these English-tuned heuristics generalize well. Have you analyzed the error patterns for languages where these assumptions are incorrect (e.g., post-posed determiners)?

2.  The personalization of PageRank by boosting the "first verb" seems biased towards verb-medial languages. While you note the system works for verb-final Hindi, could you provide a more detailed analysis of how this heuristic interacts with language typology? Does it disproportionately fail on VSO or V-final languages compared to SVO languages?

3.  The decision to exclude a `NOUN -> VERB` rule to prevent the parsing of relative clauses is a major structural limitation. Could you quantify the performance drop on the English dev set that led to this decision? What is the performance of the full system across all languages if this rule *is* included? This would clarify the trade-off between precision on some structures and the complete inability to capture others.
