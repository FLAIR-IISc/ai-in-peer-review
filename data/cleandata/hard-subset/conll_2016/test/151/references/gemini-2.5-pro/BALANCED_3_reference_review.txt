**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies on a small set of linguistically-motivated rules derived from Universal Dependencies (UD) principles. The core of the method involves three steps: (1) building a sentence graph based on potential head-dependent relations between POS tags; (2) using a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank content words, biasing the ranking towards the likely main predicate; and (3) a two-step decoding algorithm that first attaches higher-ranked content words and then attaches all function words, ensuring the latter are leaf nodes as per UD guidelines. The parser is evaluated extensively on 32 languages, demonstrating competitive performance against a strong supervised cross-lingual transfer system and superior robustness to domain shift.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **A simple, effective, and fully unsupervised parser for UD.** The primary contribution is the demonstration that a carefully designed combination of simple, POS-based rules and a graph-ranking algorithm can achieve surprisingly strong results across a wide range of languages without any training data. This system provides a new, strong, and easily reproducible baseline for unsupervised and low-resource parsing.
2.  **A novel combination of personalized PageRank and two-step decoding for UD parsing.** While the individual components have precedents, their synthesis is novel and effective. The paper shows how personalizing PageRank towards the main predicate improves parsing and how a deterministic, two-step decoding algorithm can elegantly enforce key structural constraints of the UD formalism.
3.  **An extensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the robustness of the rule-based approach.** The paper provides a thorough evaluation across 32 languages and multiple POS quality scenarios. The detailed cross-domain analysis is a key contribution, providing compelling evidence that this simple rule-based system is significantly more stable across domains than a strong supervised transfer system.

**Strengths**

1.  **Simplicity and Strong Performance:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. It relies on a small, interpretable set of rules (Table 1) and a standard algorithm, yet it achieves impressive results. On average, across 32 languages, it performs within 6.4 UAS points of a powerful multi-source supervised transfer system, which is a remarkable result for a system that uses no training data. This establishes UDP as a highly competitive baseline.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. Testing on 32 languages demonstrates the method's wide cross-lingual applicability. Including experiments with predicted POS tags provides a realistic assessment of performance and error propagation. The cross-domain analysis (Tables 6 & 7) is particularly compelling, providing strong quantitative evidence that the rule-based approach is more robust to domain shifts than the data-driven delexicalized parser. This is a valuable and important finding for the community.
3.  **Excellent Analysis and Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The method is described clearly, and the detailed walkthrough example in Section 4 is instrumental in understanding the parser's mechanics. The analysis in Section 6 is insightful; in particular, the ablation study (6.1) effectively disentangles the performance gains from the decoding algorithm and the PageRank ordering, while the POS-level breakdown (6.2) offers a transparent look at the parser's specific capabilities and limitations.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the overall system is novel and effective, its core ideas build heavily on prior work. The use of hand-crafted POS-based rules is a classic parsing technique, and using PageRank for dependency parsing was proposed by Søgaard (2012b). The paper's main contribution lies in the clever *adaptation and synthesis* of these ideas for the UD framework. The submission could be strengthened by a more direct comparison to Søgaard (2012b) to better contextualize its novelty. For example, what is the empirical impact of just adding personalization to that system's graph construction and ranking method?
2.  **Potential Anglocentric Bias in Rule Development:** The paper states that the crucial head rules (Table 1) and fixed directionality parameters were developed using the English treebank. While the strong performance on other languages suggests the rules are quite general, this introduces a potential bias. For instance, the exclusion of the `NOUN -> VERB` rule to handle English relative clauses might be suboptimal for other language families. The paper would benefit from a discussion on the sensitivity of the system to this specific rule set and how one might adapt it for typologically different languages without a development set.
3.  **Limited Comparison Baselines:** The primary comparison is against a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD), which is a strong and relevant baseline for cross-lingual transfer. However, the paper positions itself within unsupervised dependency parsing. A comparison to classic data-driven unsupervised models like the Dependency Model with Valence (DMV), even on a subset of languages, would have provided a more complete picture of where UDP stands. The authors dismiss DMV by citing a secondary source, but a direct comparison on the UD datasets would have been more conclusive.
4.  **The "Naive POS" Scenario is Unrealistic:** The UDPN evaluation, where function words are defined as the 100 most frequent word types, is an interesting stress test but does not reflect a realistic low-resource scenario. A more practical setting might involve projecting POS tags from a related language or using a universal tagger. While the current results show that UDP degrades, the practical implications of this specific setup are unclear.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you further clarify the differences between your PageRank setup (specifically graph construction) and the one used in Søgaard (2012b)? What is the empirical performance gain specifically from using personalization—i.e., comparing your system with standard PageRank versus personalized PageRank?
2.  The `NOUN -> VERB` rule was excluded during development on English. Have you experimented with including this rule and observing its impact, particularly on languages that might benefit from it (e.g., those with frequent post-nominal modifiers)? Could this choice account for part of the performance gap with the supervised model?
3.  The on-the-fly estimation of ADP directionality is a clever language-agnostic feature. You note that fixed directions for other function words (like DET) sometimes fail (e.g., in Basque). Have you considered extending this estimation technique to other function word categories to make the system even more language-independent? What challenges would you anticipate?
