### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces UDP, a simple, rule-based, and completely unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method's core idea is to directly exploit the structural constraints and cross-linguistic regularities of the UD formalism. It uses a small set of POS-based attachment rules to build a graph over the sentence's content words, runs personalized PageRank to identify the most central words, and then attaches words in a two-step decoding process. This process first builds a skeleton using the ranked content words and then attaches function words as leaves, adhering to a key UD principle. The system is language-agnostic, with the single parameter of adposition directionality being estimated on the fly from the test data. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation on 32 languages under different POS tagging conditions (gold, predicted, and naive) and a detailed cross-domain analysis.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A strong, simple, and truly unsupervised baseline for UD parsing.** The paper presents an elegant and easily implementable method that requires no training data, yet achieves surprisingly competitive performance against a strong, multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. Its effectiveness makes it an excellent benchmark for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.
2.  **A practical demonstration of the power and consistency of the Universal Dependencies framework.** The parser's success validates the design principles of UD. It shows that the framework's constraints (e.g., the treatment of function words) and consistent POS tagging scheme are sufficiently powerful to be directly operationalized into a functional parser for a wide array of typologically diverse languages.
3.  **A thorough cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation that highlights the robustness of rule-based approaches.** The experiments across 32 languages and multiple domains provide convincing evidence that this simple rule-based system is more stable to domain shift than its data-driven counterpart. This is a significant finding that reinforces the value of incorporating linguistic priors into NLP models.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Simplicity and High Performance.** The method is remarkably simple, relying on a small, linguistically-motivated rule set (Table 1), a standard graph centrality algorithm (PageRank), and a clever two-step decoding procedure. Despite this simplicity, it performs exceptionally well for an unsupervised system, achieving an average UAS within 6.4 points of a supervised multi-source delexicalized parser on gold POS tags. This combination of simplicity and effectiveness is a major strength.
2.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Evaluation.** The experimental setup is excellent. Evaluating on 32 UD languages demonstrates broad applicability. Crucially, the authors move beyond the common "gold POS" scenario to test with predicted tags, providing a more realistic measure of performance and an insightful analysis of error propagation. The cross-domain evaluation is particularly compelling, offering strong quantitative evidence (lower standard deviation in UAS scores across domains) that the rule-based UDP is more robust to domain shift than the data-driven MSD model.
3.  **Clever and Language-Agnostic Design.** The parser is almost entirely language-agnostic. The core components—the POS rules, PageRank, and the decoding algorithm—are universal. The one key typological parameter, adposition directionality (pre- vs. post-positions), is cleverly inferred directly from the test data using a simple bigram frequency heuristic. This "plug-and-play" nature makes the parser highly practical for truly low-resource settings where no development data is available.
4.  **Novel Application of Existing Techniques.** While the components themselves (rules, PageRank) are not new, their combination and application to the UD framework are novel and effective. The use of *personalized* PageRank to bias the model towards selecting a verb as the root is a simple but effective heuristic. Furthermore, the two-step decoding algorithm to explicitly enforce the UD constraint that function words are leaves is a key contribution that directly translates a linguistic principle into an algorithmic one.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Syntactic Coverage.** The parser's strength—its simple rule set—is also its primary weakness. The rules in Table 1 are highly restrictive and cannot capture many common and important syntactic phenomena. The authors acknowledge that the system cannot parse relative clauses (due to the explicit exclusion of a `NOUN -> VERB` rule). Other complex structures like coordination, gapping, or long-distance dependencies are likely handled poorly or not at all. It is unclear how this framework could be extended to cover a wider range of syntax without becoming an unwieldy, manually-engineered grammar, thus losing its core simplicity.
2.  **Heavy Reliance on High-Quality POS Tagging.** The system is entirely dependent on the quality of its input POS tags. While the analysis with predicted tags is a good step, the results from the naive `CONTENT`/`FUNCTION` tag setup (UDPN) show that performance collapses dramatically without fine-grained POS information. This undermines the "no training" claim to some extent, as it essentially outsources the learning problem to a POS tagger, which itself typically requires labeled training data. For a truly low-resource language, obtaining a high-accuracy UD-compliant POS tagger is a major challenge in itself.
3.  **Contradictory "No Training Data" Claim.** The paper states in Section 3, "Our approach does not use any training or unlabeled data," but immediately follows this by stating that the English treebank was used during development to select head rules and tune PageRank and directionality parameters. While this is a very limited form of development, it is not strictly "zero-data." A more accurate description would be a system based on a small, manually-specified grammar developed using the English UD treebank as a guide. This should be stated more transparently.
4.  **Potentially Unfair Baseline Comparison.** The MSD baseline is a strong delexicalized model from 2011. While it remains a standard baseline, the field of cross-lingual transfer has advanced considerably, particularly with the advent of large multilingual pretrained models (e.g., mBERT). While such models are data-driven, they can also perform zero-shot dependency parsing. A comparison against a more contemporary zero-shot transfer method would provide a better sense of where this rule-based approach stands in the current landscape.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The heuristic for identifying the main predicate (first verb/content word) is simple and effective. However, have you analyzed its failure cases, particularly in verb-final languages or in sentences with multiple clauses? Could the PageRank scores themselves be used more directly to identify the root, perhaps by forgoing personalization and simply picking the highest-ranked content word, and how would this affect performance?
2.  Estimating adposition directionality on the test set is a clever trick. However, on small or domain-specific test sets, this estimate could be noisy or biased. Have you quantified how many sentences are needed for this heuristic to converge to a stable decision, and what is the performance impact on the first few sentences before a reliable estimate is formed?
3.  The exclusion of the `NOUN -> VERB` rule is a pragmatic choice to simplify head selection but prevents the parsing of relative clauses. Have you considered alternative mechanisms to handle this ambiguity? For instance, could you allow the rule but use a tie-breaking strategy based on PageRank scores or linear distance to decide between a verbal head and a nominal head for another verb?
