### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised, rule-based dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The system requires no training data and instead leverages the inherent structural constraints of the UD formalism. The core of the method involves two main steps: first, it uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify and rank the importance of content words in a sentence, giving special weight to the likely main predicate. Second, it employs a two-step decoding algorithm that first builds a skeleton of the tree by attaching content words based on their rank, and then attaches all function words as leaves, adhering to UD principles. The system uses a small set of universal POS-based attachment rules, and uniquely, it estimates adposition directionality on-the-fly from the test data. The authors conduct an extensive evaluation across 32 languages, comparing their system to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser under various conditions, including gold POS, predicted POS, and a cross-domain setup.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  The development and extensive evaluation of a simple, transparent, and remarkably effective training-free dependency parser. Its strong performance across 32 languages, combined with demonstrated robustness to POS tagging errors and domain shift, establishes it as a powerful and highly valuable baseline for unsupervised and cross-lingual dependency parsing research.
2.  An effective synthesis of rule-based principles and graph-based ranking tailored specifically for the Universal Dependencies formalism. The novel combination of personalized PageRank for root identification and a two-step decoding process to enforce UD's content-function word hierarchy results in a system that is competitive with supervised transfer methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Performance for a Training-Free System:** The most compelling argument for this paper is the high performance of the proposed UDP system. Despite its simplicity and lack of any training, it achieves an average UAS that is only 6.4 points lower than a strong, supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (MSDG) on gold POS tags across 32 languages. It even surpasses the supervised baseline on Hindi. This result is significant as it demonstrates that leveraging the linguistic constraints encoded in a standardized formalism like UD can rival more complex data-driven transfer methods.

2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength of this paper. The authors go far beyond a simple UAS comparison on a few languages.
    *   **Scale:** The evaluation on 32 diverse languages from the UD collection provides strong evidence for the system's generality.
    *   **Robustness to POS quality:** The evaluation under three different POS conditions (gold, predicted, and naive content/function tags) offers a realistic assessment of the parser's utility. The analysis showing that UDP's error propagation from POS tags is lower than the supervised baseline (0.37 vs. 0.46) is a particularly strong piece of evidence for its robustness.
    *   **Cross-Domain Analysis:** The cross-domain evaluation is extensive, using both existing and newly annotated datasets. The finding that UDP exhibits lower variance (i.e., higher stability) across domains compared to the data-driven MSD parser is an important result, confirming that simple, general rule-based systems can be more resilient to domain shift.

3.  **Simplicity, Transparency, and Replicability:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. It relies on a small, interpretable set of POS-based rules (Table 1) and a straightforward decoding algorithm (Figure 1). This transparency is a valuable feature in an era of increasingly opaque models. It makes the system's behavior easy to understand and its errors easy to analyze. The authors' commitment to releasing the code and data further strengthens the paper's contribution by making it an excellent and easily replicable baseline for future work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the system as a whole is effective, its core components are based on established ideas. Rule-based parsing using POS patterns is a classic technique, and the use of PageRank for identifying important words in a sentence for dependency parsing has been explored before (e.g., Søgaard, 2012b). The main novelties are the personalization of PageRank and the two-step decoding. However, the personalization heuristic (boosting the first verb) is quite simple, and the two-step decoding is a direct procedural implementation of a core UD principle. The paper could strengthen its positioning by providing a more detailed comparison of its specific rules and PageRank implementation against prior work in this area.

2.  **Potential English Bias in "Universal" Parameters:** The paper claims the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters," but several key hyperparameters were tuned on the English development set and then applied universally. These include the specific head rules in Table 1, the PageRank teleport probability (0.05), the personalization weight (5x), and the fixed attachment directions for function words like CONJ and DET. While the empirical results are strong across many languages, this tuning on English introduces a potential source of bias. The justification that the system works better on other languages than English is not sufficient to fully dismiss this concern. A sensitivity analysis on these parameters would have made the claims of universality more convincing. For instance, why was a 5x weight chosen for personalization? How does performance change if this is varied?

3.  **Insufficient Ablation and In-depth Error Analysis:** The analysis in Section 6 is good but could be more comprehensive. The ablation in Section 6.1 compares UDP with a version using reading order (UDPNoPR), demonstrating PageRank's contribution. However, a more fine-grained ablation could better isolate the contributions of different components, such as the two-step decoding vs. a single-step process. Furthermore, the discussion of design choices is sometimes brief. For example, the decision to exclude the `NOUN -> VERB` rule is critical, as it prevents the model from handling relative clauses. While a reason is given ("makes the hierarchical relation... less conclusive"), this warrants a deeper discussion, perhaps including an analysis of how many errors this causes and whether alternative heuristics could mitigate the issue without introducing too much ambiguity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The fixed attachment directions for function words (AUX, DET, SCONJ, etc.) were determined on the English development set. Given your successful on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test data, did you experiment with a similar data-driven approach for these other function words? Could you comment on the potential benefits or challenges of doing so?
2.  The personalization of PageRank gives a 5x weight to the estimated main predicate. Could you provide more justification for this specific value? Have you performed a sensitivity analysis to show how UAS is affected by different personalization weights (e.g., 2x, 10x, or no personalization)?
3.  You explicitly excluded the `NOUN -> VERB` head rule to avoid ambiguity, which prevents the parser from correctly analyzing relative clauses. Could you elaborate on the specific ambiguities this rule introduced during development? Can you quantify the performance cost of this decision on a language where relative clauses are frequent, and have you considered alternative strategies to handle them within your framework?
