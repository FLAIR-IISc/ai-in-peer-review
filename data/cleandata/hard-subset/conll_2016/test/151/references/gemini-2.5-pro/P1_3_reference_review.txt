### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised, rule-based dependency parser designed specifically for the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The system requires no training data. Its core idea is to leverage the strong cross-lingual constraints imposed by the UD formalism, particularly the principle that function words (adpositions, determiners, auxiliaries) are always dependents of content words. The method uses a two-step decoding algorithm: it first builds a syntactic backbone by attaching content words based on an ordering determined by personalized PageRank, and then attaches all function words as leaves to this structure. The authors evaluate this system on 32 languages from the UD treebanks, comparing it against a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An impressively effective, simple, and truly training-free parsing system.** The key contribution is demonstrating that a small set of linguistically-motivated rules, derived directly from the UD guidelines and combined with a clever ranking algorithm, can approach the performance of a complex, supervised cross-lingual transfer system. This provides an extremely strong and easy-to-implement baseline for unsupervised and low-resource dependency parsing.
2.  **A rigorous cross-domain evaluation demonstrating the system's robustness.** The authors go beyond a standard multi-language evaluation by analyzing performance across different text domains for several languages. Their finding that the rule-based UDP is significantly more stable (i.e., has lower performance variance) across domains than the data-driven transfer system is a major contribution, offering compelling evidence for the value of this approach in real-world, heterogeneous data scenarios.
3.  **A novel combination of existing techniques tailored for the UD formalism.** While rule-based parsing and PageRank for word importance are not new, the specific implementation here is novel and effective. The use of PageRank *personalization* to bias the model towards a likely main predicate and the two-step decoding algorithm to enforce the content-word/function-word hierarchy are clever adaptations that directly exploit the structure of UD.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Performance for a Zero-Training System:** The parser's performance is the most compelling argument for acceptance. On average, across 32 languages, UDP with gold POS tags is only 6.4 UAS points behind a strong multi-source delexicalized (MSD) parser that is trained on thousands of sentences from multiple source languages. It even outperforms this strong baseline on Hindi. This is an excellent result that challenges the necessity of large training corpora for achieving reasonable parsing performance, especially when a well-designed annotation scheme like UD is available.
2.  **Demonstrable Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a major strength. The results in Table 6 clearly show that UDP has a much lower standard deviation in performance across different domains compared to the MSD baseline (e.g., average std. of 2.5 vs 4.5 on gold POS). This provides strong quantitative evidence that the generalized, formalism-driven rules make the parser less susceptible to the sampling biases of training data, a critical advantage for practical applications.
3.  **Simplicity, Elegance, and Reproducibility:** The proposed method is remarkably simple and transparent. It relies on a small, interpretable set of head-dependency rules (Table 1), a standard algorithm (PageRank), and a straightforward decoding procedure (Figure 1). The authors' clear explanation and the step-by-step example in Section 4 make the entire process easy to understand and reimplement. This clarity is a significant virtue.
4.  **Thorough and Broad Evaluation:** The evaluation is extensive, covering 32 languages with diverse typological properties. Furthermore, the analysis under different POS conditions (gold, predicted, and naive) provides a realistic picture of the system's utility and its sensitivity to tagger quality. The authors are also commended for comparing against a competitive MSD baseline rather than weaker unsupervised ones, which makes the results much more meaningful.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Over-reliance on English for Development and Parameter Tuning:** The paper states that the English treebank was used during development to assess head rules and tune parameters, including the fixed attachment direction for function words like AUX, DET, and SCONJ (Section 3.2). While the adposition direction is cleverly estimated from test data, fixing the direction for other function words based on English seems like a strong, language-specific assumption that undermines the claim of being "completely free of language-specific parameters." For languages with different basic word orders (e.g., VSO or SOV), these fixed directions may be systematically incorrect and limit performance.
2.  **Limited Structural Capacity Due to Simple Rules:** The set of head rules in Table 1 is very restricted. The authors explicitly note that they excluded a NOUN → VERB rule, which consequently makes the system incapable of analyzing relative clauses—a fundamental and frequent syntactic construction. While this may improve precision on other structures, it places a hard ceiling on the parser's recall and its ability to capture more complex sentences. The very low UAS for conjunctions (38%) also suggests a fundamental inability to handle coordination scope, another common and critical structure.
3.  **The "Main Predicate" Heuristic is Potentially Brittle:** The personalization of PageRank relies on a simple heuristic: the first verb (or content word) is assumed to be the main predicate. While this is a reasonable starting point, it will systematically fail in many common cases, such as in sentences with initial subordinate clauses, in questions with subject-auxiliary inversion, or in verb-final languages where the first content word is unlikely to be the main predicate. This may explain why root accuracy, while decent at 69%, is not higher.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The fixed attachment directions for function words (AUX, DET, etc.) were tuned on English. Have you analyzed the impact of this decision on typologically different languages? For instance, in VSO languages like Irish, would a right-attaching AUX rule still be appropriate? Could performance be improved by estimating these directions from test data as well, similar to your method for adpositions?
2.  You mention that the NOUN → VERB head rule was excluded during development because it made the "hierarchical relation between verbs and nouns less conclusive." Could you elaborate on this? What was the specific negative impact on performance that led to its exclusion, and does this trade-off (i.e., losing all relative clauses) seem justified for a general-purpose parser?
3.  The main predicate identification heuristic seems crucial for the personalized PageRank. Did you analyze its accuracy, particularly in multi-clausal sentences or in non-SVO languages? Have you considered alternative heuristics that might be more robust, for example, using a centrality measure other than PageRank or one that does not require personalization?
