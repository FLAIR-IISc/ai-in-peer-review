### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser that operates without any training data. The method relies on a small set of hand-crafted dependency rules derived from Universal Dependencies (UD) principles, using POS tags as its sole input. The core of the method involves a two-step decoding process. First, it uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank content words and attach them, forming a syntactic backbone. Second, it attaches function words as leaves to this backbone. The system is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD treebanks and compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. The authors claim the system is competitive, robust to POS tagging errors, and stable across domains.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  An extensive empirical evaluation of a simple, rule-based unsupervised parsing heuristic across 32 languages and multiple domains.
2.  A compelling demonstration that this simple system, while not state-of-the-art, exhibits greater robustness to domain shift than a standard data-driven transfer parsing model.
3.  The proposed parsing algorithm itself, which combines several heuristics (PageRank, personalization, two-step decoding, on-the-fly directionality estimation) into a single, straightforward system.

### Strengths

1.  **Extensive Evaluation:** The authors are to be commended for the breadth of their evaluation. Testing the system on 32 languages provides a strong empirical foundation for their claims and allows for a comprehensive view of the method's performance across typologically diverse languages.
2.  **Robustness Analysis:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a significant strength of the paper. The finding that the rule-based UDP parser has a lower variance in performance across domains compared to the data-driven MSD parser (Table 6) is an important result. It provides concrete evidence for the oft-claimed but less frequently demonstrated benefit of rule-based systems in terms of generalization and stability, reinforcing earlier findings by Plank and van Noord (2010).
3.  **Simplicity and Baseline Value:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and requires no training data, making it a potentially valuable tool in truly low-resource settings. Its performance, while modest, establishes a non-trivial baseline for future unsupervised and zero-shot parsing research on Universal Dependencies. The fact that it outperforms a simple adjacency baseline by a large margin and approaches a supervised transfer system demonstrates the utility of encoding UD's core principles into a few simple rules.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Questionable Methodological Soundness and "Unsupervised" Claim:** My primary concern is the paper's claim of being "unsupervised" and "free of language-specific parameters." The method relies on several critical components that were explicitly tuned on the English development set:
    *   The core head-dependent rules in Table 1 were selected "to maximize precision on the development set."
    *   The attachment directions for function words like AUX, DET, and SCONJ were "determined on the English dev data."
    *   The personalization factor for PageRank (5x) was chosen during development.
    This constitutes a form of manual feature engineering and hyperparameter tuning on a single, typologically specific language (English). This "hidden supervision" critically undermines the claims of language-independence and introduces a strong bias towards English-like structures. The high performance on many European languages and lower performance on others may well be a direct result of this bias. The paper would be much stronger if this limitation were explicitly acknowledged and its impact analyzed.

2.  **Comparison to Dated and Incomplete Baselines:** The main point of comparison is a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) from McDonald et al. (2011). While a valid baseline, NLP has advanced considerably in the last decade. The paper would be more convincing if it compared its system to more contemporary work in zero-shot or unsupervised parsing. Furthermore, the authors dismiss comparison with the classic DMV model (Klein and Manning, 2004) by citing that a similar system outperformed it. This is insufficient; for a paper focused on unsupervised methods, direct comparison to such a foundational model, or at least a more thorough discussion of its expected performance, is necessary to properly contextualize the results.

3.  **Limited and Brittle Linguistic Coverage:** The rule-based nature of the system, while providing some robustness, also makes it brittle. The hand-crafted rules in Table 1 are very limited. The authors themselves note that they excluded the `NOUN -> VERB` rule, which makes the system incapable of parsing relative clauses—a fundamental syntactic construction. This decision, justified by making relations "less conclusive," highlights the limitations of the heuristic approach and suggests that the system can only model a narrow subset of syntactic phenomena. The very low UAS for punctuation (21%) and conjunctions (38%) further illustrates the parser's struggles with complex sentence structures that fall outside its small rule set.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given that the core parsing rules, several crucial directionality parameters, and the PageRank personalization factor were all tuned on English data, can you elaborate on the justification for describing the system as "unsupervised" and "free of language-specific parameters"? Could you provide an analysis of how performance changes if these parameters are tuned on a typologically distinct language, such as Hindi or Basque, to assess the extent of the English-induced bias?
2.  The exclusion of the `NOUN -> VERB` rule is a significant decision that limits the parser's syntactic competence. Could you please quantify the performance degradation on the English development set that led to this choice? Does this not suggest that the current rule set is over-tuned for precision at the expense of recall for common constructions?
3.  Your primary baseline is a strong but decade-old system. How do you situate your results with respect to more recent advances in unsupervised parsing or zero-shot cross-lingual transfer, particularly those using pre-trained representations (e.g., from mBERT)? While your system has different goals (no training data), understanding where it stands in the broader modern landscape would be valuable.
