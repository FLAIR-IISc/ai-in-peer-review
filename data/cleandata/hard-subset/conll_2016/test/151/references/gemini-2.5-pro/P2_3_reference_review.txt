### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised, rule-based dependency parser designed for the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The system requires no training data and operates solely on part-of-speech (POS) tags. Its core mechanism involves using a small set of linguistically-motivated head-attachment rules derived from UD principles, combined with a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify the sentence's predicate structure. A key design choice is a two-step decoding process that first builds the spine of the tree with content words and then attaches function words, enforcing the UD constraint that function words are leaves. The system is evaluated extensively on 32 languages, showing competitive performance against a supervised delexicalized transfer system and demonstrating superior robustness to domain shift.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The design and extensive evaluation of a simple, effective, and truly training-free baseline for UD parsing.** The system's elegant combination of UD constraints, PageRank, and a clever decoding algorithm produces surprisingly strong results across a wide range of languages. Its performance and, importantly, its robustness to domain change make it an excellent and highly valuable baseline for future work in unsupervised and low-resource parsing.
2.  **A novel and effective method for exploiting the structural constraints of a specific annotation formalism (UD).** The two-step decoding algorithm, which explicitly enforces the leaf-node status of function words, is a simple but powerful idea. This demonstrates how prior linguistic knowledge embedded in an annotation scheme can be directly operationalized in a parser, bypassing the need for data-driven learning for certain phenomena.
3.  **The application of personalized PageRank for main predicate identification.** While using PageRank for word ranking in parsing is not entirely new, the idea of using personalization to inject a strong, linguistically-motivated bias (i.e., boosting the first verb) is a clever and effective heuristic that improves both performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Strong Performance:** The proposed method is remarkably simple, transparent, and easy to implement. It relies on a handful of POS-based rules (Table 1) and a standard graph algorithm. Despite this simplicity, its performance is impressive. On gold POS tags, it achieves an average UAS of 57.5, which is highly competitive and only 6.4 points behind a much more complex multi-source delexicalized supervised system (MSDG at 63.9). For some languages (e.g., Hindi), it even surpasses the supervised baseline. This establishes the system as a very strong "zero-resource" baseline.

2.  **Extensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is a major strength of this paper. The authors test their system on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks, demonstrating its cross-linguistic applicability. Furthermore, they go beyond the standard "gold POS" setting by evaluating on predicted POS tags and a naive content/function tagset. This provides a realistic assessment of the system's utility and shows that its error propagation from POS tagging is actually lower than that of the supervised transfer system.

3.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is excellent and provides compelling evidence for a key advantage of the rule-based approach. The results in Table 6 clearly show that UDP has a much lower standard deviation in performance across different domains compared to the data-driven MSD parser. This confirms the hypothesis that systems based on general linguistic principles are less prone to overfitting to the idiosyncrasies of a specific training domain. This is a significant practical advantage.

4.  **Truly Unsupervised Nature:** The parser is genuinely training-free. It does not rely on any annotated data for parameter tuning, nor does it require parallel corpora or large unlabeled monolingual corpora. The clever on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from the test set itself is a great example of this principle in action, making the system self-contained and applicable in truly low-resource scenarios.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Expressiveness of the Rule Set:** The system's strength—its simplicity—is also its main limitation. The fixed, small set of hand-crafted rules cannot capture the full range of syntactic diversity. The authors acknowledge this by noting the system's inability to parse relative clauses (due to the deliberate exclusion of a `NOUN → VERB` rule). This limitation is also evident in the very low UAS scores for `PUNCT` (21%) and `CONJ` (38%), indicating that complex structures like coordination are not handled well. While the system is a strong baseline, its performance ceiling is inherently limited by its rule-based nature.

2.  **Heavy Reliance on High-Quality and Consistent POS Tagging:** The entire system is built upon the UD POS tagset. Its success hinges on the availability of a reasonably accurate POS tagger and, more subtly, on the consistent application of UD tagging conventions across languages. Any cross-linguistic inconsistencies in distinguishing, for example, auxiliaries from main verbs or adpositions from subordinating conjunctions, could disproportionately affect the parser's performance, as it has no mechanism to learn or adapt to such variations.

3.  **Rule Development Process is Under-specified:** The paper states that the head rules in Table 1 were developed using the English development set. This raises questions about potential bias towards English-like structures. While the results on other languages suggest the rules are fairly general, a more detailed description of the development process would be beneficial. For example, was this a manual, iterative process? What was the rationale for excluding certain rules (like the one for relative clauses), and was this decision based solely on performance on the English dev set? This lack of clarity slightly undermines the claim of full language-independence.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The rules in Table 1 are the core of the parser. Could you elaborate on the process of developing them on the English dev set? Was this process automated or based on linguistic intuition and error analysis? Specifically, regarding the exclusion of the `NOUN → VERB` rule, was this because it introduced too many incorrect attachments, and did you consider adding constraints to make such a rule more precise (e.g., only allowing it for specific verb forms)?

2.  The on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality is a great feature. However, other function words also exhibit cross-linguistic directional preferences (e.g., determiners in Basque, as noted). Did you experiment with applying a similar on-the-fly estimation for other function word categories like `DET` or `SCONJ` instead of using fixed directions determined on English data?

3.  The two-step decoding algorithm is central to enforcing the leaf-status of function words. However, this seems to preclude function words from ever taking dependents, which is not always true in UD (e.g., `case` relations attaching to adpositions in some enhanced dependency representations, or coordinated prepositions). Could you comment on how much of a problem this hard constraint is in practice on the standard UD basic dependencies, and whether you considered a softer version of this constraint?
