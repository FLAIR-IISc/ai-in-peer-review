### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a rule-based, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies. The system requires no training data and relies on a small set of POS-based attachment rules derived from the UD specification. Its core components include: 1) using personalized PageRank to identify and rank the main content words of a sentence, with a bias towards the first verb as the root's dependent; 2) a two-step decoding algorithm that first builds a skeleton of content words and then attaches function words as leaves; and 3) an on-the-fly estimation of adpositional head direction from the test data. The system is evaluated on 32 languages from UD v1.2 against a delexicalized transfer parser, showing competitive results and superior robustness to domain shift and POS tagging errors.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  The development of a simple, transparent, and surprisingly effective rule-based parser that serves as a very strong baseline for unsupervised and cross-lingual dependency parsing in the UD framework.
2.  An extensive empirical evaluation across 32 languages and several domains, which provides a compelling demonstration of the robustness of a linguistically-informed, rule-based system compared to a data-driven transfer model.
3.  The application of personalized PageRank for main predicate identification in a parsing context, which is a novel refinement of prior work.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Interpretability:** The proposed method is conceptually simple, relying on a small set of explicit rules. This makes the parser's decisions transparent and easy to analyze, which is a valuable property often missing in complex data-driven models. Its performance, despite this simplicity, is impressive.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Robustness:** The parser achieves UAS scores that are remarkably close to a competitive supervised multi-source delexicalized parser, and even surpasses it for Hindi. The analysis in Section 6.3 convincingly shows that the system is significantly more robust to domain shifts than the data-driven baseline, as evidenced by the lower standard deviation in performance across different domains. This confirms prior findings about rule-based systems and provides a strong argument for its use as a baseline.

3.  **Broad and Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering 32 languages with diverse typological properties. The inclusion of three different POS tagging scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive) provides a clear picture of the system's sensitivity to tagger quality. The cross-domain analysis, while based on small datasets for some languages, is a welcome addition that strengthens the paper's claims about robustness.

### Weaknesses

Despite its merits, the paper suffers from an accumulation of methodological ambiguities, questionable claims, and presentational flaws that detract from its overall quality.

1.  **The "Training-Free" Claim is Misleading:** The system is described as requiring "no training," but it has been extensively tuned on the English development set. This includes the selection of head rules (Table 1), the tuning of the PageRank teleport probability (0.05) and personalization weight (5x), and the fixing of attachment directions for all function words except adpositions. This is a significant amount of language-specific parameter engineering that undermines the central claim of being a truly universal, zero-resource system. The work would be more accurately framed as a minimal-supervision system heavily biased by English typology.

2.  **Methodological Opacity and Brittle Heuristics:** Several key design choices lack rigorous justification. The PageRank personalization weight ("5 times more weight") and teleport probability are presented as magic numbers. The heuristic for identifying the main predicate ("the first verb or the first content word") is overly simplistic and likely to perform poorly on languages with different word orders (e.g., VSO, V-final), even if the average results are decent. The decision to fix the attachment direction for DET, AUX, etc., based on English is a major flaw for a cross-lingual system, as acknowledged by the poor results for Basque determiners. The back-off strategy in the decoder is also vague, simply stating constraints are dropped without detailing the process or its frequency of use.

3.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Errors:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulous preparation.
    *   The reference list is severely malformed, containing stray question marks (e.g., `Agić and Ljubešić.,? 2015`), inconsistent formatting (`Page et al\.,? \Q1999\E`), and missing publication venues. This is unacceptable for a conference submission.
    *   Footnote 4 makes a claim that appears factually incorrect: "Any 10-token sentence with the POS labels shown in Table 2 would yield the same dependency tree." The decoding algorithm uses linear distance (`γ(n,m)`), which is dependent on word order, not just the POS sequence. A different ordering of the same words and POS tags would lead to different distance calculations and potentially a different tree.
    *   Footnotes 6, 7, and 8 contain anonymized URLs and a promise to release code/data, which is a violation of the anonymity policy for many venues.
    *   There are minor but noticeable typos, such as "presidental bulletin" in Table 7.

4.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper dismisses the classic DMV model by Klein and Manning (2004) on the basis of being outperformed by a *different* system (Søgaard, 2012b). For a paper on unsupervised parsing, a direct comparison to established grammar induction baselines on standard evaluation sets is expected. The adjacency baseline is mentioned but scores are not shown, making it difficult to fully contextualize the reported improvements.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you reframe the contribution of your work in light of the extensive tuning on the English development set? How does the system perform if the "magic numbers" (e.g., personalization weight, teleport probability) and fixed function-word directions are removed or determined via a more principled, language-agnostic method?

2.  Footnote 4 seems to contradict the decoding algorithm's reliance on linear word distance. Could you clarify whether the parser's output is truly independent of word order for a fixed POS sequence? If not, please correct the claim.

3.  Given the failure of the fixed (English-based) directionality for Basque determiners, have you considered applying your on-the-fly direction estimation method (used for adpositions) to other function word categories? This seems like a straightforward way to make the system more robust and less reliant on English-centric assumptions.
