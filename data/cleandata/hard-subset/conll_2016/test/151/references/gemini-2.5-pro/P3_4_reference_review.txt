### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents UDP, a dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that operates without any training data. The method is grounded in a small set of linguistically-motivated, POS-based attachment rules derived from the UD specification itself. Its core innovations are the use of a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify the sentence's predicate and rank content words for attachment, and a two-step decoding process that first builds a content-word skeleton and then attaches function words as leaves, directly enforcing a key UD constraint. The system is evaluated on an impressive 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks, showing competitive performance against a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A highly effective, simple, and parameter-light unsupervised parsing method:** The paper demonstrates that a carefully constructed rule-based system, augmented with a clever ranking mechanism (personalized PageRank), can achieve surprisingly strong performance. The method elegantly exploits the inherent structural constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism, particularly the head-final nature of function-word attachments.
2.  **A comprehensive cross-lingual evaluation and analysis:** The evaluation across 32 languages under various conditions (gold POS, predicted POS, naive POS) provides a robust and convincing demonstration of the method's viability. The results, showing UDP is on average only ~6 UAS points behind a supervised transfer system, are a significant finding for the field of unsupervised parsing.
3.  **Strong empirical evidence for the domain robustness of rule-based systems:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a standout contribution. By curating new test sets and analyzing performance variance, the authors provide compelling evidence that their simpler, less parameterized model is significantly more stable across domain shifts than its data-driven counterpart.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Simplicity:** The proposed method is clean, intuitive, and easy to understand. By combining a few high-precision head-attachment rules (Table 1), a principled ranking of content words via personalized PageRank, and a two-step decoding algorithm, the system translates core UD principles into an effective parser. This simplicity makes it highly replicable and a valuable baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.
2.  **Remarkable Performance for an Unsupervised System:** The results are the paper's strongest asset. Achieving an average of 57.5 UAS with gold POS tags, coming close to a strong supervised transfer baseline (63.9 UAS), is an excellent result for a system that uses no training data. The fact that it even outperforms the baseline on Hindi demonstrates its potential. This challenges the prevailing notion that competitive parsing absolutely requires large training corpora.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Evaluation:** The experimental design is very strong. The authors test on a massive language set, assess the impact of POS tag quality (a critical real-world factor), and perform a detailed error analysis. The cross-domain study is particularly well-executed and its conclusion—that UDP is more robust to domain shift—is well-supported by the lower standard deviation in performance compared to the MSD parser (Table 6). This has important practical implications.
4.  **Novel Use of PageRank Personalization:** While PageRank has been used in parsing before (as cited), the use of a *personalized* vector to inject a strong prior for the main predicate (the first verb) is a simple but clever innovation that, as the authors note, improves root accuracy and speeds up convergence.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The "No Training" Claim Is Not Strictly Accurate:** The system does involve parameter tuning and data-driven decisions that weaken the "training-free" claim. Specifically: (a) The head rules in Table 1 and PageRank parameters were tuned on the English development set. While this is a small amount of data, it is a form of development/tuning. (b) The crucial decision of adposition directionality (pre- vs. post-position) is estimated "on the fly on test data." This means the parser's behavior is dependent on the test set distribution, a practice generally discouraged in system evaluation. While pragmatic for a zero-shot system, it is a form of adaptation to the test set.
2.  **Limited Structural Complexity and Ceiling:** The system's power is derived from a very small, fixed set of rules. As acknowledged by the authors, this prevents it from handling more complex phenomena like relative clauses (because the `NOUN -> VERB` rule was excluded) or complex coordination. While its performance is impressive for what it does, this architectural choice imposes a hard ceiling on its capabilities, which likely explains the remaining performance gap with supervised models.
3.  **Dependency on UD Formalism:** The parser's success is tightly coupled to the specific design principles of Universal Dependencies (e.g., function words are dependents). This is presented as a feature, which is fair, but it is also a limitation. It is unclear how this method would generalize to other dependency schemes (e.g., Stanford Dependencies, where auxiliaries can be heads) without a complete redesign of the rule set. The paper could benefit from a clearer discussion of this dependency.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The exclusion of the `NOUN -> VERB` rule to avoid ambiguity in the verb-noun hierarchy seems critical to the parser's performance. Could you quantify how much performance degraded when this rule was included during development on English? Does its inclusion cause catastrophic errors, or just a minor drop in accuracy? This would help clarify the brittleness of the hand-crafted rule set.
2.  The adposition directionality heuristic is interesting. How does it perform on languages with mixed adposition strategies or on very small test sets where the bigram counts might be noisy? Have you considered a backoff, for instance, defaulting to right-attachment (for prepositions, the most common case cross-lingually) if the test set is smaller than a certain threshold (e.g., < 20 sentences)?
3.  The personalization of PageRank gives a 5x weight to the estimated main predicate. How sensitive is the model to this hyperparameter? For example, what happens if the weight is 2x or 10x? Does the benefit come from simply breaking ties, or is a strong signal necessary?
