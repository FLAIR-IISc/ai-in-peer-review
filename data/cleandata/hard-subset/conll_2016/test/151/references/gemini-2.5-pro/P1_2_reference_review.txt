### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that operates without any training data. The method is rule-based, leveraging the inherent structural constraints of the UD formalism. It works in two main stages: first, it uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank the "content words" (nouns, verbs, etc.) in a sentence. The ranking determines the order of attachment, with higher-ranked words being attached closer to the root. Second, it attaches "function words" (adpositions, determiners, etc.) as leaves to the already-formed content-word skeleton. The parser uses a small, fixed set of POS-based head-dependent rules. Crucially, it makes the system almost language-independent by estimating adposition directionality (pre- vs. post-positional) on the fly from the test data itself. The system is evaluated extensively on 32 languages from UD v1.2 and is shown to be a robust and competitive unsupervised method, particularly in its stability across different domains.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Simple and Effective Unsupervised Parsing Method:** The paper proposes a coherent and elegant method that combines several simple ideas—UD constraints, personalized PageRank, and two-step decoding—into a surprisingly effective unsupervised parser. The on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction is a particularly novel element that enhances its language-agnostic character.
2.  **Extensive Cross-Lingual and Cross-Domain Evaluation:** A major contribution is the rigorous evaluation. The parser is tested on 32 languages under both gold and predicted POS tag settings. More importantly, the detailed cross-domain analysis for several languages provides strong, quantitative evidence that this rule-based system is significantly more robust to domain shift than a strong, data-driven delexicalized transfer parser.
3.  **A Strong, Training-Free Baseline:** The paper establishes a solid and easily reproducible baseline for unsupervised and cross-lingual dependency parsing. While not state-of-the-art compared to supervised systems, it performs remarkably well for a training-free approach and serves as a valuable benchmark for future work in low-resource settings.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Simplicity:** The proposed UDP parser is built on a small set of clear, linguistically-motivated principles derived from the UD guidelines. The use of PageRank personalization to identify the main predicate and the two-step decoding to enforce leaf-node status for function words are clever and effective heuristics. This simplicity makes the system transparent and easy to understand.
2.  **Impressive Performance for an Unsupervised System:** For a system that uses no training data, the results are very strong. Achieving an average UAS within 6.4 points of a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSDG) across 32 languages is a significant achievement. The fact that it outperforms the MSDG system on Hindi demonstrates its potential.
3.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a key strength. The authors show convincingly (Table 6) that UDP has a much lower variance in performance across different domains compared to the data-driven MSD system. This provides strong support for the hypothesis that parsers based on general linguistic principles are less prone to overfitting to training data biases and are more stable, which is a valuable finding for real-world applications.
4.  **Near-Complete Language Independence:** A major advantage of UDP is its minimal reliance on language-specific resources. The head rules are based on universal POS tags, and the primary word-order parameter (adposition direction) is learned from a handful of test sentences. This makes the parser trivially adaptable to any language for which POS tags are available, fulfilling the promise of a truly cross-lingual tool.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Over-reliance on English for Development:** The paper states that head rules were selected and several key parameters (PageRank teleport probability, fixed directionality for AUX, DET, CONJ, etc.) were tuned on the English development set. This undermines the "training-free" claim to a degree. While the system generalizes well, this tuning introduces a potential bias towards English-like structures. The robustness of these fixed directional rules for typologically diverse languages (e.g., making DET right-attaching is problematic for Basque, as noted) is a concern.
2.  **Limited Syntactic Coverage:** The simplicity of the head rules (Table 1) is both a strength and a weakness. The rules are unable to capture more complex syntactic phenomena. As the authors acknowledge, the lack of a NOUN → VERB rule prevents the model from generating relative clauses correctly. The low performance on conjunctions and punctuation further highlights the model's limited structural capacity. This imposes a hard ceiling on its maximum possible accuracy.
3.  **Potentially Understated Comparison to Baselines:** The main comparison is against a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD), which is a strong and appropriate baseline. However, the field of unsupervised dependency parsing also includes grammar induction models like the Dependency Model with Valence (DMV). The authors dismiss DMV by citing an older paper, but a direct comparison on the large UD dataset would have been more conclusive. Furthermore, while UDP is competitive *for an unsupervised system*, the 6.4 UAS average gap with MSD is still substantial, and this nuance could be made clearer.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the development on English data: How sensitive are the fixed directionality rules (for AUX, DET, SCONJ, etc.) to the development language? Have you experimented with tuning these on a typologically different language (e.g., a verb-final one) to see how much the performance changes for other languages?
2.  The heuristic for personalizing PageRank gives a 5x weight to the *first* verb or content word. This seems biased towards SVO or VSO languages. You note that it works well even for verb-final languages like Hindi, which is surprising. Could you elaborate on why this simple heuristic is so robust across different word orders? Did you explore alternatives, such as boosting all verbs in the sentence equally?
3.  The adposition directionality is estimated as a binary choice (pre- or post-positional) for the entire language. Some languages may use both, or employ circumpositions. How does your model handle sentences with minority or mixed adposition strategies, and how much does this simplification hurt performance on languages where this is common?
