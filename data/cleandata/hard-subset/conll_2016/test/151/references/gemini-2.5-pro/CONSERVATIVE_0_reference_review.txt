### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents UDP, a rule-based dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that does not require a training phase. The method relies on a small, pre-defined set of part-of-speech (POS) based attachment rules, inspired by the constraints of the UD formalism. The core of the approach involves a two-step decoding algorithm that first builds a skeleton of content words and then attaches function words as leaves. The parsing order for content words is determined by a personalized PageRank algorithm, which is biased to rank the likely main predicate of the sentence higher. The system is evaluated on 32 languages, demonstrating competitive performance against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, particularly in its robustness to POS tagging errors and domain shift.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An extensive and rigorous empirical evaluation of a simple, heuristic-based parser across a large and diverse set of 32 languages. The analysis across different domains and under varying POS quality conditions provides a valuable benchmark for the field.
2.  A demonstration that a simple, linguistically-motivated method can serve as a surprisingly strong baseline, performing within a reasonable margin of a supervised delexicalized transfer system, while exhibiting greater stability across domains.
3.  The application of personalized PageRank as an effective, simple mechanism for identifying a sentence's predicate and guiding a deterministic parsing algorithm.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Thorough and Extensive Evaluation:** The paper's primary strength is its evaluation. Testing on 32 UD treebanks is comprehensive. More importantly, the analysis extends beyond the standard gold-POS setting to include performance on predicted POS tags and a more challenging naive tagging scheme. This provides a realistic assessment of the system's utility.
2.  **Valuable Cross-Domain Analysis:** The investigation into cross-domain performance (Section 6.3) is a significant highlight. The finding that this simple, rule-based system is more stable across domains than the data-driven MSD parser (as evidenced by lower standard deviation in UAS) is an important result. It reinforces previous findings about the robustness of less parameterized models and provides strong evidence for the utility of UDP as a robust baseline.
3.  **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The authors make a strong choice in comparing their system to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). This is a well-established and competitive method for cross-lingual parsing. Demonstrating that an unsupervised, heuristic-based system can approach the performance of such a baseline and even exceed it in terms of robustness is a compelling result.
4.  **Simplicity and Clarity:** The proposed method is straightforward, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The inclusion of a detailed example run in Section 4 is very effective in illustrating the mechanics of the PageRank and decoding steps. This clarity makes the work easy to understand and reproduce.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The "Without Training" Claim is Misleading:** The central framing of the paper around a "training-free" parser is problematic. The method relies on several components that were tuned using annotated data. Specifically, the paper states that the head rules in Table 1, the PageRank teleport probability, and the attachment directions for five function word categories were all determined using the English development set. This process is a form of parameter tuning and knowledge engineering that leverages labeled data. While it is not conventional training, calling it "without training" is inaccurate and overstates the unsupervised nature of the work. It would be more precise to describe it as a manually engineered parser with parameters tuned on a single language.
2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The approach is an incremental extension of existing work, particularly Søgaard (2012b), which also used PageRank and rule-based decoding. The main novel additions are the use of personalization in PageRank and the two-step decoding to enforce function words as leaves. While effective, these are relatively small modifications to an established framework. The POS-based head rules themselves are standard heuristics derived from linguistic knowledge.
3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Unsupervised Baselines:** While the comparison to the supervised MSD system is valuable, the paper fails to adequately situate its performance within the context of established *unsupervised* parsing methods. The dismissal of the DMV model is based on a citation to older work, and no comparison is made to more recent grammar induction models. Furthermore, the scores for the standard adjacency baseline are mentioned but not included in the main results table. A more thorough comparison to other unsupervised techniques is necessary to properly assess the contribution.
4.  **Questionable Generalizability of Tuned Heuristics:** Several key design choices, such as the fixed left/right attachment for AUX, DET, and CONJ, were made based on English data. The paper provides no analysis of how these English-centric heuristics perform on typologically diverse languages (e.g., verb-final languages where a right-attaching AUX rule might be consistently wrong). While the on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction is a positive step, the reliance on fixed, English-tuned directions for other function words is a methodological weakness that may limit performance on some languages.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Given that core components of the system (head rules, PageRank parameters, function word directionality) were optimized on the English development treebank, could you elaborate on why this should not be considered a form of training or parameter tuning on annotated data? Would it be more accurate to frame the approach as a "low-supervision" or "expert-driven" system?
2.  The attachment directions for several function words were fixed based on English. Have you investigated the impact of these fixed, English-derived heuristics on languages with differing word order typology? For example, how does the fixed right-attachment rule for AUX affect performance on verb-final languages?
3.  The baseline system (BLG) uses an oracle to determine the best backoff attachment direction for each language. A fairer comparison would be to have the baseline use the same on-the-fly heuristic as UDP. Could you report the performance of such a baseline? This would help isolate the performance gains attributable specifically to the PageRank and two-step decoding components.
