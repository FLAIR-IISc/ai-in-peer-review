### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces UDP, a simple yet highly effective unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The parser operates without any training data. Instead, it leverages a small set of linguistically-motivated, POS-based head-dependent rules derived from the UD specification. Its core methodological contributions are a two-step decoding algorithm that distinguishes between content and function words (enforcing UD's constraint that function words are leaves), and the use of personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate and rank content words for attachment ordering. The system is evaluated on 32 languages from UD v1.2, showing competitive performance against a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline, particularly in its robustness to POS tagging errors and domain shift.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Powerful, Training-Free Baseline:** The paper demonstrates that a carefully designed, simple rule-based system can be surprisingly effective for cross-lingual parsing. By leveraging the inherent structural constraints of the UD formalism, UDP serves as an extremely strong and easy-to-replicate baseline for any future work in unsupervised or low-resource dependency parsing. Its performance, being only ~6 UAS points on average behind a supervised transfer system on gold POS tags, challenges the community to justify more complex models.

2.  **Extensive Cross-Lingual and Cross-Domain Evaluation:** The evaluation is a significant contribution in its own right. Testing across 32 languages demonstrates the general applicability of the proposed rules. More importantly, the cross-domain analysis provides compelling evidence that this simple, parameter-light model is significantly more robust to domain shift than a data-driven transfer model, a finding with important practical implications.

3.  **An Elegant Methodological Combination:** The paper effectively combines several simple ideas—POS-based rules, PageRank for centrality, and a two-step decoding process—into a cohesive and well-motivated system. The use of personalization in PageRank to bias towards the main predicate and the on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality are clever additions that make the system completely language-independent.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Effectiveness:** The system's greatest strength is its remarkable performance given its simplicity. It relies on a handful of universal rules (Table 1) and a graph centrality algorithm, yet it achieves an average of 57.5 UAS on gold tags across 32 languages. This demonstrates the power of the UD formalism's design principles and provides a crucial sanity check for more complex, data-hungry models.

2.  **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation (Section 6.3) is excellent and provides one of the paper's most important takeaways. As shown in Table 6, UDP consistently exhibits a lower standard deviation in performance across different domains compared to the multi-source delexicalized (MSD) parser. This confirms prior findings (Plank and van Noord, 2010) on a much larger scale and highlights a key advantage of principled, rule-based systems over data-driven ones that may overfit to source-domain statistics.

3.  **Comprehensive Analysis:** The authors provide a thorough analysis of their system. The ablation study isolating the contribution of PageRank (Section 6.1) is informative, showing it adds a substantial 9 UAS points over a simpler reading-order baseline. The evaluation under different POS conditions (gold, predicted, naive) provides a realistic picture of the parser's performance in practical scenarios and its graceful degradation with noisier input. The breakdown by POS (Section 6.2) offers valuable insights into the system's successes (e.g., adposition attachment) and limitations (e.g., punctuation, relative clauses).

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and the method is described with sufficient detail (Figure 1, Tables 1 & 2) to be easily reimplemented. The decision to make the code and new datasets available is commendable and further strengthens its contribution as a baseline for the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is novel and well-executed, the individual components have been explored before. Rule-based parsers are a classic approach, and the use of PageRank for unsupervised dependency parsing was proposed by Søgaard (2012b), a work the authors build upon. The paper's contribution lies more in the careful adaptation of these ideas to the UD framework and the impressive empirical results than in groundbreaking algorithmic innovation.

2.  **Inherent Ceiling on Performance:** The system's rule-based nature imposes a hard ceiling on its capabilities. The fixed rules in Table 1 cannot capture many complex syntactic phenomena. The authors rightly note the inability to parse relative clauses because the NOUN → VERB rule was excluded. Similarly, resolving complex coordination scope or prepositional phrase attachment ambiguities, which often require lexical or semantic cues, is beyond the scope of this model. This is less a flaw and more an inherent limitation of the paradigm, but it's important to acknowledge that this approach is unlikely to close the remaining gap with fully supervised systems.

3.  **Over-reliance on English for Development:** The authors state that head rules and PageRank parameters were tuned on the English development set. While the results show these rules generalize well, this English-centric development process may introduce subtle biases. For example, the fixed left/right attachment directions for function words like AUX and CONJ might not be optimal for all 32 languages, especially those typologically distant from English. A discussion of how these fixed parameters might affect performance on, for instance, VSO or SOV languages would have strengthened the paper.

4.  **Ambiguity in Baseline Comparison:** The comparison between the main baseline "BLG" and the "UDPNoPR" system is slightly unclear. BLG is described as not necessarily yielding well-formed trees, whereas UDP's decoding ensures a valid tree structure. It is therefore difficult to disentangle how much of the performance gain of UDPNoPR over BLG is due to the superior two-step decoding algorithm versus the simple fact that it produces valid trees. A more direct comparison to a stronger rule-based baseline that also guarantees tree structures would have been more informative.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper notes that most rules and parameters were developed using the English treebank. Have you performed any analysis on the sensitivity of the system to these choices? For example, how does performance change if the fixed attachment directions for function words (AUX, DET, SCONJ, etc.) are reversed or learned from the test data in the same way as adpositions, particularly for typologically diverse languages?

2.  You mention excluding the NOUN → VERB rule because it "makes the hierarchical relation between verbs and nouns less conclusive." This is a key design choice that prevents the parsing of relative clauses. Could you elaborate on the performance degradation observed when this rule was included during development? Is there a path forward for incorporating such rules while using other signals (e.g., PageRank scores, proximity) to disambiguate attachments?

3.  Could you please clarify the key difference in the decoding/tree-construction process between your baseline (BLG) and the UDPNoPR system? The paper states BLG may not produce connected, single-root trees. How much of the 4 UAS point improvement of UDPNoPR over BLG is attributable simply to the constraint of producing a well-formed tree, versus other aspects of the decoding algorithm?
