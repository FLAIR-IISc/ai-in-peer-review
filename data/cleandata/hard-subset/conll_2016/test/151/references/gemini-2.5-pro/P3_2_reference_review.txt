### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and operates based on a small set of universal, linguistically-motivated rules derived from the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The core of the method is a two-step decoding algorithm. First, it constructs a parse tree for content words, using personalized PageRank to determine the attachment order, with the goal of prioritizing the main predicate. Second, it attaches function words as leaves to this content-word backbone, thereby enforcing a key constraint of the UD scheme. The system is evaluated on 32 languages, demonstrating performance that is surprisingly competitive with a strong, supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Simple and Robust Unsupervised Baseline for UD:** The paper presents a transparent, easy-to-implement, and entirely training-free parser that serves as a very strong baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing for UD. Its effectiveness highlights the power of leveraging the inherent constraints of a linguistic formalism.
2.  **Demonstration of Cross-Domain Stability:** The most significant contribution is the empirical finding that this simple, rule-based system is substantially more robust to domain shifts than a powerful, data-driven transfer learning model. This is shown through a rigorous multi-domain evaluation with lower performance variance for UDP, providing compelling evidence for the value of parameter-light, linguistically-informed models in real-world applications.
3.  **A Novel Synthesis of Existing Techniques for UD Parsing:** The work effectively combines personalized PageRank for predicate identification, a two-step decoding process to handle the content/function word distinction, and an on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality. While the components themselves are not entirely new, their integration into a coherent, UD-specific framework is novel and effective.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant Simplicity and Strong Performance:** The proposed method is remarkably simple, relying on a handful of POS-based attachment rules and a standard graph-ranking algorithm. Despite this, it achieves impressive results across a wide range of languages, with an average UAS only 6.4 points behind a supervised multi-source transfer system on gold POS tags. This combination of simplicity and effectiveness is a major strength.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is a clear highlight of the paper. Testing on 32 languages from UD v1.2 provides a broad and convincing assessment of the method's cross-lingual applicability. The analysis across three different POS quality scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive) provides a realistic view of the system's utility and its sensitivity to upstream errors. Furthermore, the detailed error analysis and ablation studies in Section 6 add significant depth to the results.
3.  **Excellent Analysis of Cross-Domain Robustness:** The experiments in Section 6.3 are particularly compelling. The authors go beyond standard test set evaluation by curating and testing on multiple domains for several languages. The finding that UDP exhibits significantly lower performance variance across domains compared to the delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) is a crucial result. It underscores a key practical advantage of rule-based systems over data-driven ones, which are more susceptible to sampling bias.
4.  **Truly Unsupervised and Adaptable Design:** The parser's independence from training data makes it immediately applicable to any language with a POS tagger. The approach for estimating adposition directionality directly from the test data is a clever feature that reduces the need for language-specific parameters and enhances the system's adaptability.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Anglocentric Bias in Design Choices:** Several crucial parameters and rules were tuned or selected based on the English development set. This includes the PageRank personalization weight (5x), the teleport probability, the fixed attachment directions for function words (e.g., DET, AUX), and the set of head rules in Table 1 itself. While the system generalizes surprisingly well, this reliance on English is a conceptual weakness in a paper focused on universal parsing. The failure case noted for Basque determiners highlights this issue. The work would be stronger if these choices were motivated more cross-lingually or if more parameters were estimated from the data, similar to the adposition strategy.
2.  **Limited Discussion of Novelty vs. Prior Work:** While the paper cites Søgaard (2012b), which also uses PageRank for unsupervised parsing, the distinction could be drawn more clearly. The main differences are noted as personalization and two-step decoding. However, the ablation study compares UDP with UDPNoPR (which removes PageRank entirely), rather than a version without personalization. A direct, quantitative comparison isolating the effect of personalization and the UD-specific two-step decoding on top of a more standard PageRank approach would better situate the paper's specific methodological contributions.
3.  **Inability to Model Common Syntactic Phenomena:** The simplicity of the head rules is both a strength and a weakness. The chosen rules explicitly prevent the parser from handling important structures like relative clauses (by disallowing NOUN → VERB attachments) and clausal complements. The parser also performs poorly on coordination and punctuation, which are known challenges but are fundamental to sentence structure. While perfection is not expected from a simple system, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of these structural limitations and potential paths to address them within the proposed framework.
4.  **Minor Lack of Clarity in Baseline Description:** The description of the baseline (BL) in Section 5.1 is slightly ambiguous. It references Table 5 for head rules but then describes using the rules from Table 1. It is also described as not necessarily producing well-formed trees, which makes it difficult to assess the exact contribution of UDP's decoding algorithm in ensuring tree-ness versus simply improving accuracy. A clearer, more algorithmic description of the baseline would improve reproducibility and strengthen the comparison.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The PageRank personalization heuristic boosts the "first verb or first content word". This seems well-suited for verb-initial or verb-second languages. Could you elaborate on why this heuristic does not harm, and in fact helps, performance on verb-final languages like Hindi, Basque, or Japanese (which was excluded for other reasons)? Does the graph structure created by the head rules naturally elevate the main verb's importance regardless of its linear position, making the personalization a safe "tie-breaker"?
2.  Your method for dynamically learning adposition directionality from test set bigrams is very effective. Have you considered applying a similar on-the-fly estimation strategy for the directionality of other function words like determiners and auxiliaries, which you currently fix based on English? This seems like a promising way to mitigate the Anglocentric bias and potentially improve performance on typologically diverse languages.
3.  The cross-domain results are excellent. You attribute the stability to the system's parameter-light, formalism-driven nature. Do you believe this stability would hold if the system were extended with more complex rules to handle phenomena like relative clauses or coordination, or would adding such rules inevitably re-introduce domain sensitivity?
