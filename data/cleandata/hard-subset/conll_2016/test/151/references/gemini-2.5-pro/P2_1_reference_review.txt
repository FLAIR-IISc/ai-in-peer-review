### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces UDP, a completely unsupervised dependency parser designed for the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The method requires no training data and instead relies on a small set of linguistically-motivated, POS-based head-attachment rules derived from UD principles. The core contributions of the work are:

1.  **A novel, effective, and simple training-free parsing architecture.** The main contribution is the system itself, which cleverly combines a small set of universal head-rules with a two-step decoding algorithm that enforces UD's constraint that function words are leaves. This approach yields surprisingly competitive results for an unsupervised system.
2.  **The application of personalized PageRank for syntactic head identification.** The paper innovates on prior work by using personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate of a sentence. This allows the algorithm to prioritize the attachment of central content words, providing a strong structural backbone for the parse tree.
3.  **A comprehensive cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation.** The paper presents a robust evaluation across 32 languages and various text domains, demonstrating that the rule-based system is not only competitive with a strong delexicalized transfer baseline but is also significantly more stable and robust to domain shift.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Performance for a Training-Free System:** The most significant strength of this work is its performance. Achieving an average UAS of 57.5 on gold POS tags across 32 languages, coming within 6.4 UAS points of a multi-source delexicalized supervised system (MSDG), is a remarkable result for a parser that uses no training data. This establishes UDP as a very strong and practical baseline for unsupervised and zero-shot parsing.
2.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is thorough and provides compelling evidence for a key advantage of this approach. The system consistently shows a lower standard deviation in performance across different domains compared to the data-driven MSD parser (e.g., average std. dev. of 2.5 vs 4.5 for gold POS in Table 6). This confirms earlier findings that rule-based systems can be less susceptible to the sampling biases of training data, a valuable property for real-world applications.
3.  **Simplicity and Language Independence:** The method is elegant in its simplicity. It relies on a handful of rules, a standard graph algorithm (PageRank), and a straightforward decoding procedure. The clever on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality from test set bigrams is a notable feature that enhances its language independence, avoiding the need for pre-specified language parameters.
4.  **Thorough Analysis and Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive. The authors evaluate on a large and diverse set of languages. Crucially, they test under realistic conditions with predicted POS tags (UDPP) and a lower-bound scenario (UDPN), showing that their system's performance degrades more gracefully than the supervised baseline. The ablation analysis in Section 6.1 clearly quantifies the contribution of the PageRank component (~9 UAS points), strengthening the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Hidden Supervision and English-centric Design:** The claim of being completely "unsupervised" and "free of language-specific parameters" is slightly undermined by the development process. The core head-rules in Table 1 and, more importantly, the fixed attachment directions for function words (e.g., "AUX, DET, and SCONJ are right-attaching, while CONJ and PUNCT are left-attaching") were determined on the English development set. This introduces a potential bias towards English and other head-initial SVO languages. While the results show this generalizes surprisingly well, the methodology itself is not derived purely from first principles of the UD formalism, but rather tuned on a specific language.
2.  **Limited Syntactic Coverage:** The head-rule set is minimal, which is key to its generality but also a source of systematic errors. The authors themselves note that the system cannot parse relative clauses because there is no NOUN → VERB rule. This is a significant structural limitation. Furthermore, the very low UAS scores for punctuation (21%) and conjunctions (38%) suggest that the simple proximity-based attachment for these phenomena is inadequate for capturing their more complex syntactic scope.
3.  **Heavy Reliance on High-Quality POS Tags:** The parser's mechanism is entirely dependent on POS tags. While the authors show it is more robust to tag errors than the MSD baseline, the performance collapses in the naive "CONTENT/FUNCTION" tagging scenario (UDPN), dropping to an average of 29.9 UAS. This highlights that while the *parsing* component is unsupervised, the system requires a reasonably accurate, and typically supervised, POS tagger to function effectively. This is an important caveat for its application in truly low-resource settings where such a tagger may not be available.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The fixed attachment directions for function words (AUX, DET, CONJ, etc.) were set based on English development data. Have you considered extending your on-the-fly estimation method (used for adpositions) to these other function word categories? This seems like it would remove the last major piece of English-specific tuning and make the parser more genuinely universal. Would you anticipate any challenges with data sparsity for categories other than ADP?
2.  The decision to exclude a `NOUN → VERB` rule to avoid ambiguity is understandable, but it prevents the parsing of relative clauses. Could you elaborate on the trade-offs observed during development? Specifically, what was the impact on overall performance when you experimented with including such a rule? Did it improve verb attachment at the cost of creating many incorrect noun-headed clauses?
