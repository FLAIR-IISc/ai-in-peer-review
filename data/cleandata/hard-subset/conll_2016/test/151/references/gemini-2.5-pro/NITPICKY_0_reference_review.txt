### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a rule-based, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data and instead relies on a small set of linguistically-motivated head-attachment rules derived from UD principles. The core of the method involves a two-step decoding process. First, content words are ranked using a personalized PageRank algorithm—which boosts the score of a heuristically-identified main predicate—and attached to form a syntactic backbone. Second, function words are attached as leaves to this backbone. The parser's only parameter learned from test data is the dominant adpositional direction (pre- vs. post-position). The system is evaluated extensively on 32 languages, under different POS quality scenarios, and across various text domains.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A comprehensive, large-scale evaluation of a simple, linguistically-motivated unsupervised parser. The experiments across 32 languages and multiple domains demonstrate that this approach is surprisingly competitive and serves as a strong, difficult-to-beat baseline for unsupervised UD parsing.
2.  A compelling demonstration of the robustness of a rule-based system to domain shift. The analysis in Section 6.3 provides strong evidence that the proposed parser is significantly more stable across domains than a standard data-driven, delexicalized transfer parser.
3.  The application of personalized PageRank for main predicate identification in a parsing context, which, according to the ablation study, provides a substantial performance improvement over a non-ranked approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is a key strength of this paper. The authors test their system on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 collection, which is an impressive breadth. Furthermore, they wisely evaluate under three distinct scenarios for POS tag availability (gold, predicted, and a naive content/function distinction), providing a realistic assessment of the parser's utility. This goes beyond the common practice of reporting only on gold-standard inputs.
2.  **Analysis of Domain Robustness:** The cross-domain consistency analysis in Section 6.3 is excellent and provides one of the paper's most significant findings. By curating domain-specific test sets and measuring performance variance, the authors convincingly show that their rule-based parser (UDP) exhibits significantly lower performance degradation across domains than the data-driven multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD). The lower average standard deviation (Table 6) is strong evidence supporting the long-held hypothesis that rule-based systems can be more robust than their data-driven counterparts.
3.  **Simplicity and Competitiveness:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. It relies on a small set of transparent rules and a well-understood algorithm (PageRank), making it easy to implement, interpret, and debug. Despite this simplicity, it achieves competitive performance, trailing a strong supervised transfer baseline by only 6.4% UAS on average (with gold POS) and even outperforming it on one language. This makes it an invaluable baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Contradiction in Central Claims:** The paper's claim to be "completely free of language-specific parameters" (Section 1, Contribution d) is directly contradicted by the methodology. Section 3.2 states that the attachment directions for AUX, DET, SCONJ, CONJ, and PUNCT were fixed based on development on the *English* treebank. These hard-coded, English-centric heuristics represent a significant set of language-specific parameters. This oversight undermines a core contribution, and the poor performance on Basque determiners (Section 6.2) demonstrates the practical failure of this assumption.
2.  **Insufficient Justification for Key Parameters and Heuristics:** Several critical design choices lack rigorous justification or sensitivity analysis.
    *   The PageRank personalization weight is set to 5. There is no explanation for this choice. Was it tuned? What is the impact of varying this value?
    *   The main predicate is identified as "the first verb or the first content word". This is a strong SVO/VSO bias that is likely suboptimal for the many verb-final languages in the UD collection. While the authors claim this is consistent for Hindi, a more detailed analysis is warranted.
    -   The PageRank teleport probability of 0.05 was selected via an ad-hoc incremental search. A discussion of the sensitivity to this parameter or justification for not using a standard value (e.g., 0.15) is missing.
3.  **Flawed Baseline and Unclear Statements:** The baseline system (BLG) is inadequately specified and potentially invalid. The authors state it "does not necessarily yield well-formed tress" and may produce disconnected graphs (Section 5.1). A system that fails to produce valid dependency trees is not a proper baseline for a parser. Furthermore, the fact that this flawed baseline outperforms the proposed UDPG parser on Estonian and Hindi is alarming and is not discussed. Footnote 4 also makes the imprecise claim that any sentence with a given POS sequence would yield the same tree, which is incorrect as the ADP directionality depends on statistics from the entire test set.
4.  **Superficial Analysis of Limitations:** The paper acknowledges but does not sufficiently analyze key limitations. The decision to exclude the `NOUN -> VERB` rule, which cripples the parser's ability to handle relative clauses, is poorly justified by the vague statement that it makes relations "less conclusive". This is a major structural limitation that requires a more thorough error analysis. Similarly, the poor performance on conjunctions and punctuation is noted but not explored in any depth.
5.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Flaws:** The manuscript suffers from a lack of polish that suggests a rushed preparation. The entire reference section is incorrectly formatted, containing raw BibTeX artifacts (e.g., `Agić et al\.,?`, `\Q1999\E`). There are typographical errors in the text (e.g., "presidental bulletin" in Table 7). The description of the BLG baseline is confusing. Collectively, these errors detract from the paper's professionalism and credibility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the contradiction regarding the claim that the parser is free of language-specific parameters, given that the attachment direction for five function word categories was fixed based on English? How would you propose to address this limitation to make the system truly language-agnostic?
2.  Could you provide a sensitivity analysis for the PageRank personalization weight (set to 5)? How much does performance vary with this parameter, and was it tuned on the English development set?
3.  How do you explain the cases where the simple (and potentially invalid) baseline BLG outperforms your full UDPG system (e.g., on Estonian and Hindi)? Does this suggest that the PageRank-based ordering is actively harmful for some languages or constructions?
