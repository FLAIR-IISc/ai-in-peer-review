### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces UDP, a dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that operates without any training data. The method is rule-based and designed to exploit the structural constraints inherent in the UD formalism. The core of the system is a two-step decoding algorithm. First, it uses personalized PageRank to determine an importance ranking for content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives), with a bias towards the first verb as the main predicate. These content words are then attached sequentially, starting with the highest-ranked word being attached to the root. In the second step, all function words (adpositions, determiners, etc.) are attached to the already-formed content-word structure, which enforces the UD constraint that function words should be leaves. Head-selection is guided by a small set of hand-crafted, POS-based attachment rules, proximity, and attachment direction constraints. The directionality for adpositions is estimated dynamically from the test data, while for other function words it is fixed. The system is evaluated extensively on 32 languages from UD v1.2, under gold and predicted POS tag settings, and is compared to a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline. A detailed cross-domain evaluation is also presented to assess the system's robustness.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Simple and Effective Unsupervised Parser based on UD Principles:** The primary contribution is the design and implementation of a transparent, rule-based parser that effectively leverages core principles of the Universal Dependencies formalism (e.g., the leaf status of function words). The novel combination of personalized PageRank for predicate identification with a two-step decoding algorithm results in a system that is surprisingly competitive with a supervised transfer parsing baseline, despite using no training data.
2.  **Extensive Empirical Analysis of Robustness:** The paper provides a thorough cross-lingual (32 languages) and cross-domain evaluation. This analysis serves as a significant contribution by empirically demonstrating that the proposed knowledge-based system is more robust to domain shifts and less susceptible to error propagation from POS tagging errors than a strong, data-driven delexicalized transfer parser.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Performance for a Zero-Shot System:** The parser achieves impressive results for a method that requires no training. On average across 32 languages, the proposed system (UDPG) scores 57.5 UAS, which is remarkably close to the 63.9 UAS achieved by a multi-source delexicalized supervised system (MSDG). The fact that a simple, rule-based approach can perform this well validates the idea of exploiting the cross-linguistically consistent constraints of the UD framework.
2.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Variation:** A key strength is the cross-domain evaluation presented in Section 6.3. The results in Table 6 clearly show that UDP exhibits a lower variance in performance across different domains compared to the MSD baseline (average standard deviation of 2.5 vs. 4.5 for the gold POS setting). This provides strong evidence for the hypothesis that parsers based on general linguistic principles are less prone to overfitting and more stable when applied to out-of-domain text.
3.  **Methodological Simplicity and Transparency:** The proposed method is clear, simple to implement, and its decisions are fully interpretable. It relies on a small set of explicit POS-based rules (Table 1) and a well-defined decoding algorithm (Figure 1). This transparency is a valuable quality, especially in contrast to increasingly complex and opaque neural models, and makes the system a strong and easily reproducible baseline.
4.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is extensive, covering a wide typological range with 32 languages. The analysis under different POS quality settings (gold, predicted, and naive) provides a realistic assessment of the parser's utility and limitations. The detailed breakdown by POS and the contribution analysis of the PageRank component further strengthen the paper's empirical foundation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on Manually Engineered, English-Tuned Components:** The parser's core logic relies on a set of head rules (Table 1) and fixed attachment directions for most function words that were manually selected and tuned on the English development set. This introduces a potential English-centric bias and undermines the claim of being "completely free of language-specific parameters". For example, the fixed right-attaching rule for determiners is effective for English but noted to be incorrect for Basque. This reliance on development-set tuning makes the approach less generalizable to a truly new language for which no such development data exists.
2.  **Ambiguous Framing as "Unsupervised":** The paper describes the system as "unsupervised dependency parsing," which is potentially misleading. While it uses no training data in the conventional sense, it is fundamentally a knowledge-based system that relies on pre-specified linguistic rules tuned on annotated data (the English dev set). This distinguishes it from traditional unsupervised grammar induction methods (e.g., DMV), which learn structural preferences directly from unannotated text. A more precise framing, such as "rule-based zero-shot parser" or "knowledge-based parser," would better reflect the nature of the work.
3.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The core components of the system—rule-based parsing, using PageRank for syntactic importance, and exploiting the content/function word distinction—are based on existing work, particularly Søgaard (2012b). The main novelties are the *personalization* of PageRank and the two-step decoding tailored to UD constraints. While the engineering and combination of these ideas are effective, the paper does not introduce a fundamentally new parsing algorithm or learning paradigm.
4.  **Inherent Performance Ceiling:** The simplicity of the rule set imposes a hard ceiling on performance. The paper acknowledges that the system cannot handle certain complex structures, such as relative clauses (due to the deliberate exclusion of a `NOUN -> VERB` rule) and coordination. The reported low UAS scores for punctuation (21%) and conjunctions (38%) further highlight these structural limitations. While the system is a strong baseline, it is not architecturally equipped to capture the full range of syntactic phenomena present in the treebanks.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The head rules and fixed function-word directions were developed using the English UD treebank. Could you discuss the potential impact of this English-centric tuning on the parser's performance across the 32 languages? Have you considered making the directionality of other function words (e.g., DET, AUX) dynamic, similar to how adpositions are handled, to reduce this bias?
2.  The personalization of PageRank involves giving a 5x weight to the estimated main predicate. Could you provide some insight into how this hyperparameter was chosen? How sensitive is the model's performance to this specific value, and have you analyzed whether this setting is equally effective for languages with different word orders (e.g., verb-final languages like Hindi vs. SVO languages)?
