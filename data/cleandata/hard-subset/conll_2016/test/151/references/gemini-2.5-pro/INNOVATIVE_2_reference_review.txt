### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces UDP, a novel unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data. The core idea is to leverage the inherent structural constraints of the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The method uses a small set of universal, POS-based head-attachment rules to construct a graph representation of a sentence. It then applies a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify the most probable sentential head. Finally, a deterministic two-step decoding algorithm produces the final parse tree, first attaching all content words based on their rank and then attaching function words, thereby ensuring the UD constraint that function words are leaves. The system is evaluated on 32 languages, demonstrating surprisingly competitive performance and superior robustness to domain shift compared to a strong, supervised delexicalized transfer system.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A bold and compelling demonstration of "parsing without training":** The primary contribution is the successful implementation of a completely training-free parser that achieves respectable performance across a wide range of languages. By relying on the linguistic principles embedded in the UD formalism rather than statistical patterns from data, this work presents a refreshing and significant alternative to the dominant data-heavy paradigm.
2.  **An elegant and novel parsing algorithm:** The combination of personalized PageRank for head identification and the two-step decoding strategy is a clever and effective technical contribution. This approach directly operationalizes high-level linguistic constraints (e.g., the centrality of predicates, the dependent status of function words) into a simple and efficient parsing algorithm.
3.  **An extensive cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation that goes far beyond typical parsing papers. The analysis across 32 languages, multiple POS conditions (gold, predicted, naive), and various domains provides strong evidence for the method's generality and, crucially, its robustness, making it a valuable new baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** This paper is a standout for its conceptual novelty. In a field saturated with increasingly complex, data-hungry neural models that achieve incremental gains, this work takes a step back and asks a more fundamental question: how much structure can we extract from linguistic formalism alone? The answer, it turns out, is "quite a lot." This is precisely the kind of bold, thought-provoking research that pushes the field in new directions. The "no training" approach is not just a gimmick; it's a powerful statement about the value of linguistic universals and annotation consistency.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results for an Unsupervised Method:** For a system with no training, the performance is remarkable. It doesn't just outperform trivial baselines; it closes a significant portion of the gap to a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), a supervised method. Outperforming the MSD system on Hindi and being highly competitive on several other languages and domains is a powerful result that validates the approach. The simplicity of the method makes its effectiveness all the more striking.
3.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Noise and Domain Shift:** The analysis in Section 6.3 is a key strength. The finding that UDP is more stable across different domains than the data-driven MSD parser is a significant result. It confirms the long-held hypothesis that systems based on general principles are less prone to overfitting to the idiosyncrasies of a specific training set. This has important practical implications, suggesting that for parsing text from novel or diverse domains, an approach like UDP could be more reliable than a transfer-based one. The lower error propagation from POS taggers is another strong point in its favor.
4.  **Simplicity and Elegance:** The entire system is built on a few simple, interpretable components: a handful of POS-based rules, the PageRank algorithm, and a straightforward decoding strategy. The clever on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality from test data is a particularly nice touch, making the system truly language-agnostic. This elegance makes the work not only easy to understand and reproduce but also a beautiful piece of research.

### Weaknesses

While my overall assessment is highly positive, there are some limitations that could be addressed. I view these primarily as avenues for exciting future work rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Opacity of the Rule Generation Process:** The paper states that the handful of head rules in Table 1 were developed using the English dev set. This introduces a slight murkiness to the "unsupervised" claim, as it constitutes a form of manual feature engineering and tuning on labeled data. The work would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of how these rules were selected. Were they derived purely from the UD specification, or was there an empirical selection process? How sensitive is the system to this specific rule set? While the results show the rules generalize beautifully, more transparency here would be welcome.
2.  **Inherent Limitations of the Simple Rule Set:** The approach is structurally unable to handle certain complex phenomena. The authors note the inability to parse relative clauses and the poor performance on conjunctions and punctuation. These are known challenges in parsing, but the current framework seems to have no mechanism to address them. While this is an acceptable tradeoff for the simplicity and novelty of the approach, it does limit the ceiling on the quality of the output parses.
3.  **Remaining Performance Gap:** Despite its impressive performance, the system still lags behind the supervised MSD parser by a meaningful margin on average (~6.4 UAS on gold POS). This isn't a failure of the method, but a realistic reflection of the value that source-language data still provides. This should be acknowledged as a key trade-off: the system gains robustness and dispenses with the need for training data at the cost of raw accuracy in settings where such data is available.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The personalized PageRank, which biases towards the first verb/content word, is a clever heuristic. Could you comment on its effectiveness across different language typologies? For instance, in strictly verb-final languages, does this "first verb" heuristic still consistently identify the main predicate, or does the graph structure built by the dependency rules override this initial bias?
2.  The on-the-fly estimation of head direction for adpositions is a fantastic idea. Have you considered applying a similar data-driven estimation for other function words whose attachment direction might vary (e.g., conjunctions, auxiliaries)? It seems this could provide a further boost in performance and generality with minimal additional complexity.
3.  Could you elaborate on the decision to exclude the `NOUN -> VERB` rule to prevent relative clauses? While you state it makes the hierarchy "less conclusive," it seems like a major structural limitation. What was the empirical impact of including this rule on the English dev set and perhaps a few other languages? Did it cause a catastrophic drop in performance, or just a minor one that was deemed not worth the added complexity?
