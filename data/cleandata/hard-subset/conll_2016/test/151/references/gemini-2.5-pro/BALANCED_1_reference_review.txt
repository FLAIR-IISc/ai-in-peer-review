### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents UDP, a completely unsupervised dependency parser that leverages the structural constraints inherent in the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The method requires no training data. It first constructs a graph of potential dependencies based on a small set of hand-crafted, POS-based head rules. It then uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank content words, biasing the ranking towards the likely main predicate. Finally, a two-step decoding algorithm attaches content words first, followed by function words, which enforces the UD constraint that function words should be leaves. Head direction for adpositions is estimated on-the-fly from the test set. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD treebanks, showing strong performance for an unsupervised method and notable robustness to domain shift.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A simple yet remarkably effective training-free dependency parser. By cleverly combining a small set of universal rules derived from the UD specification, personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and a two-step decoding algorithm, the system provides a very strong unsupervised baseline that is competitive across a wide range of languages.
2.  An extensive and insightful empirical evaluation. The paper not only tests on 32 languages but also analyzes performance under predicted POS tags and, most significantly, across different domains. The finding that this simple rule-based system is more stable across domains than a data-driven multi-source transfer system is a valuable contribution.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Effectiveness and Simplicity:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. It eschews complex statistical models and training regimes in favor of a few well-motivated linguistic heuristics derived from the UD formalism. Despite this, it achieves impressive results, scoring an average UAS of 57.5% with gold POS tags, which is only 6.4 points behind a strong multi-source delexicalized supervised transfer system (MSDG). This makes the system a highly practical tool for parsing in truly low-resource settings where no training data is available, and it sets a high bar for future unsupervised parsing work on UD.

2.  **Thorough and Realistic Evaluation:** The evaluation is a major strength. The authors test their system on a typologically diverse set of 32 languages, demonstrating its broad applicability. Critically, they go beyond the standard gold-POS evaluation by testing with predicted tags, showing that the performance gap to the supervised baseline remains stable. The cross-domain analysis is particularly compelling. By showing that UDP has a lower standard deviation in performance across domains than the MSD parser (Table 6), the authors provide strong evidence for the hypothesis that rule-based systems can be more robust to domain shift, a crucial consideration for real-world applications.

3.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Design:** While individual components like rule-based parsing and PageRank for word importance are not new, their combination and specific adaptation for the UD framework are novel and effective. The two key design choices are particularly well-justified: (1) using personalized PageRank to boost the score of the likely main predicate is a clever way to anchor the parse, and (2) the two-step decoding algorithm is an elegant mechanism to enforce the UD constraint that function words must be leaf nodes. These choices directly address known challenges in parsing and contribute significantly to the system's performance.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited and English-Centric Rule Set:** The parser's core logic relies on a small set of hand-crafted head rules (Table 1) that were developed using the English treebank. This raises concerns about generalizability and introduces a potential anglocentric bias. For instance, the fixed attachment directions for function words (e.g., AUX and DET as right-attaching) are determined from English and may not hold for many other languages. The paper would be significantly stronger if it discussed the rule development process in more detail, justified the rules linguistically beyond just "they work for UD", and analyzed their failure cases in non-English languages. While the results are good, the current approach feels more like a set of powerful heuristics than a truly universal, linguistically-grounded system.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison with Unsupervised Methods:** The main comparison is against a multi-source delexicalized *supervised* transfer system (MSD). While this is an important and challenging baseline, the paper lacks a direct comparison to other modern or classic *unsupervised* dependency parsers (e.g., DMV, posterior regularization methods). The DMV is dismissed by citing a 2012 paper, but a fresh comparison on the UD datasets would be much more informative. The included rule-based baseline (BLG) is weak and does not seem to guarantee well-formed trees, which may overstate the contribution of the full UDP model. A stronger comparison within the unsupervised parsing literature is needed to properly situate the paper's contribution.

3.  **Over-reliance on Brittle Heuristics:** The system relies on several strong, potentially brittle heuristics. The identification of the root as the first verb (or first content word) is a simple strategy that will fail in many common constructions (e.g., with subordinate clauses, in VSO languages, etc.). The decoding algorithm's reliance on linear distance as the primary criterion for selecting among valid heads is another simplification that ignores syntactic locality. The paper does not analyze the impact of these heuristics—for example, by reporting the true root accuracy of the "first verb" rule across languages—making it difficult to assess their limitations and potential for improvement.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the development of the head rules in Table 1? How were these specific rules chosen over other possibilities? Furthermore, how did you determine the fixed attachment directions (left/right-attaching) for function words like AUX, DET, and CONJ? Have you analyzed how often these English-derived directionality constraints are violated in the gold data for other languages in your test set?

2.  Your cross-domain robustness analysis is very interesting. The comparison is made against a *delexicalized* transfer parser (MSD). To what extent do you believe the observed instability of MSD is due to it being a data-driven method in general, versus it being specifically a delexicalized model that is highly sensitive to shifts in POS tag distributions across domains?

3.  The heuristic for root identification (first verb or content word) is central to the personalized PageRank. Could you provide an analysis of how often this heuristic correctly identifies the gold root attachment across the 32 languages? This would help in understanding its cross-lingual validity and its contribution to the final performance.
