### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a rule-based, unsupervised dependency parser designed for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method eschews training data entirely, relying instead on a small set of hand-crafted, linguistically-motivated head-selection rules based on POS tags. Its core mechanism involves a two-step decoding process: first, it uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to determine an importance ranking for content words, which are then attached to form the syntactic backbone of the sentence. Second, it attaches function words as leaves to this structure. The system determines adposition directionality on-the-fly from the test data and uses fixed heuristics for other function words. The authors evaluate this approach across 32 UD treebanks and conduct a cross-domain analysis, comparing it against a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer parsing baseline.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An extensive empirical evaluation of a simple heuristic-based parser.** The paper provides a valuable data point on the performance ceiling of a non-learning-based system across a wide variety of languages and domains. This serves as a strong, transparent baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.
2.  **A demonstration of the robustness of rule-based systems.** The analysis showing that the proposed parser is less sensitive to both domain shift and POS tagging errors compared to a data-driven transfer model is a significant finding, reinforcing earlier work in this area and highlighting a key trade-off between absolute performance and system stability.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Simplicity and Transparency.** The proposed parser is straightforward, deterministic, and easily interpretable. Its reliance on a small, explicit set of rules (Table 1) and a well-understood algorithm (PageRank) makes it easy to analyze its behavior and failure modes, which is a desirable quality in any system. It requires no training, making it highly accessible.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation.** The evaluation is a clear strength. Testing the system on 32 languages provides a broad picture of its cross-linguistic applicability. Furthermore, the inclusion of three evaluation settings (gold POS, predicted POS, and naive POS) gives a nuanced view of the system's reliance on tagger quality. The cross-domain analysis is particularly well-executed and provides compelling evidence for the system's stability.

3.  **Robustness to Domain and POS Quality Shift.** The paper's most convincing argument is its demonstration of robustness. The results in Table 6 clearly show a lower variance in performance across domains for UDP compared to the multi-source delexicalized (MSD) baseline. Likewise, the smaller performance drop when moving from gold to predicted POS tags suggests that the system's simple rules are less prone to error propagation than a more complex statistical model. This is a valuable quality for real-world applications where domain shifts and noisy inputs are common.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Performance Gap with Established Methods.** My primary concern is that, despite its robustness, the proposed system is consistently and significantly outperformed by the established MSD baseline on the main task (parsing with gold POS tags). An average gap of 6.4 UAS points (57.5 vs. 63.9) is substantial. The claim that the parser is "remarkably close" seems overly optimistic. While the system outperforms the baseline on Hindi, it lags considerably on the vast majority of languages. The central purpose of a parser is accuracy, and a novel method should be able to justify its adoption. The trade-off for robustness may be too steep in many scenarios.

2.  **Overstated "Unsupervised" and "Parameter-Free" Nature.** The paper's framing of the method as being entirely free of supervision or language-specific parameters is questionable.
    *   The head rules in Table 1 are manually engineered. The paper states development was done on the English treebank; these rules are a form of encoded expert knowledge derived from data. They are not induced.
    *   Key parameters, such as the PageRank personalization factor (5x) and teleport probability (0.05), were tuned on the English development set.
    *   Attachment directions for function words (AUX, DET, CONJ, etc.) were also fixed based on English development data.
    This heavy reliance on English for development undermines the claim of a universally applicable, parameter-free method. It is more accurately described as a rule-based system with hard-coded priors derived from English syntax.

3.  **Methodological Issues and Heuristic Limitations.** The approach relies on several strong, and potentially brittle, heuristics.
    *   The personalization of PageRank by boosting the "first verb or first content word" is a very strong bias towards a specific sentence structure that may not hold universally, despite the authors' claim of consistency.
    *   Estimating adposition directionality from bigram counts *on the test data* is a methodological weakness. While pragmatic, this constitutes using the test set to parameterize the model, violating standard evaluation protocols.
    *   The system has acknowledged syntactic limitations, such as the inability to handle relative clauses, which stem directly from its simplistic rule set. This limits its utility for capturing more complex syntactic phenomena.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The core trade-off presented is performance for robustness. A 6.4 UAS point average drop seems significant. Could you further motivate why a user should prefer your system over the more accurate, established MSD baseline in a practical scenario? At what point does the performance gap become too large to be justified by increased stability?

2.  You frame the system as unsupervised and free of language-specific parameters. However, the core head rules, PageRank parameters, and non-ADP function word directions were all determined using the English UD treebank. How confident are you that these rules and parameters are truly universal? For instance, how might the head rules or the PageRank personalization heuristic change if they had been developed on a verb-final, postpositional language like Hindi or a free-word-order language?

3.  The estimation of adposition direction from the test set is an unconventional choice. Could you comment on the sensitivity of this heuristic? How many sentences are required for a stable estimate, and what happens on test sets that might mix pre- and post-positional constructions (e.g., in German)? Have you considered alternative approaches that would not require accessing the test data for this parameterization?
