### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a simple and effective unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data and instead relies on a small set of linguistically-motivated, POS-based head-selection rules derived from the UD formalism. Its core mechanism involves a two-step decoding process: it first uses personalized PageRank to identify and attach content words, and then attaches function words as leaves, respecting a key UD constraint. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages under various POS-tagging conditions and is shown to be highly competitive with a strong, multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong, simple, and training-free baseline for UD parsing.** The paper demonstrates that a carefully designed rule-based system can achieve surprisingly competitive performance across a wide range of languages. The system's robustness to domain shift, where it often surpasses its data-driven counterpart, is a particularly significant and valuable finding.
2.  **An effective application of UD principles to unsupervised parsing.** The work successfully translates high-level constraints of the UD formalism (e.g., function words as dependents of content words) into a concrete and effective parsing algorithm. The use of personalized PageRank for main predicate identification and the two-step decoding process are clever ways to operationalize these principles.
3.  **A thorough and insightful cross-domain evaluation.** The analysis of parser performance across different domains for multiple languages (including newly annotated data) provides strong evidence for the stability of rule-based approaches and highlights the potential brittleness of data-driven models, which is an important consideration for real-world applications.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Empirical Performance and Robustness:** For a completely training-free method, the parser's performance is excellent. Achieving an average UAS of 57.5 with gold POS tags, which is only 6.4 points behind a strong multi-source supervised baseline (MSDG), is a remarkable result. The key strength highlighted by the experiments is the system's robustness. As shown in Section 5.3, the parser's performance degrades less than the supervised baseline when moving from gold to predicted POS tags. Even more compelling is the cross-domain evaluation (Section 6.3), where UDP consistently shows a lower standard deviation in performance across domains than the data-driven MSD parser (Table 6), confirming its stability.
2.  **Simplicity, Generality, and Interpretability:** The method is elegant and simple, relying on a small, fixed set of head rules (Table 1) and a clear algorithmic process. This transparency is a major advantage over complex, black-box neural models. Its strong performance across 32 typologically diverse languages—with only adposition directionality estimated from the test set—demonstrates the generality of the approach and the underlying UD principles. This makes it an ideal and easy-to-reproduce baseline for future research in unsupervised and low-resource parsing.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation and Analysis:** The authors have conducted a thorough and convincing evaluation. Testing on such a large number of languages is commendable. The three different POS setups (gold, predicted, naive) provide a clear picture of the system's reliance on tag quality. The ablation study in Section 6.1 clearly quantifies the contribution of the PageRank component (~9 UAS points), and the detailed cross-domain analysis provides valuable insights that support the central claims of the paper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the overall system is effective, its core components are based on pre-existing ideas. The use of rule-based constraints and PageRank for unsupervised parsing builds directly on prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012b). The main novelties are the adaptation to UD constraints (e.g., two-step decoding for function words) and the use of personalization in PageRank. While the combination is clever and well-executed, the work is more of an incremental and strong engineering contribution than a major conceptual breakthrough.
2.  **Heavy Reliance on an External POS Tagger:** The "without training" claim is somewhat weakened by the system's critical dependence on POS tags. In the main experimental setting with predicted tags (UDPP), a tagger trained on the standard UD training sets is used. In a truly zero-resource scenario, such a tagger would not be available. The performance cliff observed with naive frequency-based tags (UDPN, 29.9 UAS average) shows that the system is not viable without a reasonably accurate tagger. The paper should be more explicit about this dependency, as the parsing pipeline as a whole is not training-free in the realistic UDPP setting.
3.  **Inability to Model More Complex Structures:** The simplicity of the head rules is both a strength and a weakness. The rules are designed to capture the "backbone" of a sentence but, as the authors acknowledge, cannot handle important phenomena like relative clauses (due to the lack of a NOUN ← VERB rule). The very low UAS scores for punctuation (21%) and coordination (38%) further indicate that the parser struggles with common and complex syntactic structures, limiting its usefulness for downstream applications that require a full, accurate parse.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The directionality of several function words (AUX, DET, SCONJ, CONJ, PUNCT) was fixed based on tuning on English development data. Given the typological diversity of the 32 languages tested, how confident are you that these settings are optimal or even suitable for all of them (e.g., for verb-final languages)? Have you experimented with estimating these directions from the test data on the fly, similar to the approach used for adpositions?
2.  The personalization for PageRank gives a 5x weight to the heuristically-identified main predicate. This value seems crucial for performance, as it helps identify the root of the tree. How sensitive is the system's performance to this specific hyperparameter? Was its value tuned on English, and is there reason to believe it generalizes well to languages with different canonical structures (e.g., VSO languages)?
3.  For the predicted POS setup (UDPP), you use a tagger trained on in-language training data, which complicates the "training-free" narrative. A more realistic zero-shot scenario might involve a cross-lingual POS tagger. Could you comment on how you expect your system would perform in such a setup and whether its robustness to tag errors would still hold an advantage over the MSD baseline?
