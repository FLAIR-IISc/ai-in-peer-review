### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a rule-based, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method leverages a small, fixed set of head-dependent rules based on POS tags, which are derived from the core principles of the UD formalism. A key aspect of the approach is the use of personalized PageRank to identify the sentence's main predicate, which guides the parsing process. The decoding algorithm operates in two steps: first, it attaches content words based on their PageRank score and proximity to candidate heads; second, it attaches function words, ensuring they remain leaves in the final tree, a constraint imposed by UD. The system is evaluated extensively across 32 languages, under gold, predicted, and naive POS tag settings, and includes a detailed cross-domain analysis.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Simple and Effective Unsupervised Baseline:** The paper introduces a conceptually simple, language-agnostic parser that requires no training data. Despite its simplicity, it achieves surprisingly competitive performance, scoring on average only 6.4% UAS below a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline. This makes it a valuable and strong baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual dependency parsing.
2.  **Demonstration of Robustness to Domain Shift:** A significant contribution is the thorough empirical analysis showing that this rule-based system is substantially more robust to domain changes than its data-driven, supervised counterpart. This highlights a key practical advantage of leveraging universal linguistic principles over potentially brittle statistical patterns learned from specific training data.
3.  **Novel Application of Personalized PageRank:** While using PageRank for unsupervised parsing is not new, this work innovates by applying *personalized* PageRank to incorporate a strong linguistic prior—the centrality of the main predicate—into the word ranking process. This is a clever and effective way to guide the parser towards a more plausible global structure.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Performance for a Simple, Training-Free Method:** The parser's performance is a major strength. For a system that uses no training data and relies on a handful of universal rules, achieving an average UAS of 57.5% across 32 languages (with gold POS) is remarkable. The fact that it outperforms a multi-source delexicalized system on Hindi and is highly competitive on several other languages demonstrates the power of exploiting the foundational constraints of the UD formalism. This provides a strong argument that simple, linguistically-motivated heuristics can go a long way in cross-lingual parsing.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Cross-Domain Evaluation:** The cross-domain analysis in Section 6.3 is exemplary. The authors not only test on development sets but also curate and use test sets from multiple domains for several languages. The results, summarized in Table 6, clearly show that UDP has a much lower variance in performance across domains than the supervised MSD system (e.g., average std. dev. of 2.5 vs. 4.5 for gold POS). This provides strong evidence for the claim that rule-based systems can be more stable and generalizable, a finding of significant practical importance.

3.  **Comprehensive Multi-Lingual and Multi-Condition Evaluation:** The evaluation is extensive and well-designed. Testing on 32 diverse languages from the UD project robustly demonstrates the method's cross-lingual applicability. Furthermore, the analysis extends beyond the ideal "gold POS" scenario to more realistic settings with predicted POS tags. The error propagation analysis (Sec 5.3), which shows that UDP is less sensitive to POS tagging errors than the MSD parser, is particularly insightful and strengthens the paper's claims about robustness.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Components:** The paper builds heavily on existing work. Rule-based unsupervised parsing and the use of PageRank for word ranking in parsing have been explored before, notably by Søgaard (2012b), which is cited. The primary novelty lies in the specific adaptation of these ideas to the UD framework (e.g., the two-step decoding to enforce leaf-node status for function words, and the personalization of PageRank). While the combination is effective, the conceptual leap from prior work is incremental. The paper could benefit from a more direct comparison to Søgaard (2012b) to better delineate its specific contributions.

2.  **Overly Simplistic Rule Set and Its Consequences:** The strength of the system—its simplicity—is also a major weakness. The small set of rules in Table 1 is clearly insufficient to cover the complexity of syntax. The authors acknowledge that the system cannot handle relative clauses, and the very low performance on conjunctions (38% average UAS) indicates a complete failure to model coordination, a fundamental syntactic structure. This hard-codes a ceiling on the parser's maximum possible performance. The paper would be stronger if it included a more detailed discussion of the specific grammatical constructions that the current rule set cannot handle and a forward-looking discussion on how the rule set could be expanded in a principled, language-agnostic manner.

3.  **Lack of Clarity Regarding Development and Parameter Tuning:** The paper states that the English development set was used to "assess the contribution of individual head rules" and to "tune PageRank parameters". This blurs the line of the "training-free" claim. The process for deriving the final rule set in Table 1 is not described. Were these rules handcrafted based on UD principles, or were they selected from a larger set based on their performance on the English dev set? Similarly, the choice of the personalization weight (5x) and the teleport probability (0.05) seems arbitrary. A sensitivity analysis for these hyperparameters or a clearer explanation of their selection process is needed to assess the generality of the method and the extent of its (light) supervision.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the process used to select the head rules in Table 1? Specifically, to what extent was this process driven by manual inspection of UD guidelines versus empirical performance on the English development set? Understanding this is critical to evaluating the "unsupervised" nature of your parser.
2.  The personalization of PageRank is a key contribution. How sensitive is the final parsing performance to the personalization weight (set to 5x)? Have you explored alternative heuristics for identifying the main predicate beyond "the first verb or the first content word," especially for verb-final languages where this heuristic might be less effective?
3.  The inability to handle coordination is a significant limitation. Do you have any thoughts on how your framework could be extended to address this? Would it require fundamentally different rules or a more complex decoding algorithm?
