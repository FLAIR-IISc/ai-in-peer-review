### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised dependency parser that leverages the structural constraints of the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. Instead of using training data, the parser relies on a small, fixed set of head-dependent rules based on universal POS tags. The core of the method is a two-step decoding algorithm. First, it uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to determine an importance ranking for content words, which are then attached to form a syntactic backbone. The PageRank personalization boosts the score of the likely main predicate. Second, it attaches function words as leaves to this backbone, respecting the UD principle that function words are dependents of content words. The only language-specific parameter, adposition directionality, is estimated on-the-fly from the test data. The system is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD treebanks, showing competitive performance against a supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, and demonstrating superior robustness to POS tagging errors and domain shift.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and simple unsupervised parsing method tailored to Universal Dependencies.** The primary contribution is the novel combination of a two-step decoding process with personalized PageRank. This approach directly operationalizes key UD principles (e.g., function words as leaves, identifying a single root predicate) into a deterministic parsing algorithm that requires no training data.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the robustness of a rule-based approach.** The paper provides a strong, multi-faceted evaluation across 32 languages, three POS quality settings, and multiple domains. This analysis convincingly shows that the simple, principled parser is more resilient to domain shifts and POS errors than a standard data-driven transfer learning model, making it a valuable tool and a strong baseline for low-resource scenarios.

### Strengths

1.  **Effectiveness and Simplicity.** For a system with no training and a small set of hand-coded rules, the performance is remarkably strong. Achieving an average UAS of 57.5 on gold POS tags across 32 languages, only 6.4 points behind a supervised multi-source transfer system (MSDG), is a significant result. This establishes UDP as a very strong and difficult-to-beat baseline for future research in unsupervised and zero-shot cross-lingual parsing.
2.  **Demonstrated Robustness.** The paper provides compelling evidence that the rule-based system is more robust than its data-driven counterpart. The cross-domain evaluation (Section 6.3) is particularly thorough, showing that UDP's performance has a significantly lower standard deviation across different domains than the MSD parser (e.g., average standard deviation of 2.5 vs. 4.5 for gold POS). Furthermore, the analysis of error propagation from predicted POS tags shows the system is less sensitive to tagging errors, a critical feature for real-world applications where gold tags are unavailable.
3.  **Novel Application of Existing Techniques.** While its components (rule-based parsing, PageRank) are not new, their synthesis and specific adaptation to the UD formalism are novel and well-motivated. The use of personalized PageRank to anchor the parse on a main predicate and the two-step decoding algorithm to enforce the leaf-status of function words are clever design choices that directly exploit the linguistic constraints of the target formalism and contribute significantly to the parser's success.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility.** The method is described with exceptional clarity. The algorithm is presented formally (Figure 1), the rules are listed explicitly (Table 1), and the experimental setup is detailed. This transparency makes the work easy to understand and straightforward to reproduce.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited and Hand-Tuned Rule Set.** The parser's ceiling is fundamentally limited by its small, fixed set of head rules. The authors state these rules were selected based on performance on the English development set, which introduces a minor element of supervision and may bias the system towards English-like structures. This hand-crafting process is not detailed, and the rigidity of the rules prevents the model from capturing more complex phenomena, such as relative clauses (as noted by the authors regarding the NOUN → VERB rule). The system's performance is therefore contingent on the quality and universality of this specific rule set.
2.  **Heavy Reliance on High-Quality POS Tags.** The system's entire mechanism is driven by POS tags. While its robustness to POS errors is better than the baseline, its absolute performance degrades substantially with lower-quality tags. The UDPN experiment (using naive content/function tags) results in a low average UAS of 29.9%, indicating that the system is not viable without a reasonably accurate POS tagger. This dependency limits its applicability in truly zero-resource settings where such a tagger may not be available.
3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Related Work.** The paper compares its PageRank implementation to Søgaard (2012b) but does not provide an empirical comparison of the different graph-building strategies mentioned. The authors claim their strategy is superior but offer no quantitative evidence. Furthermore, while the MSD system is a relevant baseline, the paper would benefit from comparison with other unsupervised grammar induction methods, such as variants of the DMV model. Dismissing them based on prior work on different data/formalisms is not as convincing as a direct comparison on the UD datasets.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The head rules in Table 1 are critical to the system's success and were tuned on the English development set. Could you elaborate on this tuning process? For example, what was the initial pool of rules, and what was the criterion for inclusion/exclusion? How does performance change if, for instance, the NOUN → VERB rule is included across all languages?
2.  The personalization of PageRank uses a weight of 5 for the estimated main predicate. How was this value determined? Have you performed a sensitivity analysis for this hyperparameter?
3.  The estimation of adposition directionality from the test set is a key part of the "parameter-free" claim. How many sentences are required for this bigram-based heuristic to converge to a reliable estimate? Does the system's performance degrade on test sets that are too small or on languages that exhibit mixed adpositional strategies?
