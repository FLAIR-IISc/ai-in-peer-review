### **Summary**

This paper presents UDP, a rule-based, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The system requires no training data and builds dependency trees by relying on a small set of linguistically-motivated head-attachment rules derived from UD principles. Its core innovations are the use of personalized PageRank to rank content words (biasing towards the main predicate), a two-step decoding algorithm that first attaches content words and then function words to enforce the UD constraint that function words are leaves, and an on-the-fly method for estimating adposition directionality from the test data itself. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD treebanks and compared against a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline. The results show that this simple approach is surprisingly competitive, and notably more robust to domain shifts than the data-driven baseline.

### **Contributions**

1.  **An effective and elegant unsupervised parsing system:** The primary contribution is the system itself. It cleverly combines several simple ideas—a small POS-based rule set, personalized PageRank for identifying syntactic heads, and a two-step decoding process to enforce UD constraints—into a cohesive and surprisingly high-performing parser. The method is truly training-free and language-agnostic, making it a valuable tool and a strong baseline for low-resource parsing.
2.  **A comprehensive cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation:** A second major contribution is the rigorous and extensive empirical evaluation. The authors test their system on 32 languages, providing a broad picture of its capabilities. More importantly, the detailed cross-domain analysis provides strong evidence that this rule-based approach is significantly more robust to domain shift than a competitive data-driven transfer system, a finding of high practical relevance.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Simplicity and Interpretability:** The proposed method is remarkably simple, elegant, and easy to understand. It relies on a handful of general, linguistically-motivated rules (Table 1) and core principles of the UD formalism. This makes the parser's decisions interpretable and its errors easier to analyze compared to complex neural or statistical models.
2.  **Extensive and Realistic Evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages is thorough and demonstrates the generality of the approach. Comparing against a strong multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) is appropriate and effectively contextualizes the performance. The inclusion of experiments with both gold and predicted POS tags provides a realistic assessment of the system's utility in a practical pipeline.
3.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a standout feature of the paper. The finding that UDP exhibits significantly lower variance in performance across different domains compared to the MSD baseline (Table 6) is a powerful argument for its utility. In real-world applications where test data may come from unseen domains, this stability is a highly desirable property.
4.  **Truly Unsupervised Nature:** The parser's design requires no annotated training corpus. The clever heuristic to estimate adposition directionality (pre- vs. post-positional) directly from the test set further strengthens its language-independence and applicability to truly low-resource scenarios where no data is available.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Structural Coverage:** The strength of simplicity is also a weakness. The small set of head rules in Table 1 is unable to capture many important syntactic phenomena. The authors explicitly note that the system cannot parse relative clauses because the NOUN → VERB rule was excluded. The very low UAS scores for conjunctions (38% average) and punctuation (21% average) suggest that coordination and other complex sentence structures are handled poorly, if at all. This is a significant limitation on the quality of the syntactic analyses it can produce.
2.  **Heavy Reliance on High-Quality POS Tags:** The system's performance is entirely contingent on the input POS tags. While the performance drop with predicted tags is moderate, the UDPN experiment (using a naive content/function distinction) shows a near-total collapse in performance. This indicates that the system is brittle and requires a reasonably accurate UD-compliant POS tagger to function. While the parser itself is unsupervised, obtaining such a tagger for a low-resource language often requires supervision or significant resources, which slightly undermines the "zero-resource" framing.
3.  **Undeclared Tuning on Development Data:** The paper states that some parameters, such as the PageRank teleport probability and the attachment direction for function words like CONJ and SCONJ, were determined on the English development set. While this is a common practice, it introduces a small but non-zero amount of supervision and language-specific bias (from English). It would be more compelling if the paper discussed the sensitivity to these choices or proposed methods to estimate them from the test data, similar to the approach used for adpositions.
4.  **Lack of Ablation for Personalization Strategy:** The personalized PageRank is a key component. The authors state that personalization gives 5x more weight to the estimated main predicate. However, there is no analysis of how sensitive the model is to this specific value. It is unclear if this value was tuned and how performance changes with different weights or alternative personalization strategies (e.g., up-weighting all verbs). This would strengthen the claims about its contribution.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  You mention excluding the NOUN → VERB head rule because it made the verb-noun relation "less conclusive." Could you please elaborate on this and, if possible, provide quantitative results (e.g., on the English dev set) showing the impact of including this rule? Given that this prevents the parsing of relative clauses, understanding the trade-off here seems critical.
2.  The back-off strategy (first dropping the head rules `δ`, then the directionality constraint `κ`) is essential for guaranteeing a valid tree structure. Could you report how frequently these back-off steps are invoked across the different languages? This would provide insight into the coverage of your primary rule set and directionality heuristics.
3.  Could the directionality heuristics for other function words (e.g., CONJ, AUX) be estimated from the test data in the same way as for ADP? It seems that fixing their directionality based on English development data might be suboptimal for typologically different languages.
