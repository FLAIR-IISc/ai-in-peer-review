### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents UDP, a rule-based, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data. It operates in two main stages: first, it uses a personalized PageRank algorithm over a graph built from simple head-dependent POS rules to rank the sentence's content words. Second, it employs a two-step decoding algorithm that first attaches content words based on their PageRank score and then attaches function words, ensuring the latter are always leaves, in accordance with UD principles. The system's only parameters are adposition head direction, which is estimated on-the-fly from the test data, and a few fixed attachment directions for other function words tuned on English. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages from UD v1.2 and in a cross-domain setting, showing competitive performance against a multi-source delexicalized transfer system and superior robustness to domain shift.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Simple and Effective Unsupervised Baseline:** The paper introduces a straightforward, computationally efficient, and entirely rule-based parser that serves as a strong unsupervised baseline for UD parsing. Its performance is surprisingly competitive, demonstrating the effectiveness of leveraging core UD principles directly.
2.  **Extensive Cross-Lingual and Cross-Domain Evaluation:** A key contribution is the thorough evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domains for a subset of languages. The analysis of the parser's robustness to domain shift, showing lower performance variance than a data-driven transfer system, is a significant finding.

### Strengths
1.  **Methodological Simplicity:** The proposed parser is elegant in its simplicity. It relies on a small, interpretable set of rules and a well-understood algorithm (PageRank), making it easy to replicate and analyze. This simplicity makes it an excellent and accessible baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results for an Unsupervised System:** Despite not using any training data, the system achieves remarkable performance, trailing a supervised multi-source delexicalized parser by an average of only 6.4% UAS on gold POS tags and even outperforming it for Hindi.
3.  **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a major strength. The authors convincingly demonstrate through lower standard deviation in performance that their rule-based system is more stable across different text genres than the data-driven MSD parser. This empirically supports the hypothesis that systems based on general linguistic principles are less prone to dataset-specific overfitting.
4.  **Thorough Ablation and Error Analysis:** The paper includes a good ablation study isolating the contribution of PageRank (Sec 6.1) and a detailed breakdown of performance by POS tag (Sec 6.2). The authors are transparent about weaknesses, such as the poor performance on Basque determiners due to their English-tuned rules, which strengthens the analysis.

### Weaknesses
The submission, while presenting a promising direction, is marred by a substantial number of inconsistencies, methodological oversights, and formatting errors that collectively undermine its quality and call its conclusions into question.

1.  **Contradictory Claims Regarding Language Independence:** The paper's central claim of being "completely free of language-specific parameters" (Sec 1) is demonstrably false. Section 3.2 explicitly states that attachment directions for AUX, DET, SCONJ, CONJ, and PUNCT were "determined on the English dev data." This English-centric tuning fundamentally compromises the system's cross-lingual purity and is a likely source of error in typologically dissimilar languages, as admitted for Basque DETs (Sec 6.2). This is a critical methodological flaw, not a minor detail.

2.  **Numerous Factual Inconsistencies and Errors:** The paper suffers from a lack of careful proofreading, leading to several errors:
    *   **Missing Content:** Tables 3 and 4, which are explicitly referenced in the "Parser run example" section, are completely missing from the paper, making the example impossible to follow.
    *   **Incorrect Example Description:** In Section 4.2, the text claims the example sentence has 10 tokens, while Table 2 clearly shows it has 9. Furthermore, the text claims nouns have "six each" incoming edges, which contradicts Table 2, where "connection" and "extremists" are listed with 5 incoming edges.
    *   **Inconsistent Results Summary:** The conclusion (Sec 7) states the parser is competitive on "all but two UD languages." However, a quick check of Table 5 shows UDPG underperforms the simpler baseline (BLG) on at least three languages: Basque, Estonian, and Tamil.

3.  **Methodologically Questionable Practices:** The system relies on "peeking" at the test set to set parameters. Both the adposition directionality (Sec 3.2) and the content/function word distinction in the "naive POS" setting (UDPN, Sec 5.2) are determined by calculating statistics over the test set. While this may be pragmatic, it violates the standard machine learning protocol of keeping the test set entirely unseen during system configuration.

4.  **Unsubstantiated Claims:** Several performance-related claims are made without any supporting evidence. For instance, the authors state that their personalization strategy makes PageRank "converge a whole order of magnitude faster" (Sec 6.2) and that the adposition heuristic "succeeds at identifying the predominant... preference for all languages" (Sec 6.2). These are strong empirical claims that require data (e.g., runtime tables, convergence plots, per-language directionality accuracy) to be substantiated.

5.  **Unacceptable Formatting of References:** The bibliography is in a state of extreme disarray. It is plagued with inconsistent formatting, stray characters (e.g., `.,?`, `\Q`, `\E`), incorrect author listings, and duplicated entries (e.g., "Agić and Ljubešić"). This level of carelessness is highly unprofessional and suggests a rushed submission process.

### Questions to Authors
1.  How can you justify the claim of a language-parameter-free system when crucial directionality rules for five function-word POS tags are hard-coded based on English? What is the performance impact of learning these directions from the test set, similar to adpositions?
2.  Please provide the missing Tables 3 and 4 and correct the factual inconsistencies noted in Section 4 regarding sentence length and graph edge counts.
3.  Could you provide empirical evidence to support your claims that (a) personalization speeds up PageRank convergence by an order of magnitude, and (b) the adposition direction heuristic was correct for all 32 languages?
4.  The definition of content words in Footnote 2 (ADJ, NOUN, PROPN, VERB) omits NUM, yet NUM appears as a possible dependent of nouns in your head rules (Table 1). How is NUM handled by your system—as a content word or a function word? This ambiguity should be resolved.
