### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents UDP, a dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that operates without any training on annotated treebanks. The method is fundamentally rule-based, leveraging the strong linguistic constraints of the UD formalism. The core of the parser involves a two-step decoding algorithm. First, content words are ordered based on a personalized PageRank algorithm, which boosts the score of the likely main predicate, and then they are attached to the closest available head according to a small set of POS-based rules. Second, function words are attached, ensuring they remain leaf nodes as per UD guidelines. The system is designed to be language-agnostic, even estimating adposition directionality (pre- vs. post-position) on-the-fly from the test data. The authors conduct an extensive evaluation on 32 languages, comparing their system to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser under gold and predicted POS tag settings. A key finding is that the unsupervised parser is not only competitive but also significantly more robust to domain changes.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A simple, effective, and truly training-free dependency parser.** The primary contribution is the parser itself. It serves as an impressively strong baseline for unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing. By cleverly combining a small set of linguistically-motivated rules, personalized PageRank, and a two-step decoding process that directly encodes UD principles, the authors have created a practical tool that performs remarkably well for a system with no reliance on training treebanks.
2.  **An extensive and insightful multi-lingual, cross-domain evaluation.** A major contribution of this work is its thorough evaluation. Testing on 32 languages under different POS quality settings provides a broad and realistic assessment of the parser's performance. The cross-domain analysis is particularly valuable, offering strong empirical evidence that this rule-based approach is more stable across domains than a competitive data-driven transfer model.
3.  **A novel adaptation of existing techniques for the Universal Dependencies formalism.** While rule-based parsing and the use of PageRank for word importance are not new, the paper's novelty lies in their specific adaptation and combination to exploit the UD formalism. The use of personalization to identify the main predicate and the two-step decoding to enforce the leaf-status of function words are clever design choices that are directly responsible for the system's success.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Performance for a Training-Free System:** The most compelling argument for this paper is the system's performance. Achieving an average UAS of 57.5 with gold POS tags, which is only 6.4 points behind a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer system (MSDG), is an excellent result for a fully unsupervised method. It even outperforms the supervised baseline on Hindi. This demonstrates that a carefully designed set of linguistic principles can go a long way, establishing a very high bar for future unsupervised work.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Clarity:** The proposed method is elegant, simple to understand, and easy to reimplement. The paper does an excellent job of explaining the components: the PageRank setup, the head rules (Table 1), and the decoding algorithm (Figure 1) are all presented clearly. The worked example in Section 4 is particularly helpful in making the entire process concrete. This clarity and simplicity are significant strengths.
3.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a major highlight. The finding that UDP exhibits a much lower standard deviation in performance across different domains compared to the data-driven MSD baseline (Table 6) provides strong evidence for the robustness of rule-based systems. This is an important result, confirming earlier findings in a large-scale, multilingual setting and highlighting a key advantage of this approach in real-world applications where domain shifts are common.
4.  **Thoroughness of Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their system across 32 languages from the UD collection, which is a commendable effort. Furthermore, they go beyond the standard "gold POS" setting by also reporting results with predicted POS tags (a more realistic scenario) and a naive content-function tagging scheme. This multi-faceted evaluation provides a comprehensive picture of the system's strengths and limitations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Syntactic Expressiveness:** The system's strength—its simplicity—is also its main weakness. The parser is constrained by a very small set of head rules (Table 1). The authors themselves note that this prevents the model from handling phenomena like relative clauses (due to the deliberate exclusion of a NOUN → VERB rule). This inherent limitation in expressiveness likely accounts for a significant portion of the performance gap with supervised systems and restricts the complexity of the syntactic structures it can produce.
2.  **Dependence on High-Quality POS Tagging:** While the parser is "training-free" in terms of syntactic annotations, it is critically dependent on POS tags. The results show a substantial performance drop from the gold POS setting (UDPG) to the naive content/function setting (UDPN), from 57.5 to 29.9 UAS. This indicates that in a truly low-resource scenario where a high-quality POS tagger is unavailable, the parser's utility would be severely diminished. The "without training" claim should be understood as "without training on syntactic trees."
3.  **Potential English Bias in Development:** The paper states that the English dev set was used to tune PageRank parameters, select the head rules, and determine fixed attachment directions for function words (e.g., CONJ as left-attaching, DET as right-attaching). While the system performs well on a wide variety of languages, this development process introduces a potential bias towards English-like structures. For example, the fixed directionality of conjunctions or determiners may be suboptimal for languages with fundamentally different structures.
4.  **Incremental Methodological Novelty:** The core ideas of using head-attachment rules and PageRank for parsing have been explored before, notably by Søgaard (2012b), which the authors build upon. The novelty here is in the specific combination and adaptation for the UD framework (personalization, two-step decoding). While the engineering and results are excellent, the conceptual contribution is more of a refinement and effective application of existing ideas rather than a completely new paradigm.

### Questions to Authors

1.  You mention that the NOUN → VERB head rule was excluded during development because it made verb-noun relations "less conclusive." Could you quantify the impact of this decision? What was the performance on the English dev set with this rule included? Does it significantly improve structures like relative clauses at the cost of overall precision?
2.  The directionality for function words (AUX, DET, CONJ, etc.) was fixed based on the English dev set. Have you considered making this another parameter that can be estimated from the test data, similar to how adposition direction is handled? This could potentially reduce the English bias and improve performance on languages with different word order patterns.
3.  The performance on punctuation (PUNCT) is very low (21% UAS average). While punctuation is often a challenge, this seems particularly low. Is this a result of the simple left-attaching rule, or are there other factors at play? Did you experiment with other heuristics for attaching punctuation?
