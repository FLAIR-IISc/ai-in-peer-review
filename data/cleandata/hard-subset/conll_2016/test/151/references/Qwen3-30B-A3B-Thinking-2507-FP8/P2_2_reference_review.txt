

# Review: "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), a dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on explicit part-of-speech (POS) constraints defined by the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The approach uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank content words, incorporates UD's restrictions on function words being leaves, and estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and naive type-constrained POS) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), which is a strong baseline in cross-lingual parsing.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Demonstrating competitive performance with a rule-based, training-free approach**: The most significant contribution is showing that a simple rule-based dependency parser leveraging UD constraints can achieve results comparable to supervised transfer parsers, with the advantage of being language-independent and robust to domain shifts.

2. **Systematic exploration of UD constraints for parsing**: The paper provides a clear methodology for adapting dependency parsing to UD's specific formalism constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as dependents), which is a novel approach to cross-lingual parsing.

3. **Domain robustness analysis**: The paper demonstrates that rule-based approaches can be more stable across different text domains than data-driven approaches, which is a valuable insight for practical NLP applications.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of UD constraints to unsupervised parsing**: The paper makes a strong case for leveraging the UD formalism's constraints rather than relying on data-driven approaches. This is a significant contribution as it provides a principled approach to cross-lingual parsing that doesn't depend on resource-rich languages or parallel corpora. The authors clearly demonstrate how UD's specific constraints (like function words always being dependents) can be used to build a robust parser without training data.

2. **Comprehensive and well-designed evaluation**: The evaluation across 32 languages, three POS scenarios, and multiple domains provides a thorough assessment of the system's capabilities and limitations. The comparison to MSD, a strong supervised baseline, is particularly convincing. The authors go beyond standard UAS metrics to analyze domain robustness, which is a significant strength. The results showing UDP's lower standard deviation across domains (Table 6) provide compelling evidence for the stability of rule-based approaches.

3. **Practical implementation of UD formalism**: The paper successfully demonstrates that UD's constraints can be directly implemented in a parsing algorithm. The approach to estimating adposition direction from test data (Sec. 3.2) is particularly clever and addresses a common challenge in cross-lingual parsing without requiring language-specific knowledge. This is a major practical contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading claim about "no training"**: The paper's title and claims of being "without training" are misleading. While the parser itself requires no training, it depends on a POS tagger (TnT) that requires training data for each language. This is a significant limitation that the paper doesn't adequately address. The authors state "our system is a dependency parser that requires no training," but this is only true for the parser itself, not for the complete pipeline. This misrepresentation undermines the paper's main claim and could mislead readers about the system's applicability to truly low-resource scenarios.

2. **Overstated performance claims**: The paper claims UDP "compares favorably to delexicalized parsing" and "beats a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) on Hindi." However, the average difference between UDPG and MSDG is only 6.4% (Table 5), which is modest for a significant claim. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the rule-based approach can achieve such close results to a supervised system. The claim that UDP "outperforms MSDG on one language (Hindi)" is presented as a major achievement, but the difference is small (55.3 vs 54.8 UAS), and the paper doesn't discuss whether this difference is statistically significant.

3. **Limited discussion of system limitations**: The paper acknowledges that the system cannot infer relative clauses (due to excluding the NOUN→VERB rule) but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how common or problematic this limitation is. Similarly, the paper notes that PUNCT has very low UAS (21% average), but doesn't explore why this is the case or how it affects the overall utility of the system. These limitations significantly impact the system's practical applicability.

4. **Contradictory claim about language independence**: The paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters," but the system uses language-specific attachment directions for function words (e.g., "AUX, DET, and SCONJ are right-attaching, while CONJ and PUNCT are left-attaching"). This contradicts the claim of being language-independent, as the attachment direction would need to be determined for each language. The paper doesn't adequately explain how this is consistent with the language independence claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters" but uses language-specific attachment directions for function words. How do you reconcile this claim with the need for language-specific directionality information?

2. Given that the POS tagging step requires training data for each language (TnT), how would you evaluate the complete pipeline in a truly low-resource setting where no training data is available for POS tagging?

3. The paper states that "the system cannot infer relative clauses" due to excluding the NOUN→VERB head rule. Could you provide specific examples of sentences where this limitation causes significant parsing errors, and how common these errors are across the languages evaluated?

4. The paper shows that UDP outperforms MSD in some domains (Table 7), but the differences are relatively small. What specific linguistic properties of these domains make UDP more effective, and could these properties be leveraged to improve supervised approaches as well?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that a rule-based approach leveraging UD constraints can achieve competitive results without training data. The evaluation is thorough and provides important insights about the stability of rule-based approaches across domains. However, the misleading claims about "no training" and the underplayed limitations of the approach prevent this from being a strong submission.

The paper's main contribution is valuable, but the presentation of the approach as a truly training-free solution is inaccurate due to the dependency on POS tagging. The performance gains over supervised baselines are modest, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why a rule-based approach can achieve such close results to a supervised system.

**Recommendation**: Weak Accept. The paper presents a valuable approach to UD parsing that deserves attention, but the misleading claims about training requirements and the underplayed limitations of the approach need to be addressed before the paper can be accepted. The authors should clarify the training requirements of their full pipeline and provide a more nuanced discussion of the system's limitations.
