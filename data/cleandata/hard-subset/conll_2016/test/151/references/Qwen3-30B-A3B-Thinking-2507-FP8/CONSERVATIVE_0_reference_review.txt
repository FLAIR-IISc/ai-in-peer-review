

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a dependency parser (UDP) that claims to require no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints. The approach uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank content words, a two-step decoding process that treats content and function words differently according to UD formalism, and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors claim UDP achieves competitive performance compared to supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parsers (MSD) on 32 languages, with added robustness to domain shifts.

## Main Contributions

1. A rule-based dependency parser that requires no training data and relies on UD-specific constraints
2. A method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data
3. A two-step decoding algorithm that enforces UD's leaf status for function words

## Strengths

1. **Competitive performance with minimal resource requirements**: The results in Table 5 show UDPG (UDP with gold POS) achieves 93.6% of MSDG's performance on average (57.5 vs. 61.2 UAS), which is impressive for an unsupervised approach. This demonstrates that UD's constraints can indeed enable high-quality parsing without training data.

2. **Superior robustness to domain shifts**: The cross-domain analysis in Table 6 and Table 7 clearly shows UDP has significantly lower standard deviations across domains (1.8 vs. 4.5 for gold POS) compared to MSD. This practical advantage is well-documented and meaningful for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

3. **Clear methodology and interpretability**: The approach is transparent and based on explicit linguistic constraints (UD formalism), making it easy to understand and debug. The two-step decoding process with clear rationale for function word treatment is well-motivated by the UD formalism.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty and lack of proper baseline comparison**: The paper builds on Søgaard's (2012b) work but doesn't adequately compare to it or other unsupervised parsers in the literature. The authors claim "our system, however, has two key differences" but don't demonstrate that these differences are significant compared to existing work. The comparison to a "rule-based baseline" (BL) is insufficient as it doesn't represent the current state of the art in unsupervised parsing.

2. **Misleading "no training" claim**: The authors state "our system is a dependency parser that requires no training," but admit they used English treebank data for development to tune PageRank parameters and assess head rules. This contradicts their claim of being "completely free of language-specific parameters" and undermines their central contribution. The adposition direction is estimated on test data, but the core algorithm is language-specifically tuned.

3. **Insufficient analysis of POS dependency**: The paper shows UDP's performance drops when using predicted POS tags (UDPP), but doesn't adequately address whether this makes the approach practically useful. The error propagation rate analysis (0.37 vs 0.46) is interesting but doesn't provide enough context about how this compares to other approaches or whether the drop is acceptable for real-world applications.

4. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The results are presented as simple averages without any statistical tests to determine if the differences between UDP and MSD are significant. This is a critical omission for a paper claiming to present competitive results.

5. **Overstated claims about UD constraints**: The paper states "we expect that such a formalism lends itself more naturally to a simple and linguistically sound rule-based approach," but doesn't provide evidence that UD constraints are uniquely suited for this approach compared to other formalisms. The authors don't demonstrate that their approach wouldn't work equally well with other dependency formalisms.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "completely free of language-specific parameters" but used English treebank data for development and tuning. Can you clarify how this approach is truly language-agnostic and how the system would perform on languages for which no development data is available?

2. Why was the comparison to Søgaard (2012b) omitted from the experiments, given that the paper builds directly on this work? How does UDP compare to Søgaard's unsupervised parser in terms of performance and methodology?

3. The paper states "UDP, the simple unsupervised system with PageRank, outperforms the adjacency baselines (BL) by 4% on average." However, the adjacency baseline is not a standard baseline in unsupervised parsing. Why was this baseline chosen over more established baselines like those in Søgaard (2012) or Spitkovsky (2010)?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that leverages UD constraints. The results are promising for a no-training approach, particularly regarding robustness to domain shifts. However, the novelty claims are overstated, the "no training" claim is misleading, and the baseline comparison is insufficient. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing unsupervised parsers, a clearer explanation of the language-specific tuning, and proper statistical significance testing.

I would recommend rejection due to the fundamental issues with the novelty claim and the lack of proper baseline comparison. While the approach is interesting, it doesn't sufficiently advance the state of the art beyond what's already established in the unsupervised parsing literature. The authors have made a reasonable contribution to the field, but the paper doesn't present it with the rigor required for CONLL.

The paper would be significantly improved with a more accurate assessment of its novelty, a stronger baseline comparison, and a more precise explanation of the "no training" claim. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution is overstated.
