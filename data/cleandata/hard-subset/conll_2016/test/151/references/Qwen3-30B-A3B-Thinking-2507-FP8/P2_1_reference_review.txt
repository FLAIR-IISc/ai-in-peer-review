

# Review: "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The system uses a two-step decoding process: first assigning heads to content words based on personalized PageRank and UD head rules, then assigning heads to function words while ensuring they are leaves (as required by UD). The authors estimate adposition attachment direction directly from test data rather than relying on pre-defined features. The system is evaluated on 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive) and shows competitive performance against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), with the advantage of being more robust to domain changes.

## Main Contributions
1. A rule-based dependency parser that fully exploits UD constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) without any training data
2. A novel application of personalized PageRank for main predicate identification that significantly improves parsing accuracy
3. A method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data, eliminating the need for language-specific parameters

## Strengths

1. **Novel exploitation of UD constraints**: The paper makes a strong case for leveraging the UD formalism as a foundation for unsupervised parsing rather than treating it as a target for transfer learning. The authors effectively show how UD's hard constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) can be used to build a simple yet effective parser. This is a significant contribution because it shifts the paradigm from data-driven approaches to constraint-driven parsing, which is particularly valuable for low-resource languages.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The evaluation across 32 languages with multiple POS scenarios demonstrates that UDP is highly competitive with supervised transfer methods (MSD), achieving comparable UAS scores (average difference of 6.4% between UDPG and MSDG). The paper's most compelling evidence is the robustness to domain shifts, where UDP shows significantly lower standard deviations across domains (Table 6), confirming the authors' claim that "rule-based parsers can be more stable to domain shift."

3. **Clear demonstration of PageRank's contribution**: The authors conduct a thorough ablation study (Section 6.1) that isolates the contribution of personalized PageRank versus the baseline. They show that PageRank contributes 9 UAS points on average compared to a reading-order ranking system, which is a substantial improvement. This analysis provides strong evidence for the importance of their technical contribution.

4. **Practical relevance for low-resource languages**: The paper's focus on a parser that doesn't require training data or language-specific parameters directly addresses a critical need in cross-lingual NLP. The authors demonstrate that their system remains effective even when using predicted POS tags (with only a 2.2% drop in UAS compared to MSD's 2.7% drop), which is particularly valuable for low-resource languages where gold POS is unavailable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that their parser cannot handle relative clauses (Section 6.2) due to excluding the NOUN→VERB head rule, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how this limitation impacts overall parsing quality. They should quantify how many relative clauses are present in UD treebanks and how often the parser fails to handle them. Without this, it's unclear how significant this limitation is for practical applications.

2. **Overly optimistic claims about the naive POS scenario**: The naive POS scenario (UDPN), where words are tagged as either "CONTENT" or "FUNCTION" based on frequency, seems artificial and not reflective of real-world scenarios. The authors claim UDP "outperforms the adjacency baselines by 4% on average" in this scenario, but this isn't particularly meaningful since the naive POS tagging is so crude. The paper would be stronger if they focused on more realistic POS error scenarios.

3. **Lack of comparison to other unsupervised parsers**: While the paper compares to a rule-based baseline (BL) and a supervised transfer system (MSD), it doesn't compare to other unsupervised dependency parsers that might be more relevant. For example, the authors cite Søgaard (2012b) as their closest related work, but they don't compare directly to that system or other unsupervised parsers like those by Klein and Manning (2004) or Spitkovsky et al. (2010). This makes it difficult to assess how much their approach actually advances the state of unsupervised parsing.

4. **Insufficient analysis of language-specific performance**: The paper reports average performance across 32 languages but doesn't provide enough detail on why UDP performs better on some languages than others. For example, they note that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi but don't analyze why. The paper would benefit from a deeper linguistic analysis of which language features (e.g., word order, morphological complexity) most impact UDP's performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that UDP "beats a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) on Hindi." However, in Table 5, UDPG (gold POS) scores 45.7 for Hindi, while MSDG scores 46.3, meaning MSD actually performs better. Could you clarify this discrepancy?

2. In Section 6.2, the paper mentions that "the system cannot infer relative clauses" due to excluding the NOUN→VERB head rule. Could you provide statistics on how many relative clauses are present in UD treebanks, and how often the parser fails to handle them correctly? What would be the potential impact of including this rule?

3. The authors claim that "the parser requires no training data" but note that they used the English treebank during development to assess the contribution of individual head rules. How much of the "training" is actually done on English data, and how would this affect the parser's performance on languages with very different structures from English?

4. The paper states that UDP is "more robust to domain change across languages." Could you provide more specific examples of domain shifts where UDP's advantage is most pronounced, and explain why this robustness occurs?

5. The authors mention that "we exclude Japanese because the treebank is distributed without word forms." Could you elaborate on how this exclusion affects the overall evaluation, and whether the parser would work if word forms were available for Japanese?

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to unsupervised dependency parsing by effectively leveraging the constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism. The empirical results are compelling, particularly the demonstration of robustness to domain shifts, which is a significant practical advantage. The paper is well-written and the methodology is clear and elegant. While there are some limitations in the evaluation and discussion of the approach's constraints, these do not detract from the paper's main contribution. I recommend acceptance.
