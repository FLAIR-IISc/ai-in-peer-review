

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints of the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The system uses personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate (root) of a sentence, incorporates UD-specific head-dependent rules, and estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by strength):**

1. **A completely unsupervised dependency parser** that requires no training data, only relying on UD constraints and POS tags, with competitive performance compared to supervised cross-lingual transfer parsers.

2. **A novel application of personalized PageRank** for identifying the main predicate (root) of a sentence, which significantly improves parsing accuracy over baseline approaches.

3. **A method for estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data**, eliminating the need for language-specific parameters and allowing the parser to adapt to new languages without retraining.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and clear problem statement:** The paper effectively argues that UD's constraints make it more amenable to a rule-based, unsupervised approach compared to previous work. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing cross-lingual transfer approaches (which rely on unrealistic assumptions about language similarity and data availability) and unsupervised parsers (which often fall short of supervised performance). This is a significant contribution to the field, as it shifts the focus from data-driven approaches to constraint-based ones.

2. **Competitive performance with minimal assumptions:** The results demonstrate that UDP achieves remarkably close performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), with only a 6.4% average difference in UAS. This is particularly impressive given that UDP requires no training data at all. The fact that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi is especially noteworthy, as Hindi is a verb-final language where the main predicate identification strategy might be expected to perform poorly.

3. **Compelling evidence of robustness to domain shift:** The cross-domain evaluation is one of the paper's strongest points. The authors demonstrate that UDP is significantly more robust to domain changes than the MSD parser, with much lower standard deviation across domains. This has important practical implications for real-world applications where domain shifts are common. The detailed analysis showing that UDP's standard deviation across domains is 2.5-4.2 (vs. 4.5-7.3 for MSD) provides strong evidence for the claim.

4. **Comprehensive evaluation across POS scenarios:** The paper evaluates UDP under three different POS tag scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive), providing valuable insights into the system's error propagation characteristics. The finding that UDP's error propagation rate (0.37) is lower than MSD's (0.46) is particularly compelling and suggests UDP is more robust to POS errors.

5. **Clear implementation and practical contribution:** The authors provide a detailed description of their implementation and make both the parser and in-domain annotated test sets freely available, which is a significant contribution to the community. The paper also includes a detailed example of the parser's operation, making the approach more accessible to readers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in core parsing approach:** While the application of UD constraints to unsupervised parsing is novel, the core parsing algorithm (two-step decoding with personalized PageRank) builds heavily on Søgaard (2012b). The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how their approach differs from Søgaard's in terms of the parsing algorithm itself. The personalized PageRank is presented as a key contribution, but it's not clear how much of this is truly novel compared to Søgaard's work. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of their approach to Søgaard's.

2. **Overstated claims about UD constraints:** The paper states that "UD prescribes that function words like adpositions, auxiliaries, and determiners are always dependents of content words." However, the UD guidelines state "function words are usually dependents of content words" (not "always"). The paper doesn't acknowledge these nuances or discuss how their parser handles exceptions to this rule. This overstatement weakens the paper's claim about the applicability of UD constraints.

3. **Lack of comparison to other unsupervised parsers:** The paper compares UDP to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) and a rule-based baseline (BL), but doesn't compare to other state-of-the-art unsupervised dependency parsers. This makes it difficult to assess how UDP compares to the broader landscape of unsupervised parsing approaches. For example, the paper doesn't mention how UDP compares to the unsupervised parsers by Søgaard (2012b) or Spitkovsky et al. (2010).

4. **Insufficient error analysis for underperforming languages:** While the paper provides some error analysis (e.g., in Section 6.2), it doesn't thoroughly analyze why UDP makes certain errors, particularly for languages where it underperforms compared to MSD (e.g., Arabic and Tamil with 26% root accuracy). The paper notes this limitation but doesn't explore potential reasons or solutions, which limits the paper's contribution to understanding unsupervised parsing challenges.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations:** The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, the parser cannot handle relative clauses (because it doesn't allow nouns to be heads for verbs), which is a significant limitation for many languages. The paper also doesn't address how their approach would scale to languages with very different word order or syntactic structures than those in the UD treebanks.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "UD prescribes that function words like adpositions, auxiliaries, and determiners are always dependents of content words." However, the UD guidelines state "function words are usually dependents of content words" (not "always"). Could the authors clarify how their parser handles the exceptions where this rule doesn't hold, and how they've verified this in the UD treebanks they used?

2. The paper compares UDP to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), but doesn't compare to other unsupervised parsers. How does UDP compare to state-of-the-art unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., those by Søgaard, 2012; Spitkovsky et al., 2010; etc.) in terms of performance?

3. The paper mentions that UDP cannot infer relative clauses because it doesn't allow nouns to be heads for verbs. Could the authors elaborate on how this limitation affects parsing for specific languages, and whether there are potential extensions to their approach to handle such cases?

4. The paper states that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi. Could the authors provide more details about why this might be the case, especially given that Hindi is a verb-final language where the main predicate identification strategy (using the first verb) might be particularly problematic?

5. The paper reports that UDP outperforms the baseline by 4% on average on the two-type-based naive POS tag scenario. However, this scenario is extremely simplified (only two POS tags: CONTENT and FUNCTION). Could the authors provide more context about how realistic this evaluation setup is, and whether the 4% improvement would hold with a more sophisticated but still unsupervised POS tagging approach?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with a strong motivation and compelling results that demonstrate the viability of a constraint-based, unsupervised approach to dependency parsing. The paper makes a valuable contribution by showing that a simple rule-based system can achieve competitive performance without any training data, and that such systems are more robust to domain shifts than data-driven approaches.

However, the paper's claims about novelty are somewhat overstated, and it would benefit from more thorough comparisons to existing unsupervised parsers and a more nuanced discussion of UD constraints. The lack of detailed error analysis for underperforming languages and the limited discussion of the parser's limitations are also notable weaknesses.

While the paper has some weaknesses, the core contribution of demonstrating a highly effective unsupervised parser that leverages UD constraints is significant. I recommend acceptance, but the authors should address the weaknesses outlined above in the revision.

**Decision: Accept**

**Justification:** The paper presents a novel and effective approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that makes a significant contribution to the field. The results are compelling, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the system is practical and available to the community. While there are some weaknesses in the novelty claims and error analysis, the core contribution is strong and well-supported by the results. The paper provides valuable insights into the potential of constraint-based approaches to parsing, which is an important direction for future research.
