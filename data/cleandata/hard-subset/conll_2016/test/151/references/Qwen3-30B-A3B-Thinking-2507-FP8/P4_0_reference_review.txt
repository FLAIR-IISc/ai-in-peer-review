

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints. The system uses personalized PageRank to rank content words, incorporates UD-specific restrictions (such as function words being leaves), and estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data.

**Main Contributions (ordered by strength):**
1. A completely unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data and produces UD-compliant trees
2. A novel adaptation of personalized PageRank for main predicate identification specifically tailored to UD formalism
3. A method for estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data without language-specific parameters

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical evaluation across multiple scenarios:** The paper evaluates the system on 32 languages from UD v1.2 across three different POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and a naive two-POS tag scenario) and demonstrates robustness to domain shifts. The results show UDPG (UD with gold POS) is remarkably close to MSDG (multi-source delexicalized with gold POS), with an average difference of 6.4% in UAS, and even outperforms MSDG on Hindi. This comprehensive evaluation across languages and scenarios provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.

2. **Demonstrated robustness to domain shifts:** The paper provides a thorough analysis of the parser's performance across different domains (e.g., news, legal, literature). The results show UDP has a much lower standard deviation across domains compared to the supervised baseline (MSD), confirming the hypothesis that rule-based approaches are more stable to domain shifts. This finding has significant implications for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

3. **Clear technical explanation of the UD-specific adaptation:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of how the UD formalism constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) are incorporated into the parsing algorithm. The two-step decoding approach (first attaching content words, then function words) effectively enforces the UD requirement that function words are leaves, which is a key technical contribution that addresses a specific challenge in cross-lingual parsing.

## Weaknesses
1. **Overstated novelty claim:** The paper claims novelty in adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, using personalized PageRank, and estimating adposition direction directly from test data. However, the core techniques (PageRank-based ranking and two-step decoding) are directly adapted from Søgaard (2012b), with the authors using English development data to tune parameters. The paper doesn't clearly articulate what is truly novel beyond adapting existing methods to UD, making the novelty claim overstated.

2. **Lack of comparison to standard unsupervised parsers:** The paper primarily compares to a supervised transfer parser (MSD) and a rule-based baseline, but omits comparison to standard unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., Klein and Manning, 2004; Spitkovsky et al., 2010; Gillenwater et al., 2010). Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess how much the UD-specific adaptation actually improves unsupervised parsing performance, which weakens the contribution claim.

3. **Misleading "no training" claim:** While the authors state the system requires no training data, they used the English treebank during development to tune PageRank parameters and determine attachment directions for some function words (AUX, DET, SCONJ). The paper doesn't clearly distinguish between development data and training data, which misleads readers about the system's unsupervised nature.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims the system "requires no training," but mentions using English treebank during development to tune parameters. How much of the English treebank was used for development, and what specific parameters were tuned? Could the same parameters be used across all languages without language-specific tuning?

2. The authors state the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters," but they determined attachment directions for AUX, DET, and SCONJ on English dev data. How do they justify that this is not a language-specific parameter, especially since attachment directions might vary across languages (e.g., Basque has different DET behavior)?

3. The paper compares to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) but doesn't compare to other unsupervised dependency parsers. Why were these comparisons omitted, and how does UDP's performance compare to state-of-the-art unsupervised parsers like those by Klein and Manning (2004) or Spitkovsky et al. (2010)?
