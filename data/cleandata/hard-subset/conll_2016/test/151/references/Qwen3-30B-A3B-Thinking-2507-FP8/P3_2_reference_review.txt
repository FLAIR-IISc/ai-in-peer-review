

# Peer Review: "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints of the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The main contributions are:

1. A rule-based dependency parser that strictly follows UD constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as dependents only)
2. A personalized PageRank approach for identifying the main predicate
3. A two-step decoding algorithm that ensures function words are leaves (as required by UD)
4. A method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data (ADP-NOMINAL vs. NOMINAL-ADP bigrams)

The authors claim their parser achieves competitive performance compared to supervised transfer parsers while being more robust to domain changes. They evaluate on 32 languages with three POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and naive content/function POS).

## Strengths

### 1. Effective exploitation of UD constraints
The paper makes a compelling case that the UD formalism's constraints (particularly the requirement that function words are always dependents of content words) can be leveraged to create a simple yet effective unsupervised parser. Most unsupervised parsers don't explicitly exploit such linguistic constraints, instead relying on statistical patterns that may not align with UD conventions. The authors' approach of using the UD-specific head rules (Table 1) and ensuring function words are leaves (through two-step decoding) directly addresses this gap. This is a significant contribution as it demonstrates that constraint-based approaches can achieve surprisingly good results without any training data.

### 2. Strong empirical evidence for domain robustness
The paper provides compelling evidence that the rule-based UDP parser is more robust to domain shifts than data-driven approaches. The cross-domain evaluation (Tables 6 and 7) shows UDP has significantly lower standard deviations across different domains (e.g., news, legal, literature) compared to the multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD). This is particularly valuable as domain shift is a critical issue in practical NLP applications. The authors effectively demonstrate that the simplicity of their approach (relying on UD constraints rather than language-specific statistical patterns) leads to more stable performance across different text genres.

### 3. Clear methodological innovation in PageRank personalization
The personalized PageRank approach is a notable contribution that effectively addresses a key challenge in unsupervised parsing: identifying the main predicate. By giving 5x more weight to the estimated main predicate (first verb or first content word), the authors ensure the most likely root node gets the highest rank. This simple modification significantly improves performance (9 UAS points on average over UDPNoPR) while maintaining the parser's simplicity. The paper clearly explains how this personalization makes the algorithm converge faster (an order of magnitude) and avoids the uniform distribution problem that can occur with high teleport probabilities.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Overstated "no training" claim
The paper claims "our system is a dependency parser that requires no training," but this is misleading. The authors explicitly state they used the English treebank during development to "assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters and function-word directionality." This constitutes a form of training that could bias the system toward English patterns, potentially reducing its generalizability to other languages. The paper should more clearly acknowledge this limitation and clarify how much of the English treebank was used for development.

### 2. Limited evaluation on languages with challenging UD annotation
The paper evaluates on 32 languages but makes the questionable claim that "all treebanks are equally compliant" with UD (Section 6.2). However, the UD project itself acknowledges significant variation in annotation quality across languages (e.g., the Polish treebank has been noted for inconsistent annotation, and some languages have more problematic UD annotations than others). The authors don't provide evidence that the UD treebanks they use are of comparable quality. This is a significant weakness because the parser's performance could be heavily influenced by the quality of the UD annotation in the treebanks.

### 3. Inadequate comparison to other unsupervised parsers
The paper compares to a rule-based baseline and a supervised transfer parser (MSD), but doesn't compare to other unsupervised parsers (e.g., Søgaard's 2012b, Klein and Manning's DMV, or Spitkovsky et al.'s approaches). This makes it difficult to judge whether UDP is actually better than existing unsupervised methods. The authors mention Søgaard's work but don't provide a direct comparison, which is a significant omission given that Søgaard's method is cited as a foundation for their approach.

### 4. Questionable claims about "no language-specific parameters"
The paper claims "making it completely free of language-specific parameters," but this is inaccurate. The authors state that "AUX, DET, and SCONJ are right-attaching, while CONJ and PUNCT are left-attaching" - this is language-specific knowledge, even if minimal. The adposition direction is estimated from test data, but the other directionality is fixed. This undermines the paper's core claim of being language-agnostic. The paper should acknowledge that some directionality information is language-specific.

### 5. Limited discussion of limitations in challenging UD languages
The paper reports strong results on average, but doesn't adequately discuss where the parser fails or why. For example, the parser cannot handle relative clauses (Section 6.2) due to not including the NOUN → VERB head rule. The paper states this exclusion was made to "make the hierarchical relation between verbs and nouns less conclusive," but doesn't discuss how often this limitation affects performance in different languages. A more thorough error analysis would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "all treebanks are equally compliant" with UD (Section 6.2), but this seems questionable given known variations in UD annotation quality across languages. Could you provide evidence supporting this claim, or acknowledge that some treebanks might be more consistent than others?

2. In Table 5, UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi. This is surprising given that Hindi is verb-final (SOV), which should make it challenging for a system that biases toward the first verb as the main predicate. Could you explain why UDP performs better than the supervised transfer parser on Hindi?

3. The paper states they exclude Japanese because "the treebank is distributed without word forms," but the UD v1.2 Japanese treebank (UD_Japanese-GSD) does include word forms. Could you clarify why you excluded Japanese and whether you could have included it with the word forms?

4. How much of the English treebank did you use for development (tuning PageRank parameters and head rules)? The paper mentions "during development," but doesn't specify the size or portion of the English data used, which is critical for understanding the "no training" claim.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that UD constraints can be effectively exploited in an unsupervised parsing system, and provides strong evidence for the domain robustness of rule-based approaches. However, the paper needs to be more precise about the "no training" claim and should acknowledge the limitations of their approach more clearly. The comparison to other unsupervised parsers should be expanded, and the discussion of UD treebank quality should be more nuanced.

The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by showing that constraint-based parsing can achieve competitive results without training data, and the domain robustness findings are particularly significant for practical applications. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to the CONLL conference.
