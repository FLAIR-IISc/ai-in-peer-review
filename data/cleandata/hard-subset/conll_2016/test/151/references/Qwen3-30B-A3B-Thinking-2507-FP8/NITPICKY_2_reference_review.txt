

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper presents a dependency parser called UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser) that claims to require no training data and relies solely on UD constraints and explicit POS tags. The system uses personalized PageRank to rank content words and a two-step decoding process to handle content and function words according to UD formalism. The authors evaluate their parser on 32 languages in three setups (gold POS, predicted POS, and a naive POS setup) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A parser that uses UD constraints to guide dependency parsing (this is the core contribution, though not entirely novel)
2. A method for estimating adposition direction from test data (a minor but practical contribution)
3. A comparison of unsupervised parsing with supervised transfer approaches across multiple languages (not a significant contribution, as similar comparisons have been done before)

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a clear comparison of unsupervised parsing with supervised transfer approaches across multiple languages.
2. The evaluation across domains shows that their unsupervised parser is more robust to domain changes than a supervised approach, which is a useful finding.
3. The parser is simple and has low computational complexity, which is a practical advantage.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Fundamental contradiction in core claim (Major flaw)
The paper's central claim is that their system "requires no training, and relies solely on explicit part-of-speech (POS) constraints that UD imposes." However, the authors explicitly state: "We have used the English treebank during development to assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters (Sec. 3.1) and function-word directionality (Sec. 3.2)." This is a severe contradiction. The authors used training data (English treebank) to tune parameters and assess head rules, directly contradicting their main claim of "no training." This undermines the fundamental premise of the paper. A parser that requires language-specific development data cannot be considered truly unsupervised.

### 2. Incorrect average difference calculation (Critical error)
The paper claims "UDPG is remarkably close to the supervised MSDG system, with an average difference of 6.4%, even outperforming MSDG on one language (Hindi)." However, the numbers in Table 5 show that the average UAS for UDPG is 63.9 and for MSDG is 61.2, resulting in a difference of 2.7% (63.9 - 61.2 = 2.7), not 6.4%. This is a significant error that misrepresents the paper's results. The discrepancy is substantial (2.7% vs. 6.4%) and makes the paper's claims about performance inaccurate.

### 3. Unclear error propagation calculation (Unverifiable claim)
The paper states: "the error propagation is 0.37, i.e each POS error causes 0.37 additional dependency errors. In contrast, for the MSD system this error propagation is 0.46, thus higher." However, they don't provide the values used to calculate this, making it impossible to verify. The formula they provide, "Err. prop. = (E(ParseP )−E(ParseG))/E(POSP ), where E(x) = 1−Accuracy(x)," seems to have a sign error because E(ParseP) - E(ParseG) would be negative (since ParseP is worse than ParseG), but they claim it's positive (0.37). Without the actual values and clear calculation, this claim cannot be verified.

### 4. Inconsistent language count (Minor but noticeable flaw)
The paper claims to evaluate on "32 languages" in the contributions section but states "Out of 33 languages in UD v1.2. We exclude Japanese because the treebank is distributed without word forms and hence we can not provide results on predicted POS." This means they should have evaluated on 32 languages, but they excluded Japanese, so they actually evaluated on 31 languages. The paper should have stated "31 languages" instead of "32 languages" to be accurate.

### 5. Inconsistent citation formatting (Minor flaw)
The references section contains inconsistent citation formats. For example, some entries use "Agić and Ljubešić.,? 2015" while others use "Agić et al\.,? 2015." This inconsistency in the formatting of references is a minor but noticeable flaw in a technical paper.

## Questions to Authors
1. How can you claim your parser requires "no training data" when you explicitly state you used the English treebank during development to tune parameters and assess head rules? This directly contradicts your main claim.

2. Can you clarify the calculation of the 6.4% average difference between UDPG and MSDG? The numbers in Table 5 show a difference of approximately 2.7%, not 6.4%. Please provide the exact calculation.

3. Can you provide the specific values used to calculate the error propagation (0.37 for UDP and 0.46 for MSD) so that we can verify your claim? Please also explain why the formula "Err. prop. = (E(ParseP )−E(ParseG))/E(POSP )" doesn't result in a negative number as expected.

4. Can you clarify the discrepancy between the claimed 32 languages and the actual number after excluding Japanese? The paper states "32 languages" but they exclude Japanese from a total of 33, which should result in 32 languages, not 31.

5. Why did you choose a teleport probability of 0.05 for PageRank? The paper states "we chose this value incrementally in intervals of 0.01 during development until we found the smallest value that guaranteed PR convergence." This is not a standard approach, and the paper doesn't explain why 0.05 is optimal or how it generalizes to other languages.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. The fundamental contradiction between the paper's core claim of "no training" and the actual use of English treebank for development is a severe flaw that undermines the entire premise of the paper. This is not a minor detail but a critical contradiction that invalidates the main contribution. The incorrect average difference calculation (6.4% vs. 2.7%) is also a significant error that misrepresents the results. While the paper presents some interesting observations about domain robustness, these are overshadowed by the fundamental flaws in the paper's core claim and the inaccurate presentation of results. The other minor inconsistencies (language count, citation formatting) further highlight the lack of attention to detail that would be expected in a high-quality publication.
