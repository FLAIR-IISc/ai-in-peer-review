

# Peer Review: Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training

## Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The system uses personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate of a sentence, incorporates UD-specific head rules, and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate their system on 32 languages across three POS setups (gold POS, predicted POS, and naive POS) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. The results show that UDP achieves competitive performance (within 6.4% average difference in UAS) compared to supervised transfer methods while being more robust to domain shifts.

## Main Contributions
1. A rule-based unsupervised dependency parser that leverages UD's specific formal constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as dependents of content words) without requiring any training data
2. A method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data, eliminating the need for language-specific parameters
3. A demonstration that a simple rule-based approach can achieve competitive performance compared to supervised transfer methods while showing greater robustness to domain shifts

## Strengths

1. **Innovative application of UD constraints**: The paper makes a strong case for leveraging UD's specific constraints as a foundation for unsupervised parsing. Unlike previous unsupervised methods that often struggle with cross-lingual consistency, this work demonstrates that by strictly adhering to UD's formalism (particularly the constraint that function words must be dependents of content words), a simple rule-based system can achieve surprisingly good results. The authors convincingly argue that UD's constraints "lend themselves more naturally to a simple and linguistically sound rule-based approach," and their results support this claim by showing that their system outperforms previous unsupervised approaches.

2. **Superior robustness to domain shifts**: This is the paper's strongest contribution. The authors provide compelling evidence that their rule-based system is significantly more robust to domain shifts than data-driven transfer parsers. As shown in Table 6 and Table 7, UDP has substantially lower standard deviations across domains compared to the supervised MSD system (e.g., average standard deviation of 2.5 for UDPG vs. 4.2 for MSDP). This is a major practical advantage, especially for real-world applications where domain shifts are common. The paper's demonstration that UDP outperforms MSD on certain domains (like Italian questions) further strengthens this point.

3. **Competitive performance with minimal resources**: The system achieves results that are remarkably close to supervised transfer methods (only 6.4% average difference in UAS), without requiring any training data or language-specific parameters. This is particularly impressive given that they use the same POS tags for all languages (with no language-specific tuning), making the approach highly suitable for truly low-resource languages. The fact that UDP outperforms the supervised MSD system on Hindi (Table 5) is a notable achievement that deserves emphasis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental nature of the core method**: The paper's core approach (using PageRank to score words and then applying head rules) is not novel. Søgaard (2012b) already proposed a similar method for unsupervised dependency parsing. The authors acknowledge this and identify two key differences: "the usage of PageRank personalization, and of two-step decoding to treat content and function words differently according to the UD formalism." However, these differences appear to be incremental rather than transformative. The PageRank personalization is a minor tweak (giving 5x weight to the main predicate), and the two-step decoding is a straightforward application of UD constraints. The paper would have been stronger if it had more clearly demonstrated why these specific changes make such a significant difference.

2. **Lack of clear separation of contributions**: The paper doesn't adequately separate the contribution of the UD constraints from the PageRank personalization. In the ablation study (Section 6.1), the authors compare UDP to UDPNoPR (which disables PageRank), showing that PageRank contributes 9 UAS points on average. However, they don't compare to a system that uses PageRank but doesn't incorporate UD constraints (e.g., a PageRank-based system using the same head rules as Søgaard (2012b)). Without this comparison, it's difficult to determine how much of the performance gain is specifically due to the UD constraints versus just using PageRank for word scoring.

3. **Limited analysis of why it works**: The paper shows that their system works well, but provides limited analysis of why it works better than other unsupervised methods. For example, they don't analyze which specific UD constraints make the biggest difference, or why the UD formalism leads to better results than other approaches. The paper mentions that UD "lends itself more naturally to a simple and linguistically sound rule-based approach," but doesn't provide evidence or analysis to support this claim. A deeper analysis of the specific constraints that contribute most to the performance would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. In your ablation study (Section 6.1), you compare UDP to UDPNoPR, but you don't compare to a system that uses PageRank but doesn't incorporate UD constraints. How much of your system's success is specifically due to the UD constraints, as opposed to just using PageRank for word scoring?

2. The paper states that UD "prescribes that function words like adpositions, auxiliaries, and determiners are always dependents of content words." However, in UD v1.2, this isn't always strictly enforced (e.g., there are cases where determiners can be heads in some languages). How did you handle these edge cases in your head rules? Did you modify the rules to accommodate these exceptions?

3. The paper claims to estimate adposition direction "directly on test data" without needing language-specific parameters, but the method requires a minimum of 10-15 sentences to converge. How would the system handle very short documents (e.g., tweets, search queries) where this minimum might not be met? Would the system fall back to a default direction, and if so, how would that affect performance?

## Overall Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a well-executed, practical approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that leverages the Universal Dependencies formalism in a novel way. The key contribution is demonstrating that a simple rule-based system, when properly aligned with UD's constraints, can achieve competitive performance with supervised methods while being significantly more robust to domain shifts. While the core method isn't entirely novel, the application to UD and the resulting performance gains make this a valuable contribution to the field. The evaluation is thorough, covering 32 languages and multiple POS scenarios, and the results clearly demonstrate the advantages of the approach. The paper's focus on practical, low-resource applicability is particularly valuable for the field.
