- Summary: UDP is an unsupervised UD-constrained parser that assigns heads via personalized PageRank for content words and UD rules for function words, estimating adposition direction from test data and evaluated on 32 languages across three POS scenarios.
- Contribution 1: A rule-based parser that exploits UD constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) without training data.
- Contribution 2: Novel use of personalized PageRank to identify main predicates, improving parsing accuracy.
- Contribution 3: Estimation of adposition attachment direction directly from test data, removing language-specific parameters.
- Strength 1: Effectively leverages UD's hard constraints to build a simple, constraint-driven parser valuable for low-resource languages.
- Strength 2: Strong empirical results across 32 languages and POS scenarios, competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer (MSD) and more stable across domain shifts.
- Strength 3: Ablation study shows personalized PageRank contributes ~9 UAS points over a reading-order baseline.
- Strength 4: Practical relevance for low-resource scenarios; small UAS drop with predicted POS tags indicates robustness.
- Weakness 1: Insufficient analysis of limitations, notably the inability to handle relative clauses and lack of quantification of its impact.
- Weakness 2: Overly optimistic interpretation of the naive POS (UDPN) scenario, which is an artificial and crude setup.
- Weakness 3: Missing comparisons to prior unsupervised parsers (e.g., Søgaard 2012b, Klein & Manning 2004, Spitkovsky et al. 2010).
- Weakness 4: Limited analysis of language-specific performance differences and linguistic factors affecting UDP's results.
- Question 1: Clarify the apparent discrepancy where the review claims UDP beats MSD on Hindi but Table 5 shows MSDG > UDPG.
- Question 2: Provide statistics on relative clauses in UD treebanks and quantify parser failures and the potential impact of adding the NOUN→VERB rule.
- Question 3: Explain the extent of English-based development (use of the English treebank) and how that "training" affects performance on typologically different languages.
- Question 4: Give concrete domain-shift examples where UDP outperforms and explain the reasons for its robustness.
- Question 5: Explain the exclusion of Japanese due to missing word forms and whether UDP would work if word forms were available.
- Overall assessment: Strong, well-written paper with a meaningful unsupervised parsing contribution and convincing robustness results; recommend acceptance despite evaluation and analysis gaps.
