

# Review for CONLL

## Summary
The paper presents "UDP" (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that claims to require no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints. The system uses POS tags to guide dependency relations according to UD rules, employs personalized PageRank to identify main predicates, estimates adposition direction from test data, and uses a two-step decoding process to enforce UD's leaf constraint for function words. The authors claim competitive performance compared to supervised transfer approaches while being more robust to domain shifts.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A rule-based dependency parser that exploits UD constraints without training data
2. A method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data
3. A two-step decoding process that enforces UD's leaf constraint for function words

## Strengths

1. **Competitive performance with established baselines**: The results show UDPG (with gold POS) achieves UAS scores very close to the supervised MSDG (multi-source delexicalized parser), with an average difference of only 6.4%. This is impressive for an unsupervised approach, especially since UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi (55.3 vs. 54.8 UAS). The paper provides strong evidence that their method can achieve near-supervised performance without training data.

2. **Demonstrated robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides compelling evidence that UDP is more robust to domain changes than supervised approaches. The standard deviation across domains (Table 6) is significantly lower for UDP than for MSD, confirming earlier findings that rule-based parsers can be more stable to domain shifts. This is a valuable practical contribution for real-world applications.

3. **Clear documentation of UD constraints utilization**: The paper correctly identifies that UD's formalism (e.g., function words as leaves) provides constraints that can be exploited for rule-based parsing, which is a reasonable and well-founded approach rather than a novel method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of proper comparison to established unsupervised parsers**: The paper dismisses Klein & Manning (2004) as "outperformed by a system similar to ours," but fails to provide a direct comparison to the most relevant unsupervised parser in the literature (Søgaard 2012b), which is the paper they explicitly build upon. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether their approach represents a meaningful advancement over existing unsupervised methods.

2. **Misleading "no training" claim**: The paper states that their system "requires no training," yet they used the English treebank during development to assess head rules and tune PageRank parameters. This is a form of training, and the paper should be more precise about the nature of their development process. The claim of "no training" is overstated and potentially misleading.

3. **Insufficient validation of core innovation**: The paper's key innovation appears to be the personalized PageRank approach, but the ablation study (Section 6.1) only compares to a baseline that ranks words by reading order (UDPNoPR), not to standard unsupervised parsing methods that might use different scoring approaches. Without comparing to established unsupervised methods, it's impossible to determine whether the PageRank personalization provides a meaningful improvement.

4. **Inadequate evaluation of critical limitations**: The paper acknowledges that punctuation (PUNCT) has very low UAS (21% average), yet provides no discussion of how this limitation might impact real-world applications or how it might be addressed. Similarly, the exclusion of the NOUN→VERB head rule (to avoid ambiguous verb-noun relations) is noted but not analyzed for its impact on overall performance.

5. **Overstated significance of naive POS scenario**: The paper evaluates a "naive" POS scenario (UDPN) where all words are tagged as either CONTENT or FUNCTION based on word frequency, but acknowledges this is "too crude." Despite this, they present the results as meaningful, which is misleading. A more realistic evaluation of performance with lower-quality POS tags would be more valuable.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you not include a direct comparison to Søgaard (2012b), which is explicitly mentioned as a basis for your work, in your main evaluation? This is a critical omission given that Søgaard's work is the most relevant unsupervised parser in the literature.

2. How much of your performance gain comes from the English development data you used? The paper states you used English treebank for development, but doesn't quantify how much this affects the results on other languages.

3. The paper claims "no training" but used English data for development. How would you reframe your claims to be more precise about the development process?

4. Your naive POS scenario (UDPN) is acknowledged as "too crude." Can you provide a more realistic evaluation of performance with a 85-90% accurate POS tagger, rather than the 94.1% accuracy of TnT?

5. The paper notes that punctuation has very low UAS (21%), yet it's included in the evaluation. How would you improve the handling of punctuation in your system, and what impact would this have on overall performance?

## Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting approach that leverages UD constraints, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that it represents a meaningful advancement over established unsupervised parsing methods. The lack of proper comparison to relevant unsupervised baselines, the misleading "no training" claim, and insufficient validation of the core methodological innovation are significant weaknesses that undermine the paper's contribution. The results are interesting but not compelling enough to overcome these methodological shortcomings. The authors should address these issues before the work can be considered for publication.
