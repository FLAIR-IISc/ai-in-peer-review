- Summary: UDP is a training-free UD-based dependency parser using personalized PageRank, UD constraints, estimated adposition direction, and two-step decoding; evaluated on 32 languages and shown competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer (MSD) parser and more robust to domain shifts.

- Contribution 1: Shows a completely unsupervised parser can approach supervised transfer performance (within 6.4% UAS of MSDG with gold POS) and outperforms MSDG on Hindi.

- Contribution 2: Provides a thorough analysis demonstrating UDP's robustness to domain shifts and POS errors, with smaller UAS drops and lower cross-domain variance than MSD.

- Contribution 3: Establishes a new, fully training-free baseline for dependency parsing that uses no language-specific parameters, useful for low-resource languages.

- Strength 1: Practical impact — a simple rule-based method attains results comparable to supervised transfer, valuable where gold POS or training data are unavailable.

- Strength 2: Rigorous cross-domain evaluation showing markedly lower standard deviation across domains versus supervised baselines.

- Strength 3: Clear, well-explained methodology (PageRank, head-direction estimation, two-step decoding) with illustrative examples and algorithms.

- Strength 4: Strong, relevant baseline comparison to a state-of-the-art multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

- Strength 5: Practical contribution of releasing the parser and test sets and detailed analysis of POS and domain effects.

- Weakness 1: Limited methodological novelty since core techniques (PageRank, two-step decoding) have prior use; novelty mainly in UD adaptation.

- Weakness 2: Evaluation uses outdated UD v1.2 instead of UD v2.0, reducing relevance and leaving open effects on newer treebanks.

- Weakness 3: Missing comparisons to other unsupervised parsers (e.g., prior work by Søgaard and others) that could be stronger baselines.

- Weakness 4: Insufficient analysis of failure cases (e.g., relative clause handling) and their prevalence/impact across languages.

- Weakness 5: Coverage limited to 32 languages (excluding many UD languages like Japanese), raising questions about generalization.

- Question 1: Please detail why UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi and how Hindi results compare to languages where UDP lags.

- Question 2: How does estimating adposition direction from test data reconcile with the claim of being fully language-agnostic, and can this be made language-independent?

- Question 3: How frequent are relative clauses in UD treebanks and how much do the inability to model them affect overall performance?

- Question 4: How would results change on UD v2.0, especially for languages where UDP performed poorly?

- Question 5: Why does UDP perform better than English on 24/32 languages — development-data bias or linguistic explanations?

- Overall Recommendation: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to include comparisons to recent unsupervised parsers and evaluation on newer UD versions.
