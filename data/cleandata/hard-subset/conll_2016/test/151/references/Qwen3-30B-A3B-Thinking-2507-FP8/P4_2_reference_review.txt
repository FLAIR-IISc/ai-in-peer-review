

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. The system relies solely on explicit part-of-speech (POS) constraints imposed by the UD formalism, including single-rooted trees and function words (adpositions, auxiliaries, determiners) always being dependents of content words. The parser uses personalized PageRank to rank content words for main predicate identification and a two-step decoding algorithm that ensures function words are leaves (as required by UD) while properly handling head direction for different POS categories. The authors evaluate the system on 32 languages across three POS setups (gold POS, predicted POS, and a naive two-POS setup) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A rule-based dependency parser that requires no training data and strictly adheres to UD constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves).
2. A personalized PageRank approach for ranking content words to identify the main predicate, which significantly improves performance over a baseline without ranking.
3. A two-step decoding algorithm that properly handles head direction for different POS categories according to UD conventions.

## Strengths
1. **Competitive performance with supervised baselines**: The most compelling strength is that UDP achieves remarkably close performance to a supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), with an average UAS difference of only 6.4% (Table 5). The system even outperforms MSD on Hindi (55.3% vs 54.8% UAS), which is a significant result demonstrating that a purely rule-based approach can match data-driven methods when properly constrained by UD.

2. **Robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides strong evidence that the rule-based approach is more robust to domain shifts than data-driven methods. Table 6 shows UDP has a much lower standard deviation across domains (2.5 for UDPG vs 4.2 for MSDG), confirming that the parser's general constraints make it less sensitive to sampling bias in training data. This is a critical practical advantage for real-world applications.

3. **Technical soundness and clarity**: The paper provides clear, detailed explanations of the system components (PageRank setup, head direction estimation, two-step decoding). The algorithm in Figure 1 is well-structured and easy to follow. The authors demonstrate thorough understanding of the UD formalism and how to leverage its constraints for parsing.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty of core approach**: While the system is well-executed, the core approach (rule-based parsing with PageRank) builds directly on Søgaard (2012b) with incremental improvements to adapt to UD constraints. The PageRank personalization and two-step decoding are not fundamentally novel contributions but rather adaptations of existing techniques to a new formalism. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish these adaptations from the broader field of rule-aided unsupervised parsing.

2. **Heavy dependence on POS quality**: The parser's performance is highly dependent on POS tag quality. The UAS drops significantly (17.2% average decrease) when moving from gold POS (UDPG) to the naive two-POS tag setup (UDPN). This severely limits the practical applicability of the system, as the naive POS setup (which approximates a lower bound) shows the most dramatic performance drop. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't adequately address how this limitation impacts real-world deployment.

3. **Insufficient evaluation of UD-specific compliance**: The paper claims the system "adapts dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations" as a key contribution, but doesn't provide evidence that this adaptation actually leads to more UD-compliant parses. The evaluation focuses on standard UAS metrics rather than UD-specific metrics or manual inspection of parses to verify compliance with UD constraints like function word leaf status.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims the parser "requires no training data" but mentions using the English treebank during development to assess head rules and tune PageRank parameters. How much development data was used, and how does this impact the "no training" claim? Is the English treebank considered part of the training data for the system?

2. The adposition direction estimation is done directly on test data, but the paper doesn't specify the minimum amount of test data required for reliable estimation. What is the minimum number of sentences needed for accurate adposition direction estimation across languages with varying data availability?

3. The paper mentions limitations in handling multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names. How would these limitations specifically affect the parser's performance on languages with high proportions of these structures (e.g., German, which has many compound nouns and multiword expressions)?
