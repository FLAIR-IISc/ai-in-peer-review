

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper presents a dependency parser called UDP that claims to require no training and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints. The main contributions claimed by the authors are: (a) adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, (b) incorporating the UD restriction of function words being leaves, (c) using personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, and (d) making the system completely free of language-specific parameters by estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data.

However, upon careful examination, I must note that the paper's main claim of being "unsupervised" is fundamentally flawed, as the system requires POS tags that typically need training data to obtain. The authors also tuned parameters on English development data, which undermines their language-agnostic claim.

## Strengths

1. **Clear and well-structured presentation of the method**: The paper presents a rule-based approach that is logically organized and the two-step decoding algorithm is clearly explained. The use of personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate is a novel application of an existing technique.

2. **Competitive performance**: The system achieves results that are remarkably close to supervised transfer parsers (only 6.4% average difference), which is impressive for a system with no training data. The results on the predicted POS setup (UDPP) showing only a 2.2% drop compared to supervised MSDG is particularly noteworthy.

3. **Robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides strong evidence that the rule-based UDP system is more resilient to domain changes than data-driven transfer parsers, with significantly lower standard deviations across domains. This is an important contribution to the understanding of parsing robustness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Fundamental misrepresentation of the system as "unsupervised"**: The paper repeatedly claims the system is "unsupervised" while requiring POS tags that are typically obtained from a trained POS tagger. The authors use TnT (a statistical POS tagger) trained on each language's training data, which means the overall pipeline requires training data. The paper states: "Our system is a dependency parser that requires no training, and relies solely on explicit part-of-speech (POS) constraints that UD imposes." This is incorrect - the system requires POS tags, which themselves require training data. The authors should clarify that their parser is unsupervised *in the sense that it doesn't require training data for the parser itself*, but it does require POS tags, which typically require training. This misrepresentation of the system's nature is a critical flaw that undermines the paper's main contribution.

2. **Parameter tuning on English development data**: The paper states: "We have used the English treebank during development to assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters (Sec. 3.1) and function-word directionality (Sec. 3.2)." This means the system is biased toward English, contradicting the claim of being language-agnostic. The authors claim to have a "completely free of language-specific parameters" system, but they've tuned parameters on English data, which is a language-specific parameter. This fundamentally undermines the paper's main claim of being language-agnostic.

3. **Significant typos and inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous technical errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading. For example:
   - In Table 3 description: "We can see e.g. that the four incoming edges for the verb 'come' from the two nouns, plus from the adverb 'also' and the pronoun 'They'." The example sentence is "They also had a special connection to some extremists" - the verb is "had," not "come."
   - The sentence "Out of 33 languages in UD v1.2. We exclude Japanese because..." is a sentence fragment.
   - "Personalization: 1 1 5 1 1 1 1 1 1" in Table 2 is not clearly explained (why does "had" get 5?).
   - The notation is inconsistent: "UDPP" is defined as "UDP with predicted POS" but then "UDPN" is used for a different system ("PR with naive content-function POS"), which is confusing.

4. **Including punctuation in evaluation**: The paper states: "We have not excluded punctuation from the evaluation." In dependency parsing, punctuation is typically excluded from evaluation as it's not considered part of the main syntactic structure. Including punctuation artificially inflates the scores and makes the results less comparable to other work. The paper should clarify why they're including punctuation, especially since they note "the UAS for the PUNCT is low (an average of 21%, standard deviation of 9.6), even lower than the otherwise problematic CONJ."

5. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art unsupervised parsers**: The paper compares to a baseline rule-based parser and a supervised transfer parser, but doesn't compare to other unsupervised parsers like those by Klein and Manning (2004) or Spitkovsky et al. (2010). Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether their approach is truly competitive with the state of the art in unsupervised parsing.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters" by estimating adposition direction on test data. However, they must estimate this for each language, which is a language-specific parameter. Please clarify how this estimation process is truly language-agnostic.

2. The paper states: "We have used the English treebank during development to assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters (Sec. 3.1) and function-word directionality (Sec. 3.2)." How does this English bias affect performance on other languages? The paper shows UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi, but how much of this is due to English tuning?

3. The paper includes punctuation in the evaluation. Why was this decision made, and how does it affect the comparability of results with other dependency parsing work?

4. The paper claims to be "unsupervised" but requires POS tags. What would be the performance of UDP if it were truly unsupervised (i.e., without any POS tags)? How does the performance degrade without POS tags?

## Final Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting rule-based approach to dependency parsing that achieves competitive results, the fundamental misrepresentation of the system as "unsupervised" and the parameter tuning on English development data are serious flaws that undermine the paper's main claims. The numerous typos and inconsistencies further suggest a lack of attention to detail, which is critical for a CoNLL paper. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for acceptance.
