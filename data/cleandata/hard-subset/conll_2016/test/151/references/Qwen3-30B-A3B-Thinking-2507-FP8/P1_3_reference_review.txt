

# Peer Review: Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training

## Summary
This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The system relies solely on UD constraints, using personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate and UD-specific head rules to build dependency trees. The authors claim their approach achieves competitive performance compared to supervised transfer parsers while being more robust to domain shifts.

## Main Contributions
1. Demonstrating that a simple rule-based approach leveraging UD constraints can achieve competitive performance without any training data
2. Providing a comprehensive analysis of robustness to domain shifts across 32 languages
3. Creating a parser that requires no training data and is applicable across a wide range of languages

## Strengths
1. **Significant practical contribution**: The paper addresses a critical gap in cross-lingual parsing by presenting a method that requires no training data and works across diverse languages. This is particularly valuable for truly low-resource languages where training data is scarce. The authors show UDP achieves performance within 6.4% of a supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), which is remarkable for an unsupervised system.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation**: The evaluation is exceptionally thorough, covering 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive) and multiple domains. The analysis of domain robustness is particularly compelling, with UDP showing significantly lower standard deviations across domains (1.8-2.5) compared to MSD (4.2-4.5), confirming that rule-based approaches are less sensitive to domain shifts.

3. **Clear and elegant methodology**: The paper provides an excellent explanation of the approach, with a detailed example (Section 4) that clearly demonstrates how the algorithm works. The two-step decoding process (content words first, then function words) effectively enforces UD's constraint that function words must be leaves.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty**: While the approach is well-executed, the core idea of using PageRank for dependency parsing was already proposed by Søgaard (2012b), and the head rules are largely based on UD conventions. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contribution from prior work, making it difficult to assess what's truly new. The authors acknowledge building on Søgaard's work but don't clearly articulate how their specific adaptations (personalized PageRank, two-step decoding) represent significant advances.

2. **Misleading claim about language independence**: The paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters," but this is inaccurate. The authors use English development data to determine attachment directions for function words (e.g., "AUX, DET, and SCONJ are right-attaching"), which introduces language-specific bias. This undermines their central claim of being truly language-agnostic.

3. **Insufficient error analysis**: The paper identifies some limitations (e.g., inability to handle relative clauses due to excluding NOUN → VERB head rules), but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why the system performs poorly on certain languages (e.g., Arabic and Tamil with 26-27% UAS). Without understanding these failure modes, it's difficult to assess the system's limitations or potential for improvement.

4. **Development data bias**: The authors used the English treebank during development to tune parameters and determine head rules. This creates a potential English bias in the system, which is particularly problematic given the paper's claim of cross-lingual applicability. The fact that UDP performs better than English on 24 of 32 languages is impressive but doesn't fully overcome this concern.

## Questions to Authors
1. How did you determine the attachment direction for function words (e.g., DET as right-attaching) on English development data? Could this introduce bias for languages with different attachment patterns, and how did you verify this?

2. The paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters," yet you used English data to determine attachment directions. How does this align with your claim, and what would need to change to make the system truly language-agnostic?

3. The paper states that the NOUN → VERB head rule was excluded to "make the hierarchical relation between verbs and nouns less conclusive." How would including this rule affect performance across different languages, and why was this trade-off deemed necessary?

4. The average POS accuracy for TnT is 94.1%, but the error propagation rate from POS to dependency is 0.37 for UDP versus 0.46 for MSD. Could you elaborate on how this calculation was performed and whether this suggests a fundamental advantage of your approach to error propagation?

## Overall Assessment
This is a well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that UD constraints can enable effective unsupervised parsing across multiple languages. The empirical results are strong, particularly regarding domain robustness. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the paper's central claim of being "completely free of language-specific parameters" is undermined by the use of English development data to determine attachment directions. With these issues addressed, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the novelty and address the language-specific parameter issue. The paper's practical contribution and strong empirical evaluation outweigh the limitations, but the authors need to be more precise in describing their contributions and methodology.
