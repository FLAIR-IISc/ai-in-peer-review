

# Peer Review: "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on UD constraints and part-of-speech tags. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A novel unsupervised dependency parsing approach that strictly adheres to UD's formalism constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as dependents, etc.) without any language-specific training.
2. A practical method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data, eliminating the need for language-specific parameters.
3. A personalized PageRank approach for identifying the main predicate (root) of a sentence, significantly improving parsing performance over simpler ranking methods.

## Strengths

1. **Innovative use of UD constraints for unsupervised parsing**: The paper makes a compelling argument for why UD's formalism constraints can be leveraged to create a rule-based parser without training data. By strictly adhering to UD's constraints (e.g., function words as leaves), the authors have developed a parser that achieves remarkable performance without any language-specific training. This represents a fresh perspective that moves beyond traditional grammar induction approaches, which have struggled to match supervised methods. The evaluation across 32 languages with gold POS tags shows UDPG (UDP with gold POS) is remarkably close to the supervised MSDG system (only 6.4% average difference), which is a significant achievement.

2. **Rigorous evaluation of domain robustness**: The paper's demonstration of UDP's robustness to domain shifts (Table 6) is particularly compelling and addresses a critical limitation of most data-driven approaches. The authors show that UDP has a much lower standard deviation across domains compared to MSD, with the difference being statistically significant. This finding is important because it confirms earlier work by Plank and van Noord (2010) and suggests that rule-based parsers can be more stable to domain shifts than data-driven methods. The detailed domain analysis across Bulgarian, Croatian, and Italian (Table 7) provides valuable insights into which domains are most challenging for dependency parsing.

3. **Clear implementation and open-source release**: The authors have implemented their parser and made it available, which is valuable for the community. The paper provides clear descriptions of the algorithm and evaluation setup, making it easy for others to replicate and build upon the work. The inclusion of the domain shift analysis is particularly useful for researchers interested in cross-lingual and domain-adaptive parsing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to recent unsupervised parsers**: The paper does not compare its approach to several recent unsupervised parsers that have achieved strong performance, such as those using neural networks or more sophisticated grammar induction techniques. The paper mentions "Klein and Manning (2004)" as representative of unsupervised parsing, but this is outdated. A comparison to more recent unsupervised parsers (e.g., those by Spitkovsky et al. or Gillenwater et al.) would strengthen the claim that UDP is competitive with supervised methods. Without this comparison, it's unclear how UDP stacks up against the state-of-the-art in unsupervised parsing.

2. **Overstated claim about "no training"**: The paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters" and "requires no training," but the authors used the English treebank during development to assess head rules and tune PageRank parameters. This is a form of language-specific tuning that somewhat contradicts the paper's main claim. The paper should more clearly acknowledge this development process and how it might affect the parser's generalizability to truly low-resource languages. The authors' statement that "We have used the English treebank during development" (Section 3) should be more prominently discussed in the main text.

3. **Limited error analysis**: While the paper provides some error analysis (Section 6), it doesn't deeply explore the specific types of errors made by UDP. For instance, it would be valuable to know whether UDP's errors are primarily due to incorrect POS tagging, incorrect head direction estimation, or issues with the UD constraints themselves. Understanding where the parser fails would help identify directions for improvement. The paper's statement that "the system cannot infer relative clauses" (Section 6.2) is a limitation that should be more thoroughly discussed in terms of its impact on overall performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to require "no training," but the authors used the English treebank for development. How would the parser perform on a language for which no treebank was available during development? Could the parser be applied to truly low-resource languages without any language-specific tuning?

2. The paper compares UDP to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), but doesn't compare to more recent supervised parsers like those by Kondrak et al. or other neural approaches. Why were these not included in the comparison? How would UDP perform against these state-of-the-art supervised parsers?

3. In Section 3.2, the authors estimate adposition direction from test data. How sensitive is the parser to the number of sentences used for this estimation? Is there a minimum number of sentences required for reliable estimation, and how would this affect the parser's performance on very short documents?
