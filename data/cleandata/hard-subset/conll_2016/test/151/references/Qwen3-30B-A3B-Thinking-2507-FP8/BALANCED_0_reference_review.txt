

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on explicit part-of-speech (POS) constraints from the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The system uses UD-specific head rules, personalized PageRank for ranking content words, and a two-step decoding process that ensures function words are leaves.

The main contributions of this work, ordered by strength, are:

1. **A completely unsupervised dependency parser that leverages UD constraints** - The work demonstrates that the specific constraints of the UD formalism (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) can be directly exploited to build a competitive unsupervised parser, which is a fresh perspective compared to existing work that often treats UD as just another treebank format.

2. **A robust system with competitive performance** - The parser achieves results remarkably close to supervised multi-source delexicalized parsers (MSD) across 32 languages (only 6.4% average difference in UAS), while being more robust to domain shifts.

3. **Demonstrated resilience to POS errors** - The system shows significantly less performance degradation when using predicted POS tags (only 2.2% drop) compared to the supervised baseline (2.7% drop), highlighting its practical utility.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation across diverse languages and scenarios**: The evaluation on 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive POS) is comprehensive and well-designed. The results showing UDPG (gold POS) is only 6.4% behind MSDG (supervised transfer parser) is impressive for an unsupervised approach. The paper effectively demonstrates that UDP's performance is competitive with state-of-the-art supervised methods, which is a significant contribution to unsupervised parsing research.

2. **Practical relevance and simplicity**: The system requires no training data, no language-specific parameters, and can run with predicted POS tags (94.1% accuracy) with minimal performance drop (2.2%). This makes it highly practical for real-world applications in low-resource settings where gold POS is unavailable. The paper demonstrates a clear path to deployment without the need for extensive resources.

3. **Demonstrated robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides compelling evidence that UDP is more resilient to domain changes than data-driven approaches. The cross-domain evaluation (Table 7) shows UDP has significantly lower standard deviation across domains than MSD (2.5 vs. 4.2), confirming the claim that rule-based approaches can be more stable to domain shift. This is an important practical advantage that the paper effectively supports with detailed analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty in the core approach**: While the application of UD constraints is novel, the core parsing technique (using head rules and PageRank) builds directly on Søgaard (2012b) with only minor modifications (personalized PageRank and two-step decoding). The paper claims "our method goes beyond the existing work" but doesn't convincingly demonstrate how these modifications are significantly different or better than prior work. The novelty appears incremental rather than transformative, which is a significant limitation for a conference like CoNLL.

2. **Insufficient analysis of failure modes**: The paper mentions that the system cannot infer relative clauses because it excludes the NOUN→VERB head rule, but provides no detailed analysis of this limitation or its impact on overall performance. A more thorough error analysis, including examples of where the parser fails and why, would strengthen the paper's contribution and help readers understand the system's limitations.

3. **Misleading claim about "no training"**: The paper states the system requires "no training data" but actually uses the English treebank during development to tune PageRank parameters and assess head rules. This is a significant caveat that isn't sufficiently emphasized. While using a small amount of development data is common practice, the paper should be more explicit about this dependency to avoid misleading readers about the system's unsupervised nature.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the system requires "no training data," but uses the English treebank during development for parameter tuning and head rule assessment. Could you clarify the exact amount of development data used (number of sentences, treebank size) and explain why this isn't considered a form of training? How would the system perform if this development data was not available?

2. The paper claims UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (Table 5), but the average difference between UDPG and MSDG is 6.4%, with UDPG outperforming MSDG on only one language (Hindi). Given that this is presented as a key contribution, could you provide more detailed analysis of why UDP works particularly well on Hindi compared to other languages?

3. The paper attributes UDP's robustness to domain shifts to "being developed to satisfy a set of general properties of the UD syntactic formalism." Could you elaborate on how exactly the UD constraints contribute to this robustness, and whether this would hold for other formalisms that have similar constraints (e.g., the Penn Treebank)?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection** for this paper. While the work demonstrates a practical, simple unsupervised parser that achieves competitive results with minimal resources, the novelty is limited and the core approach is largely incremental. The paper would benefit from more thorough analysis of failure modes, clearer distinction from prior work, and better handling of the "no training" claim. If these weaknesses were addressed in a revised version, the paper would be significantly stronger.

However, the work has clear practical value and demonstrates an important insight about leveraging UD constraints. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above in a revised submission, particularly regarding the novelty of their approach and the "no training" claim. With these improvements, the paper could be a strong contribution to the field.
