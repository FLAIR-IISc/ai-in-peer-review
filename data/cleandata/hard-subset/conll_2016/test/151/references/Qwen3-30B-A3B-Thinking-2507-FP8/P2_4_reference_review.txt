

# Review: Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The authors leverage UD's formal constraints—particularly the restriction that function words (adpositions, auxiliaries, determiners) are always dependents of content words—to build a rule-based system. The key contributions are: (1) a completely unsupervised parser that exploits UD constraints, (2) a method to estimate adposition direction directly from test data, and (3) a personalized PageRank approach for identifying main predicates.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling application of UD formal constraints**: The paper makes a strong case for why UD's formal constraints (unlike previous formalisms) enable a rule-based approach to cross-lingual parsing. Previous unsupervised parsers didn't leverage UD's specific constraints, making this a significant insight. The paper correctly identifies that UD's restriction on function words being leaves is a key innovation that can be exploited for unsupervised parsing.

2. **Competitive performance without training**: The results are impressive—UDPG (gold POS) achieves 63.9% UAS on average, only 6.4% below the supervised MSDG system (69.5% UAS). This is remarkable for a completely unsupervised system, and the fact that UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi (55.3% vs. 55.3%—the paper claims outperformance, but Table 5 shows a tie) demonstrates robustness across diverse language structures.

3. **Superior domain robustness**: The paper provides compelling evidence that the rule-based approach is significantly more robust to domain shifts than data-driven methods. UDP has a much lower standard deviation across domains (Table 6) than the supervised MSD system (2.5 vs. 4.2 average standard deviation). This is a major practical advantage for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

4. **Practical simplicity and transparency**: The approach is straightforward, requires no training data, and has minimal parameters. This makes it particularly valuable for low-resource languages where training data is scarce. The code and evaluation data are openly available, enhancing reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in technical approach**: While the application of UD constraints is novel, the core technical approach (PageRank for ranking, two-step decoding) is largely based on Søgaard (2012b). The paper claims to "go beyond" previous work but doesn't sufficiently clarify how their personalized PageRank and two-step decoding are fundamentally different. The key innovation is the application to UD constraints, not the PageRank technique itself, which is underemphasized.

2. **Overstated "no training" claim**: The paper states the system requires "no training" but uses English treebank data during development to tune parameters (Section 3.1). This is a significant limitation that the paper doesn't adequately address. The claim of "no training" is technically inaccurate, as the English data was used for parameter tuning.

3. **Insufficient error analysis**: The paper provides limited error analysis. For example, they note that the system cannot infer relative clauses due to excluding the NOUN→VERB head rule, but don't quantify how much this affects overall performance or provide examples. A more thorough analysis would strengthen their claims about the system's limitations.

4. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to 32 languages from UD v1.2. The paper doesn't address how the approach would perform with UD v2.0 or languages with different structural properties. The exclusion of Japanese due to missing word forms (note 2) also limits the scope.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the system requires "no training," but uses English treebank data during development for tuning parameters. Can you clarify the exact role of English data in the development process, and how this affects the "no training" claim?

2. The paper states that UDP outperforms MSDG on Hindi (Table 5 shows 55.3% vs. 55.3%—a tie). Can you clarify this claim and provide evidence of actual outperformance?

3. The direction for some function words (AUX, DET, SCONJ) is determined on English dev data. How would this approach handle languages where these function words have different attachment patterns, and how would the system adapt to such cases without additional language-specific tuning?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a compelling and practical approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that leverages UD's formal constraints. The performance is impressive for a completely unsupervised system, and the demonstrated robustness to domain shifts is a significant practical advantage. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the "no training" claim is technically inaccurate due to the use of English development data. The paper would be strengthened by a clearer articulation of the key innovation (application of UD constraints) rather than the PageRank technique, and by addressing the limitations in the evaluation scope and error analysis.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the "no training" claim and better articulate the true novelty of the work. The paper makes a valuable contribution to unsupervised parsing that is particularly relevant for low-resource languages and cross-lingual applications.
