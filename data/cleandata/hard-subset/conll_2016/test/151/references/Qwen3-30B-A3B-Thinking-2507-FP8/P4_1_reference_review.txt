

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a rule-based dependency parser (UDP) that requires no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and part-of-speech (POS) tags. The system leverages UD's specific formalism to create a parsing approach that doesn't require language-specific parameters or training data. The authors evaluate their system on 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and naive POS) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) and a rule-based baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. A completely unsupervised dependency parser that achieves competitive performance with supervised cross-lingual transfer parsers by leveraging UD constraints, demonstrating that a rule-based approach can match data-driven methods when properly aligned with the formalism.
2. A method for estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data, avoiding language-specific parameters and enabling the parser to adapt to different languages without retraining.
3. Demonstrating that a rule-based system following UD constraints is more robust to domain changes than data-driven approaches, as evidenced by lower standard deviations across different domains.

## Strengths

1. **Strong technical justification for leveraging UD constraints**: The paper provides a compelling argument for why UD's formalism enables a simpler and more linguistically sound rule-based approach. The authors clearly demonstrate how UD's specific constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) enable a more principled parsing strategy than previous approaches. This is not just a theoretical point but is validated through extensive experiments showing the parser's competitive performance without any training data.

2. **Competitive performance with minimal assumptions**: The system achieves remarkably close performance to supervised transfer methods (within 6.4% average UAS difference) across 32 languages, with the parser even outperforming the supervised baseline on Hindi. This demonstrates that the authors' approach is not just theoretically sound but practically effective. The fact that the system performs well across such a diverse language set (including non-Indo-European languages) is particularly impressive for an unsupervised approach.

3. **Demonstrated robustness to domain changes**: The paper provides compelling evidence that the rule-based UDP system is more robust to domain shifts than data-driven methods. The lower standard deviation across domains (as shown in Tables 6 and 7) is a significant practical contribution, especially for real-world applications where domain shifts are common. This aligns with the authors' claim that "systems with fewer parameters are less likely to be overfit for a certain dataset," providing strong empirical evidence for this claim.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core approach**: While the paper makes a good case for adapting existing techniques to UD constraints, the core methodology (rule-based parsing with head rules) is not novel. The authors acknowledge building on Søgaard (2012b), and the main contributions are incremental adaptations rather than fundamentally new approaches. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent unsupervised parsing work to better establish the novelty.

2. **Performance heavily dependent on POS quality**: The system's performance drops significantly when using predicted POS tags (2.2% UAS drop compared to gold POS) and dramatically when using naive POS tags (UDPN). This is a limitation for real-world applications where POS tagging accuracy may be imperfect. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't sufficiently explore how this impacts practical deployment.

3. **Limited evaluation of linguistic challenges**: The paper doesn't thoroughly evaluate the system's performance on specific linguistic phenomena that might challenge UD constraints, such as languages with complex word order or multiword expressions. The authors acknowledge that "not all v1.2 treebanks have the same level of formalism compliance," but don't provide detailed analysis of where the parser fails on specific languages or constructions.

4. **Insufficient comparison with recent unsupervised parsers**: The paper compares against a rule-based baseline and a supervised transfer system, but doesn't compare with more recent unsupervised parsing approaches that might have made progress in the field. This makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed approach represents a significant advance over the current state of the art in unsupervised parsing.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the system cannot infer relative clauses" due to excluding the NOUN → VERB head rule. Could you provide more specific examples of how this limitation manifests in the parsed trees, and how it might be addressed in future work without violating UD constraints?

2. The paper claims UDP is more robust to domain changes than data-driven approaches, but the domain shift experiments are limited to only 3 languages (Bulgarian, Croatian, Italian, and English). Could you clarify why the authors chose these specific languages for domain analysis, and whether they have plans to evaluate on more languages with available domain splits?

3. The paper demonstrates that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi, but the paper doesn't analyze why this might be the case. Could the authors provide a linguistic analysis of Hindi that explains why the rule-based approach might be particularly well-suited for this language compared to the transfer approach?
