

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a novel unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The core insight is to leverage the specific constraints of the UD formalism itself (e.g., function words must be dependents of content words, trees must be single-rooted) to create a rule-based parsing system. The authors introduce a system called UDP that uses personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate and incorporates UD's constraints through specific head-dependent rules.

The strongest contributions I see from this work (in order of importance):

1. **Novel application of UD constraints to unsupervised parsing**: The paper's most significant contribution is recognizing that the UD formalism provides specific constraints that can be directly exploited for unsupervised parsing. This is a fresh perspective that goes beyond the typical data-driven approach to dependency parsing.

2. **Personalized PageRank for main predicate identification**: The adaptation of PageRank with personalization to identify the main predicate (root of the tree) is a clever, linguistically motivated technique that doesn't require any training data.

3. **Language-agnostic adposition direction estimation**: The ability to estimate whether a language uses prepositions or postpositions directly from test data (with only 10-15 sentences) is a practical and elegant solution that eliminates the need for language-specific parameters.

## Strengths

1. **Truly novel approach to unsupervised parsing**: This is the strongest strength of the paper. While unsupervised parsing is a well-established field, the authors have taken a completely different angle by leveraging the specific constraints of the UD formalism rather than trying to learn from data. This is a bold, fresh perspective that could significantly influence the field. The paper demonstrates that by designing a parser specifically for UD's constraints, you can achieve competitive results without any training data.

2. **Competitive performance with no training data**: The results are impressive - UDPG (with gold POS) is remarkably close to the supervised multi-source delexicalized parser (MSDG), with an average difference of only 6.4%. The fact that it outperforms the supervised baseline on one language (Hindi) is particularly noteworthy and demonstrates the potential of this approach.

3. **Superior robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides compelling evidence that the rule-based UDP system is significantly more robust to domain changes than the data-driven MSD system. This is a critical practical advantage for real-world applications. The results across multiple languages and domains (Bulgarian, Croatian, Italian, English) consistently show UDP has lower standard deviations across domains, confirming it's less sensitive to sampling bias.

4. **Practical applicability to low-resource languages**: The system requires no training data, making it immediately applicable to truly low-resource languages where training data is scarce or nonexistent. This is a significant practical contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP.

5. **Simple and elegant design**: The system's simplicity (no training, no language-specific parameters, minimal rules) is a strength. The authors demonstrate that with careful application of UD's constraints, you can achieve good results without complex machinery.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work**: While the application to UD is novel, the core techniques (PageRank, two-step decoding) are adaptations of Søgaard (2012). The paper acknowledges this but doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from this prior work. The novelty is in the specific application to UD constraints, not in the techniques themselves, which slightly diminishes the paper's contribution.

2. **Overreliance on gold POS tags for best performance**: The system's performance drops significantly when using predicted POS tags (2.2% drop compared to 2.7% for MSD). While the paper acknowledges this limitation, it's a significant practical concern since gold POS tags aren't available in real-world scenarios. The authors don't provide a clear path to address this limitation.

3. **Lack of comparison to other unsupervised parsers**: The paper compares to a rule-based baseline and a supervised transfer system, but doesn't compare to other unsupervised parsers (e.g., Klein & Manning, Spitkovsky et al.). Without this comparison, it's hard to assess how much better UDP is than existing unsupervised approaches.

4. **Superficial error analysis**: The error analysis is somewhat limited. The paper mentions the system can't handle relative clauses but doesn't provide deeper analysis of other failure modes or how these might be addressed in future work.

5. **Overstatement of novelty**: The paper claims "our parser goes beyond the existing work on rule-aided unsupervised dependency parsing" but the differences from Søgaard (2012) are relatively minor. The paper doesn't clearly articulate why these specific adaptations are particularly important for UD.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your system compare to other unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., the ones by Klein & Manning, Spitkovsky et al.) on the same UD datasets? The paper compares to a rule-based baseline and a supervised transfer system, but not to other unsupervised parsers.

2. The paper states that "UD is a constantly-improving effort, and not all v1.2 treebanks have the same level of formalism compliance." How does this affect your system's performance, and have you analyzed which languages or treebanks pose particular challenges?

3. The paper mentions that the system cannot infer relative clauses because it doesn't allow nouns to be heads for verbs. Are there any plans to extend the system to handle this limitation, and how would that impact the system's simplicity?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a highly novel and bold approach to unsupervised dependency parsing by leveraging the specific constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism. The core idea is refreshing and well-executed, demonstrating that by designing a parser specifically for UD's constraints, you can achieve competitive results without any training data.

While there are some methodological limitations (particularly the reliance on gold POS tags for best performance), these are outweighed by the novelty and significance of the approach. The paper's main contribution is a fresh perspective that could shift the paradigm in unsupervised parsing research.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field. The authors have taken a bold, unconventional approach that yields impressive results and opens up new research directions. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions above.

**Recommendation: Accept**
