

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism constraints. The system uses a small set of head-dependent rules adapted to UD conventions, personalized PageRank for content word ranking, and on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test data. The authors evaluate UDP across 32 languages in three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1. Demonstrating that a completely unsupervised dependency parser can achieve competitive performance with supervised transfer systems by leveraging UD formalism constraints.
2. Providing a systematic adaptation of dependency parsing to the UD framework, showing how its specific constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) can be exploited without training data.
3. Establishing empirical evidence that rule-based approaches are more robust to domain shifts than data-driven approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evidence for competitive unsupervised performance**: The paper presents compelling results showing UDPG (with gold POS) achieves an average UAS of 63.9% compared to MSDG (supervised transfer) at 61.2%, with UDPG even outperforming MSDG on Hindi. This is remarkable for an unsupervised parser, as the gap between unsupervised and supervised parsers is typically much larger (15-20% UAS points). The paper convincingly demonstrates that a rule-based approach can nearly match supervised transfer performance by leveraging UD's specific constraints.

2. **Robustness to domain shifts**: The cross-domain evaluation (Table 6-7) provides strong evidence that UDP is significantly more robust to domain shifts than MSD. UDP's standard deviation across domains is consistently lower (e.g., 2.5 vs. 4.2 for UDPG vs. MSDG), confirming Plank and van Noord's (2010) findings that rule-based parsers are less sensitive to sampling bias. This is a substantial practical contribution to cross-lingual parsing, as domain adaptation is a major challenge in real-world applications.

3. **Clear contribution to UD formalism adoption**: The paper makes a compelling case for how UD's specific constraints (function words as leaves, single-rooted trees) can be leveraged to build a robust parser without training data. The authors systematically address UD's requirements (e.g., adapting head rules, using personalized PageRank for main predicate identification), providing valuable insights for the UD community about how to better exploit the formalism's design.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading "no training" claim**: The paper claims to require "no training," but the authors explicitly state they used the English treebank during development to "assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters." This constitutes a form of training, and the authors should clarify that the system requires development data for parameter tuning but no language-specific training data. This weakens the paper's core claim and could mislead readers.

2. **Limited novelty in technical approach**: While the application to UD is novel, the core techniques (PageRank with personalization, head rules) are adaptations of Søgaard's (2012b) work. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how these adaptations specifically address UD constraints beyond what's already been done. The authors claim "our system goes beyond the existing work on rule-aided unsupervised dependency parsing," but the differences (personalized PageRank, two-step decoding) are relatively minor extensions of prior work.

3. **Inconsistent claims about language independence**: The paper states "completely free of language-specific parameters," but admits they estimate adposition direction on test data for each language (via a bigram heuristic). This contradicts the language independence claim, as the system must process each language's data to determine directionality. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this as a language-specific adaptation rather than claiming full language independence.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "no training," but uses English treebank for development. Could you clarify the exact nature of this development phase and whether it's truly minimal (e.g., just for tuning a few parameters rather than learning language-specific rules)?

2. How would the system perform if the UD constraints were relaxed (e.g., allowing function words as heads in some cases)? This would help assess whether the performance gains are specifically due to UD constraints or general rule-based parsing.

3. The paper shows UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi, but Hindi is a verb-final language where the PR personalization (which assumes first verb as main predicate) would be particularly problematic. Could you provide more details about how the system handles this language specifically?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that an unsupervised parser can achieve competitive performance with supervised transfer systems by leveraging UD's formalism constraints. The empirical results are compelling, especially regarding robustness to domain shifts. However, the paper's "no training" claim is misleading due to the development phase using English treebank data, and the technical novelty is somewhat overstated.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides a thorough evaluation across multiple languages, POS setups, and domains. It offers valuable insights for the UD community about how to exploit the formalism's design in parsing systems. With some clarification about the development phase and more precise claims about the novelty, this would be an excellent contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the misleading "no training" claim and to more accurately position the technical novelty. The paper's empirical results and contribution to UD formalism exploitation are strong enough to warrant acceptance despite these limitations.
