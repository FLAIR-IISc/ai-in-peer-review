

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), a rule-based dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and part-of-speech (POS) tags. The system uses personalized PageRank to score content words, two-step decoding to ensure function words are leaves, and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and naive POS) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. They claim UDP achieves competitive performance with supervised systems while being more robust to domain shifts.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A rule-based dependency parser that leverages UD constraints and POS tags to achieve unsupervised parsing
2. A detailed analysis of the parser's robustness across different POS scenarios and domain shifts
3. A demonstration that rule-based approaches can be more resilient to domain shifts than data-driven methods

## Strengths
1. **Clear methodology description**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of the two-step decoding algorithm and personalized PageRank approach, including pseudocode and examples. The technical details about the head rules, adposition direction estimation, and function word handling are well-articulated.

2. **Comprehensive cross-lingual evaluation**: The evaluation across 32 languages with multiple POS scenarios provides valuable insights into the parser's performance. The analysis of cross-domain consistency is particularly strong, showing that UDP maintains consistent performance across different domains while supervised methods show greater variability.

3. **Strong empirical evidence for domain robustness**: The paper provides compelling evidence that UDP is less sensitive to domain shifts than the supervised multi-source transfer parser. The results in Tables 6 and 7 clearly demonstrate this, with UDP showing lower standard deviations across domains for both gold-POS and predicted-POS setups.

## Weaknesses

1. **Contradiction in "no training" claim**: The paper claims to be "unsupervised" and "requires no training," but the authors explicitly state they "have used the English treebank during development to assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters (Sec. 3.1) and function-word directionality (Sec. 3.2)." This is a fundamental methodological flaw that contradicts the paper's core claim. Using any development data for parameter tuning constitutes training, even if it's not for the final model. The paper should clarify whether this development phase is considered training or not, and if not, how it aligns with the "no training" claim.

2. **Missing results for key evaluation scenario**: The paper mentions a "naive type-constrained two-POS tag scenario" (UDPN) multiple times (in sections 5.2, 5.3, and 6.1), but the results for this scenario are not provided in the main tables (Table 5). The authors claim UDP "outperforms the adjacency baselines (BL) by 4% on average on the two type-based naive POS tag scenario," but without the actual results, this claim lacks supporting evidence. This omission significantly weakens the evaluation.

3. **Technical inaccuracies in key sections**: 
   - In section 3.2, the paper states: "If a language has more ADP-NOMINAL bigrams, we consider all its ADP to be prepositions (and thus dependent of elements at their right)." This is technically incorrect. If ADP is a preposition, it should be "dependent of elements at their left" (prepositions come before their objects), not "at their right." This is a critical error in the algorithm description.
   - The paper uses "adpositon" instead of "adposition" in section 7, which is a clear typo that undermines professionalism.

4. **Overstated claims about performance**: The paper claims "UDP is a competitive system for all UD languages" and "Our unsupervised parser compares favorably to delexicalized parsing." However, Table 5 shows that for several languages (e.g., Basque: UDPG=45.0 vs MSDG=51.2; Croatian: UDPG=56.7 vs MSDG=69.1), UDPG is significantly worse than MSDG. The claim that UDP is "competitive for all" languages is not supported by the data.

5. **Inconsistent language evaluation**: The paper states "We evaluate our system on 32 languages" but in section 2, they mention "Out of 33 languages in UD v1.2. We exclude Japanese because the treebank is distributed without word forms." This creates confusion - the paper should clarify whether they evaluated on 32 languages (excluding Japanese) or 33 languages (with Japanese excluded from some evaluations). The table of results shows 32 languages, but the exclusion of Japanese from the predicted POS evaluation should be explicitly stated.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be "untrained" but uses English treebank data for development (parameter tuning and head rule selection). How does this align with the paper's main claim that the parser "requires no training"? If this development phase is considered training, please clarify this in the paper.

2. The results for the naive POS scenario (UDPN) are mentioned multiple times but not provided in the main results table (Table 5). Please provide these results to support the claims made about UDP's performance in this scenario.

3. In section 3.2, the paper states that prepositions are "dependent of elements at their right." This is technically incorrect. Prepositions should be "dependent of elements at their left" (e.g., "in the room" - "in" comes before "room"). Please clarify this error in the paper.

4. The paper claims UDP is "competitive for all UD languages" but shows significant performance gaps for some languages (e.g., Basque, Croatian). Please revise this claim to reflect the actual results and provide an analysis of why UDP performs poorly for these specific languages.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting rule-based approach to dependency parsing that leverages UD constraints, the methodological contradiction regarding "no training," the missing results for a key evaluation scenario, and the technical inaccuracies in the paper undermine its credibility. The paper's main claim that it requires "no training" is contradicted by the use of English treebank data for development, which is a fundamental flaw in the methodology. Additionally, the overstatement of results for all languages without qualification weakens the paper's contribution.

The paper has potential value in demonstrating the robustness of rule-based approaches to domain shifts, but these strengths are overshadowed by the significant methodological flaws and inconsistencies that prevent me from recommending acceptance.
