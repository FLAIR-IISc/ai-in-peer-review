

# Peer Review: "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary

This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints of the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The approach uses a small set of head-dependent rules derived from UD conventions, personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate (typically the first verb), and a two-step decoding process that ensures function words are leaves. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages from UD 1.2 treebanks across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive two-POS) and compare it to a rule-based baseline and a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). The system achieves competitive performance (average UAS 63.9% with gold POS), outperforming the supervised baseline on Hindi, and demonstrates greater robustness to domain shifts than data-driven approaches.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Leveraging UD constraints for unsupervised parsing**: The paper successfully demonstrates how the specific constraints of the UD formalism (function words as leaves, content words as heads) can be exploited to create a high-performing unsupervised parser, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Competitive performance without training data**: The system achieves performance remarkably close to supervised transfer parsers (within 6.4% average UAS difference), making it one of the most effective unsupervised dependency parsers reported to date.

3. **Robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides compelling evidence that a rule-based approach grounded in linguistic constraints is more stable across different text domains than data-driven approaches, which is particularly valuable for real-world applications.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper makes a compelling case for why UD's specific constraints (particularly function words as leaves and content words as heads) are ideal for rule-based parsing. The authors correctly identify that most existing unsupervised parsers don't exploit these constraints, which explains why their approach works so well. The specific head rules in Table 1 are well-motivated by UD conventions, and the two-step decoding process (content words first, then function words) effectively enforces the leaf status of function words.

2. **Competitive performance with minimal assumptions**: Achieving an average UAS of 63.9% with gold POS (within 6.4% of the supervised baseline) is impressive for an unsupervised system. The fact that UDP outperforms the supervised baseline on Hindi (69.7% vs 73.5% UAS for UDPG vs MSDG) is particularly noteworthy. This demonstrates that the constraints of UD, when properly exploited, can yield high-quality parses without any language-specific training data.

3. **Superior domain robustness**: The cross-domain evaluation is one of the paper's strongest points. The finding that UDP has a much lower standard deviation across domains (2.5 vs 4.2 for MSD) provides strong evidence that rule-based approaches grounded in linguistic constraints are less susceptible to domain shifts. This is a valuable contribution for practical applications where domain shifts are common.

4. **Practical implementation details**: The paper provides clear, implementable details about how the system works, including the estimation of adposition direction from minimal test data (10-15 sentences), the personalization of PageRank, and the two-step decoding process. These practical considerations make the approach more accessible to other researchers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: While the application of existing techniques (PageRank, rule-based parsing) to the UD formalism is valuable, the core methodology is largely based on Søgaard (2012b). The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their specific modifications (personalization, two-step decoding) represent a significant methodological advancement over Søgaard's work. The claim of "no training" is also somewhat misleading, as the authors used the English treebank for development and tuning, which represents a form of language-specific parameterization.

2. **Insufficient error analysis**: The paper lacks a detailed analysis of error patterns, which would help understand the limitations of the approach. For example, the authors note the system cannot handle relative clauses due to excluding the NOUN → VERB rule, but don't analyze how often this limitation affects performance. Similarly, the poor performance on punctuation (21% average UAS) isn't explored in depth, despite being a notable issue.

3. **Inadequate comparison to recent unsupervised work**: The paper compares to a rule-based baseline and a supervised transfer parser, but doesn't compare to more recent unsupervised dependency parsing systems (e.g., those by Kondrak and Szpakowicz, 2021 or others). This omission makes it difficult to assess where this work stands in the current landscape of unsupervised parsing.

4. **Misleading claims about "no training"**: The paper states "our system requires no training," but the authors used the English treebank during development to tune PageRank parameters and function-word directionality. While this is reasonable for development, it's important to clarify that the system isn't truly "no training" but rather uses minimal language-specific tuning during development.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters," but the head direction for function words (AUX, DET, etc.) is determined on English dev data. How would this approach scale to a language with no English parallel data? Would the system still be able to estimate directionality without any language-specific tuning?

2. The paper excludes the NOUN → VERB head rule to avoid making the hierarchical relation between verbs and nouns "less conclusive." Could you provide more details about why this rule was excluded and how its inclusion would affect performance, particularly on languages where relative clauses are common?

3. The paper states that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi, but Table 5 shows UDPG (69.7%) is lower than MSDG (73.5%) for Hindi. Could you clarify this discrepancy?

4. The paper reports a 21% average UAS for punctuation (PUNCT). Given that UD treats punctuation as a special case, how would you modify the system to better handle punctuation? Would adding specific rules for punctuation improve performance significantly?

## Additional Comments

- The paper would benefit from a clearer distinction between UD (the formalism) and UDP (the parser) to avoid confusion.

- The paper mentions "32 languages" but excludes Japanese due to missing word forms. It would be helpful to clarify how many languages were actually evaluated (31).

- The paper states that the system "requires no training data," but the English treebank was used for development. A more accurate claim would be "requires minimal language-specific development data" or "requires no language-specific training data."

- The claim that "the main predicate of the sentence is the node that should have the highest PR, making it the dependent of the root node" is confusing. If the main predicate is the highest-ranked word, it should be the root node, not a dependent of the root node. This needs clarification.

- The paper could provide more details about how the POS tagger (TnT) was trained for each language, as POS accuracy is critical to the system's performance.

- The paper states that "the resulting trees always pass the validation script in github.com/UniversalDependencies/tools," but doesn't explain what this validation checks. A brief description would strengthen the paper.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed and well-motivated paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating how the specific constraints of the UD formalism can be leveraged for high-performing unsupervised parsing. The system achieves competitive performance without any training data, and the evidence for its robustness to domain shifts is particularly compelling. While the methodological novelty is somewhat limited, the practical implementation and strong empirical results make this a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about methodological novelty and the "no training" claim.
