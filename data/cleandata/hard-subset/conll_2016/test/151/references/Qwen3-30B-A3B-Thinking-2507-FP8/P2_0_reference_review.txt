

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), a dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints. The system uses personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate, incorporates UD's restrictions on function words being leaves, estimates adposition direction from test data, and implements a two-step decoding process to handle content and function words differently. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages across three POS setups (gold, predicted, and naive) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), demonstrating competitive performance and superior robustness to domain changes.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Demonstrating that a completely unsupervised dependency parser can achieve competitive performance with supervised transfer methods** by leveraging UD's formal constraints. The paper shows UDPG (with gold POS) performs within 6.4% of the supervised MSDG system across 32 languages, and even outperforms MSDG on Hindi.

2. **Providing a thorough analysis of robustness to domain shifts and POS errors**, showing that UDP maintains performance better than the supervised baseline when POS accuracy is lower (only 2.2% drop in UAS with predicted POS vs. 2.7% for MSD) and exhibits significantly lower standard deviation across domains.

3. **Establishing a new baseline for unsupervised dependency parsing** that requires no training data and is completely free of language-specific parameters, making it particularly valuable for low-resource languages where training data is scarce.

## Strengths

1. **Significant practical contribution**: The paper demonstrates that a simple rule-based approach can achieve results comparable to supervised transfer methods, which is crucial for low-resource languages. The robustness to POS errors (only 2.2% drop in UAS with predicted POS) is particularly valuable for real-world applications where gold POS is unavailable.

2. **Rigorous cross-domain evaluation**: The authors go beyond standard test sets to evaluate across multiple domains (news, legal, literature, etc.) for several languages. The results (Table 6) clearly show UDP has a much lower standard deviation across domains than the supervised baseline, confirming that rule-based parsers can be more stable to domain shifts (as previously suggested by Plank and van Noord, 2010).

3. **Clear methodology and implementation**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of the PageRank setup, head direction estimation, and two-step decoding algorithm. The example in Section 4 makes it easy to understand how the parser works, and the algorithm in Figure 1 is well-structured and clearly explained.

4. **Strong baseline comparison**: The comparison to the multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) is appropriate and meaningful, as this is a state-of-the-art approach for cross-lingual dependency parsing. The paper doesn't just compare to simple unsupervised baselines but to a method that's designed for cross-lingual transfer.

5. **Practical implementation**: The authors make their parser and test sets freely available, which is a significant contribution to the community. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of how the system handles different POS tags and domain shifts, making it valuable for researchers and practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core methodology**: While the application to UD is novel, the core techniques (PageRank for ranking, two-step decoding) have been used before (e.g., Søgaard, 2012b). The paper's main contribution is adapting these techniques to UD constraints rather than introducing fundamentally new techniques. This makes the novelty somewhat limited, and it's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the UD-specific adaptations versus just applying existing methods.

2. **Evaluation on outdated UD version**: The paper evaluates on UD v1.2, but UD v2.0 has been released with more languages and improved annotation consistency. Evaluating on older data makes the results less compelling as the state of the art has likely improved since UD v1.2. The paper doesn't address how the results might change on the newer version.

3. **Lack of comparison to other unsupervised parsers**: The paper compares to a supervised transfer baseline and a simple rule-based baseline, but doesn't compare to other unsupervised parsers that might be more state-of-the-art. For example, it doesn't mention the recent unsupervised parsers by Søgaard (2012b) or others that might have better performance without training data.

4. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper provides some error analysis (Section 6), but doesn't deeply analyze why the system fails on certain languages or structures. For example, it mentions the limitation with relative clauses but doesn't explore how common this is or how much it affects overall performance.

5. **Small language coverage**: The paper evaluates on 32 languages, but UD has many more languages (over 100 now). The exclusion of Japanese (due to lack of word forms) might bias results toward languages with better resources, and the paper doesn't discuss how results might generalize to other languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims UDP outperforms the supervised MSD system on Hindi. Could the authors provide more details about this specific case? How does the performance on Hindi compare to other languages where the system performs worse than MSD?

2. The paper states that UDP is "completely free of language-specific parameters," but it does estimate adposition direction from test data. How does this fit with the claim of being completely language-agnostic, and is there a way to make this process fully language-independent?

3. The paper mentions that the system cannot infer relative clauses because it doesn't allow nouns to be heads for verbs. How common are relative clauses in the UD treebanks, and how much does this limitation affect overall performance across the languages evaluated?

4. The paper evaluates on UD v1.2, but UD v2.0 is now available. Could the authors provide a brief comparison of how the results might change on UD v2.0, particularly with respect to the languages where the system performed poorly?

5. The paper mentions that UDP performs better than English on 24 out of 32 languages. Could the authors elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it because the English treebank was used for development, or is there a linguistic reason for this pattern?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. The demonstration that a completely unsupervised parser can achieve competitive performance with supervised transfer methods is significant, especially for low-resource languages. The thorough evaluation across domains and POS setups is particularly impressive. While the novelty of the core methodology is somewhat limited, the paper's application to UD constraints and the comprehensive evaluation make it a valuable contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly the comparison to more recent unsupervised parsers and the evaluation on a more recent UD version.
