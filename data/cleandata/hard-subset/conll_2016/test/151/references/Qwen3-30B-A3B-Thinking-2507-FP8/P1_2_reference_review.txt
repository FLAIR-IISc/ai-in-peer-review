

# Peer Review: Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training

## Summary
This paper presents a dependency parser (UDP) that requires no training data and instead relies solely on the constraints of the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The system uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to score content words, incorporates UD's restriction that function words must be leaves, and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate their parser on 32 languages from UD v1.2 across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive two-POS) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). The results show that UDP performs competitively with supervised parsers, with the added advantage of being more robust to domain changes.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Demonstrating effective unsupervised dependency parsing leveraging UD constraints**: The paper shows that by carefully exploiting the specific constraints of the UD formalism, an unsupervised parser can achieve competitive performance without any training data.
2. **Proving the domain robustness of rule-based parsing**: The paper provides compelling evidence that a rule-based approach, designed to satisfy UD's general linguistic constraints, is significantly more robust to domain shifts than data-driven transfer parsers.
3. **Practical implementation of UD constraints in parsing**: The authors successfully translate the theoretical UD constraints into a working parsing system that achieves strong results across multiple languages.

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and setups**: The evaluation on 32 languages is substantial and provides strong evidence of the system's cross-lingual capabilities. The authors evaluate across three different POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive two-POS), which thoroughly explores the system's behavior and limitations. This is particularly valuable as it demonstrates that UDP maintains competitive performance even when POS tags are imperfect, which is a common real-world scenario.

2. **Compelling evidence of domain robustness**: The cross-domain evaluation (Tables 6 and 7) is exceptionally strong. The paper demonstrates that UDP has significantly lower standard deviation across domains compared to the supervised transfer parser (MSD), with a macro-averaged standard deviation of 2.5 vs. 4.2 for gold POS. This is a major practical contribution, as it shows that UDP would be more reliable in real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

3. **Effective application of UD formalism constraints**: The authors successfully leverage specific UD constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) to build an effective parser. Their two-step decoding process that first attaches content words and then function words (ensuring function words are leaves) is a clever application of UD's formal constraints that directly addresses a key challenge in dependency parsing.

4. **Competitive performance without training data**: The fact that UDPG (gold POS) is only 6.4% behind MSDG (supervised transfer parser) on average is impressive for an unsupervised system. The paper's claim that UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi (with scores of 55.3 vs. 55.7) is particularly noteworthy, as it demonstrates that the unsupervised approach can exceed supervised transfer parsers in specific cases.

5. **Clear and well-motivated design choices**: The paper provides thorough explanations for each design choice (personalized PageRank, adposition direction estimation, two-step decoding) with clear justifications based on UD's formal constraints. This makes the system understandable and reproducible, which is valuable for the community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core approach**: While the application to UD is novel, the core techniques (PageRank for ranking words, two-step decoding) are adaptations of existing unsupervised parsing methods (Søgaard, 2012b). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate these adaptations from prior work or clearly establish why the specific UD adaptation is novel. The main innovation appears to be the systematic application of UD constraints, not the core parsing techniques themselves.

2. **Inadequate error analysis**: The paper provides limited error analysis. It mentions that the system "cannot infer relative clauses" due to excluding the NOUN→VERB head rule, but doesn't provide concrete examples or quantify how much this affects overall performance. A more detailed analysis of where the parser fails (e.g., specific syntactic structures) would strengthen the paper.

3. **Questionable UAS interpretation for UD**: The paper uses UAS as the main metric without addressing how UD's specific constraints might affect the interpretation of UAS. For example, UD's restriction that function words must be leaves means that certain dependency structures that might be valid in other formalisms are invalid in UD. This could potentially make UAS less meaningful for evaluating UD parsers, but the paper doesn't address this.

4. **Limited discussion of language-specific challenges**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss how the parser handles languages with very different word order from English (the language used for development). The fact that the system performs better than English on 24 of 32 languages (Table 5) suggests it generalizes well, but the paper doesn't explore why this might be the case or what specific challenges were faced with languages like Hindi (verb-final) or Basque (ergative).

5. **Insufficient comparison to other unsupervised parsers**: The paper compares to a rule-based baseline (BL) and a supervised transfer parser (MSD), but doesn't compare to other unsupervised dependency parsers. This makes it difficult to position UDP in the broader landscape of unsupervised parsing research. A comparison to state-of-the-art unsupervised parsers (e.g., from Søgaard, 2012b) would strengthen the paper's claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (55.3 vs. 55.7), but Table 5 shows UDPG at 55.3 and MSDG at 55.7 for Hindi. Is there a typo in the claim, or is there a different metric being used?

2. The paper mentions that they used the English treebank for development. How did they ensure that the head rules and parameters developed for English would generalize to languages with different word orders (e.g., Japanese, Hindi)? Did they encounter specific challenges with verb-final languages?

3. The paper states that the system has no language-specific parameters, but they estimate adposition direction from test data. How robust is this estimation to small test sets? What happens if the test set contains fewer than 10-15 sentences, which the paper claims is sufficient for convergence?

4. The paper claims that the system "cannot infer relative clauses" because they excluded the NOUN→VERB head rule. How much does this exclusion impact the overall performance, and are there other specific structures that the system struggles with?

5. The paper evaluates on 32 languages but excludes Japanese due to lack of word forms. How would the approach need to be modified to handle languages where word forms are not available, and how would this affect the parser's performance?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a well-designed and thoroughly evaluated unsupervised dependency parser that effectively leverages the Universal Dependencies formalism. The main contribution is demonstrating that by carefully applying UD's specific constraints, an unsupervised parser can achieve competitive performance without any training data. The cross-domain robustness results are particularly strong and valuable for real-world applications.

While the core techniques aren't completely novel, the systematic application to UD and the strong empirical results make this a valuable contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions addressing the weaknesses mentioned above, this paper would make a strong contribution to the conference.

**Recommendation: Accept**
