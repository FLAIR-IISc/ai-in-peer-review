

# Peer Review: Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A novel approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that leverages UD's specific constraints (function words must be dependents of content words, trees must be single-rooted) to create a rule-based parser that outperforms delexicalized transfer parsers on many languages.

2. A demonstration that a rule-based parser can be more robust to domain shifts than data-driven approaches, with significantly lower standard deviation across different text domains.

3. A practical implementation of an unsupervised parser that achieves competitive performance across 32 languages without requiring any language-specific parameters.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation across multiple dimensions**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across 32 languages in three different POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and naive content/function POS), and across different text domains. The key finding that UDPG (UDP with gold POS) is remarkably close to MSDG (multi-source delexicalized parser with gold POS), with an average difference of only 6.4% UAS, is impressive for an unsupervised system. This demonstrates that UD's formal constraints can provide sufficient guidance for dependency parsing without training data.

2. **Demonstrated robustness to domain shifts**: The paper's evaluation across multiple domains (news, legal, literature, etc.) shows UDP has significantly lower standard deviation (2.5 vs 4.2 for gold POS) than the supervised MSD baseline, confirming that rule-based systems can be more stable to domain shifts. This is a significant practical contribution for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

3. **Clear methodological innovation in applying UD constraints**: The paper makes a compelling case for using UD's specific constraints as the foundation for parsing rather than relying on data-driven approaches. The personalized PageRank approach to rank content words and the two-step decoding process (content words first, function words second) effectively leverage UD's requirements that function words must be leaves and content words must be heads.

4. **Practical utility and accessibility**: The parser is freely available, with clear documentation and a simple implementation. The paper demonstrates that a rule-based approach can achieve competitive performance without requiring complex infrastructure or training data, making it accessible for resource-constrained settings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in core methodology**: While the application to UD is novel, the core approach (using PageRank for ranking content words and a rule-based system) is largely based on Søgaard (2012b). The paper acknowledges this but doesn't sufficiently clarify how the specific adaptation to UD constraints constitutes a meaningful advancement over prior work. The main novelty appears to be the specific application to UD rather than a fundamentally new approach.

2. **Misrepresentation of "no training"**: The paper claims the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters" and "requires no training," but it actually uses English treebank data during development to tune parameters and assess head rules. This is a significant limitation that undermines the "no training" claim. The authors should be more transparent about this dependency on English data.

3. **Lack of comparison to other unsupervised parsers**: The paper compares to a rule-based baseline and a supervised transfer parser, but doesn't compare to other unsupervised dependency parsers. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess how much better UDP is than existing unsupervised approaches. For example, how does it compare to the work of Klein and Manning (2004) or Søgaard (2012b) on the same data?

4. **Overly optimistic claims about domain robustness**: While the paper shows UDP is more robust to domain shifts than MSD, it doesn't thoroughly analyze where UDP fails across domains. For example, it notes that "presidential bulletin, legal and weblogs are amongst the hardest domains to parse" but doesn't provide concrete examples or explain why this might be the case.

5. **Unrealistic naive POS scenario**: The evaluation of UDP on the naive content/function POS scenario (UDPN) is interesting but not practically relevant, as this tagging approach would rarely be used in real applications. The paper acknowledges this but still presents the results as a meaningful evaluation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the system uses English treebank data during development to tune parameters and assess head rules. How much of the English treebank was used, and how does this impact the claim that the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters"?

2. The paper mentions that the system cannot infer relative clauses due to excluding the NOUN → VERB head rule. How often do relative clauses occur in the UD treebanks, and what is the impact on overall parsing accuracy?

3. The paper claims UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi. Can you provide more details on why this might be the case? Is it due to specific characteristics of Hindi or the Hindi UD treebank?

4. The paper states that UDP is "completely free of language-specific parameters" but the adposition direction is estimated on test data. How does this differ from having language-specific parameters, and why is it considered acceptable?

5. Why did the authors choose to exclude the NOUN → VERB head rule during development, and what impact does this exclusion have on the parser's ability to handle relative clauses and other constructions?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised parsing. The demonstration that UD constraints can provide sufficient guidance for dependency parsing without training data is an important insight. The authors should clarify the relationship between their work and prior unsupervised parsing approaches, and be more transparent about the English data used during development. The evaluation methodology is sound and the results are compelling, making this a strong submission that would be valuable to the NLP community.
