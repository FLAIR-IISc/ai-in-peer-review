

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper presents a dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints of the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The system, called UDP, uses UD's specific constraints (like function words being leaves, specific head-dependent rules) combined with personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate. It estimates adposition direction directly from test data rather than relying on language-specific parameters. The authors evaluate on 32 languages, showing competitive performance against supervised multi-source delexicalized parsers and demonstrating greater robustness to domain shifts.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **The core conceptual innovation**: Building a dependency parser that requires no training data by leveraging the specific constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism. This represents a fundamental paradigm shift from the current data-driven approach to dependency parsing.
2. **Demonstrating competitive performance without training**: The system achieves performance comparable to supervised transfer parsers across 32 languages, with the notable exception of Hindi where it outperforms the supervised baseline.
3. **Practical implementation of constraint exploitation**: The authors successfully implement a system that uses UD's linguistic constraints (function words as leaves, specific head rules) in a way that enables competitive performance without any training data.

## Strengths

1. **Radical novelty in approach**: This paper presents one of the most conceptually novel approaches to dependency parsing I've seen in recent years. The idea of building a dependency parser that requires no training data by leveraging the constraints of a linguistic formalism (UD) is bold and transformative. It challenges the fundamental assumption that dependency parsing requires substantial training data. The paper successfully demonstrates that this approach can yield competitive results, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The evaluation across 32 languages with multiple POS scenarios (gold, predicted, naive type-constrained) is comprehensive and convincing. The paper demonstrates that UDP outperforms the supervised multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) on 31 out of 32 languages (with a 0.6% edge on Hindi), which is remarkable for an unsupervised approach. The robustness to domain shifts (Table 7) is particularly compelling evidence of the approach's practical utility.

3. **Practical utility and simplicity**: The system is extremely lightweight (no training data needed, simple implementation), and the authors provide the code and test sets. This makes the approach immediately applicable for low-resource languages or scenarios where training data is unavailable. The fact that it achieves competitive performance with such simplicity is a major strength.

4. **Theoretical insight**: The paper demonstrates that constraint-based approaches can be competitive with data-driven methods, challenging the assumption that more data always leads to better performance in dependency parsing. This insight has broader implications for NLP beyond dependency parsing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Methodological simplicity**: While the approach is novel, the core techniques (PageRank, head rules) are not particularly innovative. The novelty is in the application of these techniques to UD constraints, not in the techniques themselves. This may make the work seem less substantial to reviewers who prioritize technical innovation.

2. **Dependence on POS tags**: The system requires accurate POS tags, which are not always available or reliable. While the authors evaluate on predicted POS tags (with only a 2.2% drop), the performance drops significantly (to 29.9 UAS) when using naive type-constrained POS tags (UDPN). This limits the practical applicability to scenarios without reliable POS tagging.

3. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The evaluation is confined to UD v1.2 treebanks, and the paper doesn't address more complex syntactic phenomena like coordination, multiword expressions, or proper names (which the authors acknowledge as future work). This limits the scope of the contribution.

4. **Lack of comparison to other unsupervised methods**: While the paper compares to a baseline (BL) and a supervised transfer system (MSD), it doesn't compare to other unsupervised dependency parsing methods that might be relevant to their approach, which would strengthen the novelty claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions using the English treebank during development to tune parameters. How much of the English treebank was used for development, and how does this affect the generalizability of the approach to languages with very different structures?

2. The paper claims UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (Table 5 shows UDPG 45.7 vs MSDG 46.3), but the difference is very small (0.6%). Could you clarify this discrepancy and provide more detail on why this is considered a significant outperformance?

3. The authors state that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi, but the average UAS for UDPG on Hindi is 45.7, while MSDG is 46.3. Could you elaborate on why this is considered an outperformance in the paper?

## Overall Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper presents a highly novel and bold approach to dependency parsing that challenges the fundamental assumption that training data is necessary for dependency parsing. While the technical components are not particularly innovative, their application to leverage UD constraints represents a significant conceptual contribution. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating competitive performance across 32 languages with greater robustness to domain shifts than supervised systems. The simplicity of the approach and its immediate practical utility make this a valuable contribution to the field.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance. The conceptual shift represented by this work is significant enough to outweigh the minor methodological limitations. The paper introduces a new direction for dependency parsing that could inspire future work and has practical implications for low-resource language processing.
