

# Review: "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper presents a dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data and relies solely on explicit POS constraints imposed by the UD formalism. The system uses personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate, incorporates UD's restriction that function words are leaves, and estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate their parser on 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and a naive content/function POS setup) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. Their results show that their unsupervised parser is competitive with supervised approaches and demonstrates greater robustness to domain shifts.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A rule-based unsupervised dependency parser that leverages UD-specific constraints (particularly the restriction that function words are leaves and the UD head rules) rather than relying on language-specific training data.
2. A personalized PageRank approach to identify the main predicate (root node) of sentences, which significantly improves parsing accuracy over a simple ordering baseline.
3. A method to estimate adposition direction (prepositions vs. postpositions) directly from test data without language-specific parameters, making the system completely free of language-specific parameters.

## Strengths
1. **Significant contribution to leveraging UD constraints**: The paper makes a compelling case for how the UD formalism enables a simpler, more linguistically sound parsing approach. By focusing on UD's specific constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves), the authors create a parser that avoids many pitfalls of previous unsupervised and cross-lingual approaches. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as it demonstrates how standardized linguistic formalisms can guide parsing methodology.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The parser achieves remarkably competitive performance with a supervised transfer parser (MSD) across 32 languages, with only a 6.4% average UAS difference. This is particularly impressive for an unsupervised approach. The paper demonstrates that by leveraging UD constraints, it's possible to achieve performance that rivals supervised methods, which is a significant advance in unsupervised parsing.

3. **Demonstrated robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides compelling evidence that UDP is more robust to domain changes than the supervised MSD parser, as evidenced by lower standard deviations across different domains. This is a practical advantage that addresses a key challenge in real-world parsing applications. The cross-domain analysis is thorough and well-structured, with detailed results for Bulgarian, Croatian, Italian, and English domains.

4. **Clear methodological innovation**: The personalized PageRank approach (Section 3.1) and two-step decoding (Section 3.3) represent a meaningful refinement of prior work on rule-aided unsupervised parsing. The authors provide clear evidence of the contribution of each component through ablation studies (Section 6.1), showing that PageRank alone contributes 9 UAS points over a baseline that ranks content words by reading order.

5. **Practical implementation and transparency**: The system is completely rule-based with no language-specific parameters, making it highly interpretable and easy to implement. The authors provide clear documentation of their approach, make their code publicly available, and conduct thorough evaluations across multiple languages and scenarios.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty of core technical approach**: While the paper correctly identifies the value of leveraging UD constraints, the core technical approach (using PageRank to score content words and two-step decoding) is largely based on Søgaard (2012b). The paper doesn't clearly distinguish how their method is significantly different from this prior work beyond using UD-specific constraints. The contributions are not sufficiently differentiated from existing unsupervised parsing methods.

2. **Inadequate comparison to other unsupervised parsers**: The paper compares to a rule-based baseline (BL) and a supervised transfer parser (MSD), but fails to compare to other unsupervised parsers that would be more directly comparable. For example, the paper doesn't compare to Søgaard's (2012b) unsupervised parser, which uses similar head rules but without the UD-specific adaptations. This omission makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the UD-specific adaptations.

3. **Overstated "no training" claim**: The paper claims to be a "parser without training," but it explicitly uses the English treebank during development to tune PageRank parameters and assess head rules. This is not fully unsupervised in the strictest sense, and the paper should be more transparent about this. The claim of "no training data" is misleading, as the authors do use a development set (English treebank) to tune their system.

4. **Lack of deeper error analysis**: The paper provides some error analysis by POS (Section 6.2), but doesn't deeply analyze why the parser fails in certain cases or what specific UD constraints it's having trouble with. For instance, the paper notes the parser can't infer relative clauses due to excluding NOUN → VERB head rules, but doesn't quantify how much this affects performance on languages where relative clauses are common.

5. **Insufficient discussion of computational complexity**: The paper mentions that decoding runs in less than O(n^2) time, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the full system, including the PageRank computation. This is important for understanding the practical applicability of the approach, especially for longer sentences.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to require "no training data," but states they used the English treebank during development to tune parameters. Could the authors clarify whether they consider this development phase as "training" and how they would handle cases where no language is available for development (e.g., for a truly zero-resource language)?

2. The paper states that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (Table 5), but the numbers show UDPG (68.1) is slightly lower than MSDG (70.5). Could the authors clarify this discrepancy?

3. The paper excludes the NOUN → VERB head rule to avoid making the verb-noun relation less conclusive. Could the authors provide an estimate of how much this limitation affects performance on languages with complex relative clauses?

4. The paper mentions that the adposition direction is estimated from 10-15 sentences. For languages with very small test sets (e.g., less than 15 sentences), how would the system handle this estimation, and what impact would it have on parsing quality?

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating how UD constraints can be leveraged to create a highly competitive unsupervised dependency parser that is also more robust to domain shifts than supervised transfer approaches. The core methodology is sound, the results are compelling, and the system is well-motivated.

However, the paper would benefit from several improvements before acceptance. The claims about "no training data" need to be more precise, and the paper should provide a more thorough comparison to other unsupervised parsers to better establish the novelty of their approach. The error analysis could also be strengthened with deeper insights into failure modes.

With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field of unsupervised parsing and cross-lingual processing. The authors should clarify their development process, provide a more comprehensive comparison to other unsupervised parsers, and conduct a deeper error analysis to strengthen their claims about the value of leveraging UD constraints.
