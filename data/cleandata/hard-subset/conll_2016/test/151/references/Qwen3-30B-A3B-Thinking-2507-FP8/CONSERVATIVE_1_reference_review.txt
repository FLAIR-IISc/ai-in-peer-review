

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), a rule-based dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints of the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The system uses a small set of head-dependent rules, personalized PageRank to score content words, and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors claim UDP achieves competitive performance with supervised multi-source delexicalized parsers (MSD) while being more robust to domain shifts.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A rule-based dependency parser that requires no training data and strictly adheres to UD formalism constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves)
2. A method for estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data without language-specific parameters
3. A two-step decoding approach that enforces UD's leaf constraint for function words

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical grounding in UD constraints**: The paper's method is well-motivated by the UD formalism, which is a well-established standard in cross-lingual parsing. The authors clearly articulate how their system adheres to UD's specific constraints (e.g., function words as leaves, single-rooted trees), which provides a solid theoretical foundation. This methodological soundness is a significant strength, especially in a field where many approaches fail to respect linguistic constraints.

2. **Competitive performance with supervised baselines**: The results demonstrate that UDP with gold POS (UDPG) achieves remarkable performance, with an average difference of only 6.4% from the supervised MSDG baseline (multi-source delexicalized parser with gold POS). The fact that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (55.3% vs 54.8% UAS) is particularly noteworthy and provides strong evidence for the approach's validity.

3. **Superior robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides compelling evidence that UDP is more robust to domain changes than MSD. The standard deviation across domains is consistently lower for UDP (Table 6), which aligns with earlier findings by Plank and van Noord (2010). This is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications where domain shifts are common, and it's well-supported by the detailed cross-domain evaluation in Table 7.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to established unsupervised baselines**: The paper compares UDP to a rule-based baseline (BL) and a supervised transfer system (MSD), but fails to compare to well-established unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., Klein and Manning, 2004; Spitkovsky et al., 2010). Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether UDP represents a meaningful improvement over the unsupervised parsing state of the art. The claim that "unsupervised parsers fall far behind approaches involving any sort of supervision" is well-known, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how UDP advances beyond existing unsupervised methods.

2. **Over-reliance on gold POS tags**: The paper shows that UDP performs well with gold POS (UDPG) but significantly worse with predicted POS (UDPP) and dramatically worse with the naive POS scenario (UDPN). The error propagation from POS to dependency is 0.37 for UDP compared to 0.46 for MSD, but this still means UDP's performance is heavily dependent on accurate POS tagging. In real-world applications, POS tagging is rarely perfect (the authors report TnT achieves 94.1% accuracy), making this dependency a significant practical limitation. The paper doesn't address how to mitigate this issue.

3. **Insufficient evidence for novelty and contribution of components**: The paper claims to build on Søgaard's (2012b) PageRank method with "two key differences" (personalized PR and two-step decoding), but the ablation study (Section 6.1) only compares UDP to a baseline without PR (UDPNoPR), not to Søgaard's original method. The paper doesn't demonstrate how much each component contributes to the overall improvement, nor does it convincingly show that these are novel contributions. The claim that "even without any training data, we parse nearly as well as a delexicalized transfer parser" is compelling, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these specific methodological choices are what enable this performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does UDP compare to established unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., Klein and Manning, 2004; Spitkovsky et al., 2010) in terms of UAS? Given that the paper claims "the performance of unsupervised parsers falls far behind the approaches involving any sort of supervision," it would be valuable to see how UDP compares to these established unsupervised methods.

2. The paper demonstrates that UDP's performance drops significantly when using predicted POS tags (UDPP vs UDPG), with an average difference of 2.2% compared to MSD's 2.7%. How would you address this dependency on accurate POS tagging in real-world applications where POS tagging is rarely perfect? Would you consider incorporating a POS tagger into the system or using a more robust approach to handle POS errors?

3. The paper claims that "UDP, the simple unsupervised system with PageRank, outperforms the adjacency baselines (BL) by 4% on average on the two type-based naive POS tag scenario." However, the adjacency baseline is a very weak baseline (all words depend on their left or right neighbor). How does UDP compare to more sophisticated rule-based or unsupervised parsers in this naive POS scenario?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a methodologically sound approach that leverages the UD formalism and demonstrates competitive performance with a strong baseline, the lack of comparison to established unsupervised parsers and the over-reliance on gold POS tags are significant weaknesses. The claimed novelty is not sufficiently substantiated, and the paper doesn't provide enough evidence that the specific methodological choices (personalized PageRank, two-step decoding) are responsible for the observed improvements over existing rule-based approaches. The paper would be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison to unsupervised parsing baselines and a more detailed analysis of the contribution of each component to the overall performance. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's claims about novelty and performance improvement not fully supported by the evidence provided.
