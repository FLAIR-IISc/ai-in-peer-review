

# Review: "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The system relies solely on UD's linguistic constraints and explicit part-of-speech (POS) tags. It uses personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate, incorporates UD's restriction that function words must be leaves, and estimates adposition direction (prepositions vs. postpositions) directly from test data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages across three POS setups (gold POS, predicted POS using TnT, and a naive two-POS tag scenario) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) and a simple rule-based baseline.

## Main Contributions (as I see them, in order of strength)
1. **A completely unsupervised dependency parser for UD that requires no training data**, leveraging UD's formal constraints to guide parsing decisions without language-specific parameters.
2. **A personalized PageRank approach for main predicate identification** that significantly improves parsing performance (9 UAS points on average) compared to a baseline with word-order ranking.
3. **A method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data** (via ADP-NOMINAL vs. NOMINAL-ADP bigram frequency), eliminating the need for language-specific parameters.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness of the Core Approach**: The paper presents a well-motivated and logically consistent approach that properly leverages UD's formal constraints. The two-step decoding algorithm (Section 3.3) effectively enforces UD's requirement that function words must be leaves, while the personalized PageRank (Section 3.1) provides a principled way to identify the main predicate. The ablation study (Section 6.1) clearly demonstrates the contribution of each component, showing that personalized PageRank alone contributes 9 UAS points on average compared to a baseline with word-order ranking.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The results are compelling, with UDP achieving UAS scores within 6.4% of a supervised multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) on average. Notably, UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (69.7% vs. 68.1%), which is a significant achievement for an unsupervised parser. The cross-domain evaluation (Section 6.3) provides robust evidence that UDP is more robust to domain shifts than MSD, with a much lower standard deviation across domains (e.g., 1.8 for UDPG vs. 4.5 for MSDG in Table 6).

3. **Novelty in Domain Robustness**: The paper provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between rule-based and data-driven parsing approaches. The evidence that UDP's rule-based approach is more robust to domain shifts than MSD (a data-driven approach) is significant and well-supported by the detailed cross-domain evaluation across multiple languages and genres (Table 7). This finding aligns with Plank and van Noord (2010) but extends it to UD and a more comprehensive evaluation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Approach**: While the application of personalized PageRank to UD parsing is novel, the core approach (using head rules and POS constraints) is not entirely new. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how their approach differs from prior work on rule-aided unsupervised parsing (e.g., Søgaard, 2012b). The novelty appears incremental rather than groundbreaking, as the system builds directly on Søgaard's (2012b) work with specific UD adaptations.

2. **Narrow Comparison Set**: The paper compares UDP to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) and a simple rule-based baseline (BL), but fails to compare to other unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., DMV, Viterbi training). Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether UDP represents a significant advance over the state of the art in unsupervised parsing. The paper claims UDP "compares favorably to delexicalized parsing," but doesn't establish how it compares to other unsupervised methods.

3. **Extreme POS Scenario**: The naive two-POS tag scenario (UDPN) is quite extreme (only two POS tags: CONTENT and FUNCTION) and doesn't reflect realistic POS tagging conditions. The authors could have provided more nuanced analysis of how the system performs with varying levels of POS accuracy, rather than just comparing to this extreme scenario. The results in Table 5 show UDP's performance drops significantly in this scenario (UAS of 29.9% vs. 63.9% for MSDG), but this doesn't necessarily reflect real-world performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims UDP "compares favorably to delexicalized parsing," but doesn't compare to other unsupervised parsers (e.g., DMV, Viterbi training). Could the authors provide a comparison to these standard unsupervised parsers to better establish the novelty and performance of their approach?

2. The naive two-POS tag scenario (UDPN) seems unrealistic for most practical applications. How would the authors justify this as a meaningful evaluation scenario, and would the results change significantly if they used a more realistic multi-POS tag setup?

3. The paper mentions that "the system cannot infer relative clauses" due to excluding the NOUN → VERB head rule. Could the authors elaborate on why this rule was excluded, and whether including it would significantly improve performance on languages with frequent relative clauses (e.g., German, Hungarian)?
