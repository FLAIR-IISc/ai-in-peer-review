

# Peer Review: Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training

## Summary of the Paper
This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and part-of-speech (POS) tags. The system uses personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate (root) of a sentence, followed by a two-step decoding process that first attaches content words and then function words according to UD's constraints. The authors claim their parser achieves competitive performance compared to supervised cross-lingual transfer parsers while being more robust to domain shifts. They evaluate the system on 32 languages from UD v1.2, comparing it to a rule-based baseline and a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, and also assess its robustness to domain changes.

## Main Contributions (ordered by strength)
1. **A practical unsupervised dependency parser that achieves competitive performance without training data**, leveraging UD's formalism constraints rather than data-driven approaches. This is significant for low-resource languages where training data is scarce.

2. **A method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data** (using bigram frequencies), which is particularly valuable for truly low-resource languages where language-specific parameters are unavailable.

3. **Thorough evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domain shifts**, demonstrating that a constraint-based approach is more robust to domain changes than data-driven methods, providing valuable insights for practical applications.

## Strengths

1. **Strong evidence of practical value for low-resource languages**: The paper demonstrates that their unsupervised parser achieves nearly the same performance as a supervised cross-lingual parser (MSD) on most languages (average difference of 6.4%), which is impressive for an unsupervised approach. This is particularly significant because it shows that by leveraging UD's formalism constraints, a parser can achieve competitive results without requiring training data, making it highly applicable to low-resource languages.

2. **Compelling evidence of domain robustness**: The authors provide thorough analysis of the parser's performance across different domains, showing that UDP has a much lower standard deviation across domains (average 1.8) compared to the supervised MSD (average 4.5). This finding is important because it demonstrates that rule-based approaches can be more robust to domain shifts than data-driven methods, which is a valuable insight for practical NLP applications where domain shifts are common.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The paper evaluates the system under three different POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and naive POS) and across multiple domains. The detailed error analysis by POS category (e.g., showing that PUNCT has low UAS scores but CONJ performs better) provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach. The inclusion of both gold and predicted POS scenarios is particularly valuable for understanding real-world applicability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient comparison with state-of-the-art unsupervised parsers**: While the paper compares with a rule-based baseline and a supervised cross-lingual parser, it doesn't adequately compare with recent unsupervised parsers that might have made progress. The paper states that "the performance of unsupervised parsers falls far behind the approaches involving any sort of supervision," but doesn't provide a comparison with the most recent unsupervised parsers (e.g., those from the CoNLL 2017 shared task). This weakens the claim that their method is competitive with unsupervised approaches.

2. **Insufficient justification for language-specific decisions**: The paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters" but then states that "AUX, DET, and SCONJ are right-attaching, while CONJ and PUNCT are left-attaching" without explaining how these decisions were made. The authors state they determined this on English dev data, which contradicts their claim of being language-independent. This undermines their main claim about the parser being truly language-agnostic.

3. **Potential overestimation due to UD's formalism**: The evaluation is conducted on UD treebanks, which are annotated according to UD's formalism. The authors don't sufficiently address whether their parser would perform as well on treebanks that don't strictly follow UD conventions. This is particularly relevant for languages where the UD treebank may not fully conform to the formalism (e.g., some languages in the UD v1.2 release). The paper lacks a discussion of how their approach would perform on non-UD treebanks.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (Table 5 shows UDPG=55.3, MSDG=55.3 - they're equal). Can you clarify if there's a typo in the table or if you're referring to a different metric (e.g., LAS) or evaluation setup?

2. The paper claims the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters" because it estimates adposition direction directly from test data. However, the authors state that "AUX, DET, and SCONJ are right-attaching, while CONJ and PUNCT are left-attaching" based on English development data. Can you clarify how these decisions are truly language-independent, and provide evidence that this holds across multiple languages?

3. The paper excludes Japanese from the evaluation because "the treebank is distributed without word forms." However, UD v1.2 has 33 languages, and the paper states they exclude Japanese "out of 33 languages." The paper should clarify why Japanese is excluded from the main evaluation but not from the count of languages, and whether the exclusion is due to technical limitations rather than language-specific issues.

## Additional Comments
The paper is well-written and the technical details are clear. The evaluation methodology is thorough and provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach. However, the claims about the parser being "completely free of language-specific parameters" need stronger justification to be convincing. The authors should clarify how their approach would generalize to languages where UD conventions differ from English (e.g., verb-final languages).

The results on domain robustness are particularly impressive and could be a major contribution to the field if properly contextualized. I recommend the authors strengthen the comparison with unsupervised parsers and provide more evidence for the language independence of their approach to make their contribution more compelling.
