

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The key insight is leveraging UD's explicit constraints (single-rooted trees, function words always being dependents of content words) to build a parser that doesn't rely on training examples or transfer learning.

**Primary Contributions (in order of strength):**

1. **Radical conceptual shift**: Proposing an unsupervised dependency parser that fundamentally relies on UD constraints rather than data-driven approaches. This is a bold and novel direction that challenges the prevailing paradigm in dependency parsing.

2. **Personalized PageRank for main predicate identification**: A clever adaptation of PageRank that incorporates UD's constraint that the main predicate should be the highest-ranked node, with a 5x weight on the estimated main predicate.

3. **Two-step decoding ensuring UD-compliant leaf status**: A novel decoding approach that first attaches content words (ensuring they're not leaves) and then attaches function words (ensuring they're leaves), directly implementing UD's constraint.

## Strengths

1. **Truly novel approach to dependency parsing**: This paper offers a completely different perspective on dependency parsing that doesn't rely on training data or transfer learning. The authors successfully demonstrate that a parser built on UD constraints can achieve performance competitive with supervised transfer methods (average 6.4% difference from MSDG). This is a significant conceptual contribution that challenges the field's prevailing data-driven assumptions.

2. **Strong empirical validation of the core idea**: The paper convincingly shows that their unsupervised approach performs remarkably well (UDPG is within 6.4% of MSDG on average), with the surprising result that it outperforms the supervised baseline on Hindi. This isn't just a theoretical curiosity—it's a practical approach that works.

3. **Superior robustness to domain shifts**: The most compelling evidence for the value of the approach is the cross-domain evaluation. UDP has significantly lower standard deviation across domains than the data-driven MSD system (Table 6), confirming that rule-based approaches can be more stable to domain shifts. This is a major practical advantage often overlooked in dependency parsing research.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading "no training" claim**: The authors state their system "requires no training," but they used English treebank data during development to assess head rules and tune PageRank parameters. This is a significant weakness as it undermines the paper's strongest claim. The paper should clearly acknowledge this development process and clarify the scope of their "no training" assertion.

2. **Lack of theoretical grounding for head rules**: The head rules in Table 1 are simply used without justification for why these specific rules would generalize across 32 languages. The paper doesn't explain why these particular POS relations should work universally, which is a critical gap given the system's dependence on these rules.

3. **Limited analysis of why the approach works**: While the paper shows the system works, it doesn't provide deep insights into why it works. The paper would be significantly stronger with more detailed error analysis, ablation studies showing which UD constraints are most critical, or analysis of how the personalized PageRank specifically contributes to performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "no training," but mentions using English treebank during development. Could you clarify the exact scope of this development process and how much it affects the "no training" claim? Would the system work equally well with development data from a non-Indo-European language?

2. The paper shows UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case? Is it related to Hindi's linguistic structure or UD conventions?

3. How would you handle languages where UD conventions don't perfectly align with actual syntactic structure (e.g., where certain words function as heads rather than dependents)? How flexible is your approach to these exceptions?

## Recommendation

**Accept with minor revisions**

This paper presents a genuinely novel and bold approach to dependency parsing that challenges the field's prevailing paradigm. The core idea of leveraging UD constraints rather than data-driven methods is significant, and the empirical results are impressive for an unsupervised approach. The cross-domain robustness finding is particularly compelling.

While the "no training" claim needs clarification and the head rules require better justification, these are fixable issues that don't undermine the paper's core contribution. The novelty and practical significance of the work outweigh these weaknesses, especially given the reviewer's preference for novel concepts over methodological perfection.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by clarifying the development process and providing more theoretical grounding for the head rules. With these revisions, the paper would make a significant contribution to the field.
