

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper presents a novel unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints of the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism and part-of-speech tags. The system, called UDP, leverages UD's specific constraints (single-rooted trees, function words always being dependents of content words) as the foundation for parsing, rather than as a target format. Key innovations include personalized PageRank for main predicate identification, two-step decoding to treat content and function words differently according to UD, and estimating adposition direction directly from test data without language-specific parameters.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A fundamentally new approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that uses UD constraints as the core guiding principle rather than as a target format
2. A method for completely eliminating language-specific parameters through on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test data
3. Demonstration that an unsupervised parser can achieve competitive performance with supervised transfer parsers while being more robust to domain shifts

## Strengths

1. **Highly Novel Core Approach**: This paper's central idea is exceptionally novel in the field. Most unsupervised dependency parsing work either ignores UD constraints or uses them as a target format, not as the foundational basis for parsing. By making UD constraints the *core* of the parsing algorithm, the authors present a fundamentally different paradigm that could inspire new research directions. This is the most significant contribution, as it represents a fresh perspective on unsupervised parsing that has been largely unexplored.

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across 32 languages in three different POS scenarios, plus domain shift analysis. The results are compelling: UDPG (gold POS) achieves 63.9% UAS compared to MSDG's 61.2% UAS (with only a 6.4% average difference), and UDP outperforms MSD on one language (Hindi). The domain shift analysis is particularly impressive, showing UDP has much lower standard deviation across domains than supervised methods, confirming its robustness.

3. **Practical Implications for Low-Resource Languages**: The approach requires no training data at all, making it uniquely suited for truly low-resource languages where training data is scarce or nonexistent. This is a significant practical contribution that addresses a major pain point in NLP for many languages.

4. **Clear Demonstration of Robustness to Domain Shift**: The paper provides strong evidence that the rule-based approach is less sensitive to domain shifts than data-driven methods. This is an important practical consideration that many supervised parsers struggle with, and the results (lower standard deviation across domains) are convincing.

5. **Elegant Methodological Simplicity**: The system is remarkably simple in its design (no training, no language-specific parameters) yet achieves competitive results. This elegance, combined with the strong performance, makes the approach particularly compelling.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Handling of UD Deviations**: The paper acknowledges that UD is "constantly-improving" and that treebanks vary in their formalism compliance, but doesn't thoroughly address how the system handles languages with significant deviations from standard UD conventions. For example, the paper mentions that "the interpretation of, e.g., the AUX-VERB or DET-PRON distinctions might differ" but doesn't provide analysis of how the system handles such cases.

2. **Development Data Bias**: The system was tuned using English treebank data, which could introduce bias toward English-like structures. While the cross-lingual results are strong (24 out of 32 languages perform better than English), this is a potential limitation that the authors should address more thoroughly.

3. **Limited Error Analysis**: The paper provides some error analysis (e.g., on POS types), but a more detailed analysis of specific linguistic phenomena where the parser fails would strengthen the contribution. For instance, the paper notes the system "cannot infer relative clauses" but doesn't provide examples or discuss how to address this limitation.

4. **Practical Implementation Concerns**: The paper mentions that Japanese was excluded due to missing word forms, but doesn't discuss how the system would handle other languages with similar issues. For a truly universal approach, the authors should address how to handle languages without full word forms.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the parser "cannot infer relative clauses" due to not including a NOUN → VERB head rule. Could you elaborate on how this limitation might be addressed in future work, and whether it's a fundamental constraint of the UD approach or could be overcome with additional rules?

2. In the domain shift evaluation, UDP outperforms MSD on some domains (e.g., Italian questions). What specific linguistic features or domain characteristics might explain why UDP performs better in certain domains?

3. The paper states that UDP uses "personalized PageRank" but doesn't provide much detail on how the personalization vector is constructed for languages with different word orders. How would the system handle languages with verb-final structures (like Japanese or Korean) without modifying the personalization strategy?

## Overall Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper presents a highly novel and bold approach to dependency parsing that fundamentally shifts the paradigm from data-driven to constraint-driven parsing. The core idea of using UD constraints as the foundation rather than as a target format is exceptionally fresh and represents a significant new direction in the field. The results are compelling, showing competitive performance with supervised methods while offering greater robustness to domain shifts. While there are some minor methodological limitations (as noted in weaknesses), these do not undermine the fundamental novelty and significance of the contribution. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel work that CoNLL should prioritize, even if it has some minor imperfections in methodology or incomplete validation. The paper makes a strong case for a new direction in unsupervised parsing that could have significant practical impact for low-resource languages.
