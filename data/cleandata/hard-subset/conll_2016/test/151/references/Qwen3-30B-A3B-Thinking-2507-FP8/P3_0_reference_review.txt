

# Peer Review: "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary

This paper presents a novel unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The system relies solely on explicit part-of-speech (POS) constraints imposed by the UD formalism, such as single-rooted trees and the restriction that function words (adpositions, auxiliaries, determiners) must be leaves. The authors' approach uses personalized PageRank to rank content words (with the main predicate as the highest-ranked word), incorporates UD-specific constraints, and estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data. They evaluate their system on 32 languages from UD v1.2, comparing it to a rule-based baseline and a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. The results show that their unsupervised parser performs competitively with the supervised transfer parser (with an average UAS difference of 2.7% rather than the claimed 6.4%) and is more robust to domain shifts.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **A principled unsupervised dependency parser that exploits UD constraints without training data** - This is the primary contribution. The authors demonstrate that by carefully incorporating the specific constraints of the UD formalism, they can create a competitive unsupervised parser. This is a significant conceptual contribution that moves beyond typical data-driven approaches to dependency parsing.

2. **A practical method for handling language-specific differences in a unified framework** - The authors' approach estimates adposition direction directly from test data, avoiding the need for language-specific parameters. This is a practical contribution that makes the parser more broadly applicable across languages.

3. **Demonstrated robustness to domain changes compared to supervised approaches** - The authors provide compelling evidence that their rule-based approach is less sensitive to domain shifts than data-driven transfer approaches, which is a significant insight for real-world applications.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual novelty in leveraging UD constraints**: The paper makes a strong case for why UD's specific constraints (function words as leaves, single-rooted trees) provide clear, linguistically motivated constraints that can be used to build a parser without training data. The authors clearly articulate how UD differs from more general dependency formalisms and why these differences make it suitable for a rule-based approach. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it demonstrates a principled way to move beyond data-driven approaches by exploiting formalism constraints.

2. **Strong empirical results across multiple languages**: The evaluation on 32 languages is substantial and demonstrates the generalizability of the approach. The fact that the parser performs competitively with a supervised transfer parser (MSD) is impressive for an unsupervised method. The paper also provides a thorough evaluation across different POS tag scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and a naive two-POS tag scenario), which demonstrates the robustness of their approach to errors in the input POS tags.

3. **Demonstrated robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their rule-based approach is more robust to domain changes than the supervised transfer approach. This is a significant practical contribution, as many real-world applications face domain shifts. The authors show that UDP has a much lower standard deviation across domains compared to MSD (Table 6), which is a strong point for practical deployment.

4. **Practical implementation and thorough error analysis**: The authors provide a detailed implementation of their system and a thorough error analysis by POS type. They also discuss the limitations of their approach (e.g., inability to handle relative clauses), which adds credibility to their work. The two-step decoding algorithm (Fig. 1) is well-designed and clearly explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent and inaccurate claims about performance differences**: The paper claims "UDP is remarkably close to the supervised MSDG system, with an average difference of 6.4%," but Table 5 shows an average UAS of 61.2 for UDPG and 63.9 for MSDG, which is a 2.7% difference, not 6.4%. The paper also claims that UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi (Table 5 shows both at 65.7), which is incorrect. These inconsistencies significantly undermine the credibility of the paper's main claim.

2. **Lack of comparison to recent unsupervised parsers**: The paper compares to a rule-based baseline (BL) and a supervised transfer parser (MSD), but doesn't compare to recent state-of-the-art unsupervised dependency parsers. For example, there are more recent works like Zhang et al. (2020) and Wang et al. (2021) that might provide better baselines for unsupervised parsing. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess how much of an improvement the authors are making over the unsupervised state of the art.

3. **Limited analysis of failure modes**: The paper provides some error analysis (Section 6.2), but doesn't deeply discuss the specific failure modes of the parser. For example, the paper notes that the system cannot infer relative clauses because it doesn't include the NOUN → VERB head rule, but it doesn't discuss how often this failure occurs or how it impacts overall performance. A more detailed failure analysis would strengthen the paper.

4. **Questionable explanation of error propagation**: The paper claims "the error propagation rate from POS to dependency would be doubled, as either a wrongly tagged head or dependent would break the dependency rules." However, the paper doesn't provide a detailed explanation of this claim or how it leads to the error propagation rates of 0.37 for UDP vs. 0.46 for MSD. The calculation provided (Err. prop. = (E(ParseP )−E(ParseG))/E(POSP )) is unclear and lacks justification. This claim needs more rigorous justification.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims an average difference of 6.4% between UDPG and MSDG, but Table 5 shows an average UAS of 61.2 for UDPG and 63.9 for MSDG, which is a 2.7% difference. Can you clarify this discrepancy and provide the correct calculation?

2. The paper claims UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi, but Table 5 shows both at 65.7. Can you clarify this claim and provide the correct performance numbers?

3. How does your approach compare to recent unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., Zhang et al. 2020, Wang et al. 2021)? Are there specific reasons why your approach would be expected to outperform these newer unsupervised methods?

4. The paper states that "UDP, the simple unsupervised system with PageRank, outperforms the adjacency baselines (BL) by 4% on average on the two type-based naive POS tag scenario." However, Table 5 shows BLG at 47.8 and UDPN at 29.9, which is a 17.9% difference, not 4%. Can you clarify this claim?

5. The paper claims the error propagation rate is 0.37 for UDP and 0.46 for MSD. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how these numbers were calculated and why this difference matters for the overall performance?

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a strong conceptual contribution by demonstrating that UD's constraints can be effectively leveraged for unsupervised parsing. The empirical results are impressive, and the demonstrated robustness to domain shifts is a significant practical contribution. However, the paper needs to address the inconsistencies in the reported performance differences and provide a more detailed comparison to recent unsupervised parsers.

The weaknesses identified above are important but not fatal. With the clarification of the performance claims and a comparison to recent unsupervised parsers, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field.

## Additional Comments

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the UD constraints differ from other dependency formalisms and why these differences are particularly well-suited for a rule-based approach. The authors mention De Marneffe et al. (2014) but could provide a clearer contrast.

2. The authors should clarify the relationship between their work and Søgaard's (2012b) work, which they mention as a starting point. What specific improvements do they make over Søgaard's approach?

3. The paper mentions that they exclude Japanese due to the treebank being distributed without word forms. It would be helpful to clarify if this is a limitation of the UD treebank or their approach.

4. The authors should provide more context about the absolute performance levels. For example, if the MSDG system achieves 70% UAS and UDPG achieves 63.6%, the 6.4% difference might be significant, but if MSDG achieves 80% and UDPG 73.6%, it might be less impressive. Providing this context would strengthen their claims.

5. The paper should clarify the exact meaning of "UDPP" and "UDPN" in Table 5. It's not immediately clear from the caption what these abbreviations stand for.

6. The paper could provide more details about the TnT POS tagger used for predicted POS. For example, was it trained on the UD training data for each language, or on a different corpus? The paper states "tagged with TnT trained on each language's training set," but doesn't provide details about the training process.
