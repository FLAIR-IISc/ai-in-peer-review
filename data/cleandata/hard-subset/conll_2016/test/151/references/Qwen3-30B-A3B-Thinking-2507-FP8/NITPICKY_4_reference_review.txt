

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary

This paper proposes UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser that claims to require no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints. The system uses personalized PageRank to rank content words, a two-step decoding process that treats content and function words differently, and estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate their system on 32 languages across three POS scenarios, comparing it to a rule-based baseline and a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. A dependency parser that claims to be unsupervised and fully leverages UD constraints without training data
2. A method for estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data
3. A two-step decoding algorithm that ensures function words are leaves (as required by UD)

## Strengths

1. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a good technical explanation of the algorithm and its components.
2. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering 32 languages across multiple POS scenarios and domain shifts.
3. The robustness to domain changes is well-documented, with extensive cross-domain evaluation showing UDP is more stable than data-driven methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Major contradiction in main claim**: The paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters" and "requires no training" (Section 1), but uses the English treebank during development to determine attachment direction for function words (AUX, DET, SCONJ are right-attaching, CONJ and PUNCT are left-attaching). This is a language-specific parameter that contradicts the paper's main claim. The paper states: "d) making it completely free of language-specific parameters by estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data." However, they do not estimate the attachment direction for AUX, DET, SCONJ, CONJ, and PUNCT on test data; they use English as a proxy. This undermines the paper's core contribution.

2. **Misleading claim of being "unsupervised"**: The authors state: "Our system does not use any training or unlabeled data. We have used the English treebank during development to assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters (Sec. 3.1) and function-word directionality (Sec. 3.2)." Using a language-specific resource (English treebank) for development to tune parameters and determine attachment direction violates the definition of unsupervised learning. This contradicts the paper's main claim of being unsupervised.

3. **Inconsistent evaluation setup**: The paper evaluates the system with predicted POS tags, but the POS tagger (TnT) is trained on each language's training data (Section 5.2). This means the POS tagging step is language-specific and supervised, contradicting the unsupervised claim. The paper states they use "no training," but they are using language-specific training data for POS tagging.

4. **Inconsistent reference to the number of languages**: The paper claims to evaluate on "32 languages" (Section 1), but states "Out of 33 languages in UD v1.2. We exclude Japanese..." (Section 2). This inconsistency (32 vs. 33) indicates poor proofreading and undermines the paper's credibility.

5. **Inconsistent description of the two-step decoding algorithm**: The text states: "To enforce the leaf status of function nodes, the algorithm first attaches all content words (C), and then all function words (F) in the second block where H is not updated, thereby ensuring leafness for all f ∈ F." However, Figure 1 shows the algorithm uses the same set H for both content and function words, which contradicts the claim that H is not updated in the second block. This inconsistency between text and algorithm undermines the clarity of the method.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you justify using English to determine the attachment direction for function words (AUX, DET, SCONJ, CONJ, PUNCT) when the paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters"? What evidence do you have that these attachment directions are universal across all languages?

2. The paper claims to be "unsupervised" but uses English treebank for development. Could you clarify what "development" means in this context, and why it's acceptable to use language-specific data for development while claiming to be unsupervised?

3. The evaluation with predicted POS tags uses a POS tagger trained on language-specific data. How is this consistent with the claim of being "completely free of language-specific parameters"?

## Additional Comments

- The paper uses "UD" and "UDS" interchangeably (e.g., "UDS formalism" in the abstract), which is inconsistent.
- The paper refers to "Table 5" for head rules in Section 5.1, but Table 5 is the evaluation table, not the head rules. The head rules are in Table 1.
- The paper's abstract doesn't mention the rule-based baseline, which is a key comparison in the paper, creating confusion about what the main comparison is.

## Recommendation

Reject

The paper's main contribution is fundamentally undermined by the contradiction between its claims and methodology. The authors claim to have created a completely unsupervised, language-agnostic parser, but they use English to determine attachment direction for function words and use language-specific data for POS tagging. This is a serious methodological flaw that invalidates the core contribution of the paper. While the paper has some interesting results, the main claim is misleading and contradicted by the methodology. The inconsistencies in the paper (number of languages, table references, algorithm description) further indicate poor proofreading and attention to detail. Given the stringent standards of CoNLL, I recommend rejection.
