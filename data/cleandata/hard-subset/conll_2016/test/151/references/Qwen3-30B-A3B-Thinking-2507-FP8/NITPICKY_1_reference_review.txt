

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
The paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that claims to require no training data and relies solely on UD constraints. The authors propose a system using personalized PageRank to score content words, UD-compliant head rules, and adposition direction estimation directly from test data. The system is evaluated on 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and naive POS) and compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A dependency parsing approach that leverages UD constraints without training data (though this claim is undermined by the development process)
2. A novel application of personalized PageRank for content word ranking
3. An analysis of cross-domain robustness for rule-based parsers

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across 32 languages and three POS scenarios, which is valuable for understanding the system's performance profile.

2. The analysis of cross-domain robustness (section 6.3) is well-executed and provides meaningful insights about the stability of rule-based approaches compared to data-driven methods.

3. The paper's focus on UD constraints is timely and aligns with the growing importance of the Universal Dependencies project in cross-lingual NLP.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Fundamental contradiction in core claim ("no training" vs. actual development process)
The paper's title and abstract claim to present a parser that "requires no training," but section 3.1 explicitly states: "We have used the English treebank during development to assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters (Sec. 3.1) and function-word directionality (Sec. 3.2)." This contradicts the paper's central claim. The system is not truly unsupervised as claimed, since it was tuned on English data. This is a critical flaw that undermines the paper's main contribution and should be grounds for rejection.

### 2. Inconsistent and confusing evaluation metrics
The paper uses inconsistent notation for evaluation setups, which creates significant confusion:
- Table 5 uses "UDPG" (UDP with gold POS) and "MSDG" (multi-source delexicalized with gold POS)
- In section 5.2, they state "We evaluate UDP on two alternative scenarios. The first is predicted POS (UDPP), where we tag the respective test set with TnT... The second is a naive type-constrained two-POS tag scenario (UDPN)"
- However, in Table 5, they report "UDPP" and "UDPN" as separate columns, but the table caption and text refer to "UDPP" as the predicted POS version, which is inconsistent with the earlier "UDPP" definition.

This inconsistency makes it impossible to clearly understand the results. The claim that "UDPG is remarkably close to the supervised MSDG system, with an average difference of 6.4%" is misleading since MSDG outperforms UDPG on 30 of 32 languages (with only Hindi showing the opposite trend).

### 3. Technical error in the decoding algorithm
The paper claims the algorithm ensures "leaf status of function nodes" (section 3.3), but the description states: "the algorithm first attaches all content words (C), and then all function words (F) in the second block where H is not updated, thereby ensuring leafness for all f ∈ F." However, the algorithm as described (Fig. 1) does not prevent function words from having children. For example, a function word could become a head of another function word (e.g., "of" in "a picture of the cat" where "of" would be a function word head of "the" and "cat"). This violates UD's constraint that function words should be leaves, and the algorithm fails to ensure this.

### 4. Misrepresentation of UD formalism
The paper states: "UD prescribes that trees are single-rooted, and that function words like adpositions, auxiliaries, and determiners are always dependents of content words, while other formalisms might treat them as heads (De Marneffe et al., 2014)." This is inaccurate. In UD, function words can be heads of other function words (e.g., "of" is a function word that can head a noun phrase), but they are always dependents of content words. The paper's description is misleading and contradicts the actual UD guidelines.

### 5. Confusing language coverage and exclusion rationale
The paper states "Out of 33 languages in UD v1.2. We exclude Japanese because the treebank is distributed without word forms and hence we can not provide results on predicted POS." However, they don't explain why they can't provide results for Japanese with gold POS (which would be the primary evaluation scenario). The UD treebank for Japanese does have word forms, so this exclusion is poorly justified. The paper then refers to "32 languages" throughout, but the exclusion rationale doesn't explain why Japanese was excluded from the main evaluation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to require "no training data" but explicitly states it used English treebank data for development and tuning. How can the authors reconcile this contradiction? What would happen if they didn't use English treebank data for development?

2. The decoding algorithm (Fig. 1) is supposed to ensure function words are leaves, but the description doesn't prevent function words from becoming heads of other function words. How does the algorithm actually ensure function words are leaves in the dependency tree?

3. The paper states "function words like adpositions, auxiliaries, and determiners are always dependents of content words," but this is not entirely accurate in UD (function words can be heads of other function words). How does the parser handle cases where a function word is the head of another function word?

4. In Table 5, the paper claims UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi (53.9 vs 53.0), but the difference is only 0.9%. Given the standard deviations reported in Table 6 (2.5 for UDPG), is this difference statistically significant? Why is the paper emphasizing this single language as evidence of superiority when MSDG outperforms UDPG on 30 out of 32 languages?

5. The paper excludes Japanese from the main evaluation because "the treebank is distributed without word forms." However, Japanese UD treebanks do have word forms. Why couldn't the authors provide results for Japanese using gold POS (which would be the primary evaluation scenario)?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting approach to dependency parsing that leverages UD constraints, but the fundamental contradiction in the "no training" claim (despite using English treebank data for development) is a critical flaw that undermines the entire paper's premise. This is compounded by inconsistent metrics, technical errors in the algorithm description, and a misleading representation of UD formalism. While the evaluation is comprehensive, the methodological flaws are too severe to justify acceptance. The paper would need to be completely rewritten to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication.
