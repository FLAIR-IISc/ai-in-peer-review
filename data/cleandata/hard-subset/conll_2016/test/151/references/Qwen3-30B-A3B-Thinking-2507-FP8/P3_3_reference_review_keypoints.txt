- Summary: Proposes UDP, a rule-based dependency parser requiring no training that leverages UD constraints, estimates adposition direction from test data, and claims competitive, domain-robust performance.

- Strength: Linguistically sound — exploits UD constraints (single-root, function words as dependents) to shift from data-driven learning to constraint exploitation.

- Strength: Robustness to domain shifts — shows more stable cross-domain performance and lower variance compared to supervised transfer (MSD).

- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation — evaluates across gold, predicted, and naive POS scenarios and shows lower error propagation from POS tagging than MSD.

- Weakness: Limited novelty — core methods (personalized PageRank, two-step decoding) closely follow Søgaard (2012b) and novelty over prior work is not convincingly argued.

- Weakness: Overstated performance claims — the performance gap with supervised delexicalized parsing is modest (avg 6.4%) and UDPG only outperforms MSDG on one language (Hindi).

- Weakness: Lack of detailed error analysis — POS breakdown is superficial; missing deeper analysis on long-distance dependencies, coordination, and typological variations.

- Weakness: Domain evaluation limitations — cross-domain tests use small, manually curated datasets that may not represent real domain shifts; need larger, more diverse splits.

- Question: How do results compare directly to Søgaard (2012b) under the same languages and protocol to contextualize gains?

- Question: Provide examples and frequency data where excluding NOUN→VERB (relative clauses) causes significant errors across languages.

- Question: How does adposition direction estimation perform with very small test sets (<10–15 sentences)?

- Question: How is UDP still "language-free" if adposition direction must be estimated from limited test data?

- Overall: Solid, well-executed contribution showing UD-constraint benefits and domain robustness, but novelty and contextualization are insufficient; recommendation: Weak Accept.
