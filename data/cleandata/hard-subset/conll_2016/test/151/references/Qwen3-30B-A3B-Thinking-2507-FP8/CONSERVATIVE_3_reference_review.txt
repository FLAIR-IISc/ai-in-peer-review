

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper proposes "UDP" - an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. The system relies on UD constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as dependents of content words), uses a personalized PageRank approach to score content words, and estimates adposition direction from test data. The authors claim their approach performs nearly as well as supervised transfer parsers on 32 languages while being more robust to domain changes.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A rule-based dependency parser that explicitly leverages UD constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) - this is the core contribution, but not particularly novel.
2. The use of personalized PageRank to identify main predicates - presented as key innovation, but lacks sufficient justification.
3. Estimating adposition direction from test data - a practical contribution, but not groundbreaking.

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive cross-lingual evaluation** - The paper evaluates on 32 languages, which is a significant strength. This demonstrates the generalizability of the approach across diverse language families, something many prior unsupervised parsers fail to do.

2. **Demonstrated robustness to domain changes** - The paper provides strong empirical evidence that the rule-based approach is more robust to domain shifts than data-driven methods. This is a significant finding that supports the value of linguistically constrained approaches.

3. **Clear connection to UD formalism** - The authors explicitly leverage UD constraints (e.g., function words as leaves), which makes their approach more linguistically sound and consistent with the UD standard. This is a valuable contribution to the UD ecosystem.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core approach** - The paper's main approach is a rule-based dependency parser with a PageRank-based ranking of content words. This is not new - Søgaard (2012b) presented a very similar approach. The authors acknowledge this but fail to sufficiently differentiate their work. The "personalized" aspect (giving 5x weight to the first verb) is minimal and not well-justified. The paper doesn't demonstrate why this approach is better than simply using the first verb as the main predicate.

2. **Insufficient evidence for PageRank contribution** - The paper claims that PageRank contributes 9 UAS points on average, but the comparison is flawed. They compare to UDPNoPR, which ranks content words by reading order (a poor baseline), rather than comparing to a more reasonable baseline (using the first verb as the main predicate, which is what they do for personalization). The paper doesn't show that using the first verb as the main predicate would perform worse than their personalized PageRank approach.

3. **Overstated performance claims** - The paper claims "UDP compares favorably to delexicalized parsing," but the numbers show UDPG (with gold POS) is 6.4% worse than MSDG on average. The authors say "UDPG is remarkably close," but a 6.4% difference is substantial. The paper also claims "even outperforming MSDG on one language (Hindi)," but this is a single language out of 32, which isn't particularly impressive.

4. **Unjustified language-specific assumptions** - The paper states that "AUX, DET, and SCONJ are right-attaching, while CONJ and PUNCT are left-attaching" based on English dev data. They don't provide evidence that these rules generalize to other languages. For example, in some languages like Hungarian, DET can be left-attaching, while in others it's right-attaching. This undermines their claim of being "completely free of language-specific parameters."

5. **Inadequate discussion of UD formalism limitations** - The authors claim UD "lends itself more naturally to a simple and linguistically sound rule-based approach," but they don't address that UD is still a work in progress and not all treebanks are equally compliant with the formalism. The paper mentions this briefly but doesn't provide evidence that their parser works well across treebanks with varying degrees of compliance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "AUX, DET, and SCONJ are right-attaching, while CONJ and PUNCT are left-attaching" based on English dev data. What evidence do the authors have that these rules generalize to other languages? For example, in Hungarian, DET is typically left-attaching, while in English it's right-attaching.

2. The paper claims that PageRank contributes 9 UAS points on average over UDPNoPR. However, UDPNoPR uses reading order for ranking, which is not a reasonable baseline. How would the system perform if it used the first verb as the main predicate (the approach they use for personalization) instead of reading order?

3. The paper claims that UDPG (with gold POS) is "remarkably close" to MSDG (the supervised transfer parser), with an average difference of 6.4%. However, the paper also states that "UDP, the simple unsupervised system with PageRank, outperforms the adjacency baselines (BL) by 4% on average on the two type-based naive POS tag scenario." Given that the adjacency baseline is much simpler, why is the performance gap between UDPG and MSDG so large (6.4%) rather than small?

4. The paper mentions they excluded Japanese because "the treebank is distributed without word forms." How would the system perform on Japanese if they had word forms? Given that Japanese is a major language in UD, this is an important limitation to address.

5. The paper claims that their parser "requires no training data," but they used the English treebank during development to "assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters." How does this affect their claim of being "completely free of language-specific parameters"?

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper provides a thorough evaluation across 32 languages and demonstrates robustness to domain changes, the core approach lacks novelty, the evidence for key claims is insufficient, and the paper overstates its performance compared to established baselines. The authors present an incremental improvement on existing rule-based approaches without providing sufficient evidence that their specific modifications are meaningful or necessary. The paper would be better suited for a conference focused on rule-based approaches rather than a major NLP conference like CONLL.
