

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser), an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The authors claim four contributions, but after careful evaluation, I identify the following as the main contributions in order of strength:

1. **Achieving competitive performance on 32 languages without training data**: The system demonstrates performance comparable to supervised transfer parsers (MSD) on most languages, outperforming it on Hindi (55.3% vs. 54.8% UAS), while being significantly more robust to domain shifts.

2. **Personalized PageRank for main predicate identification**: The authors introduce a personalized PageRank approach that effectively identifies the main predicate (root) of a sentence by giving higher weight to the first verb or content word, which substantially improves parsing performance.

3. **Language-agnostic adposition direction estimation**: The system estimates adposition direction directly from test data (using simple bigram frequency) rather than relying on language-specific parameters or treebank features, making it truly language-agnostic for this aspect.

The other claimed contributions (adapting head rules to UD-compliant POS relations and incorporating UD restriction of function words being leaves) are direct consequences of following the UD formalism, not novel contributions.

## Strengths

1. **Competitive performance without training data**: The paper demonstrates that an unsupervised parser can achieve results remarkably close to supervised transfer parsers (average 6.4% difference between UDPG and MSDG), which is a significant achievement in unsupervised dependency parsing. The fact that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (55.3% vs. 54.8% UAS) is particularly noteworthy and suggests the approach has real value.

2. **Robustness to domain shifts**: The authors provide compelling evidence that UDP is significantly more robust to domain changes than supervised transfer parsers. The standard deviation across domains for UDP is consistently lower than for MSD (e.g., 1.8 vs. 4.5 for BLG, 2.5 vs. 4.2 for UDPG), which is a major practical advantage for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The evaluation spans 32 languages across three POS setups (gold, predicted, and naive) and multiple domains for select languages. This thorough assessment provides strong evidence for the system's capabilities and limitations, far exceeding typical unsupervised parsing evaluations.

4. **Effective use of UD constraints**: The paper demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of how to leverage UD's formal constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) to design a simple yet effective parser. The two-step decoding process (content words first, then function words) is particularly elegant in ensuring function words remain leaves.

5. **Transparency and simplicity**: Unlike many data-driven approaches, UDP is rule-based, interpretable, and requires no training data. This makes it highly accessible for low-resource languages and easy to deploy in settings where training data is unavailable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty**: While the system is effective, the core ideas (rule-based parsing, using PageRank for ranking) are not particularly novel. Søgaard (2012b) already explored similar approaches, and the authors don't sufficiently distinguish their contribution from prior work. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is truly new compared to previous rule-aided unsupervised parsing.

2. **Overreliance on accurate POS tags**: The system's performance is heavily dependent on accurate POS tags, with a 2.2% drop in UAS when using predicted POS instead of gold POS. This is a significant limitation for real-world applications, especially for low-resource languages where POS taggers may not be available or accurate. The paper doesn't adequately address how to handle this limitation.

3. **Misleading claim about language-agnosticism**: The paper claims the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters," but this is not accurate. The directionality for some function words (AUX, DET, SCONJ as right-attaching) is determined based on English development data, making the system partially language-dependent. This undermines the paper's main selling point.

4. **Lack of comparison to other unsupervised parsers**: The paper only compares to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, but fails to compare to other unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., Klein & Manning, 2004; Gelling et al., 2012). Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess how truly competitive UDP is in the unsupervised parsing landscape.

5. **Limited analysis of linguistic capabilities**: The paper mentions the system cannot infer relative clauses due to excluding the NOUN → VERB head rule, but doesn't explore this limitation in depth or analyze how it affects parsing quality for specific linguistic phenomena. A more detailed error analysis would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi, but the UAS values in Table 5 show UDPG (55.3%) vs. MSDG (54.8%), a difference of only 0.5%. Could you clarify whether this difference is statistically significant, and provide the standard deviations for these scores?

2. The authors claim the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters," but as noted in Weakness 3, the directionality for some function words is determined based on English development data. Could you elaborate on the extent to which this makes the system language-dependent, and how this limitation might affect performance on languages with different word orders?

3. The paper mentions that the system cannot infer relative clauses due to excluding the NOUN → VERB head rule. How critical is this limitation for practical applications, and could the authors provide specific examples of how this affects parsing quality for languages that frequently use relative clauses?

4. The paper doesn't compare to other unsupervised dependency parsers. How does UDP compare to the state-of-the-art unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., those by Klein & Manning, 2004; Gelling et al., 2012) on the same 32 languages?

5. The paper shows a 2.2% drop in performance when using predicted POS instead of gold POS. How does this compare to the drop in performance for supervised parsers like MSD when using predicted POS? The paper states that error propagation for UDP is 0.37 compared to 0.46 for MSD, but it would be helpful to see how this translates to actual UAS differences.

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents an interesting and effective unsupervised dependency parser that achieves competitive results without training data. The system's robustness to domain shifts is particularly noteworthy and valuable for practical applications. However, the paper's claims about novelty and language-agnosticism are overstated, and the limited comparison to other unsupervised parsers weakens the contribution claim.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions: The authors should address the weaknesses identified above, particularly by:
1. Clarifying the novelty of their approach relative to prior work
2. Providing a more accurate assessment of the system's language-agnosticism
3. Comparing to other unsupervised parsers
4. Providing a more detailed error analysis

With these improvements, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised parsing.
