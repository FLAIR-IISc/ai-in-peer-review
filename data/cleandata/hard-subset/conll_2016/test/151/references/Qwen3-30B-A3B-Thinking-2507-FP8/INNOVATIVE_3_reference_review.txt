

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper presents a dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data, relying solely on explicit POS constraints imposed by the UD formalism. The authors introduce a novel approach that uses personalized PageRank to identify main predicates, UD-specific head rules, and on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test data. The system (UDP) achieves competitive performance compared to supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parsers while being more robust to domain changes.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Novel unsupervised parsing approach leveraging UD constraints** - The paper's most significant contribution is the conceptual shift to using UD's formalism constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as dependents of content words) as the foundation for parsing, rather than relying on training data or transfer learning.
2. **Personalized PageRank for main predicate identification** - The use of personalized PageRank with a focus on making the main predicate (typically the first verb) have the highest rank is a clever adaptation of existing techniques to the UD formalism.
3. **UD-specific head rules and direction estimation** - The paper's adaptation of dependency head rules to UD conventions and estimation of adposition direction directly from test data represents a significant practical contribution.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and bold conceptual approach**: The paper presents a genuinely novel perspective in dependency parsing by completely eschewing training data and instead leveraging UD's formal constraints. This is a significant conceptual shift from the mainstream approach, and the authors make a compelling case that UD's design specifically enables such a rule-based approach. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, this is the strongest point in favor of acceptance.

2. **Strong performance with minimal assumptions**: The results demonstrate that the parser achieves competitive performance (within 6.4% of a supervised multi-source delexicalized parser on average) without any training data. This is particularly impressive given the simplicity of the approach. The authors provide compelling evidence that by carefully leveraging UD's formal constraints, they can achieve performance that rivals more complex supervised approaches.

3. **Robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their rule-based approach is more robust to domain changes than data-driven approaches. This is a significant contribution to understanding parsing robustness, as it suggests that rule-based systems can be more stable across domains than data-driven ones. The cross-domain evaluation across multiple genres for multiple languages is particularly strong.

4. **Simplicity and interpretability**: The system is extremely simple and transparent compared to neural or data-driven approaches. Every decision is based on explicit rules and the UD formalism, making it highly interpretable. This is valuable for linguistic analysis and understanding, and the authors provide clear examples of the parsing process.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty in core approach**: While the application of unsupervised parsing to UD is novel, the basic idea of unsupervised dependency parsing without training data is not new (Klein and Manning, 2004; Gelling et al., 2012). The paper acknowledges this but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing unsupervised methods in the literature.

2. **Over-reliance on POS tags**: The system's performance is highly dependent on the quality of POS tags. The 2.2% drop when using predicted POS (94.1% accuracy) is significant, and the paper doesn't adequately address how this limitation would affect real-world applications where POS tagging accuracy might be lower. This is a notable practical limitation.

3. **Limited evaluation of language diversity**: The paper evaluates on 32 languages but excludes Japanese due to missing word forms, and doesn't provide detailed analysis of why certain languages (like Tamil, Arabic) perform poorly. The paper doesn't sufficiently address whether the approach would work for languages with very different grammatical structures from the ones tested.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the performance of your parser compare to recent unsupervised dependency parsing approaches that don't rely on UD constraints (e.g., those using neural networks or other advanced unsupervised techniques)?

2. The paper mentions that your system outperforms the supervised multi-source delexicalized parser on Hindi. Could you provide more details about why this might be the case, and whether this is a consistent pattern across other languages?

3. The paper states that your system uses the English treebank during development to tune parameters. How sensitive is the system to this choice of development language, and how would the system perform if developed on a non-English language?

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. While the paper has some methodological limitations (particularly the reliance on POS tags), the core contribution is a bold and novel approach to dependency parsing that leverages UD's formalism in a way that hasn't been done before. The paper demonstrates that this simple approach achieves competitive performance with strong robustness to domain shifts. The authors have made a significant conceptual contribution to the field, and the paper's novelty and boldness outweigh the methodological imperfections. The work opens a new direction for dependency parsing research that could be particularly valuable for low-resource languages and cross-lingual applications.

I'm willing to overlook the minor limitations (like the limited evaluation scope and over-reliance on POS tags) because the paper introduces a significant new concept that could change how we think about unsupervised parsing. The authors have provided a compelling argument for why UD's formalism is particularly well-suited for this rule-based approach, which is a fresh perspective in the field.
