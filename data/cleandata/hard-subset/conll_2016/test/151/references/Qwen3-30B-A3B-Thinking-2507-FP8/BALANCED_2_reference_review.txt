

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a dependency parser called UDP (Universal Dependencies Parser) that requires no training data and relies solely on the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The system uses explicit part-of-speech (POS) constraints from UD (e.g., function words being leaves, content words being heads), incorporates personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate, and estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate their system on 32 languages across three POS setups and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A completely unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data and leverages UD's formal constraints (specifically, the rules about function words being leaves and content words being heads).
2. A novel application of personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate without training data, significantly improving parsing accuracy.
3. A method for estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data, avoiding language-specific parameters.

## Strengths

1. **Novel unsupervised approach with strong empirical results**: The paper presents a genuinely unsupervised approach to dependency parsing that achieves remarkably close performance to supervised transfer systems (average UAS difference of 6.4% with MSDG). This is particularly impressive because it requires no training data whatsoever, yet outperforms a rule-based baseline by 9 UAS points on average. The claim that "our unsupervised parser compares favorably to delexicalized parsing" is well-supported by the data.

2. **Robustness to domain shifts**: The paper provides compelling evidence that UDP is significantly more robust to domain changes than data-driven approaches. The standard deviation across domains is much lower for UDP (average 2.5) compared to MSD (average 4.2), confirming the authors' claim that "rule-based parsers can be more stable to domain shift." This is a major practical contribution, as domain shift is a common real-world challenge.

3. **Practical implementation and evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation across three POS scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS, and a naive two-POS setup) and demonstrate that UDP is less sensitive to POS errors than supervised systems (error propagation rate of 0.37 vs. 0.46 for MSD). The detailed domain analysis across Bulgarian, Croatian, English, and Italian datasets provides valuable insights into the system's robustness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core approach**: While the paper presents a well-executed application of existing techniques, the core approach is not significantly different from Søgaard (2012b), who already proposed an unsupervised dependency parser using PageRank. The main differences (personalized PageRank, two-step decoding) are incremental improvements rather than fundamental innovations. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from this prior work, which weakens the contribution claim.

2. **Insufficient comparison to other unsupervised parsers**: The paper compares UDP to a rule-based baseline and a supervised transfer system (MSD), but doesn't compare it to other unsupervised dependency parsers (e.g., those using DMV, Viterbi training, or other techniques). Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether UDP represents a significant advancement in the unsupervised parsing space.

3. **Lack of detailed error analysis**: While the paper provides some error analysis (Section 6), it's limited in scope. The authors don't thoroughly analyze why the system fails on certain languages or POS categories, nor do they provide examples of incorrect parses. A more detailed error analysis would strengthen the paper by showing where the system works well and where it struggles, which is particularly important for an unsupervised approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims UDP "requires no training data," but mentions using the English treebank during development to tune PageRank parameters and determine head rules. How much of the English treebank was used for this development phase, and how does this affect the claim of being completely unsupervised? The paper should clarify this point to avoid misrepresenting the system's training requirements.

2. The paper states that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (UDPG 45.7 vs. MSDG 46.3), but this is a very small difference (0.6 UAS points). The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these results. Can the authors clarify whether this difference is statistically significant, and if so, how they determined it?

3. The paper mentions excluding Japanese from evaluation because "the treebank is distributed without word forms," but doesn't explain why this makes it impossible to provide results on predicted POS. Could the authors clarify this point, as it seems relevant to the scope of their evaluation?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The work presents a well-executed, practical approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that achieves competitive performance with supervised methods and demonstrates strong robustness to domain shifts. The contribution is significant for the field, as it shows that a carefully designed rule-based system can achieve near-supervised performance without any training data.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing a more thorough comparison to other unsupervised parsers and clarifying the relationship to prior work by Søgaard (2012b). The minor issues with the training data claim and statistical significance of the Hindi results should also be addressed.

The paper's practical value for real-world applications (where training data is scarce and domain shifts occur) is clear, and the results are well-presented. With the suggested revisions, this would be a strong contribution to the field of unsupervised parsing.
