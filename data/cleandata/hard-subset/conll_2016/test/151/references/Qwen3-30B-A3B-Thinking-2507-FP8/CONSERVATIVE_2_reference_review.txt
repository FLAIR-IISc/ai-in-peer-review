

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and part-of-speech (POS) tags. The approach uses personalized PageRank to identify main predicates, incorporates UD-specific head rules, and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors claim their system is competitive with supervised multi-source delexicalized parsers while being more robust to domain changes.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A rule-based dependency parser that exploits UD constraints without training data
2. A method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data
3. A detailed evaluation demonstrating robustness across 32 languages and multiple domains

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive cross-lingual evaluation**: The paper evaluates on 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive POS) and demonstrates the system's robustness across different domains. This thorough evaluation across languages and domains is a significant strength that provides convincing evidence of the system's generalizability.

2. **Demonstrated robustness to domain shifts**: The paper shows that UDP has significantly lower standard deviation across domains compared to supervised systems (Table 6). This is an important practical contribution, as many real-world applications require parsing across different text genres. The finding that rule-based systems may be more stable to domain shifts (confirming Plank and van Noord, 2010) is valuable for the field.

3. **Clear demonstration of the impact of UD constraints**: The paper effectively shows how the specific constraints of the UD formalism (single-rooted trees, function words as dependents) enable a simpler parsing approach. The detailed analysis of how the system handles different POS types (Table 6) provides useful insights into the strengths and limitations of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading claim of "unsupervised" parsing**: The paper claims to present an "unsupervised dependency parser" but actually relies entirely on POS tags. This is a fundamental mischaracterization of unsupervised parsing, which traditionally requires no lexical or POS information. The paper acknowledges this by evaluating on different POS scenarios, but the core approach is not truly unsupervised. This undermines a key claim of the paper and may mislead readers about the nature of the contribution.

2. **Inadequate comparison to established unsupervised baselines**: The paper compares to a rule-based baseline (BL) and a multi-source delexicalized transfer system (MSD), but doesn't properly compare to established unsupervised dependency parsing methods. The paper dismisses the DMV model by Klein and Manning (2004) as "outperformed by a system similar to ours," but doesn't provide a proper comparison to the state-of-the-art unsupervised methods. The authors should have compared to at least one established unsupervised parser (e.g., Spitkovsky et al., 2010) to properly establish the significance of their contribution.

3. **Overstated novelty**: The personalized PageRank approach is a minor modification of existing work (Søgaard, 2012b), and the authors don't clearly demonstrate why this specific modification leads to the reported improvements. The paper claims to "go beyond the existing work on rule-aided unsupervised dependency parsing," but the differences are relatively small and not sufficiently justified. The main novelty appears to be the application of existing techniques to the UD formalism, not the techniques themselves.

4. **Unjustified claim of "competitive performance"**: The paper claims UDP "compares favorably to delexicalized parsing" with only a 6.4% average UAS difference. However, the paper doesn't provide enough context about how good this is for unsupervised parsing. In the unsupervised dependency parsing literature, a 6.4% gap is substantial (the authors themselves note that unsupervised parsers generally fall "far behind" supervised approaches). The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this is a meaningful improvement over the state of the art.

5. **Development on English data creates language bias**: The paper states they used the English treebank during development to "assess the contribution of individual head rules, and to tune PageRank parameters." This creates a potential bias toward English-like languages, and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that the system generalizes well to non-Indo-European languages. The claim that "the head rules are general enough" is not adequately supported by the data presented.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims UDP is "unsupervised" but relies on POS tags. How does the authors' definition of "unsupervised" differ from the standard definition in the dependency parsing literature? The authors should clarify this distinction to avoid misleading readers.

2. The paper states that "the performance of unsupervised parsers falls far behind the approaches involving any sort of supervision" but then claims UDP is competitive with a supervised method (MSD). How does the authors reconcile this claim with the literature? What specific metric or threshold would be required for UDP to be considered "competitive" with supervised methods?

3. The authors compare to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system (MSD) but don't compare to more recent supervised approaches (e.g., recent neural models). Why was this specific baseline chosen over others that might provide a more meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art systems?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a system that is an interesting application of existing rule-based techniques to the UD formalism. However, the claims of novelty and unsupervised nature are overstated, and the paper doesn't sufficiently establish the significance of the contribution in the context of existing work. The evaluation is thorough but doesn't adequately address the core question of whether this approach represents a meaningful advance in unsupervised parsing.

As a reviewer with a preference for established methods and skepticism toward unproven approaches, I am concerned that the paper overstates its contributions while misrepresenting the nature of the approach as "unsupervised." The paper would be significantly stronger if it properly positioned its contribution as an application of existing techniques to the UD formalism rather than a novel unsupervised parsing method.

I recommend rejection, but would consider a major revision that addresses the weaknesses above, particularly the mischaracterization of the approach as "unsupervised" and the inadequate comparison to established unsupervised baselines.
