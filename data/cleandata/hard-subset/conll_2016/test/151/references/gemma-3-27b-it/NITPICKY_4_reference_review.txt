This paper presents a dependency parser that operates without training, relying instead on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and personalized PageRank. The authors claim contributions in adapting dependency head rules to UD, incorporating the function word leaf constraint, using personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and achieving language independence through on-the-fly adposition direction estimation.

I view the primary contribution as a demonstration of how strongly constrained, rule-based parsing can achieve surprisingly competitive results in a multilingual setting *given* the UD framework. The adherence to UD constraints is crucial, and the paper effectively shows how leveraging these constraints can mitigate the need for large-scale training data. A secondary contribution is the practical demonstration of personalized PageRank within this constrained parsing framework, contributing a relatively simple but effective mechanism for root identification. The claimed language independence is overstated; while adposition direction is estimated dynamically, numerous other directional constraints are fixed based on English development data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves respectable UAS scores (averaging around 55-60%) across a large number of languages (32) without any training. This is particularly impressive given the complexity of dependency parsing and the diversity of the languages tested. The comparison to a delexicalized transfer parser demonstrates a viable alternative to purely data-driven approaches.
2. **Effective Leverage of UD:** The paper showcases the power of UD as a unifying framework for cross-lingual parsing. The reliance on UD constraints (function word leaf status, basic head rules) provides a strong foundation for the parser, reducing ambiguity and guiding the parsing process.
3. **Clear and Concise Methodology:** The method is relatively simple and well-explained, making it easy to understand and potentially replicate. The two-step decoding algorithm is a clear and logical approach to handling content and function words separately.
4. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The evaluation across different domains reveals that the parser is more stable to domain changes than the delexicalized transfer parser, demonstrating an advantage of the rule-based approach.
5. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating on 32 languages and considering both gold and predicted POS tags provides a thorough assessment of the parser's performance and robustness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated Language Independence:** The claim of complete language independence is misleading. The directional constraints for AUX, DET, SCONJ, CONJ, and PUNCT are derived from English data, introducing a language-specific bias. This is a significant limitation and should be acknowledged more prominently. While the adposition direction is dynamic, these other constraints heavily influence the parsing results.
2. **Limited Rule Coverage & Potential for Errors:** The head rules in Table 1 are not exhaustive, and the handling of cases not covered by the rules relies on default behaviors (left or right attachment). This can lead to errors, especially in languages with more complex syntactic structures. The paper doesn't fully address how the fallback mechanisms impact performance.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the paper provides overall UAS scores, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying the common types of errors made by the parser (e.g., incorrect root identification, incorrect attachment of function words) would provide valuable insights for future improvement. The brief discussion in section 6.2 doesn’t offer sufficient granularity.
4. **Suboptimal PageRank Personalization:** The personalization strategy of giving 5x weight to the first verb or content word is somewhat arbitrary. A more principled approach to predicate identification, perhaps based on syntactic cues or semantic information, could potentially improve performance.
5. **Inconsistent Reporting of Standard Deviations:** The presentation of standard deviations is inconsistent throughout the paper. Table 5 reports no standard deviations, while Table 6 includes them, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the specific choice of the PageRank personalization factor (5x)? Have you explored other values or alternative personalization strategies?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of common parsing errors and their causes?
3.  How would the parser perform if the directional constraints for AUX, DET, SCONJ, CONJ, and PUNCT were learned from each individual language’s training data instead of being fixed based on English?
4. The paper mentions the system requires English treebank during development. How would the performance of the parser be affected if it were developed using another language's treebank?
5. The paper uses the term "function words" frequently. A clearer definition of this category (beyond “not content words”) and a justification for the specific POS tags included as function words would be helpful.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation in Figure 1 is somewhat unclear. Providing a more detailed explanation of the variables and functions would improve readability.
*   The reference to "http://ANONYMIZED" should be replaced with a publicly accessible link or a more descriptive explanation of the resources.
*   The discussion of related work would benefit from a more critical analysis of existing approaches, highlighting their limitations and how the proposed method addresses them.



Despite its strengths, the overstated claims of language independence and the lack of detailed error analysis prevent me from recommending acceptance. The method is a worthwhile contribution, but the paper requires significant revision to address these weaknesses. I recommend rejection with the possibility of resubmission after addressing the concerns raised in this review.
