This paper presents a surprisingly strong unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies, requiring no training data. The core idea – leveraging the constraints imposed by the UD formalism – is sound and demonstrably effective. The system consistently achieves competitive performance across a large number of languages (32), often surpassing a strong delexicalized transfer baseline, and exhibiting greater robustness to domain shift. The detailed method description, including the personalized PageRank approach and handling of function word attachment, is clear and well-motivated. The extensive experimental evaluation, encompassing varying POS tag reliability and domain changes, adds significant weight to the claims.

**Contributions:**

1. **A competitive, truly unsupervised dependency parser for UD:** This is the primary contribution. The parser demonstrates strong performance across a diverse set of languages *without any training data*, a significant achievement in the field. It’s not merely a theoretical exercise; it provides a practical parsing solution for low-resource languages.
2. **Effective integration of UD constraints:** The method’s success hinges on intelligently incorporating the Universal Dependencies’ structural constraints (function words as leaves, single-rooted trees) into the parsing process. This shows how formal linguistic knowledge can drive practical NLP solutions.
3. **Robustness to domain shift:** The experimental results clearly show the parser's resilience to changes in text domain, outperforming a supervised baseline in this regard. This highlights the benefits of a less data-dependent approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves consistently high UAS scores across 32 languages, competitive with a strong delexicalized transfer parser. This demonstrates the viability of a completely unsupervised approach. The table of results (Table 5) is well-presented and supports the claims effectively.
2. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper goes beyond standard test set evaluation. Assessing performance under different POS tag quality conditions (gold, predicted, naive) and across multiple domains (Table 6 & 7) provides a comprehensive and nuanced understanding of the system's capabilities and limitations. The inclusion of in-house annotated datasets adds to the rigor.
3. **Clear and Detailed Method Description:** The algorithm is explained in a clear, step-by-step manner (Section 3), making it easy to understand the key components (PageRank setup, head direction estimation, decoding algorithm). The inclusion of Fig. 1 is helpful.
4. **Sound Linguistic Motivation:** The design choices are well-grounded in linguistic principles, specifically the characteristics of Universal Dependencies.  The authors correctly identify a gap in existing research – the underutilization of formal linguistic constraints in parsing.
5. **Reproducibility:** Making both the parser and annotated datasets freely available is commendable and facilitates further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Handling of Complex Structures:** The parser struggles with structures not fully captured by the employed head rules, such as relative clauses (mentioned in the discussion). While the authors acknowledge this, it’s a significant limitation for languages with complex syntax. The reliance on simpler dependency patterns restricts its ability to accurately parse more nuanced constructions.
2. **Dependence on POS Tag Accuracy (despite mitigation):** While the system demonstrates robustness to POS tag errors, the performance still degrades with lower-quality tags (UDPP results). The method is not entirely immune to noisy input; a significant drop in accuracy with predicted POS indicates this reliance.
3. **English-Centric Development:** The system's development and tuning relied on English data, potentially introducing biases. Although the results across other languages are strong, it’s unclear how representative English is as a development language. The potential for language-specific biases is not thoroughly investigated.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents comprehensive results, a more detailed error analysis (beyond the general observations in Section 6) would be beneficial. Identifying common error types and their causes could inform future improvements.  For instance, a closer look at the failures on languages like Tamil (lower performance) would be insightful.
5. **Simplified POS Scenario (UDPN):** The "naive" content/function POS setup (UDPN) feels overly simplistic and doesn't represent a realistic POS tagging scenario. It's a useful lower bound, but its interpretation is limited.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the English-centric development: How sensitive are the results to the choice of development language? Did you perform any experiments using other languages for parameter tuning?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, perhaps focusing on specific error types (e.g., incorrect attachment of modifiers, misidentification of root nodes) and their prevalence across different languages?
3.  How could the parser be extended to handle more complex syntactic structures like relative clauses or coordination phrases, which are currently not well-supported?
4.  The paper mentions the possibility of reifying dependency edges.  Could you elaborate on how this might be implemented and what performance gains you anticipate?
5.  What is the computational complexity of the system? While O(n) is stated for the decoding stage, the PR stage appears more costly. A more detailed analysis of the computational requirements would be useful.

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation throughout the paper is generally clear, but a glossary of abbreviations might be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the field.
* The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
* The discussion section is insightful and provides a good overview of the system’s strengths and weaknesses.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and innovative paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. Despite some limitations, the parser achieves impressive results and demonstrates the potential of leveraging linguistic knowledge in NLP.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and responding to the questions posed above. The strengths of the work – particularly the strong empirical results and thorough evaluation – outweigh the identified weaknesses.
