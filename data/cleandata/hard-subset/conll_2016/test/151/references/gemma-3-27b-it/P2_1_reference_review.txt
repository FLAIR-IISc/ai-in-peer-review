## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a completely unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies that leverages the constraints imposed by the UD formalism – specifically, the single-rooted tree structure and the leaf-status of function words. The system eschews training data altogether, relying instead on a set of dependency head rules, personalized PageRank for content word ranking, and a dynamic estimation of adposition attachment direction. The authors evaluate UDP across 32 languages, comparing it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser and analyzing its robustness to domain shift.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical demonstration of strong parsing performance achievable solely through linguistic constraints:** The core contribution is showing that a surprisingly effective parser can be built without *any* training data, simply by rigorously applying the rules defined within the Universal Dependencies framework. This challenges the prevailing paradigm of data-driven NLP.
2. **A robust and stable baseline for cross-lingual parsing:** UDP consistently achieves competitive results, and demonstrably shows increased robustness to domain shift compared to a supervised transfer baseline. This is valuable for low-resource languages where training data is scarce.
3. **Insightful combination of techniques:** The integration of PageRank personalization and a two-step decoding process (content words first, then function words) is clever and effective within the UD constraint framework.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Impact of the Approach:** The complete absence of training data is genuinely novel in modern dependency parsing.  The demonstration that a strong parser can be built solely on linguistic rules, especially within the UD framework, is a significant result. It has the potential to shift thinking about how parsing can be approached, particularly for resource-poor languages.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages is commendable and lends substantial weight to the findings.  The comparison to a strong supervised baseline (multi-source delexicalized parser) is essential, and the analysis of domain robustness further strengthens the paper's conclusions. The inclusion of multiple evaluation setups (gold vs. predicted POS) is also a strong point.
3. **Clear and Well-Justified Methodology:** The paper clearly explains the motivation behind each design choice – the head rules, PageRank personalization, and the two-step decoding process. The rationale for these choices is linguistically grounded and well-articulated. The inclusion of an example run is particularly helpful for understanding the algorithm’s behavior.
4. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The demonstrated stability of UDP across different domains, coupled with the insightful analysis in Section 6.3, is a strong positive. This is particularly valuable in real-world applications where data distributions often vary.
5. **Availability of Resources:** Making the parser and datasets available is essential for reproducibility and future research, and a significant strength of the submission.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While Section 6 provides some error analysis, it feels relatively superficial. A more detailed breakdown of error types – specifically, *where* the parser fails most often (e.g., certain dependency relations, specific POS tags) – would provide valuable insights for future improvement.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Quality:** Although the system *attempts* to mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors through the predicted POS setup, performance is still heavily influenced by POS accuracy. This limitation needs to be explicitly acknowledged and further discussed. The performance drop on languages with lower POS tagging accuracy is significant.
3. **Lack of Comparison to other Unsupervised Methods:**  The paper mentions related work in unsupervised parsing but doesn't provide a direct empirical comparison to more recent unsupervised methods (beyond the delexicalized transfer parser). This makes it difficult to assess the relative advantages of UDP.  Even a brief comparison to simpler baselines (e.g., a system that simply assigns heads based on POS frequency) would be helpful.
4. **Rule-Based System Scalability:** While effective, the system's reliance on hand-crafted rules might limit scalability to languages with very different grammatical structures or languages for which defining the initial set of rules is challenging. This potential limitation is not fully addressed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the most frequent types of parsing errors made by UDP? What specific dependency relations or POS tag combinations are particularly challenging?
2.  How sensitive is the system to the specific choice of teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm? Did you explore a wider range of values, and what impact did they have on performance?
3.  Have you considered incorporating methods for handling non-projective dependencies, given that the current algorithm can produce non-projective trees?
4.  How would the system perform on languages that significantly deviate from the typical UD structure, such as languages with free word order or ergative-absolutive alignment?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The inclusion of both an example parse and a detailed algorithmic description is very helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual parsing. The novelty of the approach, the comprehensive evaluation, and the insightful analysis make it a compelling read. While there are some weaknesses, they are not major enough to detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the raised questions and weaknesses.
