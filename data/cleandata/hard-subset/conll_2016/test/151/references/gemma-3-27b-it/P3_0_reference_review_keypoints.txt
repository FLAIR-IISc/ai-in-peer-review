- Presents a zero-shot unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) using UD head rules, personalized PageRank, and two-step decoding without training data.  
- Evaluates on 32 languages and shows competitive performance, especially under low POS tag accuracy and domain shift.

- Demonstrates that reasonable dependency parses are achievable without any training by exploiting UD constraints.  
- Effectively combines UD-specific head rules with personalized PageRank, using personalization to identify the main predicate.  
- Shows robustness to POS tag quality degradation and domain shift compared to a supervised baseline.

- Strong empirical results across 32 languages, often outperforming or matching a delexicalized transfer baseline.  
- Clear motivation and well-explained methodology, including justified design choices like two-step decoding and personalization.  
- Comprehensive evaluation beyond UAS, testing POS scenarios and domain adaptation.  
- Authors provide code and data, supporting reproducibility.  
- Provides insightful error analysis by POS and domain.

- Core components (PageRank, head rules) are not novel; novelty is primarily in their UD-specific combination and personalization.  
- Parameter tuning on English development data may bias results; language-specific tuning not fully explored.  
- The CONTENT/FUNCTION two-POS robustness test is overly simplistic and not representative of realistic POS errors.  
- Lacks comparison to recent state-of-the-art unsupervised parsers beyond the delexicalized transfer baseline.

- Why was English chosen for parameter tuning and have language-specific tunings been tried?  
- Have the authors considered mechanisms to handle noun-verb headship errors from relative clauses?  
- Can robustness be evaluated with POS tags at varying realistic accuracy levels (e.g., different TnT training sizes)?  
- Is poor PUNCT performance due to annotation inconsistencies or inherent limitations of the rule-based approach?

- Suggests improving table labels for clarity and notes the paper is well-written with comprehensive references.

- Overall: strong, well-evaluated contribution to unsupervised dependency parsing; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
