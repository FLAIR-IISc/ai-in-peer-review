This paper presents a dependency parser, UDP, that requires no training data, relying instead on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and a personalized PageRank algorithm. The authors claim contributions in adapting head rules to UD, incorporating function word restrictions, using personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and achieving language independence through runtime adposition direction estimation.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating a surprisingly competitive, albeit rule-based, parsing performance *without* any training data on a substantial multilingual dataset. While the core ideas – leveraging UD constraints and PageRank – are not novel in isolation, their combination and successful application across 32 languages is a notable achievement.  The extensive evaluation, including a domain adaptation study, is another positive aspect.  A secondary contribution is the practical demonstration that even with relatively simple rules guided by linguistic principles (UD), reasonably accurate dependency parses can be obtained, offering a potentially robust baseline for low-resource languages. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance Without Training:** The reported UAS scores, especially considering the complete absence of training, are impressive. Achieving results comparable to a delexicalized transfer parser is a significant finding, demonstrating the power of well-defined linguistic constraints.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages, utilizing both gold and predicted POS tags, and investigating domain robustness, provides a strong and convincing assessment of the system’s capabilities and limitations. The inclusion of the multi-source delexicalized baseline is crucial for contextualizing the results.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The analysis of domain adaptation reveals UDP’s stability compared to the supervised baseline, supporting the authors’ claim of reduced overfitting and greater generalizability.  The detailed results provided in Table 6 are helpful for understanding this robustness.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The algorithm description (Fig. 1) and example run (Section 4) enhance understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Components:** The individual components – UD constraints, PageRank, and rule-based parsing – are well-established techniques. The primary novelty lies in their *combination*, but the paper doesn't sufficiently justify *why* this particular combination is expected to work so well. A more detailed analysis of the interplay between these components is needed.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper briefly touches on error analysis (POS tag errors, punctuation), a more thorough investigation of failure cases is required. Identifying specific linguistic phenomena that consistently cause errors would be valuable for guiding future improvements. What kinds of structures does UDP struggle with? Are there specific languages where performance is particularly poor and why?
3. **Reliance on English for Development:** The authors acknowledge using English for parameter tuning. This raises concerns about potential bias and sub-optimal performance on languages significantly different from English. While they attempt to mitigate this with language-independent adposition direction estimation, the extent of English bias remains unclear. More detailed cross-lingual analysis of the developed rules would be helpful.
4. **Simplified POS Handling:** The "naive" content/function POS tagging scenario, while useful, seems overly simplistic. A more nuanced evaluation with more sophisticated POS tagging errors would provide a more realistic assessment of the system's robustness.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the linguistic structures that UDP struggles to parse correctly?  Provide examples from different languages.
2.  To what extent did the development process on English influence the performance on other languages?  Have you performed any analysis to quantify the potential English bias?
3.  The personalization of PageRank with the first verb/content word seems effective. Have you explored other personalization strategies, such as weighting nouns or other content-bearing words?
4.  While the domain adaptation study demonstrates robustness, the differences observed are relatively small. Are there specific domains where UDP’s performance significantly degrades?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper’s formatting is generally good, but the table captions could be more descriptive.
*  It would be useful to include a discussion of the computational cost of the system, particularly the PageRank calculation.
*  The reference list is extensive and well-maintained.
