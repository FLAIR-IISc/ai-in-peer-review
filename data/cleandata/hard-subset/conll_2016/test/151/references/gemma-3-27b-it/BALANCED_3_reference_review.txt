This paper presents a surprisingly strong unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) that requires no training data, relying instead on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and personalized PageRank. The core idea – leveraging the structure imposed by UD to build a parser without learning – is compelling and largely successful. The reported results, comparing favorably to a delexicalized transfer parser across 32 languages, are impressive, particularly the demonstrated robustness to domain change.

**Contributions:**

1. **A competitive, fully unsupervised parser:** The primary contribution is a functional dependency parser that achieves surprisingly high accuracy without relying on any training data. This is a significant achievement, especially considering the challenges of unsupervised parsing.
2. **Effective integration of UD constraints:** The method intelligently incorporates UD’s constraints – particularly regarding function word attachment – into the parsing process. This moves beyond simply using UD as a data source and utilizes its *formalism* as a core component of the parsing algorithm.
3. **Personalized PageRank for predicate identification:** The use of personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate and guide the parsing process is a clever and effective technique, demonstrably improving performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores across a diverse range of languages, frequently outperforming or matching a strong delexicalized transfer baseline. The broad language coverage is a significant strength.
2. **Robustness to domain shift:** The evaluation on different domains demonstrates a remarkable stability compared to the baseline, a crucial advantage for real-world applications. This highlights the benefit of the rule-based and constraint-driven approach.
3. **Clear and concise method description:** The paper clearly explains the key components of the parsing algorithm, including the PageRank setup, head direction estimation, and two-step decoding. The inclusion of Fig. 1 and the detailed example in Section 4 significantly aids understanding.
4. **Thorough evaluation:** The experimental setup is well-designed, with multiple evaluation scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS) and a comparison to a strong baseline. The additional evaluation of cross-domain performance strengthens the findings.
5. **Open-source release:** Making both the parser and the annotated datasets available encourages reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited handling of complex structures:** The parser struggles with constructions requiring more sophisticated syntactic analysis, such as relative clauses (mentioned in the discussion). The hard constraints imposed by UD, while beneficial in many cases, may limit the parser’s ability to handle more nuanced structures.
2. **Dependency on POS tagger accuracy:** While the parser demonstrates robustness, performance still degrades with lower-quality POS tags. The results with predicted POS tags, though competitive, are lower than those with gold POS tags, indicating a sensitivity to POS tagging errors. This is an inherent limitation of any system reliant on POS tags, but warrants further investigation into more robust POS tagging integration strategies.
3. **Potential for language-specific bias in head rules:** Although the authors aim for language independence, the head rules, developed based on English, may introduce some bias. The observation that the parser performs better on some languages than others suggests that the rules are not universally optimal.  A more systematic way to refine the rules across languages could be beneficial.
4. **Simplification of adposition direction estimation:** While effective, estimating adposition direction based on bigram frequency is a relatively simple approach. Exploring more sophisticated methods for determining adposition attachment could further improve accuracy.
5. **Limited error analysis beyond POS:** The error analysis in Section 6 focuses primarily on the impact of POS tagging and the role of PageRank. A more detailed analysis of specific parsing errors (e.g., incorrect head attachment for specific POS tags) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the PageRank calculation, particularly for longer sentences? What are the practical limitations on sentence length?
2.  The head rules are developed using English as a reference. Have you explored methods for automatically adapting or refining these rules for other languages, potentially using a small amount of unlabeled data?
3.  You mention limitations in handling relative clauses. Are there specific modifications to the algorithm that you believe could address this issue without sacrificing the unsupervised nature of the parser?
4.  The difference between UDPG and MSDG is relatively small. Can you pinpoint the types of grammatical structures where UDPG consistently outperforms MSDG, and vice versa?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive and provides helpful context for the presented approach.
*   The notation used throughout the paper is clear and consistent.
*   The use of tables and figures effectively conveys key information.
*   The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work and suggest avenues for future research.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. The parser is surprisingly effective, demonstrating the power of leveraging linguistic constraints and a well-designed algorithm. Despite some limitations, the work is well-executed, thoroughly evaluated, and clearly presented. I recommend acceptance.
