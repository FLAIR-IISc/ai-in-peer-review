## Peer Review: Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel, completely unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The system leverages the constraints imposed by the UD formalism, employs personalized PageRank for improved predicate identification, estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data, and utilizes a two-step decoding process to treat content and function words differently. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages, comparing it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system, and also assess its robustness to domain change.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical demonstration of the power of UD constraints for unsupervised parsing:** The paper effectively shows that the inherent structure and constraints within the UD framework can be exploited to create a surprisingly competitive parser without relying on any training data. This is a significant result demonstrating the benefits of standardized linguistic formalisms.
2. **Robustness to domain shift:** The empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates that UDP is more robust to domain changes than a supervised delexicalized transfer parser, highlighting a key advantage of its rule-based and formalism-driven nature.
3. **Efficient and effective adaptation of PageRank:** The personalized PageRank approach, targeting content word identification, offers a noticeable improvement in parsing accuracy, showcasing a practical application of graph-based methods within this context.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores across a large number of languages (32), demonstrating its generalizability and effectiveness. The comparison against a strong baseline (multi-source delexicalized parser) provides a clear benchmark.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The rationale behind leveraging UD constraints and avoiding data-driven methods is well-articulated and convincing. The authors highlight the limitations of existing approaches and position their work as a viable alternative, especially for low-resource languages.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation includes not only a comparison with a baseline but also a detailed analysis of performance across different POS setups (gold, predicted, naive) and domains, providing a comprehensive assessment of the system’s strengths and weaknesses.
4. **Clear and Well-Written Paper:** The paper is clearly structured and written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and the experimental setup. The inclusion of a detailed example run is particularly helpful.
5. **Availability of Resources:** The authors state that both the parser and the annotated datasets are publicly available, which promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is novel, many of the individual components (PageRank, head rules, UD constraints) are not new. The paper doesn’t fully address how their specific implementation and integration contribute beyond existing work.  A more detailed comparison with prior rule-based approaches would be beneficial.
2. **Reliance on English for Tuning:** The system relies on English for parameter tuning (PageRank, function word directionality). While acknowledged, the impact of this reliance on performance in languages structurally distant from English isn't thoroughly investigated.  Exploring more cross-linguistically informed parameter initialization would be valuable.
3. **Simplifications in POS Handling:** The naive content/function POS setup (UDPN) seems overly simplistic and might underestimate the parser’s potential when given more accurate POS tags.  While designed as a lower bound, it feels artificially low and doesn't necessarily reflect real-world scenarios.
4. **Potential for Improvement in Rule Coverage:** The head rules (Table 1) don't cover all POS combinations, leading to fallbacks. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of strategies for expanding rule coverage, perhaps through semi-supervised learning or automated rule induction, would strengthen the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your approach to existing rule-based dependency parsers, highlighting the specific innovations and benefits of your method?
2.  How sensitive are the PageRank parameters and function word directionality decisions to the language being parsed? Could you elaborate on your experiments with other initialization strategies, potentially using a small sample of the target language for adaptation?
3.  What is the impact of the limited head rule coverage on the overall parsing accuracy?  Do you have any plans to address this limitation?
4.  Regarding the performance on Japanese (excluded from the evaluation), would it be possible to provide results using a POS tagger that *does* provide POS tags, even if word forms are not available?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of error analysis could be expanded.  Identifying common error patterns and their causes would provide valuable insights for future improvement.
*   The notation in Figure 1 could be clarified. For instance, defining H and D upfront would improve readability.
*   Please provide links to the publicly available parser and datasets in the final version of the paper.



Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. While some aspects could be further explored and refined, the demonstrated robustness, practical applicability, and insightful use of UD constraints make this work worthy of acceptance.
