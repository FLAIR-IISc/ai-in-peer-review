This paper presents a surprisingly strong unsupervised dependency parser, UDP, that requires no training data and leverages the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The core idea – relying on linguistically motivated rules and personalized PageRank – is well-executed and yields competitive results across a large number of languages (32). The system’s robustness to domain shift is also a notable strength.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A competitive unsupervised dependency parser:** The UDP system achieves UAS scores comparable to supervised, delexicalized transfer parsers on a large multilingual benchmark (32 languages). This is a significant achievement given the lack of any training data.
2. **Effective exploitation of UD constraints:** The paper demonstrates the utility of incorporating UD’s structural constraints (e.g., function words as leaves) into an unsupervised parsing framework. This distinguishes the work from previous rule-aided unsupervised approaches.
3. **Robustness to domain shift:** The system exhibits greater stability across different domains compared to a supervised baseline. This suggests the benefits of a rule-based approach that is less prone to overfitting to specific datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The parser achieves impressive performance on a wide range of languages, outperforming or matching a strong supervised baseline on several languages. This is the most compelling argument for the paper’s significance. The comprehensive evaluation on 32 languages is a substantial contribution in itself.
2. **Clear and well-motivated methodology:** The paper clearly explains the key components of the system, including the PageRank setup, head direction estimation, and decoding algorithm. The rationale behind each design choice is well-articulated and connected to the UD formalism.
3. **Thorough evaluation:** The evaluation strategy is robust, including comparisons against a competitive baseline (delexicalized transfer parser) and analysis of performance across different POS tagging scenarios (gold vs. predicted) and domains. The domain shift experiments are particularly valuable.
4. **Practical contribution:** The authors make the parser and annotated datasets publicly available, fostering reproducibility and further research.
5. **Effective error analysis:** The detailed discussion of error analysis provides valuable insights into the limitations of the system and potential areas for improvement. The breakdown by POS tags is particularly informative.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the individual components (PageRank, rule-based parsing) are not novel in themselves. The primary novelty lies in the application of these techniques within the UD framework and the specific adaptations made for unsupervised parsing.
2. **Dependence on POS tags:** The parser's performance is still heavily reliant on the quality of the POS tags. While the system is more robust to POS errors than the supervised baseline, inaccurate POS tags can still significantly degrade performance. Further investigation into techniques for mitigating the impact of POS errors would be valuable.
3. **Simplifying assumptions:** The head direction estimation based on adposition-noun bigram frequencies is a simplification that may not hold true for all languages. More sophisticated methods for estimating head direction could potentially improve performance. The hardcoded direction for other function words might also be suboptimal.
4. **Lack of comparative analysis with other unsupervised methods:** The paper primarily focuses on comparison with a supervised baseline. A more thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art unsupervised dependency parsing methods would provide a more complete picture of the system's performance.
5. **Limited discussion of linguistic limitations:** While the paper mentions that the rules do not capture relative clauses, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of other linguistic phenomena that the system struggles with and the reasons for these limitations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. You mention that the head rules were selected on English development data. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of development language? Would using a different language for rule selection significantly impact performance on other languages?
2. The system’s performance on languages with less well-defined UD treebanks is of interest. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the performance on these languages and discuss any potential issues related to annotation quality?
3. While you show results with predicted POS tags, how do the errors in POS tagging specifically affect the dependency parsing performance? Could you provide an error analysis linking specific types of POS tagging errors to dependency parsing errors?
4. What is the computational cost of the PageRank calculation, and how does it scale with sentence length and vocabulary size? Is it a bottleneck for processing very large texts?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The tables and figures are informative and effectively illustrate the key findings.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
*   The references are complete and accurate.
* The inclusion of standard deviations in the results tables is very helpful.
