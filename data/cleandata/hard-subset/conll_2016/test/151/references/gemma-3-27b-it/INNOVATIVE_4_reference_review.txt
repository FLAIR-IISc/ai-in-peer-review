This paper presents UDP, a remarkably bold approach to dependency parsing – a system requiring *no training data* and relying solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and a personalized PageRank algorithm. The core contribution is a demonstration that, despite the historical reliance on supervised learning in this field, a surprisingly competitive parser can be built by deeply exploiting the structure inherent in UD itself. A second significant contribution lies in the robustness of the system to domain shift, a common failing of data-driven approaches, stemming from its rule-based nature. Finally, the comprehensive evaluation across 32 languages, while not exhaustive in its error analysis, provides strong evidence of the method’s generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Radical Novelty:** The premise of a training-free, competitive dependency parser is truly groundbreaking. The field has become heavily reliant on increasingly complex supervised models. This work challenges that trend and opens up a potentially fruitful avenue for low-resource languages and scenarios where labeled data is unavailable. This isn't an incremental improvement; it's a fundamentally different approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance, being comparable to a delexicalized transfer parser (and even outperforming it in some cases), is impressive given the absence of training. The evaluation across a large number of languages strengthens the claim that this isn't a fluke tied to a specific linguistic structure. The consistent performance even with predicted POS tags is a testament to the robustness of the underlying UD constraints.
3. **Effective Utilization of UD:** The paper cleverly leverages the constraints imposed by UD – single-rooted trees, function word placement – to guide the parsing process. The explicit focus on these constraints is a key strength and demonstrates a deep understanding of the UD framework. The adaptation to estimate adposition direction on the fly is a particularly elegant solution.
4. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The experimental results showcasing UDP's resilience to domain changes are compelling. This addresses a critical weakness of many machine learning approaches and further solidifies the value of this rule-based system.  The comparative analysis against MSD on this front is particularly informative.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper presents a broad evaluation, a deeper dive into the types of errors UDP makes would be valuable. Identifying consistent error patterns (e.g., specific POS tag confusions, problematic dependency relations) would offer insights into potential areas for improvement. The discussion touches on some issues (e.g., relative clauses, punctuation) but lacks detailed analysis.
2. **Heuristic Nature of Rules:** The head-dependent rules, while effective, are somewhat ad-hoc and derived from English. The authors acknowledge this, but a more systematic exploration of how these rules generalize (or fail to generalize) across languages would be beneficial. A rigorous justification for the choice of these specific rules is lacking.
3. **PageRank Parameter Tuning:** The description of PageRank parameter tuning is brief. While the authors state they tuned the teleport probability on development data, the process could be elaborated upon. It's not clear how sensitive the performance is to variations in this parameter.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** The comparison is primarily against a delexicalized transfer parser. While a reasonable choice, including a much simpler baseline (e.g., a basic left-to-right dependency assignment) would further highlight the value of the proposed approach, and specifically, the contribution of the PageRank component.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the most frequent types of errors UDP makes across different languages? Are there specific POS tags or dependency relations that consistently cause problems?
2. How sensitive is the performance of UDP to the choice of the head-dependent rules? Have you explored alternative rule sets, and if so, what were the results?
3. Could you elaborate on the PageRank parameter tuning process? How did you determine the optimal teleport probability for each language, and how much does performance vary with different settings?
4. Would the performance change significantly if the head-dependent rules were derived from a multilingual corpus, instead of being primarily based on English?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The inclusion of a concrete example (Section 4) is helpful for understanding the algorithm. The availability of the code and data is highly appreciated. The authors should also discuss the computational complexity of the PageRank step and its impact on scalability for very long sentences.
