This paper presents a surprisingly strong unsupervised dependency parser, UDP, leveraging the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The core idea – building a parser *without* training data by strictly adhering to UD’s structural prescriptions – is compelling and demonstrably effective. The authors claim contributions in adapting head rules to UD, incorporating the function word leaf constraint, utilizing personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and achieving language independence through on-the-fly adposition direction estimation. I would re-order these contributions: the most significant contribution is the demonstrated ability to achieve competitive parsing performance *without any training data* by exploiting UD constraints, a second is the effective use of personalized PageRank to improve predicate identification, and a third is the method for dynamically estimating adposition attachment direction. The adaptation of rules and function word constraints are enabling components of the core approach rather than independent contributions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance Without Training:** The most significant strength is the surprisingly high UAS scores achieved without relying on any training data. Consistently achieving results near or exceeding a delexicalized transfer parser across 32 languages is a substantial achievement. This demonstrates the power of carefully exploiting linguistic constraints, specifically those enshrined in UD. The fact that performance degrades only slightly when using predicted POS tags further strengthens this claim.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The results presented in Table 6 clearly demonstrate UDP’s superior robustness to domain changes compared to the delexicalized transfer baseline. The lower standard deviations across domains confirm this. This robustness is a key advantage for real-world applications where the test data distribution may differ significantly from the training data.
3. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation behind the approach. The authors effectively connect the emergence of UD with the potential for a constraint-based parsing system. The reasoning behind each design choice, such as the personalization of PageRank, is explained logically and convincingly.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:**  The evaluation is comprehensive, encompassing 32 languages, different POS scenarios (gold, predicted), and a domain shift analysis. The inclusion of a strong baseline (multi-source delexicalized parser) provides a valuable point of comparison. The provision of in-house annotated data for a more detailed domain analysis is appreciated.
5. **Reproducibility:**  The authors state the parser and data are freely available, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is novel and effective, many of the individual components (PageRank, rule-based parsing) are not new. The key innovation lies in how these components are orchestrated within the UD framework. The paper doesn’t fully emphasize this holistic nature of the innovation.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Quality:** While the system is robust *relative* to the baseline, performance still heavily relies on the quality of the POS tags. The large performance drop when using naive POS tags (content/function) underscores this point. Addressing this dependence, potentially through integrated POS tagging, could further improve the system’s applicability.
3. **Handling of Complex Structures:** The paper acknowledges limitations in handling complex structures like relative clauses. The current rule set doesn't accommodate these, which limits the parser’s ability to handle more complex sentences accurately. A more sophisticated rule set or the incorporation of other linguistic knowledge could mitigate this limitation.
4. **Simplification of Language Variation:** The assumption of universal head direction rules, while simplifying the problem, may not hold true for all languages. The paper acknowledges some exceptions (e.g., Basque) but doesn’t explore more nuanced approaches to accommodate language-specific variations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How computationally expensive is the PageRank calculation, especially for very long sentences? Could alternative graph algorithms or approximation techniques be used to improve efficiency?
2.  You mention the possibility of extending the parser with edge labeling. What is your assessment of the feasibility and potential benefits of incorporating edge labels into the current framework?
3.  The performance on Japanese is not reported due to the lack of word forms in the treebank. Have you considered techniques to address this limitation, such as morphological analysis or character-based modeling?
4. The adposition direction estimation is done on test data. How sensitive is this estimation to the size of the test set, especially for low-resource languages where only a small amount of data is available?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures (especially Fig. 1) are very helpful for understanding the algorithm.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The discussion section provides a good analysis of the results and potential directions for future work.
* Consider adding a table showing the size of the UD treebanks used in the evaluation. This will give readers a better sense of the data resources available for each language.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to unsupervised dependency parsing. The system's ability to achieve competitive performance without training data is a significant accomplishment. Despite some limitations, the paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and offers valuable insights into the potential of constraint-based parsing. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
