This paper presents a surprisingly effective, entirely unsupervised dependency parser leveraging the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that a surprisingly accurate parser can be built *without any training data*, relying solely on UD’s structural restrictions and a clever application of personalized PageRank. While the method isn’t groundbreaking in its individual components, the combination and resulting performance—comparable to supervised transfer learning—is a significant result. A second, valuable contribution is the detailed analysis of the system's robustness to domain shift, showcasing its stability compared to data-driven approaches. Finally, the comprehensive evaluation across 32 languages is commendable and provides a strong empirical foundation for the claims.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** Building a competitive dependency parser without *any* training is a genuinely novel endeavor. The field is saturated with supervised and semi-supervised methods, making this purely rule-based, constraint-driven approach stand out. While rule-based parsing isn't new, the elegant integration with UD constraints and the use of personalized PageRank to address predicate identification is a clever and effective combination.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores across a diverse set of languages, frequently rivaling a delexicalized transfer parser. This isn’t just a theoretical exercise; the system demonstrably *works* well, especially considering the lack of training data. The comparative results are compelling.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The evaluation demonstrating increased robustness to domain changes is a significant strength. The fact that the parser's performance degrades less than the supervised baseline when applied to different domains underscores the benefit of its reliance on linguistic constraints rather than data-specific patterns.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating on 32 languages is a substantial undertaking and provides a broad assessment of the system’s generalizability. This wide-ranging evaluation adds significant weight to the claims of effectiveness.
5. **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The publicly available code and data further enhance the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide some discussion of error sources, a more detailed error analysis—identifying systematic errors and potential areas for improvement—would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the parser struggles would be valuable for future work. Specifically, a discussion on languages where performance is significantly lower than average is warranted.
2. **Dependency on POS Tag Accuracy:** While the authors acknowledge the impact of POS tag quality, the system’s performance is still inherently tied to the accuracy of the POS tagger. While it outperforms a comparable system *given* predicted POS tags, the system doesn't address the problem of poor POS tagging itself. Exploring methods for incorporating POS tag confidence into the parsing process could be a valuable extension.
3. **Simplistic Handling of Complex Structures:** The parser's inability to handle relative clauses, as acknowledged by the authors, is a limitation. While understandable given the unsupervised nature of the approach, this highlights the inherent constraints of relying solely on basic UD constraints. This constraint should be more prominently discussed in the limitations section.
4. **PageRank Parameter Tuning:** The paper mentions tuning the PageRank teleport probability on English development data. It is not clear how sensitive the performance is to this parameter. Providing a sensitivity analysis would add confidence to the choice of the final value.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common error patterns and discussing potential causes (e.g., ambiguity, limitations of the head rules)?
2. Have you considered incorporating POS tag confidence scores into the parsing process? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
3. Could you elaborate on the limitations of the chosen head rules and discuss potential strategies for expanding the rule set without introducing training data?
4. How sensitive is the performance of the parser to the choice of the teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm? Could you provide a sensitivity analysis?

The paper presents a compelling argument for the value of leveraging linguistic constraints in unsupervised parsing. Despite the minor weaknesses, the novelty and strong empirical results make this a valuable contribution to the field. I lean towards acceptance.
