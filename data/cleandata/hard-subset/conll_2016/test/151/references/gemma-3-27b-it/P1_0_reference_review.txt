## Peer Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a completely unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. It leverages the constraints imposed by the UD formalism, specifically the separation of content and function words, and employs Personalized PageRank to improve predicate identification. The system is evaluated on 32 languages, comparing performance against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, and assesses robustness to domain shift. The authors demonstrate competitive performance, particularly highlighting the system's stability across different domains.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of a surprisingly effective, truly unsupervised parsing approach within the UD framework:** The core achievement is building a parser that achieves reasonable accuracy *without any training*, relying solely on linguistic constraints and a clever application of PageRank. This is notable in a field increasingly reliant on large-scale supervised learning.
2. **Empirical Validation of UD’s Design for Unsupervised Parsing:**  The work provides compelling evidence that the UD formalism, with its defined constraints, lends itself well to rule-based and unsupervised methods. The success of UDP validates the design choices of UD in facilitating cross-lingual transfer and simplification of parsing tasks.
3. **Comprehensive Multi-Lingual Evaluation and Domain Robustness Analysis:** The paper's extensive evaluation across 32 languages, combined with the domain shift analysis, provides a solid empirical basis for the claims.  The comparison to a strong baseline and assessment of performance degradation with predicted POS tags are particularly valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:**  While rule-based and unsupervised parsing isn’t new, the specific combination of leveraging UD constraints, Personalized PageRank, and a two-step decoding process is a novel and well-motivated approach.  It’s a refreshing counterpoint to the dominant paradigm of data-hungry neural models.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The UAS scores are competitive with a delexicalized transfer parser, especially considering the absence of any training data. The performance stability across languages and domains is particularly impressive and strongly supports the claims of the paper. The consistent performance degradation with noisy POS tags is expected and validates the system's reliance on accurate POS information.
3. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The method is clearly explained, and the experimental setup and results are presented in a logical and understandable manner.  The inclusion of a detailed example (Section 4) is helpful for understanding the parsing process.
4. **Thorough Evaluation & Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting UAS scores. The analysis of PageRank’s contribution, breakdown by POS, and domain robustness provides valuable insights into the system’s behavior and limitations. This level of analysis strengthens the credibility of the findings.
5. **Publicly Available Resources:** The availability of both the parser and the annotated datasets used for evaluation encourages reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis beyond POS and Domain:** While the authors analyze performance by POS and across domains, a deeper qualitative error analysis is missing. Identifying specific types of parsing errors (e.g., incorrect attachment of specific dependency relations, difficulties with long-distance dependencies) would provide a more nuanced understanding of the system's weaknesses.
2. **Reliance on English for Parameter Tuning:**  The reliance on English for tuning PageRank parameters and head directionality for function words is a potential limitation. While the authors claim generalizability, it's unclear how sensitive the performance is to these parameters when applied to typologically diverse languages.  A brief exploration of parameter tuning on a few representative languages could strengthen this point.
3. **Simplistic POS Tagging Scenario:** While the low-resource POS tag scenario serves as a lower bound, the CONTENT/FUNCTION split is arguably *too* simplistic and might not accurately reflect the challenges of real-world POS tagging errors. Exploring a more realistic, though still low-resource, POS tagging scenario (e.g. using a smaller, less accurate tagger trained on a limited amount of data) would give a more realistic estimate.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Other Unsupervised Approaches:**  The comparison is primarily against a delexicalized transfer parser. While a relevant baseline, comparing UDP to other recent unsupervised or weakly-supervised dependency parsing methods (even if they don't specifically target UD) would provide a more complete picture of the state-of-the-art.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of parsing errors made by UDP? Specifically, are there particular dependency relations or syntactic structures that consistently cause problems?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of UDP to the PageRank parameters (teleport probability, personalization weight) when tuned on English versus tuned individually for a few other languages?
3.  Did you observe any systematic differences in performance (beyond overall UAS) based on language family or typological characteristics? Were there any language groups where UDP consistently outperformed or underperformed?
4.  The paper mentions excluding Japanese due to the lack of word forms.  Have you considered investigating alternative approaches to handle languages without explicit word segmentation, potentially adapting techniques from other related areas like morphological analysis?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper’s clear focus on the UD framework is a strength, making the contribution well-aligned with the goals of the UD project.
*   The inclusion of both positive and negative results (e.g., the limitations of the PUNCT POS) enhances the credibility of the work.
*   It would be beneficial to include a discussion of the computational complexity of the parsing process, especially concerning the PageRank calculation.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. The novel approach, compelling results, and thorough evaluation make it a valuable addition to the literature. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
