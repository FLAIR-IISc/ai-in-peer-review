This paper presents a dependency parser that operates without training, relying instead on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and a personalized PageRank algorithm. The core claim is achieving competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer parser across 32 languages, while exhibiting greater robustness to domain shift.

I identify two primary contributions. First, the systematic application of UD constraints – specifically the function word leaf status – to a rule-based parsing framework is a reasonable and potentially valuable exploration. While not a groundbreakingly novel idea, the explicit encoding of UD’s design principles into a parser is a logical step, particularly given the increasing prevalence of UD treebanks. Second, the empirical evaluation across a substantial number of languages, including an assessment of domain robustness, is a strength. The sheer scale of the evaluation provides some confidence in the generalizability of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Extensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages is commendable. This broad coverage allows for a more robust assessment of the parser’s generalizability than evaluations limited to a handful of languages. The inclusion of domain adaptation experiments, though preliminary, is also a positive step.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves surprisingly competitive results compared to a delexicalized transfer system, especially considering its lack of training data. While the margin of improvement isn’t overwhelming, it suggests the UD constraints offer a useful inductive bias. The fact that UDP outperforms MSD in certain domains, particularly Italian, is noteworthy.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The results indicating greater robustness to domain shift are promising. This is a significant advantage for a zero-training parser, as it reduces the need for domain-specific adaptation. This aligns with the authors’ stated goal of exploiting UD’s formalism.
4. **Clear Methodology:** The paper clearly outlines the core principles of the parser, including the PageRank setup and the two-step decoding algorithm. The provided details allow for replication and further investigation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core ideas – rule-based parsing, PageRank, and exploiting linguistic constraints – are not new. The primary novelty lies in the combination of these techniques within the UD framework. However, the combination feels incremental rather than transformative. The claim of a “fresh angle” feels overstated.
2. **Reliance on Gold POS Tags:** The parser’s performance is heavily reliant on high-quality POS tags. The results demonstrate a significant drop in performance when using predicted POS tags, highlighting a vulnerability that limits its applicability in truly low-resource scenarios. While the authors acknowledge this, the extent of the performance degradation is substantial. More investigation into how to mitigate this dependency on POS tag quality is needed.
3. **Oversimplification of Linguistic Complexity:** The approach, by design, simplifies complex linguistic phenomena. For example, the hardcoded head direction rules, while effective, inevitably lead to errors in languages with more flexible word order. The exclusion of noun-verb dependencies further limits its expressiveness. The paper doesn’t adequately address the trade-offs between simplicity and accuracy.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall performance, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the parser fails – specific linguistic structures, language families, etc. – would provide valuable insights for future improvements. The discussion of error analysis in Section 6 is too superficial. The table comparing performance across domains lacks sufficient detail.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the personalized PageRank algorithm to the choice of the personalization weight (currently set to 5)? Have you explored a wider range of values?
2.  The paper mentions the exclusion of the NOUN → VERB head rule. Could you provide more justification for this decision? What is the impact on the parser’s performance on languages with frequent relative clauses or other constructions where nouns modify verbs?
3. The evaluation on domain shift uses a limited number of languages. Can you provide a more comprehensive analysis of the robustness to domain shift across a larger subset of the evaluated languages?
4. How computationally expensive is the PageRank step, and how does it scale with sentence length? The claim of less than O(n^2) seems dubious given the PageRank calculation.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to other unsupervised parsing approaches, beyond the delexicalized transfer system.
*   The supplementary material should include a more detailed description of the head rules and their rationale.
*   The formatting of Table 1 and Figure 1 is somewhat inconsistent and could be improved for clarity.



Overall, this paper presents a reasonable, though not revolutionary, approach to unsupervised dependency parsing. The extensive evaluation and competitive performance are commendable. However, the reliance on high-quality POS tags and the limited novelty raise concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the weaknesses outlined above and providing more detailed responses to the questions.
