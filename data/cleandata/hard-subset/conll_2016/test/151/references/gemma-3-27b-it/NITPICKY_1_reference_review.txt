This paper presents an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that eschews training data, relying instead on UD’s inherent constraints and personalized PageRank. The authors claim contributions in adapting head rules to UD, incorporating function word constraints, using personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and achieving language independence through runtime adposition direction estimation.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a demonstration of the surprisingly strong performance achievable with a highly constrained, rule-based system leveraging the UD formalism. The evaluation across a substantial number of languages (32) provides a valuable benchmark, and the comparison to a delexicalized transfer system strengthens this assessment. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough empirical evaluation of the system’s robustness to domain shift, which is an important consideration for practical applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline Performance:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores, frequently surpassing the delexicalized transfer system (MSD) – particularly when considering domain robustness. This demonstrates the potential of a carefully crafted rule-based system when operating within a well-defined linguistic framework like UD. This is a significant result, especially given the lack of training data.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating on 32 languages, covering a diverse range of linguistic families, provides a robust assessment of the system’s generalizability. The inclusion of domain adaptation experiments further bolsters the evaluation’s realism and practical relevance.
3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The inclusion of an example parse (Section 4) is helpful for understanding the system’s operation.
4. **Robustness to POS Errors:** The results indicate the parser exhibits greater stability than the MSD system when dealing with imperfect POS tags. This is a crucial advantage in low-resource settings where high-quality taggers may not be available. The system’s reliance on broader UD constraints partially mitigates the impact of POS tagging errors.
5. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The discussion section provides a valuable error analysis, identifying specific areas for improvement and highlighting the limitations of the current approach. The analysis of POS-specific performance and domain sensitivity demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the system's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methodology:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the individual components (rule-based parsing, PageRank) are well-established. The novelty lies primarily in the specific application to the UD framework and the particular constraints implemented, rather than groundbreaking algorithmic innovation. This is a fairly incremental contribution.
2. **Overly Simplistic POS Handling:** The two-step decoding approach, particularly the handling of function words, feels somewhat ad-hoc. While effective, it lacks a strong theoretical justification and might be overly restrictive in certain languages. The reliance on English-derived rules for directionality, even with runtime adaptation for adpositions, introduces potential bias. The naive content/function POS setup for evaluation is a drastic simplification that doesn’t reflect real-world tagging performance adequately.
3. **Insufficient Detail on Rule Selection:** The paper mentions "tuning" parameters and rules on English development data, but the specific process and criteria used for rule selection are not adequately detailed. This makes it difficult to assess the objectivity and generalizability of the chosen rules. A more systematic explanation of the rule development process is needed.
4. **Inconsistent Reporting and Minor Errors:** There's a small inconsistency in the notation within Fig. 1 (colon vs. equals sign for assignment). Additionally, the description of Table 5's formatting (underlining and bolding) is inconsistent with the table itself. These small details detract from the paper's overall polish.
5. **Missing Error Analysis on Specific Linguistic Phenomena:** The error analysis could be more detailed. Specifically, it would be helpful to examine the types of errors made by the parser on specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., relative clauses, coordination structures, long-distance dependencies). A more fine-grained analysis would provide valuable insights into the system’s limitations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the rule selection process, including the specific criteria used to evaluate and refine the head-dependent rules on the English development data?
2.  How sensitive is the system’s performance to the choice of teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm? Did you explore a wider range of values, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the specific POS constraints imposed on function words? Are there languages where these constraints are particularly problematic, and if so, how do you address them?
4.  Regarding the evaluation on domain-shifted data, what is the rationale behind choosing those specific domains, and how representative are they of broader domain variations?
5.  The paper states that the system is deterministic given a POS sequence. Does this mean that any inconsistencies in parse output are solely attributable to POS tagging errors? Can you confirm this statement with experimental evidence?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is occasionally inconsistent.
*   The link to the datasets should be made more prominent.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential future directions, such as incorporating distributional semantics or exploring alternative graph-based ranking algorithms.



Despite its incremental nature, this is a solid piece of work with a clear presentation and a comprehensive evaluation. However, the issues raised above – particularly the limited novelty and the need for greater detail on rule selection – prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance at this time. A major revision addressing these concerns would be necessary for reconsideration. I lean towards **rejection** as is.
