This paper presents a dependency parser that operates without training, relying instead on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and personalized PageRank. The authors claim contributions in adapting dependency head rules to UD, incorporating the function word constraint, using personalized PageRank, and achieving language independence through test data-driven adposition attachment. 

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the demonstration of a surprisingly competitive unsupervised parser leveraging the constraints inherent in the UD framework. The secondary contribution lies in the effective use of personalized PageRank within this constrained parsing paradigm. While the method isn’t groundbreaking in its individual components, the combination yields a functional and robust system.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves respectable UAS scores across 32 languages *without any training data*. While not state-of-the-art, consistently scoring within 6.4% of a supervised delexicalized parser is a significant achievement, especially given the inherent challenges of unsupervised parsing. The stability across languages is noteworthy.
2. **Leveraging UD Constraints:** The core idea of tightly integrating the UD formalism’s constraints – particularly the function word restriction – is well-executed and demonstrably effective. This moves beyond simply applying UD treebanks and actively *uses* the principles encoded within them.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The experimental results demonstrating the system’s relative stability in the face of domain changes are compelling. This is a critical advantage over data-driven approaches which often suffer from domain-specific overfitting. The lower standard deviation in performance across domains, compared to the supervised baseline, supports this claim.
4. **Clear and Thorough Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across a large number of languages, considering both gold and predicted POS tags, and including a detailed analysis of domain sensitivity. The inclusion of a strong baseline (delexicalized transfer parser) is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Individual Components:** The individual techniques – PageRank, head rules, and POS constraints – are not novel in themselves. The innovation lies in their specific combination within the UD framework, but the paper doesn't sufficiently highlight *why* this particular combination is so successful, beyond simply stating that it "works." A deeper analysis of the interaction between these components is needed.
2. **Overly Simplistic POS Handling:** The handling of POS tags, particularly the two-type (CONTENT/FUNCTION) simplification for the "naive" scenario, feels unnecessarily crude. While intended to demonstrate robustness, it creates a scenario that isn’t particularly realistic or informative. The results are predictable, and the experiment doesn't provide substantial insight.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While domain sensitivity is explored, the error analysis lacks depth.  A more detailed examination of *where* the parser fails – specific dependency types, languages, or linguistic phenomena – would provide valuable insights and suggest avenues for improvement. The mention of limitations regarding relative clauses due to the exclusion of NOUN → VERB is a good start, but this should be expanded.
4. **Vague Descriptions & Missing Details:** Several aspects of the implementation are glossed over. For example, the specific details of the “tuning” of PageRank parameters on English data are scant. Also, the implementation of the directionality rule for function words feels ad-hoc. More detailed explanation would improve reproducibility and understanding.
5. **Presentation of Results:** Table 5 is cumbersome to read due to the numerous columns and lack of clear visual cues. Highlighting the most significant differences (e.g., bolding only the largest differences between UDPG and MSDG) would improve readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the interaction between the UD constraints, PageRank personalization, and the two-step decoding process? What are the key synergies that contribute to the observed performance?
2.  The paper states that the head rules were selected to maximize precision on the development set. Can you describe the selection process in more detail? What criteria were used, and how were conflicting rules resolved?
3.  What is the computational cost of the PageRank calculation, and how does it scale with sentence length? Is this a practical limitation for processing very long sentences?
4.  Regarding the choice to exclude NOUN -> VERB, were there other head rules you considered excluding during development and why? Could a more sophisticated approach to handling this relation be explored without introducing training data?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The code availability is a significant positive aspect.
*   The phrase "personalized PR approach" appears repeatedly without a clear definition beyond giving higher weight to the estimated main predicate. A more precise description would be helpful.
*   The sentence structure in several sections (especially sections 3.2 and 3.3) is awkward and could benefit from revision for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper lacks substantial novelty in individual components, the combination of techniques and the resulting system’s performance are impressive. The thorough evaluation and robustness to domain shifts are significant strengths. However, the paper suffers from a lack of in-depth analysis and several minor weaknesses in presentation and detail. **I recommend rejection.** The weaknesses, while not fatal, are substantial enough to warrant further refinement and analysis before publication. Addressing the points raised in the "Weaknesses" section, particularly the need for a more detailed error analysis and a deeper explanation of the core mechanisms, would significantly strengthen the paper.
