## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) that leverages the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The system eschews training data altogether, relying on a set of dependency head rules, personalized PageRank for identifying main predicates, and a strategy for estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages, comparing it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system, and assess its robustness to domain change.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A surprisingly strong baseline for unsupervised parsing:** The paper demonstrates that a simple rule-based system, guided by UD constraints and personalized PageRank, can achieve competitive performance with a more complex, data-driven transfer learning approach. This is a significant finding that challenges the assumption that supervised learning is essential for accurate parsing.
2. **Effective utilization of UD constraints:**  The work successfully demonstrates how the specific constraints of the UD framework (function word attachment, single-rooted trees) can be exploited to build a functional parser *without* training. This highlights the value of well-designed linguistic formalisms for enabling zero-shot learning.
3. **Robustness to domain shift:**  The evaluation convincingly shows that UDP is more resilient to variations in domain compared to the transfer learning baseline, suggesting its potential for real-world applications in low-resource scenarios where training data from the target domain is unavailable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across 32 languages are impressive, particularly considering the lack of training data.  Consistently achieving UAS scores comparable to, and sometimes exceeding, a delexicalized transfer system is a significant accomplishment. The thorough evaluation setup, including variations in POS tag reliability and domain shifts, adds credibility to these findings.
2. **Clear and Concise Methodology:** The paper clearly outlines the key components of the UDP system and explains the rationale behind each design choice. The two-step decoding algorithm and the use of personalized PageRank are described in a digestible manner. The ablation studies in Section 6 further clarify the contributions of each component.
3. **Sound Linguistic Motivation:** The grounding of the system in UD principles is a major strength. The authors explicitly connect their design decisions to linguistic constraints, making the system interpretable and providing a strong justification for its effectiveness.  The adaptation of dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations demonstrates a good understanding of linguistic theory.
4. **Robustness Analysis:**  The detailed examination of performance across different domains and POS tags provides valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the system. The observation that UDP is more robust to domain shift aligns with expectations for rule-based systems and is a practical benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is interesting, the individual components (PageRank, rule-based parsing, POS tagging) are not novel.  The core innovation lies in *how* these components are integrated and tailored to the UD framework, but the paper could benefit from more explicitly acknowledging the existing work on these individual elements.
2. **Dependence on POS Tag Quality:** The system is still fundamentally reliant on accurate POS tags. While the paper addresses this through evaluations with predicted POS tags, the performance degradation in this scenario is substantial. The system’s vulnerability to POS errors limits its applicability to truly low-resource languages where high-quality POS taggers are unavailable.  More exploration of methods to mitigate this dependence, such as incorporating POS tag confidence scores, would be beneficial.
3. **Rule Engineering and Language Specificity:** While the authors claim the system is free of language-specific parameters, the head direction estimation for adpositions and the fixed attachment directions for certain function words introduce a degree of language-specific tuning.  The reliance on English dev data for these decisions is a potential limitation, and a more automated or cross-linguistically informed approach to these parameters would be desirable.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  While Section 6.3 provides some initial error analysis, a more detailed and targeted investigation of parsing errors would be valuable.  Identifying the types of dependencies that are consistently misparsed (e.g., relative clauses, coordination structures) would provide insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions excluding Japanese due to the lack of word forms in the treebank. Have you considered evaluating the system on other languages with similar data limitations, such as morphologically rich languages where word forms are not always clearly defined?
2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing the specific teleport probability of 0.05 in the PageRank algorithm? Were any other values investigated, and what was the impact on performance?
3.  Regarding the language-specific head direction estimation, how sensitive is the system’s performance to errors in this estimation?  Would a more conservative approach, such as defaulting to a common attachment direction, improve overall performance?
4.  The paper highlights the robustness of UDP to domain shifts. Could you provide some examples of specific parsing errors that are more common in certain domains, and how UDP avoids these errors compared to the transfer learning baseline?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The inclusion of the code and data would greatly enhance the reproducibility of the results.
*   The comparison to the delexicalized transfer parser is appropriate, but including a comparison to other unsupervised parsing methods would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   The phrase "leaves" in relation to function words (repeatedly used) is technically incorrect. Function words are dependents, but not necessarily leaves in the tree structure.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that presents a novel and effective approach to unsupervised dependency parsing.  The system demonstrates impressive performance, particularly given the lack of training data. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
