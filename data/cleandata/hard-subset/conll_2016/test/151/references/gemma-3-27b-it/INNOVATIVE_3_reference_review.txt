This paper presents a remarkably bold approach to cross-lingual dependency parsing: a completely unsupervised system leveraging the constraints inherent in the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The core idea – eschewing training data entirely and relying on linguistic rules and personalized PageRank – is a significant departure from the dominant paradigm of transfer learning and annotation projection. The successful application of this approach across 32 languages, achieving competitive performance against a strong delexicalized baseline, is impressive. The demonstrated robustness to domain shift further strengthens the contribution.

The primary contribution is the demonstration that a highly functional dependency parser *can* be built without any training data, simply by intelligently exploiting the structural constraints of UD. A secondary contribution lies in the practical implementation of this idea, specifically the effective use of personalized PageRank to identify main predicates and the rule-based system for head direction. Finally, the extensive empirical evaluation across numerous languages and domains provides valuable insights into the system’s strengths and limitations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Leap:** The complete absence of training data is a genuinely novel approach in dependency parsing. Most existing systems, even those claiming to be unsupervised, rely on some form of pre-training or weak supervision. This work directly challenges that assumption.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving competitive performance with a delexicalized transfer parser, and demonstrating robustness to domain shift, indicates the method is not merely a theoretical exercise. The results across 32 languages show a remarkable degree of generalization.
3. **Focus on Linguistic Constraints:** The paper correctly identifies the under-exploitation of inherent linguistic constraints within frameworks like UD. By prioritizing these constraints, the authors have built a parser that is arguably more linguistically grounded than many data-driven alternatives.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, covering a large number of languages, varying POS tagging reliability, and assessing domain sensitivity. This provides a robust understanding of the system’s performance characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper touches on error sources, a more detailed error analysis would be valuable. Understanding *where* the system fails (specific linguistic phenomena, language families) would provide crucial insights for future development. The statement regarding noun-verb attachments is a good starting point, but more specifics are needed.
2. **Over-Reliance on English for Parameter Tuning:** The reliance on English data for tuning PageRank parameters and head direction is a potential limitation. While the results suggest the rules generalize well, it’s unclear whether the optimal parameters for English are universally applicable. A more systematic, language-agnostic tuning method would be ideal.
3. **Simplistic POS Scenario:** While the POS-agnostic evaluation is valuable, the “content vs. function” POS scenario is *too* simplistic to be truly informative. Real-world POS tagging errors are rarely this coarse-grained. A more realistic evaluation using predicted POS tags from standard taggers is essential (and thankfully, provided).
4. **Lack of Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** While the delexicalized transfer parser is a reasonable baseline, comparing against more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual parsing models (e.g., multilingual BERT-based parsers) would provide a clearer benchmark for the system’s performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on specific linguistic phenomena that the parser struggles with?
2. Have you experimented with language-agnostic methods for tuning the PageRank parameters and head direction rules, or are you confident that English-tuned parameters generalize sufficiently well?
3. Is there a clear reason for the lower performance on Japanese as mentioned in the introduction? Does it stem exclusively from the lack of word forms in the treebank, or are there other factors at play?
4. How sensitive is the system to the teleport probability used in PageRank? Did you explore a wider range of values during development?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The provided code and data would be a valuable resource for the research community. While the system is not perfect, its novelty and promising results justify acceptance. The willingness to challenge conventional wisdom in parsing is commendable.
