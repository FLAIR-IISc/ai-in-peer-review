This paper presents a dependency parser that operates without training, relying solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and personalized PageRank. The authors claim contributions in adapting dependency head rules to UD, incorporating the function word constraint, utilizing personalized PageRank, and achieving language independence through on-the-fly adposition direction estimation.

A notable contribution is the comprehensive evaluation of a truly unsupervised parser across a large number of UD languages (32). The systematic comparison against a delexicalized transfer system provides a useful benchmark. The exploration of domain sensitivity is also valuable, though limited in scope. The availability of the parser and datasets is commendable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Extensive Multilingual Evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages is a significant undertaking and provides a robust assessment of the parser’s generalizability. This broad coverage is a key strength, demonstrating the system’s applicability beyond a few high-resource languages.
2. **Competitive Performance (with caveats):** While not state-of-the-art, the parser achieves competitive UAS scores, especially considering its unsupervised nature. Its performance is surprisingly close to a delexicalized transfer parser, suggesting the effectiveness of leveraging UD constraints.
3. **Domain Robustness:** The results suggesting increased robustness to domain shift compared to the delexicalized parser are interesting and warrant further investigation. This is a significant advantage for applications where the target domain is unknown or variable.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methodology:** The core methodology – rule-based parsing guided by PageRank – is not entirely novel. Prior work (e.g., Søgaard, 2012b) has explored similar approaches. The personalization of PageRank is a refinement, but not a breakthrough. The claim of a significant methodological contribution feels overstated.
2. **Overly Simplistic POS Handling:** The handling of POS tags, particularly the content/function word distinction and fixed attachment directions, appears overly simplistic. While it aligns with UD principles, it seems to discard potentially useful linguistic information. The sensitivity to the choice of POS tags (evident in the performance drop with predicted POS) is a significant limitation. The authors acknowledge this, but the impact is substantial.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The error analysis is superficial. While the authors identify some limitations (e.g., handling of relative clauses), a more detailed investigation of error patterns would be beneficial. Understanding *why* the parser fails in certain cases is crucial for guiding future improvements.  The discussion of the POS-specific performance feels cursory.
4. **Minor Inconsistencies and Formatting Issues:** Several minor inconsistencies detract from the paper's polish. For instance, there's a formatting error in the table captions (e.g., “Table 5” in text refers to content that is actually Table 6.) There are also occasional grammatical lapses and stylistic awkwardness. While individually minor, these issues accumulate and create a less professional impression. The inclusion of a redundant "Github link" is unusual.
5. **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Comparing to a delexicalized transfer system is reasonable, but a comparison against more recent and powerful cross-lingual parsing models would provide a more compelling benchmark.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the most frequent error types and the linguistic factors that contribute to them?
2.  The personalization of PageRank seems to focus solely on identifying the root. Have you considered other personalization strategies, such as biasing towards specific POS tags or dependency relations?
3.  The paper notes limitations regarding relative clauses. Could you discuss potential extensions to the head rules to address this issue, even if it introduces additional complexity?
4.  Why was the parameter for the teleport probability chosen as 0.05, and what impact does varying this value have on convergence and performance?
5. The table captions in the text refer to the wrong table numbers. Please correct them.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citations are sometimes inconsistent in formatting.
* The reference list lacks DOI's.
*   Please clarify the in-house annotation setup for the English datasets.



**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper presents a valuable evaluation of an unsupervised parser, the lack of significant methodological novelty, the overly simplistic POS handling, the insufficient error analysis, and the minor polish issues outweigh the strengths.  The broad multilingual evaluation is commendable, but does not compensate for the underlying weaknesses. A major revision addressing these concerns would be necessary for reconsideration.
