## Peer Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents UDP, a novel unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that eschews training data, relying instead on explicit POS constraints and a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify main predicates. The system estimates adposition direction directly from the test data, aiming for language independence.  The authors evaluate UDP across 32 languages, comparing its performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system, and assess its robustness to domain shift.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical demonstration of surprisingly strong performance for a truly unsupervised dependency parser.**  The results showing competitive performance with a supervised transfer baseline, even with predicted POS tags, are notable and highlight the power of leveraging UD’s constraints.
2. **A well-engineered system integrating UD constraints with a graph-based parsing approach.** The combination of POS-based rules, personalized PageRank, and dynamic adposition direction estimation represents a coherent and thoughtful design.
3. **A comprehensive cross-lingual evaluation.** The evaluation across 32 languages, and subsequent domain shift analysis, provides valuable insights into the generalizability of the approach and its relative robustness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The core strength of this paper is its empirical validation.  Achieving UAS scores comparable to a delexicalized transfer parser *without any training* is a significant accomplishment. The robustness shown with predicted POS tags is particularly impressive, suggesting the method is less susceptible to errors in upstream processing.
2. **Effective Leverage of UD Constraints:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the value of explicitly incorporating UD's grammatical constraints. By designing the system around these constraints, they achieve a level of performance that would be unlikely with a purely data-driven unsupervised approach.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The multi-lingual evaluation with a large number of languages and the inclusion of domain shift analysis provide a robust assessment of the system's performance and limitations. The domain shift experiments offer valuable insights into the system's generalization capability.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The inclusion of a detailed example run (Section 4) is particularly helpful for understanding the system's operation.
5. **Reproducibility:**  The authors state the parser and datasets are freely available, which facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is effective, the individual components (PageRank, dependency rules) are not novel. The contribution primarily lies in the careful adaptation and integration of existing techniques within the UD framework. This should be acknowledged more explicitly.
2. **Dependence on English for Parameter Tuning:** The reliance on English data for tuning PageRank parameters and head direction introduces a potential bias. While the results suggest the system generalizes well, the impact of this English-centric tuning on other languages should be discussed.
3. **Simplified POS Tagging Scenario:** The “naive” two-POS tag setup (CONTENT/FUNCTION) is a very coarse simplification. While it effectively demonstrates the system’s resilience, it doesn't reflect realistic low-resource scenarios where POS taggers are imperfect, but still provide more nuanced information. The performance jump between predicted and naive POS is very large, making it difficult to interpret the system’s behaviour in more realistic settings.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide some discussion of error sources (e.g., handling of coordination), a more detailed error analysis, including examples of common parsing errors for different languages, would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding the English-centric parameter tuning:** How sensitive are the PageRank parameters and fixed directionality rules to changes based on different languages?  Have you explored methods for automating this adaptation process directly from the test data?
2. **Concerning the "naive" POS tag scenario:** Could you provide results using a more realistic, albeit imperfect, POS tagger (e.g., a pre-trained multilingual tagger) for a subset of the languages? How does the performance change compared to the current results with predicted POS tags?
3. **Regarding the potential for improved performance:** Have you investigated incorporating more sophisticated methods for identifying the root node, beyond simply the first verb or content word?  For example, could techniques based on subcategorization frames or semantic role labeling be beneficial?
4. **Error Analysis details:** Can you provide a few example sentences, for multiple languages, where UDP fails and explain why it fails according to the rules and algorithms?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., some citations include full author lists, while others don’t).
*   Table 5 includes a "direction" column (L/R). This information is useful, but could be explained more clearly in the text.
* The link to the datasets should be publicly accessible and stable.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. The authors have demonstrated that, by effectively leveraging the constraints of the Universal Dependencies framework, it is possible to build a surprisingly accurate parser without relying on training data. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
