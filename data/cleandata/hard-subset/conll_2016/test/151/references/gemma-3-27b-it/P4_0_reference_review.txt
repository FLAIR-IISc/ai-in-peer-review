This paper presents a novel unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data, relying instead on explicit POS constraints and personalized PageRank. The core idea is to leverage the structural constraints inherent in UD to build a parser without learning from annotated data. The authors claim contributions in adapting dependency head rules to UD, incorporating the UD function word constraint, utilizing personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and achieving language-agnostic adposition attachment direction estimation.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of a competitive, unsupervised dependency parser that achieves surprisingly strong performance (comparable to supervised transfer learning) across a diverse set of 32 languages, without requiring any language-specific training data. This is significant given the challenges of low-resource languages and the increasing adoption of UD as a standard. The robustness of the system to domain shift, highlighted through extensive evaluation, is a further valuable contribution. The method’s simplicity and reliance on linguistic constraints, rather than complex statistical models, offers a compelling alternative approach to cross-lingual parsing. A third, smaller contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation, including a useful analysis of performance across different domains and POS tags, and an ablation study regarding the importance of PageRank.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance for an Unsupervised System:** The reported UAS scores are impressive for a completely unsupervised parser. Consistently achieving scores near those of a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, while avoiding the need for training data, is a substantial achievement. The detailed results in Table 5 and Table 6 clearly demonstrate this performance across numerous languages and domains.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The extensive evaluation across different domains (Table 6 and Table 7) convincingly demonstrates that UDP is more robust to domain changes than the supervised baseline (MSD). This is a key advantage, as it suggests the parser is less prone to overfitting to specific datasets and can generalize better to unseen text. The lower standard deviation in performance across domains for UDP is strong evidence of this.
3. **Leveraging UD Constraints:** The parser's design effectively exploits the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies formalism. The explicit enforcement of single-rooted trees and the treatment of function words as leaves, combined with the head rules, provide a strong structural foundation for parsing. This highlights the benefits of using a well-defined linguistic framework like UD.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors present a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to a strong baseline, multiple POS tag scenarios (gold, predicted, naive), and a detailed domain adaptation study. This rigorous evaluation strengthens the credibility of the findings and provides valuable insights into the parser's strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the individual components – PageRank, head rules, and dependency parsing – are not novel in themselves. The primary novelty lies in their integration and application within the UD framework. The core algorithm (Fig. 1) is relatively simple and straightforward.
2. **Dependency on POS Tagging Accuracy:** Although the parser demonstrates resilience to POS tagging errors, its performance is still ultimately dependent on the quality of the POS tags. The evaluation with predicted POS tags (UDPP) shows a noticeable drop in performance, indicating that the parser is sensitive to POS tagging errors, even if less so than the supervised baseline.
3. **Simplified Handling of Complex Structures:** The parser’s rules appear to simplify complex linguistic structures such as coordination and relative clauses, as acknowledged by the authors. The exclusion of the NOUN → VERB rule, while improving overall performance, limits the parser’s ability to handle these constructions accurately.
4. **Black Box Personalization:** The choice of multiplying the verb rank by 5 during personalization is not well justified and feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of personalization strategies, or a justification based on linguistic principles, would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the personalization factor (5x weight for the verb) in the PageRank algorithm? Were alternative values explored, and if so, what were the results?
2.  The paper acknowledges limitations in handling complex structures (coordination, relative clauses). Are there plans to extend the parser to better handle these constructions, and if so, what approaches are being considered?
3.  Regarding the domain shift experiments, could you provide more details on the creation of the in-house annotated datasets for English (Bible, magazine, questions)? What were the annotation guidelines, and what was the inter-annotator agreement?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*   The code and data used in the experiments would be valuable for reproducibility and further research. Making these publicly available would significantly enhance the impact of the work.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The inclusion of a clear example (Section 4) is particularly helpful.
*   Table 5 could benefit from highlighting languages where UDP significantly outperforms the baseline (e.g., using bold font for statistically significant improvements).
