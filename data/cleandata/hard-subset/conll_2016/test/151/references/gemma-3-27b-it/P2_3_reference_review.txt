## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies (UD) that eschews training data, relying instead on UD’s inherent constraints and a personalized PageRank algorithm for predicate identification. The system demonstrates competitive performance across 32 languages, often rivaling supervised transfer learning methods while exhibiting greater robustness to domain shift.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **A practical, effective unsupervised parsing approach leveraging UD constraints:** The paper successfully demonstrates that a significant degree of accurate parsing can be achieved *without* any training data, simply by cleverly exploiting the structure imposed by the UD project. This is a valuable contribution given the resource limitations for many languages.
2.  **Demonstration of robustness to domain shift:** The consistent performance of UDP across different domains (and even with predicted POS tags) is a strong point, suggesting a more generalizable approach than heavily data-dependent methods.
3.  **Comprehensive evaluation across a large number of languages:** Evaluating the system on 32 languages provides a strong indication of its generalizability and highlights its potential impact for low-resource languages.



**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong empirical results:** The parser achieves impressive performance, often approaching or surpassing delexicalized transfer learning, particularly considering it requires *no* training data. The comparisons to a strong baseline (delexicalized transfer) are convincing.
2.  **Clear and well-motivated design:** The design decisions are clearly linked to the principles of UD, making the system intuitive and linguistically grounded. The explanations of PageRank personalization and two-step decoding are well-articulated.
3.  **Robustness to POS tagging errors:**  The system’s performance degradation with predicted POS tags is minimal compared to the supervised baseline, demonstrating its resilience to noisy input. This is a critical advantage for low-resource languages where high-quality taggers may not be available.
4.  **Thorough evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages and the investigation of domain sensitivity are comprehensive and strengthen the credibility of the findings. The inclusion of in-house annotated datasets for English further adds to the robustness of the evaluation.
5.  **Practical impact:** The availability of the parser and the test datasets (referenced in the conclusion) facilitates reproducibility and further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited novelty in individual components:** While the *combination* of techniques is novel, individual components (PageRank, rule-based parsing) are well-established. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this work builds upon and differentiates itself from existing methods in each of these areas.
2.  **Simplistic POS handling:** The binary content/function POS distinction for the low-resource setting seems overly simplistic and likely limits performance. While demonstrating robustness, exploring more sophisticated POS tagging strategies (even if still unsupervised) could yield further improvements.
3. **Lack of error analysis on specific linguistic phenomena:** While domain sensitivity is discussed, a more detailed error analysis focusing on specific linguistic constructions (e.g., coordination, relative clauses – acknowledged as a limitation in the conclusion) would provide valuable insights into the parser’s weaknesses and potential areas for improvement.  Why *specifically* does the system struggle with these?
4. **Dependency on English for initial parameter tuning:** While the authors state the system is language-agnostic, the reliance on English data for tuning PageRank parameters and function-word directionality introduces a potential bias. A more systematic approach to language-independent parameter selection would be desirable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on specific linguistic phenomena where the parser struggles (e.g., relative clauses, coordination)? What are the common errors, and why do you think they occur?
2.  The personalization of PageRank is applied only to the first verb.  Have you experimented with different strategies for identifying the main predicate (e.g., considering all verbs or content words)?  If so, what were the results?
3.  You mention the head rules were optimized on the English dev data. How sensitive is the performance to these head rules, and could a method for automatic rule discovery be explored?
4. The content/function POS distinction seems extremely coarse-grained. Could you elaborate on why this particular simplification was chosen, and whether more fine-grained distinctions were explored?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, but some table captions are slightly unclear.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The referencing is comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a significant contribution to unsupervised dependency parsing. The system is well-designed, effectively leverages UD constraints, and demonstrates impressive performance across a large number of languages. While some individual components may lack substantial novelty, the combination and the demonstrated robustness to domain shift and POS tagging errors are compelling. I recommend acceptance.
