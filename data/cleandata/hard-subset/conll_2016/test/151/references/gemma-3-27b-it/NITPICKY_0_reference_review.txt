This paper presents a dependency parser, UDP, which operates without training, relying solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and personalized PageRank. The authors claim contributions in adapting dependency head rules to UD, incorporating the UD function word constraint, using personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and achieving language independence through on-the-fly adposition direction estimation.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a demonstration of how strictly adhering to the UD framework can yield surprisingly competitive parsing results *without* any learning. The extensive evaluation across 32 languages is commendable and highlights the system's robustness. The thorough experimentation with different POS tag qualities (gold, predicted, naive) is also a strength.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline & Competitive Performance:** UDP achieves impressive results given its unsupervised nature. The UAS scores are consistently close to, and in some cases exceed, a delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), demonstrating the effectiveness of leveraging UD constraints. This is a significant achievement for a completely training-free approach.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages is a major strength. This allows for a broad assessment of the system's generalization capabilities and identifies potential language-specific issues. The inclusion of predicted and naive POS tag scenarios adds further rigor.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The evaluation across different domains reveals UDP’s commendable stability compared to the supervised MSD baseline. This supports the authors’ argument that a rule-based, UD-compliant system is less susceptible to overfitting to specific datasets. The quantitative data presented in Table 6 clearly demonstrates this.
4. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The method is explained in a clear and detailed manner, with a well-defined decoding algorithm (Fig. 1) and justification for design choices (e.g., the teleport probability in PageRank). The explicit head rules (Table 1) are also valuable for reproducibility and understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is interesting, the individual components (PageRank, rule-based parsing) are not novel. The personalization of PageRank is a relatively minor modification. The core innovation lies in the effective application of these existing techniques within the strict confines of the UD framework, rather than the introduction of fundamentally new algorithms.
2. **Overly Strict Evaluation & Sensitivity to Annotation Quality:** While the extensive evaluation is a strength, the reliance on UAS as the sole metric is problematic. UAS is known to be sensitive to annotation differences and may not fully capture the quality of the parse trees. Furthermore, the parser’s performance, while good, is heavily reliant on relatively high-quality POS tags. The substantial drop in performance with naive POS tags underscores this vulnerability.  The error propagation analysis is mentioned but lacks depth – a more detailed analysis of error types would be beneficial.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. The authors briefly mention issues with coordination and proper names, but a more systematic investigation of common error patterns would be valuable for understanding the system’s limitations and guiding future improvements. Why does UDP struggle with particular languages or POS tags? Addressing this would considerably strengthen the paper.
4. **Inconsistent Presentation & Minor Formatting Issues:** The paper contains some minor inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., table headings, equation numbering). More importantly, the presentation of results is occasionally unclear. For instance, the explanation of Table 5 is somewhat convoluted.  The figures could be improved for clarity. The inclusion of tables and figures is appreciated, but many are difficult to read due to the small font size.
5. **Underexplored Rule Generalization:** The development and tuning of the head rules were conducted primarily on English. While the authors demonstrate reasonable performance across other languages, there's limited discussion about the extent to which these rules are truly language-independent or if they introduce biases. Further investigation of the rules’ transferability would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common error patterns and their causes? Specifically, what types of dependencies are most frequently mispredicted, and why?
2.  The paper states the PageRank personalization is crucial. Can you quantify this contribution more precisely? What is the performance difference with and without personalization *per language*?
3. How sensitive is the system to variations in the UD annotation guidelines? Have you tested the system on treebanks with different levels of annotation consistency?
4. You mention the system doesn't handle relative clauses well due to the lack of NOUN -> VERB rules.  Are there other significant syntactic phenomena that the current rule set struggles with, and what is the rationale for excluding rules that might address them?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation for the functions γ, κ, and δ is unclear. Please provide a more precise definition of these functions.
*   The URLs for the datasets should be active links in the final version of the paper.
*   A brief discussion of the computational cost of the PageRank algorithm would be helpful.
*   The discussion of the standard deviation in domain evaluation is promising, but could be more extensive.



Overall, this paper presents a solid and well-executed study of unsupervised dependency parsing within the UD framework. However, the lack of substantial novelty and the limited error analysis prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. While the results are impressive, the paper falls short of making a truly groundbreaking contribution. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a more in-depth error analysis and discussion of the limitations of the approach.
