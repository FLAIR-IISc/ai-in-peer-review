## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents UDP, a completely unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The system leverages the constraints inherent in the UD formalism (function words as leaves, single-rooted trees) and utilizes personalized PageRank to identify main predicates. It’s a rule-based approach designed to be robust across languages without requiring any training data. The authors evaluate on 32 languages, comparing against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, and demonstrate competitive performance with increased robustness to domain shifts.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of a surprisingly effective, truly unsupervised dependency parser:**  In an era dominated by data-hungry deep learning models, showing that a strong parsing result can be achieved *without training* is a valuable contribution.  This highlights the power of well-defined linguistic constraints.
2. **Practical application of UD constraints for parsing:**  The paper effectively translates the theoretical benefits of UD’s standardized formalism into a functioning parsing system. This reinforces the value of the UD project beyond annotation.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The empirical results demonstrate the system’s comparative stability across different text domains, a significant advantage over data-driven approaches prone to overfitting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** UDP achieves impressive results, frequently approaching and sometimes surpassing the performance of a supervised delexicalized transfer parser across a wide range of languages.  The comparison to a reasonable baseline is crucial and strengthens the claim of effectiveness. The comprehensive evaluation across 32 languages provides solid support for the generalizability of the approach.
2. **Clear and Well-Defined Methodology:** The paper meticulously details the algorithm, including the head direction estimation and the two-step decoding process. The use of personalized PageRank is well-motivated, and the explanation of the decoding algorithm is clear.  The availability of the code and data further enhances the reproducibility of the work.
3. **Thoughtful Error Analysis:**  The detailed analysis of results, including the breakdown by POS and the evaluation of domain robustness, provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.  The discussion around the impact of POS tagging accuracy is particularly insightful.
4. **Relevance to Low-Resource Languages:** The ability to perform well without training data makes UDP particularly valuable for languages with limited annotated resources, furthering the aims of cross-lingual NLP.
5. **Excellent connection to existing work:** The paper thoroughly contextualizes its work with related research in grammar induction, unsupervised parsing, and cross-lingual transfer learning, clearly distinguishing its approach and contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scalability and Complexity:** While the performance is promising, the system's reliance on explicit rules and the PageRank algorithm might limit its scalability to very long and complex sentences. The computational cost of running PageRank on longer sentences needs further investigation.  The O(n x |C|) decoding time could become a bottleneck.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Quality:** Although the system is more robust than the delexicalized transfer parser to POS errors, its performance is still directly tied to the accuracy of the POS tags. A more thorough investigation of the error propagation from POS tags to dependency parses, potentially with simulated POS errors, would be beneficial. The choice of TnT as the POS tagger may not be optimal for all languages.
3. **Simplifications and Limitations of the UD Formalism:** While leveraging UD is a strength, the paper acknowledges that not all treebanks perfectly adhere to UD guidelines. The system’s performance may be affected by inconsistencies in annotation. The simplification of not modelling coordination and relative clauses (due to the lack of NOUN->VERB dependency) limits the range of structures the system can handle.
4. **Lack of Comparison with Stronger Unsupervised Baselines:** While the comparison to the delexicalized parser is strong, it would be beneficial to compare UDP against more recent and sophisticated unsupervised parsing approaches (e.g., those leveraging neural networks or more advanced graph algorithms).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the computational complexity of the system as sentence length increases, specifically with respect to the PageRank calculation? Could this become a significant bottleneck for very long sentences?
2.  You mention the potential for extending the system to handle coordination and relative clauses. Could you elaborate on how you envision incorporating these structures within the existing framework, potentially requiring adjustments to the head rules or decoding algorithm?
3.  The choice of 5x weight for the main predicate in the PageRank personalization seems somewhat arbitrary.  What experiments were conducted to determine this value, and how sensitive is the performance to changes in this weighting?
4.  You state that the system is deterministic given a POS sequence. However, what happens if multiple words are equally "closest" in the decoding algorithm? Is there a tie-breaking mechanism, and if so, how does it affect the reproducibility of the results?
5. Considering the performance drop on POS-predicted data, would it be feasible to incorporate a small amount of target-language adaptation for the POS tagger to further improve performance?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The figures and tables are clear and informative.
*   The link to the code and data is greatly appreciated.
*   It would be interesting to see a more detailed error analysis, including examples of common parsing errors and the reasons behind them.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the ethical implications of using unsupervised methods, particularly in terms of potential biases in the head rules or POS tagging models.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. The authors have successfully demonstrated a surprisingly effective system that leverages the power of linguistic constraints and offers a compelling alternative to data-driven approaches. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions and suggestions raised above.
