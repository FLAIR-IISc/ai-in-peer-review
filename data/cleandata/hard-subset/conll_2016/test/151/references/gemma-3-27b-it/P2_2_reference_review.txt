## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents UDP, a completely unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies solely on UD’s constraints and personalized PageRank. It avoids training by leveraging POS tags and a set of head-dependent rules, and dynamically estimates adposition direction. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages, comparing its performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, and analyze its robustness to domain shift.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A surprisingly effective and robust unsupervised parsing approach:** The core achievement is demonstrating that a reasonably accurate dependency parser *can* be built without any training data, solely by exploiting the constraints of the UD framework. This is a significant result, particularly given the historical difficulty of unsupervised parsing.
2. **Comprehensive multi-lingual evaluation:** The authors evaluate on a substantial number of languages (32), providing a robust assessment of the system’s cross-lingual applicability.  This is a strength, as many unsupervised methods are heavily language-dependent.
3. **Analysis of robustness to domain shift:** Evaluating performance across different domains adds valuable insight into the practical applicability of the system.  The finding that UDP is *more* robust to domain shift than a supervised baseline is noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance for an Unsupervised System:** The UAS scores achieved by UDP, particularly when using gold POS tags, are remarkably competitive with supervised transfer learning approaches. The consistent outperformance of the baseline suggests the method isn’t merely relying on chance. The results demonstrate a substantial advance in unsupervised parsing.
2. **Effective Exploitation of UD Constraints:** The paper clearly articulates and effectively utilizes the core constraints of UD (function words as leaves, single root, etc.). This is a key distinguishing factor from prior work and contributes significantly to the system’s success.
3. **Robustness and Generalizability:** The consistently good performance across many languages and across domains highlights the system's robustness and generalizability. The reduced sensitivity to domain shift is a particularly valuable finding.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The methodology is explained in detail, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The inclusion of an example parse trace (Section 4) is helpful for understanding the algorithm.
5. **Availability of Resources:** The authors make the parser and associated datasets available, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is effective, many individual components (PageRank, head-dependent rules) are not novel in themselves. The core innovation lies in the skillful orchestration of these components within the UD framework. This doesn't negate the contribution, but impacts the "breakthrough" potential.
2. **Dependence on POS Tag Accuracy:** Performance drops significantly with predicted POS tags. While still competitive with the supervised baseline in this scenario, the strong reliance on accurate POS tagging limits the practical applicability in truly low-resource settings where POS taggers are weak. More investigation into POS-tagging robustness is needed.
3. **Simplifications in Rule Application:** The rules in Table 1, while effective, seem somewhat ad-hoc and potentially incomplete. A more systematic and linguistically motivated derivation of these rules could further improve performance. The explanation for excluding the NOUN -> VERB rule feels a bit under-justified.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** Focusing solely on UAS might not fully capture the quality of the generated trees.  Considering metrics like dependency relation F1-score, or even qualitative analysis of parse errors, could provide a more nuanced evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **POS Tagging Robustness:** Could you elaborate on the error analysis regarding POS tagging errors? What types of POS errors have the biggest impact on parsing accuracy, and are there any strategies to mitigate these effects (e.g., rule-based correction of common POS errors)?
2. **Rule Derivation:** How were the head-dependent rules in Table 1 determined?  Was there a more systematic process beyond assessing on English development data?  Could you provide justification for the exclusion of the NOUN -> VERB rule?
3. **Error Analysis:**  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of parsing errors made by the system?  For instance, are there specific dependency relations that are consistently mispredicted?
4. **Computational Cost:** While you mention PR is the most computationally expensive step, can you provide more concrete numbers regarding parsing time for a sentence of a given length?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The inclusion of more details on the implementation (e.g., programming language, libraries used) would enhance reproducibility.
*   Exploring the use of more sophisticated personalization strategies for PageRank (beyond weighting the root verb) could be a promising avenue for future work.
*   The comparison to a state-of-the-art multilingual parser (like ParNLG) might be useful in placing the work within the broader landscape of multilingual parsing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a significant contribution to unsupervised parsing. The system is remarkably effective, robust, and well-evaluated.  While the individual components aren't entirely novel, the combination and skillful application within the UD framework are commendable.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
