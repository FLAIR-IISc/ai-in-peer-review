This paper presents a surprisingly strong unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) that requires no training data and relies solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints. The core idea – leveraging the structure enforced by UD to guide parsing – is compelling and effectively executed. The results demonstrate competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer parser and remarkable robustness to domain shift.

**Contributions:**

1. **A fully unsupervised, competitive dependency parser:** The primary contribution is the UDP system itself, which achieves respectable UAS scores (averaging around 64% on the test sets) *without* any training. This is a significant accomplishment, particularly given the complexity of dependency parsing.
2. **Effective exploitation of UD constraints:** The paper successfully demonstrates how the constraints inherent in the UD formalism can be leveraged for parsing. Specifically, the incorporation of function word restrictions and personalized PageRank guided by POS tags significantly boosts performance.
3. **Robustness to domain shift:**  The UDP system exhibits greater resilience to domain changes compared to a supervised transfer baseline. This is a valuable characteristic, especially for low-resource scenarios where domain-specific training data may be unavailable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of approach:**  While rule-aided unsupervised parsing exists, the commitment to a purely UD-driven, training-free approach is novel and well-motivated. The authors effectively argue that the UD formalism lends itself to this kind of parsing.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The parser consistently performs well across a diverse set of 32 languages. The comparison to a delexicalized transfer parser (MSD) provides a strong benchmark and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The performance is particularly impressive given the lack of training data.
3. **Detailed method description:** The paper provides a clear and thorough explanation of the parsing algorithm, including the PageRank setup, head direction estimation, and two-step decoding process.  The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful in visualizing the algorithm.
4. **Comprehensive evaluation:** The evaluation is well-designed, covering multiple languages, POS tag setups (gold vs. predicted), and a domain shift analysis.  The inclusion of in-house annotated datasets for English expands the domain coverage.
5. **Analysis of results:** The discussion section provides valuable insights into the performance of the parser, including the contribution of PageRank and a breakdown by POS tag. This demonstrates a good understanding of the system’s strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited handling of complex structures:** The parser’s reliance on simple head rules likely restricts its ability to capture complex syntactic structures, such as coordination or long-distance dependencies. This is hinted at in the discussion regarding nouns not being eligible heads for verbs.
2. **Dependence on POS tag quality:** Although the parser is more robust than MSD to POS tag errors, its performance still degrades significantly when using predicted POS tags.  This highlights a potential bottleneck for applications in low-resource languages with inaccurate taggers.
3. **Lack of error analysis:** While the paper provides some discussion of the system’s behavior, a more detailed error analysis (e.g., examining common types of parsing errors) would be beneficial. It could help identify areas for improvement.
4. **English-centric development:** The system development was primarily guided by the English treebank. While the authors demonstrate generalization to other languages, it is possible that the rules are biased towards English syntactic patterns. More systematic cross-lingual evaluation during development could mitigate this.
5. **Simplistic representation of adposition direction:** The use of bigram frequencies to determine adposition direction is a relatively simple approach and may not accurately capture the nuances of adposition usage in all languages.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential extensions of the parser to handle more complex structures, such as coordination or long-distance dependencies? What are the main challenges in incorporating these features into the existing framework?
2.  How sensitive is the parser to the choice of teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm?  Did you explore a wider range of values during development?
3.  The paper mentions that the head rules were chosen to maximize precision on the development set. Could you provide more details on this process? What criteria were used to evaluate precision, and how were the rules refined?
4.  Given the reliance on POS tags, what strategies could be employed to further mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors? Would integrating a POS tagger directly into the parsing pipeline be beneficial?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive and well-organized.
*   The tables are clearly presented and easy to understand.
*   The code and data should be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   The reference list is extensive and relevant.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written and insightful paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. The proposed UDP system is a promising approach for parsing low-resource languages and demonstrates the power of leveraging linguistic constraints. Despite some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and suggestions outlined above.
