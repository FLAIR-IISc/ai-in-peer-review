## Peer Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a completely unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages the constraints imposed by the UD formalism and utilizes personalized PageRank for improved predicate identification. The system eschews training data entirely, relying on a set of rules and heuristics to build dependency structures. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages, comparing its performance to a delexicalized transfer system, and assess its robustness to domain shift.

**Main Contributions (as perceived):**

1. **Demonstration of competitive unsupervised parsing leveraging UD constraints:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that a purely rule-based system, guided by UD’s inherent constraints, can achieve competitive performance against a supervised transfer learning baseline. This is a significant result as it challenges the reliance on large training datasets for cross-lingual parsing.
2. **Effective application of personalized PageRank for predicate identification:** The authors’ clever use of personalized PageRank, biasing towards the first verb or content word, demonstrably improves parsing accuracy, particularly in identifying the root of the dependency tree. This showcases a pragmatic application of a graph-based algorithm to a core NLP task.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The evaluation showing UDP’s greater stability across different domains compared to the delexicalized transfer parser is a valuable contribution. This highlights the potential for UDP as a reliable parser in low-resource settings or where domain adaptation is challenging.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves impressive performance across a broad range of languages *without any training data*.  The UAS scores are consistently competitive with, and in some cases exceed, the delexicalized transfer baseline, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach. The thorough evaluation across 32 languages is a major strength.
2. **Principled Approach:** The parser’s reliance on the UD constraints is a clear and logical design choice.  By explicitly encoding linguistic knowledge within the system, the authors avoid some of the pitfalls of purely data-driven approaches, such as learning spurious correlations.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The method is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The inclusion of a run example is particularly helpful for understanding the parsing process.
4. **Robustness Analysis:** The analysis of domain robustness is a crucial strength. It validates the claim that the parser is less susceptible to overfitting and performs consistently across diverse text types. The comparison with the delexicalized baseline in this regard is insightful.
5. **Openly Available Resources:** Providing the parser and the annotated test sets makes this work easily reproducible and builds upon the open-science spirit.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper reports performance, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the parser fails – specific linguistic phenomena, POS tagging errors, or languages – would significantly strengthen the work.  A qualitative analysis of misparses is needed.
2. **Head Direction Heuristics:**  The method for determining head direction (particularly for adpositions) seems somewhat simplistic and relies heavily on bigram frequencies calculated on-the-fly. This could be a source of error, and a more sophisticated approach (e.g., considering wider context or using a small, language-specific dictionary) might improve performance.
3. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** While the parser demonstrates resilience to POS tagging errors, performance still suffers significantly when using predicted POS tags. Addressing this dependency through integration of POS-tagging error correction or by making the parser more robust to tagging noise is an important area for future work.
4. **Potential Bias from English Development Data:** While authors claim not to tune for a specific language, the use of English development data for parameter tuning (PageRank, function word directionality) could introduce bias.  Ideally, this tuning process would be performed in a more language-agnostic manner.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common sources of parsing errors and the types of linguistic structures that the parser struggles with?  Can you categorize errors by POS tag or dependency relation?
2. **Head Direction Improvement:** Have you explored more sophisticated methods for determining head direction, particularly for adpositions? Would a small, language-specific dictionary improve performance?
3. **POS Tagging Robustness:** What steps could be taken to make the parser more robust to errors in POS tagging? Have you considered incorporating error correction techniques or adapting the parsing rules to accommodate common tagging mistakes?
4. **Development Data Bias:**  How sensitive are the tuned parameters (PageRank weighting, function word directionality) to the choice of development language?  Have you experimented with tuning on multiple languages to assess potential bias?
5. **Coordination & Multiword Expressions:** The paper does not address the handling of coordination or multiword expressions. How would these be incorporated into the parser, and what impact would they have on performance?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the UD formalism itself and how these limitations might affect the performance of the parser.
* The reference list is extensive but has some formatting inconsistencies (e.g., missing venue information).

Overall, this is a strong and innovative paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised parsing. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed through further experimentation and analysis. I recommend acceptance.
