This paper presents a novel unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) that leverages the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism to achieve competitive performance without relying on training data. The core idea is to use a rule-based approach, guided by PageRank for improved predicate identification and a mechanism to estimate adposition attachment direction directly from the test data.

The primary contribution is the development of a surprisingly effective, entirely unsupervised dependency parser for multiple languages adhering to the UD standard. While unsupervised parsing is not new, achieving performance comparable to supervised transfer learning, as demonstrated in the results, is significant. The secondary contribution lies in the pragmatic application of UD constraints – specifically the function word leaf restriction – to simplify the parsing process and improve accuracy. Finally, the comprehensive evaluation across 32 languages and varying POS tag reliability provides a valuable resource for the research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser demonstrates competitive performance compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, particularly impressive given its unsupervised nature. The UAS scores reported in Table 5 are compelling, especially the consistent performance across numerous languages.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of UD-compliant rules, personalized PageRank, and on-the-fly adposition direction estimation represents a novel approach to unsupervised parsing. It moves beyond simply adapting existing techniques to explicitly exploit the structure offered by UD.
3. **Robustness & Generalizability:** The system exhibits robustness to domain changes, outperforming the delexicalized transfer parser in many domain-specific evaluations (Table 6). This suggests the parser captures general linguistic principles well and isn’t overly sensitive to training data biases.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The extensive evaluation across a large number of languages (32) and under different conditions (gold vs. predicted POS tags) lends strong credibility to the findings. The inclusion of cross-domain evaluations strengthens the practical relevance of the work.
5. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the method and experimental setup. The algorithm description (Fig. 1) is particularly helpful, and the example run (Section 4) aids understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the discussion section touches upon error sources (e.g., difficulties with relative clauses, punctuation), a more detailed and systematic error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying specific patterns of errors could guide future improvements.
2. **Heuristic Nature of Rules:** The head direction rules (Table 1) are somewhat arbitrary and rely on development-set optimization. It’s unclear how well these rules generalize to languages not explicitly considered during development. While the results show good generalization, a justification for the specific rule choices would strengthen the work.
3. **Dependence on POS Tag Quality:** Although the parser is more robust to POS errors than the supervised baseline, performance still degrades significantly with predicted POS tags (Table 5). While unavoidable, exploring techniques to mitigate the impact of noisy POS tags further could be valuable.
4. **PageRank Personalization Justification:** The choice of multiplying the main predicate’s weight by 5 in the PageRank algorithm feels somewhat arbitrary. While the ablation study shows its benefit, a more principled justification for this value would be stronger.
5. **Lack of Comparison with More Recent Unsupervised Methods:** The comparison to Klein & Manning (2004) feels dated. A comparison with more recent, state-of-the-art unsupervised parsing methods would better position the contribution of this work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the most frequent types of parsing errors made by your system?
2. What is the rationale behind the choice of multiplying the main predicate’s weight by 5 in the PageRank algorithm? Were other values considered?
3. How sensitive is the system's performance to the choice of the teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm?
4. Have you considered incorporating techniques to explicitly handle multiword expressions or coordination structures? If so, what were the challenges?
