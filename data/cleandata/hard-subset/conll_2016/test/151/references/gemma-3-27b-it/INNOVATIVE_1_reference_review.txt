This paper presents UDP, a remarkably bold attempt to build a dependency parser *without training*. It leverages the constraints inherent in Universal Dependencies to create a rule-based system enhanced with personalized PageRank. The primary contribution is the demonstration that a surprisingly effective parser can be constructed purely from linguistic constraints and a clever application of a graph ranking algorithm, sidestepping the need for labeled data or complex transfer learning. A secondary, but still significant, contribution lies in the thorough evaluation across a large number of languages (32), showcasing the system’s robustness and surprisingly consistent performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Leap:** The core idea—a fully unsupervised parser guided by UD constraints—is genuinely novel. Most work in cross-lingual parsing relies on some form of supervision or transfer learning. This work breaks that mold and offers a compelling alternative. It challenges the assumption that data is *always* necessary for achieving reasonable parsing accuracy.
2. **Impressive Cross-Lingual Performance:** Achieving competitive results across 32 languages *without training* is a substantial accomplishment. The UAS scores, particularly when compared to a delexicalized transfer system, are encouraging and demonstrate the power of the UD framework. The fact that UDP often outperforms the supervised baseline in several languages, especially when considering domain robustness, is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The demonstration of greater stability across different domains is a strong point. The lower standard deviation of UDP’s performance across domains suggests a more generalized approach, less prone to overfitting to specific datasets—a common problem with data-driven methods. This speaks to the strength of relying on linguistic principles.
4. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The inclusion of a detailed example run (Section 4) greatly aids understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper presents results, the error analysis is somewhat superficial. A more detailed investigation into *where* the parser fails—specific linguistic phenomena or languages—would be invaluable. Understanding the limitations is crucial for future development.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** Although the paper highlights robustness to POS errors, the performance still fundamentally relies on accurate POS tags. The results using predicted POS tags, while reasonable, show a significant drop in performance. Exploring ways to mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors more effectively would strengthen the work.
3. **Rule Set Subjectivity & Lack of Formal Justification:** The head rules, while based on UD constraints, were tuned on English data. The paper doesn’t fully articulate why *these* specific rules are optimal or how they generalize across languages. A more theoretically grounded justification for the rule set would be beneficial. While it’s acknowledged this is an iterative process, the rationale could be stronger.
4. **Simplified Decoding Algorithm:** The two-step decoding algorithm, while conceptually clear, might be overly simplistic. The potential for non-projectivity and the lack of consideration for more complex linguistic structures are potential limitations.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common error patterns and the languages where the parser struggles most?
2. What modifications to the algorithm would be required to handle non-projective dependencies more effectively?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to different personalization weights in the PageRank algorithm? Was a more extensive search performed beyond the reported tuning?
4.  Are there any plans to incorporate more sophisticated techniques for handling languages with significantly different word order or grammatical structures?



**Additional Comments:**

* The notation in Figure 1 could be improved for clarity (e.g., defining 'H' and 'D' before use).
* The comparison to the DMV model is somewhat dated. Including more recent unsupervised or weakly supervised parsing methods would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
*  The availability of the code and datasets is excellent and highly appreciated.



Overall, this is a compelling and innovative paper. While some aspects could be refined, the core contribution—a functional dependency parser without training—is highly significant. The boldness of the approach and the promising results outweigh the identified weaknesses, and I recommend **acceptance**.
