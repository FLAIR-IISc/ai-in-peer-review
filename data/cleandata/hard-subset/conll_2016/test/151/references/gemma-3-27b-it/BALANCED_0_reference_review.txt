This paper presents a surprisingly strong unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies (UD), achieving competitive results across 32 languages without any training data. The core idea – leveraging UD’s constraints and personalized PageRank – is well-executed and demonstrably effective. The thorough experimental evaluation, including a compelling analysis of domain robustness, is a major strength.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A competitive unsupervised parser for UD:** The UDP system achieves UAS scores comparable to supervised, delexicalized transfer parsers, marking a significant step forward for unsupervised parsing. This is a substantial achievement given the inherent difficulties of the task.
2. **Effective exploitation of UD constraints:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the value of building a parser *around* the constraints imposed by UD (function word leaf status, etc.), rather than attempting to learn structure from scratch. This is a key insight and differentiates this work from many previous approaches to unsupervised parsing.
3. **Robustness to domain shift:** The experimental results demonstrate the UDP’s stability across different domains, a critical advantage over data-driven methods that can easily overfit to specific datasets. The detailed domain analysis provides valuable insights.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The performance of UDP, as demonstrated across a wide range of languages, is impressive. The consistent outperformance of the baseline and competitive scores compared to a delexicalized transfer parser provide strong evidence of the method’s efficacy.
2. **Clear and well-motivated approach:** The paper is easy to follow and the rationale behind each design decision (e.g., using personalized PageRank, incorporating UD constraints) is clearly explained. This makes the work accessible and understandable.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation:** The authors go beyond standard UAS metrics to assess the system’s robustness to varying POS tag quality and domain changes. The inclusion of in-house annotated datasets for English is a nice addition. The detailed analysis of results, including error analysis, is commendable.
4. **Practical significance:** The parser’s lack of training data requirement makes it especially valuable for low-resource languages, where annotated data is scarce.
5. **Thorough related work:** The paper presents a strong overview of existing work in grammar induction, unsupervised parsing, and cross-lingual transfer learning, placing the current work in context.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited error analysis:** While the paper presents a discussion of POS breakdown and domain analysis, a more detailed error analysis examining specific types of parsing errors would be valuable. For example, an analysis of common incorrect head choices or dependency relations could offer insights for future improvements.
2. **Sensitivity to POS tagger quality:** While the system is shown to be *more* robust than the delexicalized baseline, its performance still degrades significantly with noisy POS tags. Investigating techniques to further mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors (e.g., incorporating POS tag confidence scores) could be beneficial.
3. **Simplification regarding complex linguistic phenomena:** The parser’s simplicity comes at a cost; it struggles with phenomena that require more sophisticated linguistic knowledge, such as coordination or long-distance dependencies. While this is inherent to unsupervised approaches, acknowledging this limitation and suggesting potential avenues for addressing it would strengthen the paper.
4. **Head Direction Heuristic**: The rule for determining head direction (left or right attachment) for certain function words is based on English development data. While it works reasonably well, it’s unclear how representative English is of other languages. A more adaptive or language-specific approach to head direction could improve performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on common types of parsing errors and their potential causes?
2. Have you explored methods for incorporating POS tag confidence scores into the parsing process to mitigate the impact of noisy POS tags?
3. The paper mentions the limitations related to complex linguistic phenomena (coordination, long-distance dependencies). Are there any specific techniques or extensions you envision for addressing these limitations within the UDP framework?
4. How sensitive are the results to the choice of teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm? Was a more extensive parameter search conducted?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation in Figure 1 could be slightly improved for clarity.
* Table 5 could benefit from highlighting the languages where UDP significantly outperforms the baseline.
* The availability of the parser and datasets is greatly appreciated and enhances the reproducibility of the research.



Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. The proposed system is effective, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
