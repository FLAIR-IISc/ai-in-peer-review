This paper presents a surprisingly effective, entirely unsupervised dependency parser leveraging the constraints inherent in Universal Dependencies (UD). The core idea – relying solely on POS tags and UD’s structural restrictions (function words as leaves, single root) combined with a personalized PageRank for predicate identification – is genuinely novel and impressive. The paper demonstrates solid performance across a large number of languages (32), outperforming a strong delexicalized transfer baseline in many cases and exhibiting remarkable robustness to domain shift.

The primary contribution is the demonstration that a high-quality dependency parse can be achieved *without any training data*, simply by intelligently exploiting the constraints of a well-defined linguistic formalism like UD. A secondary, yet important contribution, is the practical personalization of PageRank using a simple heuristic for identifying the sentence’s main predicate, significantly improving parsing accuracy. Finally, the extensive evaluation across numerous languages and domains provides a compelling validation of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Radical Novelty:** The complete absence of training data is striking. While rule-based parsing exists, the combination with PageRank and UD-specific constraints, achieving competitive performance, is a significant departure from current methods. This is a bold approach, and the results justify the risk.
2. **Cross-Lingual Generalization:** Performance across 32 languages is a considerable achievement. This showcases the power of leveraging universal linguistic principles (as embodied in UD) over language-specific statistical models. The consistent performance across a diverse set of languages is compelling evidence of the method’s generality.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The results on domain adaptation are particularly strong. The observed stability of the parser across different text genres, compared to the supervised baseline, suggests a fundamental advantage in its ability to generalize beyond the data it was implicitly "designed" for (i.e. the UD guidelines).
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The method is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The inclusion of a concrete example (Section 4) is helpful for understanding the parsing process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper mentions some limitations (e.g., handling relative clauses), a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying systematic errors (beyond POS tagging inaccuracies) would help pinpoint areas for future improvement. For example, analyzing failures in languages with non-canonical word order would be insightful.
2. **Heuristic Nature of Predicate Identification:** The heuristic for identifying the main predicate (first verb or content word) is simple and effective, but its limitations are not fully explored. It’s not clear how this heuristic performs in sentences with multiple verbs or complex clause structures. Exploring alternative, more sophisticated methods for predicate identification could further improve performance.
3. **Dependency on POS Tag Quality:** Although the parser demonstrates robustness *relative* to the supervised baseline when POS tags are imperfect, the overall performance is still heavily dependent on the accuracy of the POS tags. The impact of POS tagging errors could be more thoroughly investigated. A targeted analysis of POS-tagging error types and their effects on parsing accuracy would be valuable.
4. **Lack of Comparison to other Unsupervised Methods:** The comparison is mainly against a supervised transfer baseline, which is good. However, a comparison to *other* unsupervised methods (even those not explicitly UD-compliant) would strengthen the paper’s claim of novelty and contribution.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the most common types of parsing errors and their potential causes?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the parser to different methods of identifying the main predicate? Have you explored any alternative heuristics or techniques?
3. Is there a clear correlation between POS tagging errors and parsing errors? Can you quantify the impact of specific POS tagging error types on parsing accuracy?
4. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the PageRank algorithm, particularly for longer sentences? Is there potential for optimization?



**Additional Comments:**

The authors mention excluding Japanese due to the lack of word forms in the treebank. It might be worth briefly discussing whether the approach could be adapted for languages with different annotation conventions (e.g., morphological tagging instead of POS tagging).

Overall, this is a highly promising paper with a genuinely novel and impactful contribution. I recommend acceptance, despite the minor weaknesses. The boldness of the approach and the impressive results outweigh the need for extensive validation and refinement at this stage.
