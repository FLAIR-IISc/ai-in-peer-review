## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a completely unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages the constraints imposed by the UD formalism (specifically, function words being leaves) and utilizes personalized PageRank for improved predicate identification. The system operates without any training data, relying on a set of pre-defined dependency rules and a dynamically estimated adposition direction. The authors evaluate UDP across 32 languages, comparing its performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser and assessing its robustness to domain shifts.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of a surprisingly effective, truly unsupervised parser:** Achieving competitive performance with a delexicalized transfer parser *without any training* is a significant accomplishment. This showcases the power of exploiting linguistic constraints inherent in formalisms like UD.
2. **Systematic integration of UD constraints into parsing:** The authors don't just acknowledge UD's constraints; they build a parser fundamentally *around* them. This is a strong argument for formalism-aware NLP.
3. **Robustness to domain shift:** The results showing UDP's greater stability across domains compared to the supervised baseline are valuable and highlight the benefits of a less data-dependent approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The UAS scores across a large number of languages (32!) are compelling. Consistently achieving performance close to a well-established delexicalized transfer parser, without training, is impressive. The detailed tables in the appendix are helpful.
2. **Clear and well-motivated approach:** The paper clearly articulates the rationale behind the design choices, linking them directly to the principles of UD and the limitations of existing approaches. The explanations of the PageRank personalization and head direction estimation are particularly well done.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation:** The evaluation setup is thorough, encompassing different POS tagging scenarios (gold, predicted, naive) and a domain shift analysis. This demonstrates a strong commitment to rigorous assessment.
4. **Practical implications:** A parser requiring no training data is extremely valuable for low-resource languages or situations where labeled data is unavailable. This makes the work highly relevant to a broad range of NLP applications.
5. **Well-written and accessible:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the specific details of dependency parsing or UD. The use of figures (e.g., Fig. 1) helps to illustrate the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components (PageRank, head rules) are not entirely novel. The core innovation lies in the specific application and integration of these techniques within the UD framework, but this might be considered incremental.  The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the related work, acknowledging the existing literature on each component.
2. **Dependency on POS tagging:**  The parser’s performance, even with the claimed robustness, is still fundamentally tied to the quality of POS tags. The results on predicted POS tags, while better than the baseline, show a substantial drop in performance.  The paper could explore strategies to make the system more resilient to POS tagging errors, perhaps through more sophisticated error handling or integration of POS tag confidence scores.
3. **Simplifications in the head direction estimation:** The simple bigram frequency approach for adposition direction estimation seems somewhat rudimentary. It's unclear how well this generalizes to languages with more complex adpositional systems. A more sophisticated approach, potentially incorporating contextual information, could be explored.
4. **Lack of error analysis:** While the paper presents quantitative results, a more detailed qualitative error analysis would be helpful.  Identifying common error patterns could provide insights into the limitations of the system and guide future improvements. What types of dependencies are frequently misparsed? Why?
5. **Limited discussion of the impact of UD formalism:** The paper argues that UD's formalism lends itself to rule-based parsing. However, it doesn't deeply explore *which* aspects of UD are most beneficial, or if certain UD features hinder the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, perhaps focusing on specific languages or dependency relations where the parser struggles?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the system to the choice of the teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm? Have you explored other methods for setting this parameter?
3.  You mention the potential for expanding the system to handle multiword expressions and coordination. Could you elaborate on how you envision addressing these challenges?
4.  The adposition direction estimation relies on bigram frequencies. How does this perform in languages where adpositions are more distant from the nouns they modify?
5.  What are the computational costs of the UDP system compared to the delexicalized transfer parser, particularly for long sentences?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the UD formalism itself. Are there any features of UD that make unsupervised parsing more difficult?
*   The link to the code and datasets should be prominently displayed in the paper.
*   The notation in Figure 1 could be made more consistent and readable.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. While the individual components may not be entirely novel, the thoughtful integration of these techniques within the UD framework, combined with the comprehensive evaluation, makes this work highly worthwhile. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
