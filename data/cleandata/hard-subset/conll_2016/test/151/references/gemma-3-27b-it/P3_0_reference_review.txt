## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) that leverages the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The system eschews training data, relying instead on a set of dependency head rules, personalized PageRank for content word ranking, and a two-step decoding process that treats content and function words differently. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages, comparing its performance to a delexicalized transfer system and assessing its robustness to domain shift.  The results demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in scenarios with limited POS tag accuracy.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of a surprisingly effective zero-shot dependency parser:**  The core achievement is showing that a reasonably accurate dependency parse can be achieved *without any training data*, solely by exploiting the constraints of the UD framework. This is a significant result, particularly given the challenges of unsupervised parsing.
2. **Effective combination of linguistic constraints and graph-based ranking:** The successful integration of UD-specific head rules with personalized PageRank represents a practical approach to leveraging linguistic knowledge in an unsupervised setting. The personalization step, focused on identifying the main predicate, is a clever enhancement.
3. **Robustness to POS tag quality and domain shift:**  The evaluation convincingly shows that UDP’s performance degrades less rapidly than a supervised baseline when POS tag quality is reduced, and demonstrates stability across different text domains, highlighting the benefits of a rule-based approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The parser achieves competitive UAS scores across a broad range of languages, consistently outperforming or matching the delexicalized transfer baseline, especially in low-resource settings (predicted POS).  The breadth of the evaluation (32 languages) is a major strength.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:**  The paper clearly articulates the motivation for the work – the limitations of data-driven methods and the potential of the UD framework. The methodology is well-explained and the design choices (e.g., two-step decoding, personalization) are justified.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments go beyond standard UAS metrics, including evaluations under different POS tagging scenarios and domain adaptation tests. This provides a more nuanced understanding of the system’s capabilities and limitations.  The inclusion of domain shifts for multiple languages is particularly valuable.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors state that the parser and data are publicly available, which is crucial for verification and further research.
5. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The discussion section provides a detailed breakdown of performance by POS and domain, offering valuable insights into the system’s strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is effective, the individual components (PageRank, head rules) are well-established in unsupervised parsing. The novelty lies primarily in the application of these techniques within the specific context of UD and the clever personalization strategy. This should be acknowledged more explicitly.
2. **Reliance on English for Parameter Tuning:** The reliance on English development data for tuning PageRank parameters and function word directionality introduces a potential bias. While the results suggest the rules generalize well, a more systematic investigation of language-specific parameter settings could be beneficial. The authors should justify *why* English is a reasonable proxy for parameter tuning.
3. **Simplified POS Representation for Robustness Test:**  The “naive type-constrained two-POS tag scenario” (CONTENT/FUNCTION) while illustrating robustness to *severe* POS error, feels a bit contrived and doesn't reflect realistic POS tagging errors. A more realistic degradation experiment, using different levels of POS tag accuracy, would be more informative.
4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Unsupervised Parsers:** The comparison is primarily against a delexicalized transfer parser. A comparison to more recent and sophisticated unsupervised parsing methods, even if not directly comparable due to the zero-shot constraint, would help position the work within the broader landscape of unsupervised parsing research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why English was chosen as the development language for parameter tuning, and have you experimented with language-specific tuning? If not, what are the potential risks and limitations associated with using English as a proxy?
2.  The paper mentions potential issues with noun-verb headship due to relative clauses. Have you considered incorporating any mechanisms to address this limitation, even if it involves a slight relaxation of the strict rule-based approach?
3.  The content/function POS scenario is useful, but somewhat artificial. Could you provide results using POS tags generated with different levels of accuracy (e.g., varying the training data size for TnT) to provide a more realistic assessment of the parser’s robustness to POS errors?
4.  You note that the PUNCT POS consistently performs poorly. Is this a result of annotation inconsistencies across treebanks, or does the rule-based approach struggle with punctuation in general?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, though the table labels could be improved for clarity (e.g., specifying the metric used).
*   The paper is well-written and easy to understand, even for readers unfamiliar with the details of unsupervised parsing.
* The references are comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a significant contribution to unsupervised dependency parsing. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the evaluation is thorough. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and application within the UD framework are innovative and effective. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
