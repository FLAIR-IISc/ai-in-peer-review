This paper presents a completely unsupervised dependency parser, UDP, leveraging the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework and employing personalized PageRank for improved predicate identification. The authors claim contributions in adapting dependency head rules to UD, incorporating function word restrictions, utilizing personalized PageRank, and achieving language independence through on-the-fly adposition direction estimation.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a demonstration of how rigorously exploiting the constraints *within* a standardized dependency framework (UD) can yield surprisingly competitive parsing performance *without* any training data. The approach is conceptually simple, relying on a set of predefined rules and a graph-based ranking algorithm. The empirical evaluation across a substantial number of languages (32) is commendable. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough evaluation, including comparisons to a delexicalized transfer parser and a robustness analysis across different domains. The fact that the system demonstrates a degree of resilience to domain shift is a notable finding, suggesting a potential advantage over data-driven approaches in low-resource scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages is a significant undertaking and provides a compelling demonstration of the system’s generalizability. The inclusion of comparisons to a strong baseline (delexicalized transfer parser) and the domain adaptation experiments strengthens the validity of the results.
2. **Principled Approach:** The reliance on UD constraints is a sensible strategy. By grounding the parsing process in a well-defined formalism, the authors sidestep many of the challenges associated with purely data-driven approaches, particularly in low-resource settings.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The observation that the UDP system is more stable to domain changes than the delexicalized transfer parser is a key finding. This suggests that the system’s reliance on linguistic principles, rather than statistical patterns learned from specific datasets, makes it more adaptable.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The inclusion of an example run (Section 4) is particularly helpful for understanding the parsing process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of techniques is reasonable, the individual components (rule-based parsing, PageRank) are well-established. The novelty lies primarily in the *application* of these techniques within the UD framework, rather than in the development of new algorithms. The claim of achieving “nearly as well as a delexicalized transfer parser” needs to be qualified. While competitive, the performance difference is not negligible, and the delexicalized parser often outperforms UDP.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:**  The paper doesn’t fully dissect the contribution of each component. While Section 6.1 attempts to isolate the effect of PageRank, a more comprehensive ablation study (e.g., removing the function word restrictions, evaluating different PageRank personalization strategies) would be beneficial for understanding the relative importance of each design choice.  Simply stating that personalization improves convergence speed is insufficient. How much does it improve UAS?
3. **Over-Reliance on English for Development:** Tuning PageRank parameters and determining function word attachment direction on English data is a potential source of bias.  While the authors acknowledge this, it’s unclear how sensitive the system is to variations in these parameters across different languages.  More extensive cross-lingual development is needed to ensure true language independence.
4. **Simplistic POS Handling:** While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the two-POS tag scenario, the performance drop is substantial. The reliance on frequency-based POS assignment in this scenario highlights a vulnerability of the system – its sensitivity to POS tagging errors. The claim of being more resilient than MSD to POS errors seems overstated given the performance differences observed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, systematically evaluating the contribution of each component (head rules, PageRank personalization, function word restrictions) to the overall performance?
2. How sensitive are the PageRank parameters and function word attachment direction estimates to variations across different languages? Did you perform any cross-lingual development to optimize these parameters?
3. The paper states the system is deterministic given a POS sequence. However, the described tie-breaking rules (closest head) seem potentially non-deterministic. Can you clarify this point and, if applicable, describe how ties are resolved?
4. You mention the limitations of the POS tag scenario in section 6.3. Would you agree that the performance decrease in this scenario suggests a significant weakness in the parser, and that improved POS tagging is critical for UDP’s effectiveness?

**Additional Comments:**

* The table formatting is somewhat inconsistent.
*  The reference list is formatted oddly; standardize it.
* The phrase "leaf status of function words" is used repeatedly; consider rephrasing for clarity.
