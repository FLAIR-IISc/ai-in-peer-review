## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

**Summary:** This paper presents a completely unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages UD’s constraints and a personalized PageRank algorithm. The system requires no training data and aims to be robust across languages and domains. The authors compare their system to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser and demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in cross-domain scenarios.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of a viable, competitive unsupervised parsing approach within the UD framework:** The paper convincingly shows that a simple, rule-based system, guided by UD principles, can achieve surprisingly good parsing results without any training. This is a significant achievement given the historical difficulty of unsupervised parsing.
2. **Practical application of UD constraints:** The work effectively demonstrates how the constraints inherent in the UD formalism (e.g., function words as leaves) can be exploited to build a parser, offering a strong argument for the benefits of standardized dependency representations.
3. **Robustness to domain shift:** The experimental results indicate that UDP exhibits higher stability to domain changes compared to a supervised transfer learning baseline. This robustness is a valuable contribution, particularly for low-resource languages or scenarios where labeled data is scarce.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores across 32 languages, consistently performing close to a strong delexicalized transfer baseline (and exceeding it in some cases). This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the approach.
2. **Thorough evaluation:** The authors evaluate the system under various conditions – gold vs. predicted POS tags and across different domains – providing a comprehensive assessment of its performance and limitations.  The inclusion of in-house annotated datasets for domain evaluation is particularly commendable.
3. **Clear and well-motivated design:** The approach is clearly explained, with a strong rationale for each design choice. The connection to UD principles and the justification for the personalization of PageRank are convincing.
4. **Reproducibility:**  The authors state that the parser and datasets are freely available, which significantly enhances the value and impact of the work.
5. **Insightful error analysis:** The detailed discussion of the breakdown by POS and the analysis of cross-domain consistency provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the system.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in individual components:** While the *combination* of techniques is novel and effective, many of the individual components (PageRank, rule-based parsing, delexicalization) have been explored previously in the literature.  The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty relative to existing work beyond simply stating the combination is new.
2. **Dependence on accurate POS tags:** While the system *works* with predicted POS tags, the performance drop is noticeable. This underscores a significant limitation: the parser’s performance is tightly coupled to the quality of the POS tagger, which could be a bottleneck in low-resource scenarios.  The authors acknowledge this, but more investigation into mitigating this dependence could strengthen the work.
3. **Simplistic handling of certain linguistic phenomena:** The parser struggles with phenomena like coordination and relative clauses due to the imposed rules. While a fully comprehensive parser is unrealistic without training, acknowledging these limitations more explicitly and discussing potential future directions (e.g., incorporating more sophisticated rules) would be beneficial.
4. **Presentation of results:** The results tables (e.g. Table 5 and 6) are dense and can be difficult to quickly parse. Adding some visual highlighting or summarizing the key findings in the text would improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the head rules were optimized on the English development set.  How sensitive are the results to the specific choice of the development language?  Did you experiment with other languages?
2.  The system performs surprisingly well even with predicted POS tags.  Could you elaborate on the characteristics of the POS taggers used for each language and how these characteristics might contribute to the observed performance? (e.g., Are they rule-based, statistical, or neural? Are they trained on similar data?)
3.  You acknowledge limitations with coordination and relative clauses. Are there specific rule modifications or extensions that you believe could address these issues without sacrificing the unsupervised nature of the parser?
4.  Can you elaborate on the choice of 0.05 as the teleport probability for PageRank? Was a more systematic exploration of different values conducted, and if so, what were the results?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.
*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The provided link to the datasets is helpful.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper.  While the individual components may not be groundbreaking, the combination of techniques, the strong empirical results, and the demonstration of a viable unsupervised parsing approach within the UD framework are significant contributions.  The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and provides valuable insights into the challenges and opportunities of unsupervised dependency parsing. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (especially regarding POS tag dependence and handling complex linguistic phenomena) could further strengthen the work.
