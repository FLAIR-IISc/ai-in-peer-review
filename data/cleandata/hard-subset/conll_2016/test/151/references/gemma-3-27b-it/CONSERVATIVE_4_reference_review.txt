This paper presents a surprisingly strong unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) leveraging the constraints imposed by the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The core idea—building a parser *without* training, relying solely on POS tags and UD’s structural constraints—is interesting, and the results are, frankly, quite impressive. The primary contribution is the demonstration that a competitive dependency parser can be constructed with minimal assumptions and no language-specific training data, relying instead on a carefully engineered set of rules and a personalized PageRank algorithm. A secondary, and valuable, contribution is the thorough evaluation across a large number of languages (32), demonstrating the robustness of the approach. Finally, the analysis of domain sensitivity is insightful and adds to the overall value of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance for an Unsupervised System:** The UDP system achieves UAS scores comparable to supervised transfer learning baselines, a remarkable feat for a completely unsupervised approach. The performance gap with the multi-source delexicalized parser is relatively small, especially considering the latter benefits from substantial training data. This strongly suggests the UD framework genuinely constrains dependency structure in a way that can be exploited effectively.
2. **Robustness and Generalizability:** The consistent performance across 32 languages suggests the system isn’t overly sensitive to language-specific quirks.  The results on the domain adaptation experiments reinforce this, demonstrating that UDP is less susceptible to domain shift than the supervised baseline. This robustness is a significant advantage for low-resource languages where obtaining large, in-domain training datasets is challenging.
3. **Sound Methodology and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, including the PageRank personalization and the two-step decoding process. The inclusion of a detailed example (Section 4) greatly aids understanding. The authors also make a concerted effort to justify design choices, such as the teleport probability in PageRank and the directionality rules for function words.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, including comparisons to a strong baseline and analysis under different POS tagging scenarios (gold, predicted, naive). The inclusion of domain adaptation experiments further strengthens the evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is interesting, the individual components (PageRank, rule-based parsing) are well-established. The key innovation lies in the specific adaptation of these components to the UD framework and the careful engineering of the rules. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently highlight *why* these specific rules are crucial, beyond stating they align with UD constraints. A more detailed analysis of the impact of each rule would be beneficial.
2. **Over-Reliance on English for Development:** While acknowledged, the heavy reliance on English for parameter tuning (PageRank, function word directionality) is a concern.  The claim that these settings generalize well to other languages needs stronger justification. The authors should present a more systematic evaluation of the sensitivity of the system to these parameters across a subset of diverse languages.
3. **Simplified POS Error Analysis:** The POS error analysis is somewhat superficial. While the authors demonstrate UDP is more robust to POS errors than the supervised baseline, they don't delve into *which* POS errors are most detrimental and *why*. A more granular error analysis, identifying specific POS tag confusions that significantly impact parsing accuracy, would provide valuable insights.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis of Parse Trees:** The paper lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis of the generated parse trees. What types of errors does UDP consistently make? Are there specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., coordination, relative clauses) that the system struggles with? Providing examples of incorrect parses and explaining the underlying reasons would greatly enhance the understanding of the system’s limitations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to quantify the contribution of each individual head rule in Table 1? Specifically, how much does removing a particular rule affect overall performance?
2.  The personalization of PageRank focuses on identifying the main predicate. Have you experimented with other personalization strategies, such as weighting content words based on their frequency or information content?
3.  You mention that the system struggles with relative clauses due to the lack of a NOUN -> VERB head rule. Have you considered incorporating this rule, potentially with a lower confidence score, to improve performance on such constructions?  What impact did adding this rule have during development?
4.  Can you provide examples of parse trees where UDP fails and explain the reason for the failure? This would help understand the limitations of the approach.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, though the code snippets could be formatted more consistently for readability.
*   It would be helpful to provide links to the code and the annotated datasets used for domain adaptation.
*   The choice of evaluating on UAS is reasonable, but exploring other metrics (e.g., labeled attachment score) might provide a more nuanced assessment of the system's performance.
