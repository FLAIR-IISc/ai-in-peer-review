This paper presents a completely unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies, eschewing training data entirely and relying solely on UD constraints and personalized PageRank. The core claim is that by rigorously adhering to UD’s structural properties, a competitive parser can be built without supervision. The authors identify four key contributions: adapting dependency head rules to UD, incorporating the UD function word constraint, using personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data.

I see the primary contribution as the demonstration of surprisingly strong performance for a genuinely unsupervised parser *within the constraints of the UD framework*. Achieving competitive results (compared to a delexicalized transfer parser) without any training data is a significant feat. The thorough exploration of PageRank personalization and its impact on predicate identification is a notable aspect. While the individual components aren't groundbreaking, their combination and application to the UD formalism yield a compelling result. A secondary contribution is the detailed empirical evaluation across a substantial number of languages (32), showcasing the system’s cross-linguistic robustness and providing a valuable benchmark for future unsupervised parsing research. The robustness to domain shift, highlighted through experiments with varying data domains, is also a valuable finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores compared to a strong baseline (delexicalized transfer parser) across a diverse set of languages. This is particularly impressive given the lack of training data. The results in Table 5 demonstrate consistent performance, indicating the method’s generalizability.
2. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper goes beyond standard UAS metrics and includes evaluations with predicted POS tags and across different data domains. This provides a more comprehensive assessment of the system's strengths and weaknesses and showcases its robustness. The domain adaptation results (Table 6) are particularly strong.
3. **Clear Explanation of Method:** The method is clearly described and well-motivated. The rationale behind the design choices (e.g., the two-step decoding algorithm, PageRank personalization) is clearly articulated, making the paper easy to follow. The example run in Section 4 is helpful for understanding the parsing process.
4. **UD Constraint Exploitation:** The explicit focus on exploiting UD constraints is a key strength. This demonstrates that leveraging linguistic formalism can lead to effective unsupervised learning. The careful consideration of function word attachment and adposition directionality exemplify this approach.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors state that the parser and datasets are freely available, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is effective, the individual components (PageRank, head rules) are not novel in themselves. The novelty lies in the specific application within the UD framework. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this work builds upon and differs from existing rule-aided unsupervised parsing methods.
2. **Sensitivity to POS Tagging Accuracy:** Although the system is more robust to POS tagging errors than the supervised baseline, performance still degrades significantly when using predicted POS tags (Table 5). This highlights the reliance on reasonably accurate POS tags, which may not always be available in low-resource settings.
3. **Over-reliance on English for Development:** The system's development and parameter tuning heavily rely on the English treebank. This raises concerns about potential bias and may limit its performance on languages significantly different from English. A more cross-linguistically informed development process would be beneficial.
4. **Simplistic Treatment of Certain Structures:** The paper acknowledges limitations in handling relative clauses (due to the lack of NOUN → VERB head rules). This and other simplifications in the rule set may limit the parser's ability to capture complex syntactic structures accurately.
5. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the paper provides some error analysis, a more detailed investigation of the types of errors made by the parser would be valuable. This could help identify areas for improvement and provide insights into the limitations of the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of UDP to other existing rule-aided unsupervised parsing methods, specifically highlighting the differences in how UD constraints are leveraged?
2. How sensitive is the system to the choice of teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm? Was any systematic exploration of this parameter conducted beyond the incremental adjustment described in Section 3.1?
3. Given the reliance on English for development, have you explored any techniques to mitigate potential bias towards English-specific linguistic features?
4. The paper mentions that the two-step decoding algorithm can lead to non-projectivity. What is the proportion of non-projective dependencies in the resulting parses? Does this affect the overall UAS score significantly?

**Additional Comments:**

* The sentence "However, without personalization, performance drops 2% on average" in Section 6.1 lacks context. Is this drop relative to UDPG or MSDG?
* Table 2 refers to "NOMINAL= {NOUN, PROPN, PRON}". It would be clearer to define this abbreviation earlier in the text.
* Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of the parser, especially the PageRank calculation.
