This paper presents a dependency parser that operates without training, relying solely on Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and a personalized PageRank algorithm. The core claim is that a rule-based system, carefully tailored to UD’s inherent structure, can achieve competitive performance against supervised transfer learning methods, while demonstrating greater robustness to domain shift.

I identify one primary contribution: a thorough exploration of how tightly constrained linguistic formalisms like UD can be leveraged to build a surprisingly effective, unsupervised parser. The systematic application of UD constraints – particularly the function word leaf restriction – combined with the PR-based content word ranking, is a reasonable and well-executed approach. The comprehensive evaluation across a large number of languages (32) is a secondary, but valuable, contribution, providing a broad testbed for the method. The demonstration of robustness to domain shift is also a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline & Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores, often approaching and sometimes exceeding, a multi-source delexicalized transfer system (MSD) – a standard baseline in cross-lingual parsing. This is significant, given the *absence* of training data.  The results, particularly the outperformance of MSD in Hindi, suggest the approach is not merely a lucky application of rules but genuinely captures some underlying linguistic principles.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The experimental results demonstrating greater stability across different domains are a significant strength.  The lower standard deviation in performance across domains for UDP compared to MSD strongly suggests that the rule-based nature of the system makes it less prone to overfitting to specific data characteristics. This aligns with prior work showing benefits of rule-based systems in this regard.
3. **Systematic Application of UD Constraints:** The paper meticulously exploits the constraints inherent in the UD formalism. This is not merely stating they *use* UD; they actively *design* the system around its principles (function word placement, dependency rules). This demonstrates a clear design philosophy and justifies the reliance on UD as more than just a data source.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating on 32 languages is a substantial undertaking. While individual language performance varies, the breadth of the evaluation provides strong evidence for the generalizability (and limitations) of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** The individual components – PageRank, rule-based parsing – are well-established techniques. The novelty lies in the *combination* and application to the UD framework. However, the authors haven't sufficiently demonstrated *why* this combination is particularly insightful or fundamentally different from prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing work, beyond the specific UD constraints.  The reliance on Søgaard's (2012b) PageRank method, with only “personalization” as a modification, feels incremental.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the paper touches on some error sources (e.g., punctuation), a more detailed error analysis would be highly beneficial. Understanding *where* the parser fails and *why* would provide valuable insights for future improvements. The claim that the system struggles with relative clauses due to the lack of a NOUN -> VERB rule is interesting but lacks supporting evidence from the error analysis.  Simply stating limitations isn't enough; evidence is needed.
3. **Overreliance on Gold POS Tags:**  The majority of results are reported with gold POS tags. While the authors acknowledge the impact of POS tagging errors, the performance drop with predicted POS is substantial. This raises concerns about the practical applicability of the parser in low-resource scenarios where high-quality taggers are unavailable.  The authors need to further address this, perhaps by exploring methods to make the system more robust to POS errors or by incorporating a rudimentary POS tagger into the pipeline.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While MSD is a reasonable baseline, comparing to more recent and powerful cross-lingual parsing methods (e.g., multilingual BERT-based parsers) would provide a more rigorous assessment of the parser’s performance.  It's possible that the advantages of UDP are diminished when compared to state-of-the-art models.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting specific error patterns and their linguistic causes?  Specifically, can you quantify the impact of errors related to relative clauses and other identified limitations?
2. How sensitive is the system to variations in the head direction rules (Table 1)?  Have you explored alternative rule sets or methods for automatically learning these rules from data?
3. The personalization of PageRank using the first verb as the root seems potentially limiting. Have you explored other methods for identifying the main predicate, such as using lexical cues or syntactic features?
4.  What is the computational cost of the PageRank step, and how does it scale with sentence length? Could this be a bottleneck for parsing very long sentences?
