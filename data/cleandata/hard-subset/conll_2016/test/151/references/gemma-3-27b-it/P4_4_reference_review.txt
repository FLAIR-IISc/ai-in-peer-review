This paper presents a completely unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD), relying solely on POS constraints and personalized PageRank. The core idea is compelling – leveraging the UD formalism to avoid training data altogether. The claimed contributions are: adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, incorporating the UD function word constraint, utilizing personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and achieving language-independence through on-the-fly adposition attachment direction estimation.

I see the most significant contribution as the demonstration of a surprisingly competitive, truly unsupervised parser across a large number of languages (32). Achieving results comparable to supervised transfer learning *without any training data* is noteworthy. The systematic exploitation of UD constraints, particularly the function word rule, appears to be key to this success. Secondly, the robust performance across different domains, outperforming a supervised baseline in terms of standard deviation, highlights the benefits of a rule-based approach less prone to overfitting. Finally, the comprehensive evaluation, including a detailed error analysis and breakdown by POS, adds to the credibility of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline & Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves respectable UAS scores across a large, diverse set of languages without relying on any language-specific training data. This is a significant accomplishment in unsupervised parsing, providing a strong baseline for future research. The consistent performance relative to a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) is particularly compelling.
2. **Effective Utilization of UD Constraints:** The paper convincingly demonstrates how the UD formalism can be exploited to guide parsing. The emphasis on function word constraints and the adaptation of dependency rules to UD-compliant POS relations are central to the parser’s success. This demonstrates a strong understanding of the UD framework and its potential for simplifying parsing.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The experimental results showcase a marked robustness to domain changes compared to the supervised MSD baseline, evidenced by a significantly lower standard deviation in UAS across different domains. This suggests that the rule-based nature of the parser offers an advantage in generalizing to unseen data.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, covering 32 languages, different POS setups (gold, predicted, naive), and domain variations. This extensive evaluation enhances the validity and generalizability of the findings. The detailed error analysis also provides valuable insights into the parser's strengths and weaknesses.
5. **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The availability of the parser and annotated test sets promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of techniques is effective, many individual components (e.g., PageRank, rule-based parsing) are not novel in themselves. The novelty lies in the specific application and combination within the UD framework, but the contribution of each component individually might not be groundbreaking.
2. **Reliance on English for Parameter Tuning:** The system relies on English for tuning PageRank parameters and function word directionality. While the authors demonstrate generalizability, this initial dependence on a specific language could introduce bias and potentially limit performance in languages significantly different from English.
3. **Handling of Complex Syntactic Phenomena:** The parser struggles with certain complex syntactic structures, such as coordination and relative clauses, as noted in the discussion. This limitation is expected for a purely rule-based system, but it's an area for future improvement.
4. **Sensitivity to POS Tagging Accuracy:** Although the parser is more robust to POS tagging errors than the supervised baseline, performance still degrades significantly when using predicted POS tags. This highlights the importance of accurate POS tagging for unsupervised parsing. The "naive" POS setup, while illustrating a lower bound, is overly simplistic and may not accurately reflect real-world scenarios.
5. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis on Specific Languages:** The error analysis is presented at a general level. A more detailed examination of errors for specific languages, particularly those with lower performance, would provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the specific head rules in Table 1? How were these rules selected and validated beyond the English development set?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm? Did you explore different values and analyze their impact on the results?
3.  Given the limitations in handling complex syntactic structures like coordination, do you have plans to incorporate mechanisms for addressing these phenomena in future work?
4.  The paper mentions that the system is deterministic for a given POS sequence. Could you provide examples of POS sequences where the parser performs poorly and explain why it struggles in those cases?
5.  How does the performance of the parser compare to other unsupervised parsing methods specifically designed for UD, if any exist?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of "L" and "R" to denote direction of backoff attachments in Table 5 is not immediately clear and could benefit from further explanation.
*   The reference to Table 2 in Section 4.1 seems to be a typo. It should probably refer to Table 1.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of the parser. While the paper mentions O(n×|C|) for decoding, the cost of PageRank is not explicitly stated.
