## Peer Review: Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training

**Summary:**

This paper presents a completely unsupervised dependency parser (UDP) for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies solely on POS constraints and does not require any training data. The system utilizes personalized PageRank to identify main predicates and dynamically estimates adposition direction on the test data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages, comparing its performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system, and analyze its robustness to domain change.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of a surprisingly strong unsupervised baseline:** The paper demonstrates that a relatively simple rule-based approach, leveraging UD constraints, can achieve competitive results in cross-lingual dependency parsing *without any training*. This is a significant result, establishing a new baseline for future research in low-resource parsing.
2. **Effective use of UD constraints:** The work shows how the inherent constraints of the UD framework (function words as dependents, etc.) can be powerfully exploited for parsing, leading to robust performance even in the absence of learned parameters.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:**  The evaluation demonstrates a higher degree of stability to domain shifts compared to a supervised transfer learning baseline. This suggests the method is less prone to overfitting to specific datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores across a diverse set of 32 languages, often outperforming the delexicalized transfer baseline, particularly in low-resource scenarios.  The results are well-presented and statistically significant (as indicated by the provided standard deviations).
2. **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The combination of personalized PageRank and the two-step decoding process, specifically tailored to UD’s constraints, is a clever approach.  The personalization of PageRank focusing on content words is particularly effective.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper goes beyond typical UAS metrics, analyzing performance across different POS tags and evaluating robustness to domain shift – a crucial aspect for real-world applicability.  The inclusion of both gold and predicted POS evaluations is particularly valuable.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The algorithm (Figure 1) and examples (Section 4) are helpful for understanding the approach.
5. **Practical Impact & Accessibility:**  The authors' commitment to making the parser and datasets freely available is commendable and will facilitate further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is insightful, the individual components (PageRank, rule-based parsing) are not novel in themselves.  The paper relies heavily on established methods. A clearer statement about the novelty would be helpful.
2. **Dependency on POS Tagging Accuracy:** Although the system shows better robustness to POS errors compared to the transfer baseline, performance is still significantly impacted by POS tagging quality. The reliance on accurate POS tags limits its applicability to truly low-resource languages where POS taggers are often unreliable.  Further investigation into mitigating this dependency is warranted.
3. **Simplifying Assumptions & Limitations:** The system's reliance on a fixed set of rules and its inability to handle certain linguistic phenomena (e.g., relative clauses as noted by the authors) are limitations.  A more detailed discussion of these limitations and potential avenues for addressing them would strengthen the paper.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While domain shift is analyzed, a more granular error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *which* dependency relations are most frequently misparsed, and *why*, could inform future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding the PageRank personalization:** You state that the first verb or content word is used for personalization. Did you experiment with other methods for identifying the main predicate (e.g., using frequency-based heuristics)?  If so, how did those compare?
2. **Addressing POS Tagging Dependency:** What steps could be taken to further reduce the parser’s reliance on accurate POS tags? Could techniques like self-training or bootstrapping be employed to refine the POS tags specifically for dependency parsing?
3. **Handling Complex Structures:** You acknowledge the limitation regarding relative clauses. Are there plans to extend the system to handle more complex syntactic structures? If so, what approaches are being considered?
4. **Error Analysis Details:** Could you provide more specific examples of parsing errors, categorized by POS tag or dependency relation type? This would help in understanding the system’s weaknesses.



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
* The in-house annotated datasets mentioned in the conclusion should be linked/made publicly available alongside the parser code.
* The notation used for functions (γ, δ, κ) could be clarified further within the text.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper. The work presents a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing, establishing a strong baseline and demonstrating the effectiveness of leveraging UD constraints. While the individual components are not entirely novel, their combination and the empirical results are compelling. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.
