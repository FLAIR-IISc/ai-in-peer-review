Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- A training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies on (i) a small set of POS-based head rules aligned with UD constraints, (ii) a two-step decoding procedure enforcing function-word leafness, and (iii) personalized PageRank (PR) to prioritize content-word attachment and root selection. The only language-specific signal is adposition attachment direction, estimated unsupervised from bigrams on the test set.
- A broad empirical study over 32 UD languages under three POS-tagging regimes (gold, supervised predicted with TnT, and a coarse content/function setup), plus a cross-domain analysis. The parser is competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer (MSD) and exhibits notably lower variance across domains.
- An ablation and error analysis that isolates the contributions of PR vs. decoding and examines POS-specific behavior and error propagation from POS to dependencies.

Strengths
- Clear, simple method grounded in UD constraints. The two-step decoding that first attaches content words and then function words explicitly encodes UD’s function-word-as-dependent bias. This design choice is linguistically motivated, implementation-light, and leads to well-formed trees with non-projectivities allowed. The approach requires no parallel data, no training, and no target-language supervision beyond POS tags (in the realistic setup), making it widely applicable.
- Strong empirical coverage and careful evaluation. Results span 32 languages and multiple domains, and include three POS regimes. The comparison against a competitive delexicalized multi-source baseline (TurboParser) is fair at the level of unlabeled attachment scores and shows UDPG within 6–7 UAS of MSDG on average, surpassing MSDG for Hindi, and degrading less when moving to predicted POS. The domain robustness study (including additional in-house annotated subsets) shows consistently lower standard deviation for UDP than MSD, supporting the claim of reduced domain sensitivity.
- Sensible ablations and diagnostics. The UDPNoPR variant and the rule-based baseline clarify where the gains come from: PR yields roughly +9 UAS over UDPNoPR on average; the decoding/rule machinery adds ~+4 UAS over the baseline. The POS-wise breakdown ties errors to known weak spots (coordination, punctuation) and highlights where simple directionality heuristics succeed (e.g., ADP UAS ~75%).
- Practicality and reproducibility. The approach is fast (graph construction plus PR per sentence) and has minimal hyperparameters (teleport prob., PR personalization factor), with code and datasets promised for release. It provides a robust baseline for UD parsing in low-resource or domain-mismatched settings.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior unsupervised/rule-aided work. Personalized PR for prioritizing content words and enforcing function-word leafness are sensible but incremental extensions of Søgaard (2012b). The core technique is a specific instantiation of known ideas (PR-based salience, UD constraints, head rules); the paper’s main contribution is empirical rather than methodological.
- Reliance on POS tags and supervised tagging in “predicted” setup. The strongest realistic results (UDPP) hinge on TnT trained on target-language training data, which presupposes labeled POS data and thus weakens the “no training” claim in practical scenarios. The coarse CF setup is too crude to be a convincing lower bound, and it underplays what modern cross-lingual POS taggers could deliver; this may distort the comparative picture.
- Transductive estimation on test data. Estimating ADP directionality from test-set bigrams introduces a form of test-time adaptation. While unsupervised, this can inflate reported UAS (especially for short test sets or skewed domains). A more rigorous evaluation would estimate on a development split or with a few initial sentences and report performance on the remainder.
- Baselines and metrics could be stronger/broader. The main supervised baseline is delexicalized multi-source parsing. Omitted are projection-based baselines that leverage bitext (acknowledged) and more recent cross-lingual approaches. Also, only UAS is reported; unlabeled but with punctuation included leads to known volatility. Additional metrics (e.g., undirected scores, punctuation-excluded UAS, or head/root accuracy by sentence length) would give a richer picture.
- Coverage limitations and rule selection. The hand-picked rule set intentionally avoids some relations (e.g., NOUN→VERB to capture relative clauses), which caps achievable UAS on certain phenomena (coordination, punctuation, RCs). The rule selection is tuned on English dev; although cross-language results are decent, this introduces potential bias that is only partially mitigated by the broad evaluation.

Questions to authors
- Test-time estimation of ADP direction: How sensitive are results to the amount of test text used for estimation? Can you report a “first k sentences for direction, rest for evaluation” setting and a language-wise curve to quantify potential transductive bias?
- PR personalization: Why a fixed 5x weight? Did you try tuning it on development data cross-linguistically or making it language-adaptive? How do results change if you personalize to all verbs or to the highest in-degree node without POS priors?
- POS tagging: How would UDPP results change with a stronger cross-lingual tagger (e.g., projected or multilingual neural taggers) instead of TnT? Conversely, can you report a setting with no target-language POS training data to match the “no training” claim end-to-end?
- Baselines: Can you include at least one projection-based baseline where bitext is available (even on a subset), to contextualize the gap to supervised cross-lingual methods? Also, how does your system compare to Søgaard (2012b) reproduced on UD?
- Robustness to mixed adposition systems and free word order: For languages with significant in-language variation (e.g., both pre- and postpositions), does the global direction assumption hurt? Could a local decision (per sentence or per bigram) improve without harming stability?
- Tree well-formedness and non-projectivity: Do you ever create multiple roots before line 4–5 enforcement, and what is the rate of non-projective arcs produced? Any impact on error patterns by sentence length?

Overall, the paper scores well on clarity, broad and careful empirical evaluation, and practical relevance. Novelty is moderate, but the robustness findings and the simplicity-to-performance ratio make this a valuable contribution and a strong baseline for UD parsing without training.
