Summary and claimed contributions
The paper presents an unsupervised, rule-driven dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The approach leverages UD’s constraints (single root; function words as dependents) and uses personalized PageRank (PR) over a graph induced by hand-crafted POS head rules to rank content words and select heads. Decoding proceeds in two steps: content words are attached first (rooted by PR), then function words are attached as leaves with simple directionality heuristics. A key runtime heuristic estimates adposition attachment direction (pre/post) from ADP–NOMINAL vs NOMINAL–ADP bigrams in the test set.

Contributions (as assessed, strongest first):
1) A simple, language-agnostic rule-based parsing framework tailored to UD that is competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer across 32 languages, and demonstrably more robust to domain shift.
2) A practical use of personalized PR for cross-lingual unsupervised parsing, together with a two-step decoding strategy that enforces UD leaf constraints for function words.
3) A lightweight, test-time estimation of adposition direction per language, avoiding language-specific parameters and improving attachments involving ADP.

Strengths
- Competitive cross-lingual performance without training and strong domain robustness:
  The parser (UDPG) averages close to a multi-source delexicalized baseline (MSDG), even surpassing it for Hindi, while requiring no target-side training data or parallel data. Under predicted POS (UDPP), its performance degrades less than the supervised transfer system (lower error propagation from POS errors). Cross-domain experiments show systematically lower standard deviation across genres than MSD, supporting the claim that the rule-based approach is less sensitive to domain shifts.

- Clear ablations isolating PageRank and decoding contributions:
  The paper provides a BL baseline and a UDPNoPR variant. UDPNoPR improves over BL (≈+4 UAS), while PR itself yields an additional ≈+9 UAS over UDPNoPR on average, indicating the main empirical gains come from PR-based root/ordering and the two-step decoding that respects UD constraints.

- Simple, transparent design aligned with UD principles:
  The method adheres to UD constraints (single root, function words as leaves) through a deterministic algorithm that is easy to inspect. The approach makes minimal assumptions, avoids lexicalization, and requires only POS tags, facilitating application to many languages.

- Practical test-time estimation of ADP direction:
  Estimating pre-/postposition preference from local ADP–NOMINAL bigrams on the test set is effective (high ADP UAS), requires few examples for convergence (10–15 sentences), and removes the need for language-specific settings.

- Broad empirical coverage and standardized evaluation:
  Results on 32 UD v1.2 languages with UAS on full test sets (rather than undirected/short-sentence protocols) provide a clear, comparable picture. Additional domain splits (including curated ones) further strengthen the evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing:
  The core ideas build directly on Søgaard (2012b) and related work. The contributions—PR personalization, two-step decoding to enforce function word leafness, and ADP direction estimation—are valuable but incremental. The approach remains a small set of hand-crafted POS rules plus PR; there is no new learning principle or broader induction framework.

- “Without training” claim is somewhat overstated:
  The paper tunes PR parameters (teleport probability and personalization weight) and selects head rules on English development data. While not supervised in the usual sense, this constitutes development-time tuning that may bias design choices. The comparison to MSD uses delexicalized models trained on sizable multilingual data, which is a stronger supervision regime; acknowledging this asymmetry would clarify positioning.

- Rule coverage and expressive limits:
  The rule inventory is deliberately small and excludes patterns like NOUN→VERB (e.g., relative clauses), yielding systematic errors on such constructions. Punctuation and coordination remain weak points (low UAS, high variance). Back-off steps that drop direction and rule constraints to ensure a tree can produce linguistically suspect attachments, yet this behavior is not deeply analyzed.

- Use of test data for ADP direction estimation:
  Inferring attachment direction from the test set risks instability for small test sets and languages with mixed or flexible adposition placements (e.g., circumpositions). The paper asserts convergence with 10–15 sentences, but provides limited quantitative robustness analysis across languages/sizes or for single-sentence parsing scenarios.

- Some clarity and specification gaps:
  The PR graph construction is only briefly specified. It is unclear how multigraph edges are weighted, how ties are broken, or how the PR stationary distribution interacts with multiple incoming edges per node. The pseudocode and tables have formatting issues, and hyperparameter choices (teleport=0.05, personalization factor=5) are tuned on English without cross-lingual sensitivity analysis.

Questions to authors
- Graph construction and PR details:
  • Are edges weighted uniformly? Do multiple eligible dependents yield parallel edges or aggregated weights?  
  • How are ties in PR ranking and head selection resolved deterministically?  
  • How sensitive are results to teleport probability and personalization factor across languages?

- ADP direction estimation:
  • How do you handle languages with few/no ADP tokens or mixed adposition strategies (e.g., circumpositions)?  
  • What happens for very small test sets (e.g., sentence-level parsing)? Can the direction be estimated reliably on-the-fly per document?

- Back-off behavior:
  • How often do you drop δ (rule) and κ (direction) constraints in practice, and what are the typical error patterns when this happens?  
  • Do these back-offs introduce non-projectivities systematically?

- Evaluation choices:
  • Did you consider excluding punctuation from UAS to align with common UD evaluation practice, and how would that affect relative rankings?  
  • Can you report undirected/neutralized variants to connect to prior unsupervised parsing literature, even if your main metric is UAS?

- Reproducibility:
  • Will the exact head rules, PR parameters, and domain-split resources be released to ensure replicability?  
  • For MSD, can you provide training data composition and hyperparameters to confirm fairness and reproducibility?

Overall, this work offers a clear, well-validated, and practically useful baseline for UD parsing without training, with strong cross-lingual coverage and domain robustness. Its novelty is moderate but the empirical contribution and clarity of the approach are solid.
