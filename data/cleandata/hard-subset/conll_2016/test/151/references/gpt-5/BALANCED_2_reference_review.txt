Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents UDP, a rule-based, training-free dependency parser tailored to the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. UDP builds a graph from a small set of POS-based head rules, ranks content words with personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate and determine attachment order, and decodes in two passes to enforce UD’s constraint that most function words are leaves. A key practical component is estimating adposition attachment direction (pre/postposition) on the fly from test bigrams. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under gold and predicted POS, and compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer (MSD) parser. The authors also assess domain robustness.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, language-agnostic, UD-aware unsupervised parsing baseline that is competitive with delexicalized transfer in UAS and notably more robust to domain shifts across many languages.
2) A practical combination of personalized PageRank and two-pass decoding that leverages UD constraints (single root; function words as dependents) without training, plus a test-time estimation of ADP directionality.
3) A broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation establishing this approach as a strong, stable baseline and diagnostic tool for UD parsing.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual coverage and robustness: UDP runs on 32 languages with consistent gains over a rule-only baseline and narrow average gaps to MSD (≈6.4 UAS with gold POS). The cross-domain analysis shows substantially lower variance across domains than MSD, supporting the claim that a small set of UD-motivated rules generalizes better and is less sensitive to dataset bias. This is practically valuable for low-resource and out-of-domain scenarios.
- Sensible UD-centered design: The two-pass decoding that attaches content words first and then function words enforces a key UD bias (function words as leaves), which many prior cross-lingual methods ignore. The test-time estimation of ADP directionality from raw bigrams is simple, effective (high ADP UAS), and avoids reliance on language-specific parameters.
- Personalized PageRank improves head selection: The ablation shows that PR contributes roughly 9 UAS over a decoding-only variant, with a clear, interpretable role in identifying the main predicate and improving content-word attachments. The personalization toward the first verb gives consistent gains, and the graph-construction is deliberately constrained by the head rules to reflect plausible UD relations.
- Transparent baseline with realistic POS setups: The paper reports gold POS, predicted POS (TnT), and a harsh content/function-only setup. The limited drop under predicted POS compared to MSD suggests lower error propagation, which is a meaningful, reproducible finding for practitioners.
- Reproducibility and scope: The approach is simple, deterministic given POS, and fast. The breadth of languages and the inclusion of multiple domains (including curated splits) increase confidence in generality.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to Søgaard (2012): The core idea of PageRank-based unsupervised parsing with rule constraints is known; the paper’s novelties (personalization, two-pass decoding specific to UD, on-the-fly ADP direction) are useful but incremental. The contribution is strongest as a broad UD baseline and analysis rather than as a fundamentally new method.
- Heavy reliance on a small, hand-tuned rule set: The head rules were selected on English dev to maximize precision; other function-word direction decisions (AUX/DET/SCONJ) are fixed globally. This risks biasing performance toward languages matching these choices and excludes phenomena like relative clauses (by design, NOUN→VERB excluded) and coordination. The fallback that drops constraints to ensure a tree can yield linguistically dubious attachments. More systematic rule induction or per-language unsupervised estimation (as done for ADP) would strengthen the claim of language-agnosticity.
- Personalization heuristic may be typologically brittle: Boosting the first verb (or first content word) assumes clause structures that may not hold in verb-final or copula-drop languages. While the authors show average gains and even improvement in Hindi, a deeper typological analysis and sensitivity study (e.g., verb position distributions, alternate personalization strategies) would better justify this choice.
- Evaluation scope and baselines: The primary supervised comparator is MSD (2011). Given the paper’s emphasis on UD and cross-linguality, comparisons to more recent transfer/projection or unsupervised methods (or stronger multi-source delexicalized parsers) would contextualize the 6–7 UAS gap better. Also, some domain splits are small and in-house annotated, raising concerns about statistical power and comparability; reporting confidence intervals or significance would help.
- Clarity and completeness: Parts of the algorithm description and tables are hard to follow (apparent formatting artifacts), and some references appear incomplete. The precise PR graph construction, tie-breaking, and fallback semantics deserve clearer specification to ensure replicability.

Questions to authors
1) Can you provide a sensitivity analysis for the PR personalization weight and teleport parameter across languages (e.g., impact on root accuracy and UAS in verb-final vs. verb-initial languages)?
2) How often does the decoding fallback drop δ (rule) and/or κ (direction) constraints, and what proportion of resulting edges violate intended UD biases? A per-POS breakdown would help.
3) Have you tried estimating directionality for DET/AUX/SCONJ per language from test bigrams (as with ADP)? If so, does it improve DET in Basque and others?
4) How does UDP fare on newer UD versions, where guidelines and treebank quality have improved? Any notable changes in robustness or POS-specific performance?
5) Could you compare against a stronger/deeper MSD setup or another recent cross-lingual baseline to better situate the 6–7 UAS gap?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is well-motivated, thorough empirically, and offers a useful, robust UD baseline with a clear error analysis. However, the methodological novelty over prior PageRank-based unsupervised parsing is modest, and several design choices are hand-tuned and potentially typology-sensitive. I encourage a revision that strengthens the methodological contribution (e.g., broader unsupervised estimation of directionality/rules, richer edge-scoring with PR, improved handling of coordination/relative clauses), clarifies algorithms and ablations, and updates baselines. Even if not accepted, releasing the code and domain splits will benefit the community.
