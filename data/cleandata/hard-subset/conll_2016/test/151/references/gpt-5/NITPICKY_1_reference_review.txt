Summary and key contributions
This paper proposes UDP, a fully rule-based, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that operates solely on POS tags. The core idea is to (i) rank content words in a sentence via personalized PageRank (PR) on a graph constructed from a small set of UD-inspired head–dependent POS rules, (ii) decode in two passes to enforce that function words become leaves, and (iii) estimate adposition attachment direction on the fly from test data using ADP–NOMINAL bigram statistics. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages, compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (TurboParser), and assessed under gold POS, predicted POS (TnT), and a coarse content/function tag scenario. The authors also report cross-domain robustness on several language/domain splits.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, deterministic, UD-constrained parsing pipeline with personalized PageRank and two-pass decoding that yields competitive unlabeled attachment scores across many languages without supervised parsing training.
2) A pragmatic test-time estimation of adposition directionality and an analysis suggesting improved robustness to domain shift and POS noise compared to a delexicalized transfer baseline.
3) A broad, multi-language evaluation and ablations (with/without PR) that clarify the contribution of PR and the decoding design.

Strengths
- Strong, simple baseline across 32 languages: Despite its simplicity, UDP attains competitive UAS (within ~6–7 points of multi-source delex on average, and better on Hindi), which is surprising given there is no supervised parsing training. The ablation (UDP vs UDPNoPR vs baseline) indicates PR contributes ~9 UAS on average, which is substantial.
- Domain robustness: The cross-domain analysis (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian) shows markedly lower variance for UDP than for the delex baseline, under both gold and predicted POS. This supports the claim that linguistically motivated, low-parameter systems can be less sensitive to distributional shifts.
- Clear exploitation of UD constraints: Two-pass decoding that enforces function-word leafness and single-root constraints is well aligned with UD design goals. The idea to use PR personalization toward the putative main predicate is intuitive and seems to help both accuracy and convergence.
- Language-agnostic adposition direction estimation: Estimating ADP direction from test data converges quickly (10–15 sentences) and appears to work reliably across languages, mitigating language-specific tuning.
- Deterministic, transparent method: The algorithm is easy to reproduce conceptually, with predictable outputs for a given POS sequence, and provides value as a strong rule-based baseline for UD.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited and claims of “no training” are overstated: The core ideas build closely on prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing and Søgaard (2012), with incremental tweaks (PR personalization, two-pass decoding). Moreover, several parameters/rules are tuned on English dev (head rules selection, teleport prob 0.05, personalization weight 5, attachment directions for AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ/PUNCT), which contradicts the strict “no training” claim and introduces language-specific biases. Estimating ADP direction on the test set itself is also a form of test-time parameter estimation.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: The supervised baseline is a delexicalized TurboParser with 20k sentences pooled from other languages; stronger modern cross-lingual baselines (e.g., with cross-lingual embeddings, annotation projection variants, or more recent parsers/taggers) are not considered. Using TnT for POS is dated and may understate robustness of delex systems under better POS. No significance testing is reported for the observed differences, and punctuation is included in UAS, which is non-standard in UD evaluations and likely depresses scores unevenly (PUNCT UAS ~21% with large variance).
- Methodological issues and missing details: 
  - PR graph construction relies on the same head rules later used in decoding, creating a circular dependency bias; the multigraph/edge weighting details are under-specified (are multi-edges summed as weights? normalization? damping factor?), as is PR iteration/stopping criterion and complexity beyond saying teleport=0.05. 
  - The decoding back-off (dropping δ, then dropping κ) is aggressive and may attach blatantly wrong heads; the frequency of back-offs and their impact is not quantified.
  - Disallowing NOUN→VERB heads precludes relative clause structures by design; this is a major structural limitation in UD parsing that likely harms accuracy on common constructions.
  - Complexity claims (O(n×|C|)) ignore the second-pass loop and do not report actual runtime or scalability; PR dominates cost but is not profiled.
- Evaluation scope is narrow: Only UAS is reported (with punctuation), no LAS, no UAS excluding punctuation, no breakdowns for key phenomena (coordination, MWE, auxiliaries vs main verbs). The CF experiment is arguably too crude to be informative; instead, experiments with realistic noisy POS or modern taggers would be more compelling. Error propagation analysis is based on a simple ratio formula without confidence intervals.
- Writing, formatting, and anonymity issues: The submission contains numerous typos and inconsistencies (“The the”, mismatched example “come” vs “had”, set vs sequence notation for F, missing/garbled footnotes), broken references (odd “? \Q1999\E”), and potential anonymity concerns (direct links to github.com/UniversalDependencies/tools; “freely available at http://ANONYMIZED” while earlier links are not anonymized). Tables/figures are hard to follow in places, and the rules in Table 1 are incomplete relative to UD POS inventory.

Questions to authors
- Training/parameterization: Which components were tuned on English dev vs fixed a priori? Please list all tuned hyperparameters (PR damping/teleport, personalization weight, function word directions, rule selection). How sensitive are results to these values? Can you re-tune on a different language and report cross-language generalization to validate the “language-agnostic” claim?
- PR details: Precisely specify the PR graph: how are multi-edges handled (summed weights vs parallel edges), how are edges normalized, what damping factor is used, and what is the stopping criterion? Please report average iterations and runtime per sentence, and ablation of personalization magnitude (e.g., 1x, 2x, 5x, 10x).
- Back-off frequency: How often do you drop δ and/or κ in practice across languages, and what is the UAS impact? Can you quantify the proportion of attachments created without rule conformity or directionality?
- Evaluation protocol: Please provide standard UD metrics: UAS excluding punctuation and LAS (if feasible with partial labels), and significance testing vs the delex baseline. Also report results with punctuation excluded to align with standard practice.
- Baselines: Can you compare against stronger cross-lingual transfer baselines (e.g., with cross-lingual embeddings) and/or a more modern delex parser and tagger? Alternatively, use a stronger POS tagger to assess error propagation fairly.
- Adposition direction estimation: Is the direction estimated once per language on a small held-out set or incrementally on the test set (risking leakage)? Please clarify the exact protocol and its robustness for small test sets. Could the same data-driven estimation be applied to DET/AUX/SCONJ directions instead of fixing them from English?
- Structural coverage: Given the explicit exclusion of NOUN→VERB heads, can you quantify the degradation on relative clauses? Any plans to handle coordination and MWEs beyond future work, or minimal additions to the rules to mitigate their current impact?

Overall, while the system is a useful, transparent baseline and the cross-domain robustness is appealing, the methodological novelty is modest, several evaluation choices and details are suboptimal or under-specified, and the writing/formatting issues are significant.
