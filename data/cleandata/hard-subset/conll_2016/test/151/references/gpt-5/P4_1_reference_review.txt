Summary
The paper presents UDP, a training-free dependency parser tailored to Universal Dependencies (UD). The parser relies on (i) a small set of UD-compliant POS-based head rules, (ii) a two-step decoding procedure that first attaches content words and then function words to enforce UD leaf constraints, and (iii) personalized PageRank (PR) over a rule-induced graph to prioritize likely heads (especially the main predicate). A key language-specific trait—adposition attachment direction—is estimated on the fly from test-set POS bigrams. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under three POS tagging conditions (gold, predicted, and a coarse CONTENT/FUNCTION heuristic), compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (TurboParser). Results show UDP is competitive in UAS and notably robust to domain shift, with ablations indicating PR contributes substantially.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, training-free UD parser that operationalizes UD constraints via a two-step decoding strategy and personalized PR to rank content-word heads. This is a practical instantiation of UD’s design goals with a deterministic parser.
2) A broad empirical evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domains showing competitive UAS versus a multi-source delexicalized baseline and lower cross-domain variance; ablations quantify the contribution of PR.
3) A lightweight, test-time estimation of adposition direction that removes language-specific parameters and works reliably across languages.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual and cross-domain coverage: The system is evaluated on 32 languages and multiple domains (including curated splits), which is unusually broad for rule-based/unsupervised parsing. UDP attains average UAS of 57.5 (gold POS) vs. 63.9 for the delexicalized baseline; with predicted POS, UDP drops by only 2.2 points (to 55.3) compared to a 2.7-point drop for the baseline, indicating robustness to noisy tagging.
- Domain robustness: Across domain splits, UDP exhibits markedly lower standard deviation than the delex baseline (both with gold and predicted POS), supporting the claim that a rule-based, constraint-driven method is less sensitive to domain shifts. In some hard domains (e.g., Italian), UDP outperforms the delex model.
- Methodological clarity and useful ablations: The two-step decoding and PR graph construction are specified, and ablations disentangle contributions: disabling PR (UDPNoPR) reduces UAS by ~9 points; decoding improvements (sans PR) still add ~4 points over a head-rule baseline. This isolates PR’s role in root/main predicate identification and content-word attachment.
- Language-agnostic design with minimal parameters: The use of UD constraints and test-time estimation of ADP direction avoids language-specific tuning. The ADP direction heuristic converges quickly and yields high ADP UAS (~75%).
- Competitive baseline comparisons under realistic setups: Results are reported with both gold and predicted POS. The multi-source delex baseline is a standard, competitive reference in cross-lingual parsing. UDP surpasses it on Hindi and remains within 6–7 UAS on average under gold POS.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The core ideas (PR-based ranking, no-training unsupervised parsing) closely follow Søgaard (2012b). The paper’s main methodological differences—PR personalization, enforcing UD leafness via two-step decoding, and test-time ADP direction—are incremental and engineering-focused. Novelty is moderate.
- Use of test data for parameter estimation: ADP direction is estimated from test-set bigrams, and the PR personalization weight and function-word directions were tuned on English development data. While acceptable in an unsupervised paradigm, using the target test set to set a key parameter raises comparability concerns versus the supervised transfer baseline. A development split or cross-validation on the target would strengthen the evaluation.
- Rule coverage and known structural limitations: The head rules intentionally avoid NOUN→VERB, which precludes modeling relative clauses and likely harms attachment around clausal modifiers. Coordination and punctuation handling are weak (e.g., PUNCT UAS ~21%, CONJ variability), and there is no mechanism for multiword expressions. These design choices cap attainable accuracy.
- Baseline fairness and reporting details: The rule-based baseline (BLG) uses an oracle choice of global left/right backoff direction per language on the test set, which is optimistic. Conversely, UDP’s ADP direction is also estimated on test sentences. A clearer, matched setting (e.g., estimating both with a held-out portion or per-sentence heuristics) would improve fairness. Statistical significance of differences is not reported.
- Clarity and presentation issues: There are formatting glitches (inline figure/code references, table/footnote artifacts), incomplete references, and some algorithmic details could be clearer (runtime/practical efficiency of PR, failure modes, handling of non-projective structures). While the core algorithm is understandable, polish is needed.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the PR personalization factor (set to 5) and to the teleport probability (0.05)? Please provide a sensitivity analysis across several languages.
- For ADP direction, is estimation done per language over the whole test set, per document, or per sentence? How stable is it under small sample sizes or mixed head-direction corpora?
- Can you report parsing runtime per sentence (including PR) and memory footprint across sentence lengths, and compare to the delex baseline?
- How often do the back-off steps (dropping δ and/or κ constraints) trigger, and what is their impact on tree well-formedness and UAS?
- Would excluding punctuation from UAS change conclusions materially, especially for domain comparisons?
- Could you include a comparison to a modern projection-based baseline where parallel data exists, or justify its omission more explicitly?

Overall assessment
The paper offers a clear, practical, and language-agnostic UD parser with no training requirements, supported by broad and careful evaluation. While methodological novelty is limited, the demonstrated domain robustness and competitive performance make it a useful baseline and a valuable contribution for low-resource and cross-domain settings. Improvements to clarity, stronger controls on parameter estimation, and extended comparisons would further strengthen the work.
