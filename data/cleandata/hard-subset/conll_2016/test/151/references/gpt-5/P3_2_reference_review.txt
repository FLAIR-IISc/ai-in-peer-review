Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes UDP, a training-free, language-agnostic dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that exploits UD’s structural constraints and a small set of POS-based head rules. The core idea is to rank content words with personalized PageRank (PR), then decode in two passes to attach content words first and force function words to be leaves, aligning with UD guidelines.
- It introduces an on-the-fly, data-driven estimate of adposition attachment direction (pre/postposition) from ADP–NOMINAL bigrams computed on the test sentences, and fixed attachment direction heuristics for other function words.
- Extensive experiments on 32 UD v1.2 languages, with gold and predicted POS as well as a naive content/function POS setting, and cross-domain evaluation, show the parser is competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (TurboParser), and more robust to domain shifts.

Strengths
- Solid multilingual and cross-domain evaluation with clear takeaways. The study spans 32 languages, multiple POS regimes (gold, target-trained predicted, and naive CF), and multiple domains (including curated domain splits). The results are consistent: with gold POS, UDP averages 57.5 UAS vs. 63.9 for the delexicalized baseline; with predicted POS, the drop is smaller for UDP than for MSD (2.2 vs. 2.7 points). The domain-robustness analysis is compelling, showing lower standard deviation across domains (Table 6), confirming that constraint-driven, low-parameter models can be more stable.
- Method leverages UD’s formal constraints in a principled way. The two-step decoding to enforce function-word leafness operationalizes a central UD design choice, distinguishing it from prior unsupervised/rule-based parsers and aligning structural biases with the target formalism. The PR personalization (bias toward the main predicate) is simple, interpretable, and empirically useful (+9 UAS over UDPNoPR).
- Minimal language-specific knowledge and simplicity. Beyond POS tags and a small set of universal rules (Table 1), the approach avoids language-specific parameters; ADP direction is inferred automatically. The algorithm is deterministic given a POS sequence, efficient in practice, and provides transparent failure modes (e.g., inability to capture relative clauses due to excluded NOUN→VERB).
- Careful ablations and error analyses. The paper quantifies contributions of PR vs. decoding (UDP vs. UDPNoPR vs. BL), analyzes POS-wise behavior (e.g., DET, ADP), and discusses problematic categories (CONJ, PUNCT). It also reports root accuracy and effects of personalization across word order types.

Weaknesses
- Transductive estimation on test data. Estimating adposition direction from test sentences introduces test-time look-ahead and violates standard evaluation protocols. While the authors argue convergence in 10–15 sentences and a minimal footprint, this is still data leakage. The paper should report variants where direction is estimated on dev or unlabeled non-test data, and quantify the sensitivity and impact per language.
- Comparability of supervision settings. The “unsupervised parsing” claim is fair, but key reported results (UDPP) use POS tags predicted by TnT trained on the target language training set, whereas the delexicalized MSD uses only source-language data. This creates an imbalance in supervision on the target side. The CF setup is a useful stress test but very crude. Additional comparisons using cross-lingual/weakly supervised POS (e.g., projected or multilingual taggers) would clarify the true zero-resource behavior.
- Novelty is incremental relative to Søgaard (2012b). The main technical differences—PR personalization, two-step decoding enforcing function-word leafness, and ADP direction estimation—are sensible but modest. The paper would be stronger with a direct reproduction of Søgaard’s setup on UD to isolate gains, or with stronger cross-lingual baselines (e.g., projection-based parsers or models leveraging multilingual embeddings/dictionaries as in Rasooli and Collins 2015; Ma and Xia 2014) evaluated on the same UD splits.
- Some evaluation choices limit interpretability. Including punctuation in UAS, while consistent across systems, depresses scores and complicates comparison to the broader literature; standard “no-punct” UAS and significance testing are missing. The rule set was tuned on English dev, and several fixed directionality choices for function words were set from English; this induces potential bias (e.g., poor DET in Basque). It would be preferable to infer all function-word directions in a data-driven fashion (as done for ADP) and/or to show that tuning on a typologically different language yields similar results.
- Missing details: runtime and structural properties. The paper claims O(n×|C|) decoding and that PR is the main cost, but does not report wall-clock performance or memory. The back-off that relaxes rule/direction constraints ensures trees but could increase non-projectivity; reporting the non-projective rate and well-formedness statistics would help characterize outputs.

Questions to authors
- How much of the final UAS is attributable to estimating ADP direction on the test set? Please report results when estimating direction on dev or on a small unlabeled corpus (not overlapping with test), and a fixed-direction baseline.
- For UDPP, since the POS tagger is trained on the target language’s labeled training data, can you add results with cross-lingual POS tagging (e.g., projected tags or multilingual taggers) to reflect a truly low-resource target?
- Can you provide UAS excluding punctuation and significance tests for key comparisons? Is MSD evaluated under the same punctuation-included regime?
- Please include a direct comparison to Søgaard (2012b) re-implemented on UD to quantify the gain from personalization and two-step decoding.
- How sensitive are results to the teleport probability and personalization weight? Any languages where personalization hurts (e.g., strict verb-final)?
- What is the non-projective arc rate and average runtime per sentence?

Overall evaluation
This is a clear, well-executed paper demonstrating that UD’s design can be exploited to build a simple, training-free parser that is surprisingly competitive and robust across languages and domains. The methodological novelty is moderate, and the transductive ADP direction estimation and supervision mismatch with the delex baseline should be addressed. With clarifications and a couple of additional controls, I would support acceptance.
