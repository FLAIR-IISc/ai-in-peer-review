Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a training-free dependency parser (UDP) designed specifically for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method builds a directed multigraph over tokens using a small set of POS-based head rules, applies personalized PageRank (PR) to rank content words and identify the sentence head, and decodes in two passes: attaching content words first, then enforcing function words as leaves with directionality constraints. Adposition attachment direction (pre/postposition) is estimated on-the-fly from test data via ADP–NOMINAL bigrams. The approach is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages with gold POS, predicted POS, and a coarse content/function POS setup, and compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline. The authors also assess cross-domain robustness.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, language-agnostic, training-free UD parser that operationalizes UD constraints (single root; function words as dependents) via a two-step decoding and personalized PR for root selection and content-word ordering.
2) An unsupervised per-language estimate of adposition attachment direction directly from test data, avoiding language-specific parameters.
3) A broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation showing competitive UAS vs. delexicalized transfer parsing and improved robustness to domain shift; informative ablations (UDP vs UDPNoPR).

Strengths
- Clear, principled exploitation of UD design: The two-step decoding that enforces function words as leaves and the restricted head rules align well with UD’s representational constraints. This makes the approach linguistically motivated and helps explain its cross-lingual stability.
- Strong multilingual coverage and competitive accuracy: Across 32 languages, UDP with gold POS is on average within ~6–7 UAS points of multi-source delexicalized transfer, and even outperforms it for Hindi. The predicted POS results are competitive while showing lower error propagation from POS to dependencies than the transfer baseline.
- Robustness to domain shift: The cross-domain experiments (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian) suggest UDP has substantially lower performance variance across genres than the supervised delexicalized model. This supports the claim that rule-based, UD-constrained methods are less sensitive to domain biases than data-driven transfer models.
- Sensible ablations and analysis: The UDPNoPR variant shows PR contributes ~9 UAS points on average; the baseline vs. UDPNoPR difference isolates the decoding/leafness benefit. The POS-wise breakdown is informative (e.g., high DET UAS except for Basque; low PUNCT/CONJ stability), and the discussion is candid about limitations (e.g., excluding NOUN→VERB prevents handling relative clauses).
- Simplicity and reproducibility: The method is easy to implement, fast, and requires only POS tags and universal POS constraints. The reliance on a small rule set enhances transparency.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-based unsupervised parsing (especially Søgaard 2012b): The core ideas—PR-based ranking and rule-based decoding—are known. The main methodological additions (PR personalization; strict two-step decoding enforcing leafness; on-the-fly ADP direction) are incremental. A direct empirical comparison to Søgaard’s original setup (not just UDPNoPR) is missing and would better quantify the gains attributable to the new elements.
- “No training” claim vs. POS reliance and English development tuning: While the parser itself is training-free, most reported results assume high-quality POS tags trained on the target language, which undermines applicability to true low-resource settings. The rules and parameters (teleport probability, functional POS directionality) were tuned on English dev data, which introduces supervision and potential bias. The CF-tag scenario is too coarse to be an informative lower bound and leads to very low UAS.
- Test-time estimation using the target test data: Estimating ADP direction from the test set bigrams is unsupervised, but still “peeks” at the test distribution. This is acceptable for a pure unsupervised objective, yet it complicates comparison to supervised transfer baselines and to scenarios where only sentence-level inputs (not large test corpora) are available.
- Baselines and evaluation scope: The hand-crafted baseline does not guarantee well-formed trees, making it a weak comparator. There is no comparison to other modern unsupervised/weakly supervised methods (e.g., posterior regularization, universal priors beyond Søgaard), nor to projection-based unsupervised baselines on UD. Only UAS is reported; LAS and structural analyses (e.g., projectivity rate, attachment length distribution, clause-level phenomena) are not provided. Statistical significance is not reported for key comparisons or domain variance differences.
- Systematic limitations in coverage: By design decisions (e.g., excluding NOUN→VERB), the system cannot handle relative clauses, and coordination remains weakly handled (low CONJ UAS). The fixed directions for AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ derived from English dev hurt languages like Basque; learning these per language (as done for ADP) would likely improve robustness.

Questions to authors
- Could you include a direct replication of Søgaard (2012b) on the same UD setup to isolate the gains from PR personalization and two-step decoding, beyond UDPNoPR?
- How sensitive are results to the PR personalization weight and teleport probability? Did you try learning/estimating these per language in an unsupervised way?
- Can you estimate attachment directionality for other function categories (DET, AUX, SCONJ, CONJ) from data, analogous to ADP, to avoid English-tuned global settings? Any preliminary results?
- How large must the test corpus be to reliably infer ADP direction? What happens for short documents or sentence-by-sentence parsing where such estimation is unstable?
- Do your conclusions hold with LAS and with punctuation excluded, and are the differences to MSD statistically significant? Similarly, are the lower domain variances significant?
- Could you release the full set of head rules and provide an error analysis on specific constructions (e.g., relative clauses, coordination, auxiliaries) to clarify where UDP loses vs MSD?

Overall, this is a well-executed and useful baseline that convincingly shows the value of UD-aware, training-free parsing. The novelty is modest, but the breadth of evaluation and the robustness findings make it a valuable contribution.
