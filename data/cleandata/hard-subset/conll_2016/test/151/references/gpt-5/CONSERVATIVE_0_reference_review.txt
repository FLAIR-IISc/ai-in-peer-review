Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a rule-based, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that operates solely on POS tags. The method uses a small set of UD-compliant head rules, a personalized PageRank (PR) ranking over a rule-induced graph to prioritize content words (and identify the main predicate), a two-pass decoding that enforces function-word leafness, and a simple test-time heuristic to infer adposition directionality from POS bigrams. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages with gold and predicted POS, and on multiple domains, against a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A strong, language-agnostic, UD-specific rule-based baseline that achieves reasonably competitive UAS across many languages without supervised parsing training and shows reduced sensitivity to domain and POS noise.
2) An incremental but effective use of personalized PageRank and a two-step decoding scheme tailored to UD constraints, with ablations demonstrating their impact.
3) A broad, multi-language, cross-domain evaluation, including analysis of POS error propagation, and the release of resources.

Strengths
- Breadth and relevance of evaluation: The paper covers 32 languages, three POS scenarios (gold, TnT-predicted, and a crude CF split), and multiple domains. This breadth is rare for unsupervised or rule-based parsers and is highly relevant for the UD community. The reported POS-to-UAS error propagation analysis is a useful diagnostic.
- Competitive performance for a very simple system: With gold POS, the method is on average ~6.4 UAS behind multi-source delex (MSDG), and it even outperforms MSDG on Hindi. With predicted POS, the relative drop is modest and comparable to the delex baseline. The ablation shows PR adds ~9 UAS, and the decoding step adds ~4 UAS over the baseline rules.
- Domain robustness: The standard deviation across domains is consistently lower than the delex baseline (both with gold and predicted POS), supporting the claim that the small, general rule set yields more stable behavior under domain shift than data-driven models.
- Clarity of the approach and utility as a baseline: The algorithm, rules, and assumptions are clearly stated. A deterministic parser that operates on POS alone is a useful, portable baseline for UD, particularly in low-resource and domain-shift settings. The promise to release code and domain splits enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and overstatement of “without training”: The core idea (PR-based ranking for unsupervised parsing) is known (Søgaard, 2012), and the additions—personalization toward the first verb, two-pass decoding for function-word leafness, and a simple bigram heuristic for ADP direction—are incremental. Moreover, several design choices are tuned on English dev data (rule selection, PR parameters), which dilutes the “no training” claim; the system is better described as rule-based with light development-time tuning on UD.
- Use of test-set statistics for directionality: Estimating adposition direction directly on the test data introduces test-time adaptation that the supervised baseline cannot exploit, muddying fairness. It also risks instability across domains (if re-estimated per split). The paper should either estimate direction from training/development data or from an external unlabeled corpus, and report both settings.
- Evaluation scope and baselines: Only UAS is reported; no LAS or labeled analysis, which is especially relevant in UD. Statistical significance is not reported. The delex baseline is reasonable, but stronger transfer/projection baselines could be included or at least discussed (e.g., more recent multi-source strategies or projection methods), and comparisons should clarify whether their data prerequisites differ. The adjacency baseline is very weak; the rule-based baseline is closer but still not a strong trained alternative.
- Linguistic coverage and limitations: The rule set is intentionally small and excludes important phenomena (coordination, relative clauses, punctuation) leading to notably low scores on PUNCT and CONJ and an explicit inability to handle relative clauses. While the simplicity is a design choice, a deeper structural error analysis (e.g., attachment types, projectivity, clause-level phenomena) is needed to quantify where the method breaks and to justify rule exclusions (e.g., NOUN→VERB).
- Dependency on POS taggers and UD version: The “predicted POS” setup uses TnT trained on target-language UD training data, which is supervised and in-domain; this undermines the training-free narrative and may overstate robustness. The CF two-tag scenario is arguably too crude to be informative. Finally, the method is tuned/evaluated on UD v1.2; stability on later UD versions (with refined guidelines) is not shown.

Questions to authors
- How exactly is ADP direction estimated for experiments—per language once on the full test set, per document/domain split, or per sentence? Could you report results when the direction is fixed from training/development data (or UD features) to ensure a fairer comparison to MSD?
- Can you provide significance tests for the main comparisons (UDPG vs MSDG; UDPP vs MSDP) and per-language differences?
- Could you evaluate LAS (or at least partial labeling) and provide a structural error breakdown (e.g., coordination, RCs, adpositional attachments) to better characterize where the rules help/hurt?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of POS tagger (beyond TnT) and to lower-resource tagging (e.g., cross-lingual projected tags)? Does the POS→UAS error propagation hold under these settings?
- Does the method retain its advantages on UD v2.x? If not, which rules need adaptation?

Overall, this is a useful and well-executed baseline paper with broad evaluation and practical relevance to UD. The methodological novelty is limited and some comparisons are debatable due to test-time adaptation and reliance on supervised POS, but the clarity, breadth, and robustness results make it a valuable reference.
