Summary and contributions
This paper proposes UDP, a training-free, rule-based dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies only on POS tags and a small set of UD-inspired head–dependent constraints. The method ranks content words using personalized PageRank (PR) computed on a graph derived from POS-based head rules, decodes in two passes to enforce UD’s “function words as leaves,” and estimates adposition attachment direction on-the-fly from test data via simple bigram counts. The parser is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under three POS conditions (gold, predicted with TnT trained per language, and a crude content/function split) and compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). The authors also analyze robustness under domain shifts.

Main contributions (as I see them), strongest first:
- A surprisingly strong, interpretable, training-free UD parser that is competitive with supervised delexicalized transfer on 32 languages, and more robust to domain shift. This provides a strong rule-based baseline for cross-lingual UD parsing.
- A simple but effective use of personalized PageRank to identify main predicates and to prioritize content-word attachment, plus a two-step decoding procedure that enforces UD’s function-word leaf constraint.
- An unsupervised estimation of adposition direction from test data, reducing reliance on language-specific parameters, and a broad empirical analysis including ablations and cross-domain evaluation.

Strengths
- Broad multilingual coverage with competitive performance: On UD v1.2, UDPG lags MSDG by only 6.4 UAS on average and even outperforms it on Hindi; with predicted POS, the drop is modest and smaller than MSD’s. This is notable given UDP requires no treebank training.
- Robustness to domain shift: The cross-domain analysis shows consistently lower UAS variance for UDP than MSD (both with gold and predicted POS), supporting the claim that the rule-based, low-parameter approach generalizes better across genres. This is practically valuable.
- Method simplicity and interpretability: The two-step decoding, explicit UD-inspired head rules, and directional constraints yield highly interpretable behavior and deterministic outputs given POS. The algorithm enforces single-root and function-word leafness while remaining fast.
- Careful ablations and analysis: The paper isolates PR vs. decoding contributions, quantifies POS-to-dependency error propagation, and analyzes POS-specific UAS (e.g., ADP and DET directionality). The ADP direction estimation is elegant and data-light.
- Sound evaluation choices and breadth: Standard UAS on full test sets, 32 languages, multiple POS scenarios, and comparison to a strong multi-source delexicalized baseline. The added domainwise slicing (including in-house English subsets) strengthens the empirical picture.

Weaknesses
- “Without training” claim vs. evaluation setup: While the parser itself is training-free, the predicted-POS setting uses TnT trained on each target language’s training set, which weakens the “no training” narrative. The content/function POS condition is too crude and not a realistic proxy for low-resource tagging. Consider adding a cross-lingual POS projection setting or fully unsupervised tagging to better support the claim.
- Limited baselines for truly unsupervised parsing: The comparison is mainly to delexicalized transfer (supervised) and a custom rule baseline. There is no direct comparison to strong unsupervised parsers (e.g., Søgaard 2012b’s baselines, or more recent unsupervised/weakly supervised UD-oriented methods), making it hard to place UDP among unsupervised SOTA.
- Rule tuning and test-time estimation blur lines: Head rules and PR parameters were tuned on English dev data; ADP direction is estimated on the test set itself. While this is unsupervised, it is still test-set peeking, and might inflate results for small test sets or atypical domains. At minimum, discuss the stability of ADP-direction estimates (variance across subsets) and the risk of overfitting English-specific choices in the rule set.
- Structural coverage gaps: Excluding NOUN→VERB heads precludes modeling relative clauses; coordination and punctuation are acknowledged weaknesses with low UAS and high variance. These phenomena are central in UD; the current approach will systematically fail in common constructions, potentially limiting downstream utility.
- Evaluation scope limitations: Only UAS is reported; UD is label-rich and function-word leafness interacts with labels. Even partial labeling or head/root accuracy stratified by sentence length and language typology (e.g., V-final) would strengthen the claims. Significance testing is also missing.

Questions to authors
- Predicted POS scenario: Since TnT is trained per target language, how should practitioners in low-resource settings obtain POS tags? Can you report results with cross-lingual POS tagging (e.g., projection or multilingual models) to better reflect “no target training”?
- ADP direction estimation: How stable is the 10–15 sentence heuristic across languages and domains? Can you report accuracy of direction detection vs. UD metadata (when available) and sensitivity analyses with fewer/more sentences?
- PageRank personalization: You bias toward the first verb (or first content word). How does this behave in V-final/head-final languages with auxiliaries or copulas? Can you give root accuracy per language family and show UDP with and without personalization for V-final languages?
- Backoff behavior: In decoding, you drop constraints when no valid heads exist. How often does backoff trigger, and what proportion of edges violate the rules/direction constraints as a result? Does this correlate with errors?
- Runtime/efficiency: What are per-sentence runtime statistics (PR + decoding) and memory costs across language treebanks? Any failure cases (disconnected PR graphs) beyond the teleport fix?

Additional comments
- The rule-based baseline uses oracle L/R for backoff; this is arguably stronger than what UDP gets (estimated ADP only). Clarify fairness and possibly report a non-oracle baseline variant.
- Consider reporting projectivity statistics of the predicted trees and error breakdowns by sentence length; many cross-lingual setups skew by length and non-projective rate.
- Releasing code and the domainwise splits is valuable; please ensure scripts reproduce all settings (PR parameters, direction estimation) and include seeds for stability.
