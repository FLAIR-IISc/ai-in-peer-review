Summary and main contributions
This paper presents a simple rule-based, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method uses (i) a small set of UD-compatible head rules, (ii) a two-step decoding procedure that first attaches content words (ranked by personalized PageRank) and then function words to enforce UD leaf constraints, and (iii) an on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality from bigram counts in the test sentences. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages under three POS settings (gold, predicted via TnT trained per language, and a crude content/function split) and compared primarily against multi-source delexicalized transfer (TurboParser). Additional analyses include ablations (disabling PageRank) and domain robustness using several genre splits.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1) A broad multilingual evaluation of a simple, UD-aware, training-free parsing baseline, including POS robustness and domain-shift analyses.
2) A personalized PageRank-based ranking for content words combined with a two-phase decoding enforcing UD’s function-word leaf constraint, with ablation showing the relative contribution.
3) A transductive heuristic to infer adposition directionality from test data, avoiding language-specific parameters.

Strengths
1) Breadth and transparency of evaluation. The authors test on 32 languages and multiple POS settings, report ablations (UDP vs. UDPNoPR), and provide cross-domain robustness analyses with standard deviations. This scope is valuable and helps position the approach as a strong, interpretable baseline for UD parsing.

2) Competitive performance with minimal supervision. Under gold POS, the method is within ~6–7 UAS points of a multi-source delex baseline on average and even surpasses it on Hindi. With predicted POS, the drop in UAS is smaller for the proposed method than for the delex baseline, suggesting some robustness to POS errors.

3) Clear, linguistically motivated constraints aligned with UD. The two-phase decoding that enforces single-root and function-word leafness, coupled with a small set of head rules, is simple and consistent with UD design goals. The analysis by POS (e.g., ADP, DET) is informative and surfaces where rules are effective and where they are not (e.g., Basque determiners).

4) Domain robustness. The reported lower standard deviations across domains relative to the delex baseline, and some cases where the proposed method outperforms delex in harder domains (e.g., Italian legal), make this a plausible out-of-domain baseline.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty over prior rule-based unsupervised parsing with PageRank. The core ideas—rule-aided unsupervised parsing and PageRank-based ranking—trace closely to Søgaard (2012b). The differences (personalization, two-phase decoding enforcing leafness, and test-time ADP direction heuristic) appear incremental. The paper would benefit from a stronger empirical and conceptual comparison to prior systems of this family, ideally including a faithful reimplementation/replication of Søgaard (2012b) under UD.

2) Transductive estimation on test data. Inferring ADP head direction from bigrams in the test sentences is an information leak under standard evaluation protocols. Although it requires only 10–15 sentences, it still uses statistics from the test set. A fairer setup would precompute from an unlabeled corpus or a development split. The paper should quantify performance when ADP direction is estimated without peeking at the test set.

3) Baseline coverage and fairness. The comparison omits stronger contemporary cross-lingual baselines (e.g., projection-based systems, multilingual embeddings, neural delexicalized models), limiting the claim of competitiveness. The rule-based BL baseline also does not guarantee well-formed trees (potentially disconnected), which weakens its diagnostic value. The DMV exclusion is reasonable, but a UD-adapted or stronger grammar induction baseline would better support the paper’s positioning.

4) “No training” claim vs. reliance on supervised POS and English-tuned rules. Several decisions (teleport probability, function-word attachment directions, head rule selection) were tuned on English. For predicted POS, TnT is trained per target language; this is supervised training, even if outside the parser. The claims should be carefully scoped: the parser itself is training-free, but the pipeline depends on trained POS taggers and English development choices.

5) Evaluation scope and depth. Only unlabeled attachment score is reported; no LAS or error analyses by relation type. Given the alignment with UD constraints, it would be informative to report LAS or partial labeling for UD-relevant relations (e.g., aux, det, case) and to quantify where the model systematically fails (e.g., coordination, relative clauses, punctuation). Statistical significance testing over languages or paired tests on domain splits would also strengthen the claims.

Questions to authors
1) Can you report results where ADP directionality is estimated from a held-out development split or a small unlabeled corpus (not the test set)? How much does UAS change?

2) How does your method compare against a reproducible implementation of Søgaard (2012b) adapted to UD (with and without your personalization)? If not feasible, can you at least add a stronger rule-based/unsupervised baseline beyond your BL?

3) Can you provide LAS (and/or labeled scores for a subset of UD relations) to assess whether your UD-motivated constraints translate into correct labels, especially for function words?

4) Could you add a stronger cross-lingual baseline (e.g., modern projection methods, multilingual embedding-based delex/neural parsers) to better contextualize “competitive” performance?

5) How sensitive is performance to the choice of teleport probability and to the global function-word attachment directions fixed from English? Can you estimate these directions per language without supervision, similar to ADP?

6) Does the decoding always produce connected, single-rooted trees in practice? Please clarify the guarantees and failure modes of the backoff mechanism when rule and direction constraints are dropped.

Overall, this is a useful, interpretable baseline with broad evaluation and interesting robustness properties. However, the methodological novelty is limited, and key evaluation choices (test-time estimation, baseline coverage) need tightening before I can strongly recommend acceptance.
