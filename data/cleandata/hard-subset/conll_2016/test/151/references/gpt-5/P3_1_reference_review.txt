Summary and contributions
This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised, rule-based dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that does not use training data. The core ideas are: (i) a small set of UD-compliant head rules and a two-step decoding procedure that first attaches content words and then attaches function words as leaves; (ii) personalized PageRank to prioritize likely main predicates and guide head attachment among content words; and (iii) estimation of adposition attachment direction (pre/postposition) directly from test data via ADP–NOMINAL bigram frequency. The authors evaluate on 32 UD v1.2 languages under three POS-tagging scenarios (gold, predicted with TnT trained on target-language data, and a naive content/function split), compare against a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (TurboParser), provide ablations, and examine cross-domain robustness on language/genre splits.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, zero-training parsing method that exploits UD formal constraints (content/function distinction, single root, function words as dependents) with a principled ranking mechanism (personalized PageRank) and a deterministic two-phase decoder.
2) A thorough multilingual and cross-domain evaluation showing the approach is competitive with a strong delexicalized transfer baseline in UAS and more robust to domain shifts, with clear ablations isolating the effect of PageRank and the two-step decoding.
3) A lightweight, test-time estimation of adposition directionality that removes language-specific parameters while retaining good accuracy.

Strengths
- Strong exploitation of UD design: The method operationalizes key UD constraints (e.g., content vs. function heads) rather than trying to learn them, and the two-step decoding neatly enforces function words as leaves. This aligns the parser with UD’s cross-lingual design and helps explain its broad-coverage behavior.
- Competitive and robust multilingual results: On 32 languages, UDP with gold POS is within ~6 UAS points of the multi-source delex baseline on average, even surpassing it for Hindi, and degrades less under predicted POS. The cross-domain analysis (multiple genres in several languages) shows substantially lower performance variance than the learned baseline, supporting the claim of robustness to domain shift.
- Clear ablations and diagnostics: The UDPNoPR experiment convincingly isolates the contribution of personalized PageRank (~9 UAS points). The root identification accuracy, ADP directionality success, and POS-wise breakdown are helpful diagnostics.
- Minimal language-specific tuning: Adposition directionality is inferred from test data and function-word directions are fixed; the approach is attractive for low-resource settings (modulo POS availability) because no treebank or parallel data are required.
- Reproducibility intent: The paper states that code and additional test splits will be released (assuming anonymity constraints are observed).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to Søgaard (2012b): Using PageRank to prioritize attachments is not new; the main algorithmic novelties are the personalization (bias to the first verb) and the two-step decoding that enforces function-word leafness. While these are sensible, they are relatively incremental. A more direct comparison to Søgaard’s exact setup (same rules/graphs) would clarify how much each innovation contributes beyond differences in UD constraints.
- Reliance on POS quality and non-zero-resource “predicted POS”: The strongest results assume gold POS; with predicted POS the tagger is trained on target-language data (TnT), which is not realistic for truly low-resource scenarios. The UDPN setting (top-100 types as FUNCTION) is too crude to be informative. It would strengthen claims to include realistic cross-lingual or projection-based POS (e.g., Agić et al. 2015) and to report UAS vs. POS accuracy curves.
- Test-time estimation and oracle choices raise fairness concerns: Estimating ADP direction on the test set is transductive. While unsupervised, it still “peeks” at test distribution. Conversely, the rule-based baseline is given an oracle choice of global left/right backoff direction per language (Table 5), which also peeks at test labels. The paper should justify these choices and, ideally, report non-oracle variants to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons with MSD.
- Head-rule coverage and linguistic limitations: The small rule set excludes key constructions (e.g., NOUN→VERB disallowed, impairing relative clauses; coordination not modeled; punctuation poorly handled). The decision to make all function words leaves is at odds with UD in cases of multiword expressions and certain fixed/flat constructions. This is recognized in the discussion but suggests the method’s ceiling may be limited without extending the rule inventory and handling MWEs/coordination.
- Baselines and evaluation choices: The MSD baseline is strong for its era, but delexicalized transfer has since been improved (e.g., with cross-lingual embeddings). Including punctuation in UAS reduces comparability to much prior work. Statistical significance is not reported; some results (e.g., very high BLG for Estonian) warrant scrutiny. More detailed construction-level analysis (coordination, relative clauses) would make the errors and implications clearer.

Questions to authors
- Can you report results where ADP directionality is estimated on a held-out slice (e.g., first N sentences) rather than the full test set, and similarly remove the oracle global direction from the BL baseline? How sensitive is UDP to misestimated ADP direction?
- How does UDP fare with more realistic POS in low-resource settings (e.g., projected POS tags or multilingual taggers without target-language training)? Could you report UAS as a function of POS accuracy?
- What is the effect of removing PR personalization to the “first verb” heuristic across verb-final languages? You report a small average gain; a language-wise breakdown would help.
- Could you provide UAS without punctuation and, if possible, projective vs. non-projective breakdowns? Also, any labeled evaluation with a small set of labels to assess how readily UDP could be extended?
- How many PR iterations and what runtime are typical per sentence? Any sentence-length sensitivity?

Additional comments
- Please clarify the exact edge set used for PR graph construction (Table 1) and whether direction matters for PR. A figure contrasting your graph with Søgaard (2012b) would help.
- Some results (e.g., Estonian BLG 73.2) look surprisingly high for a rule baseline; a brief qualitative inspection would build trust.
- Minor: verify that UD’s “function words are dependents” does not strictly imply leafness; noting this nuance would temper claims and motivate your future work on MWEs/coordination.
