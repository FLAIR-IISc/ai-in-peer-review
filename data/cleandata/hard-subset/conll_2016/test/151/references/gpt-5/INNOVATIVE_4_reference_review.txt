Summary and key contributions
- A training-free UD dependency parser (UDP) that uses only POS tags and UD-imposed constraints. The core ideas are: personalized PageRank to prioritize main predicate identification and ordering of content-word attachments; a two-step decoding that first builds the content-word backbone and then attaches function words to enforce UD leafness; language-agnostic behavior by estimating adposition direction directly from the (unlabeled) test sentences.
- Extensive multilingual and cross-domain evaluation on 32 UD v1.2 treebanks showing that a purely rule/constraint-based, training-free system can be competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, with notably better robustness to domain shift and less POS-error propagation.
- Ablations that isolate the contribution of PageRank and the two-step decoding, demonstrating that personalized PR contributes substantially (~9 UAS) and that the decoding contributes beyond a strong rule baseline (~4 UAS).

Strengths
- Conceptual boldness: A truly training-free parser for UD at scale. The paper embraces the universality of UD and shows you can get surprisingly far by enforcing a few strong, linguistically grounded constraints plus a simple centrality heuristic. The on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from the test corpus is a clever, language-agnostic twist that removes hand-tuned parameters.
- Empirical punch across 32 languages: With gold POS, UDP is on average within 6.4 UAS of a supervised multi-source delex parser and wins on Hindi. With predicted POS, the drop is small (2.2 UAS), and the measured error propagation from POS to dependencies is lower for UDP (0.37) than for MSD (0.46). These are compelling results for a training-free system.
- Cross-domain robustness: The variance across domains is consistently lower than the transfer baseline in multiple languages (Table 6), and UDP sometimes outperforms MSD in harder domains (e.g., Italian europarl/legal/wiki). This supports the claim that fewer parameters and UD-driven constraints yield more stable behavior.
- Simplicity, determinism, and reproducibility: The method is compact (a short rule table, deterministic decoding, clear graph construction). Given POS and UD tagset consistency, results are reproducible and fast. The ablation (UDPNoPR) neatly quantifies where the gains come from.
- Useful baseline for UD-era parsing: Beyond novelty, the paper establishes a robust, transparent baseline that many UD researchers could use, especially in low-resource or domain-mismatched settings.

Weaknesses
- Novelty vs. prior unsupervised PR parsers: The core mechanism—PageRank-based ordering plus rule-based/constraint parsing—builds directly on Søgaard (2012b). The incremental novelties (personalized PR, two-step decoding for UD leafness, UD-adapted head rules) are sensible but not a conceptual leap. The paper’s contribution is more in demonstrating scale and robustness under UD than in algorithmic innovation.
- Test-set peeking for adposition direction: Estimating ADP direction on the test corpus (albeit unlabeled) introduces a dataset-level parameter tuned on the evaluation set, which can inflate results and complicates fair comparison. It would be stronger to either (i) estimate online per sentence, (ii) use a non-test dev/unlabeled corpus, or (iii) show the performance without this peeking.
- POS assumptions and “low-resource” claims: The predicted-POS setup trains TnT on each target language’s training set, which is not low-resource. The CF two-tag setup is too crude to be informative. For the paper’s intended use case, results with projected or cross-lingual POS taggers would be more convincing.
- Coverage limitations and evaluation scope: The system struggles with punctuation and conjunctions and cannot handle coordination, MWEs, or relative clauses (due to excluding NOUN→VERB). Only UAS is reported; LAS or even partial label accuracy would better support claims of extensibility. While acknowledged as future work, these gaps limit practical utility.
- Baselines and significance: The chosen comparative baseline (multi-source delex) is reasonable but not the strongest of the era; projection-based parsers or more recent cross-lingual approaches could be added for context. No significance testing is reported; some differences are small and may not be robust.

Questions to authors
- ADP direction estimation: Can you report results when ADP direction is estimated without using the test corpus (e.g., on a small held-out unlabeled set or sentence-by-sentence online)? How large is the UAS drop?
- Personalization sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the 5x personalization weight and to the “first verb” heuristic? Please include ablations across languages (uniform PR, different weights, different root priors).
- POS in true low-resource: Can you add results with cross-lingual/projected POS (e.g., from MUSE/UDPipe/projection) to substantiate low-resource claims?
- Runtime and scalability: What are average PR iterations and per-sentence runtime on long sentences across languages? Any convergence pathologies?
- Stability across UD versions: Have you tried UD v1.3+? Do your fixed head rules generalize as UD guidelines evolve?

Overall
This is a neat, gutsy paper: a training-free, UD-aware parser that is competitive across 32 languages and notably robust to domain shift. While the algorithmic novelty is incremental relative to prior PR-based unsupervised parsing, the scale, clarity, and demonstrated robustness make it a valuable contribution and a practical baseline. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the ADP test-set estimation and, if possible, adding a non-peeking variant.
