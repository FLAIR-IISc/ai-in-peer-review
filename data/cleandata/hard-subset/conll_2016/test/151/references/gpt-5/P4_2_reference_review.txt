Summary
The paper presents UDP, a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies exclusively on POS tags and a small set of hand-crafted, UD-compliant head rules. The method uses personalized PageRank (PR) on a sentence-level multigraph to rank content words and select the main predicate, followed by a two-step deterministic decoding that first attaches content words and then attaches function words while enforcing UD constraints (single root, function words as leaves). Adposition attachment direction (pre/post) is estimated directly from unlabeled test sentences via simple bigram statistics. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under three POS scenarios (gold, predicted with TnT, and a coarse content/function split) and compared against a multi-source delexicalized supervised baseline (MSD). Results show UDP is competitive in UAS, more robust to POS tagging errors, and more stable across domains.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, language-agnostic, rule-based UD parser that requires no parsing training data and achieves competitive multilingual UAS, with demonstrated robustness to POS errors and cross-domain shifts.
2) Methodological additions over prior unsupervised/rule-based parsers: personalized PR for root/main predicate selection; a two-step decoding enforcing UD leaf constraints for function words; and unsupervised, test-time estimation of adposition directionality.
3) An extensive multilingual and cross-domain evaluation, including ablations (quantifying PR and decoding contributions) and an analysis of POS error propagation.

Strengths
- Broad, competitive multilingual performance with no parsing training data: On 32 languages, UDPG averages 57.5 UAS vs. 63.9 for MSDG (−6.4), and it outperforms MSDG on Hindi. Under predicted POS, UDP drops only 2.2 points vs. 2.7 for MSD, indicating lower sensitivity to POS errors. This establishes a strong baseline with minimal resources.
- Robustness to domain shift: Across multiple domain splits (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, Italian, English), UDP exhibits consistently lower UAS variance than MSD (Table 6–7), supporting the claim that simple rule-based methods aligned with UD constraints generalize better across genres.
- Clear, deterministic algorithm leveraging UD constraints: The two-step decoding guarantees single-rooted trees and function-word leafness, with explicit back-off strategies to ensure connectivity. The approach is computationally simple; complexity is dominated by per-sentence PR, with decoding at O(n × |C|).
- Transparent ablation and analysis: The paper isolates contributions: removing PR reduces UAS by ~9 points; decoding and UD constraints contribute ~4 over the head-rule baseline. Additional breakdowns (root accuracy, POS-specific UAS, adposition direction estimation effectiveness) support the method’s design choices.
- Minimal linguistic parameters and language-agnostic setup: Apart from a small, shared rule set and a global PR configuration (teleport=0.05, personalization factor=5), the only language-specific signal is the unsupervised test-time estimation of ADP direction, shown to converge with few sentences.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior rule-based unsupervised work: The core idea (PR-based ranking plus rule-based decoding) builds closely on Søgaard (2012b). The novel aspects (PR personalization, two-step decoding aligned to UD, on-the-fly ADP direction) are incremental rather than fundamentally new.
- Supervision/tuning blurs the “without training” claim: Head rules, PR parameters, and functional word directionality (except ADP) were tuned on English UD dev data. While the parser itself is not trained, this constitutes supervised development and may limit the claim of fully training-free parsing across languages.
- Test-time estimation from the evaluation set: Estimating ADP direction on the test sentences (albeit unlabeled) uses target test distributions. This is reasonable for unsupervised settings but may overstate realism; reporting results using development or held-out unlabeled text for estimation would strengthen claims.
- Evaluation scope and baselines: Results focus on unlabeled UAS; no labeled attachment or structural analyses (e.g., projectivity rates) are reported. Comparisons exclude strong projection-based or recent unsupervised/induction baselines that do not require parallel data at test time. Significance testing is absent.
- Known coverage limitations: Excluding NOUN→VERB rules blocks relative clause heads; coordination and punctuation attachment are weak (CONJ and PUNCT UAS notably lower with high variance). These limitations affect key UD phenomena and may cap performance on many languages.

Questions to authors
- Supervision and tuning: Precisely which components were tuned on the English dev treebank (head rule inventory, PR teleport and personalization, function word directionality)? How many dev sentences were used? Could you report cross-validation or sensitivity to these choices?
- ADP direction estimation: How many sentences are used per language to estimate direction, and is the estimate recomputed per document/domain? Could you report UDP results when ADP direction is estimated from a held-out unlabeled corpus (not the test set)?
- Predicted POS setup: Since TnT is trained per target language, how realistic is this for low-resource settings? Could you include a projection-based or multilingual tagger scenario to complement TnT?
- Statistical testing and error analysis: Are differences between UDP and MSD significant across languages? Can you provide projectivity rates and common structural error types (e.g., coordination, subordinate clauses) to contextualize weaknesses?
- Reproducibility: Will you release the exact rule set, PR parameters, tie-breaking and back-off details, and scripts to reproduce ADP direction estimation and domain-split evaluations? What is the average runtime per sentence including PR?

Overall, the submission is clear, technically sound for a rule-based unsupervised approach, and offers a useful, robust baseline aligned with UD. Novelty is moderate but the empirical coverage and analyses are valuable.
