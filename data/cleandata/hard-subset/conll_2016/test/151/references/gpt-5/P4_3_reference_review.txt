Summary and main contributions
The paper presents UDP, a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that exploits UD’s POS-based structural constraints and a small set of hand-crafted head rules. The parser uses personalized PageRank (PR) to prioritize content words and a two-step decoding strategy that first attaches content words and then function words, enforcing the UD constraint that function words tend to be leaves. The only per-language parameter (adposition direction) is estimated directly from test data via ADP–NOMINAL bigram counts. The approach is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under three POS settings (gold, predicted, and a two-tag content/function heuristic) and compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (TurboParser).

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A strong, training-free UD parser with deterministic two-pass decoding and personalized PR, shown competitive across 32 languages and robust to domain shift, while requiring only POS tags.
2) A thorough, multi-lingual and cross-domain evaluation with ablations isolating the gains from PR and decoding constraints, including analysis of error propagation from POS tagging.
3) A simple, test-time estimate of adposition direction that removes the main language-specific parameter without external resources.

Strengths
- Broad, carefully executed empirical evaluation: The parser is tested on 32 languages and multiple domains, with three POS scenarios. UDP with gold POS is on average 6.4 UAS points below a supervised delexicalized baseline and even outperforms it on Hindi. Under predicted POS, the drop is modest (2.2%) compared to the supervised baseline (2.7%). The domain robustness analysis shows consistently lower standard deviation across domains for UDP than for the delexicalized parser (Table 6/7), supporting claims of stability.

- Clear ablations and error propagation analysis: The paper quantifies contributions: disabling PR (UDPNoPR) reduces UAS by ~9 points; the decoding/constraint step adds ~4 over the rule baseline. The POS-to-dependency error propagation is lower for UDP (0.37) than for the supervised baseline (0.46), consistent with reduced sensitivity to POS noise.

- Method aligns well with UD formalism: The two-step decoding enforces function-word leafness and single-root constraints. Personalized PR (biasing the main predicate) improves root selection, with average root accuracy around 69% and robust behavior even for verb-final languages.

- Simplicity and resource efficiency: The method requires no treebank training, uses a small rule set, and estimates the only language-specific trait (ADP direction) on the fly from minimal data (10–15 sentences). Complexity is low; decoding is O(n × |C|), with PR being the main cost.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing rule-aided unsupervised parsing: The approach is close in spirit to Søgaard (2012b), which already used PageRank without training. The innovations—PR personalization, a two-step decoding to enforce UD function-word leafness, and test-time ADP direction estimation—are reasonable but incremental. The paper would benefit from a broader comparison to recent unsupervised or projection-based methods beyond delexicalized transfer.

- Language-specific choices tuned on English: While ADP direction is estimated from test data, other key directionality settings (e.g., AUX/DET/SCONJ right-attaching; CONJ/PUNCT left-attaching) were fixed based on English development data. This reduces cross-lingual neutrality and contributes to failures (e.g., DET in Basque, 32% UAS). Given the stated aim of being language-agnostic, these attachment preferences should be estimated per language in the same unsupervised way as ADP.

- Test-time estimation on the target test set: Estimating ADP direction directly on the test data introduces adaptation to the test distribution. While unsupervised, this departs from the strict “train/dev/test” separation and complicates fairness relative to the delexicalized baseline, which does not adapt on test data. The paper notes that few sentences are needed, but does not assess sensitivity to small test sets, mixed pre/postpositional systems, or per-domain variation.

- Structural limitations of the rule set: The head rules exclude NOUN→VERB, preventing relative clause attachments; coordination and punctuation are weak (PUNCT UAS ~21%, CONJ ~38%) with large variability. These limitations cap UAS and undercut generality. The paper acknowledges them and suggests future work, but they materially affect current accuracy and coverage.

- Baseline scope and realism in “no training” claim: The main comparator is multi-source delexicalized TurboParser. No comparisons are provided to current projection-based or neural cross-lingual methods. Additionally, the predicted-POS condition trains TnT on target-language training sets, which may be unavailable in low-resource settings; the two-tag CF setup is an informative lower bound but very crude. A more realistic cross-lingual POS setup (e.g., projected or multilingual taggers) would strengthen the claims.

Questions to authors
- ADP direction estimation: Is it computed per corpus, per document, or per sentence batch? How does accuracy vary for languages with mixed adpositions or free word order? What happens on very small test sets? Could you report UAS as a function of the number of sentences used for estimation?

- Fairness and protocol: For the delexicalized baseline, was any adaptation or hyperparameter tuning performed on target dev/test? To balance test-time ADP estimation, could you instead estimate ADP direction on target dev (when available) or an external unannotated corpus?

- Sensitivity analyses: How sensitive is performance to the teleport probability (0.05) and to the 5× personalization weight? Did you try learning or validating these parameters beyond English?

- Directionality beyond ADP: Have you tried estimating attachment direction for DET/AUX/SCONJ similarly to ADP? The Basque DET case suggests per-language estimation could help.

- Non-projectivity and tree constraints: The algorithm can yield non-projective trees. What is the observed non-projectivity rate? Do cycles ever occur before the back-off, and what is the runtime behavior of the back-off in pathological cases?

- POS realism: Could you include results with cross-lingual projected POS or multilingual taggers to better reflect low-resource settings, bridging the gap between gold/predicted POS and the CF lower bound?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound for its goals, and offers a solid, well-evaluated baseline with notable robustness. The conceptual novelty is moderate, but the breadth of evaluation and the strong results without training data make it a useful contribution.
