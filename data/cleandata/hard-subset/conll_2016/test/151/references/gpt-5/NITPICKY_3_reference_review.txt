Summary
The paper presents a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD), “UDP,” that relies on POS-only constraints and simple head rules. The parser (i) uses a personalized PageRank (PR) over a rule-induced graph to rank content words and choose a main predicate, (ii) performs two-step decoding to first attach content words and then (leaf-enforced) function words, and (iii) estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data via ADP–NOMINAL vs NOMINAL–ADP bigrams. Experiments on 32 UD v1.2 languages compare UDP to a delexicalized multi-source transfer baseline and to rule-based baselines under gold, predicted, and naive content/function POS settings, including cross-domain evaluations on select languages.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, training-free, UD-aware rule-based baseline that is surprisingly competitive with delexicalized transfer across many languages, and more robust to domain shift and POS degradation.
2) A practical use of personalized PageRank over a rule-induced multigraph to guide head assignment, with ablations suggesting PR contributes substantially to UAS.
3) A cross-domain evaluation protocol for UD languages showing lower variance across domains than a delex transfer model, plus a lightweight test-time heuristic for inferring adposition direction.

Strengths
- Broad multilingual coverage and solid baselines: Results on 32 languages with both gold and predicted POS provide a wide-coverage picture. UDP with gold tags is on average within ~6.4 UAS of the delex baseline and even beats it on Hindi; predicted POS degrades modestly relative to the delex baseline, indicating resilience to POS noise.
- Domain robustness: Cross-domain experiments (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian) show markedly lower standard deviation for UDP than for the delex model (Table 6), supporting the claim that rule-based UD constraints yield more stable behavior under domain shift.
- Ablation showing PR impact: The comparison between UDP, UDP without PR, and the baseline suggests PR contributes roughly +9 UAS on average for content-word attachments, indicating the ranking mechanism is a meaningful ingredient rather than a cosmetic addition.
- Test-time adposition direction estimation: The simple language-level heuristic (ADP–NOMINAL vs NOMINAL–ADP) converges quickly (10–15 sentences) and yields high ADP UAS on average. This reduces language-specific assumptions and replaces hand-tuned head-direction parameters.
- Clarity of the high-level idea: Despite issues in presentation (see below), the main design—UD-compliant constraints, PR for predicate identification, leaf-enforcing two-phase decoding—is straightforward and conceptually grounded in UD’s design choices.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility are inadequate: The paper omits or garbles essential details. The PR graph construction is not fully specified (edge orientation and multiplicity derived from Table 1 are ambiguous); the decoding pseudocode is malformed (e.g., C = ⟨c1,…,cm⟩; F = ⟨f1,…,fm⟩ with unclear m; κ and δ back-off behavior can violate intended constraints); several figures/tables referenced are missing or truncated; complexity claims are vague (PR cost and convergence not analyzed); and the exact set of head rules (coverage, rationale for exclusions like NOUN→VERB) is underspecified. Reproducibility is further hindered by anonymized resource links and incomplete references.
- “Unsupervised” claim is overstated: English dev data is used to select/tune rules (function word directions, PR teleport prob and personalization weight), which constitutes supervision. Adposition direction is inferred directly from the test set (transductive setting). Predicted POS relies on a supervised TnT tagger trained on target-language training data. These choices blur the training-free claim and should be explicitly framed as development-time supervision and test-time transduction.
- Baselines and comparisons are limited: The delex transfer baseline (TurboParser, 20k sentences uniformly sampled) is reasonable but not necessarily state-of-the-art; no comparison to recent cross-lingual parsers (e.g., using embeddings or multilingual representations) or to stronger unsupervised baselines beyond Søgaard (closest prior). The adjacency baseline is dismissed without full reporting. No statistical significance testing is provided for the main comparisons or domain variance differences.
- Evaluation choices and fairness: Only unlabeled attachment is reported; punctuation is included despite known annotation variance and the system’s poor performance on PUNCT/CONJ, possibly inflating cross-language variability. The oracle baseline is given best per-language attachment direction, while UDP does not use such oracle information, complicating fairness. The test-time estimation of ADP direction may conflict with realistic deployment scenarios, especially for low-resource settings.
- Presentation and formatting problems: Numerous typos and broken sentences (“The the main idea…”, “constribution”, “overal”), inconsistent or incorrect table/figure references, unexplained footnotes (e.g., “2”), and many incomplete citations with placeholders (e.g., “Agić and Ljubešić.,?”, “\Q1999\E”, “http://ANONYMIZED”). For a conference submission, the paper’s current form significantly impedes careful assessment.

Questions to Authors
- PR graph: Precisely define how edges are constructed from Table 1. Are edges added from potential dependents to potential heads or vice versa? How are multiple candidate heads/dependents handled? What happens for POS not covered by rules?
- PR parameters: How sensitive are results to the teleport probability (0.05) and the personalization weight (×5)? Please provide a sensitivity analysis across several languages.
- Decoding back-off: When δ and/or κ constraints are dropped, can UD constraints (e.g., function words as leaves) be violated? How is this prevented formally?
- Full head-rule set: Please list the complete rule inventory used (including excluded rules) and justify each major exclusion (e.g., NOUN→VERB and its impact on relative clauses).
- Adposition direction: Is the direction inferred per sentence, per document, or per language? Does it rely on gold POS? How does it behave for languages with mixed adposition strategies or frequent case-marking with adpositions?
- Baselines: Why not compare to stronger cross-lingual baselines (e.g., multilingual embeddings, projected parsers, recent neural delex models)? Can you report significance tests for UDP vs. MSD per language?
- Evaluation details: Provide domain split sizes, macro vs. micro averaging choices, and clarify whether punctuation was included by design. Can you report UAS excluding PUNCT and optionally CONJ?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, rule files, and scripts to reproduce PR graphs and decoding? Please fix the anonymized links and incomplete references.

Overall, while the idea is simple and potentially useful as a robust baseline, the paper’s current methodological gaps and presentation issues make it difficult to validate and reproduce the claims. I encourage the authors to tighten the description, broaden comparisons, and release full resources.
