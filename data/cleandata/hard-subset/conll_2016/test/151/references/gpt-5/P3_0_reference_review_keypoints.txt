- Proposed a training-free UD parser using POS tags, UD-motivated head rules, personalized PageRank, two-phase decoding (content first, functions as leaves), and test-time adposition-direction estimation.
- Broad evaluation on 32 UD v1.2 languages and multiple domains showing competitive UAS versus a delexicalized transfer baseline and greater robustness to domain shifts and POS noise.
- Analysis isolates contributions of PR and decoding, and quantifies error propagation from POS to dependencies.

- Strong cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation with lower domain variance and competitive average UAS (avg gap ~6.4% with gold POS; outperforming on Hindi).
- Method is principled and UD-aligned: two-step decoding enforces function-word-as-dependent and uses UD-compatible head rules.
- Simple, interpretable, deterministic given POS, fast at decode time, few hyperparameters, and reproducible.
- Clear ablations show PR provides ≈+9 UAS and decoding ≈+4 UAS; includes root accuracies and per-POS breakdowns.
- Domain robustness is practically valuable for real-world applications with domain shifts.

- Limited methodological novelty over prior PR-based unsupervised parsing; main novelties are personalization, two-step decoding, and ADP heuristic.
- Evaluation realism concerns: predicted POS trained on target language (not low-resource), CONTENT/FUNCTION tag scenario too coarse, and ADP direction estimated transductively from the test set.
- Restricted syntactic coverage due to minimal head-rule inventory (poor handling of coordination, relative clauses, punctuation), hurting PUNCT/CONJ and relative-clause structures.
- Baseline choice and significance: primary comparison is an older delexicalized transfer parser; lacks comparisons to recent cross-lingual methods and statistical significance testing.
- “No language-specific parameters” claim is overstated since several choices were tuned on English development data.

- How sensitive are results to PR personalization weight and teleport probability across languages?
- What is the impact of estimating ADP direction on a held-out subset or per-document instead of the full test set, and how does the method handle mixed pre/postposition languages?
- Can attachment directions for other function words (DET, AUX, SCONJ, CONJ) be estimated from data instead of fixed from English, and would that improve performance?
- What is the effect of reintroducing NOUN→VERB under constrained conditions (e.g., only with SCONJ/relative pronouns) to recover relative clauses?
- Do results hold with cross-lingual POS (projected tags or multilingual taggers) instead of in-language supervised POS to reflect low-resource scenarios?

- Overall: solid, well-executed empirical study with modest novelty but a useful, robust baseline; recommend acceptance if authors clarify evaluation realism, expand baseline discussion, report parameter sensitivity/significance, and temper “no language-specific parameters” claims.
