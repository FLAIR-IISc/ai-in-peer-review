Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a truly training-free dependency parser for UD (UDP) that relies only on POS tags and UD’s structural constraints. The core idea is to operationalize UD’s design (single-root trees, function words as dependents) via: (i) a two-step decoding that first attaches content words by PageRank (PR) order and then attaches function words while enforcing leafness; (ii) personalized PR to identify the main predicate and prioritize content heads; and (iii) fully unsupervised, test-time estimation of adposition directionality from raw bigrams, eliminating language-specific parameters. The system is evaluated on 32 UD languages and across domains, and is competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer, while being more robust to domain shifts.

I see three primary contributions:
1) A training-free UD parser that explicitly leverages UD constraints with a simple, deterministic two-stage decoding and personalized PR for head selection, showing competitive accuracy across many languages.
2) A practical, unsupervised mechanism to infer adposition head-direction from test data, avoiding manual language parameters and improving universality.
3) A thorough multilingual and cross-domain evaluation demonstrating substantially higher robustness to domain shift than a strong delexicalized transfer baseline.

Strengths
1) Novel, bold direction with high conceptual clarity. The paper shows how to convert UD’s design commitments into an effective parser that requires no training data, parallel data, or lexicalization—just POS tags. Personalized PR + two-step decoding is an elegant, easily reproducible idea with clear inductive bias. This is a meaningful shift from standard cross-lingual transfer/projection approaches.

2) Competitive results at remarkable simplicity/cost. With gold POS, UDP is on average only 6.4 UAS behind multi-source delexicalized transfer, and it even outperforms it on Hindi. With predicted POS, UDP degrades less than the supervised baseline (lower error propagation from POS: 0.37 vs. 0.46), underscoring robustness to noisy tags.

3) Cross-domain robustness. The analysis across multiple genres shows consistently lower standard deviation vs. the delexicalized baseline, with cases where UDP wins on harder domains (e.g., Italian legal/europarl). This empirically supports the claim that rule-based, UD-constrained parsing can generalize more stably than data-driven transfer.

4) Clear ablations supporting design choices. PR contributes about +9 UAS over decoding-only, and decoding contributes about +4 UAS over a strong rules baseline. Root prediction improves with personalization even in verb-final languages, and personalization speeds PR convergence.

5) Language-agnostic adposition direction estimation. The test-time bigram heuristic is simple, converges quickly, and works across all languages tested (ADP UAS ~75%), removing a major source of language-specific tuning.

Weaknesses
1) “Without training” hinges on POS supervision. While the parser itself is training-free, the main experimental setup uses supervised POS taggers trained on target-language treebanks. The UDPN “content/function” proxy is too crude to substantiate the claim in realistic low-resource settings. Including results with projected or unsupervised POS would better support the scope of the claim and quantify performance in truly data-scarce scenarios.

2) Coverage and structural limitations. The fixed head rules, tuned on English dev, exclude NOUN→VERB, inhibiting relative clause structures; PUNCT and CONJ perform poorly; MWEs and coordination aren’t handled; no labeled parsing is attempted. While the paper is transparent about these, the practical impact is visible (e.g., low PUNCT UAS, inability to capture key phenomena), and no partial remedies (e.g., limited context tests, lightweight heuristics) are explored.

3) Estimating ADP direction on test data needs more scrutiny. Using the full test set to estimate directionality risks subtle dataset biases (e.g., mixed head-direction corpora, small test splits). Although unsupervised, it still “peeks” at test distribution. A per-sentence or online adaptive variant, or a development-only estimate, would strengthen the methodological story and fairness vs. supervised baselines.

4) Comparisons could be broader. The paper focuses on a strong delexicalized transfer baseline, but does not include recent projection-based or density-driven transfer systems that leverage parallel data (which the paper cites). Even if resource requirements differ, reporting on a subset where those methods are feasible would contextualize UDP’s competitiveness.

5) Clarity and presentation details. Some reference formatting is broken; Table/figure formatting is occasionally hard to follow; key implementation details are under-specified (e.g., edge weights for PR graph, tie-breaking when κ/δ fail beyond “closest,” count/proportion of non-projective attachments, runtime figures). Including these would aid reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide results with projected or unsupervised POS (e.g., bilingual projection or small seed taggers) to substantiate the training-free claim in genuinely low-resource settings?
- How stable is the ADP-direction heuristic on small or mixed-domain test sets? Have you tried per-sentence or online estimation to avoid using global test statistics?
- Can you report the rate of non-projective attachments and their effect on UAS per language?
- Could a minimal extension of the head rules to capture relative clauses (e.g., restricted NOUN→VERB under SCONJ/RELPRON contexts) improve accuracy without destabilizing the hierarchy?
- Beyond first-verb personalization, did you try alternative seeds (e.g., most-verb-like POS windows, noun-only sentences) and how sensitive is root accuracy to this choice?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting LAS or partial labeling to show feasibility beyond UAS.
- Analyze UAS by sentence length; adjacency-heavy methods often degrade on longer sentences.
- Consider excluding PUNCT from UAS or reporting with/without PUNCT for comparability.
- Please fix typos/formatting in references and ensure code/data links are provided upon publication.

Overall, I find the idea fresh and impactful: an extremely simple, training-free, UD-aware parser that is competitive and notably robust across domains. Despite limitations and some methodological questions, the conceptual contribution merits acceptance.
