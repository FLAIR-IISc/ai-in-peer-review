Summary
The paper presents UDP, a training-free, rule-based dependency parser tailored to Universal Dependencies (UD). The core idea is to leverage UD’s formal constraints and POS tags rather than data-driven learning: (i) build a dependency-prioritization over content words using personalized PageRank on a POS-rule-induced multigraph; (ii) decode in two steps so that content words attach first and function words remain leaves, aligning with UD’s head-selection conventions; and (iii) estimate adposition attachment direction on the fly from test sentences via simple bigram statistics. The system is evaluated on 32 languages (UD v1.2), with gold and predicted POS, and across domains, and is compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (MSD). UDP is competitive overall (within ~6 UAS points of MSD on average, outperforming it on Hindi), shows lower sensitivity to POS degradation than MSD, and exhibits notably lower cross-domain variance.

Claimed/actual contributions (ordered by significance)
1) A simple, language-agnostic, UD-compliant unsupervised parsing baseline that is competitive with a strong delexicalized transfer model, evaluated broadly across 32 languages and multiple domains, with ablations and POS-noise analysis.
2) A two-step decoding procedure that enforces UD leaf-status for function words and uses personalized PageRank to robustly identify high-salience content heads, including a test-time heuristic for adposition directionality that avoids language-specific parameters.
3) An extensive empirical analysis quantifying PageRank’s contribution, POS error propagation, and cross-domain robustness (lower standard deviation than MSD across languages).

Strengths
- Breadth and robustness of evaluation: The work covers 32 languages and multiple domains, and considers gold POS, realistic predicted POS, and a degraded “content/function” tagging scenario. The cross-domain analysis (with standard deviation reports) compellingly demonstrates the method’s stability relative to MSD, which is nontrivial and practically valuable.
- Simplicity with strong results: Despite using no treebank supervision and only a small set of UD-aligned head rules, UDP achieves competitive UAS against a standard multi-source delexicalized parser, and even surpasses it for Hindi. This establishes a robust, easy-to-reproduce baseline for UD parsing.
- Clear ablations and diagnostics: The paper quantifies the gain from PageRank (≈+9 UAS over UDPNoPR, and ≈+4 UAS over the oracle-direction baseline) and analyzes POS error propagation, showing smaller propagation than MSD under predicted POS. These analyses help attribute performance to specific design choices.
- Alignment with UD principles: The two-step decoding that enforces function-word leafness and the universal head rules reflect UD conventions, arguably making the approach more interpretable and portable than generic unsupervised parsers that do not exploit the annotation scheme.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The approach closely builds on Søgaard (2012), which already used head rules and PageRank in unsupervised parsing. The main differences—personalization of PageRank, explicit two-step decoding for UD leafness, and a per-language adposition direction heuristic—are sensible but incremental. The methodological contribution may be judged modest for a top-tier venue.
- Use of test data for parameter estimation and reliance on English development: Estimating adposition direction from test sentences, while unsupervised, raises concerns about “peeking” into test distributions. Additionally, parameters (e.g., teleport probability, personalization weight, and non-ADP direction constraints) are tuned on English development data; the extent of language-specific sensitivity is only partially explored. This blurs the “no training” claim and may weaken claims of full language-agnosticism.
- Evaluation scope and baselines: The comparison is primarily to a delexicalized transfer model. While appropriate for a training-free setting, the paper does not compare to recent projection-based or cross-lingual methods that exploit parallel data (even if only as reference), nor to more recent unsupervised or weakly supervised baselines. Statistical significance testing is absent. Including or discussing stronger alternatives would better contextualize the results.
- Expressivity limits of the rule set: The small, fixed rule inventory omits important phenomena (coordination scope, relative clauses, MWE handling, punctuation), which the authors acknowledge. This likely caps UAS and may bias the method toward languages or constructions that align with the rules. Morphological features—available in UD—are not used, which could aid languages with rich morphology.
- Predicted POS setup assumptions: The predicted POS experiments rely on supervised taggers trained on target-language UD training sets, which may not reflect realistic low-resource scenarios. The CF tagging setup is very coarse and may not illuminate practical low-resource performance where cross-lingual or projected POS are available.

Questions to authors
- PageRank graph construction and sensitivity: Please clarify the exact graph specification (edge directionality, weighting in the multigraph, handling of multiple eligible heads, and whether edges are typed). How sensitive are results to the 5x personalization weight and the 0.05 teleport probability? Any per-language or per-sentence tuning tried?
- Test-time estimation of adposition direction: Is using test sentences for estimating ADP direction acceptable under your evaluation protocol? Could you instead estimate direction on a small held-out development slice or by an online/within-sentence estimator? How many sentences are typically needed for stable estimates across languages?
- Tree well-formedness and non-projectivity: Do you guarantee connected, single-root trees without cycles for all sentences? Can you report the frequency of back-off activations (dropping δ, then κ) and the proportion of non-projective attachments produced?
- POS robustness: Have you tried using cross-lingual or projected POS in lieu of supervised target-language taggers to better approximate low-resource settings? How does UDP fare with such POS?
- Rule set extensibility: Did you experiment with learning directionality for other function categories (e.g., DET, AUX) in the same way as ADP? Any evidence that simple data-driven direction estimation would improve languages like Basque? Can morphology be incorporated with minimal complexity increase?
- Significance and labeling: Are the observed differences with MSD statistically significant? Also, do you foresee a straightforward extension to labeled parsing (even partially) within your framework?

Overall, this paper offers a strong, useful baseline with broad evaluation and good analysis, but its methodological novelty is limited. Strengthening the comparisons, clarifying test-time estimation choices, and extending the rule set or features would improve its impact.
