Summary and contributions
This paper presents a training-free, UD-aware dependency parser (UDP) that relies on a small set of POS-based head rules, a two-step decoding procedure enforcing function-word leafness and single-rootedness, and personalized PageRank to rank content words (for root/main predicate identification and head assignment order). Adposition attachment direction is estimated on-the-fly from test-set bigrams. The method is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages with gold and predicted POS, and across several domains, and compared to a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser baseline.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, language-agnostic, UD-compliant rule-based parser with personalized PageRank and two-step decoding that delivers competitive UAS to a strong delexicalized transfer baseline on 32 languages.
2) A broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation showing relative robustness to domain shift and to POS noise (under predicted POS).
3) Small engineering refinements (personalized PR, test-time ADP direction estimation) that improve over prior rule-aided unsupervised approaches.

Strengths
- Broad, multilingual evaluation with some cross-domain analysis: The study covers 32 languages and multiple genres, which is rare for unsupervised/rule-based work. The comparison to a well-known multi-source delexicalized baseline is informative. The reported average gap to MSD with gold POS (6.4 UAS) is reasonably small, and the performance drop with predicted POS is modest.

- Methodological clarity and simplicity: The algorithm, rules, and PR setup are clearly described and easy to reproduce. The two-step decoding enforces UD constraints (single root, function-word leaves) in a principled, interpretable way, and the approach does not depend on bitext or any training for the parser.

- Evidence that PR matters: The paper provides an ablation (UDPNoPR) showing ~9 UAS improvement with PR ranking, suggesting the ranking choice is important rather than incidental.

- Resource-light and potentially robust baseline: The technique is computationally cheap, does not require treebanks or projections for the parser, and shows lower cross-domain variance than MSD in the reported settings. This makes it attractive as a robust baseline in UD contexts.

- Attention to error propagation from POS: The analysis of POS error propagation (lower for UDP than MSD) is a useful diagnostic and relevant for low-resource scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: The approach largely builds on established ideas (rule-based head selection and PR ranking; cf. Søgaard, 2012b). Personalized PR and the two-step decoding are incremental. Head rules and parameters were tuned on English dev; ADP direction is estimated from test. Overall, the contribution is more engineering than conceptual.

- Methodological concerns about “no training” and test-time estimation: While the parser itself is training-free, the main evaluations rely on supervised POS taggers trained per language. The CF setup shows the method collapses under very weak POS supervision. Moreover, estimating ADP direction directly from the test set (even if unsupervised) is problematic; several other directions (DET/AUX/SCONJ) are fixed based on English, hurting languages like Basque. This undercuts the language-agnostic claim.

- Baseline coverage and fairness: The paper omits comparisons to stronger cross-lingual projection systems (e.g., Rasooli & Collins 2015; Ma & Xia 2014) that are standard in this space, even if they require parallel data. It also lacks comparison to more recent unsupervised parsers beyond DMV. The MSD configuration (20k sampled sentences) may not be optimal or standardized; statistical significance testing is absent.

- Coverage gaps in the rule set and structural quality: The rules do not handle key phenomena (coordination, MWEs, relative clauses, punctuation), acknowledged by the authors. Root accuracy averages ~69%, and there is no thorough typological or sentence-length analysis. Non-projective handling is only indirectly discussed. These omissions likely cap achievable accuracy and limit comparability on UD labeled metrics.

- Cross-domain analysis is underpowered: Some genre splits are small and heterogeneous (e.g., hand-annotated 50-sentence sets). Reported lower variance is interesting but not tested for significance, and domain sizes differ. It’s hard to draw strong conclusions.

Questions to authors
- What exactly was tuned on English dev (head rule inventory, PR teleport, personalization weight, function-word directions)? Can you report results when all choices are fixed a priori and no test-time estimation (e.g., ADP direction) is done?

- How is ADP direction estimated under predicted POS, and how does performance change if you estimate direction from a small unlabeled corpus rather than the test set? For languages with both pre- and postpositions, could per-lemma or per-sentence direction help?

- Can you provide stronger baselines or justify exclusions? In particular, projection-based baselines with small parallel corpora (e.g., Bible/OPUS) and a modern unsupervised parser beyond DMV. Alternatively, a stronger delex baseline (different sampling, model variants).

- Please include detailed ablations across languages: PR personalization weight and teleport probability, two-step decoding vs. single pass, and per-POS direction settings learned per language from unlabeled text (as done for ADP). How sensitive are results?

- Are your UAS numbers computed with or without punctuation per UD official conventions? You state punctuation is included; many works exclude it. Please clarify for fair comparison.

- What are the runtime/memory costs per sentence across languages, including PR, and how does graph connectivity affect convergence? This would strengthen the practical baseline claim.
