Summary and contributions
This paper proposes UDP, a rule-based, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). UDP relies on (i) a small set of UD-compliant POS head–dependent rules; (ii) personalized PageRank to rank content words and identify the main predicate; and (iii) a two-step decoding procedure that attaches content words first and then function words to enforce UD’s leaf constraint for function words. The system estimates adposition attachment direction on the fly from the test data. Evaluations on 32 languages (gold and predicted POS) and several domain splits show competitive unlabeled attachment scores (UAS) versus a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser baseline and lower sensitivity to domain shift.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) A surprisingly strong, cross-lingual rule-based baseline for UD that requires no treebank training and attains competitive UAS across many languages, including robustness to domain shift and POS noise.
2) A simple, UD-aware decoding design: personalized PageRank over a rule-induced multigraph to prioritize content words, plus a two-phase decode that enforces function-word leafness and estimates ADP direction from test data.
3) A broad, multilingual and multi-domain evaluation with ablations indicating PageRank’s contribution and analysis of error propagation from POS to dependency errors.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual and cross-domain baseline with no treebank supervision: On 32 UD v1.2 languages, UDPG (gold POS) trails a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser by only ~6.4 UAS on average and even outperforms it on Hindi. Under predicted POS, UDP’s relative drop is smaller than the supervised baseline, supporting the claim of reduced sensitivity to POS errors.
- Design tightly aligned with UD principles: The two-step decoding that first builds the content-word backbone and then attaches function words to enforce leafness is elegant and linguistically motivated by UD guidelines. Estimating ADP direction from test bigrams is simple, data-free, and effective (high ADP UAS).
- Personalized PageRank is effective and well-motivated: PR personalizes toward the estimated main predicate and ranks content words; the ablation (UDP vs. UDPNoPR) suggests ~9 UAS improvement attributable to PR. The root identification accuracy (~69% on average) supports its utility across typologies.
- Breadth and robustness of evaluation: The paper evaluates three POS regimes (gold, supervised TnT, type-based CF), includes domain splits for several languages (plus additional English datasets), and reports lower cross-domain variance than the supervised baseline. This is valuable evidence that rule-based, UD-aware systems can generalize more stably across domains.
- Clarity and reproducibility potential: The head rules and decoding algorithm are specified; ADP direction estimation and PR parameters are described. The approach is fast and simple enough to be reimplemented and deployed as a robust baseline.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The core idea (PageRank-based ranking with rule-driven decoding) closely follows Søgaard (2012b). The main technical differences—PR personalization, two-step decoding to enforce UD function-word leafness, and ADP-direction estimation—are incremental. A stronger theoretical or empirical separation from Søgaard’s approach would strengthen the contribution.
- “No training” claim is somewhat overstated: Several choices (e.g., PR teleport=0.05, personalization weight=5, and function-word default directions for AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ/PUNCT) are tuned on English development data; the head-rule set was selected to maximize dev precision. Moreover, the predicted-POS setting relies on supervised per-language taggers trained on target-language data. This undermines the zero-resource narrative, even if the parser itself is not trained on trees.
- Comparisons are limited: The baseline supervised comparator is delexicalized TurboParser trained on 20k sentences. There is no comparison to more recent cross-lingual approaches (e.g., with multilingual embeddings or projections) that are now standard in cross-lingual parsing, nor to stronger unsupervised baselines ported to UD. While the authors justify omitting DMV, including a modern unsupervised or projection-based baseline would better contextualize performance.
- Incomplete method details and sensitivity: The graph-building rules are small and hand-picked; the paper lacks a sensitivity analysis for PR parameters (teleport, personalization factor), language-specific direction settings (beyond ADP), and head-rule coverage. The back-off strategy (dropping δ/κ to ensure a tree) is not analyzed: how often does back-off trigger, and how much does it affect accuracy? A formal guarantee for acyclicity and connectedness beyond heuristics would improve rigor.
- Known limitations are not quantitatively explored: The system cannot attach verbs under nouns (no NOUN→VERB rule), hindering relative clause analyses; coordination and punctuation are weak (low UAS, high variance). The structural profile (e.g., rate of non-projectivities introduced by the two-step decode, tree depth) is not examined, and labeled attachment is entirely omitted despite plausible heuristic labeling under UD.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify exactly which parameters or rule choices were tuned on English (head rules, function-word directions, PR teleport, personalization weight). Can you provide a sensitivity analysis to show these choices are not crucial?
- How frequently does the decoding back-off drop δ and/or κ constraints, per language and POS? Does this correlate with error spikes?
- For ADP direction, do you decide once per language/test set or adapt online? How many sentences are needed for a stable estimate, and what about languages with mixed pre-/postposition patterns?
- Can you report runtime and convergence statistics for PR (number of iterations, failures, behavior on very short sentences)?
- Could you include a comparator with a stronger multi-source baseline (e.g., models leveraging multilingual word embeddings or simple projection) to better position UDP among current cross-lingual parsers?
- To better support the “no training” claim, can you evaluate UDP with projected or cross-lingual POS (no target-language POS training), following realistic low-resource setups?

Overall, this paper offers a clean, UD-aware, training-free baseline with surprisingly strong multilingual and cross-domain performance. The contribution is primarily practical and empirical rather than conceptual; strengthening comparisons and clarifying tuning and back-off behavior would significantly improve the paper.
