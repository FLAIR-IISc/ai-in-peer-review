Summary and contributions
This paper proposes an unsupervised, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies only on POS tags and UD-inspired constraints. The core ideas are: (1) use personalized PageRank (PR) on a rule-induced graph to rank content words and identify the main predicate; (2) a two-step decoding that first builds a tree over content words and then attaches function words as leaves to enforce UD’s head/dependent conventions; (3) estimate adposition head-direction (pre/postposition) on the fly from test data via simple bigram counts. The parser is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages, under gold POS, predicted POS (TnT trained per language), and a naive content/function (CF) POS setup, and compared against a multi-source delexicalized (MSD) transfer parser. It includes ablations and a cross-domain analysis.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, UD-constrained, training-free parsing algorithm that performs competitively with delexicalized transfer on UAS across a large multilingual set, and is more robust to POS noise and domain shifts.
2) An effective use of personalized PageRank targeted to content words, plus a two-phase decoding enforcing function-word leafness; the ablation shows PR contributes substantially.
3) A practical test-time heuristic for estimating adposition directionality without language-specific parameters.

Strengths
- Strong multilingual and cross-domain evaluation: The paper evaluates on 32 languages and multiple domains (with curated splits), under gold and predicted POS. Reporting both macro results and per-domain standard deviations is valuable. The finding that the method is more stable across domains than MSD is well supported by the reported standard deviations and consistent across languages.
- Clear, linguistically motivated design aligned with UD: The two-step decoding enforcing function words as leaves directly leverages UD’s design choices. The rules are easy to understand, and the head-direction estimation for ADP is simple yet effective (high ADP UAS and correct pre/postposition identification).
- Solid ablation and error analyses: The PR ablation (≈+9 UAS) and decoding contribution (≈+4 UAS over the rule baseline) quantify where gains come from. Additional analysis on root accuracy, POS-specific scores (DET, ADP, CONJ, PUNCT), and the impact of PR personalization add credibility.
- Competitive performance with minimal supervision: With gold POS, the average UAS is only 6.4 points behind MSD, and the method even outperforms MSD on Hindi. Under predicted POS, the drop is smaller for the proposed method than for MSD, with quantified “error propagation” from POS to dependencies that favors the proposed system.
- Reproducibility intent: The authors state that code and in-domain test sets will be released, and the algorithm is sufficiently specified to reimplement.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty; largely incremental over Søgaard (2012): Using PR to rank words for unsupervised parsing and rule-based decoding is known. The main novelties are PR personalization targeted at the estimated main predicate and the two-step decoding enforcing UD leafness, plus the simple ADP direction heuristic. These are sensible but incremental rather than conceptually new.
- “Without training” claim partly undermined by reliance on POS taggers and English development tuning: While the parser itself doesn’t train on trees, key components rely on supervision: predicted POS uses TnT trained on each target language; PR parameters and head-direction constraints (except ADP) are tuned on English dev; head rules were selected using English. This weakens the “no training” framing and may introduce language-specific biases, even if results are broadly positive.
- Estimating ADP direction on test data risks corpus-specific bias: Although effective and low-data, learning head direction from the test set can adapt to corpus idiosyncrasies rather than the language, conflating language and domain (the authors partly acknowledge cross-domain concerns). A more principled unsupervised approach (e.g., from unlabeled raw text outside the test fold) would be cleaner.
- Baselines and evaluation choices could be stronger: 
  - The supervised comparison (MSD) is a reasonable delex baseline, but excluding projection-based baselines or more recent cross-lingual systems limits context. 
  - Results include punctuation in UAS; many UD evaluations exclude punctuation. Reporting both would help comparability. 
  - The adjacency baseline is only described qualitatively; actual numbers would better ground claims. 
  - Only UAS is reported; no partial labeling or LAS to assess whether UD constraints could translate into labeling gains.
- Limited coverage of key constructions and resulting errors: The rules intentionally disallow NOUN→VERB heads, so relative clauses cannot be captured; coordination and punctuation attachments are notably weak, and these phenomena are pervasive in UD. While acknowledged as future work, the impact on downstream quality is not deeply analyzed.

Questions to authors
- Personalization and PR sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the 5x personalization weight and teleport probability 0.05? Did you try sentence-level vs corpus-level personalization schemes or different priors for verb-final languages?
- ADP direction estimation protocol: Is direction estimated once per language on the whole test set (risking peeking) or incrementally within a fold? Would using raw text from the same language (but outside test) change outcomes? How many sentences do you actually need across languages for stable estimates?
- POS settings and the “without training” claim: Can you clarify the intended use case? If training a tagger on target-language UD is allowed, the setting is not fully training-free. If not, can you show results with cross-lingual projected POS or unsupervised POS induction to better match the claim?
- Punctuation and comparability: Can you provide UAS excluding punctuation to align with common UD reporting? Also, any LAS or partial labeling experiments to validate the claim that labels could be “easily” added?
- Fairness of MSD baseline: Why 20k training sentences fixed across languages? Is TurboParser tuned per source? How do results vary with different source selections or data sizes?

Overall evaluation
The paper is well written, carefully evaluated, and makes a practical, surprisingly strong case for a simple UD-aware, training-free approach. Its main value is empirical: broad multilingual and cross-domain robustness with minimal supervision. The conceptual novelty is moderate and builds on prior PR-based unsupervised parsing and simple head rules, but the UD-specific design choices and ablations are meaningful. Addressing the concerns about POS supervision and test-time direction estimation, and strengthening baselines and reporting, would further solidify the contribution.
