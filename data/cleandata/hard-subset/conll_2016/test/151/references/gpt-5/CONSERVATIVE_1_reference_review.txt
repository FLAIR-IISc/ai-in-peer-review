Summary
The paper proposes a simple, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies on a small set of POS-based head rules, a two-step decoding scheme that attaches content words before function words (to enforce UD leaf constraints), and personalized PageRank to prioritize likely heads (especially the main predicate). Adposition attachment direction is estimated on-the-fly from bigram statistics on the test data. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 treebanks under three POS settings (gold, predicted with TnT, and a crude content/function split), compared against a rule-based baseline and a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser. The authors also present a cross-domain robustness analysis on several languages.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A strong unsupervised, training-free baseline for UD parsing that achieves UAS close to a standard multi-source delexicalized transfer model across many languages, and shows lower variance under domain shift.
2) An effective use of UD constraints in a two-step, deterministic decoding procedure combined with (personalized) PageRank to identify content-word heads, notably improving over prior rule-only unsupervised parsers.
3) A broad multilingual evaluation (32 languages) and a cross-domain analysis suggesting improved robustness relative to a supervised transfer baseline.

Strengths
- Broad, careful multilingual evaluation and analysis: Results across 32 languages with both gold and predicted POS, plus domain splits for several languages, provide a good picture of where the approach works and where it fails. The stability to domain shift (lower standard deviation than the delexicalized baseline) is noteworthy and practically relevant.
- Clear, simple method with transparent inductive bias: The approach is easy to understand and implement, deterministic given POS tags, and leverages UD’s formal constraints (single root, function words as leaves) in a principled way. This is valuable as a robust baseline and diagnostic tool for UD parsing research.
- Solid ablations and error breakdowns: The paper isolates the contribution of PageRank versus rule-only decoding, quantifies root accuracies, and discusses POS-specific behaviors. The PR personalization effect and the two-step decoding contribution are convincingly demonstrated.
- Practicality and resource efficiency: No training data for the parser itself, minimal language-specific parameters, and on-the-fly estimation of ADP direction from a small amount of test data (10–15 sentences) make the method attractive in low-resource or rapid-deployment scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing: The core ingredients—rule-based constraints plus PageRank ordering—are known (cf. Søgaard 2012b). The new elements (PR personalization, strict two-step decoding enforcing UD leaf constraints, test-time ADP direction estimation) seem incremental; the paper positions them as differences but does not convincingly argue they are conceptually novel, rather than pragmatic tweaks.
- Dependence on POS quality and UD compliance: Despite the “no training” claim, the method relies critically on POS tags. With predicted POS, performance drops are moderate but still significant, and the crude CONTENT/FUNCTION tagging severely degrades results. This undermines the claimed universality in genuinely low-resource settings where POS taggers may be poor or unavailable. Moreover, cross-treebank inconsistencies in UD POS/rel conventions (e.g., AUX/VERB, DET/PRON) likely affect outcomes.
- Evaluation choices limit comparability: Only unlabeled attachment is reported; labeled accuracy is not explored even though UD provides labels and the proposed rules imply strong label priors. Punctuation is not excluded—unlike much UD evaluation—hurting comparability to prior work. There is no statistical significance testing. The primary supervised baseline is multi-source delexicalized TurboParser trained on 20k sentences; stronger and more recent cross-lingual baselines (e.g., projection-based or more modern transfer approaches) are not included, making the competitiveness claim less compelling.
- Test-time parameter estimation on the test set: Estimating ADP direction from test bigrams is unsupervised but still a form of fitting on the test data. This could bias results and complicates fair comparisons. Languages with mixed/pre/postpositional behaviors may be poorly handled by a single global direction.
- Incomplete coverage and acknowledged structural limitations: The rule set is small and excludes phenomena like coordination scope and relative clauses (explicitly, NOUN→VERB is removed, hindering relative clause attachments). These omissions cap upper-bound performance and raise the question of how robust the method is beyond the reported datasets. Several typos/inconsistencies (e.g., example text referring to “come” vs “had”) and partial rule descriptions also hamper clarity.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report UAS/LAS with the standard UD evaluation settings (e.g., excluding PUNCT and optionally excluding dep/flat tokens), to enable comparisons with prior UD parsing literature?
- How often does the ADP direction estimation pick the “wrong” majority for languages with mixed pre/postpositions, and what is the impact if you estimate direction on a small development portion rather than on test? Could you estimate directions per document to avoid cross-domain mixing?
- How sensitive are results to the specific head rules tuned on English? If you tune rules on a typologically different language or do leave-one-language-out tuning, does performance change materially?
- Can you provide significance tests for the main comparisons (UDP vs MSD), and a more extensive ablation isolating the effects of the two-step decoding (leaf enforcement) separately from PR and from direction constraints?
- What is the runtime profile versus the delexicalized baseline? PR can be a bottleneck; can you quantify average sentence-level runtime and memory across languages?

Additional comments
- Please clarify whether the rule set in Table 1 is exhaustive and provide a full list in the appendix/repo. Some descriptions (e.g., ADJ→ADV) are surprising and merit justification.
- Consider adding a small labeled prediction module (even rule-based) to demonstrate feasibility of producing labeled UD trees, as this would improve utility.
