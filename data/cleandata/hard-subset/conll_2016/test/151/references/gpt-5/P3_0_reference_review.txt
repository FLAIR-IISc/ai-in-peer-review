Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies solely on POS tags and UD-motivated constraints. The method builds a graph from a small set of POS-based head rules, uses personalized PageRank (PR) to rank content words, and decodes in two phases: attaching content words first (to respect single-rootedness) and then attaching function words as leaves (to enforce UD’s function-word-as-dependent constraint). Head direction for adpositions is estimated on the fly from ADP–NOMINAL bigrams in the test data; fixed attachment directions for other function words are set globally. The approach is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages with gold and predicted POS, compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline, and analyzed for domain robustness.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, language-agnostic, training-free UD parser that integrates personalized PR and a two-step decoding scheme to enforce UD constraints, plus a test-time heuristic to estimate adposition direction.
2) A broad multilingual and multi-domain evaluation showing competitive unlabeled attachment score (UAS) to a delexicalized transfer baseline, with stronger robustness to domain shifts and to POS noise.
3) Analysis isolating the impact of PR and decoding, and error propagation from POS to dependencies.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation: The parser is tested on 32 languages and multiple domains. The results are competitive with a well-known delexicalized transfer baseline (avg gap ~6.4% UAS with gold POS; outperforming on Hindi), and the standard deviations across domains are consistently lower than the baseline’s. This supports the claim that UD-guided rule-based parsing can be robust to domain shift.
- Method aligns with UD principles: The two-step decoding that ensures function words are leaves and the explicit use of UD-compatible head rules reflect a principled design that takes advantage of UD’s cross-linguistic consistency, rather than trying to learn these constraints indirectly.
- Simplicity and interpretability: The algorithm is easy to understand, deterministic given POS, and fast at decode time. Hyperparameters are few and explicitly stated (PR teleport 0.05; personalization weight 5). This makes the work reproducible and the error analysis straightforward (e.g., low PUNCT and CONJ UAS are expected given the rules).
- Clear ablations and analysis: The paper isolates the contribution of PR (≈+9 UAS over decoding alone) and of decoding over the baseline (≈+4 UAS), reports root accuracies, error propagation from POS noise, and per-POS breakdowns (e.g., ADP direction estimation working well for most languages).
- Domain robustness finding is practically relevant: The lower variance across domains (even with predicted POS) is valuable for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: Søgaard (2012b) already used PR for unsupervised parsing; here the main novelties are PR personalization, a two-step decoding enforcing UD leafness, and test-time ADP direction estimation. These are sensible but incremental methodological tweaks rather than a fundamentally new approach. The paper’s primary contribution is the scope and quality of the evaluation rather than algorithmic innovation.
- Evaluation realism and fairness: The “predicted POS” uses TnT trained on each target language’s training data. This is not representative of truly low-resource or cross-lingual settings. The naive CONTENT/FUNCTION tag scenario is too coarse to be informative. A more realistic middle ground (e.g., cross-lingual projected tags or multilingual POS taggers) would better support claims about robustness versus transfer parsers. Also, estimating ADP direction from the test set is a transductive choice; its impact when test sets are very small (or consist of mixed head-directions) is unclear.
- Restricted coverage of syntactic phenomena: The minimal head-rule inventory (Table 1) does not cover key constructions (coordination, relative clauses, punctuation). The explicit decision to exclude NOUN→VERB rules precludes relative clause structures; PUNCT and CONJ attachments are weak. While the simplicity is a design choice, the paper should quantify the impact on overall UAS and discuss ways to recover these cases without sacrificing generality.
- Baseline scope and significance: The main baseline is a 2011 delex transfer model (TurboParser). More recent cross-lingual parsers (e.g., those leveraging projection or modern multilingual representations) are cited but not compared, even when they require different resources. At least a discussion of resource-compatibility and expected performance differences would strengthen the contextualization. Statistical significance of the UAS differences (especially in domain splits) is not reported.
- “No language-specific parameters” claim is somewhat overstated: Although the method does not learn from treebanks, several choices were tuned on English development data (e.g., fixed attachment directions for AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ, rule selection). This does not make the method language-specific, but it is corpus-tuned; clarifying this would avoid confusion.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to the PR personalization weight and teleport probability? Can you report an ablation across values and per-language variance?
- The ADP direction heuristic is estimated on the test set. How does performance change if it is estimated on a small held-out subset (or per-document) to avoid full-test transduction? What happens in languages/datasets with mixed pre/postpositions?
- Could you estimate attachment direction for other function words (DET, AUX, SCONJ, CONJ) from the data as you do for ADP, rather than fixing them based on English? Any gains?
- How does the parser perform if you re-introduce NOUN→VERB under constraints (e.g., only with SCONJ/REL pronouns) to recover relative clauses? A targeted ablation could show whether this helps without hurting overall robustness.
- Would results and conclusions hold with cross-lingual POS (e.g., projected tags, multilingual taggers) instead of in-language supervised POS? This would better reflect low-resource scenarios and could change the relative robustness to POS noise.

Overall, this is a solid, carefully executed study with broad multilingual and multi-domain evaluation. The methodological novelty is modest, but the empirical findings on robustness and the simplicity of the approach make it a useful baseline and a compelling argument for exploiting UD structural constraints. I recommend acceptance if the authors clarify the evaluation realism, expand the discussion of baselines and parameter choices, and temper claims about “no language-specific parameters.”
