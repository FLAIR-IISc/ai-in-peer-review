Summary and main contributions
This paper presents UDP, a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that relies only on POS tags and a small set of UD-compliant head rules. The core ideas are:
1) A fully unsupervised, language-agnostic parsing pipeline that exploits UD constraints, notably a two-step decoding enforcing function-word leafness and single-rootedness.
2) Personalized PageRank over a rule-induced graph to identify main predicates and prioritize content-word attachment, with a simple, test-time estimation of adposition head-direction (pre/post) from POS bigrams.
3) Broad evaluation across 32 UD v1.2 languages and multiple domains, showing competitive UAS to a strong multi-source delexicalized baseline, better robustness to domain shifts, and lower error propagation from POS errors.

In my view, the primary contribution is the bold, conceptually clean zero-training approach that operationalizes UD’s design principles into an effective parser. Secondary contributions include the personalized PageRank for predicate identification and the test-time estimation of head direction, and finally the extensive multilingual and cross-domain evaluation.

Strengths
- Novel and bold direction: A practical UD parser without any training. Turning UD constraints into an operational parser is conceptually fresh and impactful. It challenges the assumption that cross-lingual parsing must rely on supervision, projection, or transfer. This feels like a new baseline direction for truly low-resource settings, with the added benefit of interpretability.

- Competitive accuracy with extremely low resource demands. With gold POS, the system averages within ~6.4 UAS points of a multi-source delexicalized transfer model and even outperforms it on Hindi. With predicted POS, UDP degrades less than the supervised baseline (error propagation 0.37 vs. 0.46), suggesting robustness to noisy POS—important in real-world low-resource scenarios.

- Cross-domain robustness. UDP exhibits consistently lower UAS variance across domains than the delexicalized baseline, for both gold and predicted POS. This stability is valuable for deployment and supports the claim that rule-based, constraint-driven systems overfit less to training domain specifics.

- Clever use of personalized PageRank and two-step decoding. The personalization (favoring the estimated main predicate) and the content-first, function-second decoding to enforce UD leafness are elegant design choices that translate UD principles directly into the algorithm. The ablation indicating ~9 UAS from PageRank is persuasive.

- Simple, test-time estimation of adposition direction that works. Estimating pre/post-position predominance from POS bigrams on the test set is a neat, low-data trick that converges quickly and yields strong ADP attachment performance.

Weaknesses
- Incremental relative to prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing. The approach is close in spirit to Søgaard (2012b), and the novelty hinges on UD-tailored head rules, enforcing function-word leafness via two-step decoding, and PageRank personalization. While I find the overall combination strong and timely in the UD era, the delta over earlier rule+PR paradigms is not huge methodologically. A stronger differentiation via more ablations (e.g., different PR graphs, without leafness constraint, alternative head-rule sets) would further cement novelty.

- Reliance on language-specific POS taggers for predicted-POS setup. Predicted POS is obtained by training TnT per language, which reduces the claim of zero-resource applicability for many target languages. The CF scenario is too crude to be conclusive. Including cross-lingual or projected POS taggers (or multilingual pretrained taggers) would better represent truly zero/low-resource conditions.

- Head rules tuned on English and limited phenomena coverage. The rules exclude NOUN→VERB, preventing relative clause structures, and coordination/punctuation remain weak (PUNCT UAS ~21%, CONJ ~38%). While the authors acknowledge this and suggest future extensions, this limits applicability on diverse syntactic phenomena. Expanding rule estimation (e.g., DET/AUX direction) from data like ADP would improve generality.

- Estimation on test data and fairness concerns. While unsupervised methods commonly use test text statistics, it would be good to clarify that adposition direction is estimated only from POS sequences (no labels) and to quantify sensitivity when very few sentences are available or for languages with minimal ADP. For completeness, reporting a dev-set estimation variant (when available) would address any concerns.

- Missing comparisons to stronger modern baselines. The supervised baseline is a classic delexicalized transfer system. It would strengthen the case to include comparisons to recent projection-based or cross-lingual neural baselines (even if only on a subset), or at least to stronger unsupervised baselines beyond adjacency and DMV, to contextualize the gains.

Questions to authors
- Can you report results using cross-lingual or multilingual taggers (e.g., projected POS, or a multilingual POS model) to better simulate zero-resource setups where target-language POS training is unavailable?
- How sensitive is adposition direction estimation to short test sets and to languages with mixed or weakly headed ADP behavior? Do you fall back to a default estimation when ADP is rare?
- Could you provide ablations on: (a) removing the function-word leafness constraint, (b) alternative PR graph construction (e.g., adding/removing certain edges), and (c) estimating DET/AUX direction per language?
- Have you tried an edge-ranking variant (edge reification) as suggested in future work? Even preliminary results on a subset would be valuable.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. Despite some methodological continuity with prior rule-based unsupervised parsing, the UD-centric reframing, the personalized PageRank, and the test-time head-direction estimation form a compelling, minimalist parser that is surprisingly competitive and notably robust across domains and POS quality. The paper opens a useful, bold path for zero-training UD parsing and provides strong empirical evidence across many languages.
