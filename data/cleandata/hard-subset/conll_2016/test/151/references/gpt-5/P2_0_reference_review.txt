Summary and contributions
This paper presents UDP, a training-free, rule-based dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that relies only on POS tags. It builds a graph using a small set of UD-motivated head–dependent POS rules, ranks content words with personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate and head priority, and decodes in two stages to enforce UD’s “function words as leaves” constraint. It estimates ADP head-direction (pre/postposition) directly from test-set bigrams. Experiments on 32 UD v1.2 languages compare UDP to a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser and a rule-based baseline under gold POS, predicted POS (TnT per-language), and a coarse content/function tag scenario. Additional analysis considers domain shifts across several genres. Ablations quantify the contribution of PR and two-step decoding.

Contributions as I see them (ordered by strength):
1) A simple, UD-compliant, training-free parser that is competitive with delexicalized transfer across 32 languages, with strong robustness to domain shift. With gold POS, UDP reaches 57.5 UAS on average vs. 63.9 for MSD; with predicted POS, the drop is small (2.2 for UDP vs. 2.7 for MSD), and UDP even beats MSD on Hindi.
2) A principled use of personalized PageRank to prioritize content words and pick roots within a UD rule-based decoding framework, with clear ablation showing +9 UAS from PR and +4 from decoding over a strong rule baseline.
3) A simple test-time estimation of ADP head-direction and an informative domain-robustness analysis demonstrating lower cross-domain variance than the delexicalized baseline.

Strengths
- Breadth and thoroughness of evaluation:
  - 32 languages with standard UAS on full test sets; three POS settings (gold, predicted, coarse CF); domain split evaluation across multiple genres. The domain analysis reports per-language standard deviations (e.g., average std 2.5 for UDPG vs. 4.5 for MSDG), substantiating the robustness claim.
- Strong, competitive baseline for a training-free method:
  - UDPG is on average within 6.4 UAS of the supervised delexicalized MSDG and surpasses it on Hindi. Under predicted POS, UDP’s error propagation from POS is smaller (0.37 vs. 0.46), quantitatively supporting robustness to noisy tags.
- Clear modeling aligned with UD constraints:
  - Two-step decoding directly enforces UD’s function-word leaf constraint; estimating ADP direction on-the-fly is simple and effective (ADP UAS ~75%). The approach is interpretable and linguistically motivated.
- Informative ablations and diagnostics:
  - The paper isolates the impact of PR (+9 UAS) and decoding (+4 UAS over the rule baseline), reports root accuracy (~69% average, with outliers), and discusses where the rules fail (e.g., PUNCT, coordination, relative clauses).
- Practicality and efficiency:
  - No training data required for parsing; sub-quadratic decoding; minimal per-language customization. The method is attractive as a robust baseline or fallback in low-resource or cross-domain settings.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work:
  - Using PageRank for word ranking in unsupervised parsing and rule-based decoding follows Søgaard (2012b). The main additions—personalization for root bias, two-step decoding to enforce UD leafness, and ADP direction estimation—are sensible but incremental rather than conceptually new.
- Dependence on supervised POS taggers undermines “without training” in realistic low-resource settings:
  - Predicted POS uses TnT trained per language on UD training data. While the parser itself is training-free, the primary reported setting depends on supervised POS, weakening claims of applicability to truly low-resource languages. The CF setting is acknowledgedly crude; there is no evaluation with cross-lingual projected POS or zero-shot tagging to better simulate low-resource constraints.
- Potential concerns about parameter selection and data usage:
  - English dev data is used to select head rules and tune PR/personalization and function-word directions (except ADP). ADP direction is estimated directly on test sentences (10–15 sentences suffice), which, while unsupervised, is still test-time parameter setting and may blur clean separation between setup and evaluation. Baseline BL is given an oracle left/right direction per language, whereas UDP fixes directions per POS from English dev for all languages, potentially disadvantaging UDP or making comparisons uneven.
- Coverage and phenomenon gaps:
  - The rules exclude NOUN→VERB heads, precluding relative clause attachments; coordination and punctuation remain weak (PUNCT UAS ~21, CONJ ~38) and MWEs are not handled. Only unlabeled attachment is reported; labeled performance is not explored despite UD labels being central to downstream use.
- Baseline scope and statistical rigor:
  - MSD configuration (TurboParser with 20k delexicalized sentences) is reasonable but not necessarily state-of-the-art for transfer; no comparison to more recent projection-based or cross-lingual representation baselines. No significance testing is reported; it is unclear whether observed differences (especially domain-wise) are statistically reliable.

Questions to authors
- PageRank specifics:
  - How are multigraph edges weighted (all unit weights)? Do multiple eligible dependents create parallel edges, and how does that affect PR in practice? What convergence criterion and maximum iterations are used, and is runtime negligible vs. decoding?
  - How sensitive are results to the personalization weight (set to 5) and teleport probability (0.05)? Did you try tuning these on languages other than English?
- Directionality estimation:
  - For ADP direction, what tokenization and filtering are applied to compute ADP–NOMINAL vs. NOMINAL–ADP bigrams (e.g., punctuation, sentence boundaries, MWEs)? Is the estimate sentence-internal only? Do you ever flip direction mid-document if estimates change with more sentences?
  - For DET, AUX, SCONJ, CONJ, you fix directions from English dev. Could you estimate these per language on the fly like ADP? Do you have evidence this would improve DET in Basque or other outliers?
- POS assumptions and low-resource applicability:
  - Given reliance on supervised TnT per language for predicted POS, how do you envision using UDP when no target-language POS training data exists? Could you report results with cross-lingual projected or multilingual taggers to simulate this?
- Decoding details:
  - How are ties broken when multiple candidate heads have equal distance and satisfy κ and δ? What is the observed rate of back-off (dropping δ or both δ and κ), and how does that correlate with errors?
- Baselines and significance:
  - Did you run statistical tests for differences vs. MSD across languages and domains? Could you include comparisons to other transfer baselines (e.g., multi-source with typological features, projection-based) to contextualize performance?

Overall, the paper is well-executed, empirically solid across many languages and domains, and offers a strong, interpretable, training-free baseline aligned with UD principles. Its novelty is incremental, and the reliance on supervised POS limits its low-resource claims, but the demonstrated robustness and clarity of analysis make it a valuable contribution.
