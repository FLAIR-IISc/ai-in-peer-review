Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes UDP, a training-free dependency parser for UD that relies on (i) a small set of UD-compliant POS head rules and hard constraints (single root; function words as dependents), (ii) personalized PageRank (PR) over a rule-induced graph to rank content words and choose the main predicate, and (iii) a two-step decoding that first attaches content words and then function words, with adposition attachment direction inferred directly from test data via ADP–NOMINAL bigram statistics. The system uses only POS tags and simple directionality heuristics; it is language-agnostic and has no learned language-specific parameters. Evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 treebanks, UDP approaches multi-source delexicalized transfer (MSD) performance and is notably more robust across domains and to POS noise.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, UD-aware, training-free parsing algorithm that enforces function-word leafness and uses personalized PR to select and rank content heads.
2) A practical, language-agnostic mechanism to infer adposition directionality from test data, avoiding language-specific tuning.
3) A broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation showing competitive UAS with strong robustness to domain shift and POS degradation.

Strengths
- Clear and linguistically grounded design: The two-step decoding that first builds a content-word backbone and then attaches function words closely matches UD design principles. Enforcing function-word leafness yields well-formed, interpretable trees with minimal machinery.

- Strong multilingual coverage and robustness: Results across 32 languages with both gold POS and predicted POS are thorough. The drop from gold to predicted POS is small (2.2% absolute on average), and lower error propagation than MSD is informative. The cross-domain analysis (standard deviations per domain) convincingly shows that UDP is less brittle than MSD across genres.

- Personalized PageRank is effective and well-motivated: The PR personalization for the main predicate improves root detection and attachment quality; the ablations (UDP vs. UDPNoPR) show substantial gains (≈9 UAS on average due to better content-word attachment), with additional speed/convergence benefits.

- Language-agnostic head-direction estimation: Estimating ADP direction from test data is simple, converges with minimal evidence, and yields strong ADP UAS. This reduces reliance on language-specific metadata and demonstrates a clean unsupervised test-time adaptation.

- Transparent error analysis: The paper candidly discusses limitations (e.g., coordination, punctuation, relative clauses) and reports POS-wise accuracies and outliers, which increases the credibility of findings.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing: Søgaard (2012b) also uses PR-based ranking and rule guidance. The main novelties here—personalization of PR and two-step decoding for UD leaf constraints—are useful but modest. A direct controlled comparison to Søgaard’s method on UD data (with the same POS and evaluation protocol) is missing; relying on a custom baseline and an older MSD baseline weakens claims of novelty and competitiveness.

- “Without training” claim is somewhat overstated: Head rules and parameters (teleport probability, personalization weight, direction constraints for AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ/PUNCT) are tuned on English dev data, and predicted-POS experiments require supervised taggers per language. While the parser itself is training-free with respect to dependency trees, the dependence on POS taggers and English-based rule selection blurs the “no training” message. The CF-tag experiment is too crude to be an informative lower bound.

- Baseline and evaluation choices could be stronger: MSD (McDonald et al., 2011) is a classic baseline but dated compared to more recent transfer methods (e.g., Ma & Xia, 2014; Rasooli & Collins, 2015). Also, UDP sometimes underperforms the rule baseline with gold POS (e.g., Estonian, Tamil), but this is not analyzed in depth. Punctuation is included in UAS, which is not the standard UD evaluation setting and complicates comparability. Significance testing is not reported.

- Limited linguistic coverage: By design, excluding NOUN→VERB heads precludes many relative clause attachments; coordination and punctuation are weak points with high variance. The fixed directionality for several function POS (set on English) causes notable errors in languages like Basque. The fallback that drops constraints to guarantee a tree may violate UD preferences in corner cases; the frequency and impact of such backoffs are not quantified.

- Methodological details and sensitivity: The PR graph can be disconnected; the choice of teleport=0.05 and personalization weight=5 is ad hoc, and sensitivity analyses are minimal. The frequency of non-projective outputs and how often backoff steps overrule δ/κ constraints are not reported. These details matter for understanding failure modes and reproducibility.

Questions to the authors
- Can you report results excluding punctuation (standard UD scoring) and provide significance tests for UDP vs. MSD, especially on language-average UAS?

- How often do you invoke the fallback that drops δ (head rules) and/or κ (direction) constraints? Do such backoffs correlate with specific POS or languages?

- How frequently does the algorithm produce non-projective arcs? Any correlation with language typology?

- Can you quantify sensitivity to the PR teleport probability and the personalization weight across several languages?

- Could you include a direct comparison to Søgaard (2012b) re-implemented on UD with the same POS setups, to isolate the effect of personalization and the two-step decoding?

- For languages where BLG > UDPG (e.g., Estonian, Tamil), what are the main error sources? Would estimating directionality for DET/SCONJ/CONJ from test data (as for ADP) recover performance?

- How fair is it to estimate ADP direction on the test set? Would using a held-out development portion change results materially?

- Do you have statistics on root selection accuracy by clause type (e.g., V-final vs. V-initial languages)? Does personalization ever hurt in verb-final languages?

Overall, this is a solid and thoughtfully evaluated paper. The approach is simple, broadly applicable, and demonstrates valuable robustness. To strengthen the case, I recommend adding a stronger prior-art baseline, clarifying evaluation choices (punctuation; significance), and deepening the analysis of failure modes and parameter sensitivity.
