Summary and contributions
This paper presents UDP, a rule-based, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that relies only on POS tags and a small set of UD-compliant head–dependent rules. The core ideas are: (i) two-step decoding that first attaches content words using a personalized PageRank ordering, then attaches function words to enforce their leaf status under UD; (ii) estimating adposition directionality directly from test data via simple bigram counts; and (iii) no language-specific parameters beyond those inferred at test time. The parser is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages with gold and predicted POS, compared to a multi-source delexicalized (MSD) baseline, and in multiple domains.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first):
1) A simple, language-agnostic, UD-aware rule-based parsing framework that is competitive across many languages and robust to domain shift, despite not using treebank training.
2) A practical use of personalized PageRank to order content words for head attachment, with an ablation demonstrating sizable gains over non-PR ranking.
3) A lightweight estimation of ADP attachment direction from raw test data and broader analysis of POS-level behavior and error propagation.

Strengths
- Breadth and robustness of evaluation: The paper evaluates on 32 languages and multiple domains (including curated splits), and reports results for gold POS, predicted POS, and an extreme content/function (CF) tagging regime. The cross-domain analysis (std. dev. comparisons) convincingly shows the rule-based method’s stability relative to a delexicalized model, which is valuable for practical deployment.

- Strong, simple baseline with clear ablations: The comparison among BL, UDPNoPR, and UDP separates the effect of PageRank from the structural constraints. The reported average +9 UAS points from PR (and +4 over BL from decoding) quantify the contribution of the method’s components.

- UD-aware design choices: The two-step decoding that enforces function-word leafness, the single-root constraint, and the limited, interpretable head rules are well aligned with UD guidelines. The test-time estimation of ADP direction is simple, effective (high ADP UAS), and removes the need for language-specific tuning.

- Sensible error propagation analysis: Measuring how POS errors translate to dependency errors, and comparing UDP to MSD, is informative and supports the claim that UDP degrades more gracefully with predicted POS.

- Clarity of the algorithm and determinism: The decoding algorithm and constraint/back-off mechanisms are described clearly and yield valid trees deterministically from POS sequences. This makes the approach reproducible and easy to reason about.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing: The approach builds substantially on Søgaard (2012) and related work. The two-step decoding and personalization are incremental refinements rather than a conceptual leap. While the broad UD evaluation and domain robustness are valuable, methodological novelty is modest.

- Performance lags supervised transfer on average: UDPG trails MSDG by ~6.4 UAS on average, and UDPP trails MSDP by ~5.9. The claim of “compares favorably” is somewhat overstated: it wins on Hindi but loses on most languages. A more direct comparison to modern cross-lingual baselines (beyond multi-dir MSD) or stronger unsupervised parsers would strengthen the case.

- “Without training” is somewhat misleading: The method uses English development data to select head rules and tune PR parameters, and UDPP uses supervised POS taggers trained on target-language training sets. The test-time estimation of ADP direction also uses the target test distribution. While still data-light, the framing could be more precise about what is (not) trained.

- Rule coverage and known blind spots: Excluding NOUN→VERB heads precludes handling relative clauses and likely hurts in languages with frequent nominalized/verbal relations. The fixed directions for some function words based on English dev data (e.g., DET) underperform on Basque and possibly other languages. Estimating such settings per language (as done for ADP) could improve performance.

- Missing or thin comparisons and details: DMV is dismissed, but other recent unsupervised or weakly supervised baselines are absent. More detail on graph construction (edge weights, multiplicity), PR sensitivity (teleport probability), runtime, and tree well-formedness (e.g., projectivity rates) would improve reproducibility and understanding.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the teleport probability and the personalization magnitude (weight=5)? Could you report a small sensitivity analysis across a few languages?

- For languages where DET or AUX attachment direction differs from English, have you tried estimating these directions from test data like ADP? If so, what is the impact?

- How often does the back-off (dropping δ and/or κ) trigger, and what structures does it primarily create? Any effect on non-projectivity rates?

- Can you report projective vs. non-projective attachment rates and whether enforcing projectivity would help or hurt?

- How does UDP perform with cross-lingual predicted POS (e.g., projected tags) rather than supervised TnT trained on target data? This would better reflect extremely low-resource scenarios.

- Is the code and the domain-split data publicly available (post-review), and are the rule sets and parameters fully specified there?

Additional suggestions
- Explore per-language estimation of other function-word directions (DET, AUX) from POS-only statistics to reduce English-specific bias.

- Consider adding a small set of rules to capture relative clauses or introducing a safer NOUN⇄VERB rule conditioned on local patterns.

- Provide labeled attachment for a subset of relations that the rules already imply (e.g., case/det/aux), to demonstrate easy extensibility to labeled parsing.

- Include stronger or more recent baselines (if available) to position the contribution fairly.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents a well-executed, simple, and broadly evaluated UD-aware rule-based parser with nice robustness properties. However, the methodological novelty over prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing is limited, and the average performance still trails a standard delexicalized transfer baseline. With clearer positioning, stronger comparisons, and minor extensions (e.g., per-language function-word direction estimation, handling relative clauses), this work could make a solid contribution.
