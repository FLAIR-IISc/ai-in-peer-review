Summary and contributions
- A simple, language-agnostic rule-based dependency parser for UD (UDP) that uses a small set of POS head rules, a two-step decoding scheme enforcing UD’s function-word-as-leaves constraint, and personalized PageRank to rank content words. Adposition attachment direction is estimated from surface bigrams at test time.
- A broad empirical study over 32 UD v1.2 languages under three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, coarse CF) and across domains, with ablations quantifying the contribution of PageRank and the two-step decoding.

Strengths
- Scope and usefulness as a strong baseline: The paper delivers a transparent, reproducible, no-lexicon parser that works out-of-the-box on 32 languages. As a UD-oriented baseline, it is valuable for practitioners who need a fast, parameter-light reference system.
- Method aligns with UD constraints: The two-step decoding that first attaches content words and then function words to enforce leafness is a neat operationalization of UD’s design, and the method’s simplicity is appealing.
- Solid empirical coverage and ablations: The study spans many languages and domains, and the ablations (UDP vs. UDPNoPR vs. BL) quantify that PageRank ordering contributes materially (~9 UAS on average), which helps isolate where the gains come from.
- Cross-domain robustness analysis: Reporting variability across genres and showing lower standard deviation than a delexicalized transfer baseline is informative; in some settings UDP even outperforms the transfer model, suggesting the rules generalize reasonably.
- Competitive results given simplicity: With gold POS, UDP trails delex transfer by ~6.4 UAS on average and even wins on Hindi; with predicted POS, the relative degradation is small and smaller than the delex baseline’s drop.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: Using PageRank and head rules for unsupervised parsing has been explored (e.g., Søgaard, 2012b). The proposed tweaks (personalization toward the first verb, two-step decoding, test-time ADP direction) feel incremental. The paper positions these as key contributions, but the methodological advance over established baselines is modest.
- “Without training” claim is misleading: The main predicted-POS setting relies on supervised taggers trained per language. Parameters (teleport probability, personalization weight 5x, head-rule set) are tuned on English dev. Estimating ADP direction on the test set itself constitutes test-set peeking. These choices undercut the unsupervised claim and raise concerns about evaluation purity.
- Baseline comparisons are narrow and partly unfair: The supervised comparison is limited to a particular delex TurboParser setup; there’s no comparison to other strong transfer/projection baselines of the time or to Søgaard’s model re-implemented on UD. The rule baseline uses an oracle per-language backoff direction, whereas UDP estimates only ADP direction; this asymmetry complicates interpretation. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Evaluation protocol issues: UAS appears to include punctuation, which is atypical in many UD comparisons and depresses scores unevenly. There’s no breakdown by sentence length, projectivity, or morphology-rich vs. analytic languages; labeled attachment is not attempted, making it hard to assess UD compliance beyond head selection. The backoff that drops constraints to force a tree is not quantified—this could violate UD constraints frequently.
- Heuristic fragility and potential overfitting: The ADP direction heuristic reportedly converges with 10–15 sentences; this seems brittle and language/domain sensitive. Head rules were selected for precision on English dev; although results on other languages are not worse than English, the selection bias remains. Sensitivity analyses for the PR personalization factor, teleport, and head-rule set are limited.

Questions to authors
- Clarify the “without training” claim. Can you present the core claim using only the gold POS setup (and/or fully unsupervised POS), and revise the claim accordingly? How much of the reported advantage persists if ADP direction is estimated on a held-out dev split (not the test set)?
- How often does the backoff (dropping δ and then both δ and κ) trigger across languages? What is the percentage of edges assigned after dropping constraints, and how does that correlate with errors?
- Please report UAS with punctuation excluded and, if possible, LAS/UAS with standard UD evaluation scripts, to ease comparison to prior work.
- Provide stronger and broader baselines: results for Søgaard (2012b) on UD v1.2, and additional delex transfer configurations (e.g., MSTParser, typology-informed models, or projections) to contextualize performance. Are differences statistically significant?
- Sensitivity analyses: How sensitive are results to the PR personalization weight and teleport probability? Does ranking by order (UDPNoPR) but with better tuned rules close the gap? How stable is the 10–15 sentence ADP-direction heuristic across domains?

Additional comments
- The idea of codifying UD constraints in a clean, language-agnostic baseline is valuable. However, claims about “unsupervised” and “no training” should be tempered, and comparisons broadened and made stricter (no test-set peeking, standard evaluation). If the authors address the evaluation concerns and provide stronger, fair baselines with significance testing and clearer analysis of constraint backoff, this would strengthen the case for acceptance as a solid baseline paper.
