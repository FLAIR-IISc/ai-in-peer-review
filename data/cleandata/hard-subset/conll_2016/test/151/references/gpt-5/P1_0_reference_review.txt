Summary and main contributions
This paper introduces UDP, a fully rule-based, training-free dependency parser tailored to Universal Dependencies (UD). The method exploits UD constraints (single root; function words as dependents) via:
- Personalized PageRank (PR) over a rule-induced multigraph to rank content words and identify the main predicate.
- A two-step deterministic decoding that first attaches content words (ensuring a well-formed tree) and then attaches function words as leaves, with directionality constraints.
- Test-time estimation of adposition direction (pre/postposition) from unlabeled test text via simple bigram counts.

The parser is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under gold POS, predicted POS (TnT trained per language), and a naïve content/function tag setting. It is compared with a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (TurboParser) and a rule baseline. Additional analyses probe ablations and cross-domain robustness.

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) A simple, language-agnostic, zero-training UD parser that leverages PR and UD-specific constraints to achieve competitive cross-lingual UAS (within 6.4 points of multi-source delex transfer on average; outperforming it on Hindi).
2) A thorough robustness analysis across POS quality and domains, showing lower error propagation from POS to dependencies and lower cross-domain variance than the supervised transfer baseline.
3) Clear ablations isolating the gains from PR personalization and the two-step decoding.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual performance with minimal assumptions: UDPG averages 57.5 UAS across 32 languages, only 6.4 behind MSDG (63.9), and beats MSDG on Hindi by a wide margin. This is a compelling result for a training-free method that uses only POS and a small rule set.
- Method tailored to UD’s design pays off: The two-step decoding that treats content and function words differently enforces UD’s leaf constraint for function words while still constructing single-rooted trees. The head rules (limited and UD-aligned) plus simple directionality controls yield well-formed trees that pass UD validation.
- Clear evidence of PR’s utility: Ablation shows PR contributes about +9 UAS over UDP without PR; the decoding contributes an additional +4 over a rule baseline. Personalization (5× weight on the estimated main predicate) improves root accuracy and PR convergence, including for verb-final languages.
- Robust to POS noise and domain shift: From gold to predicted POS, UDP drops only 2.2 UAS on average (vs 2.7 for MSD), with a quantified error propagation of 0.37 vs 0.46 for MSD. Across domain splits (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian), UDP has consistently lower standard deviation than MSD (e.g., 2.5 vs 4.5 with gold POS), supporting the claim of domain robustness for rule-based methods leveraging general UD constraints.
- Broad coverage and transparent, reproducible design: The approach is deterministic given a POS sequence, computationally lightweight (PR dominates), and clearly documented. The evaluation spans 32 languages and includes detailed POS-wise analyses (e.g., adpositions, determiners, punctuation, conjunctions).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior unsupervised work: The core idea builds closely on Søgaard (2012b) (PR-driven unsupervised parsing). While the paper adds UD-specific tweaks (personalized PR, two-step decoding for leafness, on-the-fly ADP direction), these feel incremental rather than fundamentally new. A stronger positioning against Søgaard’s exact setups and results would help clarify novelty.
- Potential test-set leakage in direction estimation: ADP direction is estimated “directly on test data” from ADP–NOMINAL bigrams. While unlabeled and minimal, this is still test-time adaptation and may be unfair vs. baselines that cannot use test-set statistics. It is unclear whether direction is estimated per-language over the whole test set (which would introduce peeking) or incrementally. An alternative using training/dev or external unlabeled data would be cleaner.
- English-centric rule tuning and fixed directionality for other function classes: The direction for AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ/PUNCT is fixed based on English development data, which can bias performance (e.g., DET direction fails for Basque; DET UAS 32%). It seems feasible to estimate these directions unsupervisedly per language (as done for ADP), and the paper acknowledges this.
- “Without training” claim vs. POS supervision: The main parsing model is training-free, but the predicted POS setting uses a supervised per-language TnT trained on UD training data. This is standard but should be stated more explicitly to avoid overclaiming and to ensure fair comparison with MSD (which also relies on supervised resources).
- Coverage limitations on key UD phenomena: Excluding NOUN→VERB as potential heads precludes relative clauses; coordination and punctuation remain weak (CONJ avg ~38 UAS; PUNCT ~21). While acknowledged, this limits practical utility and suggests room for simple additional rules or data-driven estimation. No labeled attachment evaluation is provided, and non-projectivity frequency/backoff rates are not reported.

Questions to authors
- How exactly is ADP direction estimated: per-sentence, incrementally over the test stream, or once using the whole test set? For the domain experiments, is direction re-estimated per domain or fixed per language? Please report performance when estimating direction on dev or external unlabeled data to rule out test leakage.
- What proportion of attachments required backoff (dropping δ and/or κ), per language? A coverage/backoff table would help understand where the rules fail.
- How sensitive is UDP to misestimated ADP direction? Could you report a controlled perturbation experiment?
- Can you estimate directionality for DET/AUX/SCONJ/CONJ per language from unlabeled text, and does this fix Basque DET and similar outliers?
- What fraction of produced trees are non-projective, and how does this compare to UD gold?

Additional comments
- Clarity: There are several typos and minor inconsistencies (e.g., table/figure references, variable names). Tighten the algorithm description (define δ/κ/γ once and cleanly) and ensure all examples align with the rules.
- Baselines: Consider adding a stronger UD head-rules baseline (e.g., UD conversion rules or UDPipe-style headfinder) that guarantees trees, and report runtime.
- Reporting: Provide POS accuracies per language for TnT; include significance tests or confidence intervals. A direct comparison to Søgaard (2012b) on overlapping languages/datasets would better quantify the gains from personalization and UD-specific constraints.
- Scope: The suggestion to extend UDP with partial labeling is interesting; a small pilot (e.g., a few high-precision labels) would strengthen the paper.
