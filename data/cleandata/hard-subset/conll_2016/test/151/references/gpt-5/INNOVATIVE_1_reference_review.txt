Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- A training-free, language-agnostic dependency parser for UD (UDP) that exploits UD’s structural constraints with a minimal set of POS-based head rules, a two-step decoding that enforces function words as leaves, and personalized PageRank to rank content words and identify the main predicate.
- Simple on-the-fly estimation of adposition attachment direction from test data via ADP–NOMINAL bigrams, removing language-specific parameters.
- Broad, multilingual and cross-domain evaluation on 32 UD v1.2 treebanks, showing competitive UAS to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline, smaller degradation under predicted POS, and lower variance across domains.

Strengths
- Conceptual boldness: a truly training-free UD parser. The paper stakes out a refreshing, principled alternative to cross-lingual transfer/projection by leaning into UD’s design (e.g., function words as dependents) instead of data. For the UD era, this is a distinctive direction, and the algorithm is simple, transparent, and deterministic.
- Personalized PageRank plus UD-aware decoding materially improves over strong rule baselines. The ablations are convincing: removing PR costs about 9 UAS points; compared to the head-rule baseline, UDP adds ~4 UAS points despite not having oracle head-direction information. The PR personalization (verb boost) is motivated and empirically useful, even for verb-final languages.
- Competitive multilingual performance with robustness benefits. On gold POS, UDP is on average only 6.4 UAS behind multi-source delexicalized transfer (MSDG) and even outperforms it on Hindi; on predicted POS, UDP’s drop (2.2) is smaller than MSD’s (2.7). The error-propagation estimate from POS to dependencies is lower for UDP (0.37 vs. 0.46). These are meaningful gains for practical, noisy settings.
- Cross-domain stability. The variance analysis across multiple domains (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian) shows notably lower standard deviation for UDP than MSD, both with gold and predicted POS. This is valuable evidence that rule-driven UD parsers can generalize more stably across genres, an often-overlooked property in cross-lingual work.
- Minimal language-specific knobs. Estimating ADP direction directly on test data is simple and effective (avg ADP UAS ~75%), and the method converges quickly (10–15 sentences). The overall approach reduces reliance on bilingual resources and language-specific engineering.

Weaknesses
- Methodological novelty is incremental relative to prior unsupervised PR-based parsing. The core ingredients—PR scoring (Søgaard 2012b), head rules, and rule-based decoding—are known; the main novelty lies in adapting them to UD (leaf-enforcing two-step decoding, PR personalization, and test-time ADP direction). While this adaptation is timely and useful, it is not a fundamentally new parsing paradigm.
- “No training” claim is diluted by reliance on target-language POS taggers for the main predicted-POS setting. Tagging with TnT trained on the target language introduces supervision and may overstate robustness in true zero-resource contexts. The CF two-tag setting is an interesting lower bound, but very crude; it likely underestimates what can be done with weak/unsupervised tagging.
- Fixed directionality for several function POS is tuned on English and can be wrong for some languages (e.g., Basque DET). This undermines the “no language-specific parameters” narrative. The paper notes this and suggests estimating these directions like ADP; however, not doing so limits universality and contributes to notable POS-specific failures (PUNCT, CONJ remain weak and variable).
- Limited linguistic coverage and structural expressivity. By design, the rules disallow NOUN→VERB heads, precluding relative clause attachments; coordination and MWEs are acknowledged gaps; punctuation and conjunction UAS are low with high variance. Labeled dependencies are not addressed. This confines the parser’s utility for downstream tasks that require richer structure.
- Baseline and comparison scope. The delexicalized baseline is reasonable, but stronger or more diverse baselines (e.g., alternative multi-source weighting/selection, projection-based systems, or other modern unsupervised/very-low-resource parsers) are not included. Also, the rule-based baseline can produce disconnected structures, whereas UDP always outputs well-formed trees, slightly complicating fairness.

Questions to authors
- How strong is the dependence on the English-tuned choices for function-word directionality? Can you estimate directionality for AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ from test data like you do for ADP, and how much does that help languages like Basque?
- What are the exact hand-crafted head rules in Table 1 (full list) and how were they selected on English dev? Could you report per-language ablations showing the effect of each rule category?
- Test-time estimation of ADP direction: do you fix the direction once per language (global) or per document? How sensitive are results when the available test material is very short?
- Runtime and scalability: what are typical sentence-level runtimes for PR+decoding, and how do they scale with sentence length? Any issues with graph disconnectedness versus teleport probability and its impact on rankings?
- Could you report non-projectivity rates and performance by typology (e.g., free word order, verb-final) to better understand where the approach shines or struggles?

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. While the algorithmic pieces are not radically new, the paper delivers a clean, bold, training-free UD parser with smart adaptations (personalized PR, two-step UD-compliant decoding, test-time ADP direction), competitive multilingual results, and strong cross-domain robustness. Given the clarity, simplicity, and practical value as a universal baseline, I recommend acceptance despite the noted limitations.
