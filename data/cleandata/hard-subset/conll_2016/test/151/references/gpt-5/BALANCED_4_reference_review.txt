Summary
This paper presents UDP, a training-free dependency parser tailored to Universal Dependencies (UD). UDP uses: (i) a small set of UD-compliant POS-based head rules; (ii) a two-step decoding that first attaches content words and then function words to enforce UD’s “function words are dependents” constraint; (iii) personalized PageRank to prioritize main predicate identification and head assignment among content words; and (iv) an unsupervised, test-time estimate of adposition directionality from bigram counts. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under three POS regimes (gold, supervised target-language POS via TnT, and a crude content/function tagging), and compared to a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser (MSD). The authors also study cross-domain robustness and provide ablations isolating the impact of PageRank.

Claimed main contributions:
- A completely training-free UD parser that encodes UD constraints, uses personalized PageRank, and estimates adposition direction at test time.
- Competitive multilingual performance relative to a delexicalized supervised transfer baseline, with better robustness to domain shift.
- Analyses on POS error propagation, ablation of PageRank, and cross-domain variance.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) Empirical demonstration that a simple, training-free, UD-constrained rule-based parser can achieve broadly competitive UAS across 32 languages and is more robust to domain changes than a delexicalized transfer baseline. This makes UDP a strong baseline and a practical tool when training data or parallel resources are unavailable.
2) Architectural design that operationalizes UD constraints via a two-step decoding and personalized PageRank to prioritize content-word heads and root selection, supported by a clear ablation showing PageRank’s large contribution (~9 UAS on average).
3) Lightweight, unsupervised estimation of adposition directionality on test data, and analysis of POS error propagation demonstrating lower propagation than the MSD baseline.

Strengths
- Breadth and robustness of evaluation: The system is evaluated on 32 languages, three POS settings, and multiple domains. Cross-domain analyses show consistently lower performance variance for UDP than MSD, corroborating claims about robustness to domain shift.
- Strong, simple baseline with good absolute performance: Despite being training-free and using few rules, UDP’s UAS is close to the multi-source delexicalized parser (average gap ~6.4 with gold POS), even outperforming it on Hindi. This is practically significant and suggests UD constraints can be profitably hard-coded.
- Careful analysis and ablations: The authors quantify the contribution of the PageRank component (≈9 UAS), report POS error propagation factors (lower for UDP than MSD), and discuss failure modes (e.g., limitations on relative clauses due to omitted NOUN→VERB rule).
- Clear alignment with UD design: Enforcing function-word leafness and single-root properties and separating content/function attachment is principled within UD and appears to yield stability benefits across languages and domains.
- Reproducibility intent: Code and domain-annotated test sets are promised for release (anonymized for review), improving impact and utility as a community baseline.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The approach is close in spirit to prior rule-based and unsupervised parsing without training (e.g., Søgaard 2012b). The main differences—personalized PageRank, two-step decoding enforcing UD leafness, and test-time ADP direction—are incremental. The paper would benefit from deeper algorithmic innovations or stronger empirical deltas against recent unsupervised/cross-lingual baselines.
- Dependence on supervised target POS for the key “predicted POS” setting: Using TnT trained on target-language training data is not realistic in truly low-resource scenarios. While the content/function tagging is an attempt at a lower bound, it is very coarse. A fairer mid-point would include projected or cross-lingual POS taggers that do not rely on target training data.
- Rule coverage and structural limitations: The compact rule set omits phenomena like relative clauses and coordination (acknowledged), which caps performance and reduces generality. Some rules were tuned on English dev data, raising concerns about subtle bias; more systematic rule induction or data-driven validation across languages would strengthen the case.
- Evaluation scope: Only unlabeled UAS is reported. No statistical significance testing is mentioned for the main comparisons. Including labeled attachment (even partial) or error-type breakdowns beyond POS would make claims about structural fidelity more persuasive, especially given the UD-specific constraints.
- Potential methodological ambiguities: Estimating adposition direction on test data is reasonable in an unsupervised setup, but it does use test-distribution information. Clarifying whether this estimation is sentence-local or corpus-wide and whether similar estimates for DET/AUX/SCONJ directionality would help would improve clarity.

Questions to Authors
- Could you provide the full list of head rules and the exact procedure used to select/tune them (beyond the subset in Table 1)? How sensitive are results to these choices across languages?
- How sensitive is performance to the PageRank teleport probability and the personalization weight? Did you tune these on English only, and what is the cross-lingual variance if perturbed?
- For adposition direction estimation, is the decision corpus-level, per-document, or adaptive per-sentence? Have you tried estimating directionality for DET/AUX/SCONJ similarly, and does it help languages like Basque where DET behaves differently?
- Can UDP be extended to guarantee connected, single-root, and acyclic trees under all backoff conditions? The description suggests yes; please clarify the proof/guarantee and the observed non-projectivity rate.
- Could you include a more realistic cross-lingual POS baseline (e.g., projection-based or multilingual/self-training taggers) and, if possible, a recent unsupervised or lightly supervised parser beyond DMV for a stronger comparison?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is well-written, thorough, and offers a useful, robust training-free baseline with broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation. However, the methodological novelty over prior rule-based and training-free parsing is limited, and the main “predicted POS” setting relies on supervised target POS. With stronger comparisons, a more realistic POS scenario, and/or expanded modeling beyond the current rule set (e.g., handling coordination/relatives or labeled parsing), this work would be a solid accept. As it stands, I view it as a valuable baseline contribution but slightly below the bar for novelty.
