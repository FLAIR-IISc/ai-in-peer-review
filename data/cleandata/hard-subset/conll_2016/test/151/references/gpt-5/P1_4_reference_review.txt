Summary and main contributions
The paper presents UDP, a fully rule-based dependency parser designed for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The core idea is to exploit UD’s cross-linguistic constraints (single root; function words as dependents) together with a small set of POS-based head rules and a personalized PageRank (PR) over a sentence-internal graph to prioritize content-word attachments. The method also estimates adposition attachment direction on-the-fly from test data via simple bigram statistics. Experiments on 32 UD v1.2 languages compare UDP to a strong multi-source delexicalized baseline (TurboParser) and analyze robustness to POS degradation and domain shift.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, training-free, UD-compliant parsing algorithm that combines personalized PageRank and two-step decoding to enforce function-word leafness, delivering competitive UAS to delexicalized transfer baselines across 32 languages.
2) A thorough, multilingual and cross-domain evaluation demonstrating notable robustness to POS noise and domain changes vs. a learned delexicalized baseline.
3) A small set of generally applicable head rules plus test-time estimation of adposition direction, showing how minimal language-agnostic heuristics can be effective under UD.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual coverage and competitive performance with no training: UDPG averages 57.5 UAS vs. 63.9 for the multi-source delex baseline (MSDG), even outperforming MSDG on Hindi. This is impressive given the method’s simplicity, lack of training, and strict reliance on POS-level constraints.
- Robustness to POS degradation and domain shift: The drop from gold to predicted POS is smaller for UDP than for MSD (reported error propagation 0.37 vs. 0.46), and domain-wise standard deviations are consistently lower for UDP across several languages. This aligns with the hypothesis that fewer parameters and UD-driven rules reduce overfitting to domains.
- Clear ablations that isolate the contribution of PageRank: Disabling PR (UDPNoPR) reduces UAS by ~9 points; decoding alone adds ~4 over a strong rule baseline. This convincingly shows PR’s centrality and the value of ranking content words for head selection.
- Simple, transparent design with well-motivated UD choices: Two-step decoding enforces function-word leafness; rules forbid NOUN→VERB to avoid systematic errors with relative clauses; ADP direction is estimated directly from test data and converges quickly. The method is easy to reason about and inspect.
- Breadth of analysis: The paper reports per-language results, POS-specific observations (e.g., DET and ADP directions), and multiple POS scenarios (gold, supervised predicted, and coarse CF tags), offering a well-rounded empirical picture.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing: The use of PageRank for scoring words and rule-based decoding closely follows Søgaard (2012b). The main differentiators—PR personalization toward the first verb/content word, explicit two-step decoding to enforce UD leafness, and ADP-direction estimation—are useful but incremental. The paper would benefit from deeper methodological innovation (e.g., edge-level PR, richer constraints).
- The “no training” claim is softened by English development tuning: Head rules and several hyperparameters (e.g., PR teleport=0.05, personalization weight=5, fixed directions for non-ADP function words) were selected on English development data. While results generalize well, this introduces potential English bias and reduces the purity of the “training-free” claim.
- Evaluation choices limit comparability: The paper evaluates UAS including punctuation; UD evaluations typically exclude punctuation, and comparisons to literature often do so. Reporting both would help situate the results. In addition, predicted POS are obtained with TnT trained on target-language gold data, which is not a realistic low-resource setup; cross-lingual or projected POS would be a fairer test of the “no training” parser in low-resource conditions.
- Incomplete treatment of key UD phenomena: The rules deliberately avoid NOUN→VERB, precluding relative clause attachment; coordination and punctuation are weak points; multiword expressions and proper names are not handled. While acknowledged as future work, the lack of strategies for these frequent UD patterns likely caps performance and limits downstream utility.
- Comparison set could be broader: The delex baseline is strong and appropriate, but no projection-based baselines are reported, and no modern unsupervised objectives beyond Søgaard’s line are compared. Ablations on ADP direction (e.g., using UD morph features when available vs. test-time estimation) and sensitivity to mixed head-direction languages would strengthen the empirical case.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to the personalization weight (5x) and teleport probability (0.05)? Did you try sentence-level tuning-free defaults (e.g., standard PR damping) and observe similar trends?
- Can you report UAS excluding punctuation to align with UD convention, and does the main ranking vs. MSD change under that metric?
- For predicted POS, do your conclusions hold when using cross-lingual/projected taggers instead of supervised in-language TnT? Any results with multilingual taggers?
- How often does the back-off (dropping δ, then κ) trigger, and what is its impact on non-projectivity rates and errors?
- For languages with substantial postpositions and mixed head orders, how stable is the ADP direction estimator, and does sentence-level vs. corpus-level estimation matter?

Additional comments
- Releasing the rule set, code, and domain splits (once de-anonymized) would be valuable for reproducibility.
- Consider reporting per-POS F1 or head-attachment accuracy for challenging categories (CONJ, ADP, PUNCT) and an error typology (e.g., coordination scope, subordination) to guide future rule extensions.
- Edge-level PR or reified-edge scoring, as suggested in the conclusion, seems a promising direction to increase novelty and accuracy without sacrificing training-free operation.

Overall evaluation
This is a clear, well-executed paper that demonstrates how far a principled, UD-aware, training-free approach can go across many languages and domains. While methodological novelty is moderate, the breadth, robustness, and transparency make it a solid contribution and a strong baseline for cross-lingual parsing under resource constraints. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying evaluation choices and reinforcing claims with punctuation-excluded and more realistic POS settings.
