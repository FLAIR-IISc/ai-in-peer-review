Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents UDP, a rule-based dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no supervised training. The method operates on POS tags only and combines (i) a small set of UD-aligned head rules, (ii) a two-step decoding procedure that first attaches content words and then function words to enforce UD-specific constraints (single root and function-word leafness), (iii) personalized PageRank (PR) to rank content words and identify the main predicate, and (iv) a test-time heuristic to infer adposition attachment direction (pre/postposition) from surface bigrams. The parser is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under gold POS, predicted POS (TnT), and a coarse content/function tagging scenario, and compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (MSD). The authors also report cross-domain robustness on several languages.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength
1) A simple, language-agnostic, rule-based parser for UD that performs competitively to delexicalized transfer across 32 languages and degrades less under domain and POS noise. The breadth and robustness results are the central contribution.
2) A principled exploitation of UD constraints via two-step decoding and PageRank personalization to improve main predicate identification and content-word attachment without supervised training.
3) A lightweight, test-time estimation of adposition directionality from unlabeled data, reducing language-specific assumptions.

Strengths
- Strong empirical coverage and robustness: The evaluation spans 32 languages and multiple domains. With gold POS, average UAS is within ~6 points of MSD, and the method even outperforms MSD on Hindi. Under predicted POS, UDP’s error propagation from POS is lower than MSD’s. The domain-robustness analysis (lower standard deviation across domains) is compelling for low-resource or out-of-domain scenarios.
- Careful, UD-aware design: The two-step decoding enforces UD’s single-root and function-word leafness in a transparent way. The PR personalization (favoring the first verb if present) is simple yet effective, contributing ~9 UAS points in ablation.
- Minimal language-specific tuning: Apart from estimating adposition direction from test data and fixing some function-word directions (from English dev), the system avoids learned parameters and handles many languages uniformly. The test-time ADP direction heuristic is simple and converges quickly.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The algorithm is well specified, with complexity considerations, ablations (with/without PR), per-POS behavior, and cross-domain analyses. The baseline comparisons are straightforward; the paper articulates where the rules help and where they fail.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior unsupervised/rule-based parsing: The core ideas (PageRank for ranking words, rule-based decoding, UD-driven constraints) build directly on Søgaard (2012b) and related work. The advances—personalized PR, two-step decoding for leafness, and a test-time ADP-direction heuristic—are useful but incremental rather than a conceptual breakthrough.
- Dependence on English development and test-time transductive estimation: Several design choices (function word directionality, PR teleport value, rule selection) are tuned on English dev; adposition direction is inferred on the test set itself (requiring 10–15 sentences). While still “unsupervised,” this mixes development-time language-specific decisions with transductive use of test distributions, complicating fairness compared to baselines trained without target-language peeking. Please clarify whether ADP direction is estimated per test sentence, per test fold, or from the whole test set.
- Structural coverage gaps and hard-coded constraints: Excluding NOUN→VERB heads precludes relative clause attachments by design, and the fixed leafness of function words hampers coordination, auxiliaries with dependents, and multiword expressions. The back-off that drops rule and direction constraints to force a tree risks arbitrary non-projective attachments and may dilute UD consistency in difficult cases (e.g., CONJ, PUNCT).
- Baselines and metrics: Evaluation is only UAS; no LAS or partial labeling, even though UD labels are central to downstream utility. The supervised baseline (McDonald et al., 2011) is solid but dated; more recent cross-lingual methods (e.g., with bilingual embeddings or projection enhancements) might change the relative positioning. The naive CF-tag scenario is arguably too coarse to be informative, while the predicted POS uses a fully supervised tagger per target language (which is not always realistic in low-resource settings).
- Some empirical details are underspecified: No statistical testing is reported; domain experiments include very small (50–75 sentence) in-house annotations; it is unclear whether the PR graph construction ever leads to undesirable biases across languages/scripts, or how frequently the back-off constraints are triggered.

Questions to authors
- ADP direction estimation: Is it computed per sentence, per document, or globally on the full test set? If global, can you report performance when restricting estimation to past sentences only (online) to avoid test peeking?
- Back-off frequency: How often do you drop δ (head rules) and κ (direction) in practice? A table of percentages across languages would help assess the extent of ad hoc attachments.
- Coordination and relative clauses: Can you quantify errors attributable to the NOUN→VERB exclusion and to conjunction scope issues? Would adding limited exceptions ameliorate these without harming other attachments?
- POS robustness: How does UDP fare with realistic cross-lingual projected POS or multilingual taggers (no target-language training), to better reflect low-resource conditions?
- LAS/partial labeling: Given your rules, how feasible is adding a small label inventory (e.g., det, case, aux) to report LAS for a subset?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider learning or inferring function-word directionality per language/test set (as done for ADP), rather than fixing from English development. This could address Basque determiners and similar divergences.
- Incorporate simple morphology (e.g., case, verb forms) when available; this may help adposition attachments and root detection in verb-final languages without sacrificing language agnosticism.
- Explore edge-scoring PR (edge reification) as suggested, and simple constraints for coordination (e.g., shared head and symmetry) to boost CONJ UAS.
- Report performance by sentence length and projectivity rates; this would contextualize where the method shines/fails.
- For fairness, include a cross-lingual baseline without target-language POS supervision (e.g., projected tags or multilingual taggers), and, if space allows, a stronger delexicalized baseline with modern features.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is well-written, thorough, and provides a strong and surprisingly competitive UD-aware unsupervised baseline with impressive robustness. However, the methodological novelty over prior rule-based/PR approaches is modest, and several design choices (English tuning, transductive ADP estimation, structural exclusions) limit generality. With stronger baselines, clearer handling of test-time estimation, and modest extensions (coordination, relative clauses, limited labeling), this could be a solid accept.
