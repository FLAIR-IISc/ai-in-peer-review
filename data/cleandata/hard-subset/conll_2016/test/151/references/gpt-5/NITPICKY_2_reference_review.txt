Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes UDP, a rule-based, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). It combines (i) a small set of UD-consistent head rules with (ii) personalized PageRank (PR) to rank content words for a two-phase decoding that enforces function-word leafness, and (iii) a test-time heuristic to infer adposition (ADP) head-direction from ADP–NOMINAL bigram counts. The system is evaluated on UD v1.2 across 32 languages under three POS regimes (gold, predicted, and a crude content/function split), and compared to a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser (MSD). The authors also report cross-domain robustness on a subset of languages.

As I see it, the main contributions (in order):
1) A simple, UD-aware rule-based parsing framework with personalized PR ranking and a two-step decoding that enforces function words as leaves, achieving competitive unlabeled accuracy across many languages while requiring no parser training.
2) A test-time estimation of ADP attachment direction from raw POS bigram statistics, avoiding language-specific parameters.
3) A relatively broad multilingual evaluation including a cross-domain analysis suggesting lower variance under domain shift than a delexicalized transfer baseline.

Strengths
- Broad multilingual scope and reasonable performance for an unsupervised approach: UDPG is on average within ~6–7 UAS points of a well-established delex baseline (MSDG), and even surpasses it for Hindi. The results are surprisingly strong for a method this simple.
- Clever exploitation of UD constraints: The two-step decoding that first builds a backbone over content words and then attaches function words ensures leafness and adheres to UD design principles. This is a clean, linguistically motivated design.
- Personalized PageRank as a lightweight global signal: Using PR with a biased prior to upweight the main predicate is novel in this exact UD setting and plausibly useful. The ablation hints that PR contributes materially to performance.
- Cross-domain robustness analysis: Reporting per-domain variances and showing lower standard deviations than MSD across languages is valuable, especially for realistic deployment where domain shifts are common.
- Minimal language-specific tuning: Estimating ADP direction on-the-fly is a nice touch that avoids per-language configuration and appears to work reliably.

Weaknesses
- Presentation, clarity, and reproducibility issues (numerous minor flaws): The paper has many typos and formatting problems (e.g., “The the main idea,” “constribution,” “overal,” “presidental”), inconsistent/malformed references (years as “?”, duplicated author lists, unusual “\Q1999\E”), and several notation glitches (C and F both indexed with m; pseudo-code formatting; ambiguous set vs sequence notation). Table/figure references and captions are at times unclear; Table 5’s header and columns are hard to interpret and formatting cues (underline/bold) are not verifiable in plain text. Claims like “Decoding runs in less than O(n^2), namely O(n×|C|)” are imprecise (worst case |C|=n). Together, these detract from readability and trust and make exact reproduction difficult.
- Methodological gaps and incomplete baselines: The paper does not compare directly against established unsupervised parsers (e.g., DMV variants with improvements; Søgaard’s methods beyond a textual claim). Given the central claim (“without training”), a head-to-head against current unsupervised baselines is essential. The MSD configuration (20k sentences sampled from all non-target UD training data) may not be state-of-the-art and details (feature sets, hyperparameters, sentence weighting) are sparse. The adjacency baseline, touted as much worse, is not reported. Ablations on the head rules (which were tuned on English) are limited.
- “Without training” claim is overstated and potentially misleading: Parameters (rules, PR settings, function-word directions) are tuned on English dev; ADP direction is estimated from target test data; predicted POS experiments rely on taggers trained on each language’s training set. While the parser itself is not trained, the headline phrasing could be softened and made more precise.
- Algorithmic specification lacks detail and rigor: The PR graph construction (edge directionality, weighting, handling of multi-edges) is under-specified; teleport choice (0.05) is ad hoc and justified only by “convergence” (PR with teleport converges regardless; what was actually optimized?). The nearest-head search with backoff is ambiguous (dropping δ and κ conditions) and may introduce non-projective or linguistically odd attachments without a clear tie-breaker policy. No discussion of time/memory in practice or sensitivity to personalization weight (5x) or to errors in main predicate estimation.
- Evaluation scope and choices raise questions: Only unlabeled attachment is reported; no LAS or partial labeling despite claiming easy extension. Punctuation and conjunctions are included with very low UAS but also high variance across treebanks; more nuanced treatment (or exclusion per UD eval norms) would be informative. Excluding Japanese entirely (even from gold-POS evaluation) is not well justified. The added in-house domain datasets are very small, and the anonymized links mean domain claims cannot be verified during review. The content/function (CF) POS scenario is extremely coarse; it is unclear that conclusions drawn from CF are meaningful.

Questions to authors
- PR graph and parameters: How exactly are edges constructed and weighted (direction to heads vs dependents, multiplicity, normalization)? Did you study sensitivity to teleport α and personalization weight? Why 5x, and is this robust across languages?
- Decoding backoff: When no candidate satisfies δ and κ, you drop constraints. In what order are constraints dropped and how are ties resolved? How often does this backoff trigger, and what is its impact on projectivity and UAS?
- Head rules: Please list the full set used, not just the subset in Table 1, and provide an ablation showing which rules matter. How were rules selected on English dev (criteria, search space)?
- Baselines: Can you include results for a strong unsupervised baseline (e.g., improved DMV/Spitkovsky, Søgaard 2012b) on the same UD setup? Also report the adjacency baseline you mention.
- Evaluation details: Why exclude Japanese entirely instead of reporting gold-POS UAS? Could you report LAS or a small set of labels (even heuristically assigned) to support the “easily augmented” claim?
- Reproducibility: Will you release exact code, rules, and scripts to reproduce PR graphs, direction estimation, and domain splits? Please fix and complete the references.

Overall, the core idea is appealing and the empirical signal is promising, but the paper’s numerous presentation issues, incomplete baselines, and underspecified methodology make it hard to endorse in its current form. With stronger baselines, clearer algorithmic detail, and improved writing/formatting, this could become a solid contribution.
