Summary
This paper proposes UDP, a training-free dependency parser designed for Universal Dependencies (UD). UDP exploits UD’s structural constraints by: (i) running personalized PageRank (PR) on a graph built from POS-based head–dependent rules to rank content words and select the root; (ii) a two-step decoding that first attaches content words (forming the backbone) and then attaches function words as leaves; and (iii) estimating adposition attachment direction (pre/postposition) directly from test text using ADP–NOMINAL bigrams. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 treebanks under gold and predicted POS, against a rule baseline and a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). UDP is close to MSD on UAS on average, outperforms it in Hindi, and shows lower variance under domain shifts.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, training-free, language-agnostic parsing pipeline that exploits UD constraints with personalized PageRank and content/function two-step decoding, achieving competitive cross-lingual UAS and strong robustness.
2) A practical, data-free mechanism to infer adposition head direction from raw test text, plus an analysis showing cross-domain stability across several languages and genres.
3) Empirical evidence that a UD-conformant rule-based method can rival delexicalized model transfer and be less sensitive to POS noise and domain shift.

Strengths
- Zero-resource practicality and portability: UDP needs no treebanks, no parallel data, and only POS tags at test time. This is valuable for low-resource or rapid-deployment scenarios.
- Breadth and clarity of evaluation: 32 languages, gold and predicted POS, standard UAS on full test sets. The reported average gap to MSDG is modest (6.4 UAS), UDP beats MSDG on Hindi, and the drop under predicted POS is smaller for UDP than MSD (error propagation 0.37 vs. 0.46).
- Cross-domain robustness: Careful genre-based analysis (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, Italian, English) shows UDP has consistently lower standard deviation across domains than MSD under both gold and predicted POS. This supports the claim that UD-constrained rules generalize better across domains than data-driven transfer models.
- Insightful ablations: The PR component contributes substantially (≈+9 UAS over the no-PR variant), and two-step decoding adds ≈+4 UAS over the baseline. The analysis of POS-wise behavior (e.g., strong DET and ADP attachment except for Basque) is informative.
- Deterministic, well-formed outputs aligned with UD: The decoding enforces single root and function-word leafness, yielding valid UD trees without training.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior unsupervised rule-based parsing: The approach closely builds on Søgaard (2012) PageRank parsing. The new elements—PR personalization toward the first verb, a UD-tailored two-step decoding, and on-the-fly ADP direction—are useful but incremental. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to Søgaard’s variants (both conceptually and empirically).
- Transductive use of test data for ADP direction: Estimating head-direction on the test set itself risks optimistic bias and weakens the “no training” claim from an evaluation standpoint. It remains unclear how performance changes if ADP direction is estimated from a small dev/unlabeled sample not overlapping with the test.
- POS dependency and realism: Predicted POS are produced by TnT trained on target-language labeled data. This is not a low-resource setting and favors UDP; the CF setup is too crude to serve as a realistic lower bound. Modern cross-lingual taggers or projected tags would be a more appropriate stress test. Relying on TnT (now dated) may also understate the potential of MSD with better tags.
- Baseline coverage: The comparison is limited to a single delexicalized transfer system (TurboParser) and trivial adjacency. There is no comparison to stronger recent transfer (e.g., density-driven selection, projection with improved alignments) or modern unsupervised baselines. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Underspecified method details and potential overfitting: Table 1 seems partial; the full set of head rules used for graph construction and decoding is not exhaustively listed. The PR teleport (0.05) and personalization weight (5×) are tuned on English dev; no sensitivity analysis is provided, raising concerns about language-specific tuning. Other algorithmic choices (tie-breaking, complexity, handling of multiple disconnected components) could be clearer.
- Linguistic coverage gaps: The approach cannot capture relative clauses (NOUN→VERB excluded), struggles with coordination and punctuation (low UAS, high variance), and treats some phenomena with global fixed directions. This limits practical utility and may cap performance.

Questions to Authors
- Please provide the complete set of head–dependent rules used to construct the PR graph and to enforce δ during decoding. Are these identical, and how are conflicts/ties resolved? Will you release the rule file/code?
- How many sentences are used to estimate ADP direction, and is this done per document or over the entire test set? Can you report results when estimating ADP direction from a separate dev or a small unlabeled sample (to avoid transductive use of test data)?
- How sensitive are results to PR parameters (personalization weight and teleport)? Please include a cross-lingual sensitivity analysis or at least ranges showing robustness.
- For predicted POS, could you add a setting with cross-lingual tags (e.g., projection or a universal tagger) to better approximate low-resource conditions?
- Can you include at least one stronger baseline (e.g., density-driven delex transfer, recent projection methods, or a stronger delex parser) and report statistical significance?
- Please report per-language root accuracy and the accuracy of ADP direction estimation, especially for verb-final languages where the “first verb” prior may mislead.

Additional comments
- Some typos/formatting (e.g., “The the main idea”; incomplete/inconsistent table/figure references); some references appear incomplete. Please ensure reproducibility for the domain splits and in-house annotations by releasing links and scripts.
