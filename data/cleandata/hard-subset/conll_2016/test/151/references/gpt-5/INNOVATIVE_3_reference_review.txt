Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies only on POS tags and a small set of UD-inspired head rules. The method uses personalized PageRank to prioritize content words (particularly the main predicate) and a two-step decoding that enforces UD constraints (single root, function words as leaves). It estimates adposition attachment direction unsupervisedly from test-data bigrams, avoiding language-specific parameters. The system is evaluated on 32 languages, with gold and predicted POS, and across domains, and compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

Key contributions (as I see them):
1) A compelling zero-resource UD parsing paradigm: a simple, deterministic pipeline that leverages UD formalism constraints plus personalized PageRank, with language-agnostic direction estimation.
2) Broad, multilingual, and cross-domain evaluation demonstrating competitiveness with delex transfer and notably higher robustness to POS noise and domain shift.
3) Clear ablation and error propagation analysis indicating where the gains come from (notably ~9 UAS from PageRank ranking).

Strengths
- Conceptual boldness and simplicity: The paper embraces the UD design as inductive bias and shows that a training-free, rule-constrained approach can be surprisingly competitive. This is a refreshing direction compared to the prevailing data-hungry transfer/projection pipelines, and it provides a useful, universal baseline for UD parsing.
- Personalized PageRank + two-step decoding: The combination is elegant and purposeful. Personalization for main predicate identification and a content-first, function-second decoding pass that enforces UD leafness yield tangible gains (ablation suggests ~9 UAS from PR, ~4 UAS from the decoding/constraints), with minimal hyperparameter tuning.
- Scale and robustness: Evaluation spans 32 languages, multiple POS regimes (gold, predicted, and content/function), and domain splits for several languages. The method shows smaller cross-domain variance than delex transfer, and better resilience to POS tagging noise (lower error propagation). This breadth and the robustness findings are valuable to the community.
- Language-agnostic direction estimation: Inferring ADP direction from test bigrams is simple and effective, reducing language-specific assumptions and yielding strong ADP UAS. The decision to estimate at inference time emphasizes the zero-resource orientation.
- Reproducibility and clarity of the algorithm: The decoding is deterministic given POS, runs in near-quadratic time, and the dependency rules are explicit. The paper includes worked examples and some ablations, and promises code/data release.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior “no-training” parsing: Søgaard (2012b) already explored PageRank-based unsupervised parsing with rules. The main methodological deltas here—personalization, UD-specific head rules, and two-step decoding—feel incremental rather than fundamentally new. A direct, quantitative comparison to Søgaard’s setup on UD would better establish novelty and added value.
- “Without training” vs reliance on supervised POS: The paper’s headline clashes with the main evaluation relying on gold POS and TnT trained on in-language data for predicted POS. While UDPN is informative, it is too crude to reflect realistic zero-resource scenarios. Cross-lingual (projected or multilingual) POS tagging or unsupervised POS would better align with the zero-resource claim.
- Limited coverage and missing phenomena: The rule set intentionally excludes certain structures (e.g., NOUN→VERB), limiting relative clause handling; coordination and MWEs are acknowledged gaps; punctuation and conjunctions perform poorly. The evaluation is UAS-only; there is no labeled accuracy or structural diagnostics (e.g., projectivity, coordination quality), which makes it harder to judge the linguistic fidelity beyond head assignment.
- Baselines: The comparison to a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser is reasonable, but not state-of-the-art for cross-lingual UD at the time (e.g., more powerful projection or hybrid methods were available). Including a stronger projection-based baseline would contextualize how far a training-free approach can go.
- Test-time estimation from the test set: Estimating ADP direction from the test sentences is pragmatic but arguably peeks at the test distribution. The paper argues few sentences suffice, but deployment assumptions (e.g., streaming or one-off parsing) should be clarified, and an analysis with small bootstrap samples would help.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Can you report results with cross-lingual or unsupervised POS tagging to better support the zero-resource narrative, and compare error propagation in that setting?
- How does your method compare directly against Søgaard (2012b) on UD with identical rule sets and graph construction, with/without personalization?
- Have you tried estimating DET/AUX/SCONJ directionality from test data (as for ADP)? In languages like Basque, would this reduce the DET error?
- How sensitive are results to the particular head rules chosen? Can you provide per-language ablations or a way to automatically expand the rule set using only UD constraints?
- What is the minimal sample size needed to estimate ADP direction reliably across languages, and how does performance degrade if only a handful of sentences are available at inference time?

Overall recommendation
This paper offers a bold and timely direction: training-free UD parsing that leverages formalism constraints plus personalized PageRank, with wide multilingual and cross-domain evidence. While aspects are incremental over prior no-training parsing, the reframing for UD and the strong, robust baseline it establishes are valuable. I lean toward acceptance for its conceptual contribution and practical impact as a universal, zero-resource baseline.
