Summary and contributions
This paper proposes UDP, a fully training-free dependency parser that leverages Universal Dependencies (UD) constraints and POS tags only. The method builds a small rule-based dependency graph over content/function POS, uses personalized PageRank (PR) to rank content words (to identify the main predicate and guide head assignment), and performs a two-step decoding that first connects content words and then attaches function words as leaves. Adposition attachment direction is inferred on the fly from test data using simple bigram statistics. UDP is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under gold POS, predicted POS (TnT), and a coarse content/function-only tagging scenario, and compared to a multi-source delexicalized TurboParser baseline. Additional cross-domain analyses are presented.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, training-free UD parser that, despite its simplicity, is competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer across 32 languages and is notably robust to domain shift. This is a pragmatic and useful baseline for UD.
2) An effective combination of UD-informed rules with personalized PageRank and a two-step decoding enforcing function-word leafness—an insightful instantiation of UD design principles into a deterministic parser.
3) A broad, multilingual, and cross-domain evaluation including ablations (impact of PR and decoding), POS sensitivity, and error propagation analysis.

Strengths
- Strong empirical coverage and robustness: UDP delivers 57.5 UAS on average with gold POS vs. 63.9 for the supervised multi-source delex baseline (6.4 points gap), and only drops by 2.2 points with predicted POS (vs. 2.7 for the delex baseline). It even outperforms delex on Hindi. The cross-domain analysis shows substantially lower performance variance than the supervised baseline (e.g., average std: UDPG 2.5 vs. MSDG 4.5), supporting claims of robustness to domain shift.
- Sensible exploitation of UD formal constraints: The two-step decoding that enforces function-word leafness matches UD design and yields clean trees. The ADP direction heuristic inferred from test data is simple yet effective (ADP UAS ~75%), and the fixed direction for other function words works well across most languages.
- Clear ablations and diagnostics: The paper quantifies the contribution of components: removing PR costs ~9 UAS, while decoding over the baseline adds ~4 UAS. Root selection benefits from PR personalization (~+2% root accuracy on average). Error propagation from POS to dependencies is lower for UDP (0.37) than for the delex baseline (0.46), which is valuable evidence given the POS-only nature of the method.
- Reproducibility and practicality: The algorithm is deterministic given POS, requires no training data, and has minimal hyperparameters (e.g., teleport prob. 0.05; PR personalization 5x). Code and additional domain splits are promised to be released, making this a useful community resource and a robust baseline for UD parsing.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior rule-aided, training-free parsing: The approach is closely related to Søgaard (2012b). The main differences (personalized PR with a 5x weight on the first verb, strict function-word leafness via two-step decoding, and test-time ADP direction estimation) feel incremental. The paper would benefit from a deeper comparison to Søgaard’s specific setups and clearer attribution of gains to novel parts beyond the provided ablation.
- Dependence on POS quality and supervised taggers: Although the predicted POS condition shows small average drop, it uses TnT trained on in-language labeled data. For genuinely low-resource targets (no labeled POS), this setup is not directly applicable. The coarse CONTENT/FUNCTION experiment is too crude to represent realistic unsupervised or cross-lingual POS scenarios; a cross-lingual POS tagging baseline (e.g., projection or multilingual models) would better validate the “no training” claim in practical low-resource settings.
- Hand-tuned rules and English-driven design: The head rules and some attachment-direction decisions were selected on English development data. While the cross-lingual results are broadly positive, this introduces design bias. Some limitations (e.g., excluding NOUN→VERB prevents relative clauses) are acknowledged but significant. A more systematic, language-agnostic way to derive or validate rules (like extending the ADP-direction estimation to DET/AUX/SCONJ) would strengthen the claim of universality.
- Evaluation scope and baselines: Only unlabeled UAS is reported (punctuation included), and some choices (e.g., inclusion of PUNCT) depress scores and vary across treebanks. Stronger comparisons to recent unsupervised or lightly supervised grammar induction methods (beyond delex transfer) are missing. Runtime/practicality is asserted but not quantified beyond big-O; more detail on PR/decoding speed across languages would help.
- Heuristic personalization may be typologically brittle: The 5x personalization on the first verb is a heuristic that could bias toward SVO patterns; while gains are reported even for verb-final languages, more typological analysis (e.g., V-final vs. free word-order languages) would make the approach’s generality more convincing.

Questions to authors
- Can you quantify runtime (PR iterations, wall-clock per sentence) and memory across language sizes, and compare to the delex baseline? How does PR personalization affect convergence empirically?
- How often does the back-off step (dropping δ/κ constraints) trigger, and what fraction of edges are created via back-off? Does this correlate with errors or with specific POS?
- Have you tried estimating attachment direction for DET/AUX/SCONJ from test data, as you do for ADP? If so, does it close the gap for languages like Basque?
- Can you provide results with a realistic cross-lingual POS tagger (projected or multilingual) to better simulate zero-resource targets?
- How sensitive are results to the 5x personalization weight and to the “first verb” heuristic? Any typology-wise breakdown (e.g., OV vs. VO languages) for root accuracy?

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed, broadly evaluated, and practically useful baseline for UD parsing without training. While methodological novelty is modest, the empirical findings—competitiveness with delex transfer, strong domain robustness, and clear ablations—are valuable. With additional analysis on POS sourcing in low-resource settings and deeper comparison to prior training-free parsers, this would be a strong contribution.
