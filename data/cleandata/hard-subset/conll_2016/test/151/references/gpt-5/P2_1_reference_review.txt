Summary and contributions
This paper presents UDP, a rule-based, training-free dependency parser tailored to Universal Dependencies (UD). The method uses:
- Personalized PageRank on a POS-rule–induced multigraph to rank content words and identify the main predicate.
- A two-step decoding strategy that first attaches content words (by PR order) and then attaches function words, enforcing UD’s leaf status for function words.
- Test-time estimation of adposition attachment direction via simple ADP–NOMINAL bigram statistics.

Evaluation spans 32 UD languages (UD v1.2) under gold POS, predicted POS (TnT trained per language), and a naïve content/function tag setting, with comparisons to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (TurboParser).

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, UD-aligned, genuinely training-free parser that is competitive with a strong delexicalized transfer baseline across many languages, and notably robust to domain variation.
2) A clear, UD-motivated decoding design (function-word leaf constraint + content-first decoding + PR personalization) with ablations quantifying the contribution of PageRank and decoding.
3) Broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation highlighting stability (lower variance across domains than the supervised transfer baseline).

Strengths
1) Strong, simple baseline with broad multilingual coverage and robustness: With gold POS, UDP averages 57.5 UAS versus 63.9 for the delex baseline (avg gap 6.4), even outperforming it on Hindi. With predicted POS, UDP’s performance drops only marginally (2.2 points), less than the delex baseline (2.7), and exhibits lower error propagation from POS to dependencies (0.37 vs. 0.46), suggesting robustness to tagging noise. Cross-domain analysis shows markedly lower standard deviation across domains for UDP, an attractive property for out-of-domain parsing.

2) UD-aligned design choices that are linguistically motivated: The two-step decoding that enforces function-word leafness and the test-time estimation of ADP direction exploit UD constraints rather than inducing them, yielding high ADP UAS and generally sensible behavior without supervision. This principled use of UD distinguishes the approach from prior unsupervised parsers developed for other formalisms.

3) Informative ablation and analysis: The paper quantifies the contributions of PR (≈+9 UAS vs. UDP without PR) and decoding (≈+4 UAS vs. baseline), analyzes root accuracy and POS-wise performance, and discusses known failure modes (coordination, relative clauses, punctuation). This makes the method’s behavior and limitations transparent.

4) Reproducibility and practicality: The algorithm and rule set are compact, the decoding is deterministic given POS, and the approach runs without training or parallel data. Code/data promises (anonymized for review) are noted.

Weaknesses
1) Limited methodological novelty: The core idea of PageRank-based unsupervised parsing without training is due to Søgaard (2012). The paper’s additions—PR personalization, two-step decoding enforcing UD leafness, and per-language ADP direction estimation—are sensible but incremental. A deeper algorithmic advance (e.g., edge-scoring PR, richer constraints, or principled global inference) would strengthen the contribution.

2) Comparison and fairness concerns: 
- ADP direction is estimated on test data, which, while unsupervised, exploits test-set distributional information; please quantify sensitivity and discuss fairness vs. the supervised baseline that cannot tune on test. 
- The “predicted POS” setting trains TnT on each target language’s training set, which weakens the real “no-training” story in practical low-resource scenarios; the naïve CF setting is too crude to bridge this gap. An evaluation with cross-lingual projected POS or truly low-resource tagging would be more informative.
- Only unlabeled UAS is reported; UD labels are central to the formalism. The lack of any labeled or partially labeled evaluation limits conclusions about UD-specific advantages.
- Competing cross-lingual baselines are limited to delex transfer; no comparisons to modern projection-based or representation-learning approaches are included.

3) Coverage gaps for key phenomena: The system cannot attach verbs under nouns (e.g., relative clauses), struggles with coordination and punctuation (low UAS with high variance), and uses fixed directionality for several function POS (DET/AUX/SCONJ) despite known language variation (e.g., Basque determiners). While acknowledged, these gaps constrain applicability and suggest that simple extensions (per-language direction estimation akin to ADP) could improve results but are not explored.

4) Clarity and detail: There are several typos and formatting issues; Table 1 and graph construction could be clearer (are edges weighted by multiplicity? what exact dependency license rules are used in PR vs. decoding?). The back-off behavior when κ/δ constraints are dropped, the frequency of such events, and the prevalence of non-projective outputs are not quantified. Runtime is discussed qualitatively without measurements.

5) Statistical rigor: No significance testing is reported for language-wise improvements or domain-variance claims. Given relatively small differences in some settings, significance would help substantiate claims.

Questions to authors
- Graph construction: Are PR edges unweighted or weighted by multiplicity? Do you include only rule-licensed edges or also proximity edges? How do POS errors impact graph connectivity and PR stability?
- ADP direction estimation: How much does estimating on test data help vs. training-set or held-out estimation? Please report UAS differences and comment on potential bias. Could the same estimation be applied to DET/AUX/SCONJ directionality?
- Constraint back-off: How often are δ (rule) and κ (direction) constraints relaxed during decoding? What proportion of final attachments result from back-off? Any correlation with non-projectivity or errors?
- Root selection: Personalization favors the first verb. How does this affect verb-final languages? Can a simple position-aware prior or language-specific verb-position estimation improve root accuracy?
- Evaluation: Can you report labeled attachment (even partial labeling where rules imply labels), or at least LAS on UD labels for the edges your rules cover? Also, provide runtime statistics per sentence/treebank.
- Domain robustness: For the in-house annotated English splits (bible, magazine, questions), will data be released? How big are these sets relative to the native web domains, and are differences significant?

Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated, and broadly evaluated UD-specific unsupervised baseline with clear strengths in simplicity and robustness. The novelty is moderate, and some comparisons and details could be strengthened. I view it as a valuable contribution and a strong baseline; with clarifications and minor extensions, it would merit acceptance.
