### **Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces UDP, a novel unsupervised dependency parser designed specifically for the Universal Dependencies (UD) formalism. The system operates without any training data, relying instead on a small, hand-crafted set of linguistically-motivated head-selection rules based on UD part-of-speech (POS) tags. The core of the method involves two key components: 1) a personalized PageRank algorithm that runs on a graph constructed from the head rules to identify the main predicate and rank the sentence's content words, and 2) a two-step decoding algorithm that first attaches content words according to their rank and then attaches function words, thereby enforcing the UD constraint that function words should be leaf nodes. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks. It is compared against a rule-based baseline and a competitive multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). The authors also conduct an extensive analysis of the system's robustness to both POS tagging errors and shifts in text domain.

### **Contributions**

1.  **The primary contribution is the design and extensive evaluation of a simple, transparent, and remarkably effective unsupervised parser that serves as a strong, no-training-data baseline for UD parsing.** The system's strength lies not just in its performance, which is competitive with a supervised transfer system, but in its demonstrated robustness. The thorough cross-domain analysis, showing that this rule-based approach is more stable across different genres than a data-driven one, is a significant and valuable finding for the community.
2.  **A secondary contribution is the novel application of personalized PageRank for main predicate identification and content word ordering in a parsing context.** While using PageRank for unsupervised parsing has been explored before, the specific use of personalization to inject a strong, linguistically-motivated bias towards the sentence's first verb is a simple but effective technique that improves both performance and convergence speed.
3.  **The paper also makes a strong empirical case for leveraging the explicit structural constraints of a standardized formalism like UD.** It demonstrates that a few well-chosen, universal rules derived from the UD guidelines can rival a complex, data-driven transfer system, highlighting the power of incorporating linguistic knowledge directly into parsing algorithms.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Impressive Performance for an Unsupervised Method:** The parser's performance is surprisingly strong. An average Unlabeled Attachment Score (UAS) of 57.5% with gold POS tags across 32 languages is highly respectable, especially when compared to the 63.9% achieved by the multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD), which is a strong supervised baseline. The fact that UDP even outperforms MSD on Hindi is a notable result and establishes it as a credible and challenging baseline for future work in cross-lingual and low-resource parsing.
2.  **Excellent Robustness Analysis:** The analysis of the parser's stability is a major strength of this work. The authors go beyond standard evaluation by testing on curated domain splits for several languages. The finding that UDP has a consistently lower standard deviation in performance across domains than the data-driven MSD parser (Table 6) provides strong evidence for its robustness. This, combined with the analysis showing lower error propagation from predicted POS tags, makes a compelling case for the value of knowledge-based systems in real-world, non-ideal conditions.
3.  **Simplicity and Interpretability:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. The entire system is based on a handful of explicit rules (Table 1) and a clear, two-step decoding algorithm (Figure 1). This transparency is a significant advantage, as it makes the parser's decisions easy to understand and its errors straightforward to analyze, in stark contrast to more opaque neural or complex statistical models. The minimal parameterization adds to this appeal.
4.  **Thorough and Broad Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering 32 diverse languages. The choice of comparison systems is appropriate, and the inclusion of experiments with gold POS, predicted POS, and a naive POS setting provides a clear picture of the system's dependencies and limitations. This breadth gives significant weight to the paper's conclusions.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on English for Development Undermines Universality Claims:** The paper states that several key parameters were set based on the English development set. This includes the selection of the head rules themselves and, more critically, the fixed attachment directions for function words like AUX, DET, SCONJ, CONJ, and PUNCT. This introduces an English-centric bias. For instance, hard-coding determiners as right-attaching is problematic for languages with post-nominal determiners. While the on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction is clever, the lack of a similar mechanism for other function words weakens the claim that the parser is "completely free of language-specific parameters."
2.  **Overly Minimalist and Potentially Biased Head Rules:** The set of head rules in Table 1 is very small. While this contributes to simplicity, it also imposes severe structural limitations. The authors themselves note that the absence of a `NOUN -> VERB` rule prevents the parser from generating relative clauses, a fundamental syntactic construction. The paper would be stronger if it included a more detailed justification for the selection of this specific rule set (beyond "maximize precision on the development set") and a discussion of the trade-offs involved.
3.  **The "Unsupervised" Framing is Potentially Misleading:** While the parser requires no training *data*, it is heavily supervised by expert human knowledge. This knowledge is encoded in two places: the UD annotation guidelines themselves, and the authors' hand-crafted rules which interpret those guidelines. This is fundamentally different from grammar induction approaches (e.g., DMV) which attempt to learn structure from raw text. A more precise framing would be a "knowledge-based" or "zero-shot rule-based" parser. This is a matter of positioning rather than a technical flaw, but it is important for clarity within the field.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The fixed attachment directions for function words (e.g., DET is right-attaching) were tuned on English. Could you elaborate on how this known English-centric bias affects performance on typologically different languages, particularly those with, for example, post-nominal determiners or pre-posed auxiliaries? Have you considered extending your on-the-fly direction estimation from adpositions to other function word categories?
2.  The exclusion of a `NOUN -> VERB` rule to handle relative clauses seems like a major structural limitation. Could you provide more insight into the decision to exclude it? Did its inclusion drastically lower precision on other structures on the development set, and if so, which ones?
3.  The personalization factor for the main predicate in PageRank was set to 5. How sensitive is the model's performance to this specific value? Does a higher value risk incorrectly forcing the first verb to be the root in sentences with complex clausal structures?
