### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces UDP, a completely unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data and relies on a small, fixed set of head-attachment rules derived from UD principles. The core of the approach involves building a dependency graph for each sentence based on these rules and then using a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify the most central content words. Personalization is used to boost the score of the likely main predicate. A two-step decoding algorithm first parses the content words according to their PageRank scores and then attaches function words, ensuring they remain leaf nodes as stipulated by the UD formalism. The parser is designed to be language-agnostic, even estimating adposition directionality on-the-fly from the test data. The authors perform an extensive evaluation on 32 languages, comparing their system to a strong, supervised multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD). The results show that UDP is surprisingly competitive and, notably, more robust to both POS tagging errors and shifts in text domain.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong, simple, and transparent unsupervised baseline for UD parsing.** The primary contribution is the UDP system itself. It is elegant, easy to understand, and requires no training data, making it an excellent baseline for future work in cross-lingual and unsupervised parsing. Its surprisingly strong performance against a supervised transfer system demonstrates the power of leveraging the structural constraints of a standardized formalism like UD.
2.  **A comprehensive and insightful cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation.** The paper's evaluation is a significant strength. Testing on 32 languages demonstrates the method's broad applicability. The analysis of robustness to predicted POS tags and, especially, the detailed cross-domain evaluation provide compelling quantitative evidence that this rule-based system is more stable than its data-driven counterpart—a valuable finding for the community.
3.  **An effective application of personalized PageRank for predicate identification in parsing.** While PageRank has been used in parsing before, the specific use of personalization to up-weight the likely main predicate, combined with the two-step decoding process, is a clever and effective mechanism for structuring the parsing process around the sentence's syntactic core.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant Simplicity and Strong Performance.** The UDP method is laudable for its simplicity. It is built on a handful of linguistic rules and a well-understood algorithm (PageRank). Despite this, it achieves remarkable results, performing within 6.4 UAS points on average of a strong, supervised delexicalized transfer system across a massive set of 32 languages. This shows that a carefully designed, linguistically-motivated unsupervised system can be a viable alternative to complex data-driven methods, particularly in low-resource settings.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation.** The experimental setup is exemplary. The evaluation across 32 diverse languages strongly supports the "universal" claim of the approach. The authors responsibly test under both gold and predicted POS tag scenarios, demonstrating that their system is less sensitive to POS errors than the supervised baseline (error propagation of 0.37 vs. 0.46). The highlight is the cross-domain evaluation (Section 6.3), which provides clear evidence that UDP is more robust to domain shift (average std. dev of 2.5 vs. 4.5 for MSDG). This is a crucial and often overlooked aspect of parser evaluation.
3.  **Truly Unsupervised and Practical.** The system is genuinely unsupervised, requiring no annotated treebanks for training. The only language-specific parameter that is learned—adposition directionality—is estimated directly from the test data. This makes the parser highly practical for truly low-resource languages where no training data exists, which is a key objective of cross-lingual NLP.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The method, including the decoding algorithm (Figure 1) and the rule set (Table 1), is described with sufficient detail to enable reimplementation. The authors' commitment to releasing their code and newly annotated data further strengthens the paper's value to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on a Hand-Crafted Rule Set.** The parser's performance hinges on the head rules in Table 1. While these rules are linguistically motivated, their selection was optimized on English development data, which introduces a manual, knowledge-engineering step that may be biased towards English-like structures. The paper lacks an analysis of the system's sensitivity to this specific rule set. For instance, the hard-coded exclusion of a `NOUN -> VERB` rule (to handle relative clauses) limits the parser's structural capabilities universally based on a development decision for one language.
2.  **Limited and Hard-Coded Head Directionality.** The system cleverly estimates directionality for adpositions but uses fixed directions for all other function words (e.g., DET, CONJ), which were also set based on English. This is a strong assumption that does not hold universally, as acknowledged by the authors with the poor performance on Basque determiners. This weakens the claim of being "completely free of language-specific parameters" and suggests the system is not as adaptable as it could be.
3.  **Comparison is Limited to One Type of Baseline.** The main comparison is against a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD). While MSD is a strong baseline, it represents only one paradigm of cross-lingual transfer. The paper would be more comprehensive if it included comparisons to other unsupervised approaches, such as a modern implementation of a DMV model on UD, or discussed its relationship to annotation projection methods, which represent another major paradigm for cross-lingual parsing. Dismissing DMV based on a 2012 paper feels insufficient given the very different context provided by the UD project.
4.  **Evaluation is Unlabeled Only.** The evaluation is based solely on Unlabeled Attachment Score (UAS). Since the work is explicitly situated within the Universal Dependencies framework—where typed dependencies are a key feature—the paper misses an opportunity for deeper analysis. A discussion of how the system could be extended to produce dependency labels (many of which are highly predictable from POS tags, e.g., `aux`, `det`) and a qualitative analysis of common error types in terms of UD relations would provide greater insight into the parser's linguistic fidelity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the process of developing the head rules in Table 1? How sensitive is the parser's performance to small changes in this rule set (e.g., adding or removing one rule)? Have you explored methods for inducing such rules automatically?
2.  The personalization for PageRank uses a weight of 5x for the estimated main predicate. This value seems somewhat arbitrary. How sensitive is performance to this hyperparameter? Did you test other values, and does the optimal value vary across verb-initial vs. verb-final languages?
3.  Your system hard-codes attachment direction for function words like DET and CONJ based on English. Have you considered extending the on-the-fly estimation method you use for adpositions to these other categories? Do you hypothesize that this would significantly improve performance on languages like Basque where the fixed rules are clearly suboptimal?
