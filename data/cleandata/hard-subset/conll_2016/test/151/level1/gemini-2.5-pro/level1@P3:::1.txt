### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies. It requires no training data and operates by combining a small set of linguistically-motivated, POS-based head-selection rules with a PageRank-based word ranking algorithm. Key aspects of the method include personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate, a two-step decoding process that treats content and function words separately (enforcing UD constraints that function words are dependents), and a simple on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality from the test data. The system is evaluated on 32 UD treebanks and compared against a rule-based baseline and a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. The authors also conduct a detailed cross-domain evaluation to demonstrate the system's robustness.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A novel, fully unsupervised parsing method (UDP) that effectively combines simple linguistic rules with personalized PageRank.** The system is elegant in its simplicity yet surprisingly effective, achieving performance that is competitive with a supervised delexicalized transfer system. Its training-free nature makes it an excellent and easily implementable "zero-shot" baseline for future work on UD parsing, particularly in low-resource settings.
2.  **A thorough cross-domain evaluation demonstrating that the proposed rule-based system is significantly more robust to domain shift than a competitive data-driven parser.** The authors provide compelling empirical evidence (Tables 6 & 7) that their system exhibits lower performance variance across different genres and domains, confirming theoretical expectations about the stability of rule-based systems. This is a valuable finding for the community, highlighting a key advantage of such approaches.

### Strengths
1.  **Simplicity and Effectiveness:** The proposed method is remarkably simple, relying on a handful of head rules (Table 1) and a standard graph-ranking algorithm. The two-step decoding process is a clever and direct way to enforce the UD principle of function words being leaves. Despite this simplicity, the parser achieves a strong average UAS of 57.5% with gold POS tags, which is commendably close to the 63.9% of a multi-source supervised transfer system (MSDG). This makes UDP a powerful and accessible baseline.
2.  **Impressive Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain analysis in Section 6.3 is a major strength. The authors systematically measure performance across multiple domains for several languages and show that UDP has a consistently lower standard deviation in performance compared to the delexicalized parser (MSD). For example, the average standard deviation for UDPG is 2.5 vs. 4.5 for MSDG (Table 6). This provides strong empirical support for the claim that systems with fewer, more general parameters are less prone to overfitting and more stable when applied to new domains.
3.  **Thorough and Realistic Evaluation:** The evaluation is extensive, covering 32 languages from the UD 1.2 collection. Critically, the authors go beyond the common (and often unrealistic) gold POS setting. By evaluating with predicted POS tags (UDPP), they demonstrate the system's utility in a practical pipeline. The analysis of error propagation, showing that UDP is less affected by POS errors than the MSD parser (0.37 vs 0.46), further strengthens the paper's claims of robustness.
4.  **Novelty in Unsupervised Parsing:** While building on prior work like Søgaard (2012b), the paper introduces meaningful and effective innovations. The use of *personalized* PageRank to boost the score of the likely main predicate is a well-motivated heuristic that improves root accuracy. The two-step decoding algorithm is novel and specifically tailored to the constraints of the UD formalism. The ablation analysis in Section 6.1 clearly demonstrates that PageRank contributes significantly to the performance (+9 UAS points over a non-ranked version).

### Weaknesses
1.  **Over-reliance on English for Development:** The paper states that several key design choices were tuned on the English development set. This includes the PageRank teleport probability, the set of head rules in Table 1, and—most critically—the fixed attachment direction for function words like AUX, DET, SCONJ, and CONJ. This compromises the claim of being a truly language-agnostic or parameter-free system. For instance, fixing determiners (DET) as right-attaching is an English-centric choice that fails for many other languages. The paper's own results show this, with a very low DET UAS of 32% for Basque. This weakness could be mitigated by extending the on-the-fly direction estimation to other function words.
2.  **Limited Syntactic Coverage of the Head Rules:** The set of rules in Table 1 is very sparse and misses several fundamental dependency relations. The authors explicitly note that the lack of a NOUN → VERB rule makes it impossible to parse relative clauses, a major syntactic construction present in most languages. The reported poor performance on conjunctions (CONJ, 38% UAS) and punctuation (PUNCT, 21% UAS) suggests the simple proximity- and direction-based attachment for these categories is insufficient. While simplicity is a strength, the current rule set may impose a hard ceiling on the parser's maximum achievable performance.
3.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison to the Rule-Based Baseline:** The paper's main baseline (BLG) is described as not necessarily producing well-formed trees (i.e., structures may be disconnected). In contrast, the UDP decoding algorithm is guaranteed to produce a single-rooted, fully connected tree. This makes the comparison between them potentially misleading. The 4 UAS point gain of UDPNoPR over BLG (Section 6.1) might be partially attributable to simply enforcing a valid tree structure, rather than the superiority of the decoding algorithm itself. A clearer description of the baseline's output and how UAS is calculated for it would be beneficial.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The decision to fix the attachment direction for function words like DET and AUX was based on English development data, which is a significant limitation. Have you considered extending the on-the-fly bigram-based estimation used for ADPs to other function words? This seems like a natural way to make the parser more genuinely language-agnostic and could potentially fix poor performance on specific constructions in languages like Basque.
2.  The head rules are quite restricted, notably excluding relative clauses (NOUN → VERB). Could you elaborate on the trade-offs considered when selecting these rules? Was the NOUN → VERB rule excluded solely because it made verb-noun relations "less conclusive," or did its inclusion empirically harm performance on the English dev set? A deeper discussion of this would help readers understand the current limitations of the rule set.
3.  Regarding the baseline (BLG), could you clarify if the reported UAS scores are calculated over potentially disconnected structures, or if some post-processing was applied to form a tree? This information is important for correctly interpreting the contribution of your two-step decoding algorithm relative to this baseline.
