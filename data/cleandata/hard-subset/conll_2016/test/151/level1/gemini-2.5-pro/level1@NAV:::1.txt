This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies. The method requires no training data, instead relying on a small set of linguistically-motivated head-attachment rules derived from UD principles. It uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify and rank prominent content words, followed by a two-step decoding process that ensures function words are treated as leaves, consistent with the UD formalism.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A comprehensive and rigorous evaluation demonstrating that a simple, unsupervised parser can be highly robust. The key finding is that UDP is significantly more stable across domains than a strong, supervised delexicalized transfer system. This empirical result is the paper's most significant contribution.
2.  An elegant and effective unsupervised parsing algorithm. The combination of personalized PageRank for predicate identification and a two-step decoding algorithm is a clever adaptation of existing techniques to the specific constraints of the UD framework, resulting in a strong and transparent baseline.

**Strengths:**
1.  The evaluation is exceptionally thorough and convincing. Testing the system across 32 languages from the UD treebanks provides strong evidence for the method's broad cross-lingual applicability. The comparison against a competitive multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) grounds the performance claims solidly.
2.  The paper’s most compelling argument is the parser's robustness to domain shift. The analysis in Section 6.3 clearly shows that UDP has a much lower performance variance across different domains compared to the data-driven MSD system. This is a valuable finding for practical NLP applications, where domain mismatch is a common challenge.
3.  The proposed method is simple, transparent, and highly accessible. Because it requires no training data, it is easy to understand, reproduce, and apply to new languages, establishing it as an excellent baseline for future research in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The methodological novelty is somewhat limited. The approach builds heavily on prior work in rule-aided unsupervised parsing, particularly the use of PageRank for ranking words. While the adaptations for UD (personalization, two-step decoding) are effective, they represent an incremental improvement rather than a fundamentally new technique.
2.  The system is not entirely language-agnostic during development. Critical design choices, including the specific head rules in Table 1 and the fixed attachment directions for function words like AUX and DET, were tuned on the English development set. This introduces a potential anglocentric bias and slightly undermines the claim of being free of language-specific parameters.
3.  The parser struggles with complex syntactic constructions. As the authors acknowledge, the current rule set prevents the system from handling relative clauses and leads to very poor performance on conjunctions and punctuation. This limits its utility for downstream tasks that require a deep and accurate syntactic analysis.
