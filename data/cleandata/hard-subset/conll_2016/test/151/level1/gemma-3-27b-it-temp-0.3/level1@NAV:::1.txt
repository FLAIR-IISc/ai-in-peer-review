## Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages PageRank and a set of dependency head rules, requiring no training data. The core idea is to exploit the constraints inherent in the UD formalism to achieve competitive parsing performance.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that a surprisingly effective dependency parser can be built *without any training data* by carefully exploiting the constraints of the Universal Dependencies framework. While unsupervised parsing isn’t new, achieving competitive results—comparable to a delexicalized transfer system—without any learning is a significant achievement. A secondary contribution lies in the effective combination of PageRank personalization (to identify main predicates) and a two-step decoding process that respects the UD constraints on function word attachment. Finally, the thorough evaluation across 32 languages, and analysis of domain robustness, provides a valuable resource and benchmark for future work in unsupervised parsing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline & Competitive Performance:** UDP achieves competitive UAS scores (averaging 61.2% on the test sets) compared to a delexicalized transfer system (MSDG, averaging 63.9%). This is remarkable for a completely unsupervised approach and demonstrates the power of leveraging UD constraints.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The experiments demonstrate that UDP is more robust to domain changes than the supervised baseline (MSD), as evidenced by the lower standard deviation in UAS scores across different domains. This is a crucial advantage for real-world applications where data distributions can vary.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages is a significant undertaking and provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of both gold and predicted POS tag scenarios provides a realistic assessment of the system’s performance in low-resource settings.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the individual components (PageRank, head rules) are not novel in themselves. The core innovation lies in their specific application within the UD framework.
2. **Dependency on POS Tagging:** Although the system performs reasonably well with predicted POS tags, its performance is still heavily reliant on the accuracy of the POS tagger. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the impact of POS tagging errors on parsing accuracy.
3. **Simplified Handling of Complex Structures:** The system struggles with complex syntactic structures like coordination and relative clauses, as acknowledged by the authors. This limits its ability to parse more complex sentences accurately.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific linguistic motivations behind the chosen head rules in Table 1? Were these rules derived from linguistic theory or empirically optimized on a development set?
2.  The paper mentions that the personalization factor for PageRank (5x weight for the estimated main predicate) was tuned on English data. How sensitive is the performance of UDP to this parameter, and did you experiment with different values on other languages?
3.  What is the computational cost of running PageRank on a sentence, and how does it scale with sentence length?
