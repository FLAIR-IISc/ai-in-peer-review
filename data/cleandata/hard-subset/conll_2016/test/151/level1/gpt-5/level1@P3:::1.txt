Summary and contributions
This paper proposes UDP, a training-free, UD-compliant dependency parser that combines a small set of UD-inspired head rules with personalized PageRank (PR) to prioritize content-word attachment and a two-step decoding procedure that enforces function words as leaves. A simple, transductive heuristic estimates adposition direction from test bigrams. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages and multiple domains, and compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A strong, language-agnostic unsupervised UD parsing baseline that is competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer across many languages and more robust to domain shift. This is the main contribution.
2) A simple, linguistically-motivated pipeline (personalized PR + two-step decoding + UD head/leaf constraints) showing that enforcing UD design choices explicitly yields robust cross-lingual performance without training.
3) An empirical study of domain robustness in UD parsing, with curated domain splits and analysis of POS noise propagation.

Strengths
1) Competitive accuracy without training and robustness to domain shift. UDPG (57.5 UAS) trails MSDG (63.9) by only 6.4 points on average, and even outperforms it on Hindi. Under predicted POS, the drop for UDP (2.2 points) is smaller than for MSD (2.7), and the estimated error propagation from POS to dependencies is lower (0.37 vs. 0.46). Cross-domain variance (Table 6) is consistently lower for UDP than MSD, corroborating the claim that rule-based, UD-constrained methods are less sensitive to domain shifts.
2) Clear, linguistically grounded design. The two-step decoding that attaches content words first and forces function words to be leaves directly exploits UD’s design (function words as dependents), aligning model bias with the target formalism. The test-time estimation of adposition direction via ADP–NOMINAL bigrams is simple and effective (avg ADP UAS ~75%).
3) Thoughtful analysis and ablations. The paper quantifies the contribution of PR (≈+9 UAS over a no-PR variant) and of the decoding step over a rules-only baseline (≈+4 UAS on average). Breakdown by POS highlights where the approach works (DET, ADP) and fails (PUNCT, CONJ), and the discussion is candid about limitations (e.g., not modeling NOUN→VERB prevents relative clauses).
4) Broad multilingual evaluation and realistic settings. Results cover 32 languages with gold and predicted POS, plus a stress test with naïve CF tags. The domain experiments span multiple genres in four languages (including additional in-house English sets), which is valuable and uncommon in unsupervised parsing papers.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior unsupervised, rule-aided parsing. The approach closely follows Søgaard (2012b) in using PageRank-based ranking and rule constraints; the main additions are personalization of PR, a two-step decoding enforcing function-word leafness aligned to UD, and a simple test-time ADP-direction heuristic. While these design choices are well-motivated and effective, they appear incremental rather than conceptually new.
2) Transductive use of test data and comparability. Estimating ADP direction on the test set is reasonable for unsupervised parsing, but it complicates fairness against supervised transfer baselines that do not adapt on the target test data. A clearer accounting of how many tokens/sentences are used to estimate direction per language, and an ablation fixing direction from development or external statistics, would help assess sensitivity and comparability.
3) Rule coverage and expressive limitations. The small ruleset leads to systematic blind spots (no NOUN→VERB prevents relative clause attachment; poor handling of coordination and punctuation). The paper acknowledges these but the impact is not quantified per construction. Several languages (e.g., Basque DET direction) violate the fixed direction assumptions, yielding large drops; a data-driven estimation analogous to ADP for AUX/DET/SCONJ might substantially help.
4) Baselines and reporting could be stronger. The delexicalized baseline is appropriate, but omitting prior unsupervised baselines (e.g., Søgaard’s exact setups, strong DMV variants adapted to UD, or universal head-rule systems) weakens the empirical positioning. Also, the oracle direction in BLG is generous, yet BLG outperforms UDPG in several languages (e.g., Estonian 73.2 vs 70.0), which calls for a per-language analysis of when PR hurts and why. No significance testing is reported.
5) Methodological clarity and reproducibility details. The PR graph construction is only briefly specified; a complete list of head rules, and precise edge definitions/directions and weighting are needed for reproducibility. Sensitivity of performance to the personalization weight (5x) and teleport probability (0.05) is not explored beyond English tuning; cross-lingual stability of these hyperparameters should be documented.

Questions to authors
- Please provide the full head-rule inventory used to build the PR graph and the δ constraints, and clarify edge direction/weighting in the multigraph. Are all eligible dependents equally weighted?
- How sensitive is UDP to the personalization weight and teleport probability across languages? Did you try no personalization or different seeds for main predicate identification?
- How many sentences are typically used to estimate ADP direction per language, and does early/late estimation materially change results? Could you report an ablation where ADP direction is fixed from development, to quantify transductive gains?
- For languages where BLG > UDPG (e.g., Estonian), what failure modes drive PR to worse decisions? Is this correlated with word-order typology or POS distribution?
- Could you estimate directionality (right/left) for AUX/DET/SCONJ from data as done for ADP? Any preliminary experiments?
- What is the runtime per sentence and memory footprint for PR and decoding, and how does it scale with sentence length?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting results excluding punctuation for comparability with standard UD evaluations, alongside the inclusive setting you use.
- A labeled attachment experiment for a subset of edges (e.g., function-word labels) could illustrate how far simple constraints can go with minimal label prediction.
- The analysis of coordination could be expanded; even a simple heuristic for attaching cc/conj to the highest-ranked conjunct may yield gains.
- Some references and tables have formatting issues; please standardize citation years and table numbering for camera-ready.
