Summary: The paper introduces UDP, a training-free parser for Universal Dependencies that combines a small set of UD-compliant head rules with a personalized PageRank (PR) ranking of content words and a two-step decoding that enforces function-word leafness. It estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test POS bigrams. Evaluated on 32 languages and multiple domains, UDP approaches multi-source delexicalized transfer (MSD) performance and exhibits lower sensitivity to POS noise and domain shifts.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A strong, simple, language-agnostic unsupervised baseline for UD with broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation, showing competitive performance with MSD and superior robustness.
2) An effective algorithmic combination of UD-driven constraints with personalized PR for predicate identification and a two-step decoding that encodes UD’s function-word leaf constraint.
3) A lightweight, test-time estimation of adposition directionality from POS bigrams, avoiding language-specific parameters.

Strengths:
- Competitive accuracy with no training data: Average UAS within ~6–7 points of MSD (gold POS) and outperforming MSD on Hindi; modest degradation with predicted POS and lower error propagation from POS errors (0.37 vs. 0.46), underscoring practicality where gold POS is unavailable.
- Cross-domain stability: Lower UAS variance across genres than MSD in all reported languages, supporting the claim that rule-based UD priors yield robust behavior under domain shift.
- Clear ablations and analysis: PR contributes a substantial ~9 UAS over a no-PR variant; additional breakdowns (root accuracy, POS-wise behavior) substantiate design choices (e.g., PR personalization).
- Simplicity and portability: Minimal assumptions, no reliance on parallel data, and near-constant hyperparameters make the approach broadly applicable and easy to reproduce.

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty: The core ideas (rule-aided parsing, PR-based ranking) build directly on prior work; personalization and two-step decoding are incremental improvements.
- English-tuned decisions and ad hoc hyperparameters: Fixed function-word attachment directions derived from English harm certain languages (e.g., Basque DET), and parameters (teleport=0.05, weight=5) are tuned on English without systematic cross-lingual sensitivity analysis.
- Strong dependence on POS quality: Performance collapses under coarse CONTENT/FUNCTION tags; claims of robustness would be stronger with more realistic noisy POS settings and cross-tagset experiments.
- Structural coverage gaps: Excluding NOUN→VERB prevents modeling relative clauses and limits coordination handling; unlabeled-only evaluation leaves open how well the approach would extend to labeled UD.

Questions to Authors (optional):
- Can the attachment directions for AUX/DET/SCONJ be estimated per language (as with ADP) to reduce English bias?
- How sensitive are results to the PR personalization weight and teleport probability across languages?
- Is ADP direction estimated per document or corpus-wide, and how many sentences are needed for reliable estimation in truly low-resource settings?
- What is the runtime profile (PR vs. decoding) and scalability to long sentences and larger corpora? Are outputs frequently non-projective?
