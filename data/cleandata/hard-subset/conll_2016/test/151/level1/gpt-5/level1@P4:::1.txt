Summary and contributions
This paper presents UDP, a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). UDP combines a small set of POS-based head rules with personalized PageRank (PR) to rank content words and a two-step decoding scheme that enforces UD’s constraint that function words are dependents. It estimates adposition (ADP) attachment direction per language directly from test data. The system is evaluated on 32 UD languages and multiple domains, and compared to a multi-source delexicalized (MSD) transfer parser.

Main contributions (as assessed here), in order of strength:
1) A simple, UD-compliant unsupervised parser with two-step decoding that enforces function-word leafness and yields competitive UAS to a strong delexicalized transfer baseline across 32 languages and multiple domains.
2) A personalized PageRank setup for ranking content words (especially root identification), with ablation showing substantial gains over a rules-only system.
3) On-the-fly estimation of ADP directionality from test text, reducing language-specific parameters, and evidence of cross-domain robustness (lower variance than MSD).

Strengths
- Broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation with competitive performance:
  • On UD v1.2 test sets (32 languages), UDP with gold POS (UDPG) averages 57.5 UAS versus 63.9 for MSDG (a 6.4-point gap) and outperforms MSDG on Hindi; with predicted POS, the drop for UDP (2.2 points) is smaller than for MSD (2.7 points). This indicates the method is surprisingly competitive given it uses no training data for parsing.
  • Cross-domain robustness is quantified on several languages (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian). UDP exhibits lower standard deviation across domains than MSD (Table 6), suggesting reduced sensitivity to domain shift—consistent with the rule-based design.
- Clear isolation of PageRank’s contribution:
  • An ablation (UDPNoPR) shows PR yields ~9 UAS improvement over the same decoding without PR, and decoding itself yields ~4 UAS over the baseline. The analysis also reports ~69% average root accuracy, and PR personalization adds ~2 UAS on average while improving convergence.
- Minimal language-specific parameters:
  • ADP direction is estimated from bigram statistics on test data and is reported to succeed across languages (average ADP UAS ~75%). Fixed directions for other function words are globally effective, with some noted exceptions (e.g., Basque determiners).
- Methodological transparency and simplicity:
  • The algorithm is described precisely (graph construction, PR setup with teleport prob. 0.05, personalization weight 5×, two-step decoding), is deterministic for a POS sequence, and guarantees a single-rooted tree with clear back-off strategies. Computational complexity is analyzed, noting PR as the main cost.
- Error propagation and POS sensitivity analysis:
  • The paper quantifies error propagation from POS to dependencies (0.37 for UDP vs. 0.46 for MSD) at 94.1% POS accuracy, supporting the claim of relative robustness to POS errors.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on supervised POS tagging in the “predicted POS” setting:
  • The “UDPP” scenario uses TnT trained on each target language’s training set, which is not consistent with a strict unsupervised or low-resource cross-lingual setting. This limits the practical applicability in truly low-resource scenarios and complicates comparison to cross-lingual transfer baselines that rely on source-side data.
- Incremental novelty relative to prior unsupervised rule-aided parsing:
  • The core ideas—PR-based ranking for unsupervised parsing (Søgaard, 2012b) and rule-aided decoding—are known. The main novelties are adaptation to UD (function-word leafness via two-step decoding), PR personalization, and ADP direction estimation. While useful, these are incremental and largely engineering choices rather than fundamentally new algorithms.
- Limited syntactic coverage and reliance on back-offs:
  • The small rule set excludes certain phenomena (e.g., NOUN→VERB disallows relative clauses), and punctuation and coordination are weak (average PUNCT UAS ~21%, CONJ ~38%, with high variance). The back-off mechanism can ignore head rules and directionality to ensure a tree, potentially violating the intended UD constraints locally and making error modes hard to interpret.
- Baselines and evaluation choices:
  • The primary supervised baseline (MSD) is strong, but the paper omits comparison to other recent cross-lingual methods that leverage typological priors or small target supervision. The rule-based baseline (BL) is oracle-informed about attachment direction yet may not yield connected trees; including projective adjacency or stronger unsupervised baselines would strengthen claims. Significance testing across languages/domains is not reported.
- Development-time choices tuned on English:
  • Head rules, PR parameters, and function-word directions (except ADP) are selected using English development data. Although cross-language results are generally strong, this introduces a risk of bias and may not transfer uniformly (e.g., Basque determiners).

Questions to authors
- Can UDP operate in a truly low-resource setting where only cross-lingually projected or unsupervised POS tags are available? How does performance compare when POS tags are projected from source languages?
- How sensitive are results to the PR teleport probability (0.05) and the personalization weight (5×)? Could these be estimated per language/sentence without development tuning?
- Have you tried estimating directionality for other function POS (DET, AUX, SCONJ) from test data, as suggested by the Basque DET issue? What are the trade-offs?
- What is the runtime profile per sentence length and language (including PR) and how does it compare to MSD at test time?
- In back-off cases where rules and directionality are dropped to ensure a tree, how often does this occur and what proportion of attachments violate UD leafness/direction constraints?
- Could the approach be extended to labeled parsing via simple heuristics (e.g., label by POS-patterns), and what is the expected label accuracy?

Additional comments
- Reporting per-language significance against MSD and UDPNoPR would help quantify robustness claims.
- Given low PUNCT performance and variability, consider reporting UAS with and without punctuation for completeness.
- Clarify any inconsistencies in examples (e.g., minor typos) and provide released code/data to ensure reproducibility.
