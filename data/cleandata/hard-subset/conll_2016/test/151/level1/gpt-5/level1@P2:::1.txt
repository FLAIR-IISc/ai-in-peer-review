Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes UDP, a fully training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). It combines (i) a small, linguistically motivated set of head-selection rules aligned with UD conventions (function words as dependents), (ii) personalized PageRank (PR) to rank content words and identify the clause head, (iii) a two-stage decoding that first attaches content words and then function words to enforce leafness, and (iv) unsupervised estimation of ADP attachment direction from raw test sentences. The system is evaluated on 32 languages and compared against a multi-source delexicalized (MSD) TurboParser baseline; robustness to domain shifts and POS accuracy degradation is analyzed.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A simple, training-free UD parser that is competitive with a strong delexicalized transfer baseline across 32 languages and is more robust under domain shift. This provides a valuable, strong, multilingual baseline and a solid empirical finding.
2) An effective use of personalized PageRank for per-sentence head ranking combined with UD-motivated two-stage decoding that enforces function-word leaf constraints. Ablations show PR contributes substantially (≈9 UAS on average).
3) A lightweight unsupervised procedure to estimate adposition directionality on the fly, plus an empirical analysis of POS error propagation and domain variance across multiple languages.

Strengths
- Strong multilingual coverage and thorough evaluation: The parser is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 treebanks, using standard UAS on full test sets. The results with gold POS (UDPG ≈57.5) are within 6–7 UAS points of the MSD baseline (≈63.9), and the gap with predicted POS is similar. UDP even surpasses MSD on Hindi. This breadth supports claims of language-agnosticism and robustness.
- Robustness to POS noise and domain shifts, with quantitative analysis: The POS error propagation is lower for UDP (0.37) than for MSD (0.46), and across curated domain splits in several languages UDP exhibits lower standard deviation than MSD, indicating better stability to domain variation. This is a meaningful property for practical deployment and a notable contribution.
- Clear, linguistically grounded design aligned with UD: The two-stage decoding that first attaches content words and then function words enforces UD function-word leafness and single-root constraints, and the small rule set is interpretable. The bigram-based direction inference for ADP is simple, converges quickly, and achieves high ADP UAS (≈75%).
- Informative ablation and error analyses: The PR ablation (UDPNoPR) shows 9 UAS contribution from PR and ~4 UAS over the baseline from the decoding/tree-enforcement step, offering insight into what drives performance. POS-wise breakdown (e.g., DET/AUX/ADP vs. CONJ/PUNCT) and root accuracy analysis are useful diagnostics.
- Simplicity and potential reproducibility: The method is easy to implement, has few tunable parameters (teleport prob, personalization weight), and promises code/data availability. This increases the work’s impact as a community baseline.

Weaknesses
- Comparative baselines are incomplete: The paper does not include a direct replication/implementation of Søgaard (2012b)’s “unsupervised without training” approach on UD, despite being the most closely related prior work cited as stronger than DMV. Including this comparison (or a principled discussion with matched settings) is important to establish novelty and empirical gains over prior unsupervised PR-based methods. Likewise, stronger cross-lingual transfer baselines (beyond MSD) could serve as informative upper bounds.
- Use of target-language supervised POS in “predicted POS” condition: TnT is trained on gold target-language training data. While this isolates parser sensitivity to POS errors, it weakens the cross-lingual/low-resource narrative and gives UDP a favorable condition not available for truly low-resource targets. The CF setup is very crude; a more realistic cross-lingual POS tagger (projected or multilingual) would strengthen the claims.
- Test-set dependence for ADP direction estimation: Directionality is estimated on the test sentences. Although unsupervised, this peeks at the test distribution and may inflate performance compared to a setting where only development or unlabeled, out-of-domain text is available. A sensitivity analysis that estimates direction on a small dev or unrelated unlabeled corpus would clarify potential bias.
- Rule design choices are tuned on English and hard-coded for several function POS: While the authors show generality across languages, AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ direction was fixed from English dev. The paper itself notes exceptions (e.g., DET in Basque). Estimating all function-word directions from data (as done for ADP) could be more principled and possibly improve performance; an ablation would help. The explicit exclusion of NOUN→VERB (to avoid noun-headed clauses) prevents relative clause captures; the impact of this decision on languages with rich subordination is not quantified.
- Limited analysis of structural properties and sensitivity: No statistical significance tests on the main comparisons are reported; there’s little analysis of non-projectivity rates, edge-length distributions, or error types beyond POS categories (e.g., coordination scope, long-distance dependencies). Parameter sensitivity (teleport prob 0.05, personalization weight 5) is not explored beyond English dev.

Questions to authors
- Can you include a direct comparison to Søgaard (2012b) on UD v1.2 with matched POS settings? If not, can you explain why it is infeasible and provide a reasoned estimate of expected differences?
- How do results change if ADP direction is estimated on a held-out development set or a small unlabeled corpus from the same language, rather than on the test set?
- Have you tried estimating directionality for other function POS (DET, AUX, SCONJ, CONJ) in the same way as ADP? If so, what are the gains/losses across languages?
- Please provide a sensitivity analysis for the PR teleport probability and personalization weight. Do verb-final languages benefit equally from the “first verb” personalization?
- Can you add experiments with cross-lingual POS taggers (e.g., projected or multilingual models) to better approximate low-resource conditions, and report UDP vs MSD under those tags?

Overall assessment
This paper presents a clear, simple, and surprisingly strong unsupervised UD parser with broad multilingual evaluation and compelling robustness claims. While the methodological novelty over earlier PR-based unsupervised parsing is incremental, the UD-specific design, thorough multilingual/domain evaluation, and practicality warrant publication. Addressing the baseline and test-dependence concerns would further strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
