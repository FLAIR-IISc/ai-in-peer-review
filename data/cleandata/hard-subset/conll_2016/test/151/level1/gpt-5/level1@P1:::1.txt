Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents UDP, a training-free (unsupervised) dependency parser tailored to Universal Dependencies (UD). The method constructs a graph over tokens using a small set of POS-based head rules, runs personalized PageRank (PR) to rank content words, and decodes in two passes: first attaching content words (respecting UD constraints and PR order), then attaching function words with fixed directionality and ensuring they are leaves. A simple test-time heuristic estimates adposition attachment direction from ADP–NOMINAL bigrams. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under gold POS, predicted POS (TnT), and a crude CONTENT/FUNCTION tag setting, and compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (TurboParser).

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple, language-agnostic, rule- and PR-based UD parser that requires no training and achieves competitive UAS to multi-source delex baselines in many languages, with notable robustness to POS noise and domain shifts.
2) Methodological refinements to prior PR-based unsupervised parsing: personalization toward the main predicate, a two-step decoding that enforces UD leaf constraints for function words, and test-time estimation of ADP directionality.
3) A broad, multi-language and multi-domain evaluation highlighting error propagation from POS to dependency and cross-domain stability.

Strengths
- Strong, scalable baseline with zero training: UDP operates with UPOS tags and a handful of UD-compatible head rules, avoiding parallel data, projection, or target-supervised tuning. Despite its simplicity, it achieves average UAS close to delex transfer (57.5 vs. 63.9 with gold POS), and even surpasses it in Hindi. This is valuable as a reproducible, language-agnostic baseline across 32 languages.

- Clear exploitation of UD formalism: The two-step decoding (content first, function leaves second) operationalizes UD’s core design choices (single root, function words as dependents). This yields linguistically plausible trees and explains why the model is robust to cross-lingual variation without explicit training.

- Personalized PageRank improves root and content attachment: The paper shows that PR contributes substantially (≈+9 UAS vs. UDP without PR) and personalization adds a further ≈+2 UAS and faster convergence. This addresses a common pain point in unsupervised parsing: reliably identifying predicates and high-centrality content nodes.

- Robustness to POS noise and domain shift: Under predicted POS, UDP degrades slightly (−2.2 points) and exhibits lower error propagation from POS to heads than the delex baseline (0.37 vs. 0.46). Cross-domain evaluations (Bulgarian, English, Italian, Croatian/Serbian) show consistently lower UAS variance for UDP than for delex transfer, strengthening the case that rule-driven approaches generalize better across genres.

- Transparent analysis and limitations: The paper candidly discusses where rules help and hurt (e.g., excluding NOUN→VERB frustrates relative clause recovery), provides POS-wise error trends (e.g., DET works well cross-linguistically except Basque), and reports root accuracies and PUNCT/CONJ variability.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior PR-based unsupervised parsing: The core idea builds on Søgaard (2012b). The new ingredients (PR personalization, two-step decoding for UD constraints, and simple ADP directional estimation) are reasonable but incremental. As a CoNLL contribution, the novelty is moderate; the main value lies in breadth and careful engineering.

- Dependency on gold UPOS and per-language taggers undermines the “training-free” narrative in realistic settings: While the parser itself is unsupervised, the predicted POS setup uses TnT trained on target-language UD training data, which may be unavailable in true low-resource scenarios. The CONTENT/FUNCTION setting is a very weak proxy. A stronger story would include projected or cross-lingual POS tags, or robustness under noisy/universal taggers.

- Sparse and fixed head rules restrict coverage and structures: The small rule set is easy to interpret but excludes important phenomena (e.g., relative clauses, coordination scope, MWEs), leading to systematic errors (low CONJ and PUNCT UAS, inability to attach verbs under nouns when appropriate). This hampers performance ceilings and may bias comparisons against data-driven baselines that can learn such patterns, even delexicalized.

- Evaluation choices and fairness: The delex baseline uses 20k training sentences sampled across languages, but not all target languages or language families may have comparable amounts/qualities of source data in UD1.2. Conversely, UDP’s hyperparameters and rule set are tuned on English dev, yet results are reported across languages; the impact of this tuning on non-Indo-European languages is not quantified. Moreover, the oracle backoff direction in the BL baseline makes it less informative, and DMV (even if outperformed elsewhere) is not reported here for completeness.

- Limited statistical testing and ablations: While average improvements and some deltas (e.g., UDP vs. UDPNoPR) are provided, the paper lacks significance testing across languages or per-language ablations (e.g., effect of ADP direction estimation, function-word direction constraints). This would strengthen claims about the contribution of each component.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to the personalization weight (currently 5×)? Can you report a sweep (e.g., 1–10) on development and a subset of typologically diverse languages?

- For POS tagging realism, can you evaluate UDP with cross-lingual/projected UPOS tags (e.g., from bilingual lexicons or simple projection) to better approximate low-resource conditions?

- Your ADP direction heuristic works well overall, but Basque DET is an exception. Could you extend the data-driven direction estimation to other function POS (DET, AUX, SCONJ), and do you expect consistent gains?

- How much does the exclusion of NOUN→VERB as a head rule hurt overall UAS and specific phenomena (relative clauses)? Could a safer, context-sensitive version of that rule mitigate ambiguity?

- Can you provide per-language domain variance plots or tests to quantify the stability differences to MSD beyond standard deviations (e.g., paired tests per domain)?

- What is the runtime profile across sentence lengths and languages, and how does PR computation scale in practice compared to decoding?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The methodological novelty is modest, but the paper offers a compelling, clean, and effective unsupervised UD parser with broad multilingual evaluation and convincing robustness to POS noise and domain shift. It provides a useful, reproducible baseline for the community and highlights how far simple UD-aware constraints and PR can go without training. Strengthening the POS realism and adding ablations would further improve the paper.
