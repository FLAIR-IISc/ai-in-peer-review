**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents an unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) called UDP. UDP uses a small set of specific dependency head rules and PageRank to parse sentences without requiring any training data. The algorithm is designed to be linguistically sound and robust to domain changes across languages. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages and compare its performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system. The results show that UDP is competitive with the transfer system and more robust to domain changes.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Unsupervised dependency parsing**: UDP is a novel approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that does not require any training data. This is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. **PageRank-based parsing**: The use of PageRank to score content words and determine the main predicate of a sentence is a key innovation of UDP.
3. **Robustness to domain changes**: UDP's ability to perform well across different domains and languages is a significant advantage over other parsing systems.

**Strengths**

1. **Competitive performance**: UDP's performance is competitive with a state-of-the-art transfer system, which is a significant achievement for an unsupervised parsing system.
2. **Robustness to domain changes**: UDP's ability to perform well across different domains and languages is a significant advantage over other parsing systems.
3. **Linguistic soundness**: UDP's use of UD-compliant POS relations and function word constraints ensures that the parser produces linguistically sound parses.
4. **Efficiency**: UDP's use of PageRank and two-step decoding makes it an efficient parsing system.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited handling of complex sentences**: UDP's performance may degrade on complex sentences with multiple clauses or nested dependencies.
2. **Dependence on POS tags**: UDP's performance is dependent on the quality of the POS tags, which may not always be accurate.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The evaluation of UDP is limited to 32 languages, and it is unclear how well the system would perform on other languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does UDP handle sentences with multiple clauses or nested dependencies?
2. Can UDP be extended to handle other types of linguistic phenomena, such as semantic role labeling or named entity recognition?
3. How does UDP's performance compare to other unsupervised parsing systems?
4. Can UDP be used as a preprocessing step for other NLP tasks, such as machine translation or text summarization?
