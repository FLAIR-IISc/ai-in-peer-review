**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that relies on personalized PageRank and a small set of head-dependent rules. The parser requires no training data and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages in three setups, depending on the reliability of available POS tags, and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system. The results show that UDP is competitive with the delexicalized transfer system, while being more robust to domain change across languages.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Unsupervised dependency parsing**: The authors propose a novel approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that relies on personalized PageRank and a small set of head-dependent rules.
2. **UD-compliant parsing**: The parser is designed to produce UD-compliant parses, which is a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual dependency parsing.
3. **Robustness to domain change**: The authors demonstrate that UDP is more robust to domain change across languages compared to a delexicalized transfer system.

**Strengths**

1. **Competitive performance**: UDP achieves competitive performance with a delexicalized transfer system on 32 languages, which is a significant achievement in the field of unsupervised dependency parsing.
2. **Robustness to domain change**: The authors demonstrate that UDP is more robust to domain change across languages, which is an important property for a parser to have.
3. **Simple and linguistically sound approach**: The authors propose a simple and linguistically sound approach to unsupervised dependency parsing, which is a significant contribution to the field.
4. **No training data required**: UDP requires no training data, which makes it a valuable tool for low-resource languages.
5. **Wide applicability**: The authors demonstrate that UDP can be applied to 32 languages, which makes it a widely applicable tool.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation metrics**: The authors only evaluate UDP using unlabeled attachment score (UAS), which may not be sufficient to fully capture the performance of the parser.
2. **No comparison to other unsupervised parsers**: The authors do not compare UDP to other unsupervised parsers, which makes it difficult to assess its performance relative to other state-of-the-art systems.
3. **Limited analysis of errors**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by UDP, which could be useful for understanding its limitations and improving its performance.
4. **No evaluation on out-of-domain data**: The authors do not evaluate UDP on out-of-domain data, which could be useful for assessing its robustness to domain change.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does UDP perform on languages with complex morphology, such as Arabic or Russian?
2. Can UDP be used for parsing languages with non-projective dependencies?
3. How does UDP handle cases where the POS tags are incorrect or missing?
4. Can UDP be used for parsing text from different genres or domains?
5. How does UDP compare to other unsupervised parsers, such as those based on graph-based methods or neural networks?
