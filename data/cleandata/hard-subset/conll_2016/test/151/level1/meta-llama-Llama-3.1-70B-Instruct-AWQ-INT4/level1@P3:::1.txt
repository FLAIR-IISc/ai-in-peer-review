**Summary**

This paper presents an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD), called UDP, which relies solely on explicit part-of-speech (POS) constraints and personalized PageRank to estimate the relevance of content words. The parser requires no training data and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages and compare its performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system, showing that UDP is competitive and more robust to domain changes.

**Contributions**

1. A novel unsupervised dependency parsing approach that exploits the constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism.
2. A personalized PageRank algorithm that effectively estimates the relevance of content words and improves main predicate identification.
3. A simple and linguistically sound rule-based approach to cross-lingual parsing that is robust to domain changes.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and innovative approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that leverages the constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of UDP on 32 languages, demonstrating its competitiveness with a supervised transfer system.
3. The paper includes a detailed analysis of the parser's performance across different parts of speech and domains, providing insights into its strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper relies heavily on the quality of the POS tags, which may not always be accurate, especially for low-resource languages.
2. The parser's performance may be affected by the choice of head rules and the estimation of adposition direction, which may not always be accurate.
3. The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other unsupervised dependency parsing approaches, making it difficult to assess the novelty and impact of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of POS tag quality, especially for low-resource languages?
2. Can you provide more details on the choice of head rules and the estimation of adposition direction, and how these affect the parser's performance?
3. How do you envision UDP being used in practice, and what are the potential applications of this approach?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and its evaluation. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the parser's performance, which is helpful in understanding its strengths and weaknesses. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other unsupervised dependency parsing approaches to assess the novelty and impact of the proposed approach.
