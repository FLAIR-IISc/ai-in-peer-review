**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that uses personalized PageRank and a small set of head-dependent rules. The parser requires no training data and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that leverages the constraints of the UD formalism.
2. A competitive parser that achieves high performance on all but two UD languages, and even outperforms a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) on Hindi.
3. A robust baseline that is less affected by deteriorating POS tags and is more resilient to domain changes.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel and linguistically sound approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that leverages the constraints of the UD formalism. This approach is distinct from traditional data-driven methods and provides a fresh perspective on the problem.
2. The parser achieves competitive performance on all but two UD languages, and even outperforms a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) on Hindi. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach and its potential for real-world applications.
3. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the parser, including an analysis of its performance on different POS setups and across domains. This provides a comprehensive understanding of the parser's strengths and weaknesses.
4. The parser is robust to domain changes, which is an important property for real-world applications. The paper demonstrates this through an evaluation on different domains and shows that the parser is less affected by domain shifts than the MSD parser.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper relies heavily on the UD formalism, which may not be applicable to all languages or domains. The parser's performance may degrade when applied to languages or domains that do not conform to the UD formalism.
2. The parser uses a small set of head-dependent rules, which may not be sufficient to capture the complexity of all languages or domains. The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the rules' coverage and limitations.
3. The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other unsupervised dependency parsers. While it mentions that the parser outperforms the DMV model by Klein and Manning (2004), it does not provide a comprehensive evaluation against other state-of-the-art unsupervised parsers.
4. The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the parser's error propagation. While it mentions that the parser is less affected by deteriorating POS tags than the MSD parser, it does not provide a detailed analysis of the error propagation rates and their impact on the parser's performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend the parser to handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the head-dependent rules' coverage and limitations?
3. How do you plan to adapt the parser to languages or domains that do not conform to the UD formalism?
4. Can you provide a more comprehensive evaluation against other state-of-the-art unsupervised dependency parsers?
5. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the error propagation rates and their impact on the parser's performance?
