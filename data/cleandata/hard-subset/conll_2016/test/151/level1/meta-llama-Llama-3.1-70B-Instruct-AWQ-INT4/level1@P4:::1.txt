**Summary**

The paper presents an unsupervised dependency parser, UDP, for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages personalized PageRank and a small set of head-dependent rules. The parser requires no training data and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages and compare its performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

**Contributions**

1. **Unsupervised dependency parsing without training**: UDP is a novel approach that does not require training data, making it a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing.
2. **Effective use of PageRank and head-dependent rules**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of personalized PageRank and a small set of head-dependent rules in achieving competitive performance on UD languages.
3. **Robustness to domain change**: UDP shows a lower standard deviation across domains compared to a multi-source delexicalized parser, indicating its robustness to domain shifts.

**Strengths**

1. **Competitive performance**: UDP achieves competitive performance on all but two UD languages, and even outperforms a multi-source delexicalized parser on Hindi.
2. **Robustness to domain change**: UDP's lower standard deviation across domains indicates its ability to generalize across different domains.
3. **Efficient use of resources**: UDP requires no training data, making it a valuable approach for low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited handling of linguistic phenomena**: UDP's performance on certain linguistic phenomena, such as relative clauses, is limited due to its reliance on a small set of head-dependent rules.
2. **Dependence on POS tags**: UDP's performance is affected by the quality of POS tags, which can be a limitation in low-resource languages.
3. **Lack of explicit error analysis**: The authors do not provide an explicit error analysis of UDP's performance, which would be helpful in understanding its strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend UDP to handle more complex linguistic phenomena, such as relative clauses?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the effectiveness of personalized PageRank in UDP, and how it compares to other ranking methods?
3. How do the authors plan to address the limitation of UDP's dependence on POS tags, particularly in low-resource languages?
