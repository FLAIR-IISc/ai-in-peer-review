- Paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised UD parser requiring no training that leverages UD constraints and personalized PageRank to find predicates.  
- Contribution: a linguistically grounded unsupervised parser exploiting UD-specific constraints (function words as leaves, head rules).  
- Contribution: demonstrates robustness to domain shifts with lower standard deviations than supervised transfer systems.  
- Contribution: practical method to estimate adposition direction from test data without language-specific parameters.  
- Strength: linguistically principled two-step decoding that enforces function words as leaves, improving over prior rule-aided unsupervised parsers.  
- Strength: competitive performance with supervised transfer (UDPG close to MSDG; UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi).  
- Strength: superior domain robustness in cross-domain evals (lower stddevs than MSD, e.g., 1.8 vs 4.5 for gold POS).  
- Strength: simple, practical implementation with no language-specific parameters, working across 32 languages with identical config.  
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—mostly builds on Søgaard (2012b) with PageRank and two-step decoding as incremental changes.  
- Weakness: primary competitiveness claim relies on gold POS comparisons, which is unrealistic for practical use.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of failure modes (e.g., PUNCT, CONJ, excluded NOUN→VERB relations) and their impact.  
- Weakness: cross-domain evaluation is limited to a few Indo-European languages and not typologically diverse enough.  
- Question: why does UDP outperform MSDG on Hindi—what language characteristics enable this?  
- Question: how would UDP perform on typologically different languages (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Hungarian)?  
- Question: how does UDP fare with more realistic POS tagger error rates (e.g., 5–10%) beyond the binary CONTENT/FUNCTION scenario?
