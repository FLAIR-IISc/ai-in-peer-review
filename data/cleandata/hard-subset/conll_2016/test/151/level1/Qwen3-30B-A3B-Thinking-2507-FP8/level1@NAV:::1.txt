

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data and relies solely on UD's linguistic constraints. The main contributions, as I see them, are: 1) a linguistically sound unsupervised parsing approach that fully exploits UD's formal constraints (particularly function words as leaves), 2) a novel personalized PageRank implementation for main predicate identification that significantly outperforms naive ranking, and 3) a system that demonstrates remarkable cross-lingual robustness to domain shifts.

Strengths: First, UDP achieves competitive performance (within 6.4% UAS of supervised delexicalized systems) across 32 languages without any training data, which is a significant contribution to unsupervised parsing. Second, the system's robustness to domain changes is particularly compelling—UDP shows substantially lower standard deviation across domains than supervised baselines (e.g., 1.8 vs. 4.5 for MSDG), confirming that rule-based approaches can be more stable than data-driven methods. Third, the paper provides a clear and principled approach to handling UD-specific constraints (like function word leaf status and adposition direction), making the parser linguistically sound and consistent with the UD formalism.

Weaknesses: First, the claim of "no training" is misleading—the authors used English treebank during development to tune head rules and PageRank parameters, which means the system isn't truly training-free. Second, the paper doesn't sufficiently address the practical limitations of estimating adposition direction on test data (requiring 10-15 sentences), which might be problematic for short texts or low-resource languages. Third, the evaluation of the "naive POS" scenario (UDPN) is overly simplistic and doesn't reflect realistic POS tagging challenges, potentially overestimating the system's robustness to POS errors.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to unsupervised parsing by demonstrating that leveraging linguistic constraints can yield competitive performance without training data, while also providing evidence for the robustness of rule-based approaches to domain shifts.
