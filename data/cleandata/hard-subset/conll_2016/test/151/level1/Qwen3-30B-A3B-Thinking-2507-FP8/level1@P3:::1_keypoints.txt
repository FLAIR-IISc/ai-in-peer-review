- Proposal: UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser that requires no training and claims three contributions: exploiting UD constraints, PageRank-based predicate identification, and test-time adposition direction estimation.

- Contribution 1: Leverages UD formal constraints (function words as leaves, specific head rules) for a linguistically motivated rule-based parser.

- Contribution 2: Uses personalized PageRank to identify main predicates without language-specific tuning.

- Contribution 3: Estimates adposition direction at test time to avoid language-specific parameters.

- Strength: Demonstrates superior robustness to domain shifts across languages with lower variance than supervised transfer systems.

- Strength: Achieves linguistic coherence by enforcing UD constraints and two-step decoding to keep function words as leaves.

- Strength: Competitive performance (within ~6.4% of supervised MSDG) across 32 languages with gold and predicted POS.

- Weakness: Unfair comparison to supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer (MSD) rather than to prior unsupervised parsers.

- Weakness: Not evaluated in true zero-resource settings; relies on reasonably accurate POS tags (tested at ~94% accuracy).

- Weakness: English-centric development and tuning (head rules and PageRank parameters), contradicting the “no language-specific parameters” claim.

- Weakness: Limited evaluation on syntactically divergent languages (excluded Japanese, little coverage of agglutinative or non-Indo-European languages).

- Question: How does UDP perform on radically different syntactic typologies (e.g., SOV Japanese, agglutinative Turkish)?

- Question: How can the approach be adapted for languages with different word orders without English-tuned parameters?

- Question: How robust is UDP to much lower POS-tagging accuracy (e.g., ~85%) — theoretical or empirical error-propagation analysis requested.

- Overall: Valuable contribution leveraging UD constraints, but claims of being “truly unsupervised” and language-independent are overstated without more rigorous low-resource and typologically diverse evaluation.
