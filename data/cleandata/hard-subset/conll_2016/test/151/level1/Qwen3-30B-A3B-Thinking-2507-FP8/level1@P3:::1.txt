

# Peer Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. The authors claim three primary contributions: (1) a linguistically sound approach that exploits UD's formal constraints (particularly function words being leaves and specific dependency head rules), (2) a personalized PageRank method for identifying main predicates without language-specific tuning, and (3) a test-time estimation of adposition direction that eliminates language-specific parameters. The authors position this as a significant step toward truly unsupervised cross-lingual parsing that avoids the biases of transfer learning.

## Strengths

1. **Robustness to domain shifts**: The paper's most compelling contribution is demonstrating UDP's superior robustness to domain changes across languages compared to supervised transfer systems. The authors provide extensive evaluation across multiple domains (e.g., Bulgarian legal vs. news, English web forums vs. Bible) showing UDP maintains consistent performance with lower standard deviations (e.g., 1.8 vs. 4.5 for MSD), confirming Plank and van Noord's (2010) findings about rule-based systems. This is a critical advantage for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

2. **Linguistic soundness through formal constraint exploitation**: The paper makes a strong case for how UD's specific constraints (single-rooted trees, function words as leaves) enable a rule-based approach. By carefully adapting head rules to UD's formalism and using two-step decoding to enforce leaf status for function words, the authors achieve a linguistically coherent parser. This is a significant improvement over prior rule-based approaches that didn't properly leverage UD's constraints.

3. **Competitive performance with minimal assumptions**: UDP achieves remarkable results (within 6.4% of supervised MSDG on average), demonstrating that a small set of linguistically motivated rules can yield competitive performance without training data. The paper's evaluation across 32 languages with both gold and predicted POS tags provides strong evidence for the practical viability of their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading comparison to transfer learning**: The paper compares UDP to a supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer system (MSD), which fundamentally differs from an unsupervised approach. The authors claim UDP "compares favorably to delexicalized parsing," but this comparison is inherently unfair since MSD requires training on other languages. A more meaningful comparison would be to other unsupervised parsers (e.g., Søgaard 2012b), which would better demonstrate the novelty of their approach.

2. **Lack of true zero-resource evaluation**: While the paper shows robustness to POS errors (only 2.2% drop when using predicted POS), it doesn't evaluate on languages with truly poor POS tagging (e.g., <85% accuracy). The authors' claim that "UDP offers a linguistically sound unsupervised alternative to cross-lingual UD parsing" is undermined by the fact that their method still relies on reasonably accurate POS tags (which are often unavailable in low-resource settings).

3. **English-centric development**: The authors used English treebank during development to tune head rules and PageRank parameters, introducing language-specific bias despite claiming "completely free of language-specific parameters." This undermines their main selling point, as the system isn't truly language-agnostic but rather adapted to English's syntactic patterns.

4. **Limited generalization to syntactically divergent languages**: The paper excludes Japanese (the only SOV language in UD v1.2) and doesn't evaluate on languages with radically different syntactic structures (e.g., agglutinative languages). The claim that UDP "is distinctly robust to domain change across languages" is not sufficiently supported by the evaluation, which primarily covers Indo-European languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would UDP perform on languages with radically different syntactic structures (e.g., SOV Japanese, agglutinative Turkish) where the current head rules may not apply? Could you provide preliminary results on a language outside the Indo-European family?

2. The paper claims UDP is "completely free of language-specific parameters," yet the authors tuned PageRank parameters on English data. How would you adapt this approach for a language with a different word order or syntactic structure without using language-specific development data?

3. The paper shows only a small drop (2.2%) in performance when using predicted POS tags, but this assumes POS accuracy of 94.1%. How would UDP perform with a POS tagger that achieves only 85% accuracy (common in truly low-resource settings)? Could you provide a theoretical or empirical analysis of this error propagation?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to unsupervised parsing by leveraging UD's formal constraints, but its claims about being a "truly unsupervised alternative" are overstated due to the English-centric development and misleading comparison to transfer learning. With more rigorous evaluation in truly low-resource settings and a clearer demonstration of language independence, this work could make a significant impact.
