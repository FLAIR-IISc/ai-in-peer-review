

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The system uses personalized PageRank and a small set of dependency head rules adapted to UD's formalism. The authors claim their approach is linguistically sound, robust to domain changes, and competitive with supervised transfer systems. The parser respects UD's constraints, particularly that function words must be leaves (i.e., dependents, not heads), and estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. The adaptation of dependency head rules to UD's specific formalism, particularly incorporating the constraint that function words must be leaves (not heads), which is a key difference from previous unsupervised approaches.
2. The use of personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate (root) of sentences, significantly improving upon naive ranking approaches.
3. The estimation of adposition attachment direction directly from test data, eliminating the need for language-specific parameters and enabling true cross-lingual applicability.

## Strengths

1. **Truly unsupervised approach with impressive performance**: The most significant contribution is that UDP requires no training data at all, yet achieves results remarkably close to a supervised transfer system (MSD). UDPG (gold POS) is only 6.4% worse on average than MSDG (the supervised baseline), which is exceptional for an unsupervised parser. This demonstrates that UD's formal constraints can be effectively exploited to create a competitive unsupervised parser, which is a major contribution to the field.

2. **Demonstrated robustness to domain changes**: The authors provide compelling evidence that UDP is significantly more robust to domain shifts than data-driven approaches. Their cross-domain evaluation (Table 6-7) shows UDP has substantially lower standard deviation across domains (e.g., 1.8 vs. 4.5 for MSDG) while maintaining competitive accuracy. This addresses an important practical limitation of many supervised parsers and demonstrates a valuable property for real-world applications.

3. **Linguistically grounded approach**: The paper correctly identifies that UD's constraints (particularly function words being leaves) make it more amenable to rule-based approaches than other dependency formalisms. By designing their parser specifically to respect these constraints, they avoid many of the pitfalls of previous unsupervised parsers (e.g., determiners being heads of nouns in German). This principled approach to leveraging linguistic formalism is a significant contribution.

4. **Practical implementation**: The parser is straightforward to implement and runs efficiently (less than O(n²) time). The system is freely available, and the authors have made their in-domain annotated test sets available as well, which will facilitate further research in this area.

## Weaknesses

1. **The "no training" claim is misleading**: While the authors claim the parser requires no training data, they used the English treebank during development to tune parameters and select head rules (Section 3). This is a common practice, but it's important to clarify that the system isn't truly "training-free" in the strictest sense. The development process on English data means the system is implicitly tuned to English's linguistic patterns, which could affect generalizability to truly low-resource languages.

2. **Limited evaluation of POS tagger impact**: The authors use TnT for POS tagging, which might not be optimal for all languages. The POS accuracy varies across languages (94.1% average), but the paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how POS errors specifically affect parsing performance for different languages. This is particularly important for low-resource languages where POS taggers often perform poorly.

3. **Significant limitations in linguistic coverage**: The parser cannot handle relative clauses (Section 6.2) because the authors excluded the NOUN→VERB head rule during development. This is a major limitation for a dependency parser, as relative clauses are a fundamental linguistic phenomenon. The paper doesn't adequately address how this limitation might be overcome in future work or how it impacts the parser's applicability to real-world text.

4. **Vague description of personalized PageRank implementation**: The paper's description of the personalized PageRank (Section 3.1) is somewhat vague. It's unclear how the "estimated main predicate" is determined for sentences without verbs (e.g., noun phrases), which could lead to inconsistent performance across different sentence types. The authors should provide more detail about this critical component.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states the system uses the English treebank during development to tune parameters and select head rules. How much of the English treebank was used for this purpose, and how was it split (development vs. test)? This is crucial for understanding the true level of supervision involved in the system.

2. The authors claim the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters," but they estimate adposition direction from test data. For languages with very limited test data (e.g., low-resource languages), how robust is this estimation? What is the minimum amount of test data needed for reliable adposition direction estimation?

3. The paper mentions the parser cannot infer relative clauses because the NOUN→VERB head rule was excluded. How would the parser's performance change if this rule were included, and why did the authors choose to exclude it despite its linguistic importance? This seems to be a significant limitation that should be addressed.

4. The paper shows UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi (Table 5), but the authors don't provide a detailed error analysis for this case. What specific linguistic phenomena in Hindi might explain why UDP performs better than the supervised transfer system in this case?
