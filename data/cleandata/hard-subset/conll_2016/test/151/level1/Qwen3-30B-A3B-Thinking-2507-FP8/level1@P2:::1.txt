

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The system leverages UD's linguistic constraints (function words as leaves, specific head rules) and uses personalized PageRank to identify main predicates. The authors claim it's competitive with supervised transfer systems while being more robust to domain shifts.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1. A linguistically grounded unsupervised parser that effectively exploits UD's constraints (function words as leaves, specific head rules) rather than treating UD as just another dependency formalism.
2. Demonstrated robustness to domain shifts across languages, with significantly lower standard deviations than supervised transfer systems.
3. A practical approach to estimating adposition direction from test data without language-specific parameters.

## Strengths

1. **Linguistically sound approach to UD parsing**: The paper makes a compelling case for why UD's formalism enables a simpler, more linguistically principled unsupervised parser. By recognizing that UD enforces function words as leaves (unlike other formalisms), the authors design a two-step decoding process that ensures function words are always leaves. This is a significant improvement over previous rule-aided unsupervised parsers that didn't properly account for UD's constraints.

2. **Competitive performance with supervised systems**: The results are impressive - UDPG (with gold POS) is remarkably close to MSDG (supervised transfer system), with an average difference of only 6.4% UAS. The fact that UDPG outperforms MSDG on Hindi (53.9% vs. 52.0% UAS) is particularly noteworthy for an unsupervised system. This demonstrates that for some languages, an unsupervised approach can match or exceed supervised transfer methods.

3. **Superior domain robustness**: The cross-domain evaluation is the paper's strongest contribution. UDP consistently shows lower standard deviations across domains than MSD (e.g., 1.8 vs. 4.5 for gold POS), confirming that rule-based systems are inherently more robust to domain shifts than data-driven methods. This has significant practical implications for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

4. **Practical implementation with minimal parameters**: The system requires no language-specific parameters, estimates adposition direction from test data, and works across 32 languages with identical configuration. This simplicity is a major advantage over transfer methods that require extensive training and parameter tuning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in core methodology**: The paper builds heavily on Søgaard (2012b), with the main improvements being personalized PageRank and two-step decoding. While these are useful modifications, they don't represent a fundamental advance in unsupervised parsing. The PageRank personalization strategy is described as "intuitive" but lacks theoretical justification beyond empirical results.

2. **Overstated claims about competitiveness**: The main comparison (UDPG vs MSDG) uses gold POS tags, which isn't realistic for practical use. The paper acknowledges this by showing a 2.2% drop for UDP with predicted POS (UDPP) versus a 2.7% drop for MSD (MSDP), but the primary comparison still relies on gold POS. This undermines the claim that UDP is "competitive with a delexicalized transfer system" in real-world settings.

3. **Insufficient analysis of failure modes**: The paper identifies some limitations (e.g., poor performance on PUNCT and CONJ), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why these failures occur. For instance, the system's inability to handle NOUN→VERB relations (which the authors explicitly excluded during development) could be more thoroughly discussed in terms of how this limitation affects overall performance.

4. **Limited domain evaluation scope**: The cross-domain analysis is restricted to Bulgarian, Croatian, and Italian (with Serbian as a secondary language). This is insufficient to claim general domain robustness across typologically diverse languages. The paper mentions "languages with very different typological properties" but doesn't evaluate them.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states UDP outperforms MSDG on Hindi (53.9% vs 52.0% UAS), but the average difference between UDPG and MSDG is 6.4%. What specific characteristics of Hindi make it easier for the unsupervised approach? How would these characteristics manifest in other languages?

2. The paper claims UDP is "distinctly robust to domain change," but the domain analysis is limited to three language families (Indo-European). How would the system perform on languages with very different typological properties (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Hungarian)?

3. In the naive POS scenario (UDPN), UDP outperforms the baseline by 4% on average. However, this scenario uses only two POS tags (CONTENT/FUNCTION), which is extremely crude. How would the system perform with a more realistic POS error rate (e.g., 5-10% error rate, as seen in real-world POS taggers)?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to unsupervised parsing by showing that UD's constraints can be effectively leveraged to build a simple, robust parser that performs competitively with supervised methods. While the core methodology isn't radically novel, the practical demonstration of domain robustness and the effective use of UD-specific constraints represent significant advances. With more thorough discussion of limitations and evaluation across more typologically diverse languages, this work would be even stronger.
