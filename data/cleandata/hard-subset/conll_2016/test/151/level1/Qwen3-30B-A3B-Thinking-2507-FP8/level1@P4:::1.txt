

Summary: The paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. It leverages UD-specific constraints, including the restriction that function words are leaves, and uses personalized PageRank to identify main predicates. The system estimates adposition direction directly from test data and applies two-step decoding to handle content and function words differently. The main contributions are: (1) a linguistically sound unsupervised parsing approach that exploits UD constraints without training data; (2) a method to estimate adposition direction on test data eliminating language-specific parameters; and (3) a demonstration that this simple approach achieves competitive performance with supervised transfer systems while being more robust to domain changes.

Strengths:
1. Technical soundness and evaluation rigor: The paper provides a clear technical description of the method, including the PageRank setup, head direction estimation, and two-step decoding algorithm. The evaluation across 32 languages with three different POS setups (gold, predicted, and naive) is thorough and well-designed. The comparison to a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (MSD) is particularly valuable for establishing the significance of the results.

2. Competitive performance: UDP achieves remarkably close results to the supervised MSD system (average 6.4% UAS difference), outperforming it on Hindi. This is a significant achievement for an unsupervised parser, demonstrating that exploiting UD constraints can yield high-quality results without training data.

3. Domain robustness: The paper provides compelling evidence that UDP is more robust to domain changes than the supervised MSD system, with lower standard deviations across different domains (Table 6). This is a practical advantage for real-world applications where domain shift is common, and the results confirm earlier findings about rule-based parsers being more stable to domain shifts.

4. Simplicity and accessibility: The parser requires no training data and is freely available, making it particularly valuable for low-resource language settings. The paper's demonstration that the approach works well across diverse language families (including non-Indo-European languages like Tamil and Hungarian) is impressive for an unsupervised method.

Weaknesses:
1. Limited novelty: While the paper adapts existing PageRank-based parsing to UD constraints, the core methodology builds directly on Søgaard (2012b) with relatively minor modifications. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing work, particularly regarding the novelty of the head rules for UD specifically.

2. POS dependency: The performance drops significantly when using predicted POS tags (2.2% UAS decrease compared to gold POS), and the paper doesn't adequately address how this dependency might limit practical deployment. The error propagation analysis is interesting but doesn't fully resolve concerns about real-world applicability where POS accuracy is not perfect.

3. Head rule limitations: The head rules in Table 1 don't provide full coverage, and the system makes ad-hoc decisions for words not covered by the rules (e.g., words with POS X or SYM). The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide a systematic way to handle it, which could lead to inconsistent performance across languages.

4. Evaluation scope: The evaluation is limited to UAS, which doesn't capture all aspects of dependency parsing quality. The paper doesn't address whether the parser produces linguistically plausible parses beyond the UAS metric, nor does it provide detailed error analysis for specific dependency types (beyond the brief POS breakdown in Section 6.2).

Questions to Authors:
1. How do the head rules in Table 1 specifically differ from those used in prior UD-compliant parsing work, and why were these particular rules chosen over others?

2. The paper states that UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi, but the results in Table 5 show only a small margin (55.3 vs 54.8 UAS for UDPG vs MSDG). Could you provide more detailed error analysis for Hindi to better understand why UDP outperforms the supervised system in this case?

3. The paper demonstrates that UDP is more robust to domain changes, but the evaluation is limited to a subset of languages (Bulgarian, Croatian, Italian). How would you expect the domain robustness to hold for the remaining languages in the UD treebanks?
