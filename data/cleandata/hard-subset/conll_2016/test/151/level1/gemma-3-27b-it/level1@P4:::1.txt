This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) leveraging PageRank and dependency head rules. The core claim is that UDP achieves competitive performance to a delexicalized transfer system *without* any training, and exhibits greater robustness to domain change. The authors identify four contributions: adapting dependency head rules to UD, incorporating the UD function word constraint, utilizing personalized PageRank, and estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data. I believe the most significant contribution is the demonstration of a reasonably effective, truly unsupervised parser within the UD framework, especially its resilience to domain shift, rather than any single methodological innovation. The detailed analysis of performance across languages and domains, alongside the publicly released code, also represents a valuable contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline & Competitive Performance:** UDP achieves impressive results for a genuinely unsupervised parser. The comparison to a delexicalized transfer system (MSD) – a strong, supervised baseline – highlights the effectiveness of the approach.  The observation that UDP often outperforms MSD, particularly in scenarios with domain shift, is compelling. Table 5 clearly demonstrates this competitiveness, with UDPG often exceeding MSDG.
2. **Domain Robustness:** The extensive evaluation across diverse domains (Table 6 & 7) is a major strength. The results consistently show that UDP’s performance is less sensitive to domain changes compared to the supervised MSD system, supporting the authors’ claim of robustness. The lower standard deviation in UAS across domains for UDP confirms this.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation & Analysis:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation across 32 languages, using standard metrics (UAS). The investigation of performance under different POS tagging conditions (gold, predicted, naive) provides valuable insights into the system's sensitivity to input quality. The detailed error analysis, including breakdowns by POS and domain, provides a nuanced understanding of the system’s strengths and weaknesses.
4. **Clear & Reproducible Methodology:** The method is presented clearly and logically, with a step-by-step explanation of the PageRank setup, head direction estimation, and decoding algorithm.  The public availability of the parser and annotated datasets facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of techniques is reasonable, the individual components (PageRank, head rules, UD constraints) are not novel in themselves. The novelty lies in their specific *application* within the UD framework and the resulting performance, but the paper could better emphasize this.
2. **Dependence on English for Initial Tuning:** The reliance on English treebank data for parameter tuning (PageRank parameters, function word directionality) introduces a potential bias. While the authors acknowledge this, it’s unclear how sensitive the results are to this initial tuning step.  A more systematic exploration of parameter tuning strategies across multiple languages would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
3. **Simplistic POS Scenario:** The "naive" two-POS tag scenario (CONTENT/FUNCTION) seems overly simplistic and may not accurately represent real-world low-resource conditions. It's a useful lower bound, but more realistic POS error models would provide a more informative assessment of robustness.
4. **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the paper provides some error analysis, a deeper investigation of specific failure cases (e.g., languages where performance is significantly lower) and the underlying linguistic reasons would be valuable. Understanding *why* the parser struggles in certain scenarios could guide future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the PageRank parameters and function word directionality settings to the language used for initial tuning (currently English)?  Could a different language selection significantly impact performance?
2.  The paper mentions exclusion of Japanese due to missing word forms. Were other languages excluded and why? Was there any attempt to adapt the method to languages with different morphological characteristics (e.g., agglutinative languages)?
3.  Could you provide more details on the annotation process for the in-house English datasets? (e.g. inter-annotator agreement, annotation guidelines).
4.  The paper states that personalized PR makes the algorithm converge faster. Could you quantify this speedup?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of "L" and "R" to denote head direction is not immediately clear without referring to the text. A brief explanation in Table 5 would be helpful.
*   The reference list is extensive, but the formatting is inconsistent.
*   The phrasing “zero-shot” would be more appropriate than "without training" to highlight the unsupervised nature of the approach.
