This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) leveraging PageRank and dependency head rules. The core idea is to exploit the constraints inherent in the UD formalism to build a parser without relying on any training data, achieving competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer system and exhibiting robustness to domain shift.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A fully unsupervised, competitive UD parser:** The most significant contribution is the demonstration that a reasonably accurate dependency parser can be built *without any training data* by effectively leveraging the UD constraints and a clever application of PageRank.  This is particularly noteworthy given the current reliance on large, annotated datasets in NLP.
2. **Effective combination of rule-based and graph-based methods:**  The authors successfully combine linguistically motivated dependency head rules with the PageRank algorithm for improved predicate identification and overall parsing accuracy. The two-step decoding process, separating content and function words based on UD principles, is a key aspect of this approach.
3. **Robustness to domain shift:** The demonstrated robustness of UDP to domain changes is a valuable contribution. The results consistently show that UDP maintains performance across different domains better than the supervised baseline, suggesting the benefits of its reliance on general linguistic principles rather than domain-specific patterns.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The parser achieves surprisingly competitive performance on 32 languages, consistently reaching UAS scores comparable to a delexicalized transfer system. The consistent performance across a diverse set of languages demonstrates the generality of the approach.
2. **Clear and well-motivated methodology:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind the approach, grounding it in the limitations of existing methods and the strengths of the UD framework. The design choices, such as the personalization of PageRank and the two-step decoding process, are well-explained and justified.
3. **Thorough evaluation:**  The evaluation is comprehensive, encompassing multiple evaluation scenarios (gold vs. predicted POS tags) and a detailed analysis of cross-domain performance. The inclusion of both automatic evaluation and qualitative error analysis strengthens the findings.
4. **Reproducibility:**  The authors explicitly state that the parser and the datasets used are freely available, fostering reproducibility and further research.
5. **Insightful error analysis:** The discussion section provides a useful analysis of the parser's strengths and weaknesses, identifying areas for future improvement. The observation about the stability of the rule-based parser compared to the data-driven one is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the individual components (PageRank, dependency head rules) are not novel in themselves. The novelty lies in their application within the specific context of UD and the clever integration of these techniques.
2. **Dependency on POS tags:** The parser's performance is still dependent on the quality of POS tags. While the results show robustness to errors in POS tagging, the reliance on POS tags remains a limitation. An ideal fully unsupervised system would not require POS tags as input.
3. **Simplification of linguistic phenomena:** The reliance on a limited set of dependency head rules inevitably leads to simplifications of complex linguistic phenomena. For example, the inability to handle relative clauses as noted in the paper is a significant limitation.
4. **Limited analysis of failure cases:** While the paper provides some error analysis, a more in-depth investigation of the types of errors made by the parser would be beneficial. Specifically, analyzing errors related to specific POS tags or dependency relations could provide valuable insights for improvement.
5. **Presentation of results:** The numerous tables can be a bit overwhelming, and the main takeaways from the results could be highlighted more effectively.  A more concise presentation of the key findings would improve readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific criteria used to select the dependency head rules presented in Table 1? What methods were used to evaluate the contribution of each rule during development?
2.  The paper mentions that the personalization weight for the main predicate (5x) was tuned on English data. Did you experiment with different personalization weights on other languages, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Regarding the simplification of linguistic phenomena (e.g., relative clauses), are there plans to extend the parser to handle more complex structures in the future? If so, what approaches are being considered?
4.  The domain evaluation shows variations in performance across domains. Could you provide examples of specific linguistic characteristics of the "difficult" domains (e.g., bulletin, legal) that contribute to lower parsing accuracy?
5.  You mention that the UDP parser is deterministic for a certain input POS sequence. Could this determinism be a potential drawback in scenarios where multiple valid parse trees exist?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and demonstrates a thorough understanding of the relevant literature.
*   The writing is clear and concise, making the paper easy to follow.
*   The use of figures and tables is effective in illustrating the methodology and results.
