## Review for “Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training”

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) leveraging PageRank and linguistically motivated head rules. The core idea – exploiting UD’s constraints to build a parser without training – is promising, and the authors demonstrate competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer system across a substantial number of languages (32). The main contribution lies in the successful application of personalized PageRank within a UD-compliant framework, demonstrating that a simple, rule-based approach can achieve surprisingly strong results. A secondary contribution is the detailed analysis of the system’s robustness to domain shift, showing a relative advantage over a supervised baseline in this regard. 

The strengths of this submission are significant. The breadth of the evaluation—covering a large and diverse set of languages—is commendable. The experiments clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, particularly in low-resource scenarios where training data is scarce. The ablation study (demonstrating the contribution of PageRank) is well-executed and insightful. The comprehensive error analysis, particularly the assessment of cross-domain performance, further strengthens the paper, showcasing UDP's relative stability compared to data-driven approaches. The authors also make their parser and datasets available, promoting reproducibility.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the method is presented clearly, the novelty of the individual components (PageRank, head rules) is limited. The personalization of PageRank, though effective, isn’t a fundamentally new technique. The impact of specific head rules isn't deeply investigated; the rationale behind choosing the particular rules feels somewhat arbitrary. While the domain shift analysis is strong, the selected domains are not uniformly represented, potentially skewing the conclusions.  Furthermore, the evaluation relies heavily on POS tags. Although experiments with predicted tags are included, the system's sensitivity to POS accuracy could be more thoroughly explored.  The claim of “completely free of language-specific parameters” is somewhat misleading, as the directionality of adpositions is estimated *on* the test data, constituting a form of language-specific adaptation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the chosen head rules, perhaps through an error analysis of rules that were considered but ultimately excluded?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm?  Was a more systematic exploration of this parameter performed?
3.  The claim about being "free of language-specific parameters" is concerning, given the adposition direction estimation. Can you clarify this claim and potentially rephrase it to reflect this adaptation step?
4.   You mention potential extensions for handling multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names. Could you elaborate on the challenges in adapting the current method to these phenomena and outline a potential roadmap for future work in these areas?
