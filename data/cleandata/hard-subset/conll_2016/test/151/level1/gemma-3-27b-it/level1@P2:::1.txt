This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies, based on PageRank and a set of dependency head rules. The core idea is compelling: leveraging the constraints inherent in the UD formalism to build a parser *without* training data. The main contributions, as I see them, are 1) the successful adaptation of dependency head rules to the UD framework, enabling a rule-based approach; 2) the incorporation of personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, a crucial step in unsupervised parsing; and 3) the demonstration of competitive performance – comparable to a delexicalized transfer system – and increased robustness to domain change.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** While unsupervised parsing is not new, the explicit focus on exploiting UD constraints is a significant and novel aspect. The authors convincingly argue that UD's consistent formalism lends itself well to a rule-based approach, and they demonstrate this with a surprisingly effective system.  The fact that it avoids training altogether is a notable distinction.

2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves competitive UAS scores (averaging around 61.2%) across 32 languages *without* any language-specific training. This is impressive, especially when compared to a delexicalized transfer system (MSDG, averaging around 63.9%). The results demonstrate the viability of the approach and its potential for low-resource languages.  The robustness to domain change, highlighted in Section 6.3, is a particularly valuable finding.

3. **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The step-by-step decoding algorithm (Fig. 1) and the detailed example in Section 4 are particularly helpful for understanding the inner workings of the parser. The comprehensive experimental setup, including multiple evaluation scenarios (gold POS vs. predicted POS, domain change), strengthens the credibility of the results.

4. **Robustness Analysis:**  The examination of performance across different domains (Table 6) demonstrates the strength of the method. The lower standard deviation compared to the supervised baseline MSD suggests greater stability. This makes UDP a more reliable option when dealing with diverse text types.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Rule Coverage:** While the head rules are effectively adapted to UD, their coverage is not complete. The paper acknowledges this and provides a baseline comparison. However, the impact of incomplete rule coverage on parsing accuracy isn't fully explored, and the reliance on a fallback mechanism could introduce errors. It's unclear how systematically the rules were developed and whether there's a principled way to expand them.

2. **Sensitivity to POS Tagging:**  Although UDP demonstrates robustness *relative* to the supervised baseline when using predicted POS tags, the performance drop (around 10 UAS points on average) is still substantial. This highlights a critical dependency on POS tagging accuracy.  While inevitable, a deeper analysis of error propagation from POS tagging to dependency parsing would be beneficial.  The authors acknowledge this somewhat, but more detailed investigation is warranted.

3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** Beyond the high-level discussion of POS tagging errors, the paper lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Identifying specific types of parsing errors (e.g., errors related to coordination, relative clauses, or specific POS tags) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.  Knowing *where* the parser struggles is crucial for guiding further development.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How were the initial dependency head rules determined? Was there a systematic process for selecting and refining these rules, beyond the English development data?

2. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of POS tagging errors on parsing accuracy?  Specifically, what types of POS tagging errors lead to the most significant degradation in parsing performance?

3. The paper mentions potential extensions to handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names. Could you elaborate on how you envision addressing these challenges within the UDP framework?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation in Fig. 1 could be slightly improved for clarity (e.g., defining the functions γ, δ, and κ more explicitly).
* The references are extensive and relevant.
* The availability of both the parser and the datasets is a significant contribution to the research community.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to unsupervised dependency parsing. The results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and thoroughly evaluated. Despite some minor weaknesses, the potential benefits of this approach, particularly for low-resource languages and domains, are significant. I recommend acceptance.
