Summary
This paper presents UDP, a deterministic, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that relies on a small set of POS-based head rules, a personalized PageRank (PR) ranking over a graph defined by those permitted dependencies, and a two-phase decoding scheme. Words are first ranked (with extra prior mass on the main predicate) and content words are attached in PR order to their closest permissible head; then function words are attached with directionality constraints, yielding single-root trees with function words as leaves. The method is evaluated on 32 UD languages (gold and predicted POS) and several domain splits, showing competitive UAS compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline and improved robustness to domain shift and POS errors.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A thorough multilingual and cross-domain evaluation establishing a strong, simple training-free baseline for UD parsing that is competitive with delexicalized transfer.
2) A deterministic unsupervised parsing algorithm that combines POS head rules with personalized PageRank and a two-step decoding that enforces UD-style headedness of function words.
3) An empirical finding that test-time estimation of adposition directionality converges quickly and yields robust performance across languages.

Strengths
- Breadth and robustness of evaluation: 32 languages, multiple POS conditions (gold/predicted/CF), and cross-domain analysis (Tables 5–7). UDP’s low variance across domains and reduced error propagation from POS to dependencies are convincingly supported (e.g., lower std devs in Table 6 and error propagation analysis).
- Simplicity and determinism: The parsing procedure is straightforward, fast, and easy to reproduce. Deterministic decoding with minimal parameters is valuable as a community baseline, especially for low-resource settings.
- Competitive results without training: With only POS tags and head rules, UDPG is on average within ~6–7 UAS of a delexicalized transfer system and even outperforms it on Hindi. The predicted POS setup degrades gracefully.
- Clear ablations: Section 6.1 quantifies PR’s contribution (~+9 UAS) and the gains from the two-step decoding over a rule-only baseline (~+4 UAS), supporting the design choices.
- Clarity of presentation: The algorithm is well described, with a worked example and transparent design constraints aligned with UD principles.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to Søgaard (2012): The core pipeline (dependency-rule graph + PR ranking + deterministic decoding) is very close to Søgaard’s unsupervised PR-based approach. The new elements—PR personalization and a two-step decoding to force function words as leaves—are useful but incremental. The paper would benefit from deeper analytical insights or stronger algorithmic innovations.
- Potential test-set tuning/data snooping: Adposition directionality is estimated on the test set using ADP–NOMINAL vs NOMINAL–ADP bigram counts. Even if unsupervised, this departs from standard experimental hygiene and complicates fairness when comparing to trained baselines. The paper argues convergence after 10–15 sentences, but does not quantify the impact or explore train/dev-only estimation.
- Missing structural diagnostics of the head-rule graph and back-off behavior: The PR graph is often disconnected by design; there is no analysis of how often this occurs, the distribution of component sizes, or how that correlates with PR quality and downstream UAS. Similarly, the decoding includes progressive constraint back-offs (drop δ; then drop δ and κ) but the frequency and distribution of these back-offs are not reported.
- Tie-breaking is unspecified: Both PR can yield equal scores (especially in small/disconnected graphs) and the “closest head” rule can yield equidistant candidates. Without a documented tie-breaking policy, determinism and reproducibility across toolchains are unclear; ties could systematically bias attachments or impact language-specific outcomes.
- Missing oracle upper bounds per constraint: It is hard to attribute errors to (i) head-rule coverage, (ii) directionality constraints, or (iii) distance minimization. Per-constraint oracle studies would expose ceilings (e.g., perfect directionality; perfect head-rule coverage; relaxing distance; or oracle PR ranking) and clarify which constraint is the main bottleneck per language.

Questions to Authors
- PR graph diagnostics: What fraction of sentences yield disconnected PR graphs? What are the distributions of component sizes? Do more/ larger components correlate with lower UAS? How sensitive are results to the teleport probability 0.05?
- Back-off usage: In decoding, how often do you drop δ (rule) and/or κ (direction) constraints across languages? Please report rates and UAS with/without back-offs to quantify their impact.
- Tie-breaking: How do you break ties in PR ranking and in nearest-head selection when multiple candidates are equidistant and permitted? Have you measured whether alternative tie-breakers (e.g., leftmost, rightmost, PR of head) change UAS?
- Directionality estimation: Can you re-run with directionality estimated from training/dev POS (or from raw unlabeled text) rather than test, and quantify the difference? How quickly does the estimate stabilize per language (learning curve)?
- Oracle analyses: Please report oracle UAS for (a) perfect ADP directionality; (b) perfect head-rule coverage; (c) ignoring distance minimization (attach to the best licensed gold head when permitted); and (d) oracle PR ranking of content words.
- Sensitivity to head-rule inventory: How robust are results to small perturbations of Table 1? Do languages with lower performance require language-specific rule tweaks, or are errors mainly due to phenomena like relative clauses and coordination?

Additional comments and minor edits
- Head-rule/corpus analysis: Please add corpus statistics on (i) frequency of disconnected PR graphs and component sizes; (ii) percentage of tokens covered by head rules; (iii) proportion of attachments created via each back-off level; and (iv) per-language ADP directionality estimates vs. gold.
- Fairness note: Consider re-estimating all global decisions (e.g., directionality for ADP and other function words) from train/dev or via cross-validation to avoid test-time peeking.
- Consider estimating directionality for DET/AUX/SCONJ per language (as done for ADP) rather than fixing from English dev; Basque DET is a clear counterexample.
- Table formatting: Reorder columns in Table 5 to match Table 7 (prefer Table 7 order), to ease cross-table comparisons.
- Typos/wording:
  - Sec. 3.1: “The the main idea” → “The main idea”
  - Sec. 4.1: “we obtain rank” → “we obtain the rank”
  - Sec. 6.1: “isolate the constribution of both” → “isolate the contribution of both”
  - Various: “adpositon” → “adposition”
- Overall: Interesting, clearly presented, and useful as a strong unsupervised baseline, but with limited novelty over Søgaard (2012). Strengthening the experimental hygiene (directionality estimation), providing graph/back-off diagnostics, and adding per-constraint oracle bounds would significantly improve the paper.
