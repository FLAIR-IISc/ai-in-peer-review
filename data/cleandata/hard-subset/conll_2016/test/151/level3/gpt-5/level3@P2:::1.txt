Summary and contributions
This paper presents UDP, a deterministic, training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method constructs a POS-constrained dependency multigraph, ranks content words with personalized PageRank (PR), and decodes in two stages: first attaching content words (enforcing a single root) and then attaching function words as leaves, guided by head-direction heuristics. The system estimates adposition (ADP) directionality directly from test data via bigram counts and fixes directionality for other function words based on development observations. UDP is evaluated on 32 UD languages and several domain splits and compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline.

Contributions as I see them (ordered by strength):
1) A simple, fully unsupervised UD parser that is competitive with delexicalized transfer across many languages, with notably strong robustness to POS noise and domain shifts.
2) A linguistically motivated decoding scheme that enforces UD constraints (single root; function words as leaves), paired with personalized PR over a POS-rule-induced graph to identify main predicates and guide attachment.
3) A practical, language-agnostic procedure to infer adposition attachment direction on the fly from test data.

Strengths
- Broad, careful evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domains, with standard UAS. The model is on average within ~6.4 UAS of multi-source delexicalized parsing with gold POS and even surpasses it for Hindi. The predicted-POS setting shows smaller degradation than delexicalized transfer (2.2% vs. 2.7%), indicating robustness to POS errors.
- Cross-domain robustness convincingly demonstrated. The analysis shows markedly lower performance variance across genres than the delex baseline, in both gold and predicted POS conditions, consistent with the method’s low-parameter, rule-based nature.
- Clear, reproducible algorithmic description. The deterministic two-stage decoding and explicit rule set make the approach easy to implement and reason about. The complexity discussion and the ablation indicating 9 UAS improvement due to PR and 4 UAS from decoding over the baseline are informative.
- Language-agnostic and training-free. No reliance on source-language models or parallel data, and minimal per-language assumptions (ADP direction inferred from test text). This is a useful baseline for truly low-resource scenarios.
- Empirical insights about UD-specific constraints. The personalization toward the first verb improves root accuracy (by ~2%), and ADP direction estimation yields strong ADP UAS (~75%) across languages.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to Søgaard (2012). The approach is very close conceptually (PR-based unsupervised parsing with rules); the main differences are PR personalization and a UD-specific two-stage decoding. A direct, controlled head-to-head comparison to Søgaard (2012b) under UD would strengthen the claim of contribution.
- Potential concerns about tuning on test data. ADP direction is estimated directly on the test set (albeit from a small number of sentences), and function-word directionality is fixed from English development. While the authors argue it converges quickly, clearer safeguards against leakage and per-language accuracy of the estimation are needed to ensure a fair cross-lingual setting.
- Graph construction and PR details under-specified. The method notes that graphs can be disconnected and uses a low teleport probability. However, there is no analysis of how often sentence graphs are disconnected, the size distribution of components, or how this affects PR stability and downstream attachments.
- Missing oracle/upper-bound analyses per constraint. It would be helpful to quantify ceilings attributable to specific components: oracle ADP direction, oracle function-word directionality, oracle root/main predicate detection, and oracle head-rule licensing versus pure nearest-head selection. These would clarify where most errors originate and the headroom for each design choice.
- Tie-breaking and determinism in PR/decoding not fully specified. It is unclear how PR score ties are broken and whether tie-breaking impacts reproducibility and performance across languages and domains. Similarly, decoding back-off behavior (when δ and κ prune all options) could be clarified with statistics on how often constraints are relaxed.

Questions to authors
- ADP direction estimation: How many sentences/types are required per language to reach a stable decision in practice? Please report per-language accuracy of the inferred direction vs. the ground-truth directionality in the treebanks, and the fraction of sentences used before convergence.
- Graph connectivity: How often are sentence graphs disconnected under your rule set? What is the distribution of component counts and sizes, and how does this correlate with PR accuracy for picking the root and with UAS?
- PR and tie-breaking: How are PR ties handled? Is tie-breaking deterministic across platforms? Do ties occur frequently, and what is their observed impact on UAS?
- Oracles/ablations: Please report oracle upper bounds for (a) correct ADP direction, (b) correct global function-word direction choices, (c) oracle root/main predicate, and (d) oracle head-rule licensing (vs. pure nearest-head). Additionally, include a direct comparison to Søgaard (2012b) reimplemented under UD v1.2 to isolate the gains from personalization and two-stage decoding.
- Sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the teleport probability and the personalization weight (set to 5)? Provide a brief sensitivity analysis.
