Summary and main contributions
This paper introduces UDP, a simple, training-free parser for Universal Dependencies that exploits UD priors and PageRank-based word ranking. The system builds a graph using a small set of POS-based head rules, runs personalized PageRank to prioritize likely predicates among content words, and then decodes in two stages: attaching content words by PR order and constraints, followed by function words with directionality priors, thereby enforcing UD’s single-root and function-word-as-leaf constraints. Adposition directionality is inferred on-the-fly from test data via ADP–NOMINAL bigram frequencies. Evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages (gold and predicted POS), UDP is competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline, more robust to domain shift, and less sensitive to POS noise. Ablations show PageRank and two-step decoding are the primary drivers of accuracy.

As I see them, the key contributions (in order of strength):
1) A language-agnostic, unsupervised UD parser that operationalizes UD priors (function word leafness, single root) through personalized PageRank and a two-step decoding scheme, achieving competitive cross-lingual accuracy and strong robustness to domain variation.
2) A training-free, data-driven estimation of adposition head-direction directly from test data with minimal samples, eliminating language-specific parameters and improving portability.
3) A careful empirical analysis across 32 languages, three POS regimes, and multiple domains, with clear ablations (e.g., +9 UAS from PR; +4 UAS from decoding over a rule baseline), POS error propagation analysis (0.37 vs 0.46 for delex), and POS-level breakdowns.

Strengths
- Strong, simple idea grounded in UD priors
  The design tightly integrates UD’s structural constraints: personalized PR targets the predicate, and two-step decoding enforces function-word leafness. This yields deterministic parses given POS, and the constraints are linguistically justified. The approach is easy to reproduce and extend.

- Competitive cross-lingual performance without training
  Despite being training-free, UDP with gold POS is on average only ~6.4 UAS behind multi-source delexicalized parsing (MSDG) and even outperforms it on Hindi. With predicted POS, UDP degrades only marginally (~2.2 UAS on average), remaining close to the supervised transfer baseline.

- Robustness to POS noise and domain shift
  Error propagation from POS to dependencies is lower for UDP (0.37) than for MSD (0.46), indicating better tolerance to tagging errors. Across multiple domain splits (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, Italian, English), UDP exhibits substantially lower UAS variance than MSD, and even surpasses MSD in hard domains (e.g., several Italian domains). This stability is a meaningful practical advantage for multilingual, cross-domain applications.

- Clear evidence that PageRank personalization matters
  Ablations quantify the contribution of PR (+9 UAS over UDP without PR) and of the decoding step (+4 over the rule baseline), and root accuracies (~69% on average) improve with PR personalization even in verb-final languages. The PR setup is specified (graph definition, teleport 0.05, 5x personalization to the estimated predicate), aiding reproducibility.

- Well-written and transparent
  The paper is detailed, clearly motivates how prior information is introduced, and provides algorithmic specifics, runtime complexity discussion, and comprehensive evaluations (32 languages, three POS regimes, cross-domain, POS-wise breakdown). The forward-looking discussion (MWEs, coordination, reifying edges for PR, partial labeling) articulates promising next steps.

Weaknesses
- Limited coverage of key syntactic phenomena
  Excluding the NOUN→VERB rule prevents capturing relative clauses; coordination, punctuation, and proper names remain weak (e.g., low PUNCT and variable CONJ UAS). While in line with the design’s simplicity, this limits applicability in syntax-heavy settings and may cap headroom on several languages.

- Reliance on reasonably accurate POS tags
  Although more robust than MSD, UDP still depends on POS quality; the extreme content/function-only setting collapses performance. In truly low-resource scenarios with poor taggers or domain mismatch, the gains of the training-free approach may be undermined.

- Fixed directionality assumptions for several function POS
  DET, AUX, SCONJ (right-attaching) and CONJ, PUNCT (left-attaching) are globally fixed based on English development, which misfires in specific languages (e.g., Basque determiners). While the ADP direction is learned from test data, generalizing this learning to other function POS could further reduce cross-lingual brittleness.

- Evaluation scope and baselines
  The comparison excludes some alternative unsupervised/weakly supervised approaches beyond DMV and delex transfer (e.g., projection with stronger alignment or more recent multi-source/neural variants). The focus on UAS (no labeled evaluation) underplays how well UD relation inventories would be supported by this pipeline.

- Hyperparameter and graph-construction choices are English-tuned
  The 5x personalization weight and teleport probability (0.05) were tuned on English dev; the PR graph is built only from a small rule set (Table 1), which may inject bias, and there is limited analysis of sensitivity or cross-lingual optimality.

Questions to authors (to clarify during response)
- Sensitivity: How do UAS and root accuracy vary with the personalization weight, teleport probability, and graph-building rules? Any cross-lingual tuning experiments or per-language robustness analysis?
- Extending direction estimation: Can the test-time directionality estimation used for ADP be applied to DET, AUX, SCONJ, and CONJ to avoid English-specific priors?
- Relative clauses: What was the quantitative trade-off that led to dropping NOUN→VERB? Could you use a back-off rule (e.g., allow NOUN→VERB only when verb already has a head) to recover relative clauses without harming global structure?
- Complexity and runtime: What are sentence-level runtimes (PR + decoding) across languages and average sentence lengths? Any scalability concerns for large-scale parsing?
- Labeling: Given the clear head rules, can you produce coarse UD labels (e.g., for function words) to report LAS or partial labeling accuracy?
- Low-resource applicability: How does UDP fare with noisy POS from cross-lingual projection or very small tagger training sets, beyond TnT on UD training data? Could integrating simple morphological cues improve resilience?

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed, clearly written paper that contributes a deceptively simple yet effective, training-free baseline for UD parsing. It advances unsupervised parsing by leveraging UD priors in a principled way, shows strong cross-lingual breadth and domain robustness, and opens several concrete avenues for future work (direction estimation beyond ADP, edge reification for PR, MWEs/coordination, partial labeling). Despite limitations on complex constructions and POS dependence, the contribution is substantial and timely for unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.
