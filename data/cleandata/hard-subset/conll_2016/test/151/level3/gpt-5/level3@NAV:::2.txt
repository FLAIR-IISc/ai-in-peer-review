Summary: This paper introduces UDP, a training-free, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that combines personalized PageRank over sentence graphs with a compact set of UD-compliant head rules and a two-step decoding that enforces function-word leafness. The method estimates adposition attachment direction directly from test data, requires only POS tags, and is evaluated across 32 languages and multiple domains. Empirically, UDP approaches multi-source delexicalized transfer performance, outperforms it in Hindi, and shows stronger robustness to POS noise and domain shift.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A linguistically grounded, training-free UD parser leveraging personalized PageRank plus UD-informed constraints and two-step decoding, including test-time estimation of adposition direction.
2) A broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation demonstrating competitive accuracy with delexicalized transfer and improved robustness to POS errors and domain shift.
3) A clear, reproducible rule set and analysis that position UDP as a strong, low-resource baseline for unsupervised parsing.

Strengths:
- Competitive multilingual performance with minimal assumptions: UDP nearly matches multi-source delexicalized transfer on average and beats it on Hindi, despite requiring no training data.
- Robustness to noisy POS and domain shift: Smaller accuracy drops with predicted POS and lower cross-domain variance indicate improved stability over data-driven transfer, supporting claims of generality.
- Methodological clarity and linguistic grounding: The use of UD constraints (single root, function-word leafness) and a two-step decoding is well motivated and clearly explained; the intuition behind added priors is transparent.
- Solid analytical evidence: Ablations indicate substantial gains from PageRank personalization and the decoding strategy, helping isolate where accuracy comes from.
- Practicality: No reliance on parallel corpora or source models; simple to deploy in low-resource or cross-domain settings.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is moderate: The approach builds on prior rule-based/PR methods; the main advance is their UD-specific integration and evaluation breadth rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.
- Dependence on POS tagging: Best results assume strong POS tags; predicted-POS still requires supervised taggers per language, limiting fully zero-resource applicability.
- Limited linguistic coverage and evaluation: Unlabeled attachment only; known gaps in coordination, relative clauses, and punctuation reduce structural fidelity.
- Potential English-centric tuning: Some attachment directions and rule choices were decided on English; cross-lingual generality of these choices could be further validated or data-driven.

Questions to Authors (optional):
- How sensitive is performance to the personalization weight and teleport probability in PageRank across languages?
- Can directionality estimation be extended beyond ADP (e.g., DET/AUX/SCONJ) to reduce English-biased heuristics?
- What is the runtime profile of UDP versus delexicalized transfer at scale, and could edge-level PR (reification) be made efficient?
