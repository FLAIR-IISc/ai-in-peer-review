Summary
The paper introduces UDP, a deterministic, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). UDP combines (i) a small set of POS-based head rules that enforce UD constraints (notably, function words as leaves and single-rooted trees), (ii) a personalized PageRank (PR) over a rule-induced multigraph to prioritize content words and identify the main predicate, and (iii) a two-step decoding that first attaches content words by PR order and then attaches function words by local directionality and proximity. Adposition head direction (pre/post) is inferred on the fly from test data via simple bigram counts. The system requires no training and is evaluated on 32 UD languages under gold POS, predicted POS (TnT), and a crude content/function-only POS scenario, and compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). UDP is on average close to MSD in UAS, surpasses it on Hindi, and exhibits lower variance across domains.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A strong, training-free baseline for UD parsing with broad multilingual and cross-domain evidence of robustness, competitive with delexicalized transfer under realistic POS conditions. This empirical finding is the most impactful result.
2) A simple, UD-aware decoding design—personalized PR over a rule-induced graph plus two-step attachment that enforces function-word leafness—yielding consistent gains over a rule-only baseline and a no-PR variant.
3) A language-agnostic, test-time estimation of adposition directionality that works with minimal evidence, avoiding language-specific parameters.

Strengths
- Robust, competitive performance without training: UDPG is within ~6–7 UAS of MSDG on average and even outperforms MSDG on Hindi; with predicted POS, UDP’s performance drop is smaller than MSD’s (lower error propagation from POS to dependencies).
- Cross-domain stability: The reported standard deviations across domains are consistently lower for UDP than for MSD (both with gold and predicted POS), supporting the claim that the rule-driven approach generalizes better across genres.
- Clear ablations and interpretable design: The UDPNoPR vs UDP comparison isolates a sizable PR benefit (~9 UAS), and the rule-based decoding itself adds ~4 UAS over a strong rule baseline. The use of UD constraints (function-word leafness, single root) is explicit and linguistically motivated.
- Language-agnostic directionality estimation: Inferring ADP direction from test data is simple, fast (converges with 10–15 sentences), and effective (high ADP UAS; correct pre/post identification across languages).
- Deterministic, resource-light, and easy to reproduce: No reliance on parallel data, aligners, or source models; implementation choices (teleport=0.05, 5x personalization for first verb/content word) are transparent.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior unsupervised work: The approach is close in spirit to Søgaard (2012), with incremental changes (personalization, two-step decoding, UD-specific constraints). The paper would benefit from deeper positioning and/or stronger empirical advantages over earlier PR-based unsupervised baselines beyond averages.
- Heavy reliance on hand-crafted head rules and English-driven design: Several directionality settings for function words and the head rule inventory were tuned/selected on English dev, which may miss language-specific phenomena (e.g., Basque determiners). The exclusion of NOUN→VERB precludes relative clause structures and may cap potential performance.
- Test-time estimation raises evaluation concerns: While estimating ADP direction on test data is pragmatic, it blurs the line between model and evaluation protocol. A sensitivity analysis and a “frozen” setting based on development-only evidence (or few-shot unlabeled target text) would clarify fairness and reproducibility.
- Missing analysis of graph properties and tie-breaking: The PR graph can be disconnected; the paper does not quantify how often that occurs, component sizes, or whether this affects PR stability/quality. Tie-breaking strategies in PR ranking and in the closest-head selection are unspecified; their frequency and impact on UAS are unknown.
- Scope limitations and evaluation coverage: The system cannot handle coordination and relative clauses well (acknowledged). No labeled attachment results, and comparison to more recent unsupervised/weakly supervised baselines beyond DMV is limited. Sensitivity to PR hyperparameters (teleport, personalization weight, predicate heuristic) is not reported.

Questions to Authors
- Graph connectivity: How frequently are PR graphs disconnected across languages/domains? What are the average and max component sizes, and do these correlate with UAS?
- Tie-breaking: How are PR rank ties and distance ties resolved during decoding? How often do ties occur, and what is their measured impact on UAS?
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: How sensitive are results to teleport probability and the 5x personalization weight? Does choosing the “first verb” vs. alternative predicate heuristics (e.g., last verb for verb-final languages) change root accuracy or overall UAS?
- Directionality estimation protocol: Can you report results where ADP directionality is fixed from development-only text (or a small held-out unlabeled sample) to assess fairness vs. MSD? For other function words (DET/AUX/SCONJ), can directionality be inferred as you do for ADP, and would that help in languages like Basque?
- Oracle analyses: Please provide oracle upper bounds for each constraint separately (e.g., oracle head direction, oracle rule coverage, oracle nearest-head distance) to quantify where the remaining errors come from.
