Summary
This paper proposes UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that operates solely on POS tags. It builds a graph using a small set of UD-motivated head rules, runs a modified PageRank that downweights function words and personalizes the main predicate, and then decodes in two passes: first attaching content words (rooted by the highest-ranked predicate), then attaching function words as leaves with fixed or estimated directionality. Adposition direction is inferred on-the-fly from test data. UDP is evaluated on 32 languages and across domains, and is competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A strong, UD-compliant, training-free baseline that leverages POS-only PageRank ranking plus two-pass decoding and simple directionality estimation, with broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation.
2) A pragmatic personalization scheme in PageRank that boosts the main predicate and implicitly downweights stop words by ranking content words and attaching function words later.
3) A simple, data-driven estimation of ADP direction from test bigrams.

Strengths
- Clear, simple baseline with broad coverage: The method requires no treebank training, uses only POS tags and a compact ruleset, and works across 32 languages. This is a valuable reference system for UD parsing and multilingual research.
- Competitive performance and robustness: With gold POS, UDP is reasonably close to multi-source delexicalized transfer; with predicted POS it degrades less than the supervised baseline on average. The cross-domain experiments show lower variance than the supervised system, reinforcing the robustness claim.
- Sensible exploitation of UD constraints: Two-pass decoding that enforces function-word leafness and a language-agnostic way to estimate ADP direction are linguistically well-motivated and effective.
- Transparent analysis: The paper includes ablations (UDP vs. UDPNoPR vs. BL) showing that PageRank notably contributes to UAS, plus a POS-wise analysis that is informative for future rule extensions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty over Søgaard (2012): The core idea—ranking words via PageRank before decoding—is very close to Søgaard (2012). The additions (two-pass decoding tailored to UD leafness and a personalization that favors the main predicate) are incremental. A direct, controlled comparison to Søgaard’s exact setup (including graph construction choices) is missing.
- Under-explored personalization: Boosting only the main predicate is sensible but narrow. There is no systematic exploration of alternative personalization vectors (e.g., verbs vs. all content words, downweighting stop words explicitly, multi-root candidates) or of how personalization affects non-root attachments.
- Arbitrary personalization weight: The choice of “5×” weight for the main predicate appears ad hoc and is not justified theoretically or empirically. No sensitivity analysis is provided, and the impact on both accuracy and convergence is not quantified beyond a brief statement.
- Evaluation presentation and diagnostics: Results are presented almost exclusively in tables. Visualizations (e.g., scatter plots of POS accuracy vs. UAS drop per language, bar charts of cross-domain variance, ablation curves for personalization) would make trends clearer and strengthen claims about error propagation and robustness.
- POS tagging effects insufficiently reported: While an average TnT accuracy is given and a global error-propagation number is derived, the paper does not report per-language POS tagging accuracies in prediction mode nor correlate them with per-language UAS degradations. This limits interpretability of the predicted-POS results.

Questions to Authors
- Novelty and baselines: Can you include a direct replication of Søgaard (2012) with your data and graph-building rules, and an ablation that isolates only the personalization and the two-pass UD-specific decoding? What is the delta over Søgaard when controlling for all other choices?
- Personalization design: Why 5×? Did you try sweeping the weight (e.g., 1–10) and reporting UAS and root accuracy? Did you explore personalizing all verbs, nouns, or content words; or downweighting function words explicitly in the PR vector? Any results for personalizing multiple predicate candidates?
- Graph construction: You state ranking is relevant only for content words, yet function words also appear in the graph via edges. Did you try excluding function words from the PR nodes altogether or reweighting their edges? How sensitive are results to the teleport probability (0.05)?
- POS prediction: Please report per-language POS accuracies in prediction mode and provide a scatter plot correlating POS accuracy with UAS drop. Are languages with larger POS degradation the same ones driving UDP’s variance? Does error propagation differ by POS category?
- “No training” claim: You tuned parameters and rules on English dev. Can you clarify which choices were made with access to English gold trees and assess their impact when removed or tuned unsupervised?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Add visualizations: (i) POS accuracy vs. UAS drop per language; (ii) domain-wise variance plots comparing UDP vs. MSD; (iii) ablation curves for personalization weight and for including/excluding specific head rules.
- Report significance: For language-level comparisons to MSD, include significance testing or confidence intervals.
- Consider extending personalization beyond the root: e.g., verb and noun classes, or edge-level PR (reified edges), which you already mention as future work—adding a small experiment now would strengthen the contribution.
