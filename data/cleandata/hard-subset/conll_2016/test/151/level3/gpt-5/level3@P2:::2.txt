Summary and contributions
This paper proposes UDP, a training-free, UD-specific dependency parser that combines a small set of explicit POS-based head rules with personalized PageRank (PR) over sentence graphs to rank content words and guide decoding. It enforces key UD constraints (single root; function words as dependents) via a two-step decoding procedure and estimates adposition head-direction on-the-fly from test data. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under gold and predicted POS, and in cross-domain settings, and is compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (MSD).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, linguistically grounded, training-free UD parser that uses personalized PageRank plus UD-compliant head rules to produce competitive UAS across many languages, with notably strong robustness to domain shift.
2) A decoding strategy that enforces UD constraints by first attaching content words and then function words, effectively guaranteeing leafhood of function words and producing well-formed trees without training.
3) A lightweight, data-driven estimation of adposition attachment direction directly from test text, removing language-specific parameters and improving cross-lingual applicability.

Strengths
1) Strong, training-free baseline with competitive accuracy: UDP achieves UAS close to a supervised multi-source delexicalized parser across 32 languages (average gap ≈6–7 points with gold POS, outperforming MSD on Hindi). This is notable for an unsupervised system using only POS tags and a small, interpretable rule set. The ablation shows PR yields a sizeable gain (~9 UAS) over a rules-only variant, indicating the ranking component is genuinely useful.
2) Robustness to domain shift: Across multiple domain splits (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian), UDP exhibits consistently lower performance variance than the delexicalized baseline. This is an important practical property for low-resource and cross-domain deployment and aligns with the design goal of relying on general UD constraints rather than data-specific patterns.
3) Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is well written and the algorithmic choices are clearly motivated by UD principles. Personalization in PR is explained and justified; decoding guarantees single-rootedness and function-word leafhood; adposition direction is estimated transparently from bigram frequencies. The inclusion of ablations (UDP vs. UDPNoPR vs. BL) helps attribute gains to components.
4) Broad multilingual evaluation and error analysis: Evaluation on 32 languages and additional domain splits, plus analysis by POS categories (e.g., adpositions, determiners, punctuation, conjunctions), gives a thorough view of where the method works and where it struggles. The discussion of poor handling of punctuation and coordination is honest and informative, and future work is well scoped.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty relative to prior rule-aided unsupervised parsing is incremental: The approach builds closely on Søgaard (2012b) and related work, adding personalized PR, two-step decoding for UD leaf constraints, and test-time adposition direction estimation. While effective, the methodological novelty is modest; most gains seem to stem from careful exploitation of UD conventions and a small set of rules rather than a fundamentally new learning or inference technique.
2) Dependence on POS quality and evaluation setup: Although the paper reports predicted-POS results, these use a supervised tagger trained on the target language’s training set, which weakens the “no training” narrative in realistic low-resource scenarios. The CF (content/function) setting is too crude to be a practical lower bound. A more realistic cross-lingual POS transfer setup (e.g., projected or multilingual taggers) would better characterize UDP’s robustness when no target-language POS training data is available.
3) Limited coverage and structural expressivity: The fixed head rules restrict certain structures (e.g., relative clauses, some coordination patterns); the authors note excluding NOUN→VERB rules harms hierarchy, which leaves relative clauses largely unsupported. UAS for PUNCT and CONJ is low with high variance. The method is unlabeled and UD-specific; applicability beyond UD or to labeled parsing is only sketched.
4) Potential transductive tuning and language variation: Estimating adposition direction from the test set is reasonable for unsupervised parsing, but it is transductive and may be brittle for languages or domains with mixed or less frequent ADP patterns. Other function word directions (e.g., DET in Basque) are fixed globally from English dev; learning these per language from unlabeled text (as with ADP) might improve fairness and performance.
5) Baseline scope: The main supervised comparator (multi-source delexicalized TurboParser) is reasonable but not the strongest among contemporary cross-lingual methods that leverage projection or modern multilingual representations. Including stronger projection-based baselines or discussing why they are omitted would better situate the contribution.

Questions to authors
- POS reliance: Can you report results with cross-lingual or projected POS tags (e.g., Agić et al. 2015; multilingual taggers) to approximate a true low-resource scenario without target-language POS training? How does UDP compare there versus MSD?
- Directionality estimation: For languages with scarce adpositions or mixed pre-/postpositions, how stable is the bigram-based direction estimate? Did you consider estimating direction with confidence thresholds or per-sentence adaptation?
- Personalization choice: You set the personalized node as the first verb (or first content word). Can you quantify performance on verb-final languages when choosing the last verb, or with a learned heuristic (e.g., simple verb-position priors per language inferred from unlabeled text)?
- Rule set extension: Did you try a small set of additional rules (e.g., limited NOUN→VERB for relative clauses conditioned on SCONJ/PRON cues) without hurting overall performance? Any evidence that a few carefully targeted rules can improve CONJ and PUNCT attachments?
- Labeled parsing: Since the method produces unlabeled trees, do you have preliminary results on lightweight label assignment (e.g., rule-based or POS-pattern-based labels)? How much labeled accuracy could be recovered with minimal supervision?

Overall, this is a well-written, thoughtful paper that provides a simple, robust, and surprisingly competitive training-free UD parser. While the methodological novelty is moderate, the contribution as a strong, domain-stable, and interpretable baseline for unsupervised UD parsing is substantial and likely to spur follow-up work. I lean toward acceptance.
