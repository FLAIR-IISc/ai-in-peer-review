Summary
The paper introduces UDP, a training-free, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that combines a small set of UD-aligned head rules with personalized PageRank to rank content words and a two-step decoding scheme that enforces UD’s “function words as leaves” constraint. Head-direction for adpositions is estimated on-the-fly from test sentences via simple bigram counts, removing language-specific parameters. The system is evaluated on 32 UD v1.2 languages under gold and predicted POS tags, compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser, and assessed for domain robustness. Despite its simplicity and lack of training data, UDP achieves competitive UAS and shows strong stability across domains.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A practical, training-free UD parser that encodes UD constraints directly and uses personalized PageRank to identify sentence nuclei, combined with two-step decoding to separate content vs. function attachment and guarantee leafness of function words.
- A rigorous, multilingual and cross-domain evaluation demonstrating that an unsupervised, rule-guided method approaches supervised delexicalized transfer performance and is more robust to domain shift.
- A simple, data-driven estimation of adposition attachment direction from test data, removing language-specific parameters and yielding strong performance for ADP attachments.

Strengths
- Strong empirical coverage and competitiveness: UDP with gold POS (57.5 UAS avg.) is close to a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (63.9), with only a 6.4 UAS gap on average and even outperforming it on Hindi. With predicted POS, the relative drop is modest (≈2.2 vs. 2.7 for delex transfer), suggesting resilience to realistic tagging noise.
- Clear, principled exploitation of UD constraints: The parser’s architecture explicitly enforces UD desiderata (single root, function words as dependents), and its two-step decoding makes these constraints operational without complex learning. The design aligns well with UD’s cross-lingual consistency goals.
- Careful analysis of PageRank’s contribution: Ablations quantify that disabling PR costs ≈9 UAS, while decoding alone contributes ≈4 UAS over a head-rule baseline. Personalized PR (bias to the first verb/content word) improves root selection (average ≈69% root accuracy) and speeds convergence; even verb-final languages benefit on average.
- Robustness to domain shift: Across genre splits in multiple languages (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, Italian, English), UDP exhibits substantially lower standard deviation of UAS compared to the delexicalized model, under both gold and predicted POS. This supports the claim that rule-based, low-parameter approaches generalize better across domains.
- Simplicity and resource efficiency: No training data, no parallel corpora, and only POS tags are needed. The adposition direction estimator converges with minimal data (10–15 sentences). This makes UDP an attractive baseline for low-resource or rapidly expanding UD settings.

Weaknesses
- Limited structural coverage by design: Excluding NOUN→VERB heads precludes recovering many relative clauses; coordination and punctuation are handled weakly (e.g., very low PUNCT UAS, modest CONJ UAS), and MWEs and proper names are not addressed. These choices restrict ceiling performance and limit applicability in downstream tasks that rely on these phenomena.
- Dependence on POS quality and mapping: While the drop with predicted POS is moderate, performance degrades sharply under coarse content/function tags, indicating brittleness when POS taggers are poor or UD POS mapping is noisy. The method’s reliance on POS also means that annotation inconsistencies (e.g., AUX vs. VERB, DET vs. PRON) can materially affect results.
- Tuning and use of test data: Although “unsupervised,” the system uses English dev data to select head rules and tune PR parameters, and estimates adposition direction from target test sentences. While this avoids language-specific parameters, it raises questions about fairness and potential leakage in realistic evaluation settings where test splits should be sacrosanct.
- Greedy decoding and unlabeled evaluation: The decoding is greedy and may create non-projective structures; there is no exploration or global optimization. The evaluation is unlabeled UAS only; without labeled attachment, it is unclear how well the method supports full UD parsing. No comparison to more recent unsupervised grammar induction baselines beyond Søgaard-like setups is presented.
- Limited per-language error analysis: The paper offers insightful global analyses (e.g., POS-wise breakdown, PR contribution) but relatively little language-specific diagnosis beyond ADP direction and a few DET exceptions (e.g., Basque). Understanding where rules fail across typologies could guide more targeted improvements.

Questions to Authors
- Scope of tuning: Which hyperparameters or rule choices were tuned on English dev, and how sensitive are results to those choices? Could you quantify performance variability if different development languages are used?
- Estimating attachment direction on test data: Is the ADP direction estimated once per language on held-out text, or recomputed per test fold? How would performance change if direction were estimated on development data only?
- POS sensitivity: Do you have results with modern cross-lingual taggers or projected tags to better characterize robustness in low-resource settings? How does accuracy vary with controlled POS noise levels?
- Coordination and punctuation: Have you tried simple additional rules for coordination scope and punctuation attachment? What are the trade-offs (e.g., precision vs. recall) when adding such rules?
- Labeled parsing and edge scoring: Could the PageRank idea be extended to reified edge graphs to provide labeled attachments with minimal supervision? Any preliminary results?
- Computational profile: What are typical runtimes per sentence and for full treebanks? Any heuristics to further reduce PR cost without losing accuracy?

Overall, this is a well-written and clear paper that makes a substantive contribution: it demonstrates that a simple, linguistically grounded, training-free UD parser can be competitive with delexicalized transfer and substantially more robust across domains. Despite limitations in structural coverage and reliance on POS quality, the work sets a strong baseline and opens promising avenues for future improvements. I lean toward acceptance.
