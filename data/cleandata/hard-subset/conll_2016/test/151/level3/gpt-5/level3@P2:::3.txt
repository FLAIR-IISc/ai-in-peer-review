Summary and contributions
This paper presents UDP, a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that uses only POS tags and a small set of UD-consistent head rules. The method ranks content words within each sentence using a personalized PageRank (PR) graph built from POS-based head–dependent rules, then decodes in two steps: attach content words first (enforcing single root), followed by function words (enforcing leafness). ADP attachment direction (pre/postposition) is estimated on the fly from ADP–NOMINAL bigrams in the test data. The system is evaluated on 32 UD languages and several domain splits, and compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A broad multilingual and cross-domain evaluation showing that an extremely simple, UD-constrained, training-free parser can be competitive with delexicalized transfer and more robust to domain shifts.
2) A practical, language-agnostic design: UD-tailored head rules, two-step decoding that enforces function-word leafness, and test-time estimation of ADP direction.
3) A modest methodological tweak over prior unsupervised work (Søgaard, 2012b): the use of personalized PR to bias root identification and order content-word attachments.

Strengths
- Strong empirical breadth and robustness: UDP with gold POS is within ~6.4 UAS points on average of MSDG and even outperforms MSDG on Hindi. With predicted POS, UDP degrades only ~2.2 points on average vs. ~2.7 for MSD, indicating favorable error propagation (reported 0.37 vs. 0.46). Across domain splits (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian), UDP shows consistently lower UAS variance than MSD, supporting the claim of domain robustness for the rule-based approach.
- Minimal supervision and simplicity: The parser needs only POS tags and no parallel data or source models. Test-time estimation of ADP direction avoids language-specific parameters, and two-step decoding adheres to core UD constraints (single root, function words as leaves), making the approach easy to port.
- Clear ablation evidence for design choices: The analysis reports that personalized PR contributes ~9 UAS points over a UDP variant without PR, while the decoding/rules contribute an additional ~4 UAS points over a naive head-rule baseline, indicating both parts are impactful.
- Practical baseline for low-resource scenarios: Even with a crude content/function tag setting, UDP outperforms adjacency baselines, suggesting usefulness when POS quality is poor.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The approach closely mirrors Søgaard (2012b) in using PR for unsupervised parsing. The main difference is UD-specific constraints and a simple personalization on the presumed main predicate. The paper frames this as “unsupervised UD parsing,” but algorithmically the step beyond prior work is modest.
- Personalization heuristic under-justified and under-explored: Boosting only the first verb (or first content word) by a factor of 5 appears ad hoc. There is no sensitivity analysis to the weight, nor exploration of alternative personalization schemes (e.g., seeding all verbs, or data-driven priors). Claims of cross-linguistic robustness would be stronger with such analysis, especially for verb-final languages.
- Evaluation presentation and diagnostics: Results are only in large tables; visual summaries (per-language deltas vs. MSD, variance across domains) would make the story clearer. The paper reports average POS accuracy but does not provide per-language POS tagging scores in the predicted setting, nor correlation analyses between POS accuracy and UAS degradation.
- Baseline coverage and positioning: DMV is omitted; while the paper argues it is outperformed by similar baselines, a direct reproduction under UD (or at least a re-implementation of Søgaard’s PR parser on UD) would calibrate the incremental contribution more precisely. As written, the empirical delta over Søgaard (2012b) remains somewhat unclear.
- Cross-linguistic rule choices derived from English dev: Fixed directions for AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ were decided on English and do not generalize uniformly (e.g., poor DET behavior in Basque). Extending the test-time direction estimation beyond ADP would likely improve robustness and reduce English bias.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Personalization: Why a weight of 5? Please provide a sensitivity analysis (e.g., weights {1,2,3,5,10}) and alternatives (seed all verbs; seed content words by simple verb/noun priors; or estimate seed distributions from POS n-grams).
- Direct comparison to Søgaard (2012b): Can you provide results for a UD adaptation of Søgaard’s method without your new components, then add your changes one by one (two-step decoding, leafness, ADP direction estimation, personalization) to quantify the incremental gains?
- POS tagging effects: Please report per-language TnT accuracies in the predicted POS setting and include correlations between POS accuracy and UAS degradation. Do certain POS confusions dominate the error propagation?
- Graph construction and runtime: Are multi-edges equally weighted? How often are PR graphs disconnected, and how critical is the 0.05 teleport probability? Please report average runtime per sentence and convergence iterations across languages.
- Estimating function-word directions: Beyond ADP, can you estimate directions for AUX/DET/SCONJ/CONJ at test time similarly to ADP? If so, does it improve Basque-like cases?
