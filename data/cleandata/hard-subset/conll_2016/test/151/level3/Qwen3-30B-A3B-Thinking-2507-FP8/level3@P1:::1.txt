

Summary: This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. The core contribution is a deterministic parsing algorithm that uses POS-based head rules to define permitted dependencies, ranks content words via personalized PageRank over a graph defined by those rules, and assigns each word to its closest allowable head respecting directionality constraints. The authors evaluate UDP across 32 languages in three POS scenarios and demonstrate competitive performance with a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), while exhibiting superior robustness to domain changes.

Main contributions: The primary contribution is the development of a linguistically grounded, completely unsupervised dependency parsing system that leverages UD's formal constraints. The second contribution is the empirical demonstration of robust cross-lingual parsing performance without language-specific training. The third contribution is the demonstration of significantly reduced sensitivity to domain shifts compared to supervised transfer approaches.

Strengths: 
1) The system achieves competitive performance with supervised transfer parsers (MSD) across 32 languages, with UDPG (gold POS) averaging only 6.4% behind MSDG (gold POS), and even outperforming MSDG on Hindi. This represents a substantial improvement over previous unsupervised parsers.
2) The system's robustness to domain shifts is compelling evidence of its generalizability. UDP's standard deviation across domains is consistently lower than MSD's (e.g., 2.5 vs. 4.2 for UDPG vs. MSDG), confirming that rule-based approaches can be more stable to domain shifts than data-driven methods.
3) The approach is exceptionally simple and transparent, using only UD's formal constraints and a small set of head rules. The parser is completely free of language-specific parameters, making it a valuable baseline for cross-lingual parsing research.

Weaknesses:
1) The core algorithm is a direct extension of Søgaard (2012b), with minimal novelty beyond the specific adaptation to UD constraints. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently differentiate their contribution from prior work.
2) The head directionality for adpositions is estimated directly on test data, which is a form of data leakage. While the authors note it converges quickly (10-15 sentences), this approach should be evaluated on a proper development set.
3) The paper lacks analysis of head rule coverage and corpus properties. The authors should report on how often the graph is disconnected (affecting PageRank) and the size of components in disconnected cases.
4) The evaluation of constraints (head rules, directionality, distance) lacks oracle analysis. Without knowing the upper bound each constraint provides, it's hard to assess their relative importance.

Questions to Authors:
1) How are PageRank ties broken, and does this affect performance? The paper states the algorithm is deterministic, but tie-breaking strategy is not specified.
2) Please provide the frequency of disconnected graphs in the corpus and their component sizes. This would help assess how often the graph-building strategy fails to produce a single connected component.
3) Can you provide oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to demonstrate the upper bound each contributes to the final score?

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, with thorough evaluation across multiple languages and domains. The methodology is sound and the results are impressive for an unsupervised approach. However, the novelty is limited, and the test-time tuning of directionality should be addressed. The authors should clarify the relationship to Søgaard (2012) and provide more detailed analysis of their head rules' coverage. Despite these weaknesses, the robust performance and simplicity of the approach make this a valuable contribution to the field, particularly for scenarios where training data is unavailable.
