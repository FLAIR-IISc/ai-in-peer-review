

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. The approach combines personalized PageRank on content words with a small set of UD-compliant dependency head rules to build dependency trees. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves competitive performance with supervised transfer systems while being significantly more robust to domain shifts across languages.

The primary contribution is a training-free parsing algorithm that leverages UD's linguistic constraints to produce high-quality parses without any language-specific parameters. This represents a substantial advancement in unsupervised parsing research, as it successfully addresses key limitations of previous approaches. A secondary contribution is the automatic estimation of adposition direction from test data, eliminating the need for language-specific parameter tuning. The paper also presents a two-step decoding algorithm that ensures function words are leaves, adhering to UD conventions.

Strengths: The paper's most compelling strength is its demonstration that an unsupervised approach can match supervised transfer systems in performance (within 6.4% average difference), challenging the prevailing assumption that unsupervised parsing cannot compete with supervised methods. The authors' thorough evaluation across 32 languages and multiple POS scenarios provides strong evidence for their claims. The method's robustness to domain shifts, with significantly lower standard deviations across domains than supervised baselines, is another major strength. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the linguistic intuition behind their approach.

Weaknesses: The evaluation is limited to languages with UD treebanks that include word forms, excluding Japanese and other languages with different annotation practices. The performance drop when using predicted POS tags (2.2% compared to gold POS) is less impressive than claimed, as the POS accuracy is high (94.1%). The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the method would perform on languages with radically different syntactic structures from those tested, such as SOV languages beyond the 32 evaluated. The comparison to recent unsupervised methods (post-2012) is incomplete, potentially overstating the novelty.
