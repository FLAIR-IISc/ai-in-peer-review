- UDP is an unsupervised dependency parser that uses UD constraints, a small set of head-dependent rules, and personalized PageRank to parse without training data.  
- Contribution 1: A rule-based unsupervised parser that fully exploits UD linguistic constraints (e.g., function words as leaves, specific head rules).  
- Contribution 2: A method to estimate adposition direction directly from test data with no language-specific parameters.  
- Contribution 3: A system achieving competitive performance with supervised transfer parsers and demonstrating superior robustness to domain shifts.  

- Strength: Achieves competitive average UAS (63.9) without training data, outperforming a supervised delexicalized baseline on some languages (e.g., Hindi).  
- Strength: Shows greater robustness to domain shifts with lower UAS variance across domains (2.5 vs. 4.2).  
- Strength: Provides a clear linguistic justification for design choices by aligning with UD formal constraints.  
- Strength: Evaluation is comprehensive (32 languages, three POS scenarios, multiple domains) and analyzes POS→dependency error propagation.  

- Weakness: Performance collapses under very coarse content/function POS tagging (63.9 → 29.9 UAS), limiting applicability for truly low-resource languages.  
- Weakness: Excludes Japanese from evaluation, leaving open how the method handles radically different word orders (e.g., SOV).  
- Weakness: Cannot handle relative clauses due to omission of the NOUN→VERB head rule, reducing applicability for languages with frequent relative clauses.  
- Weakness: Limited comparison to other unsupervised parsers, making it hard to situate UDP against the current state of the art.  

- Q: How would UDP perform on languages with very different word order patterns (e.g., SOV languages like Japanese)?  
- Q: Why was the NOUN→VERB head rule excluded, and could it be incorporated without substantially harming performance?  
- Q: How does the personalization strategy (e.g., choosing first verb as main predicate) perform across verb-final vs. verb-initial languages, given UDP’s strong result on Hindi?
