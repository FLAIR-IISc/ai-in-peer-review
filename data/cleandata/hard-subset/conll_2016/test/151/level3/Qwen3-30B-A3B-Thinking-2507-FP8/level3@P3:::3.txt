

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data and relies solely on POS tags and a modified PageRank algorithm. The authors claim to adapt dependency head rules to UD conventions, incorporate UD's restriction of function words being leaves, use personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, and estimate adposition direction directly from test data.

As I see them, the paper's primary contributions are:
1. A rule-based unsupervised dependency parser that achieves competitive results with a delexicalized transfer system
2. Demonstration of robustness to domain changes compared to data-driven approaches

The paper's novelty is limited, as its core approach closely mirrors Søgaard (2012), with only minor modifications. The adaptation to UD conventions is largely a matter of applying existing rules to a new formalism rather than a significant methodological contribution.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a well-structured and clear presentation of the approach, with detailed explanations of the algorithm, including the two-step decoding process and the handling of function words as leaves.

2. The evaluation across 32 languages and three POS setups (gold, predicted, and naive) is comprehensive and provides valuable insights into the robustness of the approach, particularly regarding the relationship between POS accuracy and parsing performance.

3. The cross-domain analysis (Table 7) effectively demonstrates the robustness of the rule-based approach compared to data-driven methods, showing that UDP has lower standard deviation across domains than the supervised transfer system, which is an important contribution to understanding parsing system stability.

## Weaknesses

1. The novelty claim is weak. The paper closely follows Søgaard (2012) with only minor modifications (personalized PageRank for main predicate, two-step decoding for UD constraints). The authors fail to clearly articulate how these modifications substantially improve upon Søgaard's work, making the contribution appear incremental rather than significant.

2. The personalization weight of 5 is arbitrary and unjustified. The paper provides no empirical evidence or ablation studies showing how performance varies with different weight values. The choice of 5 appears to be a heuristic without theoretical or experimental justification.

3. The evaluation lacks visualizations. The results are presented in tables that make it difficult to interpret performance trends across languages and setups. Charts would have made the comparative results much clearer and more accessible.

4. The paper does not report POS tagging scores in prediction mode, which would help determine if parsing degradation correlates with tagging degradation. This is a significant omission, as the authors claim their system is less affected by deteriorating POS tags than the supervised alternative.

5. The paper contains a minor typo at line 207 ("The the main idea" → "The main idea"), suggesting carelessness in proofreading.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your approach with Søgaard (2012), specifically highlighting the novel contributions that make your method superior or different in meaningful ways?

2. Why was the personalization weight set to 5 specifically? Could you provide any empirical evidence or ablation studies showing how performance varies with different weight values?

3. Would it be possible to include visualizations (e.g., bar charts or line graphs) to better illustrate the comparative performance across languages and setups, rather than relying solely on tables?

4. Could you report the POS tagging accuracy in prediction mode for the languages where you used predicted POS tags, to help understand the correlation between tagging and parsing errors?
