

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that uses POS-based head rules and personalized PageRank. The main contributions are: 1) Adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations; 2) Incorporating personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification; and 3) Creating a system completely free of language-specific parameters by estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data.

Strengths: The parser is remarkably simple yet competitive with delexicalized transfer systems, achieving average UAS within 6.4% of supervised baselines. The comprehensive evaluation across 32 languages and three POS setups demonstrates strong cross-lingual performance. The analysis of cross-domain robustness is particularly compelling, showing UDP's superior stability to domain shifts compared to data-driven approaches.

Weaknesses: The novelty is limited; the approach is only slightly different from Søgaard (2012), and the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why these modifications matter. The head directionality was tuned on the test set rather than training/dev, which is methodologically concerning despite quick convergence. The paper lacks analysis of head rules' corpus behavior (e.g., frequency of disconnected graphs), which would help understand algorithmic limitations.

Questions to authors: How is PageRank tie-breaking handled, and does it impact performance? Could you report oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to show upper-bound impacts? Could you clarify the rationale for tuning head directionality on test data rather than training/dev, and whether this would affect generalization to unseen domains?

Minor editorial suggestions: Fix phrasing in 4.1 ("we obtain [the] rank"), reorder Table 5 columns to match Table 7, and correct wording in 6.1 ("isolate the [contribution] of both"). The paper is well-written and clearly presented, but these minor issues reduce its overall polish. While the work is interesting and the evaluation robust, the limited novelty compared to prior work and methodological concerns about test set tuning prevent it from being a strong contribution to the field.
