

Summary: This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. The authors propose a linguistically grounded approach that leverages UD's formal constraints through a small set of head-dependent rules and personalized PageRank. The main contributions are: (1) a rule-based unsupervised parser that fully exploits UD's linguistic constraints (function words as leaves, specific head rules), (2) a method for estimating adposition direction directly from test data without language-specific parameters, and (3) a system that achieves competitive performance with supervised transfer parsers while demonstrating superior robustness to domain shifts.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a genuinely unsupervised approach that achieves competitive performance (average 63.9 UAS vs. 61.2 UAS for supervised multi-source delexicalized parser) without any training data. The parser's performance is remarkably close to supervised baselines, with UDP outperforming the supervised system on Hindi (69.7 UAS vs. 67.9 UAS), which is particularly impressive given the absence of training data.

2. The system's robustness to domain shifts is a significant contribution. UDP shows substantially lower standard deviations across domains (2.5 UAS) compared to the supervised baseline (4.2 UAS), confirming that rule-based systems can be more stable to domain shifts. This is a critical advantage for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

3. The paper provides a compelling linguistic justification for the parser's design choices, particularly the use of UD's constraints (function words as leaves, specific head rules). The authors demonstrate how these constraints lead to a more linguistically sound parser that aligns with UD's formalism, rather than imposing arbitrary constraints.

4. The evaluation setup is comprehensive, covering 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and content/function) and multiple domains. The analysis of error propagation from POS to dependency errors (0.37 for UDP vs. 0.46 for supervised baseline) provides valuable insights into the system's resilience to POS tagging errors.

Weaknesses:
1. The parser's performance drops dramatically (from 63.9 UAS to 29.9 UAS) when using a very coarse content-function POS tagging scheme, which may be the most realistic scenario for truly low-resource languages. This significant performance gap raises questions about the practical utility of the approach when POS tagging is challenging.

2. The exclusion of Japanese (despite being part of UD v1.2) limits the evaluation's scope, particularly since Japanese has a fundamentally different word order (SOV) compared to the Indo-European languages predominantly evaluated. This omission prevents assessing the system's performance on a language with a radically different structure.

3. The parser cannot handle relative clauses due to the exclusion of the NOUN → VERB head rule, which the authors acknowledge but do not address. This limitation is particularly problematic for languages where relative clauses are common, potentially reducing the parser's applicability in real-world scenarios.

4. The comparison with other unsupervised parsers is limited, as the authors note that the DMV model by Klein and Manning (2004) has been outperformed by a system similar to theirs (Søgaard, 2012b). This makes it difficult to assess how UDP compares to the current state of unsupervised parsing.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would the system perform on languages with very different word order patterns (e.g., SOV languages like Japanese, which was excluded from the evaluation)? Given the system's reliance on directionality heuristics, would this approach generalize to such languages?

2. The paper states that the parser cannot infer relative clauses due to the exclusion of the NOUN → VERB head rule. Could the authors provide more insight into why this rule was excluded during development, and whether it would be feasible to incorporate it without significantly harming performance?

3. The paper reports that UDP outperforms the supervised baseline on Hindi. Given that Hindi is a verb-final language, how does the parser's personalization strategy (choosing the first verb as main predicate) perform on verb-final languages compared to verb-initial languages?
