

# Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary
The paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training data. The system uses POS-based head rules to define permitted dependencies, ranks words using a biased PageRank over a graph defined by those rules, and decodes the dependency tree using a two-step process that ensures content words are non-leaf nodes while function words are leaves. The authors evaluate UDP across 32 languages in three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive two-POS) and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system. The results show UDP is competitive with supervised methods and more robust to domain changes.

## Main Contributions
1. A linguistically sound unsupervised parsing approach that effectively leverages UD constraints (function words as leaves, content words as heads) without requiring training data.
2. A clear demonstration that a simple rule-based parser can achieve competitive results with supervised transfer systems while exhibiting greater robustness to domain shifts.
3. A practical, easy-to-implement parsing system that provides a reliable baseline for UD parsing, particularly valuable for low-resource languages.

## Strengths
1. **Strong linguistic motivation**: The paper effectively leverages UD's specific constraints (function words as leaves, content words as heads) to create a parsing system that is both linguistically grounded and highly competitive with supervised methods. This approach is well-suited to UD's formalism and avoids the pitfalls of other unsupervised parsers.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The evaluation across 32 languages with multiple POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive two-POS) and across different domains provides compelling evidence of UDP's robustness and generalizability. The cross-domain consistency analysis (Table 7) particularly highlights the system's stability compared to supervised transfer methods.
3. **Clear presentation**: The paper is well-structured with a detailed example (Section 4), thorough analysis of key components (Section 6), and clear presentation of results. The two-step decoding algorithm (Figure 1) is particularly well-explained.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty**: The approach builds directly on Søgaard (2012) with only minor modifications (UD-specific head rules, personalized PageRank, two-step decoding). The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with Søgaard (2012) to precisely quantify the improvements and their significance.
2. **Test set tuning**: The head directionality (particularly for adpositions) is estimated directly from the test data (Section 3.2), which is not ideal for a proper evaluation. While the authors note this converges quickly, this represents a methodological weakness that could bias results.
3. **Lack of constraint analysis**: The paper doesn't provide analysis of head rules in relation to the corpus (e.g., frequency of disconnected graphs, component sizes). This would help understand the limitations of the approach and when the head rules fail.
4. **PageRank tie-breaking**: The paper doesn't address how PageRank tie-breaking is handled when multiple words have the same rank, or whether this affects performance. This is an important detail for a deterministic system.
5. **Missing oracle results**: The authors don't report oracle results for individual constraints (head rules, directionality, distance), which would provide valuable insights into the upper-bound impact of each component.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you clarify how head directionality was estimated from the test data? Specifically, what portion of the test set was used for this estimation, and how does this compare to tuning on development data?
2. How is PageRank tie-breaking handled in cases where multiple words have identical ranks, and does this affect performance? If so, how much?
3. Could you provide an analysis of the frequency of disconnected graphs in your dataset (including component sizes) and how often the head rules fail to form a connected graph?
4. Could you report oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to show the upper-bound impact of each component on parsing performance?
