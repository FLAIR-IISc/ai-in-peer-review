

# Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data. The system leverages UD's linguistic constraints (specifically, function words as leaves and specific head-dependent rules) combined with personalized PageRank to identify main predicates. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages across three POS setups and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD).

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. A linguistically principled unsupervised parsing approach that effectively exploits UD's formal constraints rather than relying on data-driven methods, demonstrating that such constraints can guide effective unsupervised parsing.

2. A robust parsing system that achieves competitive performance (within 6.4% of a supervised transfer system) while being significantly more resilient to domain shifts than data-driven approaches.

3. A training-free methodology that eliminates the need for language-specific parameters through on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction, making the system truly cross-lingual.

## Strengths

1. The paper makes a compelling case for leveraging UD's formal constraints in unsupervised parsing. The authors demonstrate that by incorporating UD's specific requirements (function words as leaves, specific head rules), they achieve a 4-9 UAS point improvement over a baseline rule-based parser, and a performance gap of only 6.4% compared to a supervised transfer system. This shows that UD's constraints are not just theoretical but practically valuable for unsupervised parsing.

2. The robustness to domain shifts is a significant strength. The authors provide thorough evidence that UDP (with gold POS) has a lower standard deviation across domains (2.5) compared to MSD (4.2) across multiple languages and domains (Table 6). This confirms that rule-based approaches can be more stable to domain shifts than data-driven transfer methods, a finding with clear practical implications for real-world NLP applications.

3. The evaluation is exceptionally well-designed and comprehensive. The authors evaluate on 32 languages across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive type-constrained POS), provide detailed error analysis by POS category, and assess cross-domain consistency. The comparison with MSD, a strong baseline in cross-lingual parsing, is particularly convincing and well-executed.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper lacks sufficient justification for the specific head rules chosen. While the authors mention they were selected to maximize precision on development data, they don't explain why these particular rules work better than alternatives or how they would generalize to languages outside the UD framework. This weakens the claim that the approach is truly language-agnostic.

2. The naive POS evaluation scenario (UDPN) is somewhat artificial and doesn't reflect realistic linguistic processing pipelines. The authors note this but don't sufficiently justify why this extreme case is meaningful for their contribution, making the results in this setup less convincing.

3. The claim that the system is "completely free of language-specific parameters" is problematic. While the adposition direction is estimated on-the-fly from test data, the system still needs to know whether a language uses prepositions or postpositions, which is language-specific information. The paper doesn't clearly address how this would be handled for languages not in the training set.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims UDP is "completely free of language-specific parameters," yet it requires language-specific information to determine adposition direction (pre- vs. postpositions). How do you reconcile this claim with the need to estimate directionality for each language?

2. In Table 5, UDP outperforms MSD on Hindi. Given that Hindi is verb-final (a language where the first verb is not necessarily the main predicate), why does this system perform better than the supervised transfer approach for this language?

3. The paper states that punctuation is excluded from evaluation (Section 6.2), but the UAS metric typically includes punctuation. Can you clarify how the evaluation was conducted and why punctuation was excluded?
