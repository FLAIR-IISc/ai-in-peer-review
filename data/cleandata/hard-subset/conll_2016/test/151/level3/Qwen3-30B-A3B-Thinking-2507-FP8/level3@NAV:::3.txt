

**Review**

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that uses POS tags and a modified PageRank algorithm. The authors claim four contributions: (a) adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, (b) incorporating UD's restriction that function words are leaves, (c) using personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, and (d) making the system completely free of language-specific parameters by estimating adposition direction directly from test data.

**Strengths:** The parser's cross-lingual robustness is compelling, with consistently good performance across 32 languages. The evaluation across multiple domains demonstrates UDP's greater stability to domain shifts compared to supervised baselines (MSD), a significant practical advantage. The system's simplicity and lack of training requirements make it an attractive unsupervised alternative for resource-constrained settings.

**Weaknesses:** The core method closely mirrors Søgaard (2012), with only minor modifications (personalization and two-step decoding) representing limited novelty. The personalization weight of 5 appears arbitrary without justification or ablation studies. The evaluation would benefit from visualizations (charts) to complement the dense tables, as results are difficult to interpret from tables alone. The paper fails to report POS tagging scores in prediction mode, making it impossible to determine if parsing degradation correlates with tagging degradation. Overall, the contribution over Søgaard (2012) is marginal.

**Questions to Authors:** Why was the personalization weight set to 5 specifically? Could you provide ablation studies showing how different weights affect performance? Additionally, could you include visualizations to better illustrate the performance differences across languages and evaluation setups?
