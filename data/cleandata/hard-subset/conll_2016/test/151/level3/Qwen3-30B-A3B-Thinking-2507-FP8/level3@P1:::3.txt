

Summary: This paper proposes UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that uses only POS tags and a modified PageRank algorithm. The approach builds on Søgaard (2012) but incorporates UD-specific constraints, including function words as leaves, personalized PageRank for main predicate identification, and on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction.

Main contributions: The authors claim four contributions: (1) adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, (2) incorporating UD's restriction of function words being leaves, (3) using personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, and (4) making the parser completely free of language-specific parameters. However, the primary contribution is limited to adapting Søgaard (2012) to UD constraints, with the personalized PageRank component being the most substantive but under-explored.

Strengths: 
1. The system demonstrates remarkable cross-lingual robustness, outperforming delexicalized transfer systems (MSD) on average by 6.4% (UDPG vs MSDG) and showing significantly lower standard deviations across domains (0.45 vs 1.45 for MSD), confirming that rule-based systems can be more stable to domain shifts. This robustness is particularly valuable for low-resource languages.
2. The evaluation across three POS scenarios (gold, predicted, and naive) provides a comprehensive picture of the system's performance under realistic conditions, revealing that UDP's error propagation (0.37) is lower than MSD's (0.46) despite using predicted POS tags.
3. The system's simplicity and lack of training requirements make it a valuable baseline for cross-lingual parsing research, especially for truly low-resource languages where parallel data is unavailable.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty is minimal; the approach closely mirrors Søgaard (2012) with only minor adaptations for UD. The "personalized PageRank" component is applied to the main predicate only, which is a narrow application of a well-known technique and lacks sufficient justification for the arbitrary weight of 5.
2. The paper lacks visualizations to help interpret results; the tables (especially Table 5) make it difficult to assess performance trends across languages and setups.
3. The authors fail to report POS tagging scores in prediction mode, which would help determine whether parsing degradation correlates with tagging degradation. Without this, the claim that "UDP drops only marginally (2.2%) compared to MSD" lacks context.
4. The paper contains a minor but distracting typo ("The the main idea" on line 207), suggesting insufficient proofreading.

Questions to Authors:
1. What is the rationale for choosing the personalization weight of 5? How sensitive is the system to this parameter?
2. Could you provide visualizations (e.g., bar charts) comparing UDPG vs MSDG across languages to better illustrate the performance trends?
3. Could you report the POS tagging accuracy for the languages used in the predicted POS setup (UDPP) to help contextualize the parsing results?
