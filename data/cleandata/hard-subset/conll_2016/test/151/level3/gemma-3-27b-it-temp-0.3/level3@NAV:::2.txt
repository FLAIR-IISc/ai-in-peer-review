## Review of “Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training”

This paper presents UDP, a novel unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages PageRank and a set of hard-coded rules, requiring no training data. The core idea – exploiting the constraints inherent in the UD formalism – is well-motivated and the paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the intuition behind the design choices. The authors demonstrate competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer system across 32 languages, and importantly, show greater robustness to domain shift.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful demonstration of a surprisingly effective, truly unsupervised parsing approach within the UD framework. While unsupervised parsing is not new, achieving competitive results *without any training* and with demonstrable robustness is a significant advancement. A second, valuable contribution lies in the clever use of personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, effectively guiding the parsing process. Finally, the method of dynamically estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data is a practical and effective adaptation to language-specific variations.

The strengths of this submission are numerous. The clarity of the explanation is paramount; the paper is easy to follow and understand the rationale behind each step. The extensive evaluation across a large number of languages, and the inclusion of domain adaptation experiments, provide strong evidence for the generalizability and robustness of the approach. The comparison to a strong baseline (delexicalized transfer parsing) further solidifies the contribution. The authors also proactively identify potential future work, which is likely to spur further research in this area.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the head rules are well-explained, their origin (beyond development on English) isn’t fully transparent. A more detailed justification for these specific rules, perhaps with an ablation study, would be beneficial. Additionally, the reliance on POS tags, even though not *trained* on, is a dependency. The performance drop with predicted POS tags, while less severe than the baseline, is still noticeable. Exploring methods to mitigate this dependency, or incorporating a more robust POS tagging component, could be a valuable extension. Finally, the evaluation primarily focuses on UAS. While standard, exploring other metrics (e.g., dependency distance) could provide a more nuanced understanding of the parser’s performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the process of selecting the initial head rules? Was it purely based on performance on the English development set, or were there linguistic considerations beyond that?
2.  Have you considered any methods to improve the robustness of the parser to errors in POS tagging?
3.  Would you consider exploring other evaluation metrics beyond UAS to provide a more comprehensive assessment of parsing quality?
