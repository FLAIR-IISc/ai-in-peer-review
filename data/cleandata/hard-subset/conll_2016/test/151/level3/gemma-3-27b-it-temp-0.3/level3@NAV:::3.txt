## Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) leveraging PageRank and a set of dependency head rules. The core idea is to exploit the constraints imposed by the UD formalism to achieve competitive parsing without any training data. The authors claim contributions in adapting head rules to UD, incorporating function word restrictions, using personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and estimating adposition direction on test data.

I believe the primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating a surprisingly strong baseline for unsupervised parsing *within* the UD framework. While the individual components aren’t groundbreaking, the combination provides a solid and robust parser, particularly when considering its lack of training. The comparison to a delexicalized transfer system highlights the potential of a purely rule-based approach when guided by a consistent formalism like UD. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of UDP’s robustness to domain shift, a valuable characteristic for low-resource scenarios.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Baseline:** UDP achieves competitive UAS scores, approaching those of a supervised transfer system, which is impressive for an unsupervised approach.
*   **Robustness:** The evaluation across multiple languages and, importantly, across different domains, demonstrates the system’s stability and generalizability. The results showing less sensitivity to domain shift compared to the supervised baseline are noteworthy.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, making it easy to understand the core concepts.
*   **Availability:** Making the parser and annotated datasets freely available is a positive contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of using PageRank for dependency parsing is directly inspired by Søgaard (2012), and the improvements presented feel incremental rather than transformative. The personalization boosting of the main predicate, while useful, is applied in a rather simplistic manner.
*   **Arbitrary Parameter:** The personalization weight of 5 feels arbitrary and lacks justification. A more systematic exploration of this parameter, perhaps with an ablation study, would strengthen the results.
*   **Evaluation Presentation:** The results are presented primarily in tables, which makes it difficult to quickly grasp the relative performance across languages and domains. Visualizations (e.g., charts) would significantly improve interpretability.
*   **POS Tagging Evaluation:** The paper doesn't report POS tagging accuracy in prediction mode. It would be valuable to assess whether parsing degradation correlates with errors in POS tagging, providing insight into the system’s sensitivity to tagger quality.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the personalization weight of 5? Did you explore other values, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Would you consider providing visualizations of the results (e.g., scatter plots of UAS vs. language, or bar charts showing performance across domains)?
3.  Can you report the POS tagging accuracy of the TnT tagger used for the predicted POS evaluation? This would help assess the impact of tagging errors on parsing performance.

**Additional Comments:**

*   There is a typo on line 207 ("The the main idea" → "The main idea").
*   The discussion section could be expanded to delve deeper into the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research.
