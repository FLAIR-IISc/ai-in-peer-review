## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) leveraging PageRank and a small set of hard-coded rules. The core strength of this work lies in its surprisingly competitive performance without relying on any training data, offering a robust alternative to supervised transfer methods, particularly concerning domain adaptation. The paper is exceptionally well-written, providing clear explanations for the intuition behind the algorithm’s design choices, including the personalization of PageRank and the two-step decoding process. 

**Summary:** The authors address the challenges of cross-lingual dependency parsing, particularly the difficulties associated with data-driven approaches and domain adaptation. They propose a novel unsupervised parsing algorithm, UDP, which utilizes PageRank to rank content words and applies a set of pre-defined dependency rules based on the UD framework. The algorithm operates in two steps: first assigning heads to content words based on PageRank scores and dependency rules, then assigning heads to function words based on proximity and predefined directionality. The method is evaluated on 32 languages, comparing performance to a delexicalized transfer system and assessing robustness to domain change.

**Contributions:** I identify three primary contributions:

1. **A competitive, truly unsupervised UD parser:** This is the most significant contribution. Achieving performance comparable to a delexicalized transfer system *without any training* is a substantial feat in unsupervised parsing. This opens avenues for parsing low-resource languages where training data is scarce.
2. **Effective integration of UD constraints:** The paper demonstrates the power of explicitly incorporating linguistic constraints (specifically those defined by UD) into an unsupervised parsing algorithm. The use of UD’s function word restrictions and well-defined POS tags is central to the success of UDP.
3. **Robustness to domain shift:** The experimental results clearly demonstrate that UDP is more resilient to domain changes than a delexicalized transfer system. This is a critical advantage in real-world applications where parsing models often encounter diverse data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper rigorously evaluates UDP against a strong baseline (a delexicalized transfer system) across a large number of languages (32). The inclusion of a domain adaptation analysis strengthens the claims about UDP’s robustness. The use of standard UAS as a metric also facilitates comparison with existing work.
2. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly articulates the rationale behind each design choice. The explanation of the PageRank personalization strategy and the two-step decoding process is particularly insightful. The inclusion of Fig. 1, illustrating the decoding algorithm, significantly enhances understanding.
3. **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of leveraging the UD framework and its inherent constraints. The reasoning behind incorporating function word restrictions and utilizing a personalized PageRank is clearly explained and supported by the results.
4. **Practical Impact & Availability:** The authors make the parser and annotated test sets freely available, promoting reproducibility and further research in the field. This is a valuable contribution to the community.
5. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed breakdown by POS tags and domain reveals strengths and weaknesses of the system, providing valuable insights for future improvements. The analysis highlighting the limitations of PUNCT tagging is particularly useful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the *combination* of PageRank and UD constraints is effective, the individual components (PageRank and rule-based parsing) are not novel. The key novelty lies in the specific adaptation and integration of these techniques within the UD framework.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Quality:** Although the system is shown to be *more* robust than the baseline to POS tagging errors, its performance is still heavily reliant on accurate POS tags. The results on predicted POS show a significant drop in performance, highlighting this limitation. Further exploration of techniques to mitigate POS tagging errors would be beneficial.
3. **Simplistic Handling of Complex Structures:** The algorithm’s inability to handle relative clauses (as acknowledged by the authors) limits its applicability to more complex sentences. Expanding the rule set to accommodate these structures could improve performance.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions tuning the teleport probability for PageRank, it’s unclear how sensitive the system is to other parameter choices, such as the weighting factor for PageRank personalization. A more detailed sensitivity analysis would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of running PageRank for long sentences? Is there a practical limit to the sentence length that UDP can handle efficiently?
2.  The paper mentions excluding Japanese due to the lack of word forms in the treebank. Have you considered exploring subword tokenization or character-based approaches to adapt UDP for languages with morphological complexity?
3. The system doesn’t handle relative clauses. Are there plans to expand the rule set to incorporate such structures, and what challenges do you anticipate in doing so?
4. How sensitive is the performance to the weighting factor used in the PageRank personalization (the factor of 5)? Was this value chosen through extensive experimentation, or is it relatively stable?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the UD framework itself and how these limitations might impact the performance of UDP.
*   A comparison with other recent unsupervised parsing approaches (beyond the delexicalized transfer system) would further contextualize the contributions of this work.
