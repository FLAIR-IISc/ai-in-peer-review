This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages PageRank and dependency head rules, requiring no training data. The core idea is to exploit the constraints inherent in the UD formalism to build a parser competitive with supervised transfer systems. The authors claim contributions in adapting head rules to UD, incorporating function word restrictions, using personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and achieving language-agnostic adposition attachment.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is a demonstration of the viability of a surprisingly strong unsupervised parser within the UD framework. The ability to achieve competitive performance – approaching that of a delexicalized transfer parser – without any training data is notable. This provides a valuable baseline and proof-of-concept for future research in unsupervised parsing, particularly within the constraints of a standardized dependency formalism like UD. A secondary contribution lies in the systematic exploration of how UD constraints can be directly incorporated into parsing rules, which is demonstrated in the head rules used by the parser. The empirical evaluation across 32 languages, while using existing treebanks, provides a broad assessment of the method’s robustness.

The strengths of this submission are several. First, the method’s resilience to domain shift is a significant advantage. The authors provide evidence suggesting UDP is less susceptible to performance degradation across different text genres compared to a supervised baseline, a crucial characteristic for cross-lingual parsing in real-world applications. Second, the simplicity and clarity of the approach are commendable. The reliance on a small set of rules and a well-defined algorithm makes the system interpretable and potentially adaptable. Third, the comprehensive evaluation across a large number of languages provides a strong empirical basis for the claims made. Fourth, the paper explicitly acknowledges related work and positions its contributions accordingly, demonstrating a good understanding of the field.

However, there are several weaknesses that should be addressed. The most significant concern is the limited novelty of the proposed approach. While the authors build upon existing work, notably Søgaard (2012), the presented improvements – especially the personalized PageRank – feel incremental. The impact of the personalization is not thoroughly investigated; the weight of 5 assigned to the main predicate appears arbitrary and lacks justification. Further experimentation with different weights and personalization strategies is necessary to demonstrate its true potential. A second weakness is the lack of detailed error analysis. While the authors acknowledge the challenges with punctuation and coordination, a more granular analysis of parsing errors would provide valuable insights for future improvements. The evaluation could be more visually accessible with the addition of charts to complement the tables. Finally, the paper would benefit from reporting the POS tagging accuracy when using predicted POS tags, allowing for a clearer correlation between tagging performance and parsing degradation. This is vital to understanding the robustness of the system.

Specifically, the impact of the PageRank personalization scheme feels under-developed.  Is a value of 5 optimal?  How sensitive is the system to this parameter? An ablation study demonstrating the impact of personalization would substantially strengthen the paper.  The adposition direction estimation, while a practical solution, could be expanded upon; exploring more sophisticated methods for determining adposition attachment direction could further enhance performance. The reliance on English for initial development and parameter tuning might introduce a bias.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the justification for choosing a personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate in the PageRank algorithm? Have you explored other values or strategies for personalization?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the most common types of parsing errors and their causes?
3.  What is the POS tagging accuracy when using predicted POS tags, and how does this correlate with the observed parsing degradation?
4.  To what extent do you believe the initial development and tuning using English data might introduce a bias into the system?
