This paper presents UDP, a novel unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies (UD) that distinguishes itself by requiring no training data. The core idea is to leverage PageRank to estimate word relevance, combined with a set of dependency head rules tailored to UD’s constraints. The algorithm effectively bypasses the need for supervised learning or cross-lingual transfer, offering a linguistically sound and robust alternative, particularly in low-resource scenarios.  The authors demonstrate competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer system across 32 languages, highlighting the method’s generality and resilience to domain change.

The primary contribution of this work is the development of a surprisingly effective, completely unsupervised parser for UD. While unsupervised parsing is not a new field, achieving competitive results *without any training data* is a significant accomplishment.  The combination of PageRank, judiciously applied to a dependency graph built using UD-specific rules, is a clever and well-executed approach. A second notable contribution lies in the demonstrated robustness to domain shift. The parser exhibits consistent performance across different text genres, suggesting it’s less prone to overfitting than data-driven methods. This is a valuable characteristic for real-world applications where the test domain may not perfectly match the training data. Finally, the comprehensive evaluation across a large number of languages (32) and a thorough comparison against a strong baseline (delexicalized transfer parsing) provide strong evidence for the method’s effectiveness and generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Effectiveness of the Unsupervised Approach:** The core strength of this work is its ability to achieve competitive parsing accuracy without relying on any training data. This is particularly impactful given the challenges of data scarcity in many languages. The use of PageRank, informed by UD’s linguistic constraints, is a novel and effective way to establish a dependency structure.  The reported results, consistently nearing the performance of a supervised delexicalized transfer parser, are compelling.
2. **Robustness to Domain Change:** The experimental results demonstrate the parser's stability across various domains.  The authors convincingly show that UDP is less susceptible to performance degradation when applied to text from different genres than the multi-source delexicalized parser.  This robustness is a significant advantage for practical applications where encountering diverse text types is common.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The evaluation across 32 languages, utilizing the UD framework, is a substantial undertaking. This wide-ranging evaluation provides strong evidence for the generality and applicability of the proposed method. The inclusion of both gold-standard POS tags and predicted POS tags offers a realistic assessment of performance in scenarios with limited resources.
4. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:**  The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The rationale behind each design choice, such as the personalized PageRank and the two-step decoding algorithm, is thoroughly articulated. The inclusion of a detailed example run further enhances understanding.
5. **Availability of Resources:** The authors mention that both the parser and the datasets are freely available, promoting reproducibility and further research. This is a positive aspect that facilitates community engagement and advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependency on POS Tagging Accuracy:** While the parser doesn’t require *training* data, it inherently relies on the accuracy of POS tags. The performance degradation observed when using predicted POS tags, although less severe than for the delexicalized parser, highlights this dependency. Exploring methods to mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors (e.g., incorporating POS tag confidence scores into the PageRank algorithm) could be a valuable direction for future research.
2. **Limited Handling of Complex Linguistic Phenomena:** The current implementation relies on a relatively small set of hard-coded rules. This simplicity may limit its ability to accurately parse sentences with complex grammatical structures, such as those involving coordination or long-distance dependencies. The authors acknowledge this limitation, suggesting extensions to handle these cases.
3. **Rule Engineering and Language Specificity:** While the rules are based on UD’s universal principles, some degree of language-specific adaptation (e.g., determining adposition direction) is still required.  This adaptation, while minimal, introduces a potential source of bias or error. Further investigation into automating this process or developing more universally applicable rules would be beneficial.
4. **PageRank Parameter Tuning:** The paper states that the teleport probability for PageRank was tuned on the English development data. While a reasonable approach, it's unclear whether this optimal value generalizes well to other languages. A more systematic exploration of parameter tuning across a diverse set of languages would strengthen the argument for the method’s robustness.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the head rules in Table 1? Are there specific linguistic constructions that are consistently misparsed due to the incompleteness of these rules?
2.  The paper mentions that the personalization factor for PageRank (giving 5x weight to the estimated main predicate) was determined empirically. What specific experiments led to the choice of 5x, and how sensitive is the performance to this parameter?
3.  You observed a difference in standard deviation across domains between UDP and MSD. Could you provide more insight into *why* UDP demonstrates greater stability? Is it primarily due to the simpler rule set, the avoidance of data-driven biases, or other factors?
4. The results show UDP’s performance on Bulgarian bulletin is relatively low compared to other domains. Is this because of the specific linguistic characteristics of that domain and the Bulgarian language or is there any other reason?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis, providing specific examples of misparsed sentences and identifying the common sources of errors.
* It would be interesting to compare UDP's performance to other state-of-the-art unsupervised parsing methods, beyond the delexicalized transfer parser.
