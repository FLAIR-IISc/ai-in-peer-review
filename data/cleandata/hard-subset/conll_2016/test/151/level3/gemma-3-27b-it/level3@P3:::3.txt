## Review

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) leveraging PageRank and dependency head rules, aiming to be competitive with delexicalized transfer systems. While the core idea of exploiting UD constraints in an unsupervised manner is sound, the contribution feels incremental and lacks significant novelty.

**Summary:** The authors propose a parser that eliminates the need for training data by relying on POS constraints inherent in UD and a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify the main predicate. They evaluate UDP on 32 languages, comparing its performance to a delexicalized transfer system, and also assess its robustness to domain shifts. The results demonstrate competitive performance, particularly regarding stability across domains.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical implementation leveraging UD constraints:** The paper offers a concrete, working system that demonstrates the potential of directly exploiting the UD framework for unsupervised parsing. This provides a useful baseline and starting point for future work in this area.
2. **Demonstration of robustness to domain change:** The experimental results convincingly show that UDP is more stable across different domains compared to a supervised transfer baseline, highlighting a key advantage of the unsupervised approach.
3. **Personalization of PageRank for predicate identification:** The incorporation of a personalized PageRank, biased towards the likely predicate, is a reasonable and effective enhancement, although its exploration remains limited.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive performance:** UDP achieves results comparable to a delexicalized transfer system, which is a significant accomplishment for an unsupervised parser. This indicates the effectiveness of the chosen approach.
2. **Robustness to domain change:** The demonstrated stability across domains is a major strength. This makes UDP a viable option for parsing data from diverse sources where labeled data is scarce. The results presented in Table 6 are particularly compelling.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation:** Evaluating the system on 32 languages provides a broad assessment of its generalization ability. The inclusion of multiple evaluation scenarios (gold POS, predicted POS) adds further depth.
4. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The method is described in detail, and the experimental setup is clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty:** The core idea of using PageRank for unsupervised parsing closely mirrors Søgaard (2012b). The authors acknowledge this but position their contribution as extending that work. However, the extensions – incorporating UD constraints and personalization – feel relatively minor. The novelty claim feels overstated.
2. **Under-explored personalization:** While the personalization of PageRank to boost the main predicate is a sensible addition, it is applied only to this single element and the weight of 5 appears arbitrary. A more thorough exploration of different personalization strategies and weights could significantly improve performance. Justification for the chosen weight is lacking.
3. **Lack of visualizations:** The results are presented primarily in tabular format. Visualizations, such as charts comparing the performance of UDP and the baseline across languages, would greatly enhance the interpretability of the results and make the key findings more accessible.
4. **Missing POS tagging performance correlation:** Reporting POS tagging accuracy in prediction mode (as done) is good, but the paper would benefit from explicitly analyzing the correlation between degradation in POS tagging performance and degradation in parsing performance. This would provide insights into the system's sensitivity to tagging errors.
5. **Minor Typo:** A typo exists at l.207 ("The the main idea").

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing a personalization weight of 5 for PageRank? Were any other weights explored, and if so, what were the results?
2. How would the performance of UDP change if the personalization were extended to multiple potential predicates (e.g., the top N ranked verbs)?
3. Would you consider providing visualizations (e.g., bar charts) comparing the performance of UDP and the baseline across languages to aid in understanding the results?
4. Have you investigated the correlation between POS tagging accuracy and parsing accuracy when using predicted POS tags?

**Additional Comments:**

* The references are extensive and well-curated.
* The availability of the parser and datasets is commendable.
