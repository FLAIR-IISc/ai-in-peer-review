This paper presents UDP, a novel unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies, leveraging PageRank and a set of linguistically motivated head rules. The core idea is to build a dependency graph based on permitted POS-based head-dependent relations and then rank words using a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify likely head-dependent pairings. The system operates deterministically, requiring no training data and demonstrating competitive performance across 32 languages.

The primary contribution of this work is a practical and surprisingly effective unsupervised parsing method grounded in the constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism. While unsupervised dependency parsing is an established field, the approach presented here delivers competitive results, achieving performance comparable to a delexicalized transfer system, particularly notable given its lack of training data. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration of robustness to domain change, a crucial advantage for practical cross-lingual parsing applications. Finally, the extensive empirical evaluation across a large number of languages, combined with a detailed ablation study, provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of the different components of the system.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves impressive performance for an unsupervised system, consistently reaching UAS scores competitive with supervised transfer learning approaches (as shown in Table 5). The results on the cross-domain evaluation (Table 6) convincingly demonstrate the robustness of the system compared to the supervised baseline, supporting the claim of improved resilience to domain shifts.
2. **Linguistic Grounding:** The parser’s reliance on Universal Dependencies constraints is a significant strength. By explicitly incorporating linguistic principles, the system avoids many of the pitfalls associated with purely data-driven approaches, such as the tendency to learn spurious correlations. This grounding is particularly evident in the handling of function words and the head direction estimation.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the system, including comparisons to a strong baseline, ablation studies examining the contribution of different components (PageRank, head rules), and analysis of performance across different POS tags and domains. This comprehensive evaluation strengthens the credibility of the findings.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and experimental setup. The inclusion of a detailed example (Section 4) is particularly helpful in understanding the parsing process.
5. **Availability of Resources:** The authors make the parser and annotated datasets publicly available, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While effective, the core algorithm builds heavily on existing techniques, particularly Søgaard (2012b). The personalization of PageRank and the two-step decoding process represent incremental improvements, but the fundamental approach remains similar. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the relationship to prior work and a clearer articulation of the novel aspects of the method.
2. **Test-Set Driven Parameter Tuning:** The head directionality estimation is performed directly on the test data, which could introduce a degree of overfitting and potentially inflate the reported performance. While the authors acknowledge the rapid convergence of this estimation, it raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
3. **Tie-Breaking in PageRank:** The paper does not explicitly address how ties are broken during the PageRank ranking process. If multiple words have the same rank, the choice of head could significantly impact the final parse tree. Understanding the tie-breaking mechanism is crucial for fully evaluating the determinism and reliability of the system.
4. **Lack of Oracle Studies:** To better understand the potential of the approach, it would be beneficial to present oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance). Reporting the performance achievable if each constraint were perfectly satisfied would provide a clearer picture of the limiting factors and the potential for further improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the tie-breaking mechanism used during the PageRank ranking process? Does the method’s performance vary depending on the tie-breaking strategy?
2. Could you provide an analysis of the head rules in relation to the corpus? Specifically, what percentage of sentences result in disconnected graphs, and what is the typical size of the connected components?
3. While the convergence of adposition direction estimation is fast, could you provide any evidence showing the distribution of estimated directions and their frequency across languages?

**Additional Comments:**

- In Section 4.1, the phrasing "we obtain the rank" could be improved to "we obtain the ranking."
- In Tables 5 and 7, consider reordering the columns to prioritize the order presented in Table 7 (BLG, UDPG, MSDG, UDPP, MSDP), for consistency.
- In Section 6.1, replace “isolate the contribution of both” with “isolate the contributions of each.”
