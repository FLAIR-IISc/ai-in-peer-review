## Review for "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

This paper presents UDP, a novel unsupervised parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies that impressively eschews any training data. The core idea—leveraging PageRank on dependency graphs constrained by UD’s inherent rules—is well-executed and clearly explained. The authors successfully demonstrate that UDP achieves competitive performance compared to a delexicalized transfer system, while also exhibiting greater robustness to domain shift. 

The primary contribution lies in the effective combination of linguistically-informed rules with a graph-based ranking algorithm. While unsupervised parsing is an established field, this work significantly advances the state-of-the-art by cleverly exploiting the constraints imposed by the UD framework. A second key contribution is the demonstration of UDP’s resilience to domain changes, a practical concern often overlooked in parsing research. The detailed explanation of how PageRank personalization is used to identify main predicates and the method for estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data are particularly insightful.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a thorough exploration of the method, including a clear example illustrating the decoding process. The experimental setup is robust, evaluating on a large number of languages and comparing against a strong baseline. The error analysis, particularly the discussion of domain robustness, is valuable and demonstrates a deep understanding of the system's strengths and limitations. 

However, a potential weakness is the reliance on gold POS tags for the main evaluation. While the authors do present results with predicted POS tags, the performance drop is substantial, suggesting the parser's sensitivity to POS tagging errors. Further investigation into methods to mitigate this sensitivity, perhaps by incorporating POS information directly into the PageRank graph construction, could be beneficial. Additionally, while the paper establishes competitiveness with a delexicalized transfer system, a comparison to more recent and sophisticated transfer learning approaches would further solidify the contribution. Finally, the limited exploration of error types beyond domain shift could be expanded.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The personalization of PageRank focuses on identifying the main predicate as a verb or content word. Have you experimented with other personalization strategies, such as biasing towards nouns or other POS tags, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Given the observed sensitivity to POS tagging errors, have you considered methods to incorporate POS tag confidence scores into the PageRank algorithm or the decoding process?
3.  Could you elaborate on the potential impact of the head-dependent rules, particularly the decision to exclude NOUN -> VERB? Would incorporating this rule, potentially with some form of weighting or filtering, improve performance on languages where relative clauses are common?
