This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) leveraging PageRank and a set of dependency head rules. The core idea is to exploit the constraints imposed by the UD formalism to achieve competitive parsing performance without relying on any training data. The authors claim contributions in adapting dependency head rules to UD, incorporating the UD function word constraint, using personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data.

The most significant contribution of this work, in my view, is the demonstration of a surprisingly strong baseline for unsupervised parsing across a large number of languages (32) using a relatively simple and linguistically motivated approach. The results show UDP compares favorably to a delexicalized transfer system, showcasing the potential of rule-based methods in a modern cross-lingual parsing setting. The extensive evaluation across languages and, importantly, across different domains, provides a solid empirical foundation for these claims and is a strong point of the paper. The thoroughness of the experimental setup, including comparisons to a strong baseline and analysis of performance degradation with predicted POS tags, is commendable. The analysis of cross-domain consistency further strengthens the argument for the robustness of the proposed approach.

A second notable contribution lies in the application of personalized PageRank to improve the identification of main predicates. While PageRank has been used in unsupervised parsing before (Søgaard, 2012), the personalization aspect, albeit simple, appears effective and highlights the potential for incorporating prior knowledge into the algorithm.

Finally, the direct estimation of adposition attachment direction on test data, avoiding reliance on potentially unreliable annotations, is a practical and sensible addition that contributes to the parser’s robustness.

However, the paper’s novelty is limited. The method builds heavily on prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012), and the enhancements presented do not represent a significant departure from established techniques. The personalization of PageRank, while effective, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The choice of a personalization weight of 5 lacks clear justification and could benefit from a more systematic exploration of different values. It’s not clear why only the main predicate is boosted, and whether extending this personalization to other content words might further improve performance.

A major weakness is the lack of visual representations of the results. Tables containing numerous numbers are difficult to quickly interpret. Charts illustrating performance differences across languages or domains would significantly enhance the paper’s readability and impact.

Furthermore, while the authors report results with predicted POS tags, they do not explicitly correlate parsing performance degradation with POS tagging accuracy. Reporting POS tagging scores (precision, recall, F1) in the prediction mode would help assess whether observed drops in UAS are directly attributable to tagging errors, thus providing a clearer understanding of the system's robustness.

Finally, the paper occasionally suffers from overly assertive language regarding the contributions. For example, the claim of "completely free of language-specific parameters" is somewhat misleading, as the head direction rules (even if determined dynamically) represent a form of language-specific knowledge.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the personalization weight of 5 in the PageRank algorithm? Have you explored other values and, if so, what were the results?
2.  Would you consider extending the PageRank personalization to other content words besides the identified main predicate? If not, could you explain the rationale behind this decision?
3.  Please provide POS tagging scores (precision, recall, F1) when using predicted POS tags to enable a better correlation analysis between tagging errors and parsing performance degradation.
4.  Could you provide examples of dependency trees generated by UDP, highlighting cases where the parser succeeds and fails? Visualizing example outputs would provide valuable insights into the system's behavior.
