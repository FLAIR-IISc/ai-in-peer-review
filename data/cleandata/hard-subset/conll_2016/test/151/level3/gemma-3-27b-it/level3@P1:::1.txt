This paper presents UDP, a novel unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies, leveraging POS-based head rules and a personalized PageRank algorithm. The core idea is to construct a dependency graph based on linguistically motivated rules, rank content words using PageRank, and then deterministically decode the tree structure by assigning each word to its closest, allowable head. The parser requires no training data and demonstrates competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer system across 32 languages, exhibiting notable robustness to domain shift.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of a surprisingly effective, entirely unsupervised parsing approach within the UD framework. While unsupervised dependency parsing is an established area, achieving competitive results *without any training* is a significant accomplishment. The parser's robustness to domain change is also a notable strength, suggesting a generalizability that data-driven methods may struggle to achieve. A second, related contribution lies in the successful adaptation of PageRank for this task, specifically the personalization aspect that prioritizes likely predicate identification. Finally, the thorough evaluation across a large number of languages and the inclusion of domain adaptation experiments provide a valuable resource and benchmark for future research.

Several aspects of this work strongly support its acceptance. First, the parser achieves competitive performance, often surpassing a delexicalized transfer system – a strong baseline – despite its lack of training data. This is particularly impressive given the inherent challenges of unsupervised parsing. Second, the demonstrated robustness to domain shift is a compelling advantage.  The experimental results demonstrate the parser's ability to maintain performance across different genres, highlighting its potential for real-world applications. Third, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents a complex algorithm. The inclusion of a detailed example run (Section 4) significantly aids understanding. Finally, the comprehensive evaluation across 32 languages, and the availability of the parser and data, contribute to the reproducibility and impact of the work. 

However, there are also weaknesses to consider. The core algorithm, while effective, builds heavily on previous work, particularly Søgaard (2012b).  While the authors highlight differences (personalized PageRank, two-step decoding), the fundamental approach of using PageRank and head rules is not entirely novel. Second, the method of determining adposition direction directly on the test data, while pragmatic, introduces a potential for data contamination and raises questions about the generalizability to truly unseen languages. Though the authors state the process converges quickly, tuning on the test set is generally discouraged.  Third, the reliance on the UD formalism, while a strength in terms of cross-linguistic consistency, also introduces a potential limitation: performance may be highly sensitive to the quality and consistency of UD annotation across languages. 

To address these concerns, several questions should be answered during the author response period.  First, could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the head rules, specifically the frequency of cases where the rules do not fully connect the graph and the typical size of disconnected components? This would shed light on the practical impact of rule coverage. Second, how are ties broken during the PageRank ranking and dependency assignment processes?  The deterministic nature of the algorithm suggests a clear tie-breaking strategy must exist, and it’s important to understand how this might affect performance.  Third, to better understand the contribution of each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance), could the authors report oracle results – i.e., the performance achievable if each constraint were perfectly satisfied? This would help to quantify the limitations imposed by imperfect rules or directionality estimation. Finally, what is the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly concerning the PageRank computation for larger sentences?
