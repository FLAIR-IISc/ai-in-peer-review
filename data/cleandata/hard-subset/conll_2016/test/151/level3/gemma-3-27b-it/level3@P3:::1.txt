## Review

This paper presents UDP, a novel unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm for Universal Dependencies, leveraging PageRank and POS-based head rules. The core idea—combining linguistically motivated rules with a graph-based ranking approach—is a sensible one, and the evaluation across a substantial number of languages is commendable. The parser achieves competitive performance, approaching that of a delexicalized transfer system, and exhibits robustness to domain change.

**Contributions:**

1. **A competitive, unsupervised dependency parser:** The most significant contribution is the UDP parser itself, demonstrating that reasonable parsing accuracy can be achieved *without* training data, relying solely on UD constraints and a PageRank-based scoring mechanism. This is particularly valuable for low-resource languages.
2. **Extensive multilingual evaluation:** The comprehensive evaluation on 32 languages, coupled with an analysis of domain sensitivity, provides strong evidence for the generalizability and robustness of the approach. This level of broad testing is a notable strength.
3. **Effective integration of linguistic constraints:** The careful adaptation of dependency head rules to UD's POS relations, and the incorporation of function word restrictions, represent a thoughtful application of linguistic principles to the parsing problem.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline & Competitive Results:** UDP performs remarkably well compared to a delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), demonstrating the effectiveness of leveraging UD constraints. The average performance difference of 6.4% is small, especially considering the unsupervised nature of the approach. The consistent outperformance on Hindi is a particularly noteworthy result.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The domain change evaluation (Table 6 & 7) convincingly demonstrates UDP’s superior stability compared to MSD. The lower standard deviation in UAS scores across domains highlights the benefit of the rule-based foundation. This is a crucial advantage for real-world applications where data distribution can vary.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The method is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The inclusion of an example run (Section 4) is helpful for understanding the algorithm’s operation.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation Metrics:** Using standard UAS as the primary evaluation metric is appropriate and facilitates comparison with existing work. The investigation of performance under different POS tagging scenarios (gold, predicted, naive) provides a nuanced understanding of the system’s limitations.
5. **Freely Available Resources:** Providing both the parser and the annotated test sets enhances reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the approach is effective, the core idea of combining head rules with graph-based ranking builds directly on Søgaard (2012). The extensions—personalized PageRank and two-step decoding—are incremental improvements rather than a radical departure. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how it distinguishes itself from existing work beyond these specific modifications.
2. **Head Directionality Tuning:** The decision to tune head directionality on the *test* set is problematic. While the authors acknowledge rapid convergence, it introduces a potential for data contamination. Ideally, this parameter should be tuned on a separate development set.
3. **PageRank Tie-Breaking:** The paper does not explicitly address how ties in the PageRank ranking are handled during the decoding process. This information is important because ties could potentially impact performance, and a clear explanation of the tie-breaking strategy would enhance the reproducibility and understanding of the algorithm.
4. **Lack of Oracle Results:** Reporting oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) would provide valuable insight into the potential performance gains achievable by relaxing each constraint individually. This would help quantify the impact of each component of the system and identify areas for future improvement. For example, knowing the UAS achievable if directionality was always correct would highlight the value of accurate direction estimation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How are ties in the PageRank ranking resolved during the decoding process? Does the tie-breaking mechanism affect the overall performance of the parser?
2. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the frequency of disconnected graphs encountered during PageRank graph construction? What is the distribution of component sizes in these disconnected graphs?
3. Would you be willing to re-tune the head directionality parameter on a separate development set to address the concern about potential data contamination?

**Additional Comments:**

* In Section 4.1, change “we obtain rank” to “we obtain the rank”.
* Reorder the columns in Table 5 to match the order in Table 7 for consistency.
* In Section 6.1, change “isolate the of both” to “isolate the contribution of both”.
