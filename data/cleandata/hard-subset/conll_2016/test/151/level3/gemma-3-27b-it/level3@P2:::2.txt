This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that eschews training data in favor of PageRank and a set of dependency head rules. The authors demonstrate competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer system across 32 languages, alongside improved robustness to domain shift.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that surprisingly strong dependency parsing results can be achieved *without any training data* by leveraging the constraints inherent in the Universal Dependencies formalism and a carefully designed PageRank-based algorithm. While unsupervised parsing is not a new area, achieving competitive performance with a method so fundamentally different from existing approaches – especially those relying on large-scale data – is significant. A secondary contribution lies in the effective adaptation of dependency head rules to the UD framework, specifically incorporating the UD restriction on function words. Finally, the thorough and well-documented experimental evaluation across a wide range of languages and domains, including a detailed analysis of domain robustness, represents a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of achieving competitive parsing *without training* is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from the prevailing paradigm in dependency parsing. The reliance on linguistic constraints (UD formalism) and a graph-based ranking algorithm offers a fresh perspective and addresses the limitations of data-driven methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser performs remarkably well, consistently achieving competitive UAS scores compared to a delexicalized transfer system, especially given the lack of training data. The results across 32 languages demonstrate the broad applicability of the approach.  The fact that UDP often outperforms the supervised baseline on certain languages (e.g., Hindi) is particularly impressive.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The experimental results clearly demonstrate UDP’s greater resilience to domain changes compared to the delexicalized transfer system. This robustness is a crucial advantage in real-world applications where parsing models often encounter data from diverse sources. The detailed analysis of domain effects is insightful and strengthens the validity of this claim.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough and well-structured evaluation, including a comparison with relevant baselines, analysis of performance across different POS tag scenarios (gold vs. predicted), and examination of domain sensitivity.  The inclusion of both automatic metrics and error analysis enhances the credibility of the findings.
5. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, the intuition behind the design choices (e.g., PageRank personalization), and the experimental setup. The step-by-step example of parsing a sentence helps readers understand the inner workings of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper acknowledges the limitations related to specific POS tags (PUNCT and CONJ), the error analysis remains relatively superficial. A more in-depth examination of the types of errors made by the parser, particularly in challenging cases, would provide valuable insights for future improvement. It would be helpful to understand *why* the parser fails on certain structures.
2. **Sensitivity to POS Tag Quality:** Although UDP demonstrates better robustness than the supervised baseline regarding POS tag errors, performance still degrades significantly when using predicted POS tags. Investigating methods to mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors, perhaps through incorporating more robust POS tagging techniques or integrating POS tag confidence scores into the parsing process, would be beneficial.
3. **Rule Engineering:** The initial head rules were determined on English data. While the results indicate generalizability, the process of creating and tuning these rules could be more systematically described.  The authors mention a development set was used, but more detail regarding the criteria for rule selection and tuning would be helpful.
4. **Scalability Concerns:** The PageRank computation is computationally expensive. While the paper mentions the algorithm runs in less than O(n^2), a discussion of the practical scalability of the approach to very long sentences or large-scale datasets would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on specific types of errors made by UDP and potential reasons for these errors?
2. Have you explored strategies for mitigating the impact of POS tagging errors, such as integrating POS tag confidence scores or using more robust POS taggers?
3.  Can you elaborate on the process of selecting and tuning the initial head rules? What criteria were used, and how sensitive is the performance to variations in these rules?
4. What is the computational cost of the PageRank calculation for very long sentences (e.g., >100 tokens), and are there any plans to optimize the algorithm for improved scalability?



**Additional Comments:**

- It would be useful to briefly mention any limitations of the UD framework itself that might affect the performance of the parser.



Overall, this is a well-executed and highly impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised dependency parsing. The novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the clear presentation make it a strong candidate for acceptance. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.
