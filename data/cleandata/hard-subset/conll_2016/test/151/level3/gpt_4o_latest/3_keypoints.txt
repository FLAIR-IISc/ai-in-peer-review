- UDP is an unsupervised dependency parser using POS tags, a small set of head rules, and a modified PageRank, estimating adposition directionality from test data; linguistically sound but only incremental over Søgaard (2012).
- Primary contribution: novel use of personalized PageRank for main predicate identification, though the personalization choice (e.g., weighting the first verb) feels arbitrary and underexplored.
- Secondary contribution: practical, linguistically grounded use of UD constraints (e.g., treating function words as leaves).
- Tertiary contribution: on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality from test data, useful for low-resource languages.
- Strength: robust to domain change, outperforming a supervised delexicalized parser in some setups (notably Hindi).
- Strength: simple, efficient, lightweight, interpretable, and requires no training data.
- Strength: extensive evaluation across 32 languages and three POS tagging scenarios (gold, predicted, naive).
- Weakness: limited novelty, largely resembling Søgaard (2012) with marginal gains from PageRank personalization.
- Weakness: presentation issues—dense tables hinder interpretability; visualizations would help.
- Weakness: lacks detailed analysis of correlation between POS tagging accuracy and parsing performance degradation.
- Weakness: arbitrary design choices (e.g., PageRank weighting) lack theoretical justification or empirical tuning.
- Weakness: minor typographical error noted ("The the main idea" → "The main idea").
- Question: How was the PageRank personalization factor chosen and might alternative schemes improve results?
- Question: Have you considered extending the method to multiword expressions or coordination, and what challenges do you expect?
- Question: Can you provide a more detailed analysis of POS tagging accuracy versus parsing degradation?
