**Paper Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm called UDP (Unsupervised Dependency Parser) that requires no training data. The algorithm uses POS-based head rules to define permitted dependencies and ranks words using a biased PageRank over a graph defined by those permitted dependencies. Each word is linked to its closest allowable head that preserves tree structure and respects directionality. The authors evaluate their system across 32 languages and compare it to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. The authors propose a novel unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm that requires no training data, making it a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. The algorithm's use of POS-based head rules and biased PageRank is a unique approach to dependency parsing, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness across multiple languages.
3. The evaluation of the system across 32 languages and comparison to a state-of-the-art supervised system provide a comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's strengths and weaknesses.

**Strengths of the Submission**

1. The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to unsupervised dependency parsing, which is a challenging task in natural language processing.
2. The evaluation of the system across multiple languages and comparison to a state-of-the-art supervised system provide a comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's strengths and weaknesses.
3. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the system's performance, including error analysis and discussion of the results.

**Weaknesses of the Submission**

1. The algorithm's performance is not significantly better than the state-of-the-art supervised system, which may limit its practical applications.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the head rules and their impact on the system's performance, which may be an important aspect of the algorithm.
3. The evaluation of the system is limited to unlabeled attachment score, which may not provide a complete understanding of the algorithm's performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the head rules and their impact on the system's performance?
2. How do you plan to extend the algorithm to handle more complex linguistic phenomena, such as multiword expressions and coordination?
3. Can you provide a comparison of the system's performance to other unsupervised dependency parsing algorithms?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the algorithm and its evaluation.
2. The authors provide a comprehensive list of references, which is helpful for understanding the context of the work.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the system's performance, including error analysis and discussion of the results.
