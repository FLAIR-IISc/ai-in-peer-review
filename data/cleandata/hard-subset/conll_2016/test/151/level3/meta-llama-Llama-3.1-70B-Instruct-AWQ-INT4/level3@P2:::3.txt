**Summary**

This paper proposes UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that uses a modified PageRank algorithm and a small set of head-dependent rules. The parser requires no training data and estimates adposition direction directly from test data. The authors evaluate UDP on 32 languages and compare its performance to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser.

**Contributions**

1. The authors propose a novel approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that leverages the constraints of the UD formalism.
2. They develop a modified PageRank algorithm that incorporates personalization to boost the main predicate.
3. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large set of languages, including some low-resource languages.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that is competitive with supervised methods.
2. The use of personalized PageRank is a useful innovation that improves the parser's performance.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their system on a large set of languages, including some low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. The idea of using PageRank for unsupervised dependency parsing is not new and closely mirrors the work of Søgaard (2012).
2. The personalization weight of 5 appears arbitrary and unjustified, and the authors do not provide a clear explanation for this choice.
3. The evaluation could benefit from visualizations, such as charts, to make the results easier to interpret.
4. The authors do not report POS tagging scores in prediction mode, which would be useful to check whether parsing degradation correlates with tagging degradation.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more justification for the choice of personalization weight of 5?
2. How do you plan to extend the parser to handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names?
3. Can you provide more details on the implementation of the PageRank algorithm and how it is modified to incorporate personalization?
4. How do you plan to address the issue of parsing degradation in low-resource languages?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its advantages. However, the novelty of the approach is limited, and the authors could have provided more justification for the choice of personalization weight. The evaluation is thorough, but could benefit from visualizations to make the results easier to interpret.
