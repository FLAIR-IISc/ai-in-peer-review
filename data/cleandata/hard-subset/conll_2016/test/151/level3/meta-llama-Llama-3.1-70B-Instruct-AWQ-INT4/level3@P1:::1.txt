This paper proposes a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm, UDP, which is evaluated across 32 languages. The core algorithm uses POS-based head rules to define permitted dependencies, and words are ranked by a biased PageRank over a graph defined by those permitted dependencies. Each word is linked to its closest allowable head that preserves tree structure and respects directionality.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel, unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm that requires no training data and is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system.
2. A linguistically sound approach to cross-lingual UD parsing that is robust to domain change across languages.
3. A thorough evaluation of the algorithm across 32 languages, including an analysis of its performance on different POS setups and across domains.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The algorithm is clearly presented and easy to understand, making it a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. The evaluation is thorough and comprehensive, covering a wide range of languages and POS setups.
3. The results are impressive, showing that the algorithm is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system and robust to domain change.

However, there are some weaknesses to the paper:

1. The algorithm is only slightly different from Søgaard (2012), which may raise concerns about its novelty and originality.
2. The head directionality was tuned on the test set rather than the training/dev set, which may not be ideal.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the head rules in relation to the corpus, such as the frequency of disconnected graphs and component sizes.

To address these concerns, the authors could consider the following:

1. Provide a more detailed comparison of their algorithm with Søgaard (2012), highlighting the key differences and advantages of their approach.
2. Consider tuning the head directionality on the training/dev set instead of the test set to ensure that the results are more generalizable.
3. Provide a more detailed analysis of the head rules in relation to the corpus, including the frequency of disconnected graphs and component sizes.

Questions to authors:

1. How do you handle PageRank tie-breaking, and do ties affect performance?
2. Can you report oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to show upper-bound impacts?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the head rules in relation to the corpus, including the frequency of disconnected graphs and component sizes?
