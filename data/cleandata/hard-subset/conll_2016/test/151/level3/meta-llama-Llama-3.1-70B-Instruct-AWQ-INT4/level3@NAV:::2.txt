**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes an unsupervised, training-free algorithm for Universal Dependencies parsing, called UDP. The algorithm uses PageRank on words combined with a small set of hard-coded rules to perform tagging. The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the algorithm on 32 languages and demonstrates its competitiveness with a delexicalized transfer system.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Unsupervised Dependency Parsing**: The paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised dependency parsing that does not require any training data.
2. **PageRank-based Tagging**: The algorithm uses PageRank to estimate the relevance of content words in a sentence, which is a key contribution to the field of unsupervised parsing.
3. **Robustness to Domain Change**: The paper demonstrates that UDP is more robust to domain changes across languages compared to a delexicalized transfer system.

**Strengths**

1. **Competitive Performance**: UDP achieves competitive performance on all but two UD languages, and even beats a multi-source delexicalized parser on Hindi.
2. **Robustness to Domain Change**: The algorithm is less affected by deteriorating POS tags than the delexicalized transfer system and is more resilient to domain changes.
3. **Simple and Linguistically Sound**: The approach is based on a small set of hard-coded rules that are linguistically sound and do not require any training data.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Handling of Multiword Expressions**: The algorithm does not handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names, which are important aspects of natural language processing.
2. **Over-Simplification of POS Tagging**: The algorithm uses a simple POS tagging approach that may not be effective for all languages or domains.
3. **Limited Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the algorithm on a limited set of languages and domains, which may not be representative of the entire range of languages and domains.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How can the algorithm be extended to handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names?
2. Can the algorithm be improved by using more sophisticated POS tagging approaches?
3. How can the algorithm be evaluated on a larger set of languages and domains to demonstrate its robustness and effectiveness?
