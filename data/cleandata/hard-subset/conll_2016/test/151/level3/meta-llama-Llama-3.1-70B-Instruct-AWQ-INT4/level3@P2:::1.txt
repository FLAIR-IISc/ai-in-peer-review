**Summary**

This paper proposes a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm, UDP, which uses a combination of POS-based head rules and a biased PageRank algorithm to define permitted dependencies. The algorithm is evaluated across 32 languages and shows competitive performance with a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the authors' argument.

**Contributions**

1. **Unsupervised dependency parsing algorithm**: The authors propose a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm that uses POS-based head rules and a biased PageRank algorithm to define permitted dependencies. This algorithm is novel and shows competitive performance with state-of-the-art supervised parsers.
2. **Evaluation across multiple languages**: The authors evaluate their algorithm across 32 languages, demonstrating its robustness and generalizability.
3. **Analysis of error propagation**: The authors analyze the error propagation from POS tagging to dependency parsing and show that their algorithm is less affected by deteriorating POS tags than a supervised parser.

**Strengths**

1. **Competitive performance**: The authors demonstrate that their algorithm shows competitive performance with a state-of-the-art supervised parser, making it a viable alternative for low-resource languages.
2. **Robustness to domain shift**: The authors show that their algorithm is more resilient to domain changes than a supervised parser, which is an important property for real-world applications.
3. **Clear presentation**: The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the authors' argument.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited novelty**: The algorithm is only slightly different from Søgaard (2012), which may limit its impact.
2. **Tuning on test set**: The authors tuned the head directionality on the test set rather than the training/dev set, which may not be ideal.
3. **Lack of analysis**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the head rules in relation to the corpus, such as the frequency of disconnected graphs and component sizes.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you handle PageRank tie-breaking, and does it affect performance?
2. Can you provide oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to show upper-bound impacts?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the head rules in relation to the corpus, such as the frequency of disconnected graphs and component sizes?
