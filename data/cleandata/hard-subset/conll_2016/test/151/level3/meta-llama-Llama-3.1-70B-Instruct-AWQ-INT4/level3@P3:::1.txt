This paper proposes a new deterministic dependency parsing algorithm, UDP, which is evaluated across 32 languages. The core algorithm uses POS-based head rules to define permitted dependencies, and words are ranked by a biased PageRank over a graph defined by those permitted dependencies. Each word is linked to its closest allowable head that preserves tree structure and respects directionality. The parser requires no training data and estimates adposition direction directly on test data.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, (2) incorporating the UD restriction of function words being leaves, (3) using personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, and (4) making it completely free of language-specific parameters by estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The use of PageRank to rank words is an interesting approach, and the evaluation across multiple languages is a significant contribution.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. One of the main concerns is that the head directionality was tuned on the test set rather than the training/dev set, which may have introduced some bias into the results. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the head rules in relation to the corpus, such as the frequency of disconnected graphs and component sizes.

The authors could also provide more information on how PageRank tie-breaking is handled and whether ties affect performance. Furthermore, reporting oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) would be useful in showing the upper-bound impacts of each component.

Minor editorial suggestions include fixing the phrasing in 4.1 ("we obtain [the] rank"), reordering Table 5 columns to match Table 7 (prefer Table 7 order), and fixing the wording in 6.1 ("isolate the [contribution] of both").

Strengths:

1. The paper proposes a novel and interesting approach to dependency parsing using personalized PageRank.
2. The evaluation across 32 languages is a significant contribution, demonstrating the robustness of the approach.
3. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

Weaknesses:

1. The head directionality was tuned on the test set rather than the training/dev set, which may have introduced some bias into the results.
2. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the head rules in relation to the corpus.
3. The authors do not provide enough information on how PageRank tie-breaking is handled and whether ties affect performance.

Questions to Authors:

1. How do you handle PageRank tie-breaking, and does it affect performance?
2. Can you provide more information on the frequency of disconnected graphs and component sizes in the corpus?
3. How do you plan to extend the parser to handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names?
4. Can you report oracle results for each constraint (head rules, directionality, distance) to show the upper-bound impacts of each component?

Additional Comments:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other unsupervised dependency parsing approaches.
* The authors may want to consider evaluating the parser on other metrics, such as labeled attachment score or dependency accuracy.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the implications of the results and how they relate to the broader field of natural language processing.
