**Summary of the paper and contributions:**
This paper presents UDP, a deterministic, unsupervised dependency parser designed for Universal Dependencies (UD). The algorithm uses a small set of universal, POS-based head rules to construct a dependency graph for a given sentence. A personalized PageRank algorithm then ranks the sentence's content words, biasing the main verb to be the predicate. Finally, a two-step decoding process attaches content words according to their rank and then attaches function words, ensuring the latter remain leaves. Heads are selected based on linear distance, subject to the head rules and directionality constraints.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  An extensive and valuable empirical evaluation of a simple, unsupervised parsing method across 32 languages. The analysis of the parser's robustness to both POS tag quality and domain shift is particularly strong.
2.  A clear adaptation of a PageRank-based parsing approach (Søgaard, 2012b) to the UD framework, incorporating personalized ranking to improve predicate identification and a decoding strategy that respects UD formalism.

**Strengths:**
1.  The paper’s primary strength is its comprehensive evaluation. Testing the method on 32 UD treebanks with gold, predicted, and naive POS tags, supplemented by a detailed cross-domain analysis, provides a solid and convincing benchmark for this class of parser.
2.  The method is simple, intuitive, and exceptionally well-described. The clarity of the writing and the inclusion of a worked example make the approach easy to understand and reproduce.
3.  The results demonstrating the system's robustness are significant. The finding that this simple rule-based system is less sensitive to domain shift and POS tagging errors than a competitive delexicalized transfer system is an important practical result.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The novelty of the core method is limited, as it is a direct extension of Søgaard (2012b). The main differences—personalized PageRank and a UD-specific two-step decoding—are effective but represent incremental improvements rather than a fundamentally new approach.
2.  A key methodological choice is concerning: the head directionality for adpositions is estimated on the test set. While the authors state this parameter converges quickly, this is technically tuning on test data and violates standard evaluation practice.
3.  The analysis lacks depth regarding the model's core components. For instance, an analysis of the graphs generated by the head rules (e.g., frequency of disconnected components, component sizes) is absent but would provide valuable insight into the behavior and limitations of the PageRank step.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Could you clarify how ties are handled in the PageRank ranking and subsequent sorting? Do ties occur frequently, and does the tie-breaking strategy impact performance?
2.  To better isolate error sources, have you considered reporting oracle results for each of the model's constraints (head rules, directionality, closest-head attachment)? This would help quantify the performance ceiling imposed by each component.
