### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised and deterministic dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data. It operates by first building a graph of potential dependencies for a sentence based on a small, fixed set of POS-based head-rule constraints. Personalized PageRank is then used to rank the content words, with a bias given to the likely main predicate (the first verb). Finally, a two-step decoding algorithm attaches heads: it first processes content words in order of their PageRank score, and then attaches all function words. This ensures function words are always leaves, as required by UD. Attachments are made to the closest available head that is permitted by the rules and respects head-directionality constraints. The system is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks, showing competitive performance against a multi-source delexicalized transfer system, and superior robustness to domain shifts.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation and robustness analysis of a simple unsupervised baseline for UD parsing.** The evaluation across 32 languages, three POS-tagging scenarios, and multiple domains is the paper's key strength. The finding that this simple parser is more robust to domain shift than a supervised delexicalized system is significant and provides a valuable data point for the community.
2.  **An effective adaptation of a rule-and-rank parsing strategy to the UD formalism.** While the core ideas build on previous work, the specific combination of personalized PageRank to identify the root, a two-step decoding process to enforce UD's function-word-as-leaf constraint, and on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction results in a strong, simple, and entirely training-free baseline system.

### Strengths

1.  **Extensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The parser is evaluated on a very broad set of 32 languages, providing a wide-ranging benchmark for unsupervised UD parsing. The comparison is not just against a simple baseline but against a competitive multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD), which properly contextualizes the performance of the proposed system.
2.  **Excellent Robustness Analysis:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a major strength. By testing on different domains for several languages, the authors convincingly demonstrate that their rule-based system (UDP) exhibits lower variance and is more stable across domains than the data-driven MSD parser. This empirically validates the hypothesis that simpler, formalism-driven models can be less susceptible to overfitting and sampling bias. The analysis under both gold and predicted POS tags further strengthens these claims.
3.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed method is straightforward, deterministic, and very clearly explained. The paper is well-written, with helpful examples (Section 4) that make the algorithm easy to understand and reproduce. Its simplicity makes it an excellent candidate for a standard unsupervised baseline in future UD parsing research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The proposed approach is an incremental improvement over existing work, particularly Søgaard (2012b), which also used PageRank over a graph of head-rule dependencies. The primary novelties—personalizing PageRank and using a two-step decoding scheme—are adaptations to better fit the UD formalism. While effective, this makes the algorithmic contribution feel more like a skillful application of known techniques rather than a fundamentally new parsing method.
2.  **Use of Test Data for Parameter Estimation:** The system estimates adposition head directionality (pre- or post-positional) on-the-fly using the test data itself (Section 3.2). Although the authors state that this parameter converges very quickly, this is a methodological weakness that violates the standard separation of training, development, and test sets. Using any information from the test set to configure the model, however small, compromises the reported results. This should have been estimated from a development set or justified more thoroughly.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Ablation and Error Analysis:** The analysis could be strengthened by providing more insight into the behavior of the core components. For example, the head rules in Table 1 are critical, but their coverage is not analyzed. An ablation study showing the upper-bound performance if each constraint were an oracle (e.g., oracle head rules, oracle directionality, oracle distance metric) would be very informative for understanding the primary sources of error and the headroom for future improvements.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The PageRank algorithm can produce identical scores for multiple nodes, leading to ties in the content word ranking. How does your deterministic decoding algorithm handle such ties? Does the method for breaking ties impact performance?
2.  Your method for estimating adposition direction on the test set is concerning. Could you comment on the sensitivity of your parser to this parameter? For example, what is the performance drop on a prepositional language if it is incorrectly classified as postpositional, and vice versa?
3.  Could you provide some analysis of the graphs constructed from your head rules prior to running PageRank? Specifically, how often are the graphs for sentences in the test sets disconnected, and what is the typical distribution of connected component sizes? This would help clarify the importance of the PageRank teleport probability.

### Additional Comments

The paper is well-written, but there are a few minor editorial issues:
-   Section 4.1: "we obtain rank of each word" -> "we obtain **the** rank of each word".
-   Section 6.1: "isolate the constribution of both parts" -> "isolate the **contribution** of both parts".
-   For better readability, I suggest reordering the columns in Table 5 to match the more logical grouping used in Table 7 (e.g., BLG, UDPG, UDPP, MSDG, MSDP).
