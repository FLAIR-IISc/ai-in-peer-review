**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies. The method operates without any training data, relying solely on part-of-speech (POS) tags to construct a graph of potential dependencies. It then employs a modified PageRank algorithm—which downweights stop words and uses personalization to boost the main predicate—to determine the attachment order. The primary contribution of this work is the novel application of personalized PageRank to inject a strong bias for main predicate identification into a simple, graph-based parsing framework. A secondary contribution is the extensive empirical evaluation across 32 languages, demonstrating that this training-free approach is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system and is notably robust to domain shift.

**Strengths**

1.  The key strength of the proposed method is its elegant simplicity and its training-free nature. This makes it a valuable baseline and a practical tool for low-resource scenarios where only POS-tagged data may be available. Its demonstrated robustness across domains is a direct and significant benefit of this design.
2.  The idea of using personalized PageRank to incorporate linguistic priors is conceptually interesting. Boosting the main predicate is a principled way to guide the parser towards a more plausible global structure, and this technique could potentially be extended to encode other linguistic constraints.
3.  The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across a large set of languages (32) and under different conditions (gold vs. predicted POS). This broad testing helps situate the method's performance and provides useful data points for the community.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The most significant weakness is the limited novelty of the overall approach. The core method, which uses PageRank on a graph built from POS-based rules, closely mirrors the work of Søgaard (2012). The incremental contribution over this prior work is small, and the paper should do more to clearly differentiate its specific contributions.
2.  The main novel component, the personalization of PageRank, feels under-explored and its implementation is not well-justified. The choice of a personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate appears arbitrary and is presented without ablation or sensitivity analysis. The decision to apply this boost only to the main predicate, and not other content words, is also not fully motivated.
3.  The evaluation, while broad, could be strengthened. First, the presentation of results exclusively in large tables makes trends difficult to discern; visualizations such as bar charts would significantly improve interpretability. Second, for the predicted-POS setting, the authors should report the POS tagging accuracy. This is crucial for analyzing error propagation and understanding whether parsing degradation correlates with tagging degradation.
