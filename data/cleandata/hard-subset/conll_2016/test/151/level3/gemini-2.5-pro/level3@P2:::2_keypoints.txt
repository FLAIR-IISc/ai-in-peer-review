- Paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised, training-free UD parser using a small set of hard-coded UD rules, personalized PageRank to rank content words, hierarchical attachment of content words, then attachment of function words as leaves, and dynamic estimation of adposition directionality.
- Evaluated on 32 UD languages against a rule baseline and a supervised multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD), using gold and predicted POS tags, with thorough cross-domain analysis; UDP is competitive with MSD and far more robust to POS errors and domain shift.

Contributions:
- Proposes a simple, effective, entirely training-free UD parser combining universal rules with personalized PageRank, providing a strong baseline for unsupervised/zero-shot parsing.
- Demonstrates that a rule-based, formalism-aware approach yields much greater robustness to domain shift than a data-driven delexicalized transfer system.
- Introduces a novel use of personalized PageRank to bias word ranking toward the main predicate as a linguistic prior.

Strengths:
- Method is simple, transparent, reproducible, and achieves strong cross-lingual performance (only ~6.4% UAS behind a supervised baseline on average).
- Evaluation is extensive and rigorous across 32 typologically diverse languages, including analysis with predicted POS and detailed cross-domain experiments showing lower variance across domains.
- Paper is very clear and well-explained, with formal algorithm presentation and a step-by-step example.
- Methodological design is sound: two-step decoding leveraging UD function-word-as-leaf convention and on-the-fly adposition direction estimation are practical and effective.

Weaknesses:
- Heavy dependency on accurate POS tags makes the system not truly data-free and limits applicability in low-resource settings without taggers; integration with unsupervised POS induction is not addressed.
- Rule set is small and manually curated, making the parser brittle and unable to handle constructions (e.g., relative clauses) excluded by chosen rules; limited coverage and extension effort are concerns.
- Some parameters and rules (head rules, PageRank weight, function-word directionalities) were tuned on English dev data, which undermines the claim of being fully parameter-free and raises sensitivity questions.

Questions to authors:
- What performance trade-offs occurred when including the NOUN→VERB rule, and could lower priority or higher distance penalties be used instead of excluding it?
- Could on-the-fly directionality estimation be extended to other function-word classes (like determiners) to handle typologically diverse languages (e.g., Basque)?
- Have you analyzed whether UDP’s robustness to domain shift is stronger for particular language types (rigid word order) or particular types of domain shifts (formal→informal)?
