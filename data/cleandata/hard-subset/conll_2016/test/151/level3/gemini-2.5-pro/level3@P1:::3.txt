### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser designed for the Universal Dependencies (UD) framework. The method is entirely rule-based and requires no training data. It operates by first constructing a dependency graph based on a small, hand-crafted set of head-dependent rules derived from Part-of-Speech (POS) tags. The system then uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank the content words in a sentence, with a strong bias towards an estimated main predicate. A two-step decoding algorithm then constructs the final dependency tree, first attaching content words based on their PageRank score and proximity, and then attaching function words to ensure they remain leaves, as stipulated by UD guidelines. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks. Its performance is compared to a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD), and its robustness is assessed under both predicted POS tags and cross-domain settings.

### Contributions
1.  **Extensive Empirical Evaluation of a Training-Free UD Parser:** The primary contribution of this work is the comprehensive evaluation of a simple, rule-based unsupervised parser across a very wide set of 32 languages. By tailoring the rules and decoding process to UD principles, the authors show that such a system can achieve surprisingly competitive results, approaching the performance of a supervised delexicalized system without requiring any training data.

2.  **Analysis of Cross-Domain and POS Error Robustness:** A second significant contribution is the detailed analysis of the parser's stability. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that the proposed unsupervised system (UDP) is more robust to domain shifts and less affected by errors from predicted POS taggers than its data-driven, delexicalized counterpart (MSD). This provides strong empirical evidence for the stability of grammar-driven approaches in real-world scenarios where domain mismatches and noisy inputs are common.

### Strengths
1.  **Simplicity and Effectiveness:** The proposed UDP system is elegant in its simplicity. It relies on a very small set of universal rules (Table 1), a well-understood algorithm (PageRank), and a straightforward decoding process. Despite this simplicity, it achieves a strong average UAS of 57.5% across 32 languages, which is remarkably close to the 63.9% achieved by the supervised multi-source delexicalized parser. This demonstrates the power of leveraging the structural constraints inherent in the UD formalism.

2.  **Thorough Multilingual and Cross-Domain Evaluation:** The paper's evaluation is a key strength. Testing on 32 languages provides a robust and convincing assessment of the method's cross-lingual applicability. Furthermore, the cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is particularly insightful. The finding that UDP exhibits a lower standard deviation in performance across different domains than the MSD baseline (average Std. Dev. of 2.5 vs. 4.5 for gold POS) is a strong argument for its practical utility and generalizability.

3.  **Genuinely Unsupervised and Language-Agnostic:** The proposed parser is truly training-free, distinguishing it from many "unsupervised" methods that still require large amounts of unlabeled text for training models like DMV. The only language-specific information it requires is POS tags, and it even includes a "naive" setup (UDPN) that relies only on word frequency. This makes the approach highly applicable to genuinely low-resource settings where annotated or even large raw corpora are unavailable. The on-the-fly estimation of adposition directionality further enhances its language-agnostic character.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core methodology builds very heavily on prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012b), which also proposed using PageRank over a graph constructed from POS-based rules for unsupervised parsing. The key novelties claimed in this paper—adapting rules for UD, using personalized PageRank, and two-step decoding—feel more like incremental adaptations than a significant conceptual advance. The two-step decoding to ensure function words are leaves is a direct and logical application of UD principles rather than a novel parsing strategy, and personalization is a standard feature of the PageRank algorithm. Consequently, the paper's primary contribution lies more in its application and extensive evaluation within the UD context than in a new parsing technique.

2.  **Arbitrary Parameterization and Under-exploration of Personalization:** The use of personalized PageRank to boost the score of the main predicate is a well-motivated and effective idea. However, its implementation is not sufficiently justified or explored. The personalization weight is set to a seemingly arbitrary value of 5, without any sensitivity analysis or ablation study to show how performance is affected by this choice. Furthermore, personalization is only applied to a single, heuristically-identified predicate. It is unclear why other strategies, such as assigning a smaller boost to all verbs or other content words, were not considered. This makes the approach feel somewhat brittle and its parameters under-analyzed.

3.  **Presentation of Results and Missing Analysis:** The results are presented almost exclusively in large tables, which makes it very difficult to discern trends and make comparisons across the 32 languages. Visualizations, such as a scatter plot comparing UDPG and MSDG scores, or bar charts showing the performance drop from gold to predicted POS tags per language, would have made the results much more interpretable and the paper's claims easier to assess. Additionally, while the authors calculate an average error propagation rate from POS tagging, they fail to report the per-language POS accuracies. This is a critical omission, as it prevents reviewers from analyzing whether the parser's degradation on specific languages is correlated with the difficulty of the POS tagging task for those languages.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide a justification for choosing the PageRank personalization weight of 5? Have you performed any sensitivity analysis on this parameter, and if so, how does performance vary with different weights?
2.  The personalization strategy is currently focused on a single main predicate. Have you considered or experimented with alternative personalization schemes, such as assigning a smaller, uniform boost to all verbs or using a more distributed weighting based on different POS categories?
3.  To better understand the impact of POS tagging errors, could you provide the per-language POS tagging accuracies for the UDPP/MSDP experiments? This would allow for a more detailed analysis of the relationship between POS tagger performance and parser performance degradation.
