### Summary

This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data and operates solely on part-of-speech (POS) tags. It constructs a graph based on a small set of universal, POS-based dependency rules derived from UD principles. The core of the method uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify the most salient content words in a sentence, with a heuristic to boost the rank of the likely main predicate. A subsequent two-step decoding algorithm first builds a skeleton of content words and then attaches function words, ensuring they remain leaves as per UD guidelines. The system is evaluated extensively on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks, showing competitive performance against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. A key finding is that the proposed system is significantly more robust to domain shifts than its data-driven counterpart.

### Contributions

1.  **Extensive Empirical Evaluation and a Strong Unsupervised Baseline:** The primary contribution of this work is a thorough and large-scale evaluation of a simple, training-free parsing method across 32 languages. By testing on gold POS, predicted POS, and in cross-domain settings, the paper establishes a strong, reproducible, and language-agnostic baseline for unsupervised UD parsing. This is valuable for the community as it provides a clear point of comparison for more complex cross-lingual and unsupervised models.
2.  **Demonstration of Robustness to Domain Shift:** The paper provides compelling evidence that its simple, rule-based approach is significantly more robust to domain variation than a competitive data-driven transfer system. The analysis in Section 6.3, quantified by lower standard deviation in performance across various domains, is a significant finding that highlights a key advantage of linguistically-motivated, parameter-light models in real-world scenarios where test data often mismatches training data.
3.  **Adaptation of a PageRank-based method for Universal Dependencies:** The work successfully adapts an existing PageRank-based parsing idea to the specific constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism. While the novelty of the core algorithm is incremental, the specific modifications—notably the two-step decoding to enforce UD's leaf-node constraint for function words and the use of personalization to improve root identification—are effective and well-suited to the UD framework.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and Wide-Ranging Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is a significant strength. Testing the parser on 32 languages provides a comprehensive view of its capabilities. Furthermore, the experiments go beyond the ideal "gold POS" setting to include a realistic "predicted POS" scenario and a lower-bound "naive POS" scenario. This multi-faceted evaluation provides a clear understanding of the system's performance and its sensitivity to the quality of input POS tags.
2.  **Impressive Cross-Domain Stability:** The analysis of the parser's performance across different domains is a highlight of the paper. The results in Table 6 clearly show that UDP exhibits much lower variance (i.e., higher stability) than the multi-source delexicalized (MSD) baseline when faced with domain shifts. This empirically validates the hypothesis that systems with fewer, more general parameters are less prone to overfitting and more resilient to data variation, which is an important consideration for practical NLP applications.
3.  **Simplicity and Competitiveness:** The UDP parser is elegant, simple, and requires no training data, making it extremely easy to apply to new languages. Despite this simplicity, it achieves impressive results, trailing a strong supervised transfer baseline by only 6.4% UAS on average with gold POS tags, and even outperforming it on Hindi. This demonstrates that a carefully designed, rule-based system can be surprisingly effective by exploiting the inherent structural constraints of a framework like Universal Dependencies.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** The proposed approach is very closely related to prior work, particularly Søgaard (2012b), which also used PageRank over a graph of POS-based rules for unsupervised parsing. The paper's main technical contributions—personalizing PageRank and a two-step decoding process—are incremental adaptations to fit the UD formalism rather than fundamentally new techniques. A direct comparison against a reimplementation of the Søgaard (2012b) system on the same UD data would have been valuable to precisely quantify the gains from the specific modifications introduced here.
2.  **Arbitrary Parameter Choices and Under-Developed Personalization:** The use of personalized PageRank is a key element, yet it feels under-explored. The choice of boosting only the heuristically-identified main predicate with a fixed weight of 5 seems arbitrary. The paper offers no justification for this specific value, nor does it present a sensitivity analysis to show how performance varies with different weights. This lack of analysis makes a core component of the method appear ad-hoc.
3.  **Clarity of Reporting and Analysis:** The extensive results are presented almost exclusively in large tables, which makes it difficult for the reader to identify trends and draw conclusions. Visualizations, such as bar charts comparing performance across languages or line graphs showing the performance drop from gold to predicted POS tags, would greatly improve the paper's readability and impact. Furthermore, while the paper reports error propagation from POS tagging to parsing, it omits the per-language POS tagging accuracies. Including these scores would allow for a more detailed analysis of the correlation between tagging difficulty and parsing degradation across different languages.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide justification for selecting a personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate? Have you conducted a sensitivity analysis on this hyperparameter, and if so, how does performance change with different values?
2.  Given the strong similarity to Søgaard (2012b), could you provide a more direct empirical comparison to that work? A comparison on the UD datasets would help to clearly isolate and measure the performance improvements attributable to your specific contributions (i.e., PageRank personalization and the two-step decoding scheme).
3.  For the experiments using predicted POS tags, could you please report the per-language POS tagging accuracies achieved by the TnT tagger? This information would enable a more nuanced understanding of how your parser's performance relates to the difficulty of POS tagging in each language, especially in comparison to the MSD baseline.
