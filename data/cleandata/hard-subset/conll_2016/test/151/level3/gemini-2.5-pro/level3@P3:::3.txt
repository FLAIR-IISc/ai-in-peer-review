**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) treebanks. The core idea is to generate dependency trees using only part-of-speech (POS) tags, a small set of linguistically-motivated head-attachment rules, and a modified PageRank algorithm. The method first builds a graph based on potential head-dependent relationships derived from POS tags. It then uses a personalized PageRank to rank the "content" words, giving a significant boost to a heuristically-identified main predicate (the first verb or content word). Finally, a two-step decoding algorithm attaches content words based on their rank and then attaches function words, ensuring the latter are always leaves, in adherence with UD principles. The system requires no training data and is evaluated on 32 languages, where it performs competitively with a multi-source delexicalized transfer system. The authors also show that their system is more robust to domain changes than the data-driven baseline.

**Contributions of this work**

1.  **An extensive empirical evaluation of a simple, unsupervised parser across 32 UD languages.** The main contribution is not a novel parsing paradigm but rather the demonstration that a simple, rule-based method building on prior work can serve as a strong and competitive baseline for UD parsing, especially in settings without training data.
2.  **An analysis of cross-domain robustness.** The paper provides a valuable analysis showing that its simple, rule-based system is more stable across different text domains than a standard data-driven delexicalized transfer parser. This confirms findings from earlier work in a broad, multilingual UD context.

**Strengths**

1.  **Simplicity and Effectiveness as a Baseline:** The proposed method is conceptually simple, requires no training, and is easy to implement. Despite its simplicity, it achieves impressive results, performing within 6.4 UAS points of a strong, multi-source delexicalized transfer system on average (UDPG vs. MSDG). This makes it an excellent and highly accessible baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.
2.  **Comprehensive Multilingual Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, covering 32 languages from the Universal Dependencies project. By testing on gold POS tags, predicted POS tags, and a naive content/function tagset, the authors provide a clear picture of the system's performance under different conditions and its sensitivity to POS tagging quality.
3.  **Valuable Cross-Domain Analysis:** The investigation into cross-domain performance is a key strength. The results in Tables 6 and 7 convincingly show that the rule-based UDP parser has lower variance across domains than the data-driven MSD parser. This highlights a practical advantage of the approach: its stability and predictability when applied to new, unseen domains.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty Over Existing Work:** The core method is heavily based on Søgaard (2012b), which also used PageRank over a POS-based graph for unsupervised dependency parsing. The authors identify their contributions as the use of PageRank personalization and a two-step decoding process. However, these feel like incremental improvements rather than significant conceptual advances. The two-step decoding is a straightforward way to enforce UD's leaf-node constraint on function words, and the personalization is based on a simple, singular heuristic. The overall contribution over the prior art is therefore modest.
2.  **Under-justified Methodological Choices:** The personalization strategy, a claimed contribution, is not fully explored. The system boosts only the heuristically-chosen main predicate, and the choice of a personalization weight of 5 seems arbitrary. The paper provides no justification, ablation study, or sensitivity analysis for this specific value, which weakens the scientific rigor of the method. It is unclear how this value was chosen or how performance would change with different weights.
3.  **Incomplete Error Analysis and Presentation:** The analysis of performance degradation with predicted POS tags would be much stronger if per-language POS tagging scores were reported. The paper provides only an average accuracy (94.1%), which masks language-specific variations. This makes it impossible to verify whether the languages with the largest UAS drop from gold to predicted tags are indeed the ones with the worst taggers. Furthermore, the presentation of results in large tables (e.g., Table 5) makes comparisons difficult. Visualizations like bar charts would significantly improve the clarity and interpretability of the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The personalization weight of 5 for the main predicate is a key parameter. Could you clarify how this value was chosen? Was it tuned on the English development set? How sensitive is the system's performance to this hyperparameter?
2.  Your personalization strategy boosts a single, heuristically-identified main predicate. Did you experiment with alternative strategies, such as boosting all verbs, or using a softer weighting scheme based on different content-word POS tags?
3.  To better understand the relationship between tagging and parsing errors, could you provide the per-language POS tagging accuracies for the UDPP setting? This would allow for a more direct analysis of how POS quality impacts your parser's performance across different languages.
