### **Summary of the paper**

This paper presents UDP, a deterministic, unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data and is evaluated across 32 languages. The core of the algorithm involves three main steps. First, a directed graph is constructed over the words in a sentence using a small, fixed set of linguistically-motivated head-dependent rules based on universal POS tags (e.g., adverbs depend on verbs, adjectives depend on nouns). Second, personalized PageRank is run on this graph to produce a ranked list of content words, with a bias towards verbs to identify the likely predicate. Finally, a two-step decoding algorithm attaches words to their heads. In the first step, content words are processed in their PageRank order, with each attached to its closest available head that is permitted by the rules and directionality constraints. In the second step, function words are attached, ensuring they remain leaves in the final tree, a key constraint in the UD formalism. The authors show that their system is competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer system and is notably more robust to domain shift.

### **Contributions**

1.  **An Extensive Cross-Lingual Evaluation of a Simple Unsupervised Parser:** The primary contribution is the comprehensive evaluation of a simple, rule-based parsing method on 32 languages from the Universal Dependencies project. The scale of this evaluation, including the analysis of robustness to domain shift and predicted POS tags, provides a valuable benchmark and demonstrates the surprising effectiveness of a few general linguistic principles across a wide typological range.
2.  **A Clear and Reproducible Baseline for UD Parsing:** The paper presents a straightforward and easily reproducible algorithm that serves as a strong baseline for unsupervised and low-resource dependency parsing. The combination of simple POS-based rules, graph centrality via PageRank, and a decoder that enforces UD-specific constraints (e.g., function words as leaves) is an effective and transparent approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, and the proposed method is simple to understand and implement. The head rules are explicit, the algorithm is described precisely, and the overall architecture is transparent. This makes the work highly valuable for reproducibility and as a foundation for future research.
2.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across 32 languages is a significant strength, providing strong evidence for the general applicability of the underlying linguistic assumptions. Comparing the system on gold POS, predicted POS, and a naive POS setting gives a clear picture of its behavior under realistic conditions.
3.  **Important Robustness Analysis:** The cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 is a highlight of the paper. The finding that this simple, parameter-light system exhibits lower variance and greater stability across different domains compared to a data-driven delexicalized parser is an important result. It provides strong empirical support for the idea that parsers based on general linguistic principles can be more robust than those sensitive to the statistical quirks of a specific training domain.
4.  **Strong Unsupervised Performance:** For a system that uses no training data, the results are impressively strong. UDP is competitive with a multi-source delexicalized transfer system, which represents a respectable supervised baseline. This performance establishes UDP as a non-trivial baseline for future work in this area.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty over Prior Work:** The core methodology bears a strong resemblance to Søgaard (2012b), which also used head rules to build a graph, applied PageRank for word ranking, and used proximity for attachment. The key novelties cited in this paper—personalized PageRank and a two-step decoding process tailored to UD constraints—feel more like effective, incremental adaptations than a fundamentally new parsing paradigm. The paper would benefit from a more direct and detailed comparison to this prior work to better situate its contribution.
2.  **Questionable Evaluation Practice:** Head directionality for adpositions is determined "directly on test data" (Section 3.2). Although the authors state that this parameter converges quickly on just 10-15 sentences, this is still a form of tuning on the test set and violates standard evaluation protocols. A more methodologically sound approach would be to determine this on the development sets or, if none are available, by holding out the first *n* sentences of the test set for this purpose and excluding them from the final evaluation calculation.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The head rules in Table 1 are fundamental to the parser's success, yet there is no analysis of their properties or interaction with the data. For instance, an analysis of the graphs generated by these rules—such as the frequency of disconnected components and their sizes across different languages—would provide crucial insight into the algorithm's behavior. Without this, it is difficult to understand the conditions under which the method is likely to succeed or fail.
4.  **Absence of Oracle Experiments:** The paper lacks an oracle analysis that could quantify the performance ceiling of its components. For example, what would the UAS be if the head rules and directionality constraints were kept, but an oracle selected the best possible attachment for each word (i.e., oracle ranking and distance)? Such experiments would help isolate the primary sources of error (e.g., faulty rules, suboptimal ranking, or the proximity heuristic) and better frame the potential of this approach.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The PageRank algorithm can produce identical scores for multiple nodes, resulting in ties. How are such ties handled when creating the sorted list of content words `C` for the decoding algorithm? Is the original sentence order used as a tie-breaker? Could this choice significantly affect performance?
2.  Regarding the estimation of adposition directionality on the test set: Could you explain the rationale for this choice over using the provided development sets? Would applying a directionality parameter tuned on the development set significantly alter the results?
3.  Could you provide some statistics about the graphs constructed by your head rules across the different languages? Specifically, how often are the graphs disconnected, and what is the typical size of the connected components? This would help in understanding the structures on which PageRank operates.
