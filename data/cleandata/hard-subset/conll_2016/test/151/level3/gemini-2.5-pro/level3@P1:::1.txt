### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces UDP, an unsupervised, deterministic dependency parser designed for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data and relies on a small, universal set of part-of-speech (POS) based head-selection rules. The core of the algorithm involves two main stages. First, a graph is constructed over the words in a sentence, where edges represent potential dependencies licensed by the head-selection rules. Personalized PageRank is then run on this graph to generate a prominence ranking of the content words, with a bias towards the predicted main predicate. Second, a two-step decoding algorithm attaches words to their heads. It first processes content words in order of their PageRank score, attaching each to the closest available head that satisfies the POS rules and directionality constraints. It then attaches all function words, ensuring they remain leaf nodes in the final tree, as stipulated by UD conventions. The system is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks and compared against a multi-source delexicalized transfer parser. The results show that this simple, unsupervised method is competitive with the supervised transfer baseline and is notably more robust to shifts in text domain.

### Contributions

1.  **A Comprehensive Empirical Analysis of a Simple Unsupervised Parser:** The primary contribution of this work is the extensive evaluation of a straightforward, rule-based parsing method across 32 languages. The analysis is thorough, covering performance under gold POS, predicted POS, and naive POS settings. The cross-domain evaluation is particularly valuable, as it provides strong quantitative evidence (Table 6) that a simple, linguistically-motivated system can be significantly more stable across different genres than a data-driven transfer system. This finding is an important contribution to the discussion on the trade-offs between rule-based and data-driven approaches in cross-lingual and cross-domain NLP.
2.  **A Simple, Effective, and Reproducible Parsing Algorithm:** The paper presents UDP, a complete and well-defined algorithm that integrates several simple heuristics (POS-based constraints, personalized PageRank, and nearest-attachment decoding) into a functional unsupervised parser. While the individual components are not entirely novel, their combination, specific adaptation to UD principles (e.g., forcing function words to be leaves), and clear presentation (including a worked example) make the system a useful and easily reproducible baseline for future research in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.

### Strengths

1.  **Extensive and Insightful Evaluation:** The paper's strongest aspect is its rigorous evaluation. Testing on 32 languages provides a broad perspective on the method's applicability. The comparison against a competitive multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) provides a solid benchmark for the unsupervised approach. Most impressively, the cross-domain analysis in Section 6.3 clearly demonstrates the system's robustness. The significantly lower standard deviation in performance across domains for UDP compared to MSD (e.g., an average standard deviation of 2.5 vs. 4.5 for UDPG vs. MSDG in Table 6) is compelling evidence that its reliance on general linguistic principles makes it less susceptible to the sampling biases inherent in data-driven models.
2.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity and is described with exceptional clarity. The algorithm is deterministic, requires no training, and relies on a small, understandable set of rules. The step-by-step example in Section 4 is very effective in illustrating the complete parsing process, from graph construction and PageRank computation to the final decoding steps. This clarity enhances the paper's value and makes the work easy to replicate.
3.  **Strong Unsupervised Performance:** For a system that uses no training data, UDP achieves remarkably strong performance. It is competitive with the MSD baseline in many languages and even outperforms it on Hindi. The small performance drop between gold-POS and predicted-POS settings (2.2% for UDP vs. 2.7% for MSD) further highlights its resilience and practical utility in scenarios where high-quality taggers may not be available.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The paper's central ideas draw heavily from previous work, particularly Søgaard (2012b), which also used PageRank over graphs derived from dependency rules. The authors frame their contributions as the use of PageRank personalization and a two-step decoding process. While these are valid refinements, they feel more like incremental improvements than a major conceptual leap. The paper would be strengthened by a more direct and detailed comparison with prior art to better delineate the significance of its modifications.
2.  **Methodological Concern Regarding Test Set Usage:** The system estimates adposition head directionality (e.g., pre- vs. post-positional) by calculating bigram frequencies directly on the test data (Section 3.2). While the authors claim this parameter converges very quickly, any form of estimation or tuning on the test set is a methodological flaw that compromises the integrity of the evaluation. For a truly "zero-resource" parser, this information should either be provided as a language-agnostic default or derived without access to the test set.
3.  **Insufficient Analysis of the Head Rules:** The head rules presented in Table 1 are the foundation of the entire parser, defining both the PageRank graph and the decoding constraints. However, the paper provides no analysis of these rules. For instance, how often do these rules fail to produce a connected graph for a sentence, thus requiring the use of a PageRank teleport probability? What is the coverage of these rules across the diverse set of languages tested? A deeper analysis of the properties of these rules and the graphs they generate would offer valuable insight into the parser's behavior and limitations.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The PageRank algorithm can produce identical scores for different nodes, leading to ties in the ranking. Your decoding algorithm appears to be deterministic. How are ties in the PageRank scores of content words handled when creating the sorted list `C`? Have you investigated if the frequency of such ties or the specific tie-breaking strategy has any impact on parsing performance?
2.  Regarding the estimation of adposition direction on the test set: could you quantify the impact of this choice? For example, what is the performance degradation if a default direction (e.g., always right-attaching for ADP) is used for all languages, or if you use an oracle direction derived from the training data? This would help clarify how sensitive the model is to this specific parameter.
3.  To better isolate the contributions of different components of your model, have you considered running an oracle analysis? For example, what UAS could be achieved if the PageRank component provided a perfect, oracle ranking of content words, but the rest of the distance-based decoding algorithm remained the same? This would help to distinguish errors originating from the ranking stage versus those from the attachment heuristics.

-   In Section 6.1, "isolate the constribution of both parts" should be "isolate the contribution of both parts".
