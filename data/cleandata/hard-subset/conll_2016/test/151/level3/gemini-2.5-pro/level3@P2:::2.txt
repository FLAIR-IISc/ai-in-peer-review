### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces UDP, a novel, completely unsupervised algorithm for parsing Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data and instead relies on a small set of hard-coded, linguistically-motivated dependency rules derived from UD principles. The core of the algorithm involves a two-step decoding process. First, it uses personalized PageRank to score and rank the content words of a sentence, biasing the ranking towards identifying the main predicate. These content words are then attached hierarchically based on their rank and proximity to valid heads. In the second step, function words are attached to the existing content-word structure, enforcing the UD constraint that they must be leaf nodes. The parser dynamically estimates adposition directionality from the test data itself.

The authors evaluate their parser across 32 languages from the UD v1.2 treebanks. They compare its performance against a simple rule-based baseline and a strong, supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer parser (MSD). The evaluation is conducted using both gold and predicted POS tags. A key part of the evaluation is a thorough cross-domain analysis for several languages, testing the parser's robustness. The results show that UDP is highly competitive with the supervised MSD system and is significantly more robust to both POS tagging errors and, most notably, shifts in text domain.

### Contributions

1.  **A simple, effective, and entirely training-free parser for Universal Dependencies.** The primary contribution is the design and extensive evaluation of UDP. By cleverly combining a small set of universal syntactic rules with a personalized PageRank ranking scheme, the authors have created a parser that achieves surprisingly strong performance across a diverse set of 32 languages without using any training data. This work provides an invaluable, strong baseline for future research in unsupervised and zero-shot parsing.
2.  **A compelling demonstration of the robustness of a rule-based, formalism-aware approach.** Through a rigorous cross-domain evaluation, the paper provides strong empirical evidence that a system built on the general principles of a linguistic formalism (UD) is significantly more stable and less sensitive to domain shift than a data-driven, delexicalized transfer system. This is a crucial finding for the practical application of NLP tools in real-world, heterogeneous data environments.
3.  **A novel application of personalized PageRank for predicate identification.** The paper introduces a clever technique of using PageRank personalization to inject a strong linguistic prior—the centrality of the main predicate—into the word ranking process. This is an elegant and effective method for guiding the unsupervised parsing algorithm.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and High Performance:** The proposed method is remarkably simple, transparent, and easy to reproduce. Despite this simplicity and its training-free nature, it achieves impressive results, trailing a competitive supervised delexicalized parser by only 6.4% UAS on average over 32 languages. Its ability to outperform the supervised baseline on Hindi demonstrates the power of its linguistically-informed heuristics.
2.  **Extensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper's evaluation is exceptionally thorough. Testing across 32 typologically diverse languages validates the cross-lingual applicability of the approach. Furthermore, the analysis of performance degradation with predicted POS tags and, especially, the detailed cross-domain evaluation in Section 6.3 are major strengths. The finding that UDP has a much lower variance in performance across domains than the MSD baseline (average std. dev of 2.5 vs 4.5 on gold POS) is a significant and impactful result.
3.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is extremely well-written and easy to follow. The method is clearly motivated, the algorithm is presented formally (Figure 1), and a concrete, step-by-step example (Section 4) makes the entire process perfectly clear. This high level of clarity greatly enhances the paper's contribution to the community.
4.  **Sound Methodological Design:** The core ideas of the parser are well-motivated. Exploiting the UD convention that function words are leaves via a two-step decoding process is a key insight. Similarly, estimating adposition directionality on the fly from test data is a practical solution that makes the system more parameter-free and adaptable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Dependency on POS Tags:** The entire system is built upon the availability of accurate POS tags. While the authors commendably evaluate the impact of predicted tags, the performance in the naive content-function POS scenario (UDPN) is very low (average 29.9 UAS). This highlights that the system is not truly "data-free" but rather relies heavily on a separate, pre-existing tool (a POS tagger). This limits its direct applicability in truly low-resource settings where such taggers are unavailable. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how this method could be integrated with unsupervised POS induction.
2.  **Brittleness and Limited Coverage of the Rule Set:** The parser's syntax is encoded in a small, manually curated set of rules (Table 1). The authors note that they explicitly excluded the `NOUN → VERB` rule to improve precision, which consequently makes the parser incapable of handling phenomena like relative clauses. This raises concerns about the brittleness of the system and the manual effort required to extend it to cover more complex syntactic constructions or languages that challenge the initial rule set. A more detailed error analysis on which dependency types the parser fails on most would be insightful.
3.  **Potential Over-tuning on Development Language:** The paper notes that the head rules, PageRank parameters (e.g., personalization weight of 5), and fixed function-word directionalities were determined using the English development set. While this is a standard practice, it slightly undermines the claim of a completely parameter-free system, as it has been tuned to perform well on one specific language's data. It is unclear how sensitive the system is to these specific parameter choices and whether performance could be improved by tuning them for other language families.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The exclusion of the `NOUN → VERB` rule seems like a significant structural limitation. Could you provide more detail on the performance trade-off you observed when this rule was included? Have you considered alternative strategies to handle such rules, for example, by assigning them a lower priority or a higher distance penalty during the head-selection step in decoding?
2.  You demonstrate that estimating adposition direction on-the-fly is highly effective. Given that the fixed right-attachment rule for determiners fails on Basque, have you considered applying a similar on-the-fly estimation heuristic for other function words to make the parser even more adaptable to typologically diverse languages?
3.  Your finding that UDP is more robust to domain shift is very interesting. Have you performed any analysis to see if this robustness benefit is more pronounced for certain types of languages (e.g., those with more rigid word order, where proximity is a stronger cue) or for certain types of domain shifts (e.g., formal to informal text)?
