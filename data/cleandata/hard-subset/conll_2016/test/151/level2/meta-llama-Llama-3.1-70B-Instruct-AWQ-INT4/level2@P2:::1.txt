The paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised dependency parsing for Universal Dependencies (UD) using a combination of personalized PageRank and a small set of specific dependency head rules. The authors claim that their method, UDP, is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system and offers a linguistically sound unsupervised alternative to cross-lingual UD parsing.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. Adapting dependency head rules to UD-compliant POS relations, incorporating the UD restriction of function words being leaves, and using personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification.
2. Estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data, making the parser completely free of language-specific parameters.
3. Achieving competitive performance on all but two UD languages, and even beating a multi-source delexicalized parser (MSD) on Hindi.

Strengths:

1. The paper presents a novel and linguistically sound approach to unsupervised dependency parsing, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their system on 32 languages, including a comparison with a supervised cross-lingual system (MSD).
3. The paper includes a detailed error analysis, which helps to understand the strengths and weaknesses of the system.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper relies heavily on the quality of the POS tags, which may not always be accurate, especially for low-resource languages.
2. The system's performance drops significantly when using predicted POS tags, which may limit its applicability in real-world scenarios.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed comparison with other unsupervised dependency parsing systems, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty and impact of their approach.

Questions to Authors:

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of POS tag quality, which seems to be a significant bottleneck for the system's performance?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the reasons behind the system's poor performance on Tamil and why it is an outlier in the evaluation results?
3. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to handle more complex linguistic phenomena, such as multi-word expressions, coordination, and proper names?

Additional Comments:

* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach and evaluation results.
* The use of personalized PageRank and dependency head rules is an innovative approach to unsupervised dependency parsing.
* The evaluation results are promising, and the system's performance on Hindi is particularly impressive.
* The authors' decision to make the parser and in-domain annotated test sets freely available is commendable and will likely facilitate further research in this area.
