**Summary**

This paper presents an unsupervised dependency parser, UDP, for Universal Dependencies (UD). The parser relies on a small set of specific dependency head rules and PageRank to estimate the relevance of content words. UDP requires no training data and is competitive with a delexicalized transfer system. The parser is also robust to domain change across languages.

**Contributions**

1. A novel unsupervised dependency parsing approach that leverages the constraints of the Universal Dependencies formalism.
2. A personalized PageRank method to improve main predicate identification.
3. A simple and linguistically sound rule-based approach to cross-lingual parsing.

**Strengths**

1. The parser achieves competitive performance on all but two UD languages, and even beats a multi-source delexicalized parser on Hindi.
2. UDP is less affected by deteriorating POS tags than the supervised parser, and is more resilient to domain changes.
3. The parser is unsupervised, requiring no training data, and can be easily extended to handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names.

**Weaknesses**

1. The parser relies on a small set of specific dependency head rules, which may not be sufficient for all languages or domains.
2. The use of PageRank may not be optimal for all languages, and the teleport probability value of 0.05 may not be the best choice.
3. The parser does not handle punctuation well, with a low UAS score for PUNCT.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend the parser to handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names?
2. Have you considered using other methods, such as edge reification, to directly score the potential dependency edges?
3. How do you plan to improve the parser's performance on punctuation and other problematic POS tags?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to unsupervised dependency parsing. The use of personalized PageRank and a small set of specific dependency head rules is a promising direction for future research. However, the parser's performance on punctuation and other problematic POS tags needs to be improved. Additionally, the authors should consider extending the parser to handle multiword expressions, coordination, and proper names.
