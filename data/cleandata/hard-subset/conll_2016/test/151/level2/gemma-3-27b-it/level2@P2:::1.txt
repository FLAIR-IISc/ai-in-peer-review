This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages PageRank and a set of dependency head rules. The core idea is to bypass training entirely, relying instead on inherent linguistic constraints within the UD formalism. The authors claim contributions in adapting head rules to UD, incorporating the function word constraint, using personalized PageRank for predicate identification, and eliminating language-specific parameters.

The most significant contribution of this work is the demonstration that a reasonably accurate dependency parser *can* be built without any training data, relying solely on the UD constraints and a well-designed algorithm. Achieving competitive performance against a delexicalized transfer system, while exhibiting greater robustness to domain shift, is a notable accomplishment. The thorough evaluation across 32 languages, and the inclusion of domain adaptation experiments, strengthens this claim. The clear articulation of the method and the provided example are also positive aspects.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** Completely removing the need for training in dependency parsing is a significant departure from the dominant paradigm. While unsupervised parsing exists, achieving competitive performance *without* any learning is uncommon and valuable. This sets the paper apart.
2. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The demonstrated resilience to domain change is a key strength. The experiments convincingly show that UDP maintains performance across different genres better than a supervised transfer baseline. This is especially important for low-resource scenarios where adapting to new domains is crucial.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating the parser on 32 languages is a substantial undertaking, providing broad coverage and demonstrating the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of a predicted POS setting and domain adaptation experiments adds further depth to the evaluation.
4. **Clear and Well-Documented Method:** The description of the algorithm, particularly the use of personalized PageRank and the two-step decoding process, is clear and easy to follow. The example provided (Section 4) further enhances understanding. The availability of the parser and datasets is also a benefit.
5. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** Comparing against a delexicalized transfer system provides a relevant benchmark. The fact that UDP achieves competitive results, and even outperforms in some cases, reinforces the value of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall performance, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding the specific types of errors UDP makes (e.g., frequent misattachments for specific POS tags or dependency relations) would provide valuable insights for future improvements. The discussion touches on some limitations but lacks depth.
2. **Reliance on Gold POS Tags:** Although the predicted POS setting is included, the primary results are based on gold POS tags. The performance drop with predicted POS, while reasonable, highlights a sensitivity to POS tagging accuracy. Exploring methods to make the parser more robust to noisy POS tags could be a valuable direction. The system's performance is heavily influenced by the quality of POS tagging, which is a dependency when claiming unsupervised status.
3. **Rule Engineering:** The head rules, while based on UD constraints, still require some degree of engineering and selection. The paper acknowledges this, but it's not entirely clear how these rules were determined and whether the process is easily replicable for other languages. A more systematic approach to rule acquisition could be explored. The claim of "no language-specific parameters" is slightly misleading given this initial rule selection.
4. **Simplified Evaluation Metrics:** Relying solely on UAS may not fully capture the quality of the parse trees. Considering metrics like dependency accuracy (DEP) or even qualitative analysis of the resulting trees could provide a more nuanced assessment.
5. **Limited Comparison to Other Unsupervised Methods:** The paper primarily positions itself against supervised transfer methods. A more thorough comparison to other existing unsupervised parsing approaches (beyond the cited DMV parser) would better contextualize the contributions.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the most common types of errors made by UDP?
2. How were the head rules selected, and what criteria were used to ensure their generalizability across languages? Could this process be automated or made more data-driven?
3. Have you investigated methods to make UDP more robust to noisy POS tags, such as incorporating POS tag confidence scores into the PageRank calculation or using a more sophisticated POS tagging error correction technique?
4. How sensitive is the performance of UDP to the choice of teleport probability in the PageRank algorithm? What is the rationale for selecting 0.05?
5. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of UDP compared to the delexicalized transfer system, particularly in terms of parsing time?

**Additional Comments:**

- The reference list is quite extensive, which is commendable.
- The notation in Figure 1 could be improved for clarity (e.g., defining the functions γ, δ, and κ more explicitly).
- The discussion of the personalization vector in Section 3.1 could be expanded. Why is a factor of 5 used? Was this value optimized?
