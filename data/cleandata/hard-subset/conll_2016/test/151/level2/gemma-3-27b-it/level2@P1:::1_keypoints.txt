- Competitive unsupervised parser: UDP attains strong, near-delexicalized-transfer performance across 32 languages, showing high-accuracy parsing without supervised training.  
- Robustness to domain change: The rule-based approach is more stable across domains than a supervised baseline, valuable for low-resource settings.  
- Effective use of UD constraints: The method leverages UD principles (function word attachment, single-rooted trees) to guide parsing effectively.  

- Strong empirical results: Impressive multi-language performance and a meaningful benchmark comparison to delexicalized transfer.  
- Clear explanation: Methodology (PageRank setup, head direction estimation, decoding) is well-explained with clarifying examples.  
- Thorough evaluation: Extensive tests including gold vs. predicted POS and cross-domain analysis, plus in-house English annotations.  
- Practical implications: Domain robustness makes UDP useful for real-world, low-resource, and cross-domain applications.  
- Resource availability: Parser and datasets are reported as available, aiding reproducibility.  

- Limited novelty of components: Uses established techniques (PageRank, rule-based parsing); the novelty is mainly their combination/adaptation.  
- Sensitivity to POS quality: Performance drops substantially with predicted POS tags, indicating reliance on accurate POS tagging.  
- Simplified linguistic coverage: Excluding rules like NOUN → VERB limits handling of complex structures (e.g., relative clauses).  
- Shallow error analysis: Lacks detailed breakdown of common error types and problematic linguistic constructions.  
- Table 5 formatting issues: Visual presentation (underlines/bolding) makes the table hard to parse.  

- Request more detail on head-dependent rules: How were rules in Table 1 selected and conflicts resolved?  
- Teleport probability sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the PageRank teleport parameter across languages?  
- Incorporating NOUN → VERB: Have you explored adding this rule without harming performance?  
- Errors in free-word-order languages: What systematic biases or error patterns occur in languages like Russian?  
- Computational cost: How does runtime/resource use compare to the delexicalized transfer system?  

- Reference list and related work: References are extensive and related-work discussion is good.  
- Writing quality: Paper is well-written and easy to follow.  
- Dataset link: Provide dataset link or clarify availability.  
- Overall recommendation: Solid, valuable work with promising results; reviewer recommends acceptance.
