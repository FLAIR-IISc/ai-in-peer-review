This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies, relying on PageRank and a set of head-dependent rules. The core idea is compelling: leverage the constraints inherent in UD to build a parser *without* training data. The authors claim contributions in adapting rules to UD, incorporating function word constraints, using personalized PageRank, and achieving language-agnostic adposition attachment.

**Contributions:**

1. **A competitive unsupervised parser:** The parser achieves surprisingly strong performance, nearing that of a delexicalized transfer system across 32 languages. This is the primary and most significant contribution, demonstrating the feasibility of high-accuracy parsing without supervised learning.
2. **Robustness to domain change:** The system shows better stability across domains compared to a supervised baseline, suggesting the benefits of a rule-based approach less prone to overfitting to specific datasets. This is a valuable observation, particularly for low-resource languages where training data is limited and domain diversity is high.
3. **Effective integration of UD constraints:** The method successfully utilizes the core principles of UD (function word attachment, single-rooted trees) to guide the parsing process, showcasing a practical application of the UD formalism.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The performance numbers are impressive for an unsupervised parser. The comparison to a delexicalized transfer system provides a good benchmark and demonstrates UDP's practicality. The extensive multi-language evaluation is a major strength.
2. **Clear and concise explanation:** The method is explained well, with a clear breakdown of the PageRank setup, head direction estimation, and decoding algorithm. The example provided in Section 4 further clarifies the process.
3. **Thorough evaluation:** The evaluation covers multiple scenarios – gold vs. predicted POS tags, and cross-domain analysis – providing a comprehensive assessment of the system’s strengths and weaknesses.  The inclusion of in-house annotated datasets for English enhances this evaluation.
4. **Practical implications:** The domain robustness demonstrated has significant practical implications for applying parsing technology to real-world data, especially in scenarios where labeled data is scarce or inconsistent across domains.
5. **Availability of resources:**  The authors mention the availability of both the parser and the datasets, which is a positive step towards reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in core components:** While the *combination* of techniques is interesting, the individual components (PageRank, rule-based parsing) are well-established. The claim of novelty feels somewhat overstated. The personalized PageRank adaptation is a good addition, but not a revolutionary breakthrough.
2. **Sensitivity to POS tag quality:** Although the system outperforms the supervised baseline under predicted POS, the performance drop is still substantial. This highlights the critical dependence on reasonably accurate POS tagging, a challenge for many languages.
3. **Simplified treatment of certain linguistic phenomena:** The decision to exclude NOUN → VERB rules, while understandable for simplicity, limits the system’s ability to handle more complex sentence structures (e.g., relative clauses). This is a tradeoff acknowledged by the authors but worth noting.
4. **Lack of error analysis detail:**  While the paper mentions error analysis (e.g., PUNCT being difficult), it would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of common error types and potential improvements. What specific linguistic structures consistently cause problems for UDP?
5. **Table 5 formatting:** The table is a bit difficult to parse visually due to the use of underlines and bolding to denote comparisons. A more standard visual representation might improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the specific head-dependent rules used in Table 1? What was the process for selecting these rules, and how were conflicts resolved?
2.  The paper mentions that the teleport probability in PageRank was tuned on the English data. How sensitive is the system's performance to this parameter in other languages?
3.  You state that excluding the NOUN → VERB rule simplifies the system. Have you investigated methods for incorporating this rule without significantly compromising performance?
4.  How would you characterize the types of errors UDP makes in languages with very free word order (e.g., Russian)? Is there a systematic bias in the resulting dependency trees?
5.  What is the computational cost of running UDP compared to the delexicalized transfer system?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*   The discussion of related work provides a good overview of the field.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The link to the datasets should be provided if publicly available, or clarification given.

Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper. The contributions are valuable, and the results are promising. While the individual components aren't groundbreaking, the combination and application to the UD framework demonstrate a significant step forward in unsupervised dependency parsing. I recommend acceptance.
