The paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies, based on PageRank and dependency head rules. The central claim is that this parser achieves competitive performance without training, offering robustness to domain change. The primary contribution is the development of a completely unsupervised parsing approach leveraging UD constraints, achieved through adapting dependency head rules, incorporating function word restrictions, utilizing personalized PageRank, and estimating adposition attachment direction directly from test data. A secondary contribution lies in the extensive evaluation across 32 languages and an analysis of domain sensitivity. Finally, the provision of a publicly available parser and annotated test sets constitutes a valuable resource for the research community.

Strengths:

1. **Strong Performance Without Training:** The parser demonstrably achieves competitive results (UAS scores often approaching supervised systems) without relying on any training data. This is a significant achievement in unsupervised parsing, especially given the complexities of dependency structure. The comparison to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system (MSD) provides a clear benchmark, showcasing UDP’s viability as an alternative approach.

2. **Robustness to Domain Change:** The evaluation across different domains reveals a commendable stability of UDP, exhibiting lower standard deviation in performance across domains compared to the supervised MSD baseline. This highlights the benefit of relying on general linguistic constraints encoded in the UD formalism, rather than being overly specialized to specific training corpora. The detailed domain-specific results presented in Table 6 are informative and support this claim.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The work includes a thorough evaluation across a large number of languages (32) using standard metrics (UAS). The exploration of different POS tagging scenarios (gold, predicted, naive) provides valuable insight into the parser’s sensitivity to input quality and its inherent robustness. The inclusion of additional, newly annotated datasets for English further strengthens the analysis.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the combination of techniques is notable, the individual components—PageRank, dependency head rules—are not entirely novel. The contribution primarily resides in the *specific application* and *adaptation* of these techniques within the UD framework. A more detailed discussion of how UDP differs from existing PageRank-based parsers, beyond simply mentioning UD constraints, would be beneficial.

2. **Lack of Error Analysis beyond Domain:** While the domain shift analysis is strong, a more in-depth qualitative error analysis would be valuable. Identifying specific linguistic phenomena that UDP struggles with (e.g., long-distance dependencies, non-projectivity, complex coordination structures) would help pinpoint areas for future improvement. 

3. **Presentation of Results:** Table 5 presents a large amount of data, but its presentation could be improved for clarity. Highlighting statistically significant differences between UDP and other systems (using, e.g., boldface or asterisks) would make the key findings more readily apparent. The use of “R” and “L” to denote backoff direction could be better explained within the table caption.

Questions to Authors:

1. Could the authors elaborate on the specific linguistic motivations behind the chosen dependency head rules (Table 1)? Are there any rules that proved particularly effective or problematic during development?

2.  The paper mentions that personalization in PageRank gives five times more weight to the estimated main predicate. How sensitive is the performance of UDP to this weighting factor? Was a more extensive hyperparameter search conducted?

3.  The system relies on the first verb or content word as the main predicate. How does this simple heuristic affect performance on languages with non-canonical word order (e.g., verb-final languages)?

Additional Comments:

*   The paper would benefit from a visual example of a parsed sentence, illustrating the application of the head rules and the PageRank algorithm. This would help readers understand the parsing process more concretely.
*   The abbreviations used (e.g., UAS, MSD, PR) should be defined at their first occurrence in the text.
*   The links to the data and code should be explicitly provided in the final version of the paper.
