

Summary and Contributions: This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that requires no training and relies solely on UD's constraints. The main contributions are: (1) a linguistically sound unsupervised parser that leverages UD's specific constraints (function words as leaves, content words as heads), (2) a robust system that outperforms naive baselines and is competitive with supervised cross-lingual transfer methods, and (3) a demonstrated resilience to domain shifts across languages.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution by specifically targeting UD's formalism constraints rather than using generic unsupervised parsing approaches. The authors correctly identify that UD's constraints (function words as leaves, content words as heads) provide a natural structure for unsupervised parsing, which most prior work doesn't leverage. This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a gap in the literature.

2. The evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, testing on 32 languages across three POS setups (gold, predicted, and naive content/function POS). The results show UDP is remarkably competitive with a supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer system (MSD), with only a 6.4% average UAS difference (and outperforming MSD on Hindi). This is a strong empirical result that substantiates the main claim.

3. The demonstration of domain robustness across languages is particularly valuable. The paper shows UDP has significantly lower standard deviations across domains compared to MSD (Table 6), confirming that rule-based approaches can be more stable to domain shifts. This is an important practical contribution for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

4. The methodological innovation of using personalized PageRank to identify the main predicate (with PR personalization) is well-motivated and effective, as shown in Section 6.1 (9 UAS points improvement over the non-PR version). This represents a meaningful advancement over prior unsupervised parsing methods.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper claims to be "unsupervised" but admits to using English treebank during development to tune head rules and PageRank parameters. This undermines the "no training" claim, as the system is language-dependent in its development phase. The authors should clarify this limitation and explicitly state that the system requires a small amount of development data (in one language) rather than being completely unsupervised.

2. The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other unsupervised parsers in the literature. While it references Søgaard (2012b), it doesn't provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art unsupervised parsers on the same dataset. Without this, it's difficult to fully assess how much of the performance gain is due to the UD-specific constraints versus the PageRank approach.

3. The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach. For example, the exclusion of the NOUN→VERB head rule (which prevents relative clause parsing) is mentioned but not analyzed in terms of its impact on overall performance. A more detailed error analysis would strengthen the paper.

4. The evaluation of domain robustness is limited to a few languages (Bulgarian, Croatian, Italian) and domains. The paper claims UDP is "distinctly robust to domain change across languages," but the evidence is not comprehensive enough to support this strong claim. More extensive domain testing across multiple languages would be needed.

5. The paper claims to be "completely free of language-specific parameters," but the adposition direction estimation (Section 3.2) requires language-specific tuning based on the test data (e.g., calculating ADP-NOMINAL vs. NOMINAL-ADP frequencies). This contradicts the claim of being completely language-agnostic.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper states that UDP "requires no training," but development on English treebank is used. How much development data (in terms of sentences or languages) is actually required for the system to work effectively? Could the authors provide a more precise definition of "no training"?

2. The paper claims UDP is "competitive with a delexicalized transfer system" but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why it outperforms MSD on Hindi (Table 5). Could the authors provide insights into what makes UDP particularly effective for Hindi?

3. The exclusion of the NOUN→VERB head rule (to avoid "hierarchical relation between verbs and nouns less conclusive") is mentioned but not quantified. What is the performance impact of this exclusion on overall UAS across languages?
