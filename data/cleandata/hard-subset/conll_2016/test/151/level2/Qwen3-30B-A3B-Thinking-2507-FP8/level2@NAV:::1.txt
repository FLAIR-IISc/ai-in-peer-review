

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that requires no training data and relies solely on UD-specific constraints. The main claims are: 1) UDP is competitive with supervised delexicalized transfer systems despite having no training data; 2) UDP is robust to domain shifts across languages; and 3) the system leverages UD's linguistic constraints in a novel way.

The primary contribution is the adaptation of dependency head rules to UD's formalism, particularly the leaf status constraint for function words. A secondary contribution is the use of personalized PageRank to identify main predicates, and a third is the on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test data. These contributions are well-supported by extensive experiments across 32 languages with three POS scenarios.

Strengths: The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that UDP achieves competitive performance (within 6.4% of a supervised baseline on average) while being more robust to domain shifts than data-driven approaches. The cross-domain evaluation across multiple language-specific domains (e.g., Bulgarian legal documents, Italian news) demonstrates UDP's stability, with significantly lower standard deviations (2.5 vs. 4.2) than the baseline. The approach is also linguistically sound, directly leveraging UD's formal constraints rather than relying on data-driven heuristics.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient analysis of why UDP outperforms the baseline on Hindi (which is the only language where it beats the supervised system). The performance drop in the naive POS scenario (UDPN) is substantial (29.9% average UAS), suggesting the system's sensitivity to POS quality. Additionally, the paper doesn't adequately address the low performance on punctuation (21% UAS), which is a significant limitation for practical applications.

The work is well-positioned within the field, with comprehensive related work discussion. However, the authors should clarify their personalization strategy for non-verb-final languages (e.g., Hindi) and provide more detailed error analysis for punctuation.

Questions to Authors: How does UDP handle cases where the first verb isn't the main predicate (common in verb-final languages)? What specific linguistic properties cause the poor punctuation performance, and how might they be addressed?
