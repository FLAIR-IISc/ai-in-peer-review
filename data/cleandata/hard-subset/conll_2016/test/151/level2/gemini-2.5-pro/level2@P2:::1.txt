**Summary and Contributions**
This paper presents UDP, a completely unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data and instead relies on a small, hand-crafted set of dependency head rules based on UD part-of-speech (POS) tags. It uses a personalized PageRank algorithm to rank the content words in a sentence, biasing the first verb to be the main predicate. A subsequent two-step decoding algorithm first constructs a syntactic backbone by attaching content words based on their rank and then attaches function words as leaves, adhering to UD principles. Adposition directionality is cleverly estimated on-the-fly from the test data. The parser is evaluated on 32 languages from the UD treebanks and compared against a strong, supervised multi-source delexicalized transfer system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The proposal of a simple, elegant, and effective unsupervised parsing method that demonstrates the power of leveraging the inherent linguistic constraints of the Universal Dependencies framework. Its competitive performance against a strong supervised delexicalized parser is a significant and surprising result, highlighting the value of linguistically-informed, rule-based approaches in the UD ecosystem.
2.  An extensive cross-lingual and cross-domain evaluation that establishes the parser's robustness. By showing that the method is less sensitive to both POS tagging errors and domain shift than its data-driven counterpart, the paper provides a strong and practical baseline for future work in unsupervised and cross-lingual parsing.

**Strengths**
1.  **Simplicity and High Performance:** The proposed method is remarkably simple, relying only on a handful of POS-based rules, PageRank, and a straightforward decoding algorithm. Despite this, it achieves impressive results, trailing a supervised multi-source delexicalized parser by only 6.4% UAS on average across 32 languages on gold POS tags, and even outperforming it on Hindi. This is a very compelling result that effectively showcases how much parsing performance can be extracted directly from the design principles of the UD annotation scheme itself, without any training data.
2.  **Extensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper's empirical evaluation is a major strength. The system is tested on a very broad set of 32 languages, which demonstrates its wide applicability. The evaluation is conducted under multiple realistic conditions, including both gold and predicted POS tags, providing a clear picture of the system's performance and error propagation characteristics. The comparison against a competitive delexicalized transfer parser is well-chosen and effectively contextualizes the system's achievements.
3.  **Demonstrated Robustness to Domain Shift:** The cross-domain evaluation is thorough and provides one of the paper's key insights. The authors convincingly show that UDP is significantly more stable across different domains than the data-driven MSD parser, as evidenced by its consistently lower standard deviation in UAS (Table 6). This provides strong support for the hypothesis that parsers based on general linguistic principles are less prone to overfitting the statistical quirks of a specific training domain, a finding with important practical implications.
4.  **Genuinely Unsupervised and Language-Agnostic Design:** The parser is truly training-free. The clever on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test data is a novel feature that helps the system adapt to a crucial typological parameter without requiring pre-existing knowledge or tuning. This makes the system extremely easy to apply to any language for which a POS tagger is available, positioning it as an excellent tool for genuinely low-resource scenarios.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Linguistic Coverage of Head Rules:** The set of head rules presented in Table 1 is very small. While this is a key aspect of the system's simplicity, it also imposes strong constraints on the types of structures the parser can produce. For example, as the authors note, the lack of a `NOUN -> VERB` rule makes it impossible to generate analyses for relative clauses. This limitation is likely a significant contributor to the performance gap with the MSD parser. A more detailed discussion of the syntactic phenomena that are systematically un-analyzable by the current rule set would strengthen the paper and clarify the trade-offs between rule simplicity and linguistic coverage.
2.  **Reliance on English for Development:** The paper states that the head rules, PageRank personalization parameter (factor of 5), and fixed attachment directions for function words (e.g., AUX, DET) were all determined using the English development set. This, to some extent, qualifies the claim of being free of "language-specific parameters," as the system is implicitly tuned to the properties of English UD. While the excellent cross-lingual results show that these choices generalize well, it raises questions about their optimality for typologically different languages (e.g., verb-final languages). A discussion of the sensitivity to these hyperparameters would be welcome.
3.  **Potential Ambiguity in Decoding:** The decoding algorithm (Figure 1, lines 7 and 13) specifies that the head `h` is chosen as the `argmin` of the linear distance function `γ(j, c)`. It is not specified how ties are broken if a word is equidistant from two or more valid potential heads. While this may be a minor point, clarifying this detail is important for full reproducibility of the work.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The fixed attachment directions for function words like AUX and DET were tuned on English data. How well do these fixed directions hold for typologically diverse languages? For example, Basque is mentioned as an exception for DET attachment. Have you considered extending your on-the-fly estimation method (used for adpositions) to other function words to make the system even more adaptive?
2.  Regarding the development of the head rules on the English dev set: what was the empirical impact of excluding rules like `NOUN -> VERB` for relative clauses? Did adding such a rule improve UAS for verbs but harm overall performance due to incorrect attachments in other contexts? A brief insight into this trade-off would be very informative.
