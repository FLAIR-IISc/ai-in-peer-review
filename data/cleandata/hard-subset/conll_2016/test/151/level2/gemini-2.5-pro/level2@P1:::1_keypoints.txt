- Summary: UDP is a fully unsupervised dependency parser using a small set of UD-derived head rules, personalized PageRank to pick content-word heads with a verb bias, and a second step that attaches function words as leaves; it infers adposition directionality from test data and is evaluated on 32 languages, showing competitive performance and better domain robustness than a delexicalized transfer baseline.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a simple, elegant, training-free UD parser that leverages linguistic constraints as a strong baseline competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates thorough empirical evidence that the rule-based parser is notably more robust to domain shift than data-driven baselines.

- Contribution 3: Proposes a novel use of personalized PageRank to identify sentence predicates/roots, improving root selection without supervised training.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical performance for a training-free method—on average only ~6.4 UAS behind a multi-source delexicalized parser and outperforming it on some languages (e.g., Hindi).

- Strength 2: Excellent cross-domain analysis showing lower variance and greater stability across domains and languages compared to the baseline.

- Strength 3: Method is simple, transparent, and reproducible, based on a small explicit rule set and a clear two-step decoding algorithm.

- Strength 4: Evaluation is comprehensive—32 languages and three POS-tagging conditions (gold, predicted, naive) give a clear picture of generality and sensitivity to POS quality.

- Weakness 1: Heavy dependence on high-quality POS tags; performance collapses with naive CONTENT/FUNCTION tags, limiting zero-resource applicability.

- Weakness 2: Limited linguistic coverage from the fixed head-rule set—poor handling of relative clauses, coordination, and punctuation reduces usefulness for some downstream tasks.

- Weakness 3: Some English-centric development choices (head rules, PageRank tuning, fixed function-word directions) may bias results and reduce full language-agnosticity (e.g., DET direction suboptimal for Basque).

- Question 1: How often are the back-off strategies (dropping δ rules then κ constraints) triggered across languages, and are they more frequent for typologically distant languages?

- Question 2: Given success estimating adposition directionality from small test samples, have you considered dynamically estimating attachment directions for other function-word classes (AUX, DET, SCONJ) to reduce English-centric bias?
