**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents UDP, a fully unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD). The method requires no training data, instead leveraging a small set of hand-crafted, UD-compliant head-selection rules. Its core novelty lies in combining these rules with a personalized PageRank algorithm to identify and prioritize likely predicate heads in a sentence. A two-step decoding process first builds a parse over content words and then attaches function words as leaves, directly enforcing a key constraint of the UD formalism. The primary contributions of this work are: 1) a remarkably effective and simple training-free parsing method that demonstrates the power of exploiting formalism constraints; and 2) a thorough empirical evaluation across 32 languages showing that this simple method is not only competitive with a strong, supervised delexicalized parser but is also significantly more robust to domain shifts.

**Strengths**

1.  The parser's performance is surprisingly strong for a completely unsupervised system. Averaging 57.5 UAS with gold POS tags across 32 languages, it closes a significant portion of the gap to a multi-source delexicalized transfer system (63.9 UAS). This result challenges the necessity of complex data-driven models for establishing a strong parsing baseline.
2.  The most compelling argument for this work is its demonstrated robustness. The cross-domain evaluation (Table 6) shows UDP has a much lower variance in performance across different text genres compared to the data-driven baseline. This stability makes it a more reliable tool for real-world applications where out-of-domain text is common.
3.  The method’s simplicity is a major advantage. It is clearly described, easy to implement, and serves as an excellent, reproducible baseline for future research in low-resource and unsupervised parsing.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The system's capabilities are fundamentally limited by its fixed, hand-crafted rule set. As acknowledged, this design prevents it from capturing more complex phenomena like relative clauses (due to the lack of a NOUN → VERB rule). This places a hard ceiling on its potential accuracy and ability to model diverse grammatical structures.
2.  The parser is heavily dependent on the quality of input POS tags. The significant performance drop under the naive content/function tagging scenario (UDPN) highlights this fragility. The method offers no mechanism to handle POS tagging ambiguity or errors, which could be a major issue in truly low-resource scenarios where high-quality taggers are unavailable.
3.  The head rules and PageRank parameters were tuned on the English development set. While the system generalizes well, this introduces a potential Anglocentric bias. It would be valuable to understand how the choice of development language might influence the final rule set and the system's cross-lingual performance profile.
