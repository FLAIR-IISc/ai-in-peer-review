Summary and contributions
- The paper presents UDP, a training-free, UD-oriented dependency parser that combines a small set of UD-compliant head rules with personalized PageRank and a two-step decoding scheme that first attaches content words and then function words, thereby enforcing UD’s leaf constraints for function words.
- It introduces an unsupervised, test-time estimation of adposition attachment direction via ADP–NOMINAL bigram counts, avoiding language-specific parameters.
- The work provides a broad empirical evaluation on 32 UD v1.2 languages, with ablations (no-PR) and cross-domain analyses, demonstrating competitive performance against a multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline and improved robustness to domain shift.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant exploitation of UD constraints: The two-step decoding that enforces function words as leaves aligns closely with UD design, and personalized PageRank prioritizes main predicates effectively. The ablation indicates PageRank contributes ~9 UAS on average relative to the no-PR variant, which substantiates the methodological value.
- Competitive cross-lingual performance without training: On gold POS, the parser is on average 6.4 UAS below multi-source delexicalized transfer, even surpassing it on Hindi, which is notable given zero reliance on parallel data or supervised target-side training. Performance degrades modestly with predicted POS compared to the supervised baseline (2.2 vs 2.7 UAS).
- Robustness to domain shift: Across multiple languages with domain splits (Bulgarian, Croatian/Serbian, English, Italian), standard deviations are consistently lower than the supervised delexicalized system for both gold and predicted POS, indicating better stability to domain variations.
- Minimal language-specific assumptions: The approach avoids hand-crafted, language-specific parameters; adposition direction is inferred from test data (converges within ~10–15 sentences). Fixed attachment directions for other function word classes are simple and mostly effective (e.g., high DET UAS except Basque).
- Clarity, determinism, and reproducibility: The algorithm is simple, deterministic given POS sequences, and runs in near O(n×|C|) time per sentence, with the main cost in PageRank. The paper reports head rules, decoding details, ablations, and makes code/data available.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on POS quality and resource assumptions: While the parser itself is unsupervised, the main evaluation relies on gold or TnT-predicted POS trained on target-language training data. This departs from truly low-resource scenarios. The CF (content/function) tag setup shows large performance drops, indicating sensitivity to POS granularity. A more realistic low-resource POS setup (e.g., projection-based or multilingual taggers without target training) would better validate applicability.
- Limited rule coverage and syntactic phenomena: The compact head rule set omits key constructions (e.g., coordination scope, relative clauses due to excluding NOUN→VERB, multiword expressions). This manifests in low UAS for PUNCT (avg ~21%) and modest performance for CONJ, and prevents capturing relative clause structures. Extending rules or introducing simple construction-specific heuristics could yield meaningful gains.
- Parameter tuning and sensitivity: The teleport probability (0.05) and personalization weight (5×) were tuned on English development data and then fixed. Although reported as stable, the paper lacks a systematic sensitivity analysis across languages/domains. Mixed adposition systems or languages with substantial free word order may require different settings, which is not explored.
- Test-time estimation of ADP direction is coarse: A single global pre/postposition decision may be inadequate for languages with mixed adposition behaviors or strong register variation. The paper notes DET direction exceptions (Basque) but does not estimate these directions per language/test set, though this seems feasible (and suggested).
- Evaluation scope: Only unlabeled attachment is reported; UD labels are central to downstream usability. Inclusion of LAS (even partial) and breakdowns by sentence length and projectivity would strengthen the empirical claims. Statistical significance testing across systems would clarify whether observed averages represent robust improvements.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the teleport probability and to the 5× personalization weight? Are there languages where alternative settings materially improve UAS?
- What is the empirical non-projectivity rate produced by the two-step decoding, and how does that compare to gold? Does non-projectivity correlate with errors on specific POS or constructions?
- For languages with mixed adposition patterns, how often does the single global pre/post decision misclassify tokens? Could per-sentence or local pattern estimation improve ADP attachments?
- Could directionality for other function word classes (e.g., DET, SCONJ, CONJ) be estimated on the fly, as done for ADP? Is there an ablation showing the impact of such estimation vs fixed directions?
- What are per-sentence runtimes and overall throughput across languages, including PageRank computation? Any observed scalability issues on longer sentences?
- How do results change with different POS taggers (e.g., cross-lingual projected POS, multilingual neural taggers) to better approximate low-resource conditions?

Suggestions for improvement
- Essential: Add a low-resource POS tagging condition (e.g., projection from related sources or multilingual models without target training) and report UAS, robustness, and error propagation. This better supports the “unsupervised” and “low-resource” narrative.
- Essential: Provide a sensitivity analysis for PageRank parameters and the adposition estimation threshold; report any language-specific deviations.
- Recommended: Extend the rule set or add simple heuristics for coordination scope, relative clauses, and punctuation; a post-processing step could suffice and likely improves CONJ/PUNCT UAS.
- Recommended: Report LAS and, if feasible, partial/clustered labels for edges licensed by rules; add breakdowns by sentence length and projectivity, plus significance testing.
- Optional: Explore edge-reified PageRank (as mentioned in conclusions) or local PageRank over candidate edges to refine head selection, potentially improving content-word attachments without sacrificing simplicity.
