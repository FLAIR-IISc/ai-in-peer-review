Summary and contributions
This paper presents UDP, a rule-driven, training-free dependency parser tailored to Universal Dependencies (UD). The method constructs a dependency-informed graph and applies personalized PageRank (PR) to rank content words, then decodes in two passes: first attaching content words (enforcing a single root), then attaching function words as leaves, with attachment directions partly inferred from test data (ADP) and partly fixed. The system requires only POS tags, no parallel data, and no model training.

Key contributions as I see them:
1) A simple, UD-aware unsupervised parsing algorithm that combines personalized PageRank with a small set of POS-typed head rules and a two-step decoding enforcing function-word leafness.
2) A broad and careful empirical evaluation across 32 languages and multiple domains, showing competitive UAS against multi-source delexicalized transfer (MSD), with greater robustness to POS noise and domain shift; useful ablations quantify PR’s impact.
3) A practical, language-agnostic heuristic to infer ADP head direction from test data, reducing language-specific tuning.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual coverage and competitiveness. UDPG averages 57.5 UAS and is within 6.4 points of MSDG on average, outperforming MSDG for Hindi. The method is consistently stronger than a reasonable rule baseline (BLG). The breadth (32 languages, UD v1.2) gives credibility to the generality claim.
- Robustness to POS noise and domain shift. With predicted POS, UDP drops less than MSD (2.2 vs 2.7 UAS on average) and shows lower error propagation (0.37 vs 0.46), which is non-trivial given UDP’s reliance on POS. Across genre splits, UDP exhibits lower standard deviation than MSD in all reported languages, supporting the claim of stability and making it an appealing baseline for noisy/out-of-domain scenarios.
- Methodological clarity and useful analysis. The decoding algorithm is explicit, the PR setup is described, and ablations are informative: disabling PR costs ~9 UAS; decoding over the rule baseline adds ~4 UAS. The POS-wise breakdown (e.g., DET, ADP, CONJ, PUNCT) and root accuracy analysis provide insight into where the approach succeeds/fails.
- Minimal resource requirements and interpretability. UDP needs no training data, no parallel data, no external lexicons, and only relies on POS. The rules are human-readable, making behavior inspectable and extensible. The ADP-direction inference is simple and effective.
- UD-conformant design choices. Enforcing function words as leaves and respecting UD POS constraints makes the approach linguistically principled and different from older unsupervised methods that struggled to match evaluation conventions.

Weaknesses
- The “unsupervised” claim is somewhat overstated. Several design decisions are tuned on English development data (PR teleport, personalization weight, function-word attachment directions), and the evaluation with predicted POS uses supervised taggers trained on target-language training sets. While the core parsing is training-free, these dependencies should be more clearly framed and their impact explored. For instance, DET right-attachment fails for Basque; learning such directions from data (as done for ADP) may be preferable.
- Limited rule coverage constrains syntactic phenomena. Table 1’s rules preclude NOUN→VERB attachments, blocking relative clauses; coordination and punctuation remain weak; multiword expressions and proper names are unaddressed. The fallback to nearest-head attachments relaxes constraints to ensure a tree but risks idiosyncratic attachments. These coverage gaps likely cap performance and may bias mistake patterns.
- Test-time estimation protocol needs clarification. ADP direction is inferred from test data “on the fly” with 10–15 sentences. It is unclear whether this is done incrementally per sentence, on a held-out slice, or over the whole test set before parsing. This matters for strict evaluation protocol and for replicability; per-language tuning on the test set may inadvertently benefit UDP in ways not afforded to MSD.
- Comparative baseline could be stronger or better contextualized. MSD uses TurboParser with delexicalized features trained on 20k sentences. Contemporary transfer setups often leverage better feature sets or typological/linguistic priors, and some include morphology. Without a comparison to stronger delex baselines or to Søgaard (2012b) reimplemented under UD, it is hard to precisely isolate novelty beyond personalization and two-step decoding.
- Evaluation scope is narrow in places. Only UAS is reported; LAS is understandably hard without labels, but additional attachment breakdowns (e.g., projective vs non-projective, length bins) or significance testing would strengthen claims. Runtime/efficiency is only qualitatively discussed; PR cost vs decoding cost would be useful, given potential deployment as a baseline.

Questions to authors
- How exactly is ADP direction estimated in test time? Is a fixed number of initial sentences used, or is it estimated over the entire test set before parsing, or updated online? What happens for languages with scarce ADP bigrams?
- Could you estimate other function-word directions (e.g., DET, SCONJ) from test data, as done for ADP? Do you expect this to recover the Basque DET issue and improve overall UAS?
- How sensitive is performance to the PR personalization weight (currently 5x) and teleport probability (0.05)? Please provide a sensitivity analysis across a few typologically diverse languages.
- Can you quantify the proportion of arcs produced via the back-off steps (dropping δ, then κ) and their error rates vs rule-licensed arcs?
- For MSD, what feature templates and hyperparameters were used? Would adding simple typological priors (e.g., ADP direction) narrow the gap or change the robustness comparison?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider a small extension to allow NOUN↔VERB attachments under constrained contexts (e.g., relative clause cues), or learnable exceptions, to recover relative clauses without broadly degrading verb-noun hierarchy.
- Edge-centric PR (reification) as you suggest may help disambiguate attachment among multiple plausible heads, especially for coordination and nominal modifiers.
- Estimating function-word directions from test data per language (beyond ADP) seems low-cost and likely impactful.
- Reporting per-length UAS and non-projectivity rates would help understand the algorithm’s failure modes; a brief runtime table would support the “cheap baseline” narrative.
- Given UD’s evolution, re-running on a newer UD release (if possible) would test stability to annotation refinements.
