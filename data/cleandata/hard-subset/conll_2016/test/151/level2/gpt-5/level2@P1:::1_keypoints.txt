- Paper introduces UDP: a training-free UD parser combining UD head rules, personalized PageRank, and a two-step decoder that enforces function words as dependents; adposition direction is estimated from test bigram counts.  
- Main claim: UDP is competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer across 32 languages (UAS avg 57.5 vs 63.9 with gold POS) and more robust to domain shifts.  
- Contribution: practical decoding that mixes linguistic rules with PageRank-based content ordering and on-the-fly adposition direction estimation.  

Strengths:
- Evaluation breadth: tested on 32 languages, multiple domains, gold and predicted POS, with clear comparative metrics and domain-robustness analysis.  
- Simplicity and interpretability: no training required, small UD-aligned rule set, transparent two-step decoding, and simple bigram-based ADP direction estimation (~75% ADP UAS).  
- Solid ablation and error analysis: isolates PageRank and decoding contributions, provides POS-wise error breakdowns and openly discusses limitations.  
- Domain robustness: lower cross-domain variance (avg std dev 2.5 vs 4.5 for MSD) and cases where UDP outperforms MSD in specific domains/languages.  
- Reproducibility: deterministic algorithm given POS, specified rules/PR settings, and promised code/dataset release.  

Weaknesses:
- “Unsupervised” claim limited by full reliance on POS quality; UDPP uses supervised TnT taggers trained on target data, which undermines realism for low-resource settings.  
- Tuning and test-data usage: several design choices tuned on English dev data; adposition direction is estimated on the test set, which may be unacceptable in some eval protocols and introduces language bias.  
- Limited syntactic coverage: rule omissions (e.g., disallowing NOUN→VERB) hurt phenomena like relative clauses; coordination and punctuation performance is weak (PUNCT ~21% UAS, CONJ ~38%).  
- Back-off and well-formedness: decoder back-off (dropping δ then κ) can create attachments that violate intended constraints; frequency and impact of back-offs are not reported.  
- Root heuristic and PR personalization issues: bias toward first verb/content word may fail for verb-final or pro-drop languages; root accuracy ~69% with high variance.  
- Baselines and significance: lacks statistical significance testing, fuller baseline details (TurboParser settings, source selection effects), and omitted adjacency baseline numbers.  

Questions for authors:
- ADP estimation: is adposition direction estimated per sentence, per document, or pooled over the entire test set, and is any test-set look-ahead used?  
- Could directionality for other function POS (DET, AUX, SCONJ) be estimated similarly to remove English-tuned assumptions?  
- Back-off frequency: how often does the decoder drop δ (and κ) across languages, and do these back-offs correlate with specific POS/constructions?  
- Root heuristics: were alternatives tried (e.g., highest PR without personalization, last verb in verb-final languages, morphological cues) and how sensitive are results to the 5x personalization weight?  
- Reproducibility/runtime: will the exact rule set, PR parameters, domain-split scripts, and runtime statistics (PR iterations, per-sentence latency) be released?  
- Low-resource POS: have you tried replacing TnT with cross-lingual or projected POS to emulate true low-resource pipelines, and how does UDP perform in that setting?  

Overall verdict:
- Well-executed, insightful, and robust method; addresses important domain-robust parsing questions and the reviewer leans toward acceptance pending clarifications on POS reliance, test-time estimation, and coverage.
