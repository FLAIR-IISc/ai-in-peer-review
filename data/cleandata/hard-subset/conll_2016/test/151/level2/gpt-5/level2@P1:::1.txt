Summary and contributions
This paper introduces UDP, a training-free dependency parser for Universal Dependencies that combines a small set of UD-compliant head rules with personalized PageRank and a two-step decoding procedure designed to enforce UD’s constraint that function words are dependents. Adposition attachment direction is estimated directly from test sentences via ADP–NOMINAL bigram counts. The system is evaluated on 32 languages, with both gold and predicted POS tags, and compared to a strong multi-source delexicalized transfer baseline (MSD). The authors also analyze POS sensitivity, ablations (with/without PageRank), breakdown by POS, and cross-domain robustness.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, linguistically grounded, training-free UD parser that is surprisingly competitive with multi-source delexicalized transfer across 32 languages (average UAS 57.5 vs. 63.9 with gold POS), and more robust to domain shifts.
2) A practical decoding scheme combining personalized PageRank for content-word ordering with rules that enforce function-word leafness, plus on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction from test data.
3) An empirical study showing lower error propagation from POS noise than a delexicalized transfer baseline and lower cross-domain variance (e.g., average UAS std dev across domains 2.5 for UDPG vs. 4.5 for MSDG).

Strengths
- Breadth and thoroughness of evaluation: The system is tested on 32 UD languages and across multiple domains for several languages, with both gold and predicted POS. The comparative results are clear: UDPG averages 57.5 UAS vs. 63.9 for MSDG, and with predicted POS the drop is small (55.3 for UDPP vs. 61.2 for MSDP), with quantified error propagation (0.37 vs. 0.46). The domain analysis (Tables 6–7) is particularly valuable and shows UDP’s stability (e.g., average std dev 2.5 vs. 4.5 for MSD).
- Simplicity and interpretability: UDP requires no training data, uses a short list of UD-aligned head rules, and a transparent two-step decoding that explicitly encodes the UD principle that function words are leaves. The on-the-fly estimation of adposition direction via simple bigram counts is elegant and effective (ADP UAS ~75%).
- Strong ablation and error analysis: The paper isolates PageRank’s contribution (UDPNoPR −9 UAS vs. UDP), and the decoding step’s effect over the baseline (+4 UAS vs. BL). The POS breakdown highlights known pain points (PUNCT ~21% UAS, CONJ ~38%), and the limitations are openly discussed (e.g., excluding NOUN→VERB prevents relative clause handling).
- Robustness to domain shifts: The lower cross-domain variance for UDP (and instances where UDP outperforms MSD, e.g., some Italian domains) supports the claim that simple, linguistically motivated rules can generalize better than delexicalized models that are sensitive to sampling bias.
- Reproducibility: The algorithm is deterministic given POS; rules and PR settings are specified; code and datasets are promised (with anonymized links in the submission).

Weaknesses
- “Unsupervised” scope and reliance on POS: The approach is unsupervised w.r.t. dependency learning, but depends entirely on POS quality. UDPP uses supervised taggers trained on target-language training data (TnT), which is not available in many realistic low-resource scenarios. The UDPN two-tag setup is too crude to be informative about realistic weak supervision. It would strengthen the claim to evaluate with projected or cross-lingual POS tags (e.g., from distant supervision) to approximate true low-resource pipelines.
- Tuning and use of test data: Several design choices are tuned on English development data (rule set selection, PR teleport 0.05, function-word directionality), which introduces language-specific bias. Adposition direction is estimated on the test set itself; while unsupervised, this uses test-side statistics and may not be acceptable in some evaluation protocols. Please clarify whether this is computed per test file only and whether any look-ahead across the test set is used.
- Limited coverage of syntactic phenomena: The rule set omits important structures (e.g., relative clauses due to NOUN not allowed as head of VERB), and coordination and punctuation are weak (PUNCT ~21% UAS; CONJ ~38% with high variance). The back-off that drops δ and then κ can create attachments that violate the intended linguistic constraints, potentially harming consistency. A discussion or quantitative measure of how often back-off is invoked, and the impact on well-formedness and non-projectivity, would be helpful.
- Root selection heuristic and PR personalization: Root identification is biased to the first verb (or first content word); while personalization helps (+2% on average), this may be suboptimal for verb-final or copula-drop languages. More language-agnostic heuristics or data-driven predicates (e.g., multiple candidate roots, or PR without hard personalization but with better graph construction) could improve root accuracy (currently ~69% average with large variance).
- Baselines and significance: While MSD is a strong baseline, the paper does not report statistical significance, and some baseline design details are brief (e.g., TurboParser settings, sensitivity to source selection beyond uniform sampling, or alternative delex baselines). The adjacency baseline is omitted (claimed 20% worse than BL), but including at least summary numbers in an appendix would help establish the margin.

Questions to authors
- Test-time estimation: How exactly is ADP direction estimated—per sentence, per document, or pooled over the entire test set? Would a per-sentence or small-window estimate perform comparably? Could you similarly estimate directionality for DET, AUX, SCONJ to remove English-tuned assumptions?
- Back-off behavior: How often does the decoder drop δ (and then κ) across languages? Do these back-offs correlate with particular POS or constructions (e.g., coordination)?
- Root detection: Did you try alternative root heuristics (e.g., highest PR without personalization, last verb in verb-final languages, morphological cues), and how sensitive are results to the 5x personalization weight?
- Reproducibility: Will you release the exact rule set and PR parameters used in the final submission and scripts to compute domain splits? Any runtime statistics (PR iterations, per-sentence latency) across languages?
- Low-resource scenario: Have you tried replacing TnT with cross-lingual/projected POS to emulate true low-resource settings? If so, how does UDP fare relative to projection-based parsing?

Overall, this is a well-executed, insightful paper. The method is simple yet surprisingly strong and robust, and the analysis is careful. Addressing the concerns about POS reliance, test-time estimation, and coverage would further strengthen the contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
