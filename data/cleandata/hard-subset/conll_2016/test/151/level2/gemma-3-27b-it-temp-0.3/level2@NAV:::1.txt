## Review of "Parsing for Universal Dependencies without training"

This paper presents UDP, an unsupervised dependency parser for Universal Dependencies (UD) that leverages PageRank and a set of dependency head rules, requiring no training data. The core claim is that UDP achieves competitive performance compared to a delexicalized transfer system while demonstrating greater robustness to domain change across languages. I identify three key contributions: 1) a linguistically informed unsupervised parsing approach specifically tailored to the UD framework, 2) the innovative use of personalized PageRank to improve main predicate identification, and 3) a method for dynamically estimating adposition attachment direction directly on test data, eliminating language-specific parameters.

The strength of this work lies in its comprehensive evaluation. Testing UDP across 32 languages, with comparisons to a strong baseline (delexicalized transfer parsing) under varying POS tag reliability, and a dedicated domain adaptation study, provides compelling evidence for its effectiveness. The results consistently show UDP performing favorably, and importantly, exhibiting greater stability across domains than the supervised baseline. The detailed ablation study in Section 6 further clarifies the contribution of each component (PageRank, head rules) and provides valuable insights. The authors also thoughtfully acknowledge limitations, such as the inability to handle relative clauses due to the exclusion of NOUN → VERB head rules.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The presentation of the method, particularly the decoding algorithm (Fig. 1), is dense and difficult to follow without careful study. While the authors mention the algorithm is deterministic, a more intuitive explanation of its operation would be beneficial. Furthermore, the discussion of the experimental setup could be more detailed. For example, the criteria for selecting the domains for the cross-domain evaluation are not fully explained. The comparison to other unsupervised methods is limited; a discussion of how UDP compares to more recent unsupervised parsing techniques would strengthen the work. Finally, the impact of POS tagging errors is acknowledged, but a more granular analysis of error types and their effect on parsing accuracy would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the decoding algorithm (Fig. 1), perhaps with a step-by-step example applied to a simple sentence?
2.  What criteria were used to select the domains for the cross-domain evaluation?
3.  How does UDP compare to more recent unsupervised dependency parsing techniques beyond the baselines presented?



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of unsupervised parsing. Despite some presentation and detail issues, the strong empirical results and novel approach make it a worthwhile addition to the conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
