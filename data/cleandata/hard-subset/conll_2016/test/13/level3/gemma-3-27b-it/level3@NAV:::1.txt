## Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

This paper presents a neural approach to event linking, leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to extract sentential features and achieve competitive performance on the ACE 2005 and a newly introduced ACE++ dataset. The core idea is to move away from reliance on external knowledge bases, instead focusing on learning event representations directly from text using CNNs and a limited set of linguistic features.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating that a robust event linking system can be built *without* extensive external semantic features, achieving performance comparable to feature-rich systems. The authors successfully combine word embeddings with carefully selected pairwise and local lexical features to create a strong baseline, and the use of CNNs for sentential feature extraction is a reasonable and well-executed approach. The creation of the expanded ACE++ dataset, while not publicly available at the time of review, is a valuable addition for further research. 

A secondary, though less novel, contribution is the demonstrated effectiveness of CNNs for capturing sentence-level context relevant to event coreference. The approach of combining CNN outputs with local lexical features, and then employing a logistic regression classifier, is well-motivated and yields positive results.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A significant concern is the use of gold trigger words instead of predicted ones. This creates an unfair advantage and makes direct comparisons to systems relying on full event extraction pipelines difficult. Another issue is the inconsistent use of train/test splits; adhering to established splits is crucial for fair comparison within the research community. Several aspects of the model design lack sufficient clarity. The rationale behind the specific window size around the trigger (one word before/after) is arbitrary and unexplained. Similarly, the handling of left vs. right neighbors isn’t clearly defined, and the purpose of the intermediate event representation *v<sub>e</sub>* is unclear. The claim regarding the necessity of cross-sentential information (lines 65-70) feels unsupported by the presented analysis. 

Furthermore, the statistical significance of the reported results is not established; many numbers are close, necessitating significance testing. The related work section is also incomplete, missing a citation to Wiseman et al. (2015) which presents a relevant neural coreference approach. Finally, the unavailability of ACE++ hinders reproducibility and broader impact.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was a window size of one word before and after the trigger chosen? What experiments were conducted with different window sizes?
2.  Please clarify how the left and right neighbors are represented and used, and specifically how this impacts the max-pooling operation.
3.  What is the specific role of the *v<sub>e</sub>* representation (line 330)? If it isn't used, why is it computed?
4.  How are the continuous pairwise features (e.g., distance) encoded, and are the model parameters for these features updated during training?
5.  Will the ACE++ dataset be made publicly available?
6. How does the performance degrade when using predicted triggers instead of gold triggers?
