## Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

This paper proposes a two-part model for event coreference resolution, leveraging sentential features extracted using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The model first generates representations for individual event mentions and then uses these, along with pairwise features, to calculate a coreference score. The authors demonstrate strong performance on both the ACE 2005 and a newly created dataset, ACE++, achieving state-of-the-art results. A significant contribution is the creation and initial evaluation of the ACE++ dataset, motivated by shortcomings in the size of ACE. The writing is clear and well-structured, and the error analysis provides useful insights.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses, primarily concerning motivation and comparative analysis. While the authors demonstrate that CNNs *work* well, they offer no justification for *why* they were chosen over other sequence modeling architectures like RNNs or Transformers, which are commonly used for sentence-level representation learning. The reported 2-3 point F1 gains, while positive, are not strong enough evidence to warrant adopting CNNs without a comparative study. 

Furthermore, the novelty of simply applying CNNs to sentences for classification is questionable. The authors fail to adequately situate their work within the broader landscape of continuous-space event linking methods, and the baseline comparison in Table 3 is insufficient. Expanding these baselines to include more contemporary approaches would strengthen the claim of state-of-the-art performance.

Crucially, the methodology for creating ACE++ is poorly described. Details about event-type coverage, potential biases in the data collection process, and annotation guidelines are missing. Releasing ACE++ to the research community would be invaluable, but only if accompanied by thorough documentation. Demonstrating that the proposed method outperforms existing approaches specifically on the shortcomings of ACE addressed by ACE++ is also essential.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Dataset:** The creation of ACE++ addresses a clear limitation of the existing ACE dataset and provides a valuable resource for the community.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive performance on both datasets.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Useful Error Analysis:** The analysis highlights challenging cases and potential directions for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Architectural Justification:**  No explanation for choosing CNNs over other sequence modeling techniques.
*   **Limited Novelty:** Applying CNNs to sentences is not a novel contribution in itself.
*   **Incomplete Baseline Comparison:** Insufficient comparison to existing continuous-space event linking methods.
*   **Poorly Described Dataset Creation:** ACE++ collection process lacks transparency and documentation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a detailed justification for choosing CNNs over other sequence modeling architectures like RNNs or Transformers? Have you experimented with these alternatives?
2.  Can you elaborate on the data collection process for ACE++? What steps were taken to ensure event-type coverage and minimize potential biases?
3.  Do you plan to release the ACE++ dataset to the research community, and if so, what documentation will be provided?
4.   Can you present an ablation study to provide more insight into the contribution of the individual features (pairwise, local, sentential)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider evaluating the model on other event coreference datasets, such as EventCorefBank.
*   Exploring the integration of off-the-shelf coreference systems (e.g., CoreNLP) as suggested in line 687 could be beneficial.
*   Joint entity/event coreference experiments may be worthwhile given the relatively small size of the ACE corpus.
