## Review of “Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks”

This paper presents a convolutional neural network (CNN) based approach to event linking, aiming to achieve strong performance without relying on extensive external semantic features. The authors demonstrate competitive results on the ACE 2005 and an extended ACE++ dataset, suggesting that sentential features learned via CNNs can effectively capture relevant information for this task.

**Contributions:**

1. **Strongest:** The primary contribution is a practical and competitive neural approach to event linking that substantially reduces reliance on external knowledge resources. This is particularly valuable for domain adaptation and low-resource settings where such resources may be unavailable or expensive to create. The model achieves performance comparable to feature-rich systems, demonstrating the efficacy of learned sentential representations.
2. **Significant:** The creation and use of the ACE++ dataset, while not publicly available (see concerns below), represents a valuable extension of the standard ACE 2005 corpus. Expanding the dataset provides a more robust testbed for evaluating event linking systems and encourages further research.
3. **Moderate:** The investigation into the impact of different feature combinations (pairwise, lexical, sentential) provides useful insights into the model’s behavior and the relative importance of various features. This analysis helps to understand *how* the model achieves its performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art systems on the ACE 2005 dataset, and demonstrates promising performance on the larger ACE++ dataset. This establishes the viability of the proposed approach.
2. **Reduced Reliance on External Resources:** The authors successfully demonstrate that strong performance can be achieved without relying on expensive and domain-specific external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet. This is a significant step towards more portable and adaptable event linking systems.
3. **Clear Model Architecture:** The model architecture is well-defined and clearly explained, with a logical flow from word embeddings to CNN-based feature extraction to coreference scoring. The use of max-pooling and the combination of different feature types are well-motivated.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, varying features and clustering strategies to understand the model’s sensitivity and optimal configuration. The inclusion of a detailed error analysis further strengthens the evaluation.
5. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis reveals challenging cases (e.g., pronoun resolution) and identifies potential areas for improvement, highlighting the limitations of the current approach and directions for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Gold Trigger Words:** The use of gold trigger words is a significant limitation. It unfairly advantages the model by removing the error propagation from trigger identification, a crucial step in a real-world event extraction pipeline. Comparisons to systems relying on predicted triggers are not directly comparable and may overestimate the model's performance. This is the most critical weakness.
2. **Dataset Splitting Inconsistency:** The variation in train/test splits across different papers makes it difficult to directly compare results and assess true progress in the field. The authors should adhere to established splits whenever possible to ensure fair evaluation and reproducibility.
3. **Unclear Design Choices:** Several design choices lack sufficient explanation. The rationale for using a window of one word before and after the trigger is arbitrary and should be justified. Similarly, the details of positional embedding generation (randomly initialized vs. lexicalized) and the handling of left/right neighbors in the max-pooling layer are unclear.
4. **Underutilized Event Representation:** The paper introduces `v_e` (line 330) but then doesn’t explain its subsequent use, with later sections referring only to `v_{sent+lex}`. This suggests a potential inconsistency or a missed opportunity for leveraging the full event representation.
5. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** Many of the reported results are very close, and the authors do not provide any statistical significance testing to support their claims of improvement. Without such analysis, it is difficult to determine whether observed differences are meaningful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Trigger Word Impact:** How significantly would the performance degrade if the model were trained and evaluated using predicted trigger words instead of gold ones?
2. **Positional Embeddings:** How are the positional embeddings generated? Are they randomly initialized, or are they lexicalized? Why should the same relative position share an embedding across words?
3. **Left/Right Neighbor Representation:** How are left and right neighbors represented and utilized within the max-pooling layer? Does this affect only the max-pooling operation itself?
4. **v_e Usage:** How is the event representation `v_e` (line 330) ultimately used in the model? Is it integrated into the coreference scoring process in any way beyond contributing to `v_{sent+lex}`?
5. **Pairwise Feature Encoding:** Can you provide more details on how the pairwise features are encoded? Are they represented as binary vectors, and are continuous features (e.g., distance) scaled or discretized? Are the parameters associated with these features updated during training?
6. **ACE++ Release:** Is there a plan to publicly release the ACE++ dataset? Its availability would significantly benefit the research community.

**Additional Comments:**

* Line 143 contains a redundant "that."
* There is an inconsistency in baseline naming: “same type” in Table 6 vs. “same event” in the text (line 670).
* The square function nonlinearity is an interesting choice. The authors should elaborate on its novelty and potential applicability beyond this specific task.
* Consider evaluating on entity coreference datasets (e.g., OntoNotes) to provide a broader comparison with prior work.
* The paper should cite Wiseman et al. (2015) regarding their neural coreference approach, as it’s a relevant prior work.
