This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking, framing it as a coreference resolution problem and leveraging sentential features extracted via convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The model consists of two main parts: learning event representations from sentence context using CNNs, and then learning coreference decisions between event mentions based on these representations combined with pairwise features. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE 2005 and a newly created dataset, ACE++. The paper is well-written and includes a useful analysis of argument sharing within event mentions and a compelling motivation for creating the larger ACE++ dataset.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the demonstration that strong event linking performance can be achieved *without* relying on external semantic resources, a common limitation of previous approaches. The authors successfully utilize learned sentential representations to capture relevant contextual information for coreference decisions. A secondary contribution is the creation and release (or potential release – see questions) of the ACE++ dataset, providing a larger-scale resource for event coreference research. Finally, the detailed error analysis provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges in this task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Elimination of External Resources:** The most significant strength is the ability to achieve competitive results without relying on expensive and domain-specific knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet. This dramatically increases the portability and applicability of the model to new domains and languages.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing methods on the ACE 2005 dataset and achieves strong results on the new ACE++ dataset. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Thorough Error Analysis:** The authors conduct a detailed error analysis, identifying common failure cases (pronoun resolution) and potential areas for improvement. This is a valuable contribution as it pinpoints specific challenges and guides future research.
4. **ACE++ Dataset:** The creation of the ACE++ dataset addresses a key limitation of the field – the relatively small size of existing datasets. While further details are needed (see Questions), its availability will be a boon to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Architectural Justification:** The choice of CNNs for learning event representations is not adequately justified. The authors mention recent work using sentential features, but fail to motivate *why* CNNs are particularly well-suited for this task compared to alternatives like recurrent neural networks (RNNs), which are commonly used for sequential data processing. The 2-3 point F1 gains warrant a stronger argument or comparative analysis.
2. **Limited Novelty:** While the application of CNNs to event linking is a reasonable approach, the paper lacks a clear articulation of novelty. Using CNNs for sentence classification is not inherently new. The paper needs to more explicitly compare its approach to existing continuous-space event-linking methods and contextualize its contribution within the broader literature. The baseline comparison (Table 3) is also insufficient to establish strong novelty; more competitive baselines are needed.
3. **Insufficient Dataset Details (ACE++):** The creation of ACE++ is a significant contribution, but the paper lacks crucial details about its construction. What is the event type coverage? What are the potential biases introduced during data collection? A more thorough description is necessary to assess the dataset's utility and generalizability. The authors should consider releasing ACE++ to allow for independent validation.
4. **Reporting of Results:** Table 3 reports performance with gold event mentions. While justified, it obscures the performance of the system when integrated into a full event extraction pipeline. Reporting results without gold event mentions would offer a more realistic evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **ACE++ Details:** Could you provide a more detailed description of the ACE++ dataset, including its event type distribution, source, and any potential biases introduced during annotation?
2. **Dataset Release:** Do you plan to publicly release the ACE++ dataset?
3. **Architectural Alternatives:** Have you experimented with alternative architectures for learning event representations, such as RNNs or transformers? If so, how did their performance compare to the CNN-based model?
4. **Ablation Study – Pairwise Features:** Table 5 indicates pairwise features contribute to performance. Can you provide more detail on *which* specific pairwise features are most impactful and what alternatives were considered during feature selection?
5. **Joint Modeling:** Have you considered jointly modeling event trigger identification and event coreference resolution, particularly given the relatively small size of the ACE datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

- Exploring the integration of off-the-shelf coreference systems (e.g., CoreNLP) as a baseline or for augmenting the model’s features could be a valuable addition. The example on line 687 suggests this is a viable direction.
- Evaluating the model on additional resources, such as EventCorefBank, would further strengthen the evaluation.
- The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential directions for future research.
