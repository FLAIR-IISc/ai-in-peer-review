This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking, a crucial task for document-level information extraction, by leveraging sentential features extracted via convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The core idea is to move away from reliance on external knowledge bases, often domain-specific and costly to maintain, and instead learn event representations directly from the surrounding text. The model employs a two-part architecture: the first learns representations for individual event mentions using CNNs, and the second uses these representations alongside pairwise features to determine coreference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, including a newly created dataset, ACE++, and provide an error analysis to identify future research directions.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the creation and release of the ACE++ dataset. The authors convincingly motivate the need for a larger, more diverse dataset for event linking, highlighting limitations of the commonly used ACE 2005 corpus. While the paper’s performance gains are notable, the availability of a richer dataset for community research will likely have a more lasting impact. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that strong performance on event linking *can* be achieved without relying on extensive external semantic features, offering a more portable and adaptable solution for different domains. Finally, the thorough error analysis provides valuable insights into the challenges of the task, particularly regarding pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset (ACE++):** The introduction of ACE++ addresses a clear need in the research community. The authors effectively argue that the original ACE 2005 dataset is insufficient for training robust event linking models. This expanded resource will facilitate further research and more reliable evaluation of future systems. The motivation based on the statistics of ACE++ (e.g. argument incompleteness) is well-presented.
2. **Strong Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on both ACE and ACE++, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported improvements over existing methods are significant, particularly considering the avoidance of external knowledge sources.
3. **Thorough Error Analysis:** The detailed error analysis is a valuable component of the paper. Identifying specific error types, such as pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, provides concrete directions for future work and demonstrates a deep understanding of the task’s challenges.
4. **Well-Written and Motivated:** The paper is clearly written and logically structured. The introduction effectively motivates the importance of event linking and the limitations of existing approaches. The design choices of the model are well-explained and justified.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Architectural Motivation:** The choice of CNNs for generating event representations is not adequately motivated. While CNNs have been successfully applied to various NLP tasks, the paper lacks a clear justification for *why* they are particularly well-suited for capturing the relevant semantic information for event linking. A comparative analysis with other architectures, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs), would significantly strengthen the contribution. The 3-point F1 gains need stronger architectural backing.
2. **Limited Novelty:** Utilizing CNNs to process sentences for classification is not inherently novel. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing continuous-space event-linking methods. A more comprehensive comparison to related work, including a detailed discussion of how this approach builds upon or diverges from prior research, is needed. Existing methods should be baselined more thoroughly.
3. **ACE++ Dataset Details:** The details of the ACE++ dataset creation are lacking. The paper does not provide sufficient information about the process used to collect and annotate the new data, including event-type coverage, annotation guidelines, and potential biases. A clear description of these aspects is crucial for assessing the dataset’s quality and generalizability. Considering releasing the dataset to the community would be a valuable step.
4. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** Table 3 presents a comparison to existing systems, but the baseline is relatively limited. Including more recent and competitive methods, as well as a wider range of feature configurations, would provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed model.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the rationale for choosing CNNs over other neural network architectures (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) for generating event representations? Have you experimented with these alternatives, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Please provide a detailed description of the data collection and annotation process for ACE++. What measures were taken to ensure the quality and consistency of the annotations? What is the distribution of event types in ACE++?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the features used in the model, particularly the pairwise features? Which features were found to be most informative, and how were they selected? Were any alternative feature combinations explored?
4.  The error analysis highlights challenges with pronoun resolution. Are there any specific features or model modifications that you believe could improve performance on this type of error?

**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be interesting to see the model’s performance when using gold-standard entity coreference information as input, to isolate the contribution of event linking.
*   Exploring the integration of off-the-shelf coreference systems (e.g., CoreNLP) could provide a useful baseline and potentially improve performance.
*   Evaluating the model on other publicly available resources, such as EventCorefBank, would further demonstrate its generalizability.
*   Given the relatively small size of ACE, considering joint entity and event coreference experiments may be worthwhile.
