This paper presents a novel approach to event linking, a coreference resolution task for event mentions. The authors propose a system that eschews reliance on external semantic features – a common requirement in previous work – and instead leverages sentential features extracted via convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The system encodes event mentions and their contexts using CNNs, combines these representations with lexical and pairwise features, and then learns a similarity function to determine coreference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++ dataset, and provide an error analysis to guide future research.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of a purely neural approach to event linking that minimizes dependence on expensive and domain-specific external resources. By successfully achieving competitive performance using only sentential features derived from CNNs, the authors show that rich semantic knowledge can be effectively captured directly from text, reducing the barriers to applying event linking techniques to new domains.  A secondary contribution is the creation and use of the ACE++ dataset, providing a larger testbed for event linking research, although its limited availability currently restricts its impact. Finally, the detailed error analysis provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges in this field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  The move away from reliance on external knowledge bases (WordNet, FrameNet) is a significant step.  Many event linking systems are limited by the availability and coverage of these resources. This work demonstrates a viable alternative based entirely on learned representations.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The system achieves state-of-the-art or comparable performance on both the established ACE 2005 dataset and the newly introduced ACE++ dataset. While the gains are not overwhelming, they are noteworthy given the absence of hand-crafted semantic features.
3. **Thorough Model Design:** The architecture, combining CNNs for sentential encoding with pairwise features, appears well-motivated and thoughtfully designed. The use of separate pooling for left and right context around the trigger word is a particularly interesting detail.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting results and present a qualitative error analysis, identifying common failure modes (e.g., pronoun resolution) and highlighting areas for future work. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the challenges in event linking. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Gains over Feature-Rich Baselines:** While state-of-the-art performance is reported, the improvements over existing systems that *do* utilize external knowledge are relatively modest. The authors should conduct statistical significance testing to determine if the improvements are truly meaningful. 
2. **Dataset Availability & Comparison:** The ACE++ dataset is a valuable addition, but its non-public status limits its immediate utility to the research community.  Furthermore, a direct comparison to feature-rich systems on ACE++ is missing, making it difficult to assess the true benefit of the proposed approach on this larger dataset.
3. **Lack of Clarity in Architectural Details:** Several aspects of the model architecture are not fully explained. Specifically, the method of generating positional embeddings, the rationale for using a window of only one word before and after the trigger, and the encoding of pairwise features are unclear.  The role of `v_e` (line 330) also needs clarification, given that `v_{sent+lex}` appears to be used exclusively later in the pipeline.
4. **Potential Bias from Gold Triggers:**  The use of gold event triggers, while simplifying the pipeline, introduces a potential bias. Systems relying on predicted triggers may perform differently, and a fairer comparison would involve evaluating the end-to-end system, including trigger identification.
5. **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** The choice of the square function as the nonlinearity in the similarity function (line 404) is stated as having an intuition, but lacks empirical justification or comparison to alternative activation functions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on how the positional embeddings are generated? Are they learned parameters or based on a pre-defined lexical mapping?
2.  What is the specific encoding used for the pairwise features? Are continuous features (e.g., distance) discretized, and are all parameters updated during training?
3.  Why was a window size of one word before and after the trigger chosen? Was this determined empirically, or is there a theoretical justification?
4.  How is the event-mention representation `v_e` used in subsequent processing steps? It seems to be superseded by `v_{sent+lex}`.
5.  Will the ACE++ dataset be made publicly available to the research community?
