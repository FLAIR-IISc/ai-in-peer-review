- Main contribution: a purely neural event-linking system that avoids external semantic resources by using CNN-based sentential features.  
- Secondary contribution: creation of the ACE++ dataset as a larger testbed, though currently not publicly available.  
- Empirical result: reports state-of-the-art or comparable performance on ACE 2005 and ACE++, with modest gains given the lack of hand-crafted features.  
- Model design: encodes event mentions and contexts with CNNs, pools left/right context, combines with lexical and pairwise features, and learns a similarity function for coreference.  
- Strength — novelty: demonstrates a viable alternative to reliance on resources like WordNet/FrameNet.  
- Strength — empirical: competitive performance despite using only learned sentential features.  
- Strength — architecture: thoughtful design (e.g., separate left/right pooling) and well-motivated combination of features.  
- Strength — analysis: provides detailed qualitative error analysis highlighting failure modes (e.g., pronoun resolution).  
- Weakness — marginal improvements: gains over feature-rich baselines are small; significance testing is needed.  
- Weakness — dataset availability/comparison: ACE++’s non-public status and lack of direct comparisons to feature-rich systems on ACE++ limit impact.  
- Weakness — unclear architecture details: positional embeddings, one-word window choice, pairwise feature encoding, and the role of v_e are insufficiently explained.  
- Weakness — gold-trigger bias: using gold event triggers may bias results; end-to-end evaluation with predicted triggers is needed.  
- Weakness — design justification: choice of the square nonlinearity lacks empirical comparison to alternatives.  
- Question: are positional embeddings learned or from a predefined mapping?  
- Question: how are pairwise features encoded — discretized or continuous, and are all parameters trained?  
- Question: why use a window of one word before/after the trigger — empirical choice or theoretical reason?  
- Question: how is the event-mention vector v_e used downstream given v_{sent+lex} appears to be used later?  
- Question: will the ACE++ dataset be released publicly?  
- Additional comments: fix a redundant word (line 143) and inconsistent baseline naming (“same type” vs “same event”); consider citing Wiseman et al. (2015).  
- Additional suggestion: evaluate on a standard entity coreference dataset (e.g., OntoNotes) for broader comparison.
