This paper proposes a two-part model for event entity linking, leveraging sentential features extracted using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The model first generates representations for event mentions and then scores coreference pairs. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, including a newly created dataset, ACE++. The paper is well-written and includes a useful analysis motivating the creation of ACE++ by quantifying argument sharing in the original ACE corpus.

**Contributions:**

1. **ACE++ Dataset:** The creation and use of the ACE++ dataset is the strongest contribution. The analysis provided motivates the need for a larger dataset, and the expanded corpus will be valuable to the community if made publicly available. The dataset addresses a limitation of the original ACE corpus and provides a more robust evaluation resource.
2. **Empirical Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on both ACE and ACE++ demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model and provides a strong empirical baseline for future work.
3. **Sentential Feature Exploration:** While not entirely novel (see weaknesses), the paper provides a solid empirical exploration of using CNNs to generate sentential features for event coreference resolution, offering insights into the importance of different feature classes.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the event linking task and provides a strong motivation for its importance, supported by analysis of the ACE corpus. The discussion of incomplete arguments and the need for cross-sentential inference is compelling.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies to assess the contribution of different feature classes. The comparison to existing methods, while limited (see weaknesses), provides a baseline for performance.
3. **Detailed Model Description:** The model architecture is described in detail, with clear explanations of each component and the rationale behind design choices. The inclusion of figures aids in understanding the model flow.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights into the limitations of the model and suggests avenues for future research, particularly regarding pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The language is clear and concise, and the presentation of results is effective.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Architectural Justification:** The authors do not adequately motivate the choice of CNNs for generating sentential features. There's no discussion of alternative architectures, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or transformers, and no empirical comparison to demonstrate the superiority of CNNs for this specific task. The 2-3 point F1 gains, while significant, require stronger justification based on architectural properties.
2. **Limited Novelty & Baseline Comparison:** The use of CNNs over sentences for classification is not a novel contribution in itself. The paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing continuous-space event linking methods that also leverage distributional semantics. The baselines in Table 3 are limited, and the paper should demonstrate superiority over a wider range of relevant approaches.
3. **ACE++ Dataset Description & Availability:** The paper provides insufficient detail regarding the data collection process for ACE++. Information on event type coverage, potential biases, and annotation procedures is missing. Crucially, the authors should explicitly state their plans for releasing ACE++ to the research community, as this is its primary value. Without public release, the dataset's contribution is severely limited.
4. **Gold References in Table 3:** Reporting model performance *without* gold references in Table 3 hinders interpretability. While the authors discuss using gold event mentions for a fair comparison, the lack of a baseline using automatically extracted mentions makes it difficult to assess the model’s robustness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **CNN vs. RNN/Transformers:** Why were CNNs chosen over other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) for generating sentential features? Did you experiment with other architectures, and if so, what were the results?
2. **ACE++ Data Collection:** Please provide a detailed description of the ACE++ data collection process. What are the event type distributions in ACE++? What measures were taken to mitigate potential biases during annotation? What is the plan for public release of the ACE++ dataset?
3. **Error Analysis & Pronoun Resolution:** You identify pronoun resolution as a major source of error. Have you explored techniques specifically designed to address coreference resolution with pronouns, such as incorporating coreference chains or using specialized pronoun resolution models?
4. **Automatic Event Mention Detection:** Did you evaluate the performance of your model when using automatically extracted event mentions instead of gold ones?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Exploring the integration of off-the-shelf coreference systems (e.g., CoreNLP) as suggested in line 687 would be a valuable extension.
*   Evaluation on additional resources like EventCorefBank would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model's generalizability.
*   Consider joint entity/event coreference experiments given the small size of ACE.
*   Provide a more detailed analysis of Table 5, reporting the relative importance of each feature and explaining the rationale behind their selection and any alternative features explored.
