## Review for CONLL 2016: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

This paper presents a novel approach to event linking, a coreference resolution task for event mentions, leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential feature representations. The authors aim to achieve strong performance without relying on external knowledge bases, a limitation of many prior approaches.

**Summary of Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is a competitive neural model for event linking that effectively utilizes sentential features derived from CNNs. While the individual components (CNNs, word embeddings, pairwise features) are not novel, their integration within this specific task, and the demonstrated performance comparable to feature-rich baselines, represents a significant advance. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical validation of the usefulness of sentential features for event coreference, demonstrating that they can effectively compensate for the lack of external semantic resources. Finally, the creation and use of the ACE++ dataset, though currently not publicly available, provides a larger testbed for event linking research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or comparable performance on the ACE dataset, and strong results on the newly introduced ACE++ dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness. The reported performance is encouraging, particularly given the absence of extensive external features.
2. **Novel Integration of CNNs for Event Linking:** The application of CNNs to generate sentential representations of event mentions is a thoughtful approach. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in event coreference (e.g., the importance of context) and propose a plausible architecture to address them. The combination of CNN-derived features with traditional pairwise features seems well-motivated.
3. **Demonstration of Sentential Feature Effectiveness:** The paper convincingly argues for the utility of sentential features as a substitute for expensive and domain-specific external resources. This is particularly valuable for applications where broad domain coverage is required.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a series of ablation studies and comparisons, systematically evaluating the impact of different feature classes and design choices. The error analysis provides valuable insights into the model's limitations and potential areas for improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the integration is well-done, the individual components (CNNs, word embeddings, pairwise features) are well-established in the NLP literature. The novelty lies primarily in their application to this specific task, and the demonstrated performance.
2. **Lack of Public Availability of ACE++:** A key element of the evaluation is performed on the ACE++ dataset, which is not publicly available at the time of review. This hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult for other researchers to directly compare their results. A commitment to releasing this dataset would significantly strengthen the contribution of this work.
3. **Fairness of Comparison to Prior Work:** The reliance on gold trigger words, rather than predicted ones, creates an unfair comparison to systems that use predicted triggers. Errors in trigger identification can significantly impact coreference performance. Reporting results with predicted triggers, even if lower, would provide a more realistic assessment of the model's overall effectiveness.
4. **Statistical Significance:** Many of the reported results are very close. The paper lacks statistical significance testing to demonstrate whether the observed differences are truly meaningful, making it difficult to confidently assess the model's superiority.
5. **Unclear Design Choices & Missing Details:** Several aspects of the model design are unclear or lack sufficient detail. The rationale behind the choice of a window size of one for neighboring words, the encoding of pairwise features (are they learnable or fixed?), and the exact usage of the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* are all unclear and require clarification.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **ACE++ Availability:** Will the ACE++ dataset be made publicly available? If so, when?
2. **Pairwise Feature Encoding:** How are the pairwise features encoded and integrated into the model? Are the parameters associated with these features learned during training, or are they fixed?
3. **v<sub>e</sub> Usage:** How is the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* (line 330) used in subsequent processing steps? The text later references only *v<sub>sent+lex</sub>*.
4. **Square Function:** Is the use of the square function as a non-linearity novel, and if so, can you provide additional justification for its choice over other common activation functions?
5. **Trigger Prediction Impact:** Have you investigated the impact of using predicted trigger words on the model’s performance?

**Additional Comments:**

* Line 143 contains a redundant "that."
* There is an inconsistency in baseline naming: “same type” in Table 6 vs “same event” in the text (line 670).
* The authors should consider citing Wiseman et al. (2015) for relevant work on neural coreference resolution.



Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a promising approach to event linking. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the issues related to data availability, comparison fairness, statistical significance, and clarification of design choices, would significantly strengthen the contribution and impact of this work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
