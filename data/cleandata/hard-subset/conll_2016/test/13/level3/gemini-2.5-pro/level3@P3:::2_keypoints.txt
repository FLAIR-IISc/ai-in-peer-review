- Model: two-stage neural system using a CNN to encode sentential context + local lexical features, then pairwise representations with traditional features for coreference scoring.  
- Empirical claim: matches/exceeds prior state-of-the-art on ACE 2005 without external KBs (WordNet/FrameNet).  
- Dataset: introduced ACE++, a larger corpus combining ACE 2005 with 1351 newly annotated web documents.  
- Contribution 1: shows learned sentential representations can substitute for external knowledge bases for event linking.  
- Contribution 2: provides a larger training resource (ACE++) and demonstrates improved recall with more data.  
- Contribution 3: analysis showing many coreferent event pairs lack shared arguments (51%) or share different triggers (47%), motivating richer context features.  
- Strength 1: strong results (BLANC, MUC F1) achieved without external resources, improving portability across domains.  
- Strength 2: well-written, clearly motivated paper with strong data-driven justification for sentential features.  
- Strength 3: thorough ablation/component analysis showing contributions of pairwise, local-lexical, and sentential features and impact of ACE++.  
- Weakness 1: no motivation or ablation for choosing CNNs over RNNs/LSTMs, so architectural gains are unclear.  
- Weakness 2: limited novelty and incomplete comparison to recent neural/continuous-space coreference baselines; main comparisons are older systems (2010–2014).  
- Weakness 3: insufficient description of ACE++ creation (sources, annotation guidelines, annotators, event-type distribution), hindering reproducibility and bias assessment.  
- Question 1: report end-to-end performance with predicted triggers/arguments instead of gold mentions/arguments?  
- Question 2: have you considered using an off-the-shelf entity coreference system to help resolve pronouns and event arguments?  
- Question 3: have you evaluated on other resources (e.g., EventCorefBank) or considered joint entity/event coreference experiments for generalizability?  
- Question 4: which traditional pairwise features were most discriminative in the ablation, how were they selected, and were alternatives considered?
