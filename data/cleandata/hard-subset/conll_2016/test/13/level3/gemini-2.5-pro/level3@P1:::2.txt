This review assesses the paper "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks," which proposes a deep learning model for within-document event coreference resolution.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a two-stage neural model for event coreference resolution. The first stage uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with piecewise max-pooling to generate a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention, capturing features from the entire sentence in which the mention appears. This representation is combined with local features (trigger and context word embeddings). The second stage takes a pair of these event mention representations, augments them with standard pairwise features (e.g., type agreement, distance), and feeds them into a feed-forward network that computes a coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 dataset and a new, larger dataset they created called ACE++. The main claim is that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on this task without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, making it more portable across domains.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  A demonstration that a neural model using learned sentential features can achieve competitive, state-of-the-art performance on event linking, removing the dependency on external, domain-specific knowledge bases that characterized previous top-performing systems.
2.  The creation and use of a larger dataset (ACE++) which, as shown in the experiments, helps the model generalize better and improve recall significantly.
3.  A useful analysis of the ACE dataset's properties (e.g., low argument overlap among coreferent mentions) that motivates the need for context-rich sentential features, along with an insightful error analysis that identifies key challenges for future work, such as pronominal event anaphora.

**Strengths**

1.  **Effective Independence from External Knowledge Bases:** The paper's most significant strength is achieving state-of-the-art performance while deliberately avoiding features from external resources like WordNet, FrameNet, or DBpedia. Previous leading systems, such as Liu et al. (2014), relied heavily on a large set of engineered semantic features from such resources. This reliance makes them difficult to adapt to new domains where these resources may not exist or be comprehensive. By learning features directly from the text via a CNN, this work presents a more general and portable solution to event linking, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The model demonstrates competitive or superior performance against previous systems on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark across multiple metrics (BLANC, MUC). The authors provide a thorough ablation study (Table 5) that clearly demonstrates the incremental benefit of each feature class (pairwise, local lexical, and sentential), validating their model design. The finding that all three components contribute positively to the final performance is convincing.

3.  **Clear Motivation and Good Writing:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The introduction and background sections effectively motivate the problem by analyzing the ACE dataset. For instance, the statistics showing that over half of coreferential pairs share no arguments and nearly half have different trigger words provide a strong justification for moving beyond trigger- and argument-based features and incorporating richer sentential context. The creation of the ACE++ dataset to address the data sparsity of ACE is also a well-motivated and sensible step.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Architectural Justification and Comparison:** The paper's main weakness is the lack of motivation for choosing a CNN to model the sentential context. While CNNs are a reasonable choice, other architectures like RNNs (LSTMs/GRUs) are also standard for sentence modeling and arguably better suited for capturing long-range dependencies and sequential information. The paper provides no justification for its choice, nor does it compare its CNN-based approach to any other neural architecture. The reported F1 gains over the prior state-of-the-art are modest (around 3 points on BLANC). Without a comparative analysis, it is difficult to determine whether the performance gain is attributable to the specific strengths of the CNN architecture or simply to the general benefit of using a learned sentence encoder.

2.  **Unclear Novelty and Positioning:** The core technical idea—using a CNN to learn a sentence representation for a downstream classification task—is not novel in itself and has been widely used in NLP. The paper does not adequately position its contribution with respect to other neural or continuous-space models for coreference resolution (both entity and event). A comparison to other deep learning-based approaches for similar tasks would be necessary to properly assess the novelty and impact of the proposed model. The current comparison is limited to non-neural systems that rely on different feature paradigms.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on the ACE++ Dataset:** The introduction of the new ACE++ dataset is a potentially significant contribution, but the paper provides almost no information about its creation. Key details are missing, including the source of the 1351 additional web documents, the annotation guidelines and process, inter-annotator agreement, and the distribution of event types. Without these details, it is impossible to assess potential biases in the dataset or the reproducibility of the results reported on it. Furthermore, the paper reports that the model's recall improves significantly on this larger dataset, but it is unclear if this is because the new data is simply "more of the same" or if it introduces new challenges that the model successfully handles. The authors should be strongly encouraged to release this dataset.

4.  **Reliance on Gold Mentions and Underspecified Features:** The experiments rely on gold-standard event triggers and arguments. While this isolates the event linking task, it would strengthen the paper to also report performance in a more realistic setting using system-predicted mentions to understand how robust the model is to pipeline errors. Additionally, the ablation study in Table 5 shows that pairwise features remain crucial, but their selection process is not described. The paper should provide more detail on which of these features were most important and whether alternatives were considered.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on your choice of a CNN for generating event mention representations? Have you considered or experimented with other architectures, such as RNNs or LSTMs, and how might their ability to capture sequential information compare to the CNN's n-gram-based feature extraction for this specific task?

2.  Could you provide more details about the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what were the sources of the additional documents, what was the annotation procedure, and what is the distribution of event types compared to the original ACE 2005 corpus? Do you have plans to make this dataset publicly available?

3.  The error analysis highlights pronoun resolution (e.g., "It sickens me") as a key challenge. Have you considered augmenting your model with features from an off-the-shelf entity/event coreference system to help resolve these cases?

4.  Your ablation study confirms the importance of traditional pairwise features. Could you provide more insight into which of these features were most predictive and discuss the process for selecting them?
