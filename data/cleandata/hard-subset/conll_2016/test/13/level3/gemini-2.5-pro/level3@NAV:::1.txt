**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions:**
This paper proposes a neural network model for event coreference resolution. The approach uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to create vector representations of event mentions by processing word and positional embeddings from the surrounding sentence. These representations are then combined with local lexical features and pairwise features to compute a coreference score. The primary contributions are: 1) a competitive neural architecture for event linking that reduces reliance on external knowledge bases, and 2) a clear empirical demonstration that combining learned sentential features with traditional linguistic features is essential for achieving high performance.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Reduced Feature Engineering:** The model's main strength is its ability to achieve performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems without depending on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. This makes the approach more portable and less reliant on costly, domain-specific knowledge bases.
2.  **Effective Feature Combination:** The paper convincingly argues, through a well-executed ablation study (Table 5), that its strength comes from integrating three feature types: learned sentential representations, local lexical features, and pairwise features. This provides strong evidence for the authors' central claim that embeddings alone are insufficient and that a hybrid approach is superior.
3.  **Detailed Analysis:** The inclusion of an error analysis provides valuable insights into the model's limitations and remaining challenges in the field, such as pronominal coreference. The comparison to multiple baselines also helps to properly contextualize the model's performance.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Unsound Experimental Comparisons:** The claims of achieving state-of-the-art performance are weakened by significant experimental issues. The model is evaluated using gold-standard event triggers, which makes comparisons to systems that use predicted triggers unfair. Additionally, the use of different train/test splits from prior work and the lack of statistical significance testing for results that are numerically close to competitors undermine the central performance claims.
2.  **Lack of Architectural Clarity:** Several crucial details of the model's architecture are poorly explained, hindering reproducibility. The paper is unclear on the generation and rationale of positional embeddings, the exact mechanism for piecewise max-pooling, the encoding of pairwise features, and the motivation behind the one-word context window.
3.  **Reproducibility Concerns:** A large portion of the experiments are conducted on the new ACE++ dataset, but the authors do not state if it will be released. This makes a key component of the evaluation unverifiable. The related work section also notably omits a key citation for neural coreference (Wiseman et al., 2015).

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Could you clarify the novelty and provide more intuition for using a `square` function as the non-linearity for the similarity computation?
2.  Will the ACE++ dataset be made publicly available to allow for reproduction of your results and to serve as a benchmark for future work?
3.  Your final claim regarding the need for cross-sentential information (lines 65-70) is not clearly demonstrated by the proposed model, which operates on single sentences to create mention representations. Could you elaborate on this point?
