- Introduces a two-stage neural model for within-document event coreference using CNN-based sentential representations plus local lexical and pairwise features.
- Reports state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 and a new larger ACE++ dataset without relying on external KBs (WordNet/FrameNet).
- Claims that learned sentential features can match prior systems that depended on domain-specific semantic resources, improving portability.
- Builds and uses a larger ACE++ dataset to show that more training data notably improves recall and overall performance.
- Provides a thorough ablation study demonstrating complementary contributions of pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential features.
- Offers qualitative error analysis highlighting hard phenomena (pronominal anaphora) and annotation ambiguities.
- Strong empirical results (BLANC F1 73.31) showing competitiveness with prior state-of-the-art (Liu et al., 2014).
- Well-motivated, clearly written paper with useful preliminary data analysis justifying modeling choices.
- Limited technical novelty: CNN sentence encoders are established, and the paper lacks strong justification for choosing CNNs over alternatives like LSTMs.
- Insufficient transparency about ACE++: missing source, annotation methodology, and event-type distribution, hindering reproducibility and bias assessment.
- Incomplete experimental comparison: omits other neural/continuous-space baselines and evaluates only on gold-standard mentions rather than end-to-end predicted mentions.
- Requests clarification on rationale/experiments comparing CNNs vs. alternative sequential architectures (e.g., LSTMs/BiLSTMs).
- Asks for details on which traditional pairwise features were most impactful and how they were selected.
- Seeks full disclosure about ACE++ composition, distribution differences from ACE 2005, and plans for public release.
