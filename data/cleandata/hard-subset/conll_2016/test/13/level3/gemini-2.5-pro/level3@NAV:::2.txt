**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a two-part model for event coreference resolution that uses sentential features from a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to represent event mentions. The model combines these learned representations with lexical and pairwise features to produce a coreference score. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A useful analysis of the ACE 2005 dataset, which highlights challenges like low argument sharing between coreferential mentions. This analysis motivates the need for richer contextual features and provides a strong rationale for the creation of the larger ACE++ dataset.
2.  The demonstration that a neural model relying on learned sentential features, without external knowledge bases like WordNet, can achieve state-of-the-art performance on the event linking task, enhancing the model's portability to new domains.

**Strengths**

1.  **Valuable Dataset Analysis:** The paper's analysis of argument sharing and trigger word variation in the ACE dataset is insightful. It clearly establishes the limitations of relying solely on intrinsic event properties and builds a convincing case for both the use of sentential features and the necessity of a larger-scale dataset (ACE++).
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on the ACE benchmark. Crucially, it does so without relying on handcrafted semantic features from external resources, which is a significant practical advantage over many existing systems that are difficult to adapt to new domains.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and the proposed model is explained in sufficient detail, making the work easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Architectural Justification and Comparison:** The paper's most significant weakness is its failure to motivate the choice of a CNN for mention representation. No justification is provided for why a CNN is preferable to other sequence models like RNNs or LSTMs, and no comparative experiments are presented. The reported 2–3 point F1 gains are not substantial enough to be self-evident and require stronger architectural evidence.
2.  **Unclear Novelty and Insufficient Baselines:** The technical novelty is limited; applying CNNs to sentence-level classification tasks is not a new contribution. The evaluation would be much stronger if the authors compared their approach to other existing continuous-space event linking methods and included more sophisticated neural baselines in Table 3.
3.  **Inadequate Description of ACE++:** While the creation of ACE++ is a positive contribution, the paper provides no details on its data collection or annotation process. This omission makes it impossible to assess the dataset's quality, event-type coverage, or potential biases. To validate the model's improvements, the authors should demonstrate cases where prior methods fail on ACE++ but theirs succeeds. The authors should also clarify if they intend to release this new resource.
