### **Summary**

This paper proposes a neural model for within-document event coreference resolution. The model operates in two stages: first, it uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to generate a dense vector representation for each event mention based on its sentential context. This representation is combined with local lexical features. Second, it pairs these representations for two candidate event mentions, augments them with a set of traditional pairwise features (e.g., type agreement, distance), and feeds them into a final classifier to produce a coreference score. A key claim is that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without relying on external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet, which are often domain-specific. The authors also introduce and evaluate on a larger, new dataset called ACE++, which combines ACE 2005 with newly annotated web documents.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A strong empirical demonstration that neural sentence representations can replace external knowledge bases for event linking.** The primary contribution is showing that a model using learned sentential features can match or exceed the performance of prior state-of-the-art systems that depend on extensive, hand-engineered semantic features from resources like WordNet. This is a valuable result, as it suggests a path toward more portable and domain-independent event coreference systems.

2.  **Creation and analysis of a larger dataset (ACE++) for training event linking models.** The authors highlight the data limitations of the standard ACE 2005 corpus for training neural models. By creating and using the larger ACE++ dataset, they demonstrate a significant performance boost (particularly in recall), providing concrete evidence for the value of more training data for this task.

3.  **A useful analysis of the event coreference task on the ACE corpus.** The paper provides a concise but insightful analysis motivating the need for richer contextual features. By showing that a high percentage of coreferential event pairs in their data share no arguments (51%) or have different trigger words (47%), the authors clearly justify why models must look beyond simple lexical and argument overlap.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Results Without External Resources:** The model achieves state-of-the-art F1 scores on several key metrics (BLANC, MUC) on the standard ACE 2005 dataset. Achieving this while removing the dependency on external knowledge bases is a significant step forward, as it makes the approach more easily adaptable to new domains where such resources may not be available.

2.  **Well-Written and Clearly Motivated:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The motivation for using sentential features is particularly strong, supported by a data analysis (Section 3) that clearly illustrates the limitations of relying solely on local trigger or argument information.

3.  **Thorough Ablation and Component Analysis:** The experiments in Table 5 effectively break down the contribution of the different feature classes (pairwise, local-lexical, and sentential), demonstrating that each component adds value to the final model. Similarly, the analysis in Table 4 clearly shows the impact of increased training data (from ACE++) and the choice of clustering strategy.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Motivation and Comparative Analysis for the CNN Architecture:** A major weakness is the absence of any justification for choosing a CNN over other neural architectures for encoding sentence context. Recurrent models (RNNs, LSTMs) are a standard and powerful choice for sequential data and are mentioned in the related work, but no comparison is provided. Without ablating the core architectural choice, it is difficult to attribute the model's 2–3 point F1 gains to a specific, novel architectural insight versus the general representational power of neural networks. The contribution would be much stronger if the authors demonstrated why CNNs are particularly well-suited for this task.

2.  **Unclear Novelty and Limited Comparison to Relevant Baselines:** The core technical idea—using a CNN over a sentence to produce a feature vector for a classification task—is not novel, as the authors' own citations show. The novelty must therefore come from its specific application to event linking. However, the paper fails to situate itself with respect to other continuous-space or neural coreference methods. Furthermore, the main comparison in Table 3 is against systems from 2010-2014. The field has advanced since then, and the comparison would be more compelling if it included more recent or stronger baselines to establish a more definitive state-of-the-art claim.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on the ACE++ Dataset:** The ACE++ dataset is a central part of the paper's contribution and is used to train the best-performing model. However, its creation is almost entirely undescribed. Critical details are missing: What was the source of the 1351 web documents? What was the annotation process, what were the guidelines, and who were the annotators? What is the distribution of event types, and does it differ from ACE 2005? Without this information, it is impossible to assess potential biases, understand the results, or reproduce the work. The authors should provide evidence that their model succeeds on specific phenomena present in ACE++ where prior methods fail. Releasing the dataset would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The results in Table 3 are based on gold event mentions and arguments. Could you report performance in a more realistic end-to-end setting where event triggers and arguments are predicted by an upstream system? This would provide a better estimate of the model's real-world utility.

2.  Your error analysis correctly identifies pronoun resolution as a major challenge (line 687 example: "It sickens me"). Have you considered incorporating features from an off-the-shelf entity coreference system (e.g., CoreNLP) as a simple way to help resolve event arguments, which could in turn improve linking decisions?

3.  Given the small size of ACE 2005, have you considered evaluating on other event coreference resources like EventCorefBank to test the generalizability of your approach? Further, since entity and event coreference are related, have you considered experiments on joint resolution?

4.  The ablation in Table 5 shows that traditional pairwise features remain important. Could you provide more detail on which of these features were most discriminative and how this set of features was selected? Were other alternatives considered?
