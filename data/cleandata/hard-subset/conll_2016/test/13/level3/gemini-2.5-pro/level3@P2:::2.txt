### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The model operates in two stages: first, it employs a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to generate a dense, sentential representation for each event mention. This representation is combined with local lexical features around the event trigger. Second, these mention representations for a pair of events, along with a set of standard pairwise features, are fed into a trainable similarity function to produce a final coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the standard ACE 2005 dataset and a new, larger dataset they created (ACE++), reporting state-of-the-art performance without relying on external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective neural model for event linking that avoids reliance on external knowledge bases.** The primary contribution is demonstrating that a model based on learned sentential features can achieve performance competitive with previous state-of-the-art systems, which are often dependent on domain-specific semantic resources. This is a valuable result for building more portable and adaptable event processing systems.
2.  **The creation and use of a larger dataset (ACE++) to show the benefits of more training data.** The paper provides a clear motivation for needing more data and shows that their model's performance, particularly recall, improves significantly when trained on this larger set.
3.  **A useful ablation study and error analysis.** The paper includes a thorough ablation study that clearly shows the complementary contributions of pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential features. The error analysis provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges for this task, such as pronominal anaphora and annotation ambiguities.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results without External Resources:** The paper's most significant strength is its demonstration that a model using learned representations can match or exceed the performance of prior systems that require extensive feature engineering and external knowledge bases. Achieving a BLANC F1 score of 73.31 on the ACE dataset is competitive with the state-of-the-art system of Liu et al. (2014), making a strong case for the viability of end-to-end neural approaches in this domain.
2.  **Excellent Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors provide compelling motivation for their approach, supported by a preliminary data analysis of the ACE corpus (Section 3, "Data properties"). This analysis effectively highlights key challenges, such as low argument overlap and differing trigger words between coreferential mentions, which justifies the need for models that can capture broader sentential context beyond the trigger and its arguments.
3.  **Insightful Ablation and Error Analysis:** The ablation study in Table 5 provides a clear breakdown of the impact of each feature class (pairwise, local, sentential), confirming that all components contribute positively to the final performance. The qualitative error analysis in Section 5.2 is also valuable, identifying hard phenomena like event pronominal anaphora and highlighting potential annotation inconsistencies. This analysis not only contextualizes the model's performance but also offers concrete directions for future research.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty and Insufficient Architectural Justification:** The central weakness of this work is the limited technical novelty of the proposed model. Using CNNs to generate sentence representations for NLP classification tasks is a well-established technique (e.g., in work cited by the authors). The paper fails to adequately motivate the specific choice of a CNN over other prominent architectures for sequential data, such as LSTMs or other RNNs. Given that the performance gains over prior work are modest (e.g., ~2.9 F1 points on BLANC over Liu et al., 2014, on a potentially different data split), a stronger justification of the architectural choices, perhaps through comparison with alternative neural architectures, is needed to establish a more significant technical contribution.
2.  **Lack of Transparency and Reproducibility of the ACE++ Dataset:** A substantial part of the paper's experimental validation rests on the new ACE++ dataset. However, the paper provides almost no information about the source of the 1351 additional documents, the annotation methodology, or the distribution of event types. This lack of detail makes it impossible to assess potential dataset biases, understand the properties of the data on which the model excels, or reproduce the results. For the claims about improved generalization to be fully convincing, more transparency is essential, and the authors should be strongly encouraged to release this new resource.
3.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The evaluation could be strengthened in two ways. First, the comparison to prior work in Table 3 is limited and does not include other neural or continuous-space models for coreference or relation extraction, which would help better position the work's contribution. Second, the entire evaluation is conducted using gold-standard event mentions and arguments. While this is useful for isolating the performance of the linking component, it does not reflect a realistic, end-to-end application scenario. Reporting results using predicted event mentions would provide a more practical measure of the system's utility.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing a CNN to model the sentence context? Have you experimented with or considered alternative architectures, such as LSTMs or BiLSTMs, which are commonly used for capturing sequential information, and if so, how did their performance compare?
2.  The ablation study clearly indicates that the traditional pairwise features remain critical for high performance. Could you provide more detail on which of these features were most impactful? Furthermore, how was this specific set of features selected?
3.  Could you provide more details about the composition of the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what was the source of the additional documents, and does the distribution of event types differ significantly from the original ACE 2005 corpus? Do you have plans to make this dataset publicly available?
