- Paper proposes CNN-based sentential features for event linking, combined with lexical and pairwise features, claiming state-of-the-art on ACE and ACE++ without external semantic resources.
- Contribution: shows neural sentential CNN features can achieve competitive event linking performance without domain-limited external semantic features.
- Contribution: provides evidence that combining word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features outperforms embeddings alone.
- Contribution: creates and analyzes an ACE++ dataset (contribution unclear/underemphasized).
- Strength: motivated by data showing many coreferential event pairs lack shared arguments and are often cross-sentential, supporting need for sentential features.
- Strength: thorough ablation study demonstrates meaningful contributions from pairwise, trigger-local lexical, and sentential feature classes.
- Strength: competitive results on ACE (BLANC F1 73.31%) outperforming a prior system without external semantic resources.
- Weakness: experimental comparison is unfair because gold triggers are used instead of predicted triggers, unlike most prior systems.
- Weakness: ACE++ dataset is not publicly available, preventing reproducibility and verification of claimed ACE++ results.
- Weakness: multiple technical ambiguities (positional embeddings generation, arbitrary trigger window choice, encoding of pairwise features, unclear justification for cross-sentential claim).
- Weakness: many reported performance differences are small and lack statistical significance testing.
- Weakness: fails to cite Wiseman et al. (2015), a relevant neural coreference work.
- Question: Will ACE++ be made publicly available for reproducibility and comparison?
- Question: How were positional embeddings generated and why should same relative positions share embeddings across words?
- Question: Why use the square function as the nonlinearity in the similarity layer, and is this choice novel or generally applicable?
- Question: How are pairwise features encoded (binary vs. continuous) and are those parameters updated during training?
- Additional comment: minor writing issues and inconsistent baseline naming need correction.
- Additional comment: state-of-the-art claim on ACE++ is problematic without dataset access or comparisons to feature-rich systems.
- Additional comment: error analysis on pronoun resolution is useful but would benefit from more concrete examples.
