

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

**Summary:** This paper proposes a neural network approach for event linking (coreference resolution for event mentions) that uses convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features. The model processes event mentions and their context through CNNs to create representations that are combined with lexical and pairwise features for coreference scoring. The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on ACE and an expanded ACE++ dataset without relying on external semantic features.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):** 
1. Demonstrating that a neural approach using sentential features from CNNs can achieve competitive performance on event linking without requiring external semantic features (which are domain-limited).
2. Providing empirical evidence that a combination of word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features outperforms embeddings alone.
3. Creating and analyzing the ACE++ dataset (though this isn't clearly presented as a contribution).

**Strengths:**
1. The core idea of using neural networks to generate sentential features for event linking is well-motivated by the authors' data analysis showing that 50.97% of coreferential event-mention pairs share no arguments and 55.42% are more than five sentences apart. This strongly supports the need for sentential features.
2. The careful ablation study (Table 5) demonstrates that each feature class (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, sentential) contributes meaningfully to performance, providing strong evidence for their design choices.
3. The model achieves competitive results on a standard dataset (ACE) without relying on external semantic resources, making it more broadly applicable than previous feature-rich approaches. Their F1 score (73.31%) on BLANC outperforms Liu et al.'s (2014) system (70.43%) despite not using external semantic features.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The experimental comparison is unfair due to the use of gold trigger words instead of predicted ones. Most systems use predicted triggers, so the comparison doesn't reflect a realistic setting. The authors acknowledge this caveat but don't address how their approach would perform with predicted triggers.
2. The ACE++ dataset is not publicly available, preventing reproducibility and making comparisons to other systems on this dataset impossible. The paper claims state-of-the-art performance on ACE++, but without access to the dataset, this claim cannot be verified.
3. The paper contains multiple technical ambiguities: 
   - The generation of positional embeddings (randomly initialized or lexicalized?) is unclear
   - The rationale for choosing exactly one word before and after the trigger (arbitrary window size)
   - The encoding of pairwise features (binary vector? how continuous features like distance are handled)
   - The claim about needing cross-sentential information (lines 65-70) lacks clear explanation
4. The paper reports many close performance numbers (e.g., 73.31 vs 72.19 in Table 4) without significance testing, making it difficult to assess the statistical importance of their improvements.
5. The paper fails to cite Wiseman et al. (2015), who proposed a neural coreference approach, which is highly relevant to their work.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. Will the ACE++ dataset be made publicly available to enable reproducibility and further research?
2. How were the positional embeddings generated (randomly initialized or lexicalized)? Why should the same relative position share an embedding across different words?
3. What is the rationale for using the square function as the nonlinearity in the similarity layer, and is this novel or broadly applicable?
4. How exactly are pairwise features encoded (binary vector or continuous features), and are these parameters updated during training?

**Additional Comments:** The paper contains minor issues including a redundant "that" on line 143 and inconsistent baseline naming ("same type" in Table 6 vs "same event" in text on line 670). The claim that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on ACE++ is problematic since the paper doesn't provide comparisons to feature-rich systems on this dataset. The error analysis identifying pronoun resolution as a key challenge is valuable but would be strengthened with more concrete examples.
