

This paper proposes a neural model for event coreference resolution that uses convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features, which are combined with lexical and pairwise features to compute coreference scores. The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on ACE and ACE++ datasets without relying on external semantic features.

Main contributions as I see them: (1) A neural architecture for event linking using CNNs to capture sentential context, (2) Empirical demonstration that embeddings alone are insufficient for event linking (though with questionable experimental setup), and (3) The ACE++ dataset extension (though not publicly available).

Strengths: The model addresses an important problem in event coreference resolution by reducing dependency on external semantic resources. The error analysis provides valuable insights into the challenges of pronoun resolution and boundary cases. The results on the ACE dataset show promising performance comparable to feature-rich systems.

Weaknesses: The most significant issue is the use of gold trigger words instead of predicted ones, making comparisons with other systems unfair. The ACE++ dataset is not publicly available, and comparisons to feature-rich systems on this dataset are missing. Many implementation details are unclear, including the generation of positional embeddings, the arbitrary choice of one-word context window, and the handling of pairwise features. The paper lacks significance testing for the close performance numbers. The claim about the need for cross-sentential information is not clearly explained, and the model's reliance on sentence-level features is not sufficiently justified.

Questions to authors: How exactly are positional embeddings generated (randomly initialized or lexicalized)? What is the rationale for choosing exactly one word before and after the trigger as context? Will the ACE++ dataset be made publicly available?

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field but is significantly weakened by experimental flaws and unclear implementation details that undermine the validity of the claims. While the core idea is interesting, the execution does not fully support the stated contributions.
