

Summary: This paper proposes a model for event linking that uses sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) instead of external semantic resources. The authors present a two-step architecture where CNNs generate representations for individual event mentions, which are then combined with pairwise features for coreference scoring. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 and a new dataset called ACE++, which the authors created to overcome the limited size of ACE 2005. The main contributions of this work, as seen by the reviewer, are: (1) demonstrating that event linking can achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources, (2) creating and releasing ACE++, a substantially larger dataset for event linking that enables better generalization, and (3) providing an error analysis that identifies key challenges for future work, particularly pronoun resolution.

Strengths:
1. The model's achievement of state-of-the-art performance without external semantic resources is a significant contribution for domain portability. The paper shows that systems can operate in domains where WordNet or FrameNet are unavailable, which addresses a major limitation of previous approaches. The authors' analysis of the ACE 2005 corpus showing that 23% of event mentions require cross-sentence resolution further justifies the importance of this work.

2. The creation of ACE++, a larger dataset with 1950 documents (compared to ACE 2005's 599), is well-motivated and valuable for the community. The paper demonstrates that increasing training data substantially improves recall (from 75.16% to 83.21% on ACE++) while maintaining competitive precision, providing empirical evidence that data quantity matters for this task. The release of this dataset is particularly valuable for future research.

3. The error analysis provides meaningful insights into the limitations of current approaches, particularly highlighting the difficulty of pronoun resolution. The examples of false negatives involving pronouns ("It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me.") demonstrate a fundamental challenge that remains unsolved in the field, which will guide future research directions.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks a clear justification for using CNNs over other neural architectures. The authors do not compare CNNs to RNNs or other sequence models, which are commonly used for sentence representation. The 2-3 point F1 gains over prior work require stronger architectural evidence to justify the choice of CNNs as the primary contribution.

2. The novelty claim is weak. Using CNNs for sentence representation in classification tasks is not new, and the paper does not sufficiently compare to existing continuous-space event-linking methods. The baseline comparisons in Table 3 are limited to older, feature-based approaches, while more recent neural methods for event linking are not included, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the approach.

3. The ACE++ dataset construction is inadequately described. The paper does not provide details about event-type coverage, annotation consistency, or potential biases in the web documents. Without this information, it's unclear whether ACE++ is representative of real-world text or if it has specific biases that might affect model performance. The authors should clarify these aspects and consider releasing ACE++ to the community.

4. The paper does not report performance with gold references in Table 3, which is standard practice for coreference evaluation. This omission makes it difficult to assess the contribution of the proposed model versus the extraction pipeline.

5. The paper does not explore augmenting the model with off-the-shelf coreference systems (e.g., CoreNLP), as suggested in line 687. Given that the authors' approach is designed for the linking step, integrating with existing coreference systems could provide valuable insights about the model's utility in a full pipeline.

Questions to Authors:
1. What specific characteristics of event mentions make CNNs more suitable than RNNs for this task, and how did you determine this through experimentation?
2. Can you provide more details about the ACE++ dataset creation process, including event-type coverage, document sources, and annotation consistency checks?
3. How would you evaluate the model on additional resources like EventCorefBank, which has been used in prior event linking work?
4. Could you discuss the potential benefits of jointly resolving entity and event coreference, as suggested in the conclusion?
