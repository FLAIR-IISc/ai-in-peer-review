

Summary: This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event linking that utilizes sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) instead of relying on external semantic features like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors present a two-step model: first, CNNs process coreference candidates and their context to generate latent representations; second, these representations are combined with lexical and pairwise features to produce a coreference score. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, including a newly created ACE++ dataset with 1351 additional web documents.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. A neural approach for event linking that eliminates the need for external semantic resources, making it more domain-agnostic.
2. Demonstration that sentential features from CNNs are sufficient to achieve state-of-the-art performance on event linking.
3. Empirical evidence that more training data significantly improves recall without substantially harming precision.

Strengths:
1. The model's performance on ACE++ demonstrates that the approach scales well with additional training data, achieving 83.21% recall compared to 75.16% on the smaller ACE dataset. This shows the method's potential for real-world applications where more data is available.
2. The authors provide a thorough error analysis that identifies specific challenges (e.g., pronoun resolution), which is valuable for future research directions.
3. The model outperforms prior work that relies on external semantic features (Liu et al., 2014) while eliminating the need for domain-specific knowledge bases, making it more broadly applicable.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation uses gold-standard trigger words, while most prior work uses predicted triggers. This makes comparisons unfair, as the authors' approach could be significantly worse when integrated into a full pipeline with error-prone trigger identification.
2. The ACE++ dataset is not publicly available, limiting reproducibility and preventing comparison with other systems on this dataset. The paper claims state-of-the-art results on ACE++, but without public data, these results cannot be verified or built upon.
3. The paper lacks statistical significance testing for performance improvements. The differences in results (e.g., 73.31% vs. 72.19% F1) could be within the margin of error, making it difficult to assess the true impact of the proposed approach.
4. Several technical details are unclear: the generation of positional embeddings (randomly initialized or lexicalized), the rationale for using exactly one word before and after the trigger, and how pairwise features are encoded and handled during training.
5. The claim about the need for cross-sentential information (lines 65-70) isn't clearly explained or supported by the data presented.

Questions to Authors:
1. Will the ACE++ dataset be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and future comparisons?
2. Why were exactly one word before and after the trigger used, and is this choice justified by ablation studies?
3. How are relative positional embeddings generated (randomly initialized or lexicalized), and why should the same relative position share an embedding across all words?
4. What statistical tests were performed to confirm the significance of the performance improvements over prior work?

The paper presents an interesting approach that eliminates the need for external semantic resources in event linking. While the results are promising, the lack of public data, unclear technical details, and unfair comparison setup with gold triggers significantly weaken the contribution's impact. The authors should address these issues to strengthen their claim that their approach is both novel and practically superior.
