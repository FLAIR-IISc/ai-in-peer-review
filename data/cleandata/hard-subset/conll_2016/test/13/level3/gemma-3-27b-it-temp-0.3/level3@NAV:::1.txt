## Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

This paper presents a neural approach to event linking, leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions. The core idea is to move away from reliance on external knowledge bases and instead utilize learned representations of context to determine coreference. The model combines CNN-derived representations with lexical and pairwise features to compute a coreference score. The authors report competitive performance on the ACE 2005 and a new, expanded ACE++ dataset.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating that strong performance in event linking can be achieved *without* extensive external semantic features, relying instead on a well-designed neural architecture and learned representations. While CNNs have been applied to relation/event extraction, this work specifically focuses on event *linking* and shows the effectiveness of combining CNNs with a limited set of linguistic features. A secondary contribution is the introduction of the ACE++ dataset, although its non-public availability limits its immediate impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves results comparable to feature-rich systems on the publicly available ACE dataset, indicating a viable alternative to traditional approaches.
*   **Reduced Reliance on External Resources:** The core premise of avoiding external knowledge bases is valuable, making the approach more portable and applicable to diverse domains.
*   **Clear Model Architecture:** The paper provides a relatively clear description of the CNN-based feature extraction process and the subsequent coreference scoring mechanism.
*   **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights into the challenges of event linking, particularly regarding pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Unfair Comparison:** The use of gold trigger words during evaluation introduces a significant bias, making direct comparison to systems that predict triggers unfair. This artificially inflates performance.
*   **Dataset Splitting Inconsistency:** The authors should adhere to established train/test splits used in prior work to ensure fair and reproducible comparisons.
*   **Lack of Clarity:** Several aspects of the model are unclear, including the generation of positional embeddings, the representation of left/right neighbors, the rationale for the one-word window around the trigger, and the usage of the `v_e` representation.
*   **Missing Statistical Significance:** The numerous results presented lack statistical significance testing, making it difficult to determine whether observed differences are meaningful.
*   **ACE++ Accessibility:** The lack of public availability of the ACE++ dataset hinders reproducibility and broader impact.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How would the model’s performance change if predicted trigger words were used instead of gold ones? Please provide results with predicted triggers.
2.  Can you provide more details on the generation of positional embeddings? Are they learned or pre-defined, and why is a shared embedding used for relative positions?
3.  What is the precise mechanism for incorporating left and right neighbor information, and how does it affect the max-pooling operation?
4.  What is the purpose of the `v_e` representation mentioned in line 330, and why is it not used in subsequent sections?
5.  How are the pairwise features encoded (e.g., binary vector), and are the parameters for these features updated during training?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper should cite Wiseman et al. (2015) on neural coreference.
*   Line 143 contains a redundant "that."
*   There is an inconsistency in baseline naming ("same type" vs. "same event").
