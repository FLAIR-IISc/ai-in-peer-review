## Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

This paper presents a two-part model for event coreference resolution, leveraging sentential features extracted via convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to avoid reliance on external knowledge bases. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++ dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results. The work is well-written and the motivation for creating ACE++ – addressing dataset size limitations and argument incompleteness in ACE – is clearly articulated and valuable. The analysis of argument sharing in ACE is also a useful contribution.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the creation and release of the ACE++ dataset. While the model itself shows competitive performance, the availability of a larger, more comprehensive dataset for event coreference will be a significant asset to the research community. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that strong performance can be achieved *without* relying on external semantic resources, a notable advantage for domain portability. Finally, the detailed error analysis provides valuable insights into the challenges of event coreference, particularly regarding pronoun resolution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Dataset:** The creation of ACE++ is a significant contribution, addressing a clear need in the field for larger, more representative datasets.
*   **Strong Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on both ACE and ACE++, demonstrating its effectiveness.
*   **Avoidance of External Resources:** The model's ability to perform well without relying on external knowledge bases is a key advantage.
*   **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights into the challenges of event coreference and suggests directions for future research.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Architectural Justification:** The choice of CNNs for generating sentential features is not adequately motivated. A comparison to other architectures, such as RNNs or Transformers, is crucial to demonstrate the benefits of using CNNs. The reported 2-3 point F1 gains are insufficient justification without such a comparison.
*   **Limited Novelty:** Utilizing CNNs over sentences for classification is not a novel contribution in itself. The paper needs to more clearly differentiate itself from existing continuous-space event linking methods and provide a more thorough comparison to them. The baseline comparison in Table 3 is limited.
*   **ACE++ Dataset Details:** The paper lacks a detailed description of the ACE++ data collection process. Information regarding event type coverage, potential biases introduced during annotation, and inter-annotator agreement is essential for assessing the dataset's quality and reliability. Releasing the dataset is valuable, but transparency about its creation is paramount.
*   **Missing Ablation Study:** While Table 5 provides some insight, a more comprehensive ablation study exploring the contribution of each feature class would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you provide more details about the ACE++ data collection process, including event type coverage, annotation guidelines, and inter-annotator agreement?
*   What were the primary reasons for choosing CNNs over other neural network architectures (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) for generating sentential features? Could you provide a brief comparison of performance with alternative architectures?
*   Would you consider releasing the ACE++ dataset to the research community?
