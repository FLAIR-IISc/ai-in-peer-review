**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a model for event linking, a task that involves resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document. The model uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features from event mentions and their context, which are then combined with pairwise features to compute a coreference score. The authors claim that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, without relying on external feature sources.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural approach to event linking**: The authors propose a novel approach to event linking that combines word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features using CNNs. This approach is distinct from previous methods that rely on external feature sources or hand-crafted features.
2. **Use of sentential features**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sentential features generated by CNNs in capturing contextual information that is relevant for event linking.
3. **Competitive performance**: The authors show that their model achieves performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems on ACE and ACE++ datasets.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to event linking that leverages the strengths of CNNs in capturing contextual information.
2. **Competitive performance**: The authors demonstrate that their model achieves performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems on ACE and ACE++ datasets.
3. **Analysis of errors**: The authors provide a detailed analysis of errors made by their model, which sheds light on the challenges of event linking and provides insights for future research.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Experimental concerns**: The authors use gold trigger words instead of predicted ones, which makes comparisons to systems using predicted triggers unfair. Additionally, different papers use different train/test splits, which can affect the validity of comparisons.
2. **Lack of clarity**: The authors do not clearly explain how positional embeddings are generated, how left vs right neighbors are represented/used, and why exactly one word before and after the trigger are appended to the representation.
3. **Dataset concerns**: ACE++ is not a public dataset, which makes it difficult to compare the authors' results to those of other researchers. Additionally, comparisons to feature-rich systems on ACE++ are missing.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how positional embeddings are generated and why the same relative position should share an embedding across words?
2. How do you represent and use left vs right neighbors in the model, and is this only affecting max-pooling?
3. Why do you append exactly one word before and after the trigger to the representation, and is this an arbitrary window choice?
4. Can you clarify how the event-mention representation v_e is used, since later sections reference only v_{sent+lex}?
5. How do you encode pairwise features in section 3.2, and are these parameters updated during training?

**Additional Comments**

1. The authors should consider evaluating their model on entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes) to compare with more prior work.
2. The authors should provide more details on the square function nonlinearity and its novelty and broad applicability.
3. The authors should consider releasing ACE++ as a public dataset to facilitate comparisons with other researchers.
4. The authors should provide significance testing to support their claims of competitive performance.
