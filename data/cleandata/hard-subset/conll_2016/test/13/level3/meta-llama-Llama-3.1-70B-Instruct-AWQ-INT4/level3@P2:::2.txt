**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a model for event linking, a task that involves resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document. The model uses sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to represent event mentions and a two-part architecture to compute coreference scores. The authors claim that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, without relying on external feature sources.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel use of sentential CNN features**: The authors propose using CNNs to generate sentential features for event mention representation, which is a new approach in the field of event linking.
2. **Two-part model architecture**: The authors design a two-part model that first computes event mention representations and then uses these representations to compute coreference scores.
3. **State-of-the-art performance**: The authors claim that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, without relying on external feature sources.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-written paper**: The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their model and experiments.
2. **Useful analysis**: The authors provide a useful analysis of argument sharing in ACE and motivate the creation of ACE++ by highlighting the limitations of ACE.
3. **Strong performance**: The authors demonstrate strong performance on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, which suggests that their model is effective.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of architectural evidence**: The authors do not provide sufficient evidence to justify the choice of CNNs for mention representations, and they do not compare their approach to other architectures, such as RNNs.
2. **Unclear novelty**: The use of CNNs over sentences for classification is not new, and the authors do not provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate the novelty of their approach.
3. **Limited baselines**: The authors do not provide a comprehensive set of baselines in Table 3, which makes it difficult to evaluate the performance of their model.
4. **Lack of description of ACE++**: The authors do not provide a detailed description of ACE++, including event-type coverage and biases, which makes it difficult to evaluate the performance of their model on this dataset.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more evidence to justify the choice of CNNs for mention representations, and how did you compare your approach to other architectures, such as RNNs?
2. How do you respond to the criticism that the use of CNNs over sentences for classification is not new, and what evidence do you have to demonstrate the novelty of your approach?
3. Can you provide a more comprehensive set of baselines in Table 3, including other continuous-space event-linking methods?
4. Can you provide a detailed description of ACE++, including event-type coverage and biases, and how did you collect this data?
5. How do you plan to address the limitations of your model, including the lack of fine-grained information about discourse structure and the problematic case of pronoun resolution?
