This paper proposes a novel approach to event entity linking using sentential CNN features and a two-part model for mention representation and coreference scoring. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, one of which is publicly available.

The authors' contributions are significant, as they design a system for event linking that does not rely on external semantic features but instead utilizes sentential features from convolutional neural networks. The model's performance is impressive, especially considering that it does not require extensive external semantic features.

The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise explanation of the model and its components. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the argument sharing in ACE and motivate the creation of ACE++ by highlighting the limitations of the original dataset. The error analysis is also insightful, pointing out directions for further research.

However, there are several major issues with the paper. Firstly, the authors do not motivate or analyze why CNNs were chosen for mention representations, and they provide no comparisons with other architectures, such as RNNs. The 2-3 point F1 gains reported in the paper need stronger architectural evidence to be convincing.

Secondly, the novelty of the paper is unclear. Using CNNs over sentences for classification is not a new approach, and the authors should compare their method to existing continuous-space event-linking methods and expand the baselines in Table 3.

Thirdly, the ACE++ data collection is not described in detail, leaving unclear the event-type coverage and biases in the dataset. The authors should show that earlier methods fail where theirs succeeds and consider releasing ACE++ to facilitate further research.

In terms of minor issues, the authors should report model performance without gold references in Table 3. Additionally, they could explore augmenting the model with off-the-shelf coreference systems, such as CoreNLP, as in the line-687 example. Evaluating the model on additional resources, such as EventCorefBank, and considering joint entity/event coreference experiments given ACE's small size would also be beneficial.

Finally, Table 5 implies that pairwise features remain important, and the authors should report which features mattered, how they were chosen, and what alternatives were tried.
