**Summary**

This paper proposes a neural approach to event linking, a task that involves resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document. The model uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, which are combined with pairwise features to compute a coreference score. The authors claim that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE and ACE++ datasets without relying on external semantic features.

**Main Contributions**

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. A neural approach to event linking that combines word embeddings with sentential features generated by CNNs.
2. The use of a small set of linguistic features, which is sufficient to achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on extensive external semantic features.
3. The evaluation of the model on the ACE and ACE++ datasets, which shows that the approach is effective in resolving event coreferences.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to event linking that leverages the strengths of neural networks in generating sentential features.
2. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE and ACE++ datasets, which demonstrates its effectiveness in resolving event coreferences.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model, including an analysis of the impact of different feature classes and clustering strategies.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper uses gold-annotated event mentions, which may not be realistic in practice where event mentions are typically predicted by a separate model.
2. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the final claim about the need for cross-sentential information is supported by their results.
3. The paper lacks significance testing to support the claims about the performance of the model.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of using gold-annotated event mentions in your model, and what are the implications of using predicted event mentions instead?
2. Can you provide more details about how the sentential features are generated by the CNNs, including the size of the convolution windows and the number of filters used?
3. How do you plan to extend your model to handle cross-document event coreference resolution, and what are the challenges that you anticipate in this task?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the CNN architecture used to generate sentential features.
2. The authors should consider evaluating their model on other datasets, such as OntoNotes, to demonstrate its generalizability.
3. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the related work in event coreference resolution, including the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

**Rating**

Based on the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, I would give it a rating of 7 out of 10. The paper proposes a novel approach to event linking and achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE and ACE++ datasets. However, it has some limitations, including the use of gold-annotated event mentions and the lack of significance testing. With some revisions to address these limitations, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
