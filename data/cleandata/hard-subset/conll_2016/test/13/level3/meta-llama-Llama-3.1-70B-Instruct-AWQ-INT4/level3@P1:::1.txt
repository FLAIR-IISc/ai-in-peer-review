This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking, a crucial task in natural language processing, by leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features. The authors design a two-part model that first learns event-mention representations using CNNs and then combines these representations with pairwise features to compute a coreference score. The main contribution of this work is the neural approach that combines word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features, demonstrating that embeddings alone are insufficient for effective event linking.

The paper presents several key strengths. Firstly, the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE and ACE++ datasets, outperforming prior feature-rich systems. This is a significant accomplishment, as it shows that the model can effectively capture the nuances of event linking without relying on extensive external feature sources. Secondly, the authors provide a thorough analysis of the model's performance, including an error analysis that highlights the challenges of pronoun resolution and the need for more fine-grained information about discourse structure.

However, there are also some weaknesses and areas for improvement. One concern is the use of gold trigger words instead of predicted ones, which may make comparisons to systems using predicted triggers unfair. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more clarity on certain aspects, such as the generation of positional embeddings, the representation of left and right neighbors, and the encoding of pairwise features.

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could consider evaluating their model on entity coreference tasks, such as OntoNotes, to compare with more prior work. They could also explore the use of other neural architectures, such as recursive or recurrent networks, to generate sentential features. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to release the ACE++ dataset to facilitate comparisons with feature-rich systems.

In terms of questions to the authors, it would be interesting to know more about the novelty and broad applicability of the square function nonlinearity used in the model. Additionally, the authors could provide more information on the handling of continuous features like distance and whether these parameters are updated during training.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing, and with some minor revisions and clarifications, it has the potential to make an even more substantial impact.
