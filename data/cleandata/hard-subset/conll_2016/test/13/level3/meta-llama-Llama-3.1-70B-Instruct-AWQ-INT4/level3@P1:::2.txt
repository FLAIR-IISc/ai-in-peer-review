**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a model for event linking, which is the task of resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document. The model uses sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to represent event mentions and a two-part architecture to compute coreference scores. The authors claim that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, one of which is publicly available, without relying on external semantic features.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors design a system for event linking that uses sentential features from CNNs to represent event mentions.
2. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, one of which is publicly available.
3. The authors present an analysis of the system errors to identify directions for further research.

**Strengths**

1. The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed model and its components.
2. The authors provide a useful analysis of argument sharing in the ACE dataset and motivate the creation of the ACE++ dataset.
3. The model achieves competitive performance on the ACE dataset, outperforming some state-of-the-art systems.

**Weaknesses**

1. The authors do not provide a clear motivation for choosing CNNs over other architectures, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs), for mention representations.
2. The novelty of the paper is unclear, as using CNNs over sentences for classification is not a new idea.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed description of the ACE++ dataset, including its event-type coverage and biases.
4. The paper lacks comparisons to existing continuous-space event-linking methods and does not expand the baselines in Table 3.
5. The authors do not report model performance without gold references in Table 3.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why you chose CNNs over other architectures for mention representations?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of pronoun resolution, which is a challenging problem in event linking?
3. Can you provide more information about the ACE++ dataset, including its event-type coverage and biases?
4. Have you considered comparing your model to existing continuous-space event-linking methods?
5. Can you provide more details about the features used in the pairwise comparison, including how they were chosen and what alternatives were tried?

**Additional Comments**

1. The authors may want to consider evaluating their model on additional resources, such as the EventCorefBank corpus.
2. The authors may want to explore augmenting their model with off-the-shelf coreference systems, such as CoreNLP.
3. The authors may want to consider joint entity/event coreference experiments given the small size of the ACE dataset.
