**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a neural approach to event linking, a task that involves resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document. The model uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features from event mentions and their context, which are then combined with pairwise features to compute a coreference score. The approach does not rely on external semantic features, making it more applicable to domains where such resources are limited. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, and outperforms previous feature-rich systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. A neural approach to event linking that combines word embeddings with sentential features generated by CNNs.
2. A two-part model architecture that first generates event-mention representations and then computes a coreference score based on these representations and pairwise features.
3. State-of-the-art performance on ACE and ACE++ datasets without relying on external semantic features.

**Strengths**

1. The model's ability to generate sentential features from event mentions and their context using CNNs is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The approach's independence from external semantic features makes it more applicable to domains where such resources are limited.
3. The model's state-of-the-art performance on ACE and ACE++ datasets demonstrates its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses**

1. The use of gold trigger words instead of predicted ones may make the model's performance less comparable to systems that use predicted triggers.
2. The different train/test splits used in the paper and other studies may affect the comparability of results.
3. The unclear explanation of the final claim about the need for cross-sentential information and the unclear generation of positional embeddings may require further clarification.
4. The lack of significance testing to support claims may make it difficult to determine the statistical significance of the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify the final claim about the need for cross-sentential information and provide more details on how it is incorporated into the model?
2. How are positional embeddings generated, and why do you use the same relative position to share an embedding across words?
3. Can you provide more details on how left vs. right neighbors are represented and used in the model?
4. Why do you append exactly one word before and after the trigger to the representation, and is this an arbitrary window choice?
5. Can you clarify how the event-mention representation v_e is used, and why it is not referenced in later sections?
6. How are pairwise features encoded in section 3.2, and are these parameters updated during training?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the CNN architecture and the generation of sentential features.
2. The use of a more robust evaluation metric, such as BLANC, is a positive aspect of the paper.
3. The comparison to previous feature-rich systems is a valuable contribution to the field.
4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed error analysis to identify areas for further improvement.
