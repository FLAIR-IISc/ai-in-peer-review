**Paper Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks**

**Summary of the Paper:**
The paper proposes a novel approach to event linking, a crucial task in information extraction, using sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors design a two-part model, where the first part generates latent feature representations for event mentions using CNNs, and the second part uses these representations along with pairwise features to make coreference decisions. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, without relying on external semantic features.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel use of CNNs for event linking**: The authors propose a new approach to event linking using CNNs, which has not been explored before.
2. **Two-part model architecture**: The authors design a two-part model that effectively combines the strengths of CNNs and pairwise features for event linking.
3. **State-of-the-art performance**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, without relying on external semantic features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Effective use of CNNs**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of using CNNs for generating latent feature representations for event mentions.
2. **Improved performance**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, without relying on external semantic features.
3. **Robust evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model, including comparisons with state-of-the-art systems and analysis of the impact of different feature classes.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of motivation for CNNs**: The authors do not provide a clear motivation for choosing CNNs over other neural architectures, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or recursive neural networks (RNNs).
2. **Limited novelty**: The use of CNNs for classification tasks is not new, and the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from existing methods.
3. **Unclear evaluation of ACE++**: The authors do not provide a clear description of the ACE++ dataset, including its event-type coverage and biases.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a clear motivation for choosing CNNs over other neural architectures?
2. How does your approach differ from existing methods that use CNNs for classification tasks?
3. Can you provide a more detailed description of the ACE++ dataset, including its event-type coverage and biases?
