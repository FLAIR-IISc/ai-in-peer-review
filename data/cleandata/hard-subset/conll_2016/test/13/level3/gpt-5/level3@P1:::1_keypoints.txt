- Summary: Proposes a resource-light within-document event coreference model using sentence-level CNNs, trigger-local cues, pairwise features, a learned similarity (square nonlinearity), and transitive closure clustering.
- Summary: Matches feature-rich systems on ACE and improves recall on an expanded ACE++ corpus; ablations show sentential features are key; error analysis highlights pronominal references and annotation issues.

- Contribution 1: Demonstrates sentential CNN features plus local/pairwise features can match prior feature-rich systems on ACE without external KBs.
- Contribution 2: Presents an architecture (piecewise CNN encoder, squared similarity layer) and analysis of design choices and clustering strategies with ablation studies.
- Contribution 3: Introduces a larger ACE++ corpus and provides a manual error analysis, though the dataset is not publicly available.

- Strength: Strong resource-light performance—state-of-the-art or comparable on ACE and scalable recall gains with more data on ACE++.
- Strength: Clear ablations showing sentential features materially boost precision and recall; transitive closure vs. BestLink comparison is well-motivated.
- Strength: Good engineering transparency—training details, hyperparameter exploration, and implementation choices are reported.
- Strength: Informative error analysis identifies hard phenomena (e.g., pronominal event references) and annotation inconsistencies.
- Strength: Practical motivation—avoiding external semantic features improves cross-domain applicability.

- Weakness: Evaluation fairness—uses gold triggers and varying ACE splits; lacks predicted-trigger experiments, standardized splits, and statistical significance testing.
- Weakness: Moderate novelty—CNN encoders and neural coref ideas are common; only modest methodological novelty beyond the squared nonlinearity and feature combo.
- Weakness: ACE++ validity limited—dataset not public and no comparisons to strong baselines on ACE++, reducing reproducibility and external validation.
- Weakness: Underspecified model details—positional embeddings initialization/vocabulary/rationale, left/right context handling, fixed one-token context window choice, and ambiguity in representation flow (v_e vs. v_sent+lex).
- Weakness: Pairwise features underspecified—binary vs. continuous encoding, distance normalization, and whether embeddings/weights are trained end-to-end are unclear.
- Weakness: Cross-sentential claims under-supported—paper lacks quantitative analysis of performance by sentence distance or argument overlap.

- Question: How are positional embeddings initialized, indexed, and justified for sharing across identical relative positions; were alternative position encodings tried?
- Question: Does the left/right split affect only piecewise max-pooling, and how are immediate neighbor embeddings combined with vsent?
- Question: Why use a fixed one-token trigger-local window; were larger windows, learned attention, or character/subword features evaluated?
- Question: What is the explicit role of v_e vs. v_sent+lex in the pairwise scoring stage—does v_e get used or replaced?
- Question: How are continuous pairwise features (e.g., distance) scaled/normalized and are they learned end-to-end via embeddings/MLPs?
- Question: Is the square nonlinearity for similarity novel here; were comparisons made to alternatives (ReLU/tanh, absolute difference/product, bilinear, cosine) with significance testing?
- Question: Will ACE++ splits and code be released, and can the authors provide comparisons to strong feature-rich baselines on ACE++?
- Question: Can experiments be rerun on a standard ACE split and with predicted triggers, and can bootstrap or other statistical tests be provided?
- Question: Have you considered evaluating on entity coreference benchmarks (e.g., OntoNotes) or comparing to neural coref baselines (e.g., Wiseman et al., 2015)?

- Additional comment: Add statistical significance testing for ACE metrics (especially BLANC F1) before claiming state-of-the-art.
- Additional comment: Cite and contrast with Wiseman et al. (2015) and situate the similarity function relative to common neural matching functions.
- Additional comment: Minor fixes—remove redundant wording (line 143), and standardize baseline naming (e.g., “same type” vs. “same event”).
- Additional comment: Provide a breakdown of performance by sentence distance and argument-overlap strata to strengthen cross-sentential claims.
