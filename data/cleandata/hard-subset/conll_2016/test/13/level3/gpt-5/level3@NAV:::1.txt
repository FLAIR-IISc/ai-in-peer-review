Summary and contributions
- Primary: A neural event coreference model that integrates sentential CNN features with a small set of lexical and pairwise features, demonstrating that embeddings alone are insufficient but, when combined with lightweight linguistic features, can match feature-rich systems.
- Secondary: An empirical study on ACE and a larger ACE++ corpus with ablations that quantify the contribution of sentential vs. local vs. pairwise features, and an informative error analysis highlighting discourse/pronominal challenges.
- Tertiary: A simple clustering pipeline and evidence that square-activated similarity outperforms several alternatives in this setup.

Strengths
- Competitive performance without external knowledge bases, indicating practical portability across domains; ablations convincingly show that sentential CNN features add measurable value over lexical and pairwise signals.
- Careful feature ablations and clustering comparisons provide insight into what drives performance and when BestLink helps or hurts.
- The error analysis is thoughtful and surfaces hard phenomena (pronouns, borderline annotation cases), contextualizing the observed ceilings.
- Clear, modular architecture with reproducible components (CNN + pooling for mentions; simple similarity + logistic output) and reasonable training procedure.

Weaknesses
- Fairness/comparability: use of gold triggers and non-standard splits undermines comparisons to prior work relying on predicted triggers; significance testing is missing and many numbers are close.
- Dataset concerns: ACE++ is not public; claims based on it are difficult to verify, and comparisons to feature-rich baselines on ACE++ are absent.
- Limited novelty: CNN-based sentential features for IE are established; the main novelty is the square similarity, whose general utility is not substantiated beyond this task.
- Clarity gaps: unclear positional embedding initialization/lexicalization and rationale for sharing embeddings across words at the same relative position; representation of left vs. right contexts and whether this affects only pooling; arbitrary choice of exactly one neighbor word; how ve (line 330) is used vs. vsent+lex later; encoding/training of pairwise features (binary vs. continuous, trainability). The concluding claim about cross-sentential information need is asserted but not clearly tied to experiments.

Questions to authors
- Will you release ACE++ and the precise train/dev/test splits for both datasets?
- Can you report results with predicted triggers and perform significance tests?
- How are positional embeddings initialized and shared? Why is position-tying across words appropriate?
- Precisely how are left/right contexts represented, and does this only affect max-pooling?
- Why a window of exactly ±1 token; did other windows change outcomes?
- Where is ve used, and how does it relate to vsent+lex in the final model?
- How are pairwise features encoded (binary/continuous), normalized, and are their weights updated?
- Is the square nonlinearity novel here, and does it help beyond this task? Please justify theoretically or via broader experiments.
- Consider adding an entity coreference evaluation (e.g., OntoNotes) and cite Wiseman et al. (2015).
