Summary
The paper addresses within-document event coreference (“event linking”) without relying on external semantic resources. It encodes each event mention using a sentence-level CNN with position-aware inputs and trigger-local lexical cues, augments these representations with standard pairwise features, and scores mention pairs via a learned similarity (using a square nonlinearity) followed by logistic regression. Pairwise decisions are clustered via transitive closure. The model achieves performance comparable to feature-rich systems on ACE and shows solid gains on an expanded ACE++ corpus. Ablations indicate sentential features are important, and an error analysis highlights challenges such as pronominal event references.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A resource-light event coreference model that matches prior feature-rich systems on ACE by combining CNN-based sentential features with a small set of local/pairwise features. The empirical finding that sentential features recover much of the information otherwise provided by external KB features is the primary contribution.
2) An architecture and analysis demonstrating design trade-offs: piecewise CNN sentence encoder, a distributed similarity layer with a square nonlinearity, and a comparison of clustering strategies; plus ablations isolating the value of sentential, lexical, and pairwise feature classes.
3) A larger ACE++ dataset and an informative error analysis; however, the dataset’s lack of public availability limits its impact.

Strengths
- Strong resource-light performance: The model attains state-of-the-art or on-par results on ACE without external semantic resources, improving practicality for domains lacking KB coverage. On ACE++, recall improves notably with more training data, supporting the claim that sentential features scale.
- Clear ablations and architectural analysis: The feature-class ablation convincingly shows that sentential features materially boost both precision and recall; removing them degrades performance substantially. The comparison of transitive closure vs. BestLink is well-motivated and shows the model already filters low-confidence links, making additional pruning harmful to recall.
- Sound engineering and transparency: The paper specifies training details, hyperparameter exploration, and implementation choices (TensorFlow, Adam, pre-trained word embeddings). The clustering procedure and example generation strategies are described and contrasted.
- Informative error analysis: The manual inspection of false positives/negatives surfaces hard phenomena (pronominal event references) and annotation inconsistencies, contextualizing the attainable performance and highlighting directions for future work.
- Task relevance: The motivation to avoid external semantic features is compelling for cross-domain IE; the paper addresses a practical bottleneck in deploying event linking broadly.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation comparability and fairness: Results rely on gold triggers and varying ACE train/test splits. Several prior works use predicted triggers or different preprocessing; without a standardized split and a predicted-trigger setting (or at least a sensitivity analysis), it is difficult to draw fair, definitive comparisons. Statistical significance testing is also missing, yet many numbers are close.
- Limited novelty in modeling: CNN-based sentence encoders and neural coreference architectures are now common. Beyond the square nonlinearity and specific feature combination, the methodological novelty is moderate. Related neural coreference work (e.g., Wiseman et al., 2015) should be cited and contrasted.
- ACE++ external validity: The expanded ACE++ dataset is not public and comparisons to strong feature-rich baselines on this dataset are absent. As a result, claims based on ACE++ (e.g., recall gains, clustering effects) are hard to validate externally.
- Clarity gaps in model specification: Several details are underspecified:
  • Positional embeddings: initialization, vocabulary, and rationale for sharing an embedding across tokens with the same relative position.
  • Use of left/right context: whether the left/right distinction affects only piecewise max-pooling, and how directly the one-token neighbors are incorporated.
  • Context window choice: why exactly one token on each side is appended; whether larger or learned windows were tried.
  • Representation flow: the role of v_e vs. v_sent+lex in the pairwise stage is unclear (later sections appear to use v_sent+lex, not v_e).
  • Pairwise features: precise encoding (binary vs. continuous), normalization of distances, and whether their embeddings/weights are trained end-to-end.
- Incomplete support for cross-sentential claims: While the dataset analysis suggests many positive pairs are distant and/or lack shared arguments, the paper could more directly quantify the model’s performance as a function of sentence distance or argument overlap to substantiate the central claim.

Questions to Authors
- Positional embeddings: How are they initialized (random vs. pre-trained), what is their index range, and why should all tokens at the same relative offset share an embedding? Did you try absolute position embeddings or other positional encodings?
- Left/right handling: Does the left/right split affect only piecewise max-pooling, or is it used elsewhere? How are the immediate left/right neighbor embeddings combined with vsent (concatenation only)?
- Context window: Why fix the appended trigger-local window to one token on each side? Did larger windows, learned attention, or character/subword features improve results?
- Representation use: What is the explicit role of v_e (the hidden-layer event representation) in part (b)? The text later concatenates vsent+lex with pairwise features—does v_e replace vsent+lex or is it unused?
- Pairwise features: How are continuous features (distance) scaled/normalized? Are these features passed through learned embeddings/MLPs or used as raw inputs? Are their parameters updated jointly?
- Square nonlinearity: Is its use for similarity novel here? Did you compare against alternatives (ReLU/tanh + elementwise absolute difference/product; bilinear; cosine) with significance testing?
- ACE++: Will you release the corpus/splits and code? Can you report comparisons to at least one strong feature-rich baseline on ACE++?
- Standardization: Can you re-run on a commonly used ACE split and/or with predicted triggers to improve comparability? Provide bootstrap tests for key metrics.
- Additional comparisons: Have you considered evaluating on entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes) or referencing neural coref baselines (e.g., Wiseman et al., 2015) to better situate your approach?

Additional Comments
- Please add significance testing for ACE metrics, especially BLANC F1, to support “state-of-the-art” claims.
- Cite and contrast with Wiseman et al. (2015), and briefly position your similarity function relative to common neural coreference matching functions.
- Minor: line 143 has a redundant “that.” Table/text baseline naming is inconsistent (“same type” vs. “same event”); please standardize.
- The motivation for cross-sentential information would benefit from a breakdown of performance by sentence distance and by argument-overlap strata.
