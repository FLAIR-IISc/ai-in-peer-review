Summary
The paper targets within-document event coreference (event linking) and proposes a two-part neural model: (a) a CNN-based encoder that produces a sentential representation for each event mention (with position embeddings, piecewise max-pooling, and trigger-local lexical features), and (b) a pairwise scorer that combines two mention representations with hand-engineered pairwise features, passes them through a “squared” similarity layer, and outputs a coreference score. A simple transitive-closure clustering converts pairwise decisions to clusters. The model avoids external semantic resources and reports state-of-the-art or on-par performance on ACE, plus additional experiments on a larger ACE++ collection. Ablations indicate sentential features increase recall, and an error analysis highlights pronoun-related and borderline annotation cases.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical finding: State-of-the-art event linking without external semantic resources, demonstrating that sentential neural features can replace expensive KB-derived features while maintaining strong performance.
- Data-driven analysis: Useful characterization of ACE/ACE++ (argument overlap sparsity, trigger mismatch across coreferent pairs, long-distance links) that motivates sentential modeling and discourages locality heuristics.
- Practical modeling/engineering insights: An effective instantiation combining CNN-based mention encoders, simple pairwise features, and transitive-closure clustering, with ablations clarifying the role of feature classes and clustering strategy.

Strengths
- Strong performance without external KB features. The model matches or surpasses a feature-rich state-of-the-art system (e.g., BLANC and MUC F1 vs. Liu et al., 2014), suggesting improved portability across domains lacking semantic resources.
- Convincing data analysis. The observations that many positive pairs share no arguments, have different triggers, and are distant in discourse make a strong case for sentence-level representation learning over purely intrinsic features and proximity heuristics.
- Thorough ablations and diagnostics. Experiments dissect the contributions of sentential, trigger-local, and pairwise features; they also examine clustering (BestLink vs. transitive closure), showing why transitive closure works better here. This adds practical guidance for future work.
- Clear writing and good experimental hygiene. The paper is easy to follow, reports multiple coreference metrics (including BLANC), details hyperparameter search, and includes an informative error analysis that surfaces genuine difficulties (pronoun references, annotation inconsistencies).
- Simplicity and reproducibility potential. The architecture is straightforward, implemented in TensorFlow with common components (pre-trained word embeddings, Adam), facilitating replication and adoption.

Weaknesses
- Limited architectural motivation and comparisons. The choice of CNNs for mention encoding is under-motivated; there are no comparisons to contemporaneous alternatives (BiLSTMs, RCNNs, tree-/dependency-aware models, attention/bilinear similarity). Reported gains over baselines are modest (often 2–3 F1), and there are no significance tests; stronger encoder comparisons are needed to substantiate the architectural choices.
- Unclear novelty relative to prior neural IE. Using CNNs to encode sentences is well established. The paper would benefit from comparisons to existing continuous-space event coreference/linking approaches or stronger non-neural baselines beyond those in Table 3, to better position the methodological contribution.
- ACE++ description and availability. The larger dataset is insufficiently described: sourcing, annotation protocol/ontology, domain mix, event-type distribution, inter-annotator agreement, and potential biases are unclear. Without release, it is difficult to assess generalizability and to reproduce the results that drive several key conclusions (e.g., recall improvements).
- Evaluation relies on gold event mentions (and argument overlap features). The main results use gold triggers/mentions; there is no evaluation with predicted mentions/arguments from a standard pipeline. This limits claims about robustness in realistic settings and makes comparison to systems that avoid gold supervision less direct.
- Pairwise feature role is underexplored. Table 5 implies pairwise features remain important, but the paper does not report which ones contribute most, how they were selected, or whether alternatives (e.g., discourse cues, dependency paths, lexical overlap variants) were tried. Similarly, the “square” similarity choice is justified only by intuition and limited ablation; other similarity forms (bilinear, cosine with learned temperature, absolute value) deserve systematic comparison.

Questions to Authors
- Why CNNs for mention encoding? Can you report controlled comparisons against BiLSTM/RCNN/attention encoders with comparable parameter counts and provide significance tests for the observed gains?
- What exactly is ACE++? Please detail document collection, annotation process/ontology, quality control (IAA), event-type distribution, domain coverage, and licensing. Can you release ACE++ or at least document IDs and statistics?
- How does the model perform in an end-to-end pipeline with predicted triggers/mentions (and no gold arguments), using a standard event extractor? Please report results and error propagation analysis.
- Which pairwise features contributed most to the final performance? Did you evaluate alternatives (lexical overlap metrics, dependency-path similarities, discourse structure, entity type agreement) and feature selection strategies?
- Is the squared similarity critical? How does it compare to cosine, bilinear, or absolute-value activations under matched capacity?
- Given pronoun-related errors, can incorporating an off-the-shelf entity/pronoun coreference system (e.g., CoreNLP) to enrich arguments or context reduce these errors? Any preliminary results?

Additional comments
- Please add evaluations on public event coreference resources (e.g., EventCorefBank) and consider joint entity/event coreference settings to assess broader applicability.
- Report calibration (PR curves) and statistical significance versus baselines in Table 3.
- For reproducibility, consider releasing code, trained models, and exact splits (including ACE++), and report training variance across random seeds.
- A pipeline setting would also allow reporting runtime and memory footprint, informing practical deployment.
