Summary
This paper tackles within-document event coreference (event linking) without external semantic resources. Each event mention’s sentence is encoded with word and positional embeddings passed through a CNN and piecewise (left/right-of-trigger) max-pooling to produce sentential features. These are concatenated with the trigger and its immediate left/right word embeddings to form a mention vector, which is combined with a small set of pairwise features (type agreement, distance, modality, argument overlap, discourse position). A shallow “distributed similarity” layer with a square activation produces features for a final logistic classifier. Pairwise scores are converted to clusters via transitive closure. On ACE 2005 and a larger ACE++ dataset, the model performs competitively with feature-rich systems. Ablations indicate embeddings alone are insufficient; adding lexical/pairwise features is necessary. An error analysis and comparisons of clustering strategies are provided.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple and effective neural architecture for event linking that combines CNN-based sentential features with a small set of linguistic/pairwise features, avoiding external KBs.
2) Empirical evidence via ablations that pretrained embeddings/sentential features alone are not enough: combining lexical trigger context and lightweight pairwise features is critical.
3) A larger ACE++ evaluation showing gains from more training data, plus analysis of clustering strategies and an informative error analysis.

Strengths
- Competitive accuracy without external semantic resources: The model matches or exceeds prior feature-rich systems on several metrics on ACE, showing portability to domains lacking KBs.
- Clear, modular architecture with informative ablations: The breakdown of sentential, lexical, and pairwise features (Table 5) convincingly shows their complementary contributions.
- Sensible evaluation choices and analysis: Use of BLANC, discussion of pairwise vs clustering strategies (transitive closure vs BestLink) and the observed precision/recall trade-offs are valuable. The error analysis highlights genuine challenges (e.g., pronominal anaphora to events).
- Scales with more data: ACE++ training leads to sizable recall gains, supporting the claim that the neural approach benefits from additional supervision.
- Practicality: The design avoids heavy reliance on syntactic/semantic externalities; piecewise pooling supports multiple mentions per sentence.

Weaknesses
- Fairness/replicability of evaluation: Although ACE splits vary across papers, the paper introduces its own 9:1 splits (and a new ACE++ dataset) without reporting results on widely used prior splits. Moreover, the use of gold triggers limits comparability to systems that use predicted triggers; an end-to-end evaluation or at least a reported delta with predicted triggers would be informative.
- Non-public ACE++ and incomplete comparisons: ACE++ is not publicly available. As a result, comparisons to prior feature-rich systems on ACE++ are missing, undermining the strength of claims tied to the new dataset.
- Lack of significance testing: Many improvements are small and metrics disagree (e.g., BLANC vs B^3). No statistical significance tests are reported; the claims would be stronger with paired bootstrap/permutation tests.
- Insufficient clarity on several modeling details:
  • Positional embeddings: How are they initialized (random vs lexicalized by distance tokens), tied across sentences, and updated? Why should the same relative distance share an embedding across all words?
  • Left/right handling: Beyond piecewise max-pooling, are left/right contexts parameter-shared or distinct? How are multiple mentions per sentence disambiguated during pooling?
  • Context window: Why append exactly one token to the left/right of the trigger? Was the window tuned? What happens with larger windows or learned attention over the sentence?
  • Representation usage: Equation (3) defines ve, but later sections seem to reference only vsent+lex. Which vector is actually fed into the similarity layer? Please clarify the interface.
  • Pairwise features: How are categorical features encoded (binary/one-hot)? How is distance represented (binned vs continuous)? Are these embeddings/weights learned jointly with the network?
- Ambiguous claims about cross-sentential information: Lines 65–70 assert the need for cross-sentential information, but the model uses only sentential encodings plus pairwise metadata. The mechanism by which cross-sentential information is captured is not clearly explained.
- Novelty of the square nonlinearity: The squared activation in the similarity layer is interesting but under-motivated. Is this novel in coreference/event linking? How does it compare to cosine/bilinear/MLP with ReLU/tanh in controlled ablations beyond the brief mention?

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Splits and comparability: Can you also report results on a commonly used ACE split from prior work, or at least provide a mapping? Are your ACE split lists sufficient for exact replication of Table 3? Any significance testing?
- Gold vs predicted triggers: What is the performance delta when using predicted triggers? If unavailable, can you approximate via noise injection or a realistic trigger detector?
- ACE++: Will you release ACE++ (data and splits)? If not, can you provide a comparable public surrogate or refrain from strong claims based on ACE++?
- Positional embeddings: Initialization, parameterization, and training details; maximum relative distance; clipping; evidence that sharing by relative index helps.
- Left/right context: Are different filters used for left vs right? Does piecewise pooling alone distinguish them? How is overlapping context handled with multiple mentions?
- Context window size: Why ±1 token? Did you tune this? Please report performance for different windows or an attention alternative.
- Representation flow: Is ve or vsent+lex fed into Wsim? Please reconcile Eq. (3) with Eq. (4).
- Pairwise features: Exact encodings (binary, bucketized distances), normalization, and whether their parameters are updated during training.
- Square activation: Provide quantitative ablations against ReLU/tanh/bilinear/cosine on both ACE and ACE++; discuss broader applicability.
- Cross-sentential information: Please clarify what cross-sentential features are actually used and how the model leverages them beyond distance/pairwise metadata.

Additional comments
- Related work: Please cite Wiseman et al. (2015) on neural mention-ranking for coreference and discuss similarities/differences.
- Consider evaluating entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes) to compare against more neural coref work and to test generality.
- Minor edits: Line 143 has a redundant “that.” Baseline naming inconsistency: “same type” in Table 6 vs “same event” in the text (line 670). Please standardize.
