Summary
This paper addresses within-document event coreference (“event linking”). The core idea is to replace heavy reliance on external semantic resources with sentential representations learned by convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The model has two parts: (a) a CNN-based encoder that produces an event-mention representation from the sentence (with positional and local lexical features) and (b) a similarity network that combines two mention representations with hand-crafted pairwise features to score coreference, followed by simple clustering (transitive closure). Experiments on ACE 2005 and a larger internal ACE++ corpus show competitive performance; ablations indicate sentential features contribute substantially. The authors provide useful analysis of dataset properties (argument sharing, trigger mismatch, long-distance links) and an error analysis.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: Strong, portable performance on event linking without external KB-derived features by leveraging sentential CNN features plus a small set of pairwise features. The model attains state-of-the-art or on-par BLANC/MUC F1 on ACE while avoiding domain-specific semantic resources.
2) Architectural instantiation and analysis: A simple two-stage architecture (CNN mention encoder + distributed similarity with square activation) and a comparison of clustering strategies, with evidence that, for this model, transitive closure outperforms BestLink in F1.
3) Data-driven insights: Analysis of ACE(++) showing many positive pairs lack shared arguments, many have different triggers, and long-range links are common—empirically motivating sentence-level features; ablations that quantify the importance of sentential vs. pairwise features.

Strengths
- Practical impact and portability: The system achieves competitive/SOTA results on ACE without external knowledge sources. This removes a common barrier to deploying event coreference in new domains and languages.
- Solid empirical support for sentential features: The analyses (e.g., ~51% positive pairs with no shared arguments; ~47% with different triggers; >55% of positives >5 sentences apart) justify modeling beyond local triggers/arguments, and the ablations (Table 5) show sentential features materially boost F1 over pairwise-only or local-only variants.
- Careful evaluation and comparisons: On ACE, the model outperforms or matches prior work on several metrics (e.g., higher BLANC and MUC F1 than Liu et al., 2014), despite not using external semantic features. The study of clustering (BestLink vs. transitive closure) is informative and contrasts with prior reports.
- Informative error analysis: The qualitative analysis surfaces challenging categories (e.g., pronominal anaphora to events, annotation ambiguities), helping contextualize upper bounds and future directions.
- Clear writing and motivation: The paper is easy to follow; the model is well-described; the data analyses motivate design decisions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the core technical idea: CNN sentence encoders for relation/event tasks are well-known by 2015–2016. The paper applies this paradigm to event linking but does not convincingly argue architectural novelty. The reported gains (often 2–3 F1) could plausibly stem from better engineering rather than a new method.
- Incomplete model comparisons: The choice of CNNs is not sufficiently justified. There are no comparisons to recurrent or tree-based encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/GRU, Tree-LSTM) that had strong results on relation/event tasks at the time. Similarly, there’s no head-to-head against other continuous-space event linking models or cosine-similarity baselines beyond a brief mention.
- ACE++ dataset concerns: The construction, event-type coverage, sampling, annotation process/quality control, and domain balance of ACE++ are not described, making it difficult to assess generalization claims or potential biases. Without releasing ACE++, the community cannot reproduce or build upon the key finding that scale drives recall gains.
- Evaluation scope and fairness: Results rely on gold event triggers/arguments; the paper does not report end-to-end performance with predicted triggers/arguments, which limits practical interpretability. Given known variability in ACE splits and preprocessing, stronger baselines and statistical significance testing would strengthen claims.
- Pairwise features remain influential but under-analyzed: Table 5 shows pairwise features are still important. The paper does not report which features drive performance, how they were selected, or what alternatives were considered (e.g., richer discourse features, learned similarity without hand-designed indicators).

Questions to Authors
- Why CNNs? Please provide controlled comparisons to BiLSTM sentence encoders and, if feasible, a tree-structured model. Also ablate key CNN design choices (positional embeddings, piecewise pooling, the final hidden layer) and report effect sizes.
- Similarity function: You note square activation outperforms cosine and other nonlinearities. Could you include a table with these variants and significance tests? Does a learned biaffine or cosine with temperature produce similar results?
- ACE++ details: How were documents sourced and event types annotated? What was the annotation protocol (guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, adjudication)? What is the domain distribution vs. ACE? Will you release ACE++ to enable replication?
- Robustness without gold annotations: Can you report performance with predicted triggers and arguments (e.g., using a standard event extractor) to gauge end-to-end utility?
- External coreference signals: Given many errors involve pronominal references to events, have you tried integrating an off-the-shelf coreference resolver (e.g., CoreNLP) as additional features or constraints? Any quantitative impact?
- Feature analysis: Which pairwise features contributed most (e.g., type compatibility, realis, distance, argument overlap)? Did you try replacing them with learned feature interactions only?
- Generalization across datasets: Can you evaluate on ECB/ECB+ or ProcessBank, and/or on the cross-document setting, to support broader claims? Even a zero-shot transfer or fine-tune experiment would be informative.

Additional Comments
- Please include statistical significance tests for key comparisons (e.g., vs. Liu et al., 2014).
- Consider joint entity/event coreference experiments or, at minimum, conditioning on predicted entity coreference, given the error patterns and ACE’s small size.
- Reporting precision/recall trade-offs (curves) for different clustering thresholds would contextualize BestLink vs. transitive closure choices.
