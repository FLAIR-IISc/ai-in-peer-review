Summary
The paper tackles within-document event coreference (“event linking”) without external semantic resources. It proposes a two-part model: (a) a CNN-based mention encoder that builds sentential representations conditioned on the trigger and (b) a pairwise scorer that combines the two mention vectors with hand-crafted pairwise features and produces a coreference score, clustered via transitive closure. The authors report state-of-the-art results on ACE and introduce an expanded corpus, ACE++, arguing that data scale matters. Ablations analyze clustering strategies and the relative value of sentential, trigger-local lexical, and pairwise features; an error analysis highlights pronoun-related and annotation-boundary cases.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical result: Sentential CNN features plus lightweight pairwise features can match or exceed prior ACE results without external knowledge bases, supporting portability to domains lacking such resources.
2) Data contribution: ACE++, a larger, ACE-style resource, is used to show that more training data chiefly improves recall; the analysis of argument sharing, trigger mismatch, and distance statistics motivates sentence-level modeling.
3) Analysis: Ablations (Tables 4–5) contrasting clustering strategies and feature classes, and an error analysis diagnosing core failure modes.

Strengths
- Clear problem setup and writing: The paper is easy to follow, with a well-motivated pipeline perspective and a transparent two-part architecture (Figure 1). The argument-sharing and distance statistics from ACE/ACE++ are useful and motivate sentence-level representations.
- Strong empirical showing without external KBs: On ACE, the model attains competitive to state-of-the-art performance (Table 3) despite eschewing WordNet/FrameNet/VerbOcean features relied upon by prior work. This supports the portability claim.
- Thorough ablations: The comparison of transitive closure vs. BestLink (Table 4) is careful and well-justified, and feature ablations (Table 5) reveal that all three components (sentential, trigger-local, pairwise) contribute—pairwise features remain important, a valuable finding for the community.
- Error analysis with actionable insights: The qualitative analysis (e.g., pronoun/event anaphora, inconsistent annotations across very similar cases) contextualizes the remaining gap and suggests directions (discourse, coref signals).
- Sensible training and engineering choices: Use of position embeddings, piecewise pooling, and a learned similarity function shows attention to architectural detail; the authors note alternatives explored and provide dev/test partition lists.

Weaknesses
- Architectural motivation and comparisons are insufficient: The choice of CNNs for mention encoding is not justified against strong contemporaries (BiLSTM, recursive/TreeLSTM, attention over tokens). Given the reported gains are modest (≈2–3 F1), stronger evidence is needed: head-to-head encoders on the same infrastructure (PCNN vs. BiLSTM/GRU; with/without attention; cosine vs. learned similarity; square vs. ReLU/tanh in Eq. 4) and significance tests. Without this, it’s unclear the improvements stem from the sentential CNN rather than other factors.
- Novelty is limited/unclear: Applying CNN sentence encoders to event tasks is no longer novel by 2015–2016. The paper should position itself against continuous-space pairwise or Siamese metric-learning approaches to coreference/event linking and expand baselines beyond Table 3 (e.g., avg-embed cosine, Siamese CNN/BiLSTM, attention pooling, Clark & Manning-style mention scoring adapted to events).
- ACE++ description is under-specified: The collection/annotation process, event-type coverage, sampling, domain mix, and potential biases are not described; no inter-annotator agreement is reported. It’s therefore hard to assess generalizability or what the model learned. The paper should clarify whether ACE++ mirrors ACE types, whether new types were added, and provide statistics per type, domain, and argument sparsity. Consider releasing ACE++ (or as much as licensing allows), or at least a detailed datasheet, to ensure reproducibility.
- Limited evaluation scope: Results rely on gold triggers (and possibly gold arguments); Table 3 should include performance without gold references (end-to-end with predicted triggers/arguments) to assess real-world usefulness and robustness to upstream noise. External evaluation on ECB/ECB+ (widely used for event coref) is missing; even if cross-document is out of scope, within-document subsets would strengthen claims. Given ACE’s small size, a joint entity/event coreference experiment (or using off-the-shelf entity coref to enrich arguments) would test the claim that external KBs are unnecessary when discourse signals are available.
- Pairwise feature reporting is thin: Table 5 shows pairwise features remain critical, but the paper doesn’t report which specific features mattered, how they were selected, or what alternatives were tried. This limits interpretability and reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Why CNNs? Please provide a controlled comparison of mention encoders (PCNN/PCNN+attn vs. BiLSTM/GRU vs. TreeLSTM), and of similarity functions (cosine, bilinear, MLP, square/ReLU/tanh), with significance tests. Where do the 2–3 F1 gains come from?
- ACE++: How were documents sampled? Which ACE event types are covered? Any additions/removals? What is the domain distribution (news/blog/web)? What is IAA? Any known biases (e.g., topic, language variety)? Will you release ACE++ (or annotation guidelines, splits, and label distributions)?
- Baselines: Can you add (i) a Siamese BiLSTM baseline, (ii) cosine over avg or max-pooled embeddings, and (iii) an attention-based encoder? Also, please compare to neural entity coreference-style architectures adapted to events.
- End-to-end robustness: What is performance when using predicted triggers and arguments from a standard extractor? Please add a column in Table 3 without gold supervision in upstream components.
- Pairwise features: Which features contributed most (e.g., type agreement, distance, realis, argument overlap)? How were they chosen? What alternatives were tried (e.g., discourse cues, section headers, speaker/coref chains, temporal signals)?
- Off-the-shelf coreference: In the line-687 example, you suggest leveraging entity coref. Can you report results augmenting argument overlap with CoreNLP (or similar) entity coreference outputs?

Additional Comments
- Please report variance over multiple random seeds and statistical significance for key comparisons.
- Consider evaluating on EventCorefBank (ECB/ECB+) within-document subsets and/or adding a small joint entity/event experiment to validate the discourse hypothesis.
- The learned similarity with square activation is interesting—provide ablations quantifying its benefit vs. cosine/bilinear.
- If release of ACE++ is not possible, consider releasing features, models, and detailed label distributions to improve reproducibility.
