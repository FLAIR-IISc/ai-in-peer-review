This paper addresses the task of event coreference resolution. The authors propose a neural model that, unlike previous state-of-the-art systems, does not rely on features from external knowledge bases such as WordNet or FrameNet. The model operates in two stages. First, it uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to process an event mention and its surrounding sentence, generating a latent vector representation that captures sentential context. Second, it combines the representations of two event mentions with a set of pairwise features and feeds them into a trainable similarity function to compute a coreference score. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that a neural model utilizing sentential features can achieve state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset for event linking without requiring external, domain-specific semantic resources. This suggests the approach may be more portable to new domains than previous methods.
2.  The creation and evaluation of a new, larger dataset (ACE++), which is used to show that the proposed model benefits significantly from additional training data.

### Strengths

1.  The primary strength of this paper is its successful demonstration that learned sentential features can effectively replace handcrafted features derived from external knowledge bases. By achieving state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark without relying on resources like WordNet, the authors present a compelling case for a more portable and less domain-dependent approach to event linking. This is a significant step towards developing robust event processing systems that can be applied to new domains where such resources are not available.
2.  The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained logically, and the figures effectively illustrate the two-stage process.
3.  The authors provide a strong motivation for their modeling choices through a preliminary analysis of the ACE dataset. The findings that a majority of coreferential event pairs do not share arguments (50.97%) and have different trigger words (47.29%) effectively justify the need to incorporate broader sentential context beyond local and intrinsic features. This analysis strengthens the foundation of their proposed approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  The choice of a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for generating sentential representations is not sufficiently motivated or analyzed. While CNNs are a valid choice, the paper does not discuss why this architecture is preferable to other common sentence encoders, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), for this specific task. Given that the performance improvement over the state-of-the-art is modest (e.g., ~2.8 F1 points in BLANC over Liu et al., 2014), a more thorough architectural comparison or ablation study would be necessary to establish that the CNN architecture itself provides a distinct advantage.
2.  The novelty of the technical approach is somewhat limited. The application of CNNs for sentence representation and classification is a well-established technique. The paper would be strengthened by more clearly positioning its contribution relative to other continuous-space or neural methods for coreference resolution tasks. The comparison to prior work in Table 3 could also be more comprehensive to better substantiate the state-of-the-art claim.
3.  The paper provides no details on the construction of the new ACE++ dataset. A key claim is that the proposed method is more portable across domains, but without information on the source, domain, and event type distribution of the 1,351 additional documents, it is impossible to verify this. If the new documents are from the same news domain as the original ACE corpus, it primarily demonstrates the model's ability to benefit from more in-domain data, rather than its portability. For the results to be meaningful and reproducible, details about this dataset are essential, and the authors should consider making it publicly available.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The ablation study in Table 5 indicates that pairwise features provide a substantial performance contribution. Could you elaborate on which of these features were the most effective and what process you used to arrive at the final feature set?
2.  Your error analysis correctly identifies pronoun resolution as a major source of false negatives (e.g., "It sickens me."). Have you considered how your model could be integrated with a general-purpose coreference resolution system (e.g., Stanford CoreNLP), which might resolve these pronominal references, as either a baseline or an augmentation?
3.  The evaluation is conducted using gold-standard event triggers and arguments. How would you expect the model to perform in a more realistic end-to-end setting where these annotations are predicted by an upstream system?
