### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for event coreference resolution. The proposed method aims to replace the reliance on external knowledge sources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet), common in previous work, with learned sentential features derived from a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The model operates in two stages: first, it uses a CNN to generate a vector representation for each event mention based on its sentential context. Second, it pairs these representations, augments them with standard pairwise features, and feeds them into a trainable similarity function to produce a coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 corpus and a new, larger dataset they created (ACE++), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that a neural architecture relying primarily on learned sentential features can achieve state-of-the-art performance on event linking, removing the need for features from external, domain-specific knowledge bases.
2.  The creation and use of ACE++, a larger dataset for event linking, which helps to better demonstrate the model's ability to generalize. The analysis of this dataset provides valuable motivation for the proposed approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The preliminary analysis of the ACE dataset, highlighting the low degree of argument and trigger overlap in coreferential event pairs, provides a strong justification for incorporating richer sentential context.
2.  **Effective Ablation Study:** The ablation study presented in Table 5 clearly demonstrates the contribution of each feature class (pairwise, local, and sentential). This analysis effectively shows that the learned sentential features are a critical component of the model's success.
3.  **Reduces Reliance on External Resources:** The primary goal of the paper—to build a high-performing event linking system without relying on hand-crafted semantic features from external knowledge bases—is a valuable contribution. Such a system is more portable to new domains where resources like WordNet or FrameNet may not be available or sufficient.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Motivation for Architectural Choices:** The paper fails to sufficiently motivate the choice of a CNN for generating event mention representations over other common architectures. While CNNs are a reasonable choice, alternatives like RNNs or LSTMs are also well-suited for encoding sequential information and are often used for sentence representation tasks. Given that the performance gains over the state-of-the-art are modest (around 2-3 F1 points on some metrics), a more thorough justification or an empirical comparison to alternative architectures would be necessary to convincingly argue for the superiority of this specific design.
2.  **Unclear Novelty and Limited Comparison to Related Work:** The novelty of using a CNN to generate sentence representations for a classification task is limited. The paper would be significantly strengthened by positioning its contribution more clearly with respect to other continuous-space or neural methods for coreference resolution (for events or other types). The comparison to prior work in Table 3 feels incomplete, as it seems to omit other relevant neural approaches to this or very similar tasks.
3.  **Lack of Detail on the ACE++ Dataset:** The new ACE++ dataset is a potentially significant contribution, but the paper provides no details about its creation, including the source of the documents and the annotation process. A major limitation of the original ACE corpus is its narrow distribution of event types. To support the claim that the proposed model is more domain-independent, it is crucial to know what event types ACE++ covers and how they differ from ACE. Without this information, it is difficult to assess the generality of the results. For the work to be reproducible and to facilitate future comparisons, the authors should plan to make this dataset publicly available.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The feature ablation in Table 5 shows that pairwise features contribute significantly to the model's performance. Could you provide more detail on the feature engineering process? Specifically, which features were most effective, and what other features were considered but discarded? This suggests that feature engineering remains an important component, even in a neural architecture.
2.  The error analysis notes that resolving pronouns that refer to events (e.g., "It sickens me," where "It" refers to a prior "bombing" event) is a major challenge. Have you considered how your model might be integrated with a standard pronoun coreference resolution system (e.g., the one in CoreNLP)? For the example in the paper, an off-the-shelf system might successfully link "It" to "bombing," which could potentially be leveraged.
3.  Your experiments are conducted using gold-standard event triggers and arguments. How do you expect the model to perform in a more realistic end-to-end setting where these annotations are predicted by an upstream system? It would strengthen the paper to include at least one experiment under these conditions.
4.  Given the relatively small size of even the ACE++ dataset for training deep neural networks, have you considered evaluating on other available resources for event coreference, such as EventCorefBank? This could provide further evidence for your model's effectiveness.
