### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a neural network model for event coreference resolution. The primary goal is to move away from systems that rely on rich, domain-specific external knowledge sources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet) by instead leveraging sentential features learned by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The proposed model operates in two stages: first, it uses a CNN to generate a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention by processing the mention's trigger and its surrounding sentential context. Second, it pairs these representations, augments them with a set of standard pairwise features, and feeds them into a final classifier to produce a coreference score. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the standard ACE 2005 dataset and a new, larger dataset they created, called ACE++.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A New, Larger Dataset for Event Coreference (ACE++):** The authors created a new dataset by supplementing the standard ACE 2005 corpus with 1351 additional documents. Given that data scarcity is a significant bottleneck for neural models in this domain, this is a valuable contribution that enables more robust model training and evaluation. The paper effectively uses this dataset to demonstrate how model performance, particularly recall, improves with more training data.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Results without External Semantic Resources:** The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration that a model based on learned sentential features can achieve competitive or state-of-the-art results against systems that depend heavily on external knowledge bases. This is an important finding that supports the development of more portable and domain-agnostic event processing systems.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation and Analysis:** The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to follow. The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, including an insightful ablation study (Table 5) that effectively isolates the contribution of each component of their model (pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential features). Furthermore, the qualitative error analysis provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task, such as pronominal anaphora and annotation inconsistencies, which can guide future research.

2.  **Strong Motivation and Execution for Dataset Creation:** The authors correctly identify the limited size of the ACE 2005 corpus as a key challenge. Their analysis showing that many coreferential event pairs share no arguments or trigger words provides a solid motivation for incorporating broader sentential context. Creating the ACE++ dataset to address this data sparsity is a commendable effort and a significant strength of this work.

3.  **Well-Founded Goal of Reducing Dependency on External Knowledge:** The core motivation of the paper is to reduce the reliance on external, often domain-specific, semantic resources that limit the applicability of previous systems. The success of their proposed model validates this direction and shows the promise of learning features directly from the text.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for the CNN Architecture:** The paper fails to adequately motivate the choice of a CNN for generating event mention representations. While CNNs have been successful in sentence classification, it is not obvious why they are the best choice here compared to alternatives like RNNs or LSTMs, which are explicitly designed to model sequential information. Given that the performance gains over the state-of-the-art are modest (around 2-3 F1 points on some metrics), a stronger justification for the specific architectural choice is necessary. The paper would be significantly strengthened by either a theoretical discussion of the pros and cons of CNNs for this task or an empirical comparison against other neural architectures.

2.  **Unclear Novelty and Limited Comparison to Related Methods:** The application of a CNN for a classification task is not inherently novel. The authors should more clearly position their contribution within the landscape of continuous-space and neural models for coreference resolution. The comparison systems in Table 3 are primarily feature-engineered systems. A discussion or comparison with any existing neural methods for event or entity coreference would help clarify the specific novelty of this model.

3.  **Lack of Detail Regarding the ACE++ Dataset:** While the creation of the ACE++ dataset is a major strength, its utility to the community is limited by the lack of detail provided. There is no information on how the additional documents were sourced or annotated, what event types they cover, or whether the distribution of types differs from the original ACE corpus. This information is critical for assessing the model's generalizability and supporting the claim that it is less domain-dependent. For the work to be reproducible and have a lasting impact, the authors should provide these details and clarify their plans for making the dataset publicly available.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The ablation study in Table 5 shows that pairwise features contribute significantly to the model's performance. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of these features? Specifically, which ones were most impactful, and could you describe the process used to select the final set of pairwise features?

2.  Your primary experiments rely on gold-standard event mentions. To better understand the model's practical utility in a realistic end-to-end system, could you report performance figures using system-predicted event mentions from an upstream event detector?

3.  The error analysis highlights the difficulty of resolving pronominal references to events (e.g., "It sickens me."). Have you considered incorporating features from a general-purpose coreference resolution system (like Stanford CoreNLP) that might resolve such pronouns, and do you think this could be a promising direction?

4.  Given the relatively small size of even the ACE++ dataset for training deep learning models, have you considered evaluating your model on other publicly available event coreference corpora, such as EventCorefBank, to further demonstrate its robustness?
