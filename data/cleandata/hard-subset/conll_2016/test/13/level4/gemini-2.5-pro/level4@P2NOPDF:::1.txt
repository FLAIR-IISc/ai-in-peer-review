### **Summary and Contribution**

This paper presents a neural network approach for event coreference resolution. The model leverages Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to generate vector representations for event mentions. Specifically, word embeddings within a context window are passed through a CNN with max-pooling. These learned representations are then concatenated with the word embeddings of the trigger's immediate neighbors. This combined vector, along with a set of pairwise linguistic features, is fed into a single-layer neural network to produce a final coreference score. The model is evaluated on the standard ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art feature-rich systems.

The primary contribution of this work is the development of a neural architecture for event linking that effectively integrates learned representations from word embeddings with a curated set of linguistic features. A key finding is that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for high performance, and that augmenting them with even a small, well-chosen set of linguistic features yields significant gains. Encouragingly, the features used do not depend on complex, manually-crafted external resources.

### **Major Concerns**

**1. Experimental Setup and Unfair Comparison:** The experiments appear to rely on gold-standard event triggers rather than system-predicted ones. While the authors provide a rationale for this decision, it limits the applicability of the model in a realistic end-to-end setting. More importantly, this choice makes the comparison with at least one competing system, which uses predicted triggers, fundamentally unfair. To provide a more grounded evaluation, the authors should report performance using predicted triggers as input.

**2. Data Splits and Reproducibility:** The paper notes that different train/test splits have been used in prior work, and proceeds to use a new split. This practice hinders direct and fair comparison across different studies. For the sake of reproducibility and to facilitate future comparisons, I strongly encourage the authors to adhere to previously established and commonly used data splits whenever possible.

### **Points Requiring Clarification**

The paper is generally well-written, but several methodological details require clarification:

1.  **Cross-Sentential Information:** The argument for the necessity of cross-sentential information is convincing. However, the final statement in the second paragraph of the introduction (lines 65-70) was unclear and could be rephrased for better readability.

2.  **Position Embeddings:** The generation of position embeddings is described as being "similar to word embeddings," but this is too vague. How are these embeddings initialized (e.g., randomly) and trained? It is not immediately clear why a relative position (e.g., "+1") should share the same embedding regardless of the anchor word it is relative to.

3.  **Context Representation:** The method for incorporating the left and right neighbors of a trigger to create the final representation (lines 307-311) is not fully specified. Is the distinction between left and right context handled solely by the max-pooling operation, or is there another mechanism at play?

4.  **Context Window Size:** The decision to append the word embeddings of exactly one word before and one word after the trigger seems arbitrary. The authors should provide a justification for this specific window size over other alternatives (e.g., a larger window or an attention mechanism).

5.  **Use of Event-Mention Representation:** It is unclear how the initial event-mention representation `v_e` (line 330) is ultimately used in the model. The subsequent sections on pairwise scoring only appear to use the augmented representation `v_{sent+lex}`. Please clarify the role of `v_e`.

6.  **Pairwise Feature Encoding:** The paper should detail how the pairwise features are encoded and integrated into the neural network. For binary features, a one-hot encoding is plausible, but how are continuous or integer features like distance handled? Furthermore, are the vector representations for these features kept fixed or are they fine-tuned during training?

### **Other Issues and Suggestions**

*   **Generalizability to Entity Coreference:** The proposed architecture seems potentially applicable to the more general task of entity coreference resolution. Porting the model to this task would allow for comparison against a much wider body of work and on popular datasets like OntoNotes, which would significantly broaden the paper's impact.

*   **Novelty of Non-Linearity:** The use of a square function as a non-linearity is interesting and uncommon. The authors are encouraged to comment on whether this is a novel contribution and to discuss its properties or potential applicability to other NLP tasks.

*   **Dataset Availability:** Results are presented on a new, non-public dataset (ACE++). While the creation of new resources is valuable, their private nature impedes reproducibility and follow-up research. Do the authors have plans to release this dataset? At a minimum, it would strengthen the paper to include comparisons to the feature-rich baseline systems on ACE++ as well.

*   **Significance Testing:** Some of the reported performance differences between systems are quite small. The claims of improvement would be more robust if supported by statistical significance testing.

*   **Missing Related Work:** The discussion of related work on neural coreference resolution would benefit from including the influential work by Wiseman et al. (2015), which introduced a neural mention-ranking model for entity coreference.

### **Minor Issues**

*   Line 143: The word "that" in "...assume that for an anaphoric mention..." is redundant.
*   The baseline system is referred to as "same type" in Table 6 but as "same event" in the main text (line 670). Please ensure consistent terminology.
