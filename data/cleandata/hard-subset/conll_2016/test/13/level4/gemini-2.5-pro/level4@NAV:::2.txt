This work introduces a model for event entity linking that leverages sentential features derived from a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). This approach is proposed as an alternative to prior methods that rely on external knowledge bases. The model is structured in two stages: the first stage generates a representation for each event mention, while the second stage computes a coreference score between a pair of these representations.

The manuscript is clear, well-structured, and straightforward to understand. The analysis of argument sharing among coreferent events in the ACE corpus is a notable strength. Additionally, the examination of how dataset size affects performance provides a compelling justification for the introduction of the new ACE++ dataset. However, several major concerns require attention:

- The paper lacks a clear motivation for selecting a CNN to generate mention representations. There is no discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of this choice, nor is there a comparison with alternative architectures, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). Considering the modest F1-score improvements of 2-3 points over the state-of-the-art, a more thorough justification is required to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed architecture.

- The novelty of using sentential features for event linking is not clearly articulated. The application of CNNs to classification tasks is a well-established technique, so the authors should explicitly position their contribution relative to other continuous space models for event linking. The selection of baseline models for comparison in Table 3 is not sufficiently comprehensive to establish this.

- Details regarding the collection and composition of the proposed ACE++ dataset are absent. The original ACE dataset is known for its limited and potentially biased set of event types; therefore, it is crucial to understand the scope and variety of event types covered in ACE++. This information is also necessary to substantiate the claim that the proposed approach is less dependent on domain-specific resources. To validate this, the authors should demonstrate a scenario where their model succeeds on a dataset where resource-dependent methods falter. For the benefit of future research and reproducibility, the authors are strongly encouraged to make this dataset publicly available.

Some minor issues:

- It would be beneficial for Table 3 to also include the model's performance when not using gold references, reflecting a more realistic evaluation scenario.

- An exploration of how the proposed model could be integrated with a standard entity coreference resolution system would be a valuable addition. For instance, in the example provided (line 687), an off-the-shelf system like CoreNLP correctly links 'It' to 'bombing', a signal that could potentially be incorporated into a baseline or the model itself.

- Due to the limited size of the ACE dataset, the model's robustness and generalizability would be more convincingly demonstrated by evaluating it on additional, established benchmarks. Testing on the EventCorefBank dataset alongside ACE is highly recommended.

- The results in Table 5 indicate that pairwise features contribute significantly to performance, suggesting that feature engineering remains important for this task, at least on datasets the scale of ACE. The paper would be strengthened by an ablation study or analysis detailing the relative importance of these features, the rationale for their selection, and any other feature configurations that were explored.
