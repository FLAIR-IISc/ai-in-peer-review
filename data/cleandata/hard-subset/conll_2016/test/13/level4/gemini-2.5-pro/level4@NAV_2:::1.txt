This paper presents a neural network model for event coreference resolution. The proposed method first generates a representation for each event mention by passing word and position embeddings through a convolutional neural network (CNN) with a piecewise max-pooling layer. This sentential representation is then concatenated with local lexical features (embeddings of the trigger word and its immediate context) to form a final event mention vector. To make a coreference decision, the representations of two event mentions are combined with a set of pairwise features and fed into a similarity network, which produces a final coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and a new, larger dataset (ACE++), achieving performance that is on par with or exceeds previous state-of-the-art systems that rely on extensive external knowledge bases.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A neural architecture for event linking that achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external, domain-specific semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. This significantly improves the model's portability and reduces its dependence on costly, manually-crafted knowledge bases.
2.  An empirical demonstration, supported by a detailed ablation study, that an effective combination of latent sentential features from a CNN, local lexical features, and standard pairwise features is crucial for high performance on this task.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance without External Knowledge Bases:** The model's strongest contribution is achieving competitive performance against previous systems while deliberately avoiding features derived from external knowledge bases. Many prior systems are heavily dependent on resources like WordNet, which limits their applicability to new domains. By relying only on features derivable from the text itself (via word embeddings and simple pairwise comparisons), this work presents a more general and portable solution for event linking.
2.  **Effective Combination of Feature Classes:** The paper provides a convincing ablation study (Table 5) that clearly demonstrates the value of each component of its feature set. The results show that while sentential features from the CNN are powerful, the model's performance is significantly boosted by the inclusion of both trigger-local lexical features and standard pairwise features. This highlights that for complex semantic tasks, a hybrid approach combining learned latent representations with well-chosen discrete features is highly effective.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Error Analysis:** The authors evaluate their model on the standard ACE benchmark and a new, significantly larger dataset (ACE++), demonstrating that the model benefits from more training data. The comparison against several baselines and the inclusion of a manual error analysis provide valuable insights. The analysis identifies key remaining challenges, such as resolving pronominal references to events, and notes the inherent ambiguity of the task, putting the reported performance metrics into a broader perspective.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unrealistic Experimental Setting:** The evaluation relies on gold-annotated event triggers, which is not representative of a real-world, end-to-end information extraction pipeline. This setup makes the comparison to at least one competitor system that used predicted arguments unfair and likely overestimates the model's practical performance. Furthermore, the authors note that their train/test split of the ACE dataset differs from those used in prior work, which complicates direct and rigorous comparison of results.
2.  **Lack of Clarity in Model Architecture:** Several key architectural and implementation details are underspecified, which hinders reproducibility. For example, the paper does not explain how the position embeddings are generated (e.g., are they randomly initialized and trained?), how the piecewise max-pooling operation is implemented to separate the contexts to the left and right of the trigger, or how non-binary pairwise features like sentence distance are encoded. The choice of a one-word context window for local lexical features also appears arbitrary and is not justified.
3.  **Limited Comparison on the New Dataset:** While the introduction of the larger ACE++ dataset is a positive step, the authors only use it to compare variations of their own model. The paper's claims would be substantially stronger if it also reported the performance of key baseline systems (especially the feature-rich model of Liu et al., 2014) on this new dataset. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the advantages of the proposed model hold as the data size increases.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify how the position embeddings mentioned in Section 3.1 are generated and learned? Are they randomly initialized and updated during training?
2.  Regarding the piecewise max-pooling layer, could you elaborate on how the sentence is partitioned into "left" and "right" sides relative to the event trigger, especially for multi-word triggers?
3.  Section 3.1 defines the final event-mention representation `v_e` as the output of a hidden layer. However, the description of the coreference model in Section 3.2 appears to use the concatenated representation `vsent+lex` directly as input. Could you clarify the exact vector representations for the two event mentions that are fed into the similarity network described in Section 3.2?
4.  How are the non-binary pairwise features, such as the distance between event mentions in sentences, encoded before being concatenated with the other feature vectors?
5.  The use of the `square` function as the non-linearity in the similarity layer is an interesting choice. To your knowledge, is this novel for this type of task? Have you considered its potential applicability in other similarity modeling scenarios?
6.  Given that the new ACE++ dataset was crucial for demonstrating your model's ability to generalize, are there any plans to make this dataset publicly available to facilitate future research and comparison?
