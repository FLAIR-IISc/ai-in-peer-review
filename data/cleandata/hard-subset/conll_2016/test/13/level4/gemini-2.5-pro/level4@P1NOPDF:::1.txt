This paper proposes a neural network model for event coreference resolution using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). For a given pair of event mentions, the model generates vector representations by passing word embeddings through a CNN with a max-pooling layer. These representations are then concatenated with the word embeddings of the immediate context words around each mention. Finally, this combined representation, along with a set of pairwise linguistic features, is fed into a single-layer neural network to produce a coreference score.

The model is evaluated on the standard ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to that of previous feature-rich systems. The paper's primary contribution is the development of a neural approach for event coreference that effectively combines learned representations with a small set of linguistic features. A key finding is that using word embeddings alone is insufficient for strong performance, highlighting the continued importance of incorporating explicit linguistic knowledge. A strength of the proposed approach is that the linguistic features used are simple and do not rely on extensive, manually-engineered external resources.

**Experimental Setup**

*   The experiments appear to use gold-standard event triggers rather than predicted ones. While the authors provide a justification for this choice, evaluating the model with predicted triggers would provide a more realistic measure of its performance in an end-to-end setting. This omission is particularly concerning because at least one of the competitor systems uses predicted triggers, which undermines the fairness of the comparison.
*   The use of a non-standard train/test split is a concern, as it complicates direct comparison with prior work. I would strongly encourage the authors to report results on established data splits to ensure fair and reproducible evaluation.

**Points for Clarification**

*   The statistics presented to motivate the need for cross-sentential information are convincing. However, I found the final statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) unclear and would appreciate a clarification.
*   The paper states that position embeddings are generated "in a way similar to word embeddings," but this process is not described in sufficient detail. How are these embeddings initialized (e.g., randomly)? Are they lexicalized? It is also not immediately obvious why the embedding for a relative position should be independent of the word it is relative to.
*   Could the authors clarify how the left and right context windows are used to create the final representation (lines 307-311)? Is the distinction between left and right contexts only used to inform the max-pooling operation, or does it influence the representation in other ways?
*   The model concatenates the embeddings of the single words immediately preceding and following the trigger. The choice of this one-word window seems arbitrary. Have the authors experimented with other window sizes, and if so, how does this choice affect performance?
*   The use of the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is unclear. In subsequent sections, only the v\_{sent+lex} representation is mentioned. Is v_e used in the final model, and if so, how?
*   Section 3.2 lacks detail on how the pairwise features are incorporated. I assume binary features are encoded as a binary vector, but how are continuous features like distance handled? Furthermore, are the representations for these features learned during training or are they fixed?

**Other Issues and Suggestions**

*   Have the authors considered applying this approach to entity coreference resolution? Doing so would enable comparison with a broader range of prior work on widely used datasets such as OntoNotes.
*   The use of a square function as a non-linearity is an interesting choice. Is this novel in this context? The authors might also comment on its potential applicability to other NLP tasks.
*   **Datasets**: While the ACE 2005 dataset is publicly available, the ACE++ extension is not. Do the authors plan to release this dataset to facilitate future research and replication? It would also strengthen the paper to include a comparison with the feature-rich systems on the ACE++ dataset.
*   **Results**: Many of the performance differences reported in the results tables are small. The authors should perform statistical significance testing to substantiate claims of improvement over baselines and competing systems.
*   **Related Work**: The related work section could be strengthened by discussing recent neural approaches to *entity* coreference resolution, such as the one proposed by Wiseman et al. (2015).

**Minor Issues**

*   Line 143: The word "that" appears to be redundant.
*   There is an inconsistency in terminology: a baseline is referred to as "same type" in Table 6 but "same event" in the text (line 670).
