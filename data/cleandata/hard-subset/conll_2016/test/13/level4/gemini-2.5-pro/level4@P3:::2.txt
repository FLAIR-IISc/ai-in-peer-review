### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The central idea is to replace the reliance on external knowledge bases (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet), which are common in previous systems, with learned "sentential features." The proposed two-part model first uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to generate a vector representation for each event mention by considering its trigger and the full sentential context. A second component then takes the representations of two event mentions, augments them with a set of standard pairwise features, and computes a coreference score. The model is evaluated on the standard ACE 2005 dataset and a new, larger dataset (ACE++) created by the authors, where it achieves state-of-the-art performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective demonstration of domain-independent event linking:** The primary contribution is empirical. The paper successfully demonstrates that a neural model relying on learned distributed representations can achieve state-of-the-art results on event linking without features from curated lexical-semantic resources. This is a significant result, as it points toward models that are more easily portable to new domains where such resources are unavailable.

2.  **Creation and analysis of a larger dataset:** The authors introduce a new, larger dataset (ACE++) and use it to show that their model's performance, particularly recall, improves significantly with more training data. The analysis of the impact of data size is a valuable finding for the community.

### Strengths

1.  **Reduced Dependency on External Knowledge:** The model's key strength is its departure from the heavy feature engineering and reliance on external knowledge bases that characterize previous state-of-the-art systems. By learning features directly from the text, the proposed approach is more adaptable and has the potential to be applied to domains where resources like WordNet or FrameNet have poor coverage. This is a clear and important step forward for the practical application of event coreference systems.

2.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation and Analysis:** The paper presents a strong empirical case for the model. It achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark and provides several insightful analyses. The feature ablation study in Table 5 clearly breaks down the contribution of each component (pairwise, local, and sentential features), demonstrating that all are valuable. Furthermore, the experiments on the ACE++ dataset (Table 4) provide compelling evidence that the model benefits substantially from additional training data, which is an important characteristic for data-driven neural methods.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained logically and accompanied by a helpful diagram (Figure 1). The authors effectively motivate their design choices with a preliminary analysis of the ACE dataset, highlighting challenges such as low argument overlap and varying trigger words between coreferential mentions, which justifies the need for richer contextual features.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Justification for Architectural Choices:** The core of the model's novelty lies in its use of a CNN to generate event mention representations. However, the paper does not sufficiently motivate the choice of a CNN over other prominent neural architectures for processing sequential text, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). Given that the performance improvements over the previous state-of-the-art are modest (around 2-3 F1 points on several metrics), a more rigorous justification is needed. An ablation study or comparison against an RNN-based encoder would have made the claim for this specific architecture much stronger.

2.  **Unclear Novelty in the Context of Neural Methods:** While applying a CNN to this specific task is new, the general approach of using a CNN to produce a sentence representation for a downstream classification task is a well-established technique in NLP. The paper would be strengthened by more explicitly situating the work in the context of other continuous-space or neural models for coreference resolution (both for entities and events, if they exist). The selection of competing methods in Table 3 feels incomplete, focusing only on traditional feature-based systems and missing a broader comparison to other potential representation learning approaches.

3.  **Insufficient Detail Regarding the ACE++ Dataset:** A significant portion of the paper's results and claims relies on the newly created ACE++ dataset. However, the paper provides no information on how the additional 1351 documents were sourced, annotated, or what distribution of event types they contain. The limited number and scope of event types in the original ACE 2005 corpus is a known issue. Without more detail, it is impossible to assess the diversity of ACE++ and to fully validate the paper's claim of domain independence. For the work to be reproducible and serve as a meaningful benchmark, the authors should be strongly encouraged to make this dataset and its documentation publicly available.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The error analysis correctly identifies the challenge of resolving pronominal references to events (e.g., "It sickens me"). Have you considered how your model could be augmented with, or compared against, a baseline that leverages an off-the-shelf anaphora resolution system to handle these specific cases?

2.  Table 5 shows that pairwise features provide a substantial performance boost. Could you elaborate on the feature selection process for this set? Specifically, which of these engineered features were most impactful, and were other features considered and discarded?

3.  The evaluation is conducted using gold-standard event mentions. How do you expect the model to perform in a more realistic end-to-end pipeline where event triggers and arguments are predicted by an upstream system? While a full experiment may be out of scope, any insights on the model's potential robustness to upstream errors would be valuable.
