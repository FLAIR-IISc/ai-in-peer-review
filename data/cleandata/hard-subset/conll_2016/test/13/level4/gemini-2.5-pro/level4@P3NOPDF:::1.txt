This paper presents a neural network model for event coreference resolution. Given two event mentions, the model generates vector representations by processing word embeddings of the sentential context through a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with max-pooling. This learned representation is then concatenated with embeddings of the local context words surrounding the event trigger. This combined vector, along with a set of pairwise linguistic features, is fed into a single-layer neural network to compute a final coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

**Strengths**

The main strength of this work lies in its development of a neural approach for event coreference that reduces the reliance on extensive, manually crafted features and external resources. The paper interestingly demonstrates that while word embeddings provide a powerful foundation, they are insufficient on their own for this task. The authors show that augmenting the neural representation with a small, well-chosen set of linguistic features provides a significant performance boost. This hybrid approach, combining learned representations with minimal, easily obtainable linguistic knowledge, is a valuable contribution.

**Major Concerns**

My primary concern lies with the experimental setup, which affects the validity and comparability of the results.

1.  **Use of Gold Triggers:** The model uses gold event triggers for both training and testing. While the authors provide a rationale for this choice, it limits the applicability of the model in a realistic end-to-end system. More importantly, it makes the comparison with at least one competing system, which uses predicted triggers, unfair. I strongly recommend the authors include results using predicted triggers to provide a more realistic evaluation and an equitable comparison.

2.  **Dataset Splits:** The use of a non-standard train/test split for the dataset is problematic as it hinders direct comparison with prior and future work. To ensure the reproducibility and comparability of their results, I would strongly urge the authors to adhere to established data splits used in previous work.

**Questions and Points for Clarification**

Several aspects of the methodology could be clarified to improve the reader's understanding and the paper's reproducibility.

1.  The argument for needing cross-sentential information is convincing, but the concluding statement of the second paragraph (lines 65-70) was unclear. Could the authors please rephrase or elaborate on this point?

2.  The generation of position embeddings is described as being "in a way similar to word embeddings." Could the authors provide more detail? Specifically, how are they initialized and trained? Are they lexicalized (i.e., tied to specific words)? It is not immediately clear why the embedding for a relative position should be the same regardless of the anchor word.

3.  How are the left and right context neighbors used to construct the final representation (lines 307-311)? Is the distinction between left and right only used to select different columns during the max-pooling operation, or is there another mechanism at play?

4.  The model appends the embeddings of one word before and one after the trigger. This choice seems somewhat arbitrary. Was this window size determined empirically? A brief justification or ablation study on the context window size would strengthen the paper.

5.  The event-mention representation `v_e` is defined in line 330, but it does not appear to be used in the subsequent sections, where `v_{sent+lex}` is used instead. Is this a typo, or is `v_e` used in a way that was not described? Please clarify.

6.  How are the pairwise features from Section 3.2 integrated into the model? Are the binary features encoded as a one-hot vector? How are continuous features like distance handled (e.g., discretized, normalized)? Are these feature representations learned or kept fixed during training?

**Other Suggestions**

1.  **Applicability to Entity Coreference:** Have the authors considered applying this model to nominal or pronominal entity coreference? Doing so could allow for comparison against a wider range of neural coreference systems on benchmark datasets like OntoNotes.

2.  **Square Function Non-linearity:** The use of a square function as a non-linearity is an interesting choice. Is this a novel contribution of this paper, or has it been used in similar contexts before? It would be beneficial to comment on its properties and potential applicability to other NLP tasks.

3.  **Dataset Availability:** The paper presents results on the non-public ACE++ dataset. To promote reproducibility and facilitate future comparisons, the authors should consider releasing this dataset. In its absence, it would have been valuable to see results from the feature-rich baseline systems on ACE++ as well.

4.  **Significance Testing:** Many of the reported performance differences between systems are small. The paper would be strengthened by the inclusion of statistical significance tests to substantiate the claims of improvement or comparability.

5.  **Related Work:** The related work section could be enhanced by including other neural approaches to coreference resolution, such as the contemporary work by Wiseman et al. (2015).

**Minor Issues**

*   Line 143: The word "that" in "show that how" appears to be redundant.
*   Line 670: The baseline is referred to as "same event" in the text but "same type" in Table 6. Please ensure consistent terminology.
