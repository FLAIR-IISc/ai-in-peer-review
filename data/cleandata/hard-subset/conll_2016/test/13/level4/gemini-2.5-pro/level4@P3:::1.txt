### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The model employs a two-part architecture. First, it uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with piecewise max-pooling to generate a latent feature representation for each event mention, capturing both trigger-local lexical information and broader sentential context. Second, it combines the representations of two event mentions with a set of standard pairwise features and feeds them into a single-layer network with a squared activation function, followed by a logistic regression classifier to produce a coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version (ACE++), achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art systems that rely on extensive external resources.

In my view, the contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong, resource-light neural architecture for event linking:** The primary contribution is the successful application of a CNN-based model to event coreference. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on handcrafted semantic features from external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet. This demonstrates the potential of learned sentential features for this task and significantly enhances the model's portability to new domains.

2.  **Thorough empirical evaluation and analysis:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including an ablation study that effectively demonstrates the complementary value of pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential features. Furthermore, the creation of the larger ACE++ dataset and the insightful error analysis provide a valuable resource and clear directions for future research in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-art performance without external knowledge bases:** The most significant strength of this work is its ability to match or exceed the performance of previous state-of-the-art systems (e.g., Liu et al., 2014) while deliberately avoiding complex, domain-specific features from external resources. This is a crucial step forward for event coreference resolution, as it makes the approach more generalizable and easier to adapt to new domains where resources like FrameNet may not be available.

2.  **Effective model design combining multiple feature types:** The ablation study in Table 5 convincingly shows that all three feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential) contribute positively to the model's performance. The architecture provides a principled way to integrate these diverse information sources, leveraging the strengths of both traditional, interpretable pairwise features and learned, dense representations from the CNN.

3.  **Comprehensive experiments and insightful analysis:** The paper is empirically solid. The experiments on two datasets of different sizes highlight the model's ability to benefit from more training data. The comparison between clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) is informative. The error analysis is particularly valuable, identifying challenging cases like pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies, which helps contextualize the performance of all systems on this difficult task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Issues with experimental comparability:** The main weakness concerns the experimental setup, which complicates a direct and fair comparison with prior work.
    *   The model is evaluated using gold-standard event triggers and arguments. While the authors justify this as a way to isolate the coreference task, it limits the assessment of the model's utility in a realistic end-to-end system. Crucially, at least one key competitor (Liu et al., 2014) uses system-predicted arguments, making the comparison of results potentially unfair and favouring the proposed model. Performance with predicted triggers and arguments should be reported for a more conclusive comparison.
    *   The authors acknowledge that different studies use different splits of the ACE dataset. While they release their own split, this fragmentation of evaluation standards makes it difficult to precisely track progress in the field. Adhering to a previously established split would have strengthened the claims.

2.  **Lack of statistical significance testing:** The performance differences reported in the tables, particularly between the proposed model and the system of Liu et al. (2014) in Table 3, and between different model variants in Table 5, are often small. Without statistical significance tests, it is difficult to determine whether these differences reflect a genuine improvement or are simply due to random variation in the data splits or model initialization. This is a necessary component for substantiating claims of state-of-the-art performance.

3.  **Insufficient justification for some model design choices:** Several architectural decisions seem arbitrary and lack clear motivation.
    *   The trigger-local features are formed by concatenating the embeddings of the trigger word with its immediate left and right neighbors. It is not explained why a window of size one was chosen over other possibilities.
    *   The use of the `square` function as the non-linearity in the similarity layer is interesting but not well justified. The authors state it is "backed by the intuition that for measuring similarity, an invariance under polarity changes is desirable," but this intuition is not fully unpacked. Is this a novel contribution, and has it been compared against more standard activations like ReLU or tanh in this specific layer?

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify how the position embeddings are generated and trained? The paper states they are generated "in a way similar to word embeddings." Are they randomly initialized and learned? Are they lexicalized, or is the same embedding used for a relative position (e.g., "+1") regardless of the trigger word it is relative to?

2.  There seems to be an inconsistency in the description of the model architecture. Section 3.1 describes the final event-mention representation `v_e` (Eq. 3) as the output of a hidden layer. However, Section 3.2 and Figure 1(b) seem to use `vsent+lex` (the input to that hidden layer) as the representation that is concatenated with pairwise features. Is `v_e` actually used in the final model, or is this a typo?

3.  How are the pairwise features encoded, especially the non-binary ones like the distance between mentions? Are they represented as one-hot vectors (e.g., for binned distances) or as single scalar values? Are these feature representations fixed or learned?

4.  The ACE++ dataset is a valuable resource that allows the model to generalize better. Do you have plans to release this dataset to the public to facilitate future research and standardized comparisons?

### Additional Comments

*   The discussion on applying the model to entity coreference resolution (e.g., on OntoNotes) would be a welcome addition, as it would broaden the paper's impact.
*   In the related work section, you might consider citing the neural entity coreference model of Wiseman et al. (ACL 2015), which also learns mention representations.
*   There is a minor inconsistency in naming: a baseline is referred to as "same event" in the text (line 670) but "same type" in Table 6.
