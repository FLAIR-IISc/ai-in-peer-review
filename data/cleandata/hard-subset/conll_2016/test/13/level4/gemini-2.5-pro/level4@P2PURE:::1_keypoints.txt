- Proposes a CNN-based neural model for event coreference that pools context embeddings, concatenates trigger-neighbor embeddings, and combines them with pairwise linguistic features in a single-layer NN.
- Reports performance on ACE 2005 and an expanded ACE++ dataset that is comparable to prior feature-rich systems.
- Main contribution: effective combination of learned word-embedding representations with a small curated set of linguistic features.
- Key finding: word embeddings alone are insufficient; a small set of linguistic features is crucial for strong performance.
- Experiments use gold event triggers rather than predicted triggers, which gives an optimistic and potentially unfair comparison to systems using predicted triggers.
- Uses a non-standard train/test split, hindering direct comparison and reproducibility relative to prior work.
- Evidence for the necessity of cross-sentential information is convincing, but a specific claim in the paper’s text (lines 65–70) is unclear and needs clarification.
- The method for generating position embeddings is not described—unclear whether they are randomly initialized, learned, lexicalized, or why relative position embeddings are context-independent.
- The mechanism for incorporating left/right neighbor contexts into the representation is under-explained—unclear if handled only by max-pooling or elsewhere in the model.
- The choice to concatenate only the single words immediately before and after the trigger is arbitrary and needs justification versus larger or no windows.
- The role and purpose of the event-mention representation v_e is unclear, since later sections appear to use only v_{sent+lex}.
- Section 3.2 lacks detail on how pairwise features are encoded, especially continuous features like distance, and whether feature embeddings are learned or fixed.
- Suggests evaluating/applying the model to entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes) to enable broader comparison with prior work.
- Requests clarification on the use of a square function as the non-linearity and whether this is a novel contribution applicable to other NLP tasks.
- Strongly recommends releasing the ACE++ dataset (or otherwise comparing baselines on it) to facilitate fair comparisons and future research.
- Many reported differences in scores are small; statistical significance testing should be performed to validate claimed improvements.
- Related work should discuss recent neural approaches to entity coreference (e.g., Wiseman et al., 2015).
- Minor: line 143 contains a redundant "that."
- Minor: inconsistent naming of a baseline—called "same type" in Table 6 but "same event" in the text; should be consistent.
