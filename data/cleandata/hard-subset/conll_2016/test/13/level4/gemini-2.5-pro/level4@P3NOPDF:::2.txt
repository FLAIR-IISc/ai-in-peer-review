This paper addresses the task of event entity linking, proposing a two-part model that leverages sentential features from a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) in place of external knowledge sources used by prior methods. The first part of the model learns a representation for each event mention, while the second part computes a coreference score between pairs of mentions.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow. The authors provide a valuable analysis of the ACE corpus, particularly regarding argument sharing between coreferent events. This analysis, along with the study of the dataset's size impact on performance, serves as excellent motivation for the introduction of their new ACE++ dataset.

Despite these strengths, there are several major issues that need to be addressed:

1.  **Motivation for Architectural Choices:** A significant weakness of the paper is the lack of motivation and analysis for the choice of a CNN to generate mention representations. The authors do not justify why a CNN is preferable to other sequence-modeling architectures, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). Given that the reported performance improvement over state-of-the-art models is modest (2-3 F1 points), a more thorough ablation study or comparison with alternative architectures is necessary to substantiate the claim that this specific architecture is superior for the task.

2.  **Clarity of Novelty and Baseline Comparison:** The novelty of the proposed method is not clearly articulated. While the application to event linking is interesting, the use of CNNs to extract sentential features for a classification-style task is a well-established technique. To better position their contribution, the authors should explicitly discuss and compare their work against other continuous-space or neural methods for event linking. The selection of baseline models in Table 3 feels incomplete and should be expanded to include more relevant recent work in this domain to provide a more thorough comparison.

3.  **Details and Availability of the ACE++ Dataset:** Crucial details regarding the new ACE++ dataset are missing. The paper provides no information about the data collection and annotation process. A known limitation of the original ACE dataset is its constrained set of event types; it is therefore essential to know which event types ACE++ covers and whether it offers greater diversity. Furthermore, to support the claim that the proposed model is more domain-independent (Section 5.1), the authors should demonstrate its effectiveness on a dataset where resource-heavy prior methods might fail. For the benefit of the research community and to enable meaningful future comparisons, the authors are strongly encouraged to make this dataset publicly available.

In addition, there are several minor issues and suggestions for improvement:

*   The experimental results in Table 3 should also include the model's performance using predicted mentions, not just gold mentions, to provide a more realistic assessment of its capabilities in a full pipeline.
*   It would be beneficial to discuss the potential for integrating the proposed model with a general-purpose coreference resolution system. For instance, in the example from line 687, an off-the-shelf system like CoreNLP correctly links 'It' to 'bombing'. Exploring how such information could be leveraged would strengthen the paper.
*   Given the relatively small size of the ACE dataset, evaluation on other available resources, such as the EventCorefBank, would provide more robust evidence of the model's effectiveness and generalizability. This could also motivate future work on jointly modeling entity and event coreference.
*   Table 5 indicates that pairwise features contribute significantly to performance, suggesting that feature engineering remains important. The paper would be improved by an analysis of these features, detailing which were most impactful and outlining the process for their selection over other alternatives.
