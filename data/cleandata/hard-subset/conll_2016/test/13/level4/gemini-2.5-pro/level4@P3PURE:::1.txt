This paper proposes a model for event linking that leverages Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). For each event mention, the authors generate a vector representation by passing word embeddings through a CNN, followed by a max-pooling layer. This representation is then concatenated with word embeddings from the context surrounding the mention. This combined vector, along with several pairwise features, is processed by a single-layer neural network to compute a final coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

The main contribution of this work is the development of a neural approach for event linking that successfully combines word embeddings with a set of linguistic features. A key finding is that word embeddings alone are insufficient for strong performance, demonstrating the continued importance of incorporating linguistic knowledge. A strength of the proposed feature set is that it is limited and does not rely on manually-crafted external resources.

**Experimental Setting**

*   The use of gold trigger words, rather than predicted ones, is a significant concern. While the authors justify this choice, it would be valuable to see performance with predicted triggers. This is particularly important because at least one competitor system used predicted triggers, making the comparison in its current form inequitable.
*   The use of non-standard train/test splits is concerning, as it complicates direct comparison with prior work. I strongly encourage the authors to evaluate their model on established data splits to ensure reproducibility and fair comparison.

**Questions and Points for Clarification**

*   The evidence presented for the necessity of cross-sentential information is convincing. However, the final statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) is unclear and requires further explanation.
*   The generation of position embeddings is described as being "in a way similar to word embeddings," but this needs more detail. How are they initialized (e.g., randomly)? Are they lexicalized? The paper should clarify the rationale for a relative position having the same embedding regardless of the word it is adjacent to.
*   The mechanism for incorporating left and right neighbors into the representation (lines 307-311) is not fully described. Is their influence limited to the max-pooling operation, or are they used in another way?
*   The choice to append the embeddings of only one word before and one word after the trigger seems arbitrary. The authors should provide a justification for this specific window size over other potential configurations.
*   The role of the event-mention representation `v_e` (line 330) is unclear. Subsequent sections appear to use `v_{sent+lex}` exclusively, and it is not obvious how or if `v_e` is incorporated into the final model.
*   Section 3.2 should clarify how the pairwise features are encoded and used. While most appear to be binary, it is not specified how continuous features like distance are handled. Additionally, are these feature representations fixed or updated during training?

**Other Issues and Suggestions**

*   Have the authors considered applying this approach to entity coreference resolution? Doing so would enable comparison with a broader range of previous work on widely-used datasets such as OntoNotes.
*   The use of a square function as a non-linearity is an interesting choice. Is this a novel contribution of the paper? A brief discussion of its properties and potential applicability to other tasks would be welcome.
*   While one dataset used is public, the ACE++ dataset is not. The authors should state whether they plan to release it, as this would be a valuable contribution to the community. In the absence of a public release, it would have been beneficial to include comparisons to the feature-rich baseline systems on this dataset as well.
*   Many of the reported results are numerically close. The inclusion of significance testing would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and provide more conclusive comparisons between systems.
*   In the related work section, the authors should consider citing the neural approach to entity coreference resolution by Wiseman et al. (2015).

**Minor Issues**

*   Line 143: The word "that" is redundant.
*   The baseline is referred to as "same type" in Table 6 but "same event" in the text (line 670). Please ensure consistent terminology.
