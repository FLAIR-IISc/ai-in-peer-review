This paper presents a model for event entity linking that uses sentential features generated by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), eschewing the external knowledge sources relied upon by previous methods. The proposed model consists of two components: the first learns a representation for each event mention, and the second calculates a coreference score between pairs of these representations.

The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to follow. The analysis of argument sharing between coreferent events in the ACE corpus is particularly insightful. Furthermore, the analysis of the impact of dataset size provides a compelling motivation for the creation of their new ACE++ dataset. However, several major issues need to be addressed before this paper can be considered for publication.

**Major Issues**

1.  **Motivation for CNN Architecture:** A central weakness is the lack of justification for the specific architectural choice of a CNN for generating mention representations. The paper does not discuss why a CNN was chosen over other plausible alternatives, such as an RNN, nor does it present any comparative experiments. Given that the reported improvements over the state-of-the-art are modest (2-3 F1 points), a more rigorous justification and empirical evidence are required to demonstrate the superiority of this specific architecture for the task.

2.  **Novelty and Comparison to Related Work:** The paper's novelty is not clearly articulated. While the application of sentential features to this task is the focus, using CNNs for text classification and representation learning is a well-established technique. The authors should explicitly position their work relative to any existing continuous-space or neural methods for event linking, if they exist. The selection of baseline methods in Table 3 is not comprehensive enough and should include more recent and relevant approaches to properly contextualize the paper's contribution.

3.  **ACE++ Dataset Details:** The introduction of the ACE++ dataset is a potentially valuable contribution, but it is undermined by a complete lack of information regarding its creation, scope, and annotation process. A significant limitation of the original ACE dataset is its small number of event types, which can lead to models that are biased or overfit to that specific domain. It is crucial for the authors to describe the event types covered by ACE++ to substantiate their claim that their "approach does not depend on resources with restricted" domains (Section 5.1). To validate this claim, the authors should demonstrate that their model succeeds on a dataset where knowledge-based approaches might fail. For the sake of reproducibility and to enable meaningful future comparisons, the authors are strongly encouraged to make this dataset publicly available.

**Minor Issues and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   For a more complete picture of the model's performance in a realistic setting, Table 3 should be expanded to include results from a system that does not use gold event mentions.

*   It would be beneficial to explore the potential integration of this model with a standard entity coreference resolution system. For instance, in the example from line 687, an off-the-shelf system like CoreNLP can readily link "It" to "bombing." A discussion of how such information could be leveraged, perhaps even in a baseline model, would strengthen the paper.

*   Given the relatively small scale of the ACE dataset, the model's robustness would be more convincingly demonstrated by evaluating on additional resources, such as the EventCorefBank. This would also provide further motivation for work that addresses entity and event coreference simultaneously.

*   The ablation study in Table 5 reveals the significant impact of pairwise features, which suggests that traditional feature engineering remains crucial for achieving competitive performance, at least on datasets the size of ACE. The paper would be improved by an analysis of the most effective features, a description of how the current feature set was chosen, and what other features were explored.
