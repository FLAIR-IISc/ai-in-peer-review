### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural network model for event coreference resolution (event linking). The model operates in two stages. First, it generates a vector representation for each individual event mention by applying a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) over the word and position embeddings of the containing sentence. This sentence-level representation, generated via piecewise max-pooling around the event trigger, is then concatenated with the word embeddings of the trigger and its immediate neighbors. In the second stage, the representations of two event mentions are combined with a set of standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance). This combined vector is fed into a single-layer neural network to compute a distributed similarity representation, which is then passed to a logistic regression classifier to produce a final coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded, private version (ACE++), demonstrating that its performance is comparable to previous state-of-the-art systems that rely on rich, external knowledge bases.

### Main Contributions

1.  **Effective knowledge-free event linking:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that a neural architecture can achieve state-of-the-art performance on event linking without relying on external, domain-specific semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. By learning latent sentential features directly from the data, the model offers a more portable and less feature-engineering-intensive approach compared to previous work, which is a significant advantage for applying event linking to new domains.

2.  **Ablation analysis of feature sources:** The paper provides a valuable ablation study that systematically evaluates the contribution of its three main feature classes: learned sentential features (from the CNN), local lexical features (trigger context), and standard pairwise features. This analysis clearly shows that while the learned sentential features are critical, the model's strong performance stems from the effective combination of all three sources.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong performance without external resources:** The model's ability to perform on par with feature-rich state-of-the-art systems is its most compelling strength. By replacing handcrafted features derived from knowledge bases with learned neural representations, the work successfully reduces the dependency on resources that are costly to create and limited in domain coverage. This makes the proposed approach more general and adaptable.

2.  **Thorough experimental evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments to validate their model. Beyond comparing to the state-of-the-art, they analyze the impact of dataset size (Table 4), compare different clustering strategies (Table 4), and present a detailed ablation study on feature classes (Table 5). This rigorous evaluation adds significant weight to their conclusions.

3.  **Insightful error analysis:** The paper includes a manual error analysis that identifies key remaining challenges in the task, such as resolving pronominal references to events and inconsistencies in human annotation for boundary cases. This analysis is valuable as it provides a clear-eyed perspective on the limitations of the current model and offers concrete directions for future research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Issues with experimental setup and comparability:** The experimental setup raises some concerns about the direct comparability of the results.
    *   The use of gold-annotated event triggers simplifies the task and may not reflect performance in a realistic, end-to-end system. More importantly, this makes the comparison to prior work that used predicted arguments potentially unfair.
    *   The authors acknowledge that different papers use different train/test splits of the ACE dataset, which complicates a direct comparison of reported scores. It would be preferable to adhere to a previously established split to ensure a fair and direct comparison.

2.  **Lack of clarity in model description:** Several architectural details are ambiguous or under-motivated, which hinders the full understanding and reproducibility of the work.
    *   The generation of "position embeddings" is described vaguely as being "similar to word embeddings." It is unclear if these are randomly initialized, how they are learned, or why a single relative position embedding should be applicable across different lexical contexts.
    *   The choice to use the word embeddings of exactly one word before and after the trigger seems arbitrary. An explanation or ablation study on this choice would strengthen the paper.
    *   The role of the final event-mention representation `v_e` (Eq. 3) is unclear, as subsequent sections and Figure 1 appear to use the intermediate representation `v_sent+lex` for the pairwise scoring step.

3.  **Reproducibility and evaluation concerns:** A significant portion of the analysis is performed on the ACE++ dataset, which is not publicly available. While this demonstrates the model's ability to benefit from more data, it prevents the community from verifying these results or using the dataset for future comparisons. Additionally, given that some performance differences in the tables are small, the lack of statistical significance testing makes it difficult to ascertain whether the claimed improvements are robust.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify how the position embeddings are generated and initialized? Are they learned from scratch? Are they shared across all contexts, or are they specific to the trigger word's position?

2.  What was the rationale for choosing a local context window of exactly one word before and after the trigger? Have you experimented with other window sizes (e.g., zero or two words), and if so, how did it affect performance?

3.  Could you clarify the role of the final event-mention representation `v_e` from Equation 3? It appears that `v_sent+lex` is used in the subsequent pairwise classification step (Figure 1b and Eq. 4). Is `v_e` used elsewhere, or is this a discrepancy in the description?

4.  How are the non-binary pairwise features, such as the distance between mentions and the argument overlap count, encoded before being concatenated into the feature vector `v_sent+lex+pairw`?

5.  The use of a `square` function as the non-linearity in Equation 4 is an interesting choice. Is this a novel contribution of your work? Could you elaborate on why this was more effective for learning a similarity metric compared to more standard activation functions like ReLU or tanh?

### Additional Comments

*   The proposed neural approach seems general enough to be applicable to entity coreference resolution. It would be interesting to see if this model can be adapted to that task, which would allow for comparison against a wider range of neural coreference models on popular datasets like OntoNotes.
*   In the related work section, consider citing the neural entity coreference work by Wiseman et al. (ACL 2015), which also learns mention representations for ranking.
*   There is a minor inconsistency in the text: the baseline in Table 6 is labeled "Same type," but it is referred to as "same event" in the text (line 670).
