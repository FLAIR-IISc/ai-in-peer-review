This paper presents a model for event entity linking that employs sentential features from a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), replacing the external knowledge sources used in previous work. The proposed model consists of two components: the first learns a representation for each event mention, and the second calculates a coreference score between two mention representations.

The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to follow. A notable strength is the analysis of argument sharing between coreferent events in the ACE corpus. Furthermore, the analysis of the impact of dataset size provides a strong motivation for the creation of the new ACE++ dataset. However, the paper has several major weaknesses that require attention:

-   The paper fails to adequately motivate the choice of a CNN for generating mention representations. There is no discussion of the pros and cons of this architecture or any comparison to viable alternatives, such as an RNN. Given that the model's F1 score improvement over the state-of-the-art is modest (2-3 percentage points), more evidence is needed to demonstrate that this specific architecture is superior.

-   The paper's novelty is not clearly articulated. The use of CNNs with sentential features for classification tasks is a well-established technique, making it unclear what is novel about this specific application to event linking. The authors should explicitly position their work against other continuous-space methods for this task, as the selection of baselines in Table 3 is insufficient.

-   The paper provides no information regarding the creation of the ACE++ dataset. A well-known issue with the original ACE dataset is its limited number of event types, which makes it constrained and potentially biased. It is crucial to know which event types ACE++ includes. This information is also necessary to substantiate the claim in Section 5.1 that "our approach does not depend on resources with restricted..." coverage. To validate this claim, the authors should demonstrate their model's superior performance on a dataset where resource-heavy methods falter. For the sake of reproducibility and to enable future comparisons, the authors are strongly encouraged to make the ACE++ dataset publicly available.

Some minor issues:

-   The experiments would be more comprehensive if Table 3 also included the model's performance in a setting without gold references.

-   The paper could be strengthened by exploring how the proposed model could be augmented with a standard nominal coreference resolution system. For instance, in the example provided (line 687), an off-the-shelf system like CoreNLP correctly links 'It' to 'bombing'. Leveraging such information could form a strong baseline.

-   Given the small scale of the ACE dataset, the claims of the paper would be more compelling if the model were evaluated on additional resources. Testing on the EventCorefBank in parallel with ACE would be an essential step. This also reinforces the motivation for jointly modeling entity and event coreference.

-   According to Table 5, the handcrafted pairwise features are highly effective. This suggests that traditional feature engineering remains crucial for achieving competitive performance, at least at the scale of the ACE dataset. The paper would benefit from an ablation study or analysis detailing which of these features were most impactful, the rationale for their selection, and what other features were considered.
