This paper presents a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach for the task of event linking. The authors generate vector representations for event mentions by feeding word embeddings into a CNN, which is followed by a max-pooling layer. These representations are then concatenated with the embeddings of words immediately surrounding the mention. This combined vector, along with a set of pairwise features, is fed into a single-layer neural network to produce a similarity vector and compute a final coreference score.

The proposed model is evaluated on the standard ACE dataset as well as an extended version, achieving performance that is on par with previous, heavily feature-engineered systems.

In my view, the primary contribution of this work lies in the development of a neural architecture for event linking that successfully integrates word embeddings with select linguistic features. It is a noteworthy finding that word embeddings alone are insufficient for achieving high performance. Encouragingly, the incorporated linguistic features are limited in number and do not depend on manually-crafted external resources.

**Experimental Setting**
*   The experiments appear to utilize gold-standard trigger words rather than system-predicted ones. While the authors provide a justification for this choice, an evaluation using predicted triggers would offer a more realistic measure of performance. This setup is particularly problematic as it leads to an unfair comparison with a competing system that operates on predicted triggers.
*   The use of different train/test splits from prior work is a concern for comparability. I would strongly encourage the authors to adhere to established data splits wherever possible to ensure consistent and direct comparisons with previous research.

**Unclear Points**
*   The data convincingly demonstrates the need for cross-sentential information. However, I found the final statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) to be unclear.
*   The paper states that position embeddings are generated "in a way similar to word embeddings," but this description lacks sufficient detail. How are they generated? Are they randomly initialized and learned? Are they lexicalized? The rationale for a relative position having the same embedding regardless of its adjacent word is not obvious.
*   Could the authors elaborate on how left and right neighbors are used to form the representation (lines 307-311)? Is their influence confined to the max-pooling operation?
*   Appending the embeddings of the single word immediately before and after the trigger seems like an arbitrary choice. What is the justification for this specific window size over other potential configurations?
*   The role of the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is not clear. In subsequent sections, only the v{sent+lex} representation appears to be utilized.
*   Section 3.2 should clarify how pairwise features are incorporated. I assume the binary features are encoded as a binary vector, but how are continuous values like the distance feature handled? Furthermore, are these feature representations kept fixed or updated during training?

**Other Issues and Suggestions**
*   Could this approach be adapted for entity coreference resolution? Doing so would permit comparisons with a much broader body of literature and on popular datasets like OntoNotes.
*   The use of a square function as a nonlinearity is interesting. Is this a novel choice in this context? Do the authors believe it has potential applications in other tasks?
*   Regarding the datasets, one is publicly available, but results on the ACE++ dataset, which is not public, are also presented. Are there plans to release ACE++? This would greatly aid reproducibility and future comparisons. At a minimum, it would have been beneficial to see a comparison to the feature-rich systems on this dataset as well.
*   Some of the reported performance numbers are very close. The inclusion of statistical significance testing would help to substantiate the claims of improvement.
*   In the related work section, it would be relevant to discuss the neural network approach to (entity) coreference resolution by Wiseman et al. (2015).

**Minor Issues**
*   Line 143: The word "that" is redundant.
*   There is an inconsistency in the naming of a baseline: it is called "same type" in Table 6 but "same event" in the text (line 670).

**Refs**
