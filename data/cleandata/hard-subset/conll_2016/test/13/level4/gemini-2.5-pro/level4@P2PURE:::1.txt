This paper proposes a neural model for event coreference resolution using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). For each event mention, the model generates a vector representation by applying a CNN and a max-pooling layer over the word embeddings of the mention's context. This representation is then concatenated with embeddings of the words immediately surrounding the event trigger. This combined vector, along with a set of pairwise linguistic features, is fed into a single-layer neural network to produce a final coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to that of previous feature-rich systems.

The paper's primary contribution is a neural approach to event coreference that effectively combines learned representations from word embeddings with a curated set of linguistic features. A key finding is that word embeddings alone are insufficient for achieving strong performance. The authors demonstrate that incorporating a small set of linguistic features, which do not rely on extensive external resources, is crucial for the model's success.

### Experimental Setting
- The experiments use gold event triggers, not predicted ones. While the authors justify this choice, evaluating the model with predicted triggers would provide a more realistic measure of its performance. This is a significant concern because at least one of the compared systems uses predicted triggers, making the current comparison inequitable.
- The use of a non-standard train/test split hinders direct comparison with prior work. To improve reproducibility and facilitate clearer comparisons, the authors are encouraged to adopt the data splits used in previous studies.

### Unclear Points
- The evidence presented for the necessity of cross-sentential information is convincing. However, the final statement of the second paragraph (lines 65-70) is unclear and requires clarification.
- The paper states that position embeddings are generated "in a way similar to word embeddings," but this process is not detailed. Are these embeddings randomly initialized and learned? Are they lexicalized? It is unclear why the embedding for a relative position (e.g., +1) should be context-independent and thus identical for all words.
- The mechanism for incorporating left and right neighbor contexts into the representation (lines 307-311) needs further explanation. Is this distinction handled solely within the max-pooling operation, or does it influence other parts of the model?
- The choice to concatenate the embeddings of only the single words immediately preceding and following the trigger seems arbitrary. The authors should provide a justification for this specific window size over other potential choices (e.g., a larger context window or no window).
- The role of the event-mention representation v_e, introduced in line 330, is unclear. Subsequent sections seem to exclusively use the v_{sent+lex} representation, leaving the purpose of v_e unexplained.
- Section 3.2 lacks detail on how pairwise features are incorporated. While most features are binary and can be represented as a one-hot vector, the encoding for continuous features like distance is not specified. Additionally, it is not mentioned whether these feature representations are learned or kept fixed during training.

### Other Issues and Suggestions
- The authors could strengthen the paper by discussing the applicability of their model to the related task of entity coreference resolution. An evaluation on a standard dataset like OntoNotes would enable comparison with a much broader range of prior work.
- The use of a square function for non-linearity is an interesting choice. The authors should clarify if this is a novel contribution and comment on its potential applicability to other NLP tasks.
- While one dataset used is public, the ACE++ dataset is not. The authors are strongly encouraged to release ACE++ to facilitate future research and fair comparisons. In its absence, a comparison against the feature-rich baseline systems on ACE++ would have been valuable.
- Many of the reported performance scores are very close. The authors should perform statistical significance testing to substantiate claims of improvement over baselines and competing models.
- The related work section could be improved by including a discussion of recent neural approaches to the closely related problem of entity coreference resolution, such as Wiseman et al. (2015).

### Minor Issues
- Line 143: The word "that" is redundant.
- One of the baselines is referred to as "same type" in Table 6, but "same event" in the text (line 670). This should be consistent.
