### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a neural model for event coreference resolution. For each event mention, the model generates a representation by applying a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) over the word embeddings of the sentence. This sentence-level representation, derived via piecewise max-pooling relative to the trigger word, is then concatenated with local lexical features (embeddings of the trigger and its immediate neighbors). Pairs of these event mention representations, along with a set of standard pairwise features, are fed into a final network that computes a coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance that is competitive with previous feature-rich systems.

### Contributions

The primary contribution of this work is a neural architecture for event linking that effectively combines latent, sentence-level features from a CNN with a small set of local lexical and pairwise features. The work demonstrates that this combination is sufficient to achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on expensive, manually-crafted external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet, making the approach more portable across domains.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance without External Resources:** The most significant strength of this work is achieving competitive, and in some cases superior, performance to previous state-of-the-art systems while eschewing the use of rich semantic features from external knowledge bases. This is a valuable contribution, as it lowers the barrier to applying event coreference systems to new domains where such resources may not be available.

2.  **Effective Combination of Feature Types:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that sentential features from the CNN, local lexical features around the trigger, and traditional pairwise features are all complementary and contribute to the final performance. The ablation study in Table 5 clearly illustrates the impact of each feature class, justifying the model's design.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on two datasets, including an expanded version of ACE that provides more training data. They analyze the impact of dataset size and clustering strategy, and compare their model against several sensible baselines. The inclusion of an error analysis provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task, such as pronominal event coreference.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Issues with Experimental Setting and Comparability:** The experimental setup raises two concerns that weaken the claims of state-of-the-art performance.
    *   First, the experiments rely on gold-standard event triggers. While the authors provide a rationale, this is an unrealistic setting for a real-world pipeline and makes a direct comparison with systems that use predicted triggers (such as Liu et al., 2014) problematic. Performance would likely be lower in a more realistic end-to-end scenario.
    *   Second, the authors note that different papers use different train/test splits of the ACE dataset. This known issue in the literature makes direct comparisons of reported scores difficult. While not the authors' fault, it would strengthen the paper to use a previously established split if possible, or at least to more explicitly acknowledge the potential for variance when comparing scores.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Motivation for Model Details:** Several key aspects of the model are not clearly explained, and some design choices appear arbitrary.
    *   The generation of position embeddings is described vaguely as "in a way similar to word embeddings." It is unclear if these are randomly initialized and learned, or how they are structured. For example, is the embedding for a relative position of `+1` the same regardless of the trigger word it is relative to?
    *   The choice to use a context window of exactly one word before and after the trigger for the local lexical features seems arbitrary. A justification for this specific window size, perhaps through experimentation, is missing.
    *   The final representation of an event mention, `v_e`, is introduced in Equation (3) but then appears to be unused. Equation (4) and Figure 1 suggest that the preceding representation, `v_sent+lex`, is used in the pairwise scoring step. This is confusing and needs clarification.

3.  **Dataset Availability and Baselines:** A significant portion of the experiments and analysis is performed on the ACE++ dataset, which is not publicly available. While creating and using a larger dataset is a strength, its private nature hinders reproducibility. It would also have been very informative to see a comparison with the feature-rich baseline systems on this larger dataset, not just on the standard ACE test set.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify the precise input to the similarity function in Section 3.2? The text implies that the final event representations `v_e` are used, but Equation (4) and Figure 1 seem to use the intermediate `v_sent+lex` representation. Which one is correct?

2.  How are position embeddings generated and updated? Are they randomly initialized? Are they tied to specific lexical items, or is the embedding for position `+1` the same regardless of the trigger word?

3.  How are the pairwise features from Section 3.2 encoded, particularly non-binary features like distance? Are these feature representations fixed or learned during training?

4.  The use of a square function as the non-linearity in the similarity layer is an interesting choice. Is this a novel contribution, or has it been used in similar contexts? Do you have any intuition about its applicability to other similarity-matching tasks?

5.  Do you have plans to release the ACE++ dataset? This would be a valuable resource for the community and would allow for more direct comparisons in future work.

### Additional Comments

*   The work could be situated more broadly by discussing its potential application to entity coreference resolution, which would allow for comparison against a wider range of neural coreference models on popular datasets like OntoNotes. The work of Wiseman et al. (ACL 2015) is a relevant recent example of a neural approach to entity coreference.
*   Given that some of the performance differences in the tables are small, adding significance tests would help substantiate the claims of improvement over baselines and competing systems.
*   There is a minor inconsistency in the text: the third baseline in Table 6 is referred to as "same type" in the table caption but "same event" in the main text (line 670).
