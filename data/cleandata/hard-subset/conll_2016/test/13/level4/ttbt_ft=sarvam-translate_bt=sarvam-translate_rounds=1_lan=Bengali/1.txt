This paper uses CNN to create a model for event linking. Based on the event mentions provided, the authors create vector representations based on word embeddings sent through CNN and subsequently followed by max-pooling. They combine the representations obtained with several word embeddings around the mentions. With specific pair features, they create a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network and calculate a coreference score. This model has been tested in an expanded version with an ACE dataset and comparable performance to previous feature-rich systems.

In my opinion, the main contribution of this paper is creating a neural method for entity linking by combining several linguistic features with word embeddings. It's good to know that good results cannot be achieved using only word embeddings. Fortunately, the linguistic features used are limited and do not require manually created external resources.

Experimental Context:
- It seems that gold trigger words have been used here instead of predicted words. The authors have given a reasonable explanation for this, although I would have liked to see performance with predicted triggers. This is particularly problematic because one of the competing systems used predicted triggers, so the comparison is not fair.
- Different research papers have used different training/testing partitions, which is a cause for concern. I would encourage the authors to stick to the previous partitions as much as possible.

Ambiguous Points:
- The numbers requiring cross-sentential information are plausible. However, the last statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) was not clear to me.
- Embeddings for positions are said to be "same as word embeddings." How? Are they randomly initialized? Are they lexicalized? The reason for having the same embedding for the relative position of one word next to another is not clear to me.
- How are left and right neighbors used to create representations (lines 307-11)? Does this only affect the max-pooling operation?
- The word embeddings of a word before and after trigger words are added to it. This seems somewhat contradictory. Why not prefer a word before and after and another?
- It is not clear how event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is used. Only v{sent+lex} is used in the following sections, not ve.
- How are pair features used in section 3.2? Most features are binary, so I assume that they are encoded as binary vectors, but what about, for example, the distance feature? And, are they kept constant during training?

Other Issues and Suggestions:
- Can this method also be applied to entity coreference resolution? This would help in comparing it with previous work and popular datasets like OntoNotes.
- It is interesting to use a square function. Is this new? Do you think it can be used in other tasks as well?
- Dataset: A dataset is publicly available, but the results are also presented with ACE++, which is not the case. Do you plan to publish it? This would help other researchers compare new methods. At least, it would be nice to see a comparison with feature-rich systems in this dataset.
- Results: Some of the numbers reported in the results are quite close. Significance testing will help prove the comparisons.
- Related Work: Among the work on (entity) coreference resolution, the neural network method of Weissman et al. (2015) can be mentioned.

Minor Problems:
- Line 143, the word "that" is redundant.
- Table 6 mentions a baseline as "same type," but the text (line 670) mentions "same event."

References:
