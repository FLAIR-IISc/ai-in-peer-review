This paper introduces a CNN-based model for event linking. The authors represent event mentions using vector embeddings derived from word embeddings processed through a CNN and max-pooling layer. These representations are then combined with embeddings of surrounding words and pairwise features, ultimately fed into a single-layer neural network to produce a coreference score. The model’s performance is evaluated on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving results comparable to existing feature-rich systems. 

The primary contribution lies in the development of a neural event linking approach that effectively integrates word embeddings with a selection of linguistic features. The findings highlight that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for optimal performance, while demonstrating the value of incorporating limited, non-hand-crafted linguistic features.

**Experimental Setup:**

- The use of gold trigger words, rather than predicted ones, is a potential limitation. While the authors provide justification, evaluating performance with predicted triggers would be valuable, particularly given that a competing system utilizes them, creating an uneven comparison.
- Inconsistencies in train/test splits across different papers are a concern. Adhering to established splits would enhance reproducibility and comparability.

**Unclear Points:**

- The evidence supporting the need for cross-sentential information is compelling. However, the statement in lines 65-70 of the second paragraph requires clarification.
- The generation of positional embeddings “in a way similar to word embeddings” lacks detail. Specifically, are they randomly initialized, or are they lexicalized? The rationale for assigning the same embedding to relative positions regardless of the word they are associated with is unclear.
- The method for incorporating left versus right neighbor information (lines 307-311) is not fully explained. Does this impact only the max-pooling operation?
- Appending the embeddings of only the immediately preceding and following words to the trigger word seems arbitrary. The reasoning behind this specific window size is unclear.
- The utilization of the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* (line 330) is unclear, as subsequent sections appear to only employ *v<sub>sent+lex</sub>*.
- The implementation of pairwise features in section 3.2 requires further explanation. While binary features are likely encoded as a binary vector, the handling of continuous features like distance is unclear. Additionally, it is unknown whether these features are fixed during training.

**Other Issues and Suggestions:**

- Exploring the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution would broaden its scope and allow for comparison with a wider range of existing work and established datasets like OntoNotes.
- The use of a squared function as a nonlinearity is noteworthy. Is this a novel approach, and if so, does it demonstrate potential for application in other tasks?
- Dataset availability is a concern. While one dataset is publicly accessible, results are also reported on ACE++, which is not. Releasing ACE++ would facilitate comparison with future research. At a minimum, a comparison of feature-rich systems on ACE++ would be beneficial.
- The reported results exhibit close values in some instances. Employing significance testing would strengthen the validity of the comparisons.
- The related work section could benefit from mentioning the neural network approach for (entity) coreference resolution by Wiseman et al. (2015).

**Minor Issues:**

- Line 143 contains a redundant “that.”
- Table 6 refers to a baseline as “same type,” while the text (line 670) uses “same event.”

**References:**
