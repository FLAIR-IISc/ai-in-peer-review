This paper introduces a model for event entity linking, proposing the use of sentential features extracted via CNNs as an alternative to external knowledge sources commonly employed in prior work. The model consists of two components: one for learning event mention representations and another for calculating a coreference score between event mentions. 

The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and easy to understand. The analysis of argument sharing within event coreferences on the ACE corpus is particularly insightful. Additionally, the dataset size analysis provides strong justification for the creation of the ACE++ dataset. However, several significant concerns require attention:

- The authors do not adequately justify their choice of CNNs for generating mention representations, nor do they analyze the advantages and disadvantages of this approach. A comparison to alternative architectures, such as RNNs, is missing. Given the modest performance gains – approximately 2-3 F1 points over the state-of-the-art – more substantial evidence is needed to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed CNN-based architecture.
- The novelty of utilizing sentential features for event linking is unclear, as applying CNNs for classification tasks is a well-established practice. The authors should explicitly highlight and compare their work to existing continuous space methods for event linking. The comparison in Table 3 is not comprehensive enough.
- The methodology for collecting the ACE++ dataset is not described. A known limitation of the ACE dataset is its restricted number of event types, which can introduce bias. Clarification regarding the event types covered in ACE++ is crucial, and would strengthen the claim in Section 5.1 regarding the limitations of approaches reliant on domain-specific semantic features. Demonstrating the failure of previous methods on a dataset where the proposed approach succeeds would be highly impactful. Furthermore, making ACE++ publicly available would facilitate future research and enable more meaningful comparisons.

Several minor points could also be addressed:

- Including performance results without gold references in Table 3 would be beneficial.
- Exploring the potential for integrating this model with a standard coreference resolution system would be valuable. For instance, leveraging existing systems like CoreNLP, which can readily link pronouns to event mentions (as demonstrated in line 687), could inform a stronger baseline.
- Given the limited size of the ACE dataset, evaluating the model on additional resources is recommended. This would further motivate the simultaneous consideration of entity and event coreference. Parallel evaluation on EventCorefBank alongside ACE is essential.
- Table 5 indicates the effectiveness of pairwise features, suggesting that feature engineering remains important for achieving competitive performance, particularly on the scale of the ACE dataset. Reporting the selection process for the current feature set, and detailing any alternative features explored, would be informative. Identifying the most effective features and the rationale behind their success would be insightful.
