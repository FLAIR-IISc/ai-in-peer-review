This paper uses CNN to create a model for event linking. Based on the event mentions provided, the authors create vector representations based on word embeddings sent through CNN, and subsequently follow max-pooling. They combine the representations obtained with several word embeddings around the mentions. With specific pair attributes, they use a single-layer neural network to create a vector of similarities and calculate a coreference score. This model has been tested with an ACE dataset in an extended version and has comparable performance to previous feature-enriched systems.

In my opinion, the main contribution of this paper is creating a neural method for entity linking by combining several linguistic features with word embeddings. It is good to know that good results cannot be obtained using word embeddings alone. Fortunately, the linguistic features used are limited and do not require manually created external resources.

Experimental Context:
- It seems that the word " সোনার ট্রিগার" (Sonar Trigger) has been used instead of the guessed words. The authors have given a reasonable explanation for this, although I would have liked to see performance with the guessed triggers. This is particularly problematic because one of the competing systems used a guessed trigger, so the comparison is not fair.
- Different research papers have used different training/test partitions, which is a concern. I would encourage the authors to stick to the previous partitions as much as possible.

Ambiguous Points:
- Numbers requiring cross-sentential information are credible. However, the last statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) was not clear to me.
- Embeddings for locations are said to be "same as word embeddings." How? Are they randomly initialized? Are they lexicalized? I am not clear as to why the same embedding is placed next to one word for the relative location of another word.
- How are left and right neighbors used to create representations (lines 307-11)? Does this only affect the max-pooling operation?
- The word embedding of a word before and after the trigger words is added to it. This seems somewhat contradictory. Why not prefer one word before and after and another?
- It is not clear how the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is used. Only v{sent+lex} is used in the following section, not ve.
- How are the pair attributes used in section 3.2? Most attributes are binary, so I assume they are encoded as binary vectors, but for example, what would the distance attribute be? And, are they kept constant during training?

Other Issues and Suggestions:
- Can this method also be applied to entity coreference resolution? This would help to compare it with previous work and popular datasets like OntoNotes.
- It is interesting to use a square function. Is this new? Do you think it can be used in other tasks as well?
- Dataset: A dataset is publicly available, but the results are also presented with ACE++, which is not. Do you plan to publish it? This will help other researchers to compare the new methods. At least, it would be nice to see a comparison with feature-enriched systems in this dataset.
- Results: Some of the numbers reported in the results are quite close. Significance testing will help to prove the comparisons.
- Related Work: Among the work on (entity) coreference resolution, Weissman et al. (2015)'s neural network method can be mentioned.

Minor Issues:
- Line 143, the word "that" is redundant.
- Table 6 mentions a baseline as "same type," but the text (line 670) mentions "same event."
