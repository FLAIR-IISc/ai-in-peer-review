This paper proposes a neural model for event linking, leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate vector representations of event mentions. The model combines word embeddings with sentential context, processed through a CNN and max-pooling, and incorporates additional lexical and pairwise features. A single-layer neural network then computes a coreference score based on these combined representations. The system is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and a newly created expanded version (ACE++), achieving performance comparable to previous systems that rely on more extensive feature engineering.

The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating a viable neural approach to event linking that reduces reliance on external semantic resources. The model effectively integrates distributed word representations with targeted linguistic features, showcasing that strong performance can be achieved without complex, hand-crafted features. A secondary contribution lies in the ACE++ dataset, offering a larger testbed for event linking research, though its availability remains a concern. Finally, the exploration of the squared activation function as a similarity metric is an interesting, though potentially minor, contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Reduced Reliance on External Resources:** The model's ability to achieve competitive performance without relying on knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet is a significant advantage. This makes the approach more portable to domains where such resources are limited or unavailable. The authors convincingly argue for the limitations of prior approaches dependent on these resources.
2. **Effective Feature Integration:** The model successfully combines distributed word embeddings with relevant linguistic features, demonstrating that CNNs can effectively capture event-specific information from sentence context. The inclusion of both local (trigger word) and sentential features appears crucial to performance.
3. **Competitive Performance:** Achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art systems, which typically employ more extensive feature sets, is a strong indicator of the model's effectiveness and a positive result for the research community. The provided results demonstrate a solid baseline for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Experimental Setup Concerns:** The use of gold trigger words, rather than predicted ones, introduces a potential bias and complicates the comparison with systems that employ a full information extraction pipeline. While the authors acknowledge this, a performance evaluation with predicted triggers would strengthen the conclusions. Furthermore, inconsistencies in train/test splits across different publications present challenges in direct comparison.
2. **Lack of Clarity in Design Choices:** Several aspects of the model design lack sufficient detail. The justification for using relative position embeddings, beyond the stated rationale, is unclear. The specific manner in which left/right neighbors are incorporated into the representations, and the choice of a single word context around the trigger, seem arbitrary and could benefit from further explanation. The role of `v_e` (line 330) is also unclear, as it appears unused in subsequent sections.
3. **Feature Engineering Remains Significant:** While reduced compared to previous work, the model still relies on carefully chosen pairwise features. The encoding and handling of these features, particularly continuous features like distance, are not fully explained. A more thorough ablation study examining the contribution of each feature class would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is the precise method used to generate the position embeddings? Are they randomly initialized, lexicalized, or learned jointly with the other embeddings?
2. Could you provide further justification for the choice of considering only one word before and after the trigger word when generating the contextual representation? Were other window sizes explored?
3. How is the pairwise distance feature encoded? Is it normalized, discretized, or treated as a continuous value? Are the pairwise features fixed during training, or are they updated?
4. What is the purpose of the event-mention representation `v_e` calculated in line 330? Why isn’t it used in subsequent calculations?
5. Do you have plans to release the ACE++ dataset to the research community?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from statistical significance testing to support the performance comparisons.
*   Exploring the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution, and comparison to existing literature (e.g., Wiseman et al., 2015), would broaden the scope of the work.
*   Consider clarifying the potentially ambiguous statement on lines 65-70 in the second paragraph.
*   Ensure consistent terminology throughout the paper (e.g., "same type" vs. "same event" in Table 6 and the accompanying text).
