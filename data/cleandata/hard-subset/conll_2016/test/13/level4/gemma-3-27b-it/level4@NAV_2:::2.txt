This paper presents a model for the task of event linking, proposing the use of sentential features from Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in place of external knowledge sources utilized by prior methods. The model employs a two-part architecture: the first learns event mention representations, and the second calculates a coreference score based on pairs of event mentions.

The paper is well-written, well-presented, and clearly articulated. The analysis of the ACE corpus regarding argument sharing between coreferential event mentions is insightful. Furthermore, the motivation for creating the ACE++ dataset based on dataset size impact is compelling. However, several key areas require further attention:

- The paper lacks a thorough justification for the choice of CNNs for generating mention representations. A discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of CNNs compared to alternative models (e.g., Recurrent Neural Networks) is missing. Given the modest performance improvement (2-3 F1 points) over state-of-the-art methods, stronger evidence supporting the architectural superiority of the proposed approach is needed.
- The novelty of using sentential features for event linking is not sufficiently established. The paper should explicitly compare the proposed approach to existing continuous space methods for event linking and clearly articulate its unique contributions, given the established use of CNNs for classification tasks. The selection of competing methods in Table 3 appears incomplete.
- Details regarding the creation of the ACE++ dataset are absent. A significant limitation of the ACE dataset is its restricted number of event types, potentially introducing bias. Providing information on the event types covered in ACE++ is crucial, as it supports the claim in Section 5.1 regarding the domain dependence of prior methods and their reliance on external semantic features. Furthermore, making ACE++ publicly available would facilitate future research and enable meaningful comparisons.

Minor issues:

- Including the performance of the model without gold-standard event mentions in Table 3 would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- Exploring the potential for augmenting the model with a standard coreference resolution system would be valuable. For instance, leveraging existing systems like CoreNLP to handle cases like the pronoun resolution example in line 687 could serve as a useful baseline.
- Given the limited size of the ACE dataset, evaluating the model on additional resources is warranted. Parallel evaluation on EventCorefBank alongside ACE is essential to demonstrate robustness and generalizability.
- Table 5 indicates the effectiveness of pairwise features, suggesting that feature engineering remains important. Identifying the most impactful features and detailing the selection process (including unsuccessful attempts) would enhance the paper's contribution.
