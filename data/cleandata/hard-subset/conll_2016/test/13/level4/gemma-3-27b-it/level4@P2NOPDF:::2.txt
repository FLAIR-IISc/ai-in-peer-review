This paper introduces a model for event entity linking that leverages sentential features extracted using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), foregoing the reliance on external knowledge sources common in previous approaches. The model comprises two stages: learning representations for event mentions, and calculating a coreference score between pairs of event mentions.

The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to understand. The analysis of argument sharing within event coreferences on the ACE corpus is particularly insightful. Furthermore, the authors convincingly demonstrate the limitations of the ACE dataset’s size, effectively motivating the creation of the larger ACE++ dataset. However, several key issues require attention.

First, the paper lacks a robust justification for the choice of CNNs for generating mention representations. While the approach is viable, the authors do not adequately explain *why* CNNs were selected or provide a comparative analysis against alternative architectures, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). Given the relatively modest performance gains – an improvement of only 2-3 F1 points over the state-of-the-art – more compelling evidence is needed to demonstrate the superiority of the chosen architecture.

Second, the novelty of employing sentential features for event linking is not clearly articulated. Using CNNs for classification tasks is a well-established technique, and the paper would benefit from explicitly identifying and comparing itself to existing continuous space methods specifically designed for event linking. The comparison in Table 3 appears incomplete and should be broadened to include a more comprehensive range of relevant methods.

Third, the methodology for constructing the ACE++ dataset is not described. A known limitation of the original ACE dataset is its restricted set of event types, potentially introducing bias. Providing details on the event types covered in ACE++ is crucial, particularly to support the claims made in Section 5.1 regarding the limitations of approaches dependent on domain-specific semantic resources. To substantiate these claims, the authors should demonstrate scenarios where existing methods falter while their approach succeeds. Moreover, making the ACE++ dataset publicly available would be a significant contribution to the research community and facilitate future comparisons.

Several minor points could further strengthen the paper:

*   Including performance results in Table 3 without gold references would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   Exploring the potential for integrating this model with a standard coreference resolution system would be valuable. For instance, the example on line 687 demonstrates how an off-the-shelf tool like CoreNLP can effectively link pronouns to event mentions, suggesting a potential baseline or augmentation strategy.
*   Given the limited size of the ACE dataset, testing the model on additional resources is warranted. This would provide a more robust evaluation and further support the motivation for simultaneously addressing event and entity coreference. Parallel evaluation on EventCorefBank alongside ACE is strongly recommended.
*   Table 5 indicates that pairwise features contribute significantly to performance, suggesting that feature engineering remains important, at least within the context of the ACE dataset. The authors should discuss the rationale behind the selected feature set, report which features proved most effective, and detail any other feature configurations that were explored.
