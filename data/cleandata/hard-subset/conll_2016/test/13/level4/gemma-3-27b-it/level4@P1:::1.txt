This paper models event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations based on word embeddings passed through a CNN and followed by max-pooling. They also concatenate the resulting representations with several lexical features surrounding the mention. Together with certain pairwise features, they produce a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network, and compute a coreference score. 
The model is tested on the ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

The main contribution of the paper, in my opinion, is the development of a neural approach for event linking that combines word embeddings with carefully chosen linguistic features. It is interesting to find that word embeddings alone are not sufficient for good performance. Importantly, the linguistic features used are limited and do not require manually-crafted external resources.  

**Experimental Setting**
- It appears that gold trigger words are used rather than predicted ones. The authors justify this choice, although I would have preferred to see performance with predicted triggers. This is particularly relevant as one of the competing systems used predicted triggers, making a direct comparison challenging. 
- The fact that different papers employ different train/test splits is a concern. I encourage the authors to adhere to previously established splits as much as possible. 

**Unclear Points**
- The statistics presented demonstrating the need for cross-sentential information are convincing. However, the last sentence in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) was unclear.
- Embeddings for relative positions are said to be generated “in a way similar to word embeddings.” How exactly? Are they randomly initialized? Are they learned? It is not clear why the embedding for a relative position next to one word should be the same as that next to a different word.
- How exactly are left versus right neighbors used to create the representation (lines 307-311)? Does this only affect the max-pooling operation?
- The word embeddings of one word before and one word after the trigger word are appended to it. This seems somewhat arbitrary. Why one word before and after, and not some other choice?  
- It is not clear how the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* (line 330) is used. In the following sections, only *v<sub>sent+lex</sub>* appears to be used, not *v<sub>e</sub>*.
- How are pairwise features used in section 3.2? Most features are binary, so I assume they are encoded as a binary vector, but what about the distance feature, for example? And, are these features kept fixed during training?

**Other Issues and Suggestions**
- Could this approach be applied to entity coreference resolution as well? This would allow for comparisons with more previous work and well-established datasets like OntoNotes. 
- The use of a squared function as a nonlinearity is interesting. Is this novel? Do you believe it has broader applicability in other tasks?
- Datasets: One dataset is publicly available, but results are also presented with ACE++, which is not. Do you have plans to release ACE++? This would facilitate comparisons with new methods. At the very least, it would be valuable to see a comparison of the feature-rich systems on this dataset as well.
- Results: Some of the reported results are quite close. Significance testing would help to substantiate the observed differences.
- Related work: Among work on (entity) coreference resolution, one might mention the neural network approach by Wiseman et al. (2015).  

**Minor Issues**
- Line 143, “that” is redundant. 
- The baseline is referred to as “same type” in Table 6, but “same event” in the text (line 670).
