This paper models event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations based on word embeddings passed through a CNN and followed by max-pooling. They also concatenate the resulting representations with several lexical features surrounding the mention. Together with pairwise features, they produce a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network, and compute a coreference score. The model is tested on the ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version (ACE++), achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of a competitive neural approach to event linking that minimizes reliance on external semantic resources. By effectively combining contextualized word embeddings – generated via CNNs – with limited, readily available linguistic features, the authors achieve state-of-the-art or competitive results. This is significant as it reduces the domain-specificity inherent in methods reliant on resources like WordNet or FrameNet. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental analysis, including an error analysis, which provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance without External Resources:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on established benchmarks (ACE and ACE++) *without* relying on expensive and domain-specific external knowledge bases. This is a significant advantage, making the approach more readily applicable to diverse domains and languages where such resources are limited or unavailable. The reported results demonstrate the power of leveraging sentence-level context learned through CNNs.
2. **Well-Designed Model Architecture:** The two-stage architecture—learning event representations followed by a coreference decision—is logically sound and effectively integrates various feature types. The use of CNNs to encode sentential context, combined with lexical and pairwise features, appears to be a successful strategy for capturing relevant information for event linking. The design choices, such as separate pooling for left and right context, demonstrate a nuanced understanding of the task.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation, including a comparison to several state-of-the-art methods. They also investigate the impact of different feature classes and clustering strategies, providing valuable insights into the model’s behavior and the relative importance of various components. The error analysis, while limited in scope, highlights common error types and potential directions for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Comparison with Predicted Triggers:** The use of gold standard event triggers in the evaluation, while acknowledged by the authors, introduces a potential bias in the comparison to systems that predict triggers. This makes it difficult to directly assess the overall performance of the proposed system in a realistic end-to-end pipeline. A comparison using predicted triggers, even with some performance degradation, would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Dataset Splitting Consistency:** The differing train/test splits used across different papers in the literature pose a challenge for fair comparison. While the authors acknowledge this issue, striving for consistency with previously published splits, as much as possible, would enhance the reproducibility and comparability of the results. 
3. **Lack of Justification for Design Choices:** Several design choices appear somewhat arbitrary without strong justification. For example, the decision to use only one word of context on either side of the trigger word is not adequately explained. A more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of these choices would be beneficial. The choice of a squared activation function in the final layer also lacks sufficient motivation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the implementation of position embeddings? Specifically, how are these embeddings initialized and learned?
2.  The paper states that `v_e` is generated in line 330, but then only `v_{sent+lex}` is used in subsequent sections. Is `v_e` actually utilized anywhere in the system, or was it a design element that was later removed? If removed, why?
3.  How is the distance feature (part of the pairwise features) encoded for input to the neural network? Is it a discrete or continuous value, and is it scaled or normalized in any way?
4.  Do you have plans to release the ACE++ dataset to the research community? Its availability would greatly facilitate further research in this area.
