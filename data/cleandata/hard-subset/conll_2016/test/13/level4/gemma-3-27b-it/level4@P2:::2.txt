This paper presents a model for event linking, proposing the use of sentential features extracted via Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) as an alternative to external knowledge sources commonly used in previous approaches. The model comprises two stages: generating event mention representations and calculating a coreference score for event mention pairs. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, with a particularly insightful analysis of argument sharing within event coreferences in the ACE corpus, and a strong motivation for the creation of the expanded ACE++ dataset. However, several key areas require further development.

The primary weakness of the paper is a lack of thorough justification for the architectural choices. While the use of CNNs is presented as a core component, the authors do not adequately motivate this choice or compare its performance to alternative approaches, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). Given that the performance gains over state-of-the-art methods are modest (approximately 2-3 F1 points), stronger evidence is needed to demonstrate the superiority of the CNN-based architecture. 

A second significant concern is the limited novelty of applying CNNs to event linking with sentential features. The paper doesn’t sufficiently contextualize this approach within existing continuous space methods for event linking, and lacks a comprehensive comparison to related work. The selection of competing methods in Table 3 appears incomplete.

Finally, the paper lacks crucial details about the construction of the ACE++ dataset. The ACE dataset’s limited event type coverage is a known issue, potentially leading to biased results. Providing information about the event types included in ACE++ is essential, both to substantiate the claims in Section 5.1 regarding the limitations of approaches reliant on domain-specific semantic features and to facilitate future research. Making the dataset publicly available would further enhance its impact.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that event linking can be effectively performed *without* relying on external semantic knowledge sources, achieving competitive performance using only sentential features extracted with CNNs. A secondary contribution is the creation of the ACE++ dataset, motivated by the limitations of the original ACE corpus, and providing a larger and potentially more robust testbed. A further, albeit smaller, contribution is the analysis of event coreference patterns in ACE, specifically regarding argument sharing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE dataset and demonstrates strong performance on the newly introduced ACE++ dataset. This suggests the approach is viable and offers a practical alternative to methods relying on expensive and domain-specific external resources. The results indicate the effectiveness of the proposed sentential feature approach.
2. **Insightful Data Analysis:** The analysis of event coreference patterns within the ACE corpus, particularly the identification of the frequency of argument sharing, is a valuable contribution. This analysis directly motivates the development of the ACE++ dataset and provides a better understanding of the challenges inherent in event linking.
3. **Motivated Dataset Creation:** The creation of the ACE++ dataset addresses a key limitation of the original ACE dataset—its limited size and event type coverage. This extended dataset provides a more realistic and challenging testbed for event linking models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Architectural Justification:** The paper does not adequately justify the choice of CNNs over other neural network architectures (e.g., RNNs). A comparative analysis would strengthen the argument for the chosen architecture. The modest performance gains necessitate a clearer understanding of *why* CNNs are effective in this context.
2. **Insufficient Novelty Discussion:** The paper fails to adequately position the work within the broader context of continuous space methods for event linking. A more thorough comparison to existing approaches and a clear articulation of the novel aspects of this work are required.
3. **Insufficient Dataset Details:** The lack of information regarding the construction of the ACE++ dataset is a major limitation. Details about the event type coverage are crucial for understanding the dataset’s scope and interpreting the experimental results. Public release of the dataset would also be highly beneficial.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While an error analysis is provided, it is relatively superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors the model makes, and the reasons behind those errors, would provide valuable insights for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What specific considerations led to the choice of CNNs over other neural network architectures (e.g., RNNs) for generating event mention representations? Could you provide a comparative analysis?
2. Could you provide details about the event type coverage of the ACE++ dataset?
3. Will the ACE++ dataset be made publicly available?
4.  What specific error cases were observed during the error analysis that consistently challenged the model's performance, beyond the pronoun resolution issue?
