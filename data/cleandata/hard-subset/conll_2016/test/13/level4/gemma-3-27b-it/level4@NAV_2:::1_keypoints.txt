- Model: CNN-based event linking that produces mention vectors from word embeddings + max-pooling, concatenates lexical/context embeddings and pairwise features, then computes similarity with a single-layer NN to score coreference.  
- Primary contribution: neural approach combining word embeddings with limited linguistic features, showing embeddings alone are insufficient.  
- Secondary contribution: achieves state-of-the-art on ACE2005 and ACE++ without external semantic resources.  

Strengths:
- Strong performance: competitive results comparable to feature-rich systems.  
- Reduced reliance on external resources: avoids WordNet/FrameNet, improving portability.  
- Effective feature combination: CNN sentential features complement lexical and pairwise features.  

Weaknesses:
- Non-standard evaluation: uses gold triggers (not predicted), complicating fair comparison with full-pipeline systems.  
- Dataset splitting concerns: inconsistent train/test splits reduce reproducibility and comparability.  
- Implementation clarity lacking: unclear details on position embeddings, neighbor use in pooling, use of v_e, and pairwise feature encoding/training.  

Questions for authors:
- How were position embeddings initialized/encoded and why share embeddings across relative positions?  
- How exactly are left/right neighbors used beyond influencing max-pooling?  
- Is the event-mention representation v_e used downstream or only v_sent+lex?  
- How are pairwise features (especially continuous distance) encoded and are they learned or fixed?  
- Why use a square nonlinearity in similarity calculation; is this novel and generalizable?  

Additional comments:
- Suggest exploring application to entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes) for broader validation.  
- Recommend releasing ACE++ and comparing feature-rich systems on it for clearer benchmarks.  
- Perform significance testing to strengthen result claims.  
- Related work should include recent neural coreference approaches (e.g., Wiseman et al., 2015).  
- Minor edits: remove "that" on line 143 and standardize terminology in Table 6 ("same type" vs "same event").
