This paper introduces a model for event entity linking, leveraging sentential features extracted via CNNs as an alternative to external knowledge sources commonly used in prior work. The model consists of two components: one for learning event mention representations, and another for calculating a coreference score between event entity mentions.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to understand. The analysis of argument sharing between event coreferences on the ACE corpus is particularly insightful, and the dataset size analysis provides strong motivation for the creation of the ACE++ dataset. However, several significant concerns require attention.

First, the rationale behind employing CNNs for generating mention representations is not sufficiently justified or critically analyzed. The paper lacks a discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of this architectural choice, as well as comparative results against alternative models like RNNs. Considering the modest performance gains – approximately 2-3 F1 points over the state-of-the-art – further evidence is needed to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed CNN-based architecture.

Second, the novelty of utilizing sentential features for event linking is unclear. Applying CNNs for classification tasks is a well-established technique, and the authors should explicitly compare their work to existing continuous space methods for event linking. The comparative analysis presented in Table 3 appears incomplete.

Third, the methodology for constructing the ACE++ dataset is not described. A known limitation of the ACE dataset is its restricted number of event types, which can introduce bias. It is crucial to disclose the event types included in ACE++ to support the claims made in Section 5.1 regarding the domain-dependence of other approaches. To substantiate these claims, the authors should demonstrate cases where their approach outperforms existing methods on datasets where the latter struggle. Furthermore, releasing ACE++ publicly would facilitate future research and enable meaningful comparisons. 

Several minor points could further strengthen the paper:

- Including model performance results from Table 3 without gold references would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
- Exploring the potential for integrating this model with a standard coreference resolution system would be valuable. For instance, leveraging readily available coreference links from tools like CoreNLP, as illustrated in line 687, could inform the design of a stronger baseline.
- Given the limited size of the ACE dataset, evaluating the model on additional resources is recommended. This would also bolster the argument for simultaneously addressing entity and event coreference. Parallel evaluation on EventCorefBank alongside ACE is strongly encouraged.
- The effectiveness of pairwise features highlighted in Table 5 suggests that feature engineering remains important for achieving competitive results, particularly on the ACE dataset. Providing insights into the feature selection process – the rationale behind the chosen features and results from other attempted configurations – would be beneficial.
