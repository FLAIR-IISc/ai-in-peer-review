- Proposes a CNN-based neural model for event linking that combines word embeddings, sentential context, and lexical/pairwise features to produce coreference scores.
- Primary contribution: demonstrates a viable neural approach that reduces reliance on external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet).
- Secondary contribution: introduces ACE++, an expanded ACE 2005 dataset for event linking (availability unclear).
- Notes the exploration of squared activation as a similarity metric, presented as an interesting but minor contribution.

Strengths:
- Reduced reliance on external resources increases portability to domains lacking semantic KBs.
- Effective integration of distributed word embeddings with targeted linguistic features (trigger and sentential context).
- Competitive performance comparable to state-of-the-art systems with more extensive feature engineering.

Weaknesses:
- Experimental setup uses gold trigger words, biasing results and hindering comparison to full-pipeline systems; train/test split inconsistencies across works complicate comparisons.
- Insufficient clarity on several design choices: relative position embeddings, incorporation of left/right neighbors, single-word context window, and the unused event-mention vector v_e.
- Model still depends on carefully chosen pairwise features; encoding/handling of continuous features (e.g., distance) and lack of thorough ablation study are concerns.

Questions for authors:
- How are position embeddings generated and trained (random init, lexicalized, jointly learned)?
- Why was only one word on each side of the trigger used for context; were other window sizes evaluated?
- How is the pairwise distance feature encoded (normalized, discretized, continuous) and are pairwise features updated during training?
- What is the purpose of v_e (line 330) and why isn’t it used later?
- Will the ACE++ dataset be released to the research community?

Additional comments:
- Recommend performing statistical significance testing for performance comparisons.
- Suggest exploring applicability to entity coreference and comparing with related literature (e.g., Wiseman et al., 2015).
- Clarify ambiguous phrasing in the indicated paragraph (lines 65–70).
- Ensure consistent terminology (e.g., "same type" vs. "same event" in Table 6 and text).
