This paper presents a CNN-based model for event linking. The authors generate vector representations of event mentions by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling. These representations are then concatenated with additional word embeddings of surrounding context. A single-layer neural network, incorporating these combined representations alongside pairwise features, is used to produce a similarity vector and ultimately a coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE dataset and an expanded version thereof, achieving performance comparable to previous, feature-rich systems. 

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the development of a neural approach to event linking that effectively combines word embeddings with a selection of linguistic features. The finding that word embeddings alone are insufficient for strong performance is notable. Importantly, the linguistic features employed are limited in scope and do not rely on extensive, manually-constructed external resources.

Experimental Setting:
- The use of gold trigger words, rather than predicted ones, is a potential limitation. While the authors offer a justification for this choice, I would have preferred to see results with predicted triggers. This is particularly relevant as a competing system utilizes predicted triggers, rendering a direct comparison somewhat unfair.
- Inconsistencies in train/test splits across different papers are concerning. I recommend that the authors adhere to established splits whenever possible to ensure comparability.

Unclear Points:
- The quantitative evidence supporting the need for cross-sentential information is convincing. However, the statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) remains unclear to me.
- The description of positional embeddings – generated “in a way similar to word embeddings” – lacks detail. Specifically, are these embeddings randomly initialized, or are they lexicalized? Furthermore, the rationale for assigning the same embedding to relative positions regardless of the adjacent word is unclear. 
- The method for incorporating left and right neighbor embeddings into the representation (lines 307-311) requires clarification. Does this influence solely the max-pooling operation?
- Appending the embeddings of the single word preceding and following the trigger appears arbitrary. The motivation for this specific window size is not fully explained.
- The utilization of the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* (line 330) is unclear, as subsequent sections only reference *v<sub>sent+lex</sub>*, and not *v<sub>e</sub>*.
- The implementation of pairwise features in section 3.2 could be elaborated. Given that most features are binary, I assume they are encoded as a binary vector; however, the handling of continuous features, such as distance, is not specified. Additionally, it is unclear whether these features are fixed during training. 

Other Issues and Suggestions:
- Investigating the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution would facilitate comparison with a broader range of prior work and established datasets like OntoNotes.
- The use of a squared function as a nonlinearity is an interesting design choice. Is this novel? Do the authors believe it could be beneficial in other tasks?
- While one dataset is publicly available, results are also presented on ACE++, which is not. Releasing ACE++ would enable more comprehensive evaluation of new methods. At a minimum, a comparison with feature-rich systems on ACE++ would be valuable.
- The reported results exhibit close numerical values. Statistical significance testing would strengthen the validity of the comparisons.
- In the Related Work section, the neural network approach by Wiseman et al. (2015) for (entity) coreference resolution should be mentioned.

Minor Issues:
- line 143, the word “that” is redundant.
- Table 6 refers to one baseline as “same type,” while the text (line 670) uses “same event.”

References:
- Wiseman, S., Rush, A. M., Weston, J., & Shieber, S. M. (2015). Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. *ACL 2015*.
