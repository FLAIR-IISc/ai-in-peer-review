This paper presents a CNN-based approach to event linking. The authors generate vector representations of event mentions by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling, and then concatenate these with contextual word embeddings. These combined representations, along with pairwise features, are fed into a single-layer neural network to produce a coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and an extended version, achieving performance competitive with previous feature-rich systems.

The primary contribution lies in developing a neural model for event coreference that effectively combines word embeddings with a selection of linguistic features. The findings highlight that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for strong performance, but importantly, the linguistic features employed are limited and do not necessitate extensive manual feature engineering or external resources.

**Experimental Setup Concerns:**

The use of gold trigger words, rather than predicted ones, is a notable aspect of the experimental setup. While the authors justify this decision, I believe including results with predicted triggers would provide a more comprehensive evaluation, particularly given that a competing system utilizes them, making a direct comparison inequitable. Furthermore, the variability in train/test splits across different papers is a concern; adhering to established splits whenever possible would improve reproducibility and facilitate fairer comparisons.

**Clarification Needed:**

Several points require further clarification. While the evidence presented supports the need for cross-sentential information, the statement in lines 65-70 of the second paragraph was unclear. Regarding positional embeddings, the description of their generation as “similar to word embeddings” lacks detail. Are they randomly initialized, or are they learned in a lexicalized manner? The rationale for assigning identical embeddings to relative positions regardless of the surrounding word is also unclear. 

I also request clarification on how left and right neighbor embeddings contribute to the overall representation (lines 307-311) – specifically, does this influence only the max-pooling operation? The decision to append embeddings of only one word before and after the trigger also seems arbitrary. What motivated this specific window size? 

The utilization of the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* (line 330) is unclear, as subsequent sections appear to rely solely on *v<sub>sent+lex</sub>*. Finally, the application of pairwise features in section 3.2 warrants elaboration. Given that many features are binary, I assume they are represented as a binary vector, but the handling of continuous features like distance remains unclear. Are these pairwise features fixed during training, or are they also learned?

**Suggestions and Potential Extensions:**

Exploring the applicability of this approach to standard entity coreference resolution tasks would be valuable, allowing for comparison with a broader range of existing work and established datasets like OntoNotes. The use of a squared function as a nonlinearity is an interesting choice; investigating its novelty and potential applicability to other tasks could be fruitful.

Regarding datasets, while one dataset is publicly available, results are also reported on ACE++, which is not. Releasing ACE++ would significantly benefit the research community, enabling more comprehensive comparisons of novel methods. At a minimum, a direct comparison of the proposed system against feature-rich baselines on ACE++ would strengthen the reported results. The reported performance differences are often small, and statistical significance testing would help validate the observed improvements.

In the related work section, the neural coreference model by Wiseman et al. (2015) is a relevant contribution and should be included.
