This paper models event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling. These representations are then concatenated with several surrounding word embeddings. Together with certain pairwise features, this combined vector is fed into a single-layer neural network to produce a similarity vector and ultimately a coreference score. The model is tested on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

The main contribution of this paper, in my opinion, lies in its development of a neural approach to event linking that combines word embeddings with carefully chosen linguistic features. It’s noteworthy that relying solely on word embeddings proves insufficient for strong performance. Fortunately, the linguistic features employed are limited and do not necessitate manually-crafted external resources.

**Experimental Setup**

*   Gold trigger words are used instead of predicted ones. While the authors provide a rationale for this choice, I would have appreciated seeing results with predicted triggers, especially given that a competing system *did* utilize them, making a direct comparison less fair.
*   The differing train/test splits used across papers is a concern. I encourage the authors to adhere to previously established splits as closely as possible.

**Unclear Points**

*   The results demonstrating the need for cross-sentential information are compelling. However, the final statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) was unclear to me.
*   The paper states that positional embeddings are generated “in a way similar to word embeddings.” Could you elaborate on this? Are they randomly initialized? Are they lexicalized? It’s unclear why a relative position adjacent to one word should have the same embedding as the same relative position next to a different word.
*   How are left and right neighbors used to create the representation (lines 307-311)? Does this impact only the max-pooling operation?
*   The word embeddings of the word immediately before and after the trigger are appended to the trigger’s representation. This seems somewhat arbitrary. Why specifically one word before and after, rather than another choice?
*   It’s unclear how the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* (line 330) is used. Subsequent sections appear to utilize *v<sub>sent+lex</sub>* exclusively, without reference to *v<sub>e</sub>*.
*   Regarding the pairwise features in section 3.2, most appear to be binary and therefore encoded as a binary vector. However, what about the distance feature – how is it represented? Furthermore, are these features kept fixed during training?

**Other Issues and Suggestions**

*   Could this approach be applied to entity coreference resolution? This would enable a comparison with a broader range of prior work and popular datasets like OntoNotes.
*   The use of a squared function as a nonlinearity is interesting. Is this a novel approach? Do you believe it has broader applicability to other tasks?
*   **Datasets:** One dataset is publicly available, but results are also presented using ACE++, which is not. Do you have plans to release ACE++? This would greatly facilitate comparison with new methods by other researchers. At the very least, it would be beneficial to see a comparison of the feature-rich systems on this dataset as well.
*   **Results:** Several reported results are very close in value. Significance testing would help to support the reported comparisons.
*   **Related Work:** Within the related work on (entity) coreference resolution, the neural network approach by Wiseman et al. (2015) is relevant and should be mentioned.

**Minor Issues**

*   Line 143, “that” is redundant.
*   One baseline is referred to as “same type” in table 6, but “same event” in the text (line 670).

**References**

*   Wiseman, S., Rush, A. M., Weston, J., & Shieber, S. M. (2015). Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. *ACL 2015*.
