This paper presents a model for event entity linking, proposing the use of sentential features extracted via Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) as a replacement for external knowledge sources traditionally employed in this task. The model consists of two components: one learns a representation for event mentions, and the other calculates a coreference score between pairs of event mentions.

The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to follow. The analysis of argument sharing between event coreferences within the ACE corpus is particularly insightful. Moreover, the investigation into the impact of dataset size provides strong motivation for the creation of the novel ACE++ dataset. However, several significant issues require attention.

A primary concern is the lack of justification for the choice of CNNs for generating mention representations. The authors do not adequately motivate this design decision, nor do they provide a comparative analysis against alternative architectures such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). Given the relatively modest improvements – 2 to 3 F1 points – over state-of-the-art methods, more robust evidence is needed to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed architecture.

Furthermore, the novelty of tackling event linking with purely sentential features is unclear. Utilizing CNNs for classification tasks is a well-established approach, and the paper lacks a focused comparison to existing continuous space methods specifically designed for event linking. The selection of methods in Table 3 appears incomplete and does not offer a comprehensive evaluation against relevant baselines.

Crucially, the paper provides insufficient detail regarding the creation of the ACE++ dataset. The limited number of event types in the original ACE dataset is a known limitation, potentially introducing bias. Therefore, a clear description of the event types included in ACE++ is essential. This information is also vital to substantiate the claim in Section 5.1 that prior approaches are heavily reliant on domain-specific semantic resources, while the current approach is not. To support this claim effectively, the authors should demonstrate scenarios where their model outperforms existing methods on datasets where those earlier approaches falter. Finally, to facilitate future research and enable meaningful comparisons, the authors should strongly consider making the ACE++ dataset publicly available.

Several minor points could further strengthen the paper:

*   The inclusion of results in Table 3 showing performance without gold references would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   Exploring the potential for augmenting the model with a standard coreference resolution system would be valuable. For example, readily available systems like CoreNLP can successfully link pronouns to event mentions (as illustrated in line 687), and investigating how to leverage such capabilities could improve performance.
*   Given the limited size of the ACE dataset, evaluating the model on additional publicly available resources is recommended. This would further motivate the simultaneous consideration of entity and event coreference resolution. Parallel evaluation on EventCorefBank alongside ACE would be particularly beneficial.
*   Table 5 demonstrates the effectiveness of pairwise features, suggesting that feature engineering remains important for achieving competitive results, especially at the scale of the ACE dataset. Further insight into the selection process for these features – including what alternatives were considered and why – would be helpful. Identifying the most impactful features and the reasoning behind their effectiveness would also be valuable.
