This paper introduces a model for event entity linking, proposing the use of sentential features extracted via Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) as an alternative to external knowledge sources employed in previous approaches. The model consists of two components: one to learn representations of event mentions, and another to calculate a coreference score between pairs of event mentions. 

The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to understand. The analysis of argument sharing between event coreferences within the ACE corpus is particularly insightful. Moreover, the investigation into the impact of dataset size provides strong motivation for the creation of the ACE++ dataset. However, several significant issues require attention:

- The authors do not sufficiently justify their choice of CNNs for generating mention representations, nor do they analyze the advantages and disadvantages of this approach. The rationale for selecting CNNs is unclear, and a comparison to alternative models, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), is missing. Given the relatively modest performance improvement – 2 to 3 F1 points over the state-of-the-art – stronger evidence is needed to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed architecture.
- The novelty of addressing event linking with sentential features is not clearly established, as applying CNNs for classification tasks is a well-established practice. The authors should explicitly delineate and compare their work to existing continuous space methods for event linking. The selection of methods for comparison in Table 3 appears limited in scope.
- The methodology behind the construction of the ACE++ dataset is not described. A key limitation of the ACE dataset is its restricted number of event types, which introduces potential bias. Information regarding the event types included in ACE++ is crucial, and would also strengthen the claim in Section 5.1 that ‘other approaches are strongly tied to the domain where these semantic features are available…our approach does not depend on resources with restricted…’. To support this claim, the authors should demonstrate failure cases for prior methods on a dataset where their approach succeeds. Furthermore, making ACE++ publicly available would facilitate future research and comparisons.

Minor points for consideration:

- Including the model’s performance without gold references in Table 3 would be beneficial.
- Exploring the potential for augmenting the model with a standard coreference resolution system would be valuable. For instance, readily available tools like CoreNLP can successfully link pronouns to event mentions, as demonstrated in line 687, and this capability could be integrated into a baseline event entity linking system.
- Given the limited size of the ACE dataset, validating the model on additional resources is essential for establishing its robustness. This further underscores the value of simultaneously addressing entity and event coreference. Parallel evaluation on EventCorefBank alongside ACE is highly recommended.
- Table 5 indicates that pairwise features contribute significantly to performance, suggesting that feature engineering remains important for achieving competitive results – particularly on the scale of the ACE dataset. It would be helpful to identify the most effective features and explain the rationale behind the current feature set, including details of features that were considered and discarded.
