This paper presents a CNN-based approach to event linking. The authors generate vector representations of event mentions by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling. These representations are then concatenated with additional word embeddings of surrounding context, and combined with pairwise features using a single-layer neural network to produce a coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version (ACE++), achieving performance comparable to previous, more feature-engineered systems. 

The primary contribution of this work lies in the development of a neural event linking model that effectively integrates word embeddings with a carefully selected set of linguistic features. The findings demonstrate that word embeddings alone are insufficient for strong performance, highlighting the importance of incorporating linguistic information. Importantly, the features used are limited and do not rely on extensive, manually-crafted external resources.

**Experimental Setting:**

A key concern is the use of gold trigger words rather than predicted ones. While the authors provide justification for this choice, I believe evaluating performance with predicted triggers would provide a more complete picture, and a fairer comparison to systems like [competitor system name] which *do* utilize predicted triggers. The differing train/test splits used across papers is also problematic. Adhering to established splits would facilitate more reliable comparisons and reproducibility.

**Unclear Points:**

The results supporting the need for cross-sentential information are compelling. However, the statement in lines 65-70 of the second paragraph remains unclear. Further clarification is needed. 

The description of positional embeddings – generated “in a way similar to word embeddings” – lacks detail. Are these embeddings randomly initialized? Are they lexicalized in any way? It’s not immediately obvious why the embedding for a relative position should be independent of the word it’s relative *to*. 

The representation of event mentions using left and right neighbors (lines 307-311) requires clarification. Specifically, how does this affect the max-pooling operation?

The choice of appending embeddings for only one word before and after the trigger is somewhat arbitrary. Providing a rationale for this specific window size would strengthen the paper.

The role of the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* (line 330) is unclear, as it doesn’t appear to be used in subsequent sections where *v<sub>sent+lex</sub>* is utilized.

Finally, the usage of pairwise features in Section 3.2 needs further explanation. While the binary features are likely encoded as a binary vector, the handling of the continuous distance feature is not specified. Are these features fixed during training, or are they also learned?

**Other Issues and Suggestions:**

Exploring the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution would be a valuable extension. This would enable comparisons with a broader range of previous work and established datasets such as OntoNotes.

The use of the square function as a nonlinearity is an interesting design choice. Is this novel, and if so, are there potential benefits for its application to other tasks?

Regarding datasets, while one dataset is publicly available, the results also include ACE++. Making ACE++ publicly available would greatly benefit the research community, allowing for more direct comparisons of new methods. At a minimum, including a comparison of feature-rich systems on ACE++ would be highly valuable.

The reported results contain several close values. Performing statistical significance testing would strengthen the validity of the performance comparisons.

In the related work section, the neural network approach to coreference resolution by Wiseman et al. (2015) should be mentioned.
