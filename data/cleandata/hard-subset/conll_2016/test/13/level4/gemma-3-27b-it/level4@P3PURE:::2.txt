This paper presents a model for event entity linking, proposing the use of sentential features from CNNs in place of external knowledge sources commonly employed in earlier methods. The model is trained in two parts: the first learns a representation for event mentions, and the second calculates a coreference score given two such mentions. 

The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to follow. I particularly appreciate the analysis conducted on the ACE corpus regarding argument sharing between event coreferences. Furthermore, the analysis of dataset size impact provides strong motivation for the creation of their ACE++ dataset. However, several major issues require attention:

- The authors do not adequately motivate or analyze the advantages and disadvantages of using CNNs for generating mention representations. The rationale behind choosing CNNs is not discussed, and no comparisons are made to alternative models, such as RNNs. Given that the model’s improvement over state-of-the-art methods is only 2-3 F1 points, further evidence is needed to demonstrate the superiority of this architecture.
- The novelty of tackling event linking with sentential features is unclear, as applying CNNs in this manner to a classification task is not novel. The authors should explicitly highlight and primarily compare their approach to existing continuous space methods for event linking. The selection of methods in Table 3 appears insufficient for a thorough comparison.
- The methodology for collecting the ACE++ dataset is not described. A known limitation of the ACE dataset is its limited number of event types, which can lead to constrained and biased results. It is crucial to specify the event types covered by ACE++. This information would also strengthen the claim in Section 5.1 that “other approaches are strongly tied to the domain where these semantic features are available…our approach does not depend on resources with restricted…”, requiring demonstration of prior methods failing on a dataset where your approach succeeds. Moreover, to enable meaningful future comparisons, the authors should consider making this dataset publicly available.

Some minor issues:

- I would have appreciated seeing the model’s performance in Table 3 without gold references.
- It would be beneficial to explore whether and how this model can be augmented with a standard coreference resolution system. For example, the off-the-shelf CoreNLP system readily links ‘It’ to ‘bombing’ in line 687, and such capabilities could inform an event entity linking baseline.
- Given the relatively small size of the ACE dataset, a compelling model requires evaluation on additional resources. This further motivates investigating entity and event coreference simultaneously. I believe testing on EventCorefBank in parallel with ACE is essential.
- Table 5 demonstrates the effectiveness of pairwise features, suggesting that feature engineering remains crucial for achieving a competitive model, particularly at the scale of the ACE dataset. It would be valuable to identify which features were most effective and explain the rationale behind the current feature set’s selection, as well as what other features were explored.
