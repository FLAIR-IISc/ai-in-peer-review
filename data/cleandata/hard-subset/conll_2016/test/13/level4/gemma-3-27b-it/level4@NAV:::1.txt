This paper investigates event linking using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors generate vector representations of event mentions by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling, and then concatenate these representations with embeddings of surrounding words. A single-layer neural network, incorporating pairwise features, is then used to produce similarity vectors and ultimately a coreference score. The model was evaluated on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance competitive with existing feature-rich systems.

The primary contribution lies in the development of a neural approach to event linking that effectively integrates word embeddings with a selection of linguistic features. The findings demonstrate that word embeddings alone are insufficient for achieving strong performance, while the chosen linguistic features are efficiently incorporated without requiring extensive manual feature engineering.

**Experimental Setup:**

- The use of gold trigger words, rather than predicted ones, raises a concern. While the authors provide justification, results with predicted triggers would be valuable, particularly as a competing system utilizes them, creating an uneven comparison.
- Inconsistencies in train/test splits across different papers are a potential issue. Adhering to established splits would improve comparability.

**Unclear Points:**

- The evidence supporting the need for cross-sentential information is compelling. However, the statement in lines 65-70 of the second paragraph lacked clarity.
- The generation of positional embeddings “in a way similar to word embeddings” requires further explanation. Specifically, are these embeddings randomly initialized, or are they lexicalized? The rationale for assigning the same embedding to relative positions regardless of the adjacent word is unclear.
- The precise method for incorporating left versus right neighbor embeddings into the representation (lines 307-311) is ambiguous. Does this impact only the max-pooling layer?
- Appending the embeddings of only the immediately preceding and following words to the trigger word seems arbitrary. The reasoning behind this specific window size is unclear.
- The usage of the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* (line 330) is unclear, as subsequent sections appear to solely utilize *v<sub>sent+lex</sub>*.
- The integration of pairwise features in section 3.2 requires clarification. While binary features are likely encoded as a binary vector, the treatment of continuous features like distance is unspecified. It's also unclear whether these features are fixed during training.

**Other Issues and Suggestions:**

- Exploring the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution would broaden the scope of comparison with prior work and established datasets like OntoNotes.
- The employment of a square function as the nonlinearity is intriguing. Investigating its novelty and potential transferability to other tasks would be worthwhile.
- The datasets used: While one dataset is publicly accessible, results are also reported on ACE++, which is not. Releasing ACE++ would facilitate broader research and allow for more direct comparisons. A comparison of the feature-rich systems on ACE++ would be particularly useful.
- The reported results exhibit some close values. Statistical significance testing would help validate the observed differences.
- In the related work section, the neural network approach for coreference resolution by Wiseman et al. (2015) should be mentioned.

**Minor Issues:**

- Line 143: The word "that" is redundant.
- Table 6 refers to a baseline as "same type," while the text (line 670) labels it as "same event."
