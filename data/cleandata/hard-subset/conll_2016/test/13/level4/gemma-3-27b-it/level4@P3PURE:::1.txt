This paper models event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations based on word embeddings passed through a CNN followed by max-pooling. These representations are then concatenated with several surrounding word embeddings. Together with certain pairwise features, this combined representation is used to produce a vector of similarities via a single-layer neural network, which is then used to compute a coreference score. The model is tested on the ACE dataset and an expanded version thereof, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

The main contribution of the paper, in my opinion, lies in its development of a neural approach to event linking that combines word embeddings with several linguistic features. It is interesting to observe that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for achieving strong performance. Fortunately, the linguistic features employed are limited and do not require manually-crafted external resources.

**Experimental Setting**

*   Gold trigger words are used rather than predicted ones. The authors provide a justification for this choice, but I would have still appreciated seeing performance results with predicted triggers. This is particularly concerning as one of the competitor systems utilizes predicted triggers, rendering the comparison somewhat unfair.
*   The use of differing train/test splits across papers is a concern. I encourage the authors to adhere to previously established splits as much as possible.

**Unclear Points**

*   The results indicating the necessity of cross-sentential information are convincing. However, the final statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) was unclear to me.
*   Embeddings for positions are stated to be generated “in a way similar to word embeddings.” How exactly? Are they randomly initialized? Are they lexicalized? It is unclear why a relative position next to one word should have the same embedding as a relative position next to a different word.
*   How precisely are left versus right neighbors used to create the representation (lines 307-311)? Does this only affect the max-pooling operation?
*   The word embeddings of the single word preceding and following the trigger word are appended to its representation. This choice appears somewhat arbitrary. Why only one word before and after, and not some other window size?
*   It is unclear how the event-mention representation v<sub>e</sub> (line 330) is utilized. In subsequent sections, only v<sub>{sent+lex}</sub> appears to be used, not v<sub>e</sub>.
*   How are pairwise features incorporated in section 3.2? Most features are binary, suggesting they are encoded as a binary vector; however, what about the distance feature, for example? And are these features kept fixed during training?

**Other Issues and Suggestions**

*   Could this approach be applied to entity coreference resolution? This would facilitate a comparison with a wider range of previous work and popular datasets such as OntoNotes.
*   The use of a square function as a nonlinearity is intriguing. Is this novel? Do the authors believe it has potential applicability in other tasks?
*   **Datasets:** One dataset is publicly available, but results are also presented with ACE++, which is not. Do the authors have plans to release ACE++? This would greatly assist other researchers in comparing new methods. At a minimum, a comparison to feature-rich systems on ACE++ would be beneficial.
*   **Results:** Some of the reported numerical results are very close. Significance testing would help to substantiate the reported comparisons.
*   **Related Work:** Within the realm of (entity) coreference resolution, the neural network approach by Wiseman et al. (2015) might be mentioned.

**Minor Issues**

*   Line 143, “that” is redundant.
*   One of the baselines is referred to as “same type” in Table 6, but as “same event” in the text (line 670).

**References**

*   Wiseman, S., Rush, A. M., Weston, J., & Shieber, S. M. (2015). Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. *ACL 2015*.
