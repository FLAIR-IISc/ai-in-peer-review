This paper presents a model for the task of event linking, proposing the use of sentential features from Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in place of external knowledge sources utilized by previous methods. The authors train a two-part model: the first learns an event mention representation, and the second calculates a coreference score given two event mentions.

The paper is well-written, well-presented, and easy to follow. The analysis performed on the ACE corpus regarding argument sharing between coreferential event mentions is insightful. Furthermore, the analysis demonstrating the impact of dataset size motivates the creation of the ACE++ dataset. However, several major issues require addressing.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that event linking can achieve state-of-the-art performance *without* relying on external semantic resources, by leveraging sentential features extracted using CNNs. A secondary contribution is the creation and release of the ACE++ dataset, expanding the available training data for this task. Finally, the detailed error analysis provides valuable insights into the challenges of event coreference resolution.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art performance without external resources:** The model achieves competitive results on established datasets (ACE and ACE++) without relying on expensive and domain-specific external knowledge bases. This is a significant advancement, increasing the portability and applicability of event linking systems to new domains. The reported improvements, while modest (2-3 points on F1), are nonetheless noteworthy given the maturity of the field.
2. **Creation of the ACE++ dataset:** The expansion of the ACE dataset to ACE++ provides a more substantial resource for training and evaluating event linking models. This dataset is crucial for enabling further research and development in this area. The initial analysis justifying its creation (argument incompleteness, differing trigger words) is sound and clearly presented.
3. **Detailed error analysis:** The manual error analysis, highlighting difficulties with pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, provides valuable guidance for future research directions. Identifying the limitations of the current model is a constructive step towards improving event linking performance.
4. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture and experimental setup are clearly described, facilitating reproducibility and further experimentation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient justification for CNN architecture:** The authors do not adequately motivate the choice of CNNs for generating mention representations. A comparative analysis against other architectures, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), is missing. While CNNs are a reasonable choice, demonstrating their superiority through empirical comparison is essential. The claim that the CNN architecture is *superior* requires stronger evidence than a modest F1 score improvement.
2. **Limited novelty and related work discussion:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of existing continuous space methods for event linking that also leverage sentential features. While the authors acknowledge recent work utilizing sentential features for relation/event extraction, they fail to position their work within this landscape comprehensively. The choice of methods for comparison in Table 3 is not exhaustive and does not convincingly demonstrate the novelty of the proposed approach.
3. **Lack of ACE++ dataset details:** Critical information regarding the ACE++ dataset is missing. Specifically, the paper does not detail the event types covered in ACE++, which is crucial for understanding the dataset's scope and potential biases. The claim that the approach avoids dependence on domain-specific resources is weakened without demonstrating generalization to events not well-represented in the original ACE dataset. The authors should prioritize publicly releasing the dataset to enable broader validation.
4. **Limited ablation study:** While Table 5 provides *some* insight into feature importance, the ablation study feels incomplete. A more detailed analysis of individual pairwise features, and a justification for their selection, would be beneficial. Understanding *which* features are most effective, and why, could inform future feature engineering efforts.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a detailed breakdown of the event types included in the ACE++ dataset?
2. Did you explore alternative neural network architectures (e.g., RNNs, LSTMs) for generating event representations? If so, what were the results? If not, why?
3. Can you elaborate on the process of selecting the specific pairwise features used in the model? What other features were considered and discarded, and why?
4. The error analysis mentions difficulties with pronoun resolution. Have you considered incorporating an off-the-shelf coreference resolution system as a pre-processing step to address this issue?

**Additional Comments:**

* It would be informative to see the model's performance without gold-annotated event mentions, to assess its robustness to errors in the upstream event extraction stage.
* Testing the model on the EventCorefBank dataset, in addition to ACE and ACE++, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its generalizability.
* The observation that pairwise features are effective suggests that feature engineering remains important, even with the use of neural networks. Further investigation in this area could yield significant performance gains.
