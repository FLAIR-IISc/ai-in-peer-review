This paper models event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations based on word embeddings passed through a CNN and followed by max-pooling. They also concatenate the resulting representations with several lexical embeddings around the mention. Together with certain pairwise features, they produce a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network, and compute a coreference score. 
The model is tested on the ACE 2005 dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

The primary contribution of this work is the development of a neural approach for event linking that combines word embeddings with several linguistic features. It is notable that good performance requires more than just word embeddings alone. Furthermore, the linguistic features utilized are limited and do not necessitate manually-crafted external resources. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on two datasets without relying on external semantic features, addressing a limitation of prior work. 

Strengths:
1. **Strong Performance:** The model achieves competitive results on the ACE 2005 dataset, comparable to systems relying on extensive external features. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach using only sentential features and limited lexical information.
2. **Reduced Reliance on External Resources:** The method avoids dependence on potentially domain-specific and costly external resources like WordNet or FrameNet. This enhances the portability and applicability of the model to new domains.
3. **Effective Feature Combination:** The integration of CNN-generated sentential features with lexical features and pairwise features proves successful, suggesting a complementary relationship between these feature types for event coreference resolution.

Weaknesses:
1. **Non-Standard Evaluation Setup:** The use of gold trigger words, rather than predicted ones, introduces a potential bias in the evaluation. While the authors provide a justification, it complicates direct comparison with systems that employ a full event extraction pipeline. The comparison is not entirely fair given that one competitor system used predicted triggers.
2. **Dataset Splitting Concerns:** The inconsistency in train/test splits across different papers is problematic. Adherence to previously established splits would improve the reproducibility and comparability of results.
3. **Lack of Clarity in Implementation Details:** Several aspects of the implementation remain unclear, specifically regarding the generation of position embeddings, the utilization of left vs. right neighbors during max-pooling, the use of the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>*, and the encoding/training of pairwise features.

Questions to Authors:
1. How were position embeddings generated? Specifically, were they randomly initialized or lexicalized, and why is a shared embedding used for relative positions regardless of the surrounding word?
2. Could you clarify how the left and right neighbors are utilized in creating the event representation, beyond influencing the max-pooling operation?
3. Is the event-mention representation *v<sub>e</sub>* actually used in subsequent calculations, or is *v<sub>sent+lex</sub>* used exclusively?
4. How are the pairwise features encoded, particularly the continuous distance feature? Are these features fixed during training or learned?
5. Could you elaborate on the justification for using a square function as the nonlinearity in the similarity calculation? Is it a novel approach, and do you foresee its applicability in other tasks?

Additional Comments:
- Exploring the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution, and comparing it to existing work on datasets like OntoNotes, would be valuable.
- Releasing the ACE++ dataset would facilitate further research and comparison of new methods. A comparison of the feature-rich systems on ACE++ would also be informative.
- Performing significance testing on the reported results would strengthen the validity of the comparisons.
- The related work section could benefit from including recent neural network approaches to coreference resolution, such as Wiseman et al. (2015).
- Line 143, remove "that".
- In Table 6, standardize the terminology: either "same type" or "same event," but not both.
