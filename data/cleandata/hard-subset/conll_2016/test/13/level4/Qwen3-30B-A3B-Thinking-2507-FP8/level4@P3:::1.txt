

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**  
This paper proposes a neural network approach for event linking (resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document) that eliminates reliance on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. Instead, the model generates sentential features via convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to represent event mentions, combining these with lexical and pairwise features for coreference decisions. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE 2005 and ACE++, a larger expanded version), showing that domain-agnostic sentential features can achieve competitive results without costly external resources.  

The primary contribution is a novel event linking framework that achieves strong performance without external semantic features, addressing a key limitation of prior work. The second contribution is empirical validation of sentential features' importance, supported by analysis showing 50.97% of coreferential pairs share no arguments and 47.29% have different triggers. The third contribution is a detailed error analysis identifying persistent challenges like pronoun resolution and ambiguous annotation cases.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Eliminating dependency on external resources**: The model achieves SOTA results without relying on domain-specific semantic resources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet), which are costly to create and have limited cross-domain applicability. This is a significant practical contribution, as prior systems (e.g., Liu et al., 2014) require "105 semantic features" from such resources. The authors convincingly argue that sentential features from CNNs can replace these, broadening the applicability of event linking systems.  

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides compelling evidence for the necessity of sentential features. The analysis showing 55.42% of coreferential mentions are >5 sentences apart and 50.97% share no arguments demonstrates that local features alone are insufficient. The ablation study (Table 5) further confirms that removing sentential features degrades performance (e.g., F1 drops from 76.90% to 64.12% when removed), while pairwise features alone yield poor results (F1=56.69%).  

3. **Actionable error analysis**: The manual error analysis (100 false positives/negatives) identifies two key challenges: (a) pronoun resolution (e.g., "It" referring to prior events), which is a known hard problem, and (b) boundary cases where human annotators disagree (e.g., 17 false negatives matched 17 false positives in annotation inconsistencies). This contextualizes the model’s performance and provides clear directions for future work.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Unfair comparison due to gold-standard triggers**: The authors use gold-standard event triggers in evaluation, while Liu et al. (2014) (a key comparator) uses predicted triggers. This creates an uneven comparison, as the model’s performance would likely degrade in a full pipeline setting. The authors acknowledge this but do not address it with experiments on predicted triggers, weakening the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance in realistic scenarios.  

2. **ACE++ dataset not publicly available**: The paper reports results on ACE++ (a larger dataset), but does not release it. While ACE 2005 is publicly available, ACE++ is not, hindering reproducibility and comparison with future work. The authors note "one dataset is publicly available," but ACE++ is central to their results, making this omission problematic.  

3. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: Performance differences between the model and competitors (e.g., 73.31% vs. 72.19% F1) are small, yet the paper does not provide significance testing. Without this, it is unclear whether the improvements are meaningful or due to random variation.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Could you provide results using predicted event triggers (not gold-standard) to better align with pipeline evaluation and enable fairer comparisons with prior work?  

2. When will you release the ACE++ dataset? This would significantly aid reproducibility and future research.  

3. Have you considered applying this approach to entity coreference resolution? This would allow comparison with established datasets (e.g., OntoNotes) and broader relevance to coreference literature.  

**Additional Comments**  
- The paper claims "an error analysis points out directions for further research," but the error analysis could be strengthened by explicitly linking specific error patterns to potential model improvements (e.g., incorporating discourse-level features for pronoun resolution).  
- The use of the square activation in the similarity layer is interesting but not sufficiently motivated. Is this a novel contribution, or a minor design choice?  
- The baselines in Table 6 are not clearly distinguished from the model’s components (e.g., "same type" vs. "same event" in Table 6). Clarifying this would improve readability.
