

This paper proposes a CNN-based approach for event linking, generating vector representations from word embeddings processed through a CNN and max-pooling. These representations are augmented by concatenating surrounding word embeddings (one word before and after the event trigger) and incorporating pairwise features. A single-layer neural network then computes similarity vectors to produce coreference scores. Evaluated on ACE and ACE++, the model achieves performance competitive with prior feature-rich systems.

The primary contribution lies in demonstrating a neural framework for event linking that effectively combines word embeddings with minimal, linguistically motivated features—eliminating the need for manually crafted external resources. Notably, the work establishes that raw word embeddings alone are insufficient, underscoring the value of the integrated linguistic features.

**Experimental Concerns**  
The use of *gold* trigger words (rather than predicted ones) for evaluation raises fairness concerns. While the authors justify this choice, the comparison becomes skewed since a key competitor (Mihalcea et al.) uses predicted triggers. A fairer comparison should include results with predicted triggers. Additionally, inconsistent train/test splits across studies (e.g., ACE vs. ACE++) hinder reproducibility; the authors should adhere to established splits where possible.

**Unclear Methodology**  
Several technical details require clarification:  
1. Position embeddings (used for relative mention positions) are described as "generated similarly to word embeddings." It is unclear whether these are randomly initialized, lexicalized, or context-dependent. Why should relative position *x* next to word *A* share the same embedding as position *x* next to word *B*?  
2. The handling of left/right neighbors (lines 307–311) is ambiguous. Does this affect only the max-pooling operation, or is it integrated earlier?  
3. The event-mention representation **v**<sub>e</sub> (line 330) is referenced but never used in subsequent sections—only **v**<sub>sent+lex</sub> appears. Its purpose and role are unclear.  
4. Pairwise features (Section 3.2) include binary features (e.g., event type match) and distance. How is distance encoded (e.g., discretized, continuous)? Are these features fixed during training or learned?  

**Suggestions for Improvement**  
- Extend the approach to *entity coreference resolution* (e.g., OntoNotes) to enable broader comparisons with established neural methods (e.g., Wiseman et al., ACL 2015).  
- Justify the use of a *square activation function*—is this novel? What broader applicability might it have?  
- Release ACE++ data to facilitate reproducibility and cross-method comparison. At minimum, include results on ACE++ for all baselines.  
- Apply *statistical significance testing* to results, as some performance differences (e.g., Table 5) are marginal.  

**Minor Issues**  
- Line 143: Remove redundant "that" ("the paper *that* models...").  
- Table 6 labels a baseline "same type," but the text (line 670) uses "same event." Standardize terminology.  
- Correct typo: "generaties" → "generated" (line 307).  
- Cite Wiseman et al. (ACL 2015) in related work for neural coreference.  

**Summary**  
The paper presents a compelling neural event linking model that effectively leverages minimal linguistic features with word embeddings. While the core idea is sound and results are competitive, key experimental choices (gold triggers, inconsistent splits) and methodological ambiguities (position embeddings, **v**<sub>e</sub> usage) weaken the contribution. Addressing these—particularly by releasing ACE++ data and reporting results with predicted triggers—would significantly strengthen the work. The approach’s potential for entity coreference resolution merits exploration. With clarifications and expanded evaluation, this could make a valuable contribution to the field.
