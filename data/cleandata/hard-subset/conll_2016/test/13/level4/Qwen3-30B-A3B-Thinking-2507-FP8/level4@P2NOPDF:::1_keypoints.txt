- Proposes a CNN-based event linking model using word embeddings, CNN+max-pooling, neighbor concatenation, pairwise features, and a single-layer network for coreference scores; competitive on ACE/ACE++.
- Main contribution: neural framework that combines word embeddings with minimal linguistically motivated features, showing raw embeddings alone are insufficient.
- Experimental concern: evaluation uses gold triggers, making comparisons unfair versus baselines that use predicted triggers; results with predicted triggers should be reported.
- Experimental concern: inconsistent train/test splits (ACE vs. ACE++) hurt reproducibility; authors should use established splits where possible.
- Methodology unclear: how position embeddings are generated/initialized and why the same embedding would apply across different lexical contexts.
- Methodology unclear: ambiguous description of left/right neighbor handling (lines 307–311)—whether it affects only max-pooling or earlier layers.
- Methodology unclear: event-mention representation v_e is introduced but never used later; its purpose and role must be clarified.
- Methodology unclear: encoding of pairwise features (especially distance) is unspecified and unclear whether these features are fixed or learned.
- Suggestion: evaluate/extend the approach to entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes) to compare with established neural methods like Wiseman et al. (ACL 2015).
- Suggestion: justify the use of a square activation function and discuss its novelty and broader applicability.
- Suggestion: release ACE++ data or at least report all baselines on ACE++ to facilitate reproducibility and comparison.
- Suggestion: perform statistical significance testing for marginal performance differences reported (e.g., Table 5).
- Minor issues: fix wording/typos (remove redundant “that” line 143; “generaties”→“generated” line 307), standardize “same type” vs. “same event” (Table 6/line 670), and cite Wiseman et al. (ACL 2015).
- Summary: promising and competitive model, but experimental choices (gold triggers, splits) and methodological ambiguities weaken the contribution; addressing these would substantially strengthen the paper.
