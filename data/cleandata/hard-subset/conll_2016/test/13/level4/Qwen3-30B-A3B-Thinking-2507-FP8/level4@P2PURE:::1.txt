

This paper models event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations from word embeddings processed through a CNN followed by max-pooling. They concatenate these representations with word embeddings from surrounding context. Combined with pairwise features, they produce a similarity vector via a single-layer neural network to compute coreference scores. The model achieves performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems on the ACE dataset and an expanded version (ACE++).  

The primary contribution lies in developing a neural approach for event linking that integrates word embeddings with limited linguistic features, avoiding manually-crafted external resources. Notably, word embeddings alone prove insufficient for strong performance.  

**Experimental Setting**  
- Gold trigger words are used instead of predicted ones, which the authors justify, though I recommend reporting results with predicted triggers for fairer comparison (as one competitor uses predicted triggers).  
- Different train/test splits across papers are concerning; the authors should adopt prior splits where feasible.  

**Unclear Points**  
- The evidence for cross-sentential information needs is convincing, but the final statement in lines 65–70 remains unclear.  
- Position embeddings are described as "generated similarly to word embeddings." How? Are they randomly initialized or lexicalized? Why should relative position embeddings (e.g., "left of word X" vs. "left of word Y") be identical?  
- The method for using left/right neighbors (lines 307–311) is ambiguous: does this affect only max-pooling?  
- Appending one word before/after triggers appears arbitrary; why not alternative window sizes?  
- The event-mention representation \(v_e\) (line 330) is unclear in usage; subsequent sections reference only \(v_{\text{sent+lex}}\), not \(v_e\).  
- Pairwise features (Section 3.2) include binary features (encoded as binary vectors) and distance. How is distance handled, and are features fixed during training?  

**Other Issues and Suggestions**  
- Could the approach extend to entity coreference resolution (e.g., OntoNotes)? This would enable comparison with more prior work.  
- The square nonlinearity is interesting: is it novel? Does it generalize to other tasks?  
- ACE++ results are reported without public release. Plans to share ACE++ would aid reproducibility; a comparison with feature-rich systems on ACE++ would also strengthen the evaluation.  
- Results show close numerical values; significance testing would bolster comparisons.  
- Related work: Mention Wiseman et al. (2015) for neural coreference resolution.  

**Minor Issues**  
- Line 143: "that" is redundant.  
- Table 6 refers to a baseline as "same type," but the text (line 670) uses "same event."  

**References**
