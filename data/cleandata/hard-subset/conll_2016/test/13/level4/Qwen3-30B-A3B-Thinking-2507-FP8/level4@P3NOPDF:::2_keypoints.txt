- Strengths: clear writing, well-structured analysis of argument sharing in ACE-2005, and strong motivation for creating ACE++ via empirical dataset-size analysis.  
- Major concern: no justification for using CNNs to generate mention representations; authors must compare against RNNs/LSTMs and provide ablation studies.  
- Performance concern: only marginal improvements (2–3 F1); without evidence CNNs fundamentally outperform sequence models, contribution seems incremental.  
- Novelty weak: sentential features’ novelty is not established and the paper fails to contrast with existing continuous-space methods (e.g., pre-trained embeddings, GNNs); key recent baselines are omitted.  
- ACE++ documentation lacking: paper fails to specify event types, scope, or how ACE++ addresses ACE-2005 biases, undermining the dataset’s claimed benefits.  
- Reproducibility concern: authors must commit to publicly releasing ACE++ to enable comparisons and validation.  
- Comparative claim missing: need experiments showing prior resource-dependent methods (e.g., FrameNet-based) perform poorly on ACE++ while the proposed method succeeds.  
- Table 3 issue: should include results without gold mentions to assess real-world applicability.  
- Baseline omission: integrate standard coreference systems (e.g., CoreNLP) as baselines, given examples where they resolve events without event-specific processing.  
- Generalizability: test on additional datasets like EventCorefBank beyond ACE-2005 to validate broader applicability.  
- Feature analysis: clarify how pairwise features were selected, run ablation studies, and report which features contribute most to performance.  
- Overall verdict: paper has merit but needs stronger justification for architectural choices, clearer novelty claims, ACE++ transparency, and broader validation to avoid being seen as a minor incremental improvement.
