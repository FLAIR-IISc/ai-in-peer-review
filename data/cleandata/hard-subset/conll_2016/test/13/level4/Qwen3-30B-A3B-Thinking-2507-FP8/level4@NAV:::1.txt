

This paper models event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling. They concatenate these representations with contextual word embeddings surrounding each mention. Combined with specific pairwise features, these elements form a similarity vector via a single-layer neural network, yielding a coreference score.  
The model is evaluated on the ACE dataset and an expanded ACE++ version, achieving performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems.  
The primary contribution lies in introducing a neural approach for entity linking that integrates word embeddings with linguistic features. Notably, word embeddings alone prove insufficient for strong performance, while the incorporated linguistic features are minimal and require no manually crafted external resources.  

Experimental setting  
- Gold trigger words are used instead of predicted ones. Although the authors justify this choice, performance with predicted triggers should be included for fair comparison, especially since a competitor system employs predicted triggers.  
- Inconsistent train/test splits across studies are concerning; the authors should adopt standard splits where possible.  

Unclear points  
- The evidence for cross-sentential information necessity is convincing, but the final statement in lines 65–70 lacks clarity.  
- Position embeddings are described as "generated similarly to word embeddings." Clarify: Are they randomly initialized, lexicalized, or derived differently? Why should relative position embeddings remain consistent across distinct words?  
- The handling of left/right neighbors (lines 307–311) requires elaboration: Does this affect only max-pooling?  
- Appending one word before/after triggers (line 307) appears arbitrary; justify the choice of one-word context.  
- The event-mention representation \(v_e\) (line 330) is unclear, as subsequent sections exclusively use \(v_{\text{sent+lex}}\).  
- Pairwise features (Section 3.2) are not fully explained: How are binary features encoded? How is the distance feature handled, and are features fixed during training?  

Other issues and suggestions  
- Can this approach extend to entity coreference resolution (e.g., OntoNotes)? This would enable broader comparisons with established work.  
- The square nonlinearity is novel; discuss its potential applicability beyond this task.  
- ACE++ results are reported without public release. Sharing ACE++ would facilitate reproducibility and fair comparisons. At minimum, include feature-rich system results on ACE++.  
- Some performance differences are marginal; significance testing would strengthen comparisons.  
- In related work, cite Wiseman et al. (2015) on neural coreference resolution.  

Minor issues  
- Line 143: "that" is redundant.  
- Table 6 refers to a baseline as "same type," while the text (line 670) calls it "same event."  

Refs  
- Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. Sam Wiseman, Alexander M. Rush, Jason Weston, and Stuart M. Shieber. ACL 2015.
