

This paper proposes a novel approach to event coreference resolution that avoids reliance on external semantic resources by leveraging sentential features generated through convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors design a two-part model: the first part uses CNNs to learn representations of individual event mentions by processing their surrounding sentences, and the second part combines these representations with pairwise features to calculate coreference scores. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, with one being publicly available.

Main contributions (ordered by strength):
1. A CNN-based architecture for generating sentential features for event mentions, eliminating the need for external semantic resources that are domain-limited.
2. Comprehensive evaluation showing that the model achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic features, demonstrating that domain-specific performance limitations can be overcome.
3. An analysis of the model's error patterns, which provides valuable insights for future research directions.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a strong motivation for their approach through thorough analysis of the ACE 2005 corpus, showing that a significant portion of event mentions require cross-sentence resolution. The authors convincingly demonstrate that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart, making locality-based heuristics insufficient.
2. The creation and analysis of the ACE++ dataset is well-justified and provides valuable insights into the impact of dataset size on model performance. The paper clearly shows that increasing training data from ACE to ACE++ boosts recall from 75.16% to 83.21% while maintaining reasonable precision.
3. The error analysis is particularly insightful, identifying that a significant portion of false negatives involves pronoun resolution (e.g., "It" referring to a previous event mention), which represents a challenging but important direction for future work.

Weaknesses:
1. The authors fail to adequately justify why they chose CNNs over alternative architectures like RNNs or LSTMs for generating event representations. Given that their model only achieves 2-3 point improvements in F1 score over state-of-the-art, more thorough architectural comparisons are needed to justify their choice. The paper lacks ablation studies comparing different neural architectures for the first part of their model.
2. The novelty claim is not sufficiently supported. The paper does not clearly differentiate their use of CNNs for event linking from prior work on using neural networks for relation/event extraction. The authors should explicitly compare to continuous space methods for event linking and more thoroughly justify their methodological choices.
3. The ACE++ dataset is inadequately described, making it difficult to assess the generalizability of their results. The paper doesn't specify what event types ACE++ covers or how it differs from ACE in terms of domain coverage. This is critical for supporting their claim that their approach "does not depend on resources with restricted domain availability." Without knowing the event type coverage of ACE++, it's impossible to verify whether their approach generalizes to domains where previous methods fail.
4. The paper lacks a comparison with non-gold event mentions in Table 3, which would better reflect real-world application scenarios. The current evaluation only uses gold-standard event mentions, which doesn't reflect the error propagation that would occur in an end-to-end pipeline.
5. The authors don't explore how their model could be integrated with or augmented by existing coreference resolution systems. For example, the paper mentions cases where pronoun resolution is challenging, yet fails to consider how off-the-shelf systems like CoreNLP might help address these cases.

Questions to authors:
1. Can you provide more details about the event types included in ACE++ and how they differ from ACE 2005? This would help assess the domain coverage and generalizability of your results.
2. Could you provide results for your model using non-gold event mentions to better reflect real-world application scenarios?
3. How would your model perform when integrated with a standard coreference resolution system for handling pronoun references, as mentioned in the error analysis?
4. Have you considered testing your model on other event coreference datasets like EventCorefBank or ProcessBank to further validate its generalizability?
5. Can you provide more details on the ablation studies comparing different neural architectures for the first part of your model?
