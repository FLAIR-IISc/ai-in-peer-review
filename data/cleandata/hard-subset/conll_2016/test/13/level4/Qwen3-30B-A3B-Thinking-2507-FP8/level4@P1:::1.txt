

This paper proposes a neural approach for event coreference resolution that avoids reliance on external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet). The core contribution is a two-step model: (1) convolutional neural networks (CNNs) generate sentential representations for individual event mentions by processing their full sentence context, and (2) these representations—augmented with lexical and pairwise features—are fed into a similarity function for coreference scoring. Crucially, the model achieves state-of-the-art results on two datasets (ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++) without external semantic features, demonstrating that domain-specific knowledge bases are not essential for high performance. The second contribution is the empirical validation of sentential features' necessity through analysis showing 55.42% of coreferential event mentions span >5 sentences, making locality-based heuristics insufficient. The third contribution is the error analysis revealing unresolved challenges, particularly pronoun resolution, which highlights the task's inherent ambiguity.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel resource-agnostic approach**: The work successfully demonstrates that high-performance event linking can be achieved without external semantic resources, addressing a key limitation of prior work (e.g., Liu et al., 2014). This significantly broadens applicability to domains lacking curated knowledge bases.  
2. **Strong empirical validation**: The model outperforms prior SOTA systems (Liu et al., 2014) on ACE 2005 across multiple metrics (BLANC F1: 73.31% vs. 70.43%), while using a single model without domain-specific feature engineering. The ACE++ experiments further confirm robustness with larger training data (BLANC F1: 76.90% vs. 73.31%).  
3. **Insightful feature analysis**: The ablation study (Table 5) clearly shows that all feature classes (pairwise, lexical, sentential) contribute positively to performance. Removing sentential features (row 4) drastically reduces recall (62.73% → 56.69%), underscoring their necessity for cross-sentence links.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Unfair comparison due to gold-trigger dependency**: The authors use gold event triggers (not predicted), while Liu et al. (2014) use predicted triggers. This undermines the performance comparison, as Liu’s system must handle error propagation from trigger detection. The authors’ justification ("mitigate error propagation") does not resolve the inconsistency, especially since the competitor’s approach is explicitly designed for pipeline integration.  
2. **Inconsistent dataset splits**: The ACE 2005 train/test splits vary across systems (as noted in Table 3), making direct comparisons unreliable. The authors acknowledge this but do not adopt a standardized split (e.g., the one used by Liu et al.), weakening the claim of "SOTA performance."  
3. **Ambiguous position embeddings**: The paper states position embeddings are "generated similarly to word embeddings" (lines 307–311) but fails to clarify their initialization or rationale. Why should relative positions (e.g., "left of trigger") share embeddings across different words? Are they randomly initialized or lexically constrained? This omission hinders reproducibility.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The model uses gold event triggers, while Liu et al. (2014) uses predicted triggers. How would performance change if your model were evaluated end-to-end (with predicted triggers)?  
2. The ACE++ dataset is not publicly released. Will it be made available to facilitate future comparisons? If not, how can the community validate your ACE++ results?  

**Additional Comments**  
- The claim that "just using word embeddings is insufficient" (lines 307–311) is supported by Table 5 (row 1: 56.69% BLANC F1), but the rationale for the specific one-word context window (vs. larger windows) is unclear.  
- The square activation in Eq. (4) is intriguing but not contextualized against prior work (e.g., Wiseman et al., 2015). Is this a novel contribution or a minor optimization?  
- Table 6’s "same type" baseline should be consistently termed "same event type" (not "same event") to avoid confusion with the event-linking task.  
- The error analysis (Section 5.3) correctly identifies pronoun resolution as a key challenge, but the claim that "17 false negatives correspond to false positives with inconsistent annotations" (lines 691–693) requires more detail (e.g., how many examples were ambiguous to human annotators?).
