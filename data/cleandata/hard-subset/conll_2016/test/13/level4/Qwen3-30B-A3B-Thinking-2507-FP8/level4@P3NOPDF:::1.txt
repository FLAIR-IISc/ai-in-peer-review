

This paper proposes a CNN-based approach for event linking, generating mention representations via word embeddings processed through a CNN with max-pooling. These representations are augmented with contextual word embeddings (one word before/after the trigger) and pairwise features, combined via a single-layer neural network to compute coreference scores. Evaluated on ACE and an expanded ACE++ dataset, the model achieves performance comparable to feature-rich baselines. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating a neural approach that effectively integrates word embeddings with minimal, non-manually-constructed linguistic features—highlighting that embeddings alone are insufficient for strong performance.

**Experimental Validity Concerns**  
The use of *gold trigger words* (rather than predicted ones) undermines comparability. While the authors justify this choice, the comparison is unfair since a key competitor (e.g., [Baseline X]) uses predicted triggers. The authors should report results with predicted triggers to enable a valid comparison. Additionally, inconsistent train/test splits across studies (e.g., ACE vs. ACE++) raise reproducibility concerns; adherence to established splits is strongly recommended.

**Key Unclear Points**  
1.  **Position Embeddings**: The paper states position embeddings are "generated similarly to word embeddings" but does not clarify their initialization (random, lexicalized) or rationale. Why should relative position embeddings (e.g., "1 word left") be shared across *all* contexts rather than being context-specific? This needs explicit justification.  
2.  **Neighbor Representation**: The handling of left/right neighbors (lines 307–311) is ambiguous. Does this affect only the max-pooling step, or does it alter the input representation? A clearer technical description is required.  
3.  **Contextual Embedding Choice**: Appending *exactly one* word before/after the trigger (line 307) appears arbitrary. The authors should justify this choice (e.g., via ablation studies) and discuss alternatives.  
4.  **Event Representation Usage**: The event-mention vector \(v_e\) (line 330) is introduced but not used in subsequent sections—only \(v_{\text{sent+lex}}\) appears. Its purpose and integration must be clarified.  
5.  **Pairwise Feature Handling**: Section 3.2 describes binary pairwise features but omits details on non-binary features (e.g., distance). How are these encoded (e.g., discretized, continuous)? Are they trainable or fixed during training?  

**Additional Suggestions**  
- **Broader Applicability**: The approach could be extended to *entity coreference resolution* (e.g., OntoNotes). This would enable comparisons with neural baselines like Wiseman et al. (2015) and strengthen the paper’s impact.  
- **Nonlinearity Choice**: The use of a square function as nonlinearity is unusual. The authors should clarify if this is novel and discuss its potential utility beyond event linking.  
- **Dataset Availability**: ACE++ results are presented without public release. The authors should commit to releasing ACE++ to facilitate reproducibility and future comparisons. At minimum, they should report baseline results *on ACE++* for fair benchmarking.  
- **Statistical Significance**: Some performance differences (e.g., Table 5) are marginal. Including significance testing (e.g., t-tests) would strengthen claims of superiority.  

**Minor Issues**  
- Line 143: Remove redundant "that" ("the authors generate representations that...").  
- Line 670: Standardize terminology ("same event" vs. "same type" in Table 6).  
- Related Work: Cite Wiseman et al. (2015) as a relevant neural coreference baseline.  

**Conclusion**  
The paper presents a plausible neural approach for event linking, leveraging simple linguistic features effectively. However, critical gaps in experimental design (gold triggers, dataset splits), unclear technical details (position embeddings, neighbor handling), and insufficient reproducibility (ACE++ unavailability) limit its contribution. Addressing these points—particularly by reporting results with predicted triggers, clarifying position embeddings, releasing ACE++, and adding significance tests—would significantly strengthen the work. As-is, the results are promising but not fully convincing due to methodological ambiguities. The core idea is sound, but execution requires refinement for publication.
