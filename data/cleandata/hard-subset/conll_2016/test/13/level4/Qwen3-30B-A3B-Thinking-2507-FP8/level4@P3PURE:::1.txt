

This paper models event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling. They also concatenate these representations with word embeddings from surrounding words. Combined with specific pairwise features, this produces a similarity vector via a single-layer neural network, yielding a coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE dataset and an expanded version (ACE++), achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

The main contribution lies in developing a neural approach for event linking that integrates word embeddings with linguistic features. It is noteworthy that word embeddings alone are insufficient for strong performance; fortunately, the required linguistic features are limited and do not necessitate manually-crafted external resources.

**Experimental Setting**
- Gold trigger words are used instead of predicted ones. While the authors provide justification, performance with predicted triggers would be valuable for a fairer comparison, especially since one competitor system uses predicted triggers.
- The use of inconsistent train/test splits across papers is concerning. The authors should adhere to established splits where possible.

**Unclear Points**
- The evidence for cross-sentential information being necessary is convincing. However, the concluding statement in lines 65–70 remains unclear.
- Position embeddings are described as "generated in a way similar to word embeddings." It is unclear whether they are randomly initialized, lexicalized, or how relative position embeddings (e.g., "one word left") remain consistent across different trigger words.
- The mechanism for incorporating left vs. right neighbors (lines 307–311) is ambiguous: does this affect only the max-pooling operation?
- The inclusion of one word before and after the trigger (appended to embeddings) appears arbitrary; justification for this specific choice is lacking.
- The event-mention representation \(v_e\) (line 330) is not clearly utilized; subsequent sections reference only \(v_{\text{sent+lex}}\), not \(v_e\).
- Pairwise features (Section 3.2) are not fully explained: binary features likely form a binary vector, but the handling of continuous features (e.g., distance) and whether they are fixed during training requires clarification.

**Other Issues and Suggestions**
- The approach could potentially extend to entity coreference resolution, enabling comparison with established datasets (e.g., OntoNotes) and prior work.
- The use of a square function as a nonlinearity is intriguing. Is this novel? Does it hold broader applicability?
- ACE++ results are presented without public availability. Releasing ACE++ would facilitate reproducibility and method comparison; at minimum, results for feature-rich baselines on ACE++ should be included.
- Reported results show close numerical differences; significance testing would strengthen the claims.
- Related work should cite Wiseman et al. (2015) for neural coreference resolution (ACL 2015).

**Minor Issues**
- Line 143: "that" is redundant.
- Table 6 refers to a baseline as "same type," while the text (line 670) uses "same event."
