

This paper proposes a novel approach to event entity linking by leveraging sentential features from CNNs instead of external knowledge resources, presenting a two-part model that first learns event mention representations and then computes coreference scores between mentions. The writing is clear and well-structured, and the analysis of argument sharing in ACE-2005 is insightful. The motivation for creating the ACE++ dataset through empirical analysis of dataset size effects is particularly strong. However, significant methodological and presentation gaps must be addressed before publication.

The primary concern is the lack of justification for using CNNs to generate mention representations. The authors do not explain why CNNs were chosen over established alternatives like RNNs or LSTMs, nor do they provide comparative results to demonstrate superiority. Given that the proposed model achieves only marginal improvements (2–3 F1 points) over state-of-the-art baselines, the paper must rigorously justify this architectural choice. Without evidence that CNNs fundamentally outperform simpler or more common sequence models, the contribution appears incremental rather than innovative. The authors should conduct ablation studies comparing CNNs against RNNs/LSTMs on the same task and dataset to validate their design decision.

Second, the novelty of using sentential features for event linking is inadequately established. While CNN-based feature extraction is common in classification tasks, the paper fails to explicitly contrast its approach with existing continuous-space methods for event coreference (e.g., those using pre-trained embeddings or graph neural networks). Table 3’s comparison is insufficient; it omits key recent baselines like those in [Citation1, Citation2] that use similar feature engineering. The authors must clarify how their method differs from prior work and why sentential features specifically address limitations of previous approaches. A clear statement of novelty in the introduction and related work is essential.

Third, the ACE++ dataset’s construction and scope are poorly documented, undermining the paper’s core argument. The ACE-2005 dataset is criticized for its limited event type coverage, yet the paper does not specify which event types ACE++ includes or how it addresses this bias. To support the claim in Section 5.1 that "our approach does not depend on resources with restricted [semantic features]," the authors must demonstrate that their method succeeds where domain-specific approaches fail *on the same dataset*. For instance, they should show that prior methods (e.g., those relying on FrameNet) perform poorly on ACE++’s expanded event types while their model excels. Furthermore, the authors must commit to publicly releasing ACE++ to enable reproducibility and future comparisons—this is non-negotiable for community impact.

Minor but important points:  
1. Table 3 should include results without gold mentions to assess real-world applicability.  
2. The paper should explore integrating standard coreference systems (e.g., CoreNLP) as a baseline, especially since Line 687 cites an example where CoreNLP resolves "It" to "bombing" without event-specific processing.  
3. Testing on additional resources like EventCorefBank (beyond ACE-2005) is critical to validate generalizability, particularly given ACE-2005’s small size. The paper’s conclusion about "testing event and entity coreference simultaneously" is compelling but untested.  
4. Table 5’s success with pairwise features suggests feature engineering remains vital for ACE-scale datasets. The authors should clarify how their feature set was selected (e.g., ablation studies on individual features) and report which features contributed most to performance.

In summary, the paper’s core idea has merit, but the current submission lacks sufficient justification for its key technical choices, fails to establish novelty against relevant prior work, and omits critical details about its dataset. The marginal performance gains necessitate stronger evidence of architectural superiority. Addressing these points—particularly the CNN justification, novelty clarification, ACE++ transparency, and broader dataset validation—would substantially strengthen the contribution. Without these revisions, the paper risks being perceived as a minor incremental improvement rather than a meaningful advance for the field.
