

This paper introduces a novel approach to event entity linking by leveraging CNN-based sentential features instead of external knowledge sources, proposing a two-part model: one for event mention representation and another for computing coreference scores between mentions. The work is well-structured, clearly presented, and the analysis of argument sharing in the ACE corpus is particularly compelling. The motivation for creating the ACE++ dataset through dataset size analysis is also well-justified. However, several critical issues require addressing before publication.

First, the paper lacks sufficient justification for using CNNs to generate mention representations. The choice of CNN over alternatives (e.g., RNNs) is not discussed, nor are comparative experiments provided. Given the marginal gains (2–3 F1 points) over state-of-the-art baselines, stronger evidence is required to demonstrate that this architecture is genuinely superior. Without such analysis, the methodological choice appears arbitrary.

Second, the novelty of using sentential features for event linking is unclear. CNN-based feature extraction for classification tasks is not new, and the paper fails to explicitly contrast its approach with existing continuous-space methods for event linking. The literature comparison in Table 3 is inadequate; a more thorough benchmark against relevant prior work is essential to establish the contribution.

Third, the construction methodology for the ACE++ dataset is unspecified. The ACE corpus suffers from limited event type coverage, introducing bias—a key limitation the authors aim to address. To substantiate Section 5.1’s claim that "other approaches are strongly tied to domains where semantic features are available," the authors must demonstrate that prior methods fail on ACE++’s expanded event types while their model succeeds. Additionally, making ACE++ publicly available would enable reproducibility and future comparisons.

Minor concerns include:
- Table 3 should include performance without gold references to assess practical utility.
- The model’s compatibility with vanilla coreference systems (e.g., CoreNLP resolving "It" → "bombing" as in line 687) warrants exploration, as this could inform stronger baselines.
- Given ACE’s small size, testing on additional resources (e.g., EventCorefBank) is necessary to validate the model’s robustness and motivate joint entity-event coreference resolution.
- Table 5’s success with pairwise features suggests feature engineering remains critical. The authors should clarify which features were most effective, how the current feature set was selected, and what alternatives were discarded.

In summary, while the paper presents a reasonable approach with strong presentation and insightful analysis, the lack of methodological justification, unclear novelty, and insufficient dataset transparency undermine its contribution. Addressing these points—particularly providing CNN comparisons, clarifying novelty against existing work, detailing ACE++ construction, and expanding experimental validation—is crucial for the work to make a meaningful impact. The marginal performance gains also necessitate stronger evidence of architectural superiority.
