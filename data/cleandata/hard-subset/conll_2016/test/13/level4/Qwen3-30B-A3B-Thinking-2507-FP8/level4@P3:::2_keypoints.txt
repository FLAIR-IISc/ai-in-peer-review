- Proposes a resource-light event coreference model: CNN-based sentential feature extractor plus lexical/pairwise scoring, claiming SOTA on ACE2005 and ACE++.  
- Shows sentential features are crucial via ablations and outperform purely lexical/pairwise methods.  
- Provides error analysis highlighting pronoun-based coreference as a major remaining challenge and suggesting future directions.  
- Strength: convincingly eliminates dependency on external semantic resources (WordNet/FrameNet) while improving performance.  
- Strength: thorough empirical validation—ablation and clustering comparisons clarify feature and strategy importance.  
- Strength: manual error analysis (200 errors) identifies systematic weaknesses and annotation ambiguities.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification for choosing CNNs over alternatives (RNNs/LSTMs); architecture choice seems arbitrary given small gains.  
- Weakness: novelty of "sentential features" is underdeveloped and not well contrasted with prior event-linking work.  
- Weakness: ACE++ dataset description is inadequate—creation process, coverage, and annotation details are missing.  
- Weakness: evaluation only uses gold mentions; no results with automatically extracted mentions, limiting practical relevance.  
- Question: How was ACE++ constructed, what event types does it cover beyond ACE2005, and how were the additional web documents annotated?  
- Question: Why choose CNNs over RNNs/LSTMs for feature extraction, and can you provide architecture comparisons?  
- Question: How were pairwise features in Table 5 selected and were other feature combinations tested?  
- Question: Could integrating a standard coreference resolver (e.g., CoreNLP) address pronoun errors and how would that affect performance?  
- Additional: Clarify whether ACE++ is publicly available and provide a repository link for reproducibility.  
- Additional: Strengthen the claim about external resources restricting domain applicability with a concrete failure/success example.  
- Additional: Acknowledge that a high "all singletons" baseline suggests dataset/task bias toward singletons as a limitation.
