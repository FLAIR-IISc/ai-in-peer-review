

This paper proposes a neural approach for event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling, then concatenate these with contextual word embeddings surrounding the mention. Pairwise features and these combined representations are fed into a single-layer neural network to compute similarity scores and coreference probabilities. The model achieves performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems on ACE and an expanded ACE++ dataset.

The primary contribution lies in demonstrating a neural framework for event linking that effectively integrates word embeddings with limited linguistic features, without requiring manually crafted external resources. Notably, the authors show that word embeddings alone are insufficient for strong performance, highlighting the necessity of these additional features.

**Experimental Setting**  
The use of gold trigger words (rather than predicted ones) is justified, but the comparison becomes less meaningful since one key baseline uses predicted triggers. Reporting results with predicted triggers would strengthen the evaluation. Additionally, inconsistent train/test splits across studies undermine comparability; the authors should adopt standard splits where possible.

**Unclear Points**  
- The evidence for cross-sentential information’s necessity is convincing, but the final statement in lines 65–70 is unclear.  
- Embeddings for relative positions (e.g., "1 word before trigger") are described as "generated similarly to word embeddings." Are they randomly initialized, lexicalized, or learned? Crucially, it is unclear why a position relative to one word should share embeddings with the same relative position relative to another word.  
- The handling of left vs. right neighbors (lines 307–311) is ambiguous: does this only affect max-pooling, or is there additional processing?  
- Appending *exactly one* word before and after triggers seems arbitrary. Why not two words or a different window?  
- The event-mention representation \(v_e\) (line 330) is mentioned but not used in subsequent sections; only \(v_{\text{sent+lex}}\) appears. Its role requires clarification.  
- Pairwise features (Section 3.2) include binary features and distance. How is distance encoded (e.g., as a continuous value or binned)? Are all features fixed during training?

**Other Issues and Suggestions**  
- Could this approach extend to entity coreference resolution? Applying it to OntoNotes would enable comparison with established baselines like Wiseman et al. (2015).  
- The use of a square function as a nonlinearity is noted but not contextualized. Is this novel? Does it generalize to other tasks?  
- ACE++ results are reported, but the dataset is not publicly available. Releasing ACE++ would facilitate reproducibility and fairer comparisons. At minimum, results on ACE++ should be compared against feature-rich baselines.  
- Some reported performance differences are small; significance testing would bolster claims about improvements.  
- The related work should cite Wiseman et al.’s neural coreference approach (ACL 2015) alongside entity coreference literature.

**Minor Issues**  
- Line 143: "that" is redundant.  
- Table 6 refers to a "same type" baseline, while line 670 uses "same event." These must be consistent.  

**References**
