This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) that achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external feature sources. The authors design a two-part model that first generates vector representations for individual event mentions using word embeddings, CNNs, and max-pooling, and then combines these representations with pairwise features to determine coreference scores.

The main contributions of this paper are the development of a neural approach to event linking that effectively combines word embeddings with limited linguistic features, and the demonstration that this approach can achieve comparable performance to previous feature-rich systems.

Summary of the paper:
The paper addresses the task of event linking, which involves resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document. The authors propose a CNN-based model that generates vector representations for individual event mentions and then combines these representations with pairwise features to determine coreference scores. The model is trained on the ACE dataset and its expanded version, ACE++, and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

Main contributions:
1. Development of a neural approach to event linking that combines word embeddings with limited linguistic features.
2. Demonstration that this approach can achieve comparable performance to previous feature-rich systems.

Strengths:
1. The paper proposes a novel approach to event linking that effectively combines word embeddings with limited linguistic features.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including an error analysis that highlights the challenges of the task.
3. The paper demonstrates that the proposed approach can achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on external feature sources.

Weaknesses:
1. The use of gold trigger words rather than predicted ones may not be representative of real-world scenarios.
2. The comparison with previous systems may not be entirely fair due to differences in train/test splits and preprocessing components.
3. Some aspects of the model, such as the use of relative position embeddings and the concatenation of word embeddings, may not be entirely clear.

Questions to authors:
1. Can you provide more details on how the relative position embeddings are generated and why they are necessary?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of using gold trigger words rather than predicted ones in future work?
3. Can you provide more information on the ACE++ dataset and whether it will be made publicly available?
4. Have you considered applying this approach to entity coreference resolution, and if so, what are the potential challenges and benefits?
5. Can you provide more details on the use of the square function as nonlinearity and whether it has applicability in other tasks?
