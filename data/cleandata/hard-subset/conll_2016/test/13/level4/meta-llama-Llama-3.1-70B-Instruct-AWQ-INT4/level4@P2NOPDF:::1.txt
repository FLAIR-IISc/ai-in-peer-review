This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to model event linking, given event mentions. The authors generate vector representations by passing word embeddings through a CNN, followed by max-pooling, and then concatenate the resulting representations with several word embeddings surrounding the mention. Alongside certain pairwise features, they produce a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network and compute a coreference score.

The model is evaluated on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems. The primary contribution of this paper lies in developing a neural approach for entity linking that combines word embeddings with several linguistic features. Notably, the authors demonstrate that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for optimal performance. Fortunately, the linguistic features employed are limited and do not require manually-crafted external resources.

Experimental Setting:

- The use of gold trigger words instead of predicted ones raises concerns about the fairness of comparison with competitor systems that utilize predicted triggers. Although the authors provide a justification for this choice, it would be beneficial to include results with predicted triggers for a more comprehensive evaluation.
- The inconsistent train/test splits across different papers are problematic. To facilitate comparison and ensure reproducibility, it is recommended that the authors adhere to established splits as closely as possible.

Unclear Points:

- While the numbers indicating the need for cross-sentential information are convincing, the last statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) is unclear and requires further explanation.
- The method of generating position embeddings, described as being "in a way similar to word embeddings," lacks clarity. Specifically, it is unclear whether these embeddings are randomly initialized, lexicalized, or why a relative position next to one word should have the same embedding as a relative position next to a different word.
- The process of utilizing left and right neighbors to create the representation (lines 307-311) is not well-explained. It is also unclear whether this only affects the max-pooling operation.
- The decision to append the word embeddings of one word before and one word after the trigger words appears arbitrary. A more detailed explanation for this choice, such as why one word before and after was chosen, would be beneficial.
- The event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is not clearly utilized in the subsequent sections, where only v{sent+lex} appears to be used.
- The pairwise features used in section 3.2 are not well-explained. Specifically, it is unclear how the distance feature is encoded and whether these features are kept fixed during training.

Other Issues and Suggestions:

- It would be beneficial to explore the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution, allowing for comparison with more previous work and popular datasets like OntoNotes.
- The use of a square function as nonlinearity is an interesting choice. It would be helpful to discuss its novelty and potential applicability to other tasks.
- While one dataset is publicly available, results are also presented with ACE++, which is not. Consider releasing ACE++ to facilitate comparison with new methods. Alternatively, it would be beneficial to include a comparison with feature-rich systems on this dataset.
- To substantiate the comparisons, significance testing would be a valuable addition to the results.
- In the related work section, it would be relevant to mention the neural network approach by Wiseman et al. (2015) for (entity) coreference resolution.

Minor Issues:

- Line 143 contains a redundant "that."
- There is an inconsistency in referring to one of the baselines as "same type" in table 6 and "same event" in the text (line 670).

References:

- Wiseman, S., Rush, A. M., Weston, J., & Shieber, S. M. (2015). Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. ACL 2015.
