This paper presents a model for the task of event entity linking, where the authors propose to use sentential features from Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in place of external knowledge sources which earlier methods have used. They train a two-part model: the first part learns an event mention representation, and the second part learns to calculate a coreference score given two event entity mentions.

**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

The authors propose a novel approach to event entity linking by utilizing sentential features from CNNs. The main contributions of this work are:

1. The use of CNNs to generate sentential features for event entity linking, which eliminates the need for external knowledge sources.
2. A two-part model architecture that first learns event mention representations and then calculates coreference scores.
3. State-of-the-art performance on the ACE dataset without relying on external feature sources.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper is well-written, well-presented, and easy to follow.
2. The analysis on the ACE corpus regarding argument sharing between event coreferences is insightful.
3. The creation of the ACE++ dataset is a significant contribution, as it provides a larger dataset for event entity linking.
4. The model's performance on the ACE dataset is competitive with state-of-the-art systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The authors fail to motivate and analyze the pros and cons of using CNNs for generating mention representations. A comparison with other models, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), would be beneficial.
2. The novelty of the idea of tackling event linking with sentential features is not clearly established. The authors should explicitly compare their approach to existing continuous space methods for event linking.
3. The choice of methods in Table 3 is not thorough enough, and the authors should consider including more state-of-the-art systems for comparison.
4. There is no information regarding how the ACE++ dataset was collected, and its event types are not specified. This lack of information makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the model.

**Questions to authors:**

1. Can you provide more information on how the ACE++ dataset was collected, and what event types it covers?
2. How do you plan to make the ACE++ dataset publicly available for future research?
3. Can you provide a comparison of your model's performance with other state-of-the-art systems on the ACE++ dataset?
4. How do you think your model can be augmented with a vanilla coreference resolution system, and what potential benefits or drawbacks do you see in this approach?

**Additional comments:**

1. It would be interesting to see the performance of the model without gold references in Table 3.
2. The authors may consider exploring how their model can be used in conjunction with other coreference resolution systems.
3. Testing the model on other available resources, such as EventCorefBank, would provide further insights into its generalizability.
4. The authors may want to report on the feature engineering process and the most effective features used in their model.
