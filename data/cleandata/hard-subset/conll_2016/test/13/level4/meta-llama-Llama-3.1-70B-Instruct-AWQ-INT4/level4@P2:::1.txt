**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a model for event linking that utilizes sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The model generates vector representations for event mentions based on word embeddings passed through a CNN and followed by max-pooling. These representations are then concatenated with word embeddings around the mention and pairwise features to produce a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network, which computes a coreference score. The model is tested on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

**Main Contributions**

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a neural approach for event linking that combines word embeddings with several linguistic features. The authors demonstrate that using word embeddings alone is not sufficient for good performance and that the addition of linguistic features, which do not require manually crafted external resources, significantly improves the model's performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Approach**: The paper proposes a novel approach to event linking that leverages the strengths of CNNs in capturing sentential features.
2. **Improved Performance**: The model achieves performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Limited Requirements**: The linguistic features used in the model are limited and do not require manually crafted external resources, making it a more practical solution.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Gold Trigger Words**: The model uses gold trigger words, which may not be available in real-world scenarios. It would be beneficial to evaluate the model's performance with predicted triggers.
2. **Different Train/Test Splits**: The paper uses a different train/test split than previous studies, which may affect the comparison of results.
3. **Unclear Points**: Some aspects of the model, such as the generation of embeddings for positions and the use of left vs. right neighbors, are not clearly explained.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the generation of embeddings for positions and how they are used in the model?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of using gold trigger words in real-world scenarios?
3. Can you provide more information on the comparison of results with previous studies, particularly with regards to the different train/test splits used?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of results with previous studies, including a discussion of the implications of using different train/test splits.
2. The authors may consider evaluating the model's performance on other datasets, such as OntoNotes, to further demonstrate its effectiveness.
3. The use of a square function as nonlinearity is an interesting choice, and the authors may consider discussing its novelty and potential applicability in other tasks.
