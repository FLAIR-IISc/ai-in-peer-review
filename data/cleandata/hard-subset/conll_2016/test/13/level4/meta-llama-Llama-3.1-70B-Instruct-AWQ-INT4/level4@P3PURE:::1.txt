This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling. These representations are then concatenated with several word embeddings surrounding the mention. In conjunction with pairwise features, they produce a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network and compute a coreference score.

The model is evaluated on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems. In my opinion, the primary contribution of this paper lies in developing a neural approach for entity linking that combines word embeddings with several linguistic features. Notably, the authors demonstrate that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for good performance. Fortunately, the linguistic features employed are limited and do not require manually crafted external resources.

Experimental Setting:
- The authors' decision to use gold trigger words instead of predicted ones warrants discussion. Although they provide a reasonable argument for this choice, I would have liked to see performance with predicted triggers, especially since one of the competitor systems used predicted triggers, making the comparison unfair.
- The fact that different papers employ different train/test splits is concerning. I encourage the authors to adhere to previous splits as closely as possible to ensure consistency.

Unclear Points:
- While the numbers indicating the need for cross-sentential information are convincing, the last statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) is unclear.
- The embeddings for positions are said to be generated "in a way similar to word embeddings." However, the exact method is unclear. Are they randomly initialized or lexicalized? It is also unclear why a relative position next to one word should have the same embedding as a relative position next to a different word.
- The use of left versus right neighbors to create the representation (lines 307-311) is unclear. Does this only affect the max-pooling operation?
- The decision to append the word embeddings of one word before and one word after the trigger words appears arbitrary. Why choose one word before and after, rather than a different number?
- The event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is unclear. In the following sections, only v{sent+lex} appears to be used, not v_e.
- The use of pairwise features in section 3.2 is unclear. Most features are binary, so I assume they are encoded as a binary vector, but what about the distance feature, for example? Are these features kept fixed during training?

Other Issues and Suggestions:
- It would be interesting to explore whether the approach can be applied to entity coreference resolution, allowing for comparison with more previous work and popular datasets like OntoNotes.
- The use of a square function as nonlinearity is intriguing. Is this a novel approach, and do you think it has applicability in other tasks?
- Regarding datasets, while one dataset is publicly available, results are also presented with ACE++, which is not. Do you have plans to release it? This would facilitate comparison with new methods. At the very least, it would be beneficial to see a comparison with feature-rich systems on this dataset.
- The results show some close numbers. Significance testing would help substantiate the comparisons.
- In the related work section, it would be worth mentioning the neural network approach by Wiseman et al. (2015) for (entity) coreference resolution.

Minor Issues:
- Line 143 contains a redundant "that."
- One of the baselines is referred to as "same type" in table 6, but "same event" in the text (line 670).

References:
- Wiseman, S., Rush, A. M., Weston, J., & Shieber, S. M. (2015). Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. ACL 2015.
