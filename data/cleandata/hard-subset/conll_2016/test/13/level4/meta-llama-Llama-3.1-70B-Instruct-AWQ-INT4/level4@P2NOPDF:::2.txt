This paper proposes a novel approach to event entity linking, leveraging sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) instead of relying on external knowledge sources. The authors develop a two-part model, where the first component learns an event mention representation, and the second component calculates a coreference score between two event entity mentions.

The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and easy to follow. The analysis of argument sharing between event coreferences in the ACE corpus is insightful, and the discussion on the impact of dataset size is a compelling motivation for creating the ACE++ dataset. However, there are several major concerns that need to be addressed:

Firstly, the authors fail to provide a thorough motivation and analysis of the pros and cons of using CNNs for generating mention representations. It is unclear why CNNs were chosen over other models, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and no comparisons are made to alternative architectures. Given the modest improvement of 2-3 points in F1 score over state-of-the-art models, more evidence is needed to demonstrate the superiority of this architecture.

Secondly, it is unclear what is novel about the idea of using sentential features for event linking, as this approach is not new in the context of classification tasks. The authors should explicitly highlight the contributions of their work and compare it to existing continuous space methods for event linking. The selection of methods in Table 3 is not comprehensive enough.

Thirdly, there is a lack of information about the collection process of the ACE++ dataset. The original ACE dataset has limited event types, making it constrained and biased. It is essential to know what event types are covered in ACE++ to support the claim that the proposed approach is more domain-independent. Making the ACE++ dataset publicly available would also facilitate meaningful comparisons in future work.

Some minor issues also need to be addressed:

* It would be beneficial to include the performance of the model without gold references in Table 3 for completeness.
* Exploring the potential of augmenting the model with a vanilla coreference resolution system could be valuable. For instance, the CoreNLP system correctly links 'It' to 'bombing' in the example provided, which could be leveraged in an event entity linking baseline.
* Given the small size of the ACE dataset, testing the model on other available resources, such as EventCorefBank, is crucial to demonstrate its robustness and generalizability.
* The effectiveness of pairwise features, as shown in Table 5, suggests that feature engineering is still essential for developing a competitive model. It would be helpful to report on the most effective features, the process of selecting the current set, and any alternative features that were tried.

Addressing these concerns would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.
