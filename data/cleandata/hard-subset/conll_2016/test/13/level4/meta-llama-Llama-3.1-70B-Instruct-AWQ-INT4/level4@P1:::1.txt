This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors generate vector representations of event mentions by passing word embeddings through a CNN and max-pooling, and then concatenate these representations with word embeddings around the mention. They also incorporate pairwise features to produce a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network, and compute a coreference score.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a neural approach to event linking that combines word embeddings with linguistic features, without relying on manually-crafted external resources. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems.

The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its components. The experimental setting is also well-described, with a thorough discussion of the results and their implications.

However, there are a few points that require clarification. The authors use gold trigger words in their experiments, which may not be representative of real-world scenarios where trigger words are predicted. It would be interesting to see the performance of the model with predicted triggers. Additionally, the use of different train/test splits across papers is concerning, and the authors should consider sticking to previous splits to ensure fair comparisons.

The authors also mention that cross-sentential information is necessary for event linking, but the last statement in the second paragraph is unclear. Furthermore, the generation of embeddings for positions is not well-explained, and it is unclear why a relative position next to one word should have the same embedding as a relative position next to a different word.

The use of left and right neighbors to create the representation is also not clearly explained, and it is unclear how the event-mention representation is used in the subsequent sections. The pairwise features are also not well-explained, and it is unclear how they are encoded and used during training.

Despite these minor issues, the paper presents a novel and effective approach to event linking. The use of a square function as nonlinearity is interesting, and it would be useful to explore its applicability in other tasks. The authors should also consider releasing the ACE++ dataset to facilitate comparisons with other methods.

In terms of related work, the authors could have mentioned the neural network approach to coreference resolution by Wiseman et al. (2015). Additionally, the paper could benefit from significance testing to substantiate the comparisons between different models.

Minor issues include a redundant "that" in line 143, and inconsistent naming of the "same type" baseline in table 6 and the text.

Overall, the paper presents a strong contribution to the field of event linking, and with some minor revisions, it has the potential to be a valuable resource for researchers in the field.
