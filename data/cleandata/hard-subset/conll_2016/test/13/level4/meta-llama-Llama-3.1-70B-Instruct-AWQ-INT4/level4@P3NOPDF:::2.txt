This paper proposes a novel approach to event entity linking by leveraging sentential features from Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in place of traditional external knowledge sources. The authors develop a two-part model, where the first component learns an event mention representation, and the second component calculates a coreference score given two event entity mentions. 

The paper is well-written, well-presented, and easy to follow. The analysis on the ACE corpus regarding argument sharing between event coreferences is insightful, and the discussion on the impact of dataset size is a compelling motivation for creating the ACE++ dataset. However, several major issues need to be addressed to strengthen the paper.

Firstly, the authors fail to provide a clear motivation for using CNNs to generate mention representations. A thorough analysis of the pros and cons of this choice, including comparisons to other models such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), is lacking. Given that the proposed model only achieves a 2-3 point improvement in F1 score over the state-of-the-art, more evidence is needed to demonstrate the superiority of this architecture.

Secondly, it is unclear what is novel about the idea of tackling event linking with sentential features, as using CNNs in this fashion for classification tasks is not new. The authors should explicitly highlight the contributions of their approach and compare it to existing continuous space methods for event linking. The selection of methods in Table 3 is not comprehensive, and a more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper.

Thirdly, the paper lacks information on how the ACE++ dataset was collected. The ACE dataset has limited event types, making it constrained and biased. It is essential to know what event types ACE++ covers to support the claim in Section 5.1 that the proposed approach is more robust and domain-independent. To facilitate meaningful comparisons in future work, the authors should consider making the ACE++ dataset publicly available.

Several minor issues also need to be addressed. It would be beneficial to include the performance of the model without gold references in Table 3 for a more comprehensive comparison. Exploring how the model can be augmented with a vanilla coreference resolution system, such as the CoreNLP system, could also provide valuable insights. Testing the model on other available resources, such as EventCorefBank, would further demonstrate its robustness and motivate the development of a more comprehensive model that tackles entity and event coreference simultaneously. Finally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the pairwise features in Table 5, including which features were most effective and how they were selected.

Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and provide more convincing evidence for the proposed approach to event entity linking.
