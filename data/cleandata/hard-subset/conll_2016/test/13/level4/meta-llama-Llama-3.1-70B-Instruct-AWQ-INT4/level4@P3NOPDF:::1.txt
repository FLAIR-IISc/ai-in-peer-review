This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). The authors generate vector representations of event mentions by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling. These representations are then concatenated with word embeddings of surrounding words and pairwise features to produce a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network, ultimately computing a coreference score.

The model is evaluated on the ACE dataset and its expanded version, ACE++, demonstrating performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems. The primary contribution of this paper lies in developing a neural approach to entity linking that effectively combines word embeddings with linguistic features. Notably, the authors find that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for optimal performance. Fortunately, the linguistic features employed are limited and do not require manually crafted external resources.

**Experimental Setting**

The authors' decision to use gold trigger words instead of predicted ones is noteworthy. Although they provide a reasonable justification, it would be beneficial to include performance results with predicted triggers, especially since one of the competitor systems utilized predicted triggers, making the comparison somewhat unfair.

The varying train/test splits used across different papers are concerning. To facilitate more accurate comparisons, it would be desirable for the authors to adhere to established splits as closely as possible.

**Unclear Points**

The numbers provided in the paper convincingly demonstrate the need for cross-sentential information. However, the final statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) is unclear and warrants further explanation.

The method for generating position embeddings, described as being "in a way similar to word embeddings," requires more detail. Specifically, it is unclear whether these embeddings are randomly initialized, lexicalized, or if the relative position next to different words shares the same embedding.

The process of utilizing left and right neighbors to create the representation (lines 307-311) is not well-explained. It is also unclear whether this only affects the max-pooling operation.

The decision to append word embeddings of one word before and after the trigger words appears somewhat arbitrary. A clearer justification for this choice would be beneficial.

The event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is not clearly used in the subsequent sections, where only v{sent+lex} appears to be utilized.

The incorporation of pairwise features in section 3.2 is not explicitly described. Assuming binary features are encoded as a binary vector, it is unclear how non-binary features, such as distance, are handled. Additionally, are these features kept fixed during training?

**Other Issues and Suggestions**

It would be interesting to explore the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution, enabling comparisons with more previous work and popular datasets like OntoNotes.

The use of a square function as nonlinearity is an intriguing choice. It is unclear whether this is a novel application or if it has potential uses in other tasks.

The authors should consider releasing the ACE++ dataset, as it is currently not publicly available. This would facilitate more accurate comparisons with new methods. At the very least, it would be beneficial to include a comparison with feature-rich systems on this dataset.

To substantiate the comparisons, significance testing would be a valuable addition to the results. Some of the reported numbers are quite close, and this would provide more confidence in the findings.

In the related work section, the neural network approach by Wiseman et al. (2015) is a notable omission.

**Minor Issues**

Line 143 contains a redundant "that."

One of the baselines is referred to as "same type" in table 6, but "same event" in the text (line 670).
