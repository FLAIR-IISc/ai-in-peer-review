This paper proposes a CNN-based approach for event linking, where vector representations of event mentions are generated by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling, and then concatenating the resulting representations with word embeddings around the mention. Alongside certain pairwise features, these representations are used to produce a vector of similarities via a single-layer neural network, ultimately computing a coreference score.

The model is evaluated on both the ACE dataset and its expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems. In my opinion, the primary contribution of this paper lies in developing a neural approach for entity linking that effectively combines word embeddings with several linguistic features. Notably, the authors demonstrate that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for optimal performance, and the incorporation of linguistic features, although limited, does not necessitate manually-crafted external resources.

Experimental setting:
- The use of gold trigger words instead of predicted ones is a concern, as it may not accurately reflect real-world scenarios. Although the authors provide a reasonable argument for this choice, I would have liked to see performance results with predicted triggers, especially since one of the competitor systems utilized predicted triggers, rendering the comparison unfair.
- The varying train/test splits employed in different papers are worrisome, and I would encourage the authors to adhere to previous splits as closely as possible to facilitate more accurate comparisons.

Unclear points:
- While the numbers indicating the necessity of cross-sentential information are convincing, the last statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) is unclear.
- The generation of position embeddings, stated to be similar to word embeddings, lacks clarity. Are these embeddings randomly initialized or lexicalized? Furthermore, it is unclear why relative positions adjacent to different words should share the same embedding.
- The utilization of left and right neighbors in creating the representation (lines 307-311) is unclear, and it is uncertain whether this only affects the max-pooling operation.
- The choice of appending word embeddings of one word before and one word after the trigger words appears arbitrary. Why this specific choice was made, rather than an alternative, is not explained.
- The event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is unclearly used, as only v{sent+lex} appears to be utilized in subsequent sections.
- The employment of pairwise features in section 3.2 is unclear, particularly regarding the encoding of non-binary features, such as distance, and whether these features remain fixed during training.

Other issues and suggestions:
- It would be beneficial to explore the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution, allowing for comparisons with more previous work and popular datasets like OntoNotes.
- The use of a square function as nonlinearity is intriguing. Is this a novel approach, and do the authors believe it has potential applications in other tasks?
- Regarding datasets, while one is publicly available, results are also presented with ACE++, which is not. The authors should consider releasing ACE++ to facilitate comparisons with new methods. At the very least, it would be beneficial to include a comparison with feature-rich systems on this dataset.
- To substantiate the comparisons, significance testing would be helpful for the reported results, as some numbers are quite close.
- In the related work section, the neural network approach by Wiseman et al. (2015) could be mentioned as a relevant work on (entity) coreference resolution.

Minor issues:
- The word "that" in line 143 is redundant.
- There is an inconsistency in referring to one of the baselines as "same type" in table 6 and "same event" in the text (line 670).

Refs:
- Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. Sam Wiseman, Alexander M. Rush, Jason Weston, and Stuart M. Shieber. ACL 2015.
