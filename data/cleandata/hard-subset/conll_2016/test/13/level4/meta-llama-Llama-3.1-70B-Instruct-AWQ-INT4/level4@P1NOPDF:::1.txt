This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). Given event mentions, the authors generate vector representations by passing word embeddings through a CNN and applying max-pooling. They also concatenate the resulting representations with several word embeddings surrounding the mention. In conjunction with certain pairwise features, they produce a vector of similarities using a single-layer neural network and compute a coreference score.

The model is evaluated on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems. In my opinion, the main contribution of this paper lies in developing a neural approach for entity linking that combines word embeddings with several linguistic features. It is noteworthy that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for optimal performance. Fortunately, the linguistic features employed are limited and do not require manually crafted external resources.

Experimental Setting:
- The use of gold trigger words instead of predicted ones is a notable aspect of the experimental setup. Although the authors provide a reasonable justification for this choice, I would have liked to see the performance with predicted triggers. This is particularly important since one of the competitor systems used predicted triggers, rendering the comparison unfair.
- The fact that different papers employ different train/test splits is concerning. I would encourage the authors to adhere to previous splits as much as possible to facilitate more accurate comparisons.

Unclear Points:
- The numbers indicating the need for cross-sentential information are convincing. However, the last statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) was unclear to me.
- The method of generating embeddings for positions is described as being "similar to word embeddings." However, the exact process is unclear. Are these embeddings randomly initialized or lexicalized? It is also unclear why a relative position next to one word should have the same embedding as a relative position next to a different word.
- The use of left versus right neighbors to create the representation (lines 307-311) is not well-explained. Does this only affect the max-pooling operation?
- The decision to append the word embeddings of one word before and one word after the trigger words appears somewhat arbitrary. Why choose one word before and after, rather than a different number?
- The event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is not clearly explained. In the following sections, only v{sent+lex} appears to be used, with no mention of v_e.
- The use of pairwise features in section 3.2 is unclear. Most features are binary, so I assume they are encoded as a binary vector. However, the distance feature, for example, is not clearly explained. Are these features kept fixed during training?

Other Issues and Suggestions:
- It would be interesting to explore whether this approach can be applied to entity coreference resolution as well. This would enable comparisons with more previous work and popular datasets like OntoNotes.
- The use of a square function as nonlinearity is an interesting choice. Is this novel? Do you think it has applicability in other tasks?
- The use of two datasets, one publicly available and the other not (ACE++), raises some concerns. Do you have plans to release ACE++? This would facilitate comparisons with new methods. At the very least, it would have been beneficial to include a comparison with feature-rich systems on this dataset as well.
- The results show some close numbers. Significance testing would help substantiate the comparisons.
- In the related work section, it would be worth mentioning the neural network approach by Wiseman et al. (2015) for (entity) coreference resolution.

Minor Issues:
- Line 143: The word "that" is redundant and can be removed.
- One of the baselines is referred to as "same type" in table 6, but as "same event" in the text (line 670).

References:
- Wiseman, S., Rush, A. M., Weston, J., & Shieber, S. M. (2015). Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. ACL 2015.
