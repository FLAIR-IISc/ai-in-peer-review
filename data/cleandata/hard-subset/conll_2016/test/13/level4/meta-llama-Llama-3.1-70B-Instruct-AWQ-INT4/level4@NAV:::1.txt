This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for event linking, generating vector representations of event mentions by combining word embeddings with a CNN and max-pooling, followed by the concatenation of word embeddings surrounding the mention. The resulting representations are then combined with pairwise features using a single-layer neural network to produce a vector of similarities and compute a coreference score.

The authors evaluate their model on the ACE dataset and an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to previous feature-rich systems. The primary contribution of this work lies in the development of a neural approach for entity linking that integrates word embeddings with linguistic features. Notably, the results indicate that relying solely on word embeddings is insufficient for optimal performance. Fortunately, the linguistic features employed are limited and do not require manually crafted external resources.

Experimental Setting:
- The use of gold trigger words instead of predicted ones raises concerns, particularly since one of the competitor systems utilized predicted triggers, making the comparison unfair. It would be beneficial to include performance with predicted triggers.
- The inconsistent train/test splits across different papers are problematic. Adhering to previous splits as much as possible would be desirable.

Unclear Points:
- Although the numbers demonstrating the need for cross-sentential information are convincing, the last statement in the second paragraph (lines 65-70) is unclear.
- The method of generating position embeddings, described as being "in a way similar to word embeddings," requires further explanation. Are they randomly initialized or lexicalized? It is unclear why a relative position next to one word should have the same embedding as a relative position next to a different word.
- The process of using left versus right neighbors to create the representation (lines 307-311) is unclear. Does this only affect the max-pooling operation?
- The decision to append word embeddings of one word before and one word after the trigger words appears arbitrary. Why choose one word before and after, rather than another option?
- The usage of the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is unclear, as only v{sent+lex} appears to be used in subsequent sections.
- The application of pairwise features in section 3.2 is unclear. Are binary features encoded as a binary vector, and how is the distance feature handled? Are these features fixed during training?

Other Issues and Suggestions:
- Investigating the applicability of this approach to entity coreference resolution would enable comparison with more previous work and popular datasets like OntoNotes.
- The use of a square function as nonlinearity is intriguing. Is this novel, and do you think it has applicability in other tasks?
- Releasing the ACE++ dataset or comparing the approach to feature-rich systems on this dataset would facilitate comparisons for other researchers.
- Conducting significance testing would help substantiate the comparisons, as some of the reported numbers are quite close.
- The related work section could benefit from the inclusion of Wiseman et al.'s (2015) neural network approach to (entity) coreference resolution.

Minor Issues:
- The word "that" in line 143 is redundant.
- The baseline referred to as "same type" in table 6 is inconsistent with the "same event" label in the text (line 670).

References:
