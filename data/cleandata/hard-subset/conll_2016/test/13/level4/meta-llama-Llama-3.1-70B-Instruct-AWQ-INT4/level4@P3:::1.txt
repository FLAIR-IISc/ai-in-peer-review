**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking using sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors aim to improve the state-of-the-art performance on event linking tasks without relying on external feature sources. They design a two-part model that first generates latent-feature representations for individual event mentions using CNNs and then combines these representations with pairwise features to determine coreference scores. The model is evaluated on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, and achieves comparable performance to previous feature-rich systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural approach for event linking**: The paper develops a neural approach that combines word embeddings with linguistic features to model event linking. This approach is novel and interesting, as it does not rely on manually crafted external resources.
2. **Use of sentential features**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of using sentential features generated by CNNs for event linking tasks. These features are shown to capture relevant information for coreference decisions.
3. **Comparison to state-of-the-art systems**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed model against state-of-the-art systems on the ACE dataset, highlighting its competitive performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of neural networks**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of using CNNs to generate sentential features for event linking tasks.
2. **Competitive performance**: The proposed model achieves comparable performance to previous feature-rich systems on the ACE dataset.
3. **Novel approach**: The paper presents a novel approach to event linking that does not rely on external feature sources.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Use of gold trigger words**: The authors use gold trigger words instead of predicted ones, which may not be representative of real-world scenarios.
2. **Different train/test splits**: The paper uses different train/test splits compared to other papers, which may affect the fairness of the comparison.
3. **Unclear points**: Some aspects of the paper, such as the generation of position embeddings and the use of left vs. right neighbors, are not clearly explained.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the generation of position embeddings and how they are used in the model?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of using gold trigger words instead of predicted ones in future work?
3. Can you provide more insights on the use of sentential features for event linking tasks and their potential applications in other areas?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the neural network architecture and the use of sentential features.
2. The authors may consider providing more analysis on the errors made by the model to gain insights into its limitations.
3. The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to other neural network approaches for event linking tasks.
