- Proposes a CNN-based neural method for event linking that combines word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features; finds embeddings alone are insufficient.  
- Evaluated on ACE and ACE++, achieving performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems.  
- Experiments use gold triggers; results with predicted triggers are needed because competitors use predicted triggers, making comparisons uneven.  
- Train/test split variation across papers is concerning; authors should adhere to previously used splits for fair comparison.  
- Evidence for cross-sentential information is persuasive, but a final claim (lines 65–70) is unclear and needs clarification.  
- Unclear how position embeddings are generated (random init, learned, lexicalized) and why same relative position shares an embedding across different words.  
- Unclear how left vs right neighbors are incorporated—does the distinction only affect max-pooling or earlier layers as well?  
- Appending exactly one left and one right neighbor seems arbitrary; justify the chosen window size.  
- Unclear how event-mention representation v_e is ultimately used, since later sections appear to rely on v_sent+lex instead.  
- Need details on pairwise feature encoding and usage: binary encoding for most, but how is distance handled (binned/scaled/embedded) and are features learned or fixed?  
- Ask whether the approach can be extended to entity coreference to enable comparisons on datasets like OntoNotes.  
- The square activation function is intriguing—clarify novelty in this context and potential effectiveness on other tasks.  
- ACE++ is not public; request release of ACE++ or at minimum comparisons to feature-rich systems on that dataset.  
- Several reported numbers are close; recommend statistical significance testing to strengthen claims.  
- Suggest adding citation to Wiseman et al. (2015) among neural coreference approaches.  
- Minor: remove redundant “that” on line 143.  
- Minor: standardize baseline naming—Table 6 uses “same type” while text (line 670) uses “same event.”
