- Paper proposes a CNN-based event coreference model that combines pooled word-embedding representations with embeddings of adjacent tokens and pairwise features to produce a coreference score.
- Main contribution: a neural event-linking approach blending word embeddings with a modest set of lightweight linguistic features; embeddings alone are insufficient.
- Feature set avoids reliance on manually curated external resources.
- Experiments use gold triggers rather than predicted ones; results with predicted triggers are needed for fair comparison to some baselines.
- Inconsistent train/test splits across papers; authors should align splits with prior work where feasible.
- The claim motivating cross-sentential reasoning (lines 65–70) is not clearly explained.
- Position embeddings generation is unclear: initialization, training regime, relation to token identity, and why same relative index shares an embedding.
- It is unclear how left vs. right contexts are incorporated—whether distinction is only via piecewise max-pooling or elsewhere.
- Appending exactly one token left/right of the trigger seems ad hoc; no justification for the chosen window size or alternatives.
- The role and usage of the event-mention representation ve (line 330) are unclear; later sections appear to use vsent+lex instead.
- Encoding of non-binary pairwise features (e.g., distance) is unspecified, and it’s unclear which feature parameters are learned versus fixed.
- Suggest evaluating/adapting the approach to entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes) to broaden comparisons.
- The square activation as similarity nonlinearity is interesting but its novelty and applicability to other tasks are not discussed.
- ACE++ is not public; please state plans to release ACE++ and include comparisons of feature-rich systems on ACE++ for reproducibility.
- Several result differences are small; include statistical significance tests to support claims.
- Related work: cite and discuss the neural coreference model of Wiseman et al. (2015).
- Minor edits: remove superfluous “that” (line 143); standardize baseline naming (“same type” vs “same event” in Table 6/text).
