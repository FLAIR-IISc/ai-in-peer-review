This paper models event linking with convolutional neural networks. Given event mentions, the authors build vector representations by feeding word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling, then concatenate the resulting vectors with embeddings of neighboring words around the trigger. Combined with pairwise features, these representations are passed to a single-layer neural network to compute similarity scores and a coreference decision. The model is evaluated on ACE and an expanded ACE++ set, achieving performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems.

In my view, the main contribution is a neural approach to event linking that integrates word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features. The finding that embeddings alone are insufficient is noteworthy, and the chosen linguistic features are limited and do not rely on heavy external resources.

Experimental setup
- It appears the experiments use gold triggers rather than predicted ones. While the authors argue this is reasonable, I would still like to see results with predicted triggers, especially since at least one competitor uses predicted triggers, making the comparison uneven.
- Different train/test splits across papers are concerning. I encourage adhering to established splits where possible to facilitate fair comparison.

Points needing clarification
- The evidence that cross-sentential information is important is convincing. However, the final claim in the second paragraph (lines 65–70) was unclear to me; please elaborate.
- Position embeddings are said to be generated “similar to word embeddings.” How exactly? Are they randomly initialized and learned, or tied to lexical content? Why should the relative position adjacent to one word share the same embedding as the same relative position adjacent to a different word?
- How are left vs. right neighbors incorporated into the representation (lines 307–311)? Does the distinction only affect max-pooling, or does it also influence convolutional filters or concatenation?
- Appending the embeddings of exactly one word before and after the trigger seems arbitrary. Why this window size rather than a different one? Was this tuned on development data?
- The role of the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is unclear. Subsequent sections seem to use only v_{sent+lex}. Where is v_e used in the final scoring?
- In Section 3.2, how are pairwise features encoded and used? If most are binary, are they concatenated as a binary vector? How is the distance feature handled (e.g., bucketed, normalized real value)? Are these feature parameters fixed or learned during training?

Other comments and suggestions
- Can this approach be applied to entity coreference as well? That would enable comparisons to a larger body of work and datasets such as OntoNotes.
- The choice of the square function as a nonlinearity is intriguing. Is this novel in this context? Do you expect it to generalize to other tasks?
- Datasets: While one dataset is public, ACE++ is not. Do you plan to release it to facilitate reproducibility and comparison? At minimum, it would be useful to compare against feature-rich baselines on the public dataset as well.
- Results: Several differences are small. Statistical significance testing would strengthen the comparisons.
- Related work: Please consider citing neural coreference work such as Wiseman et al. (2015).

Minor issues
- Line 143: “that” appears redundant.
- In Table 6, one baseline is labeled “same type,” but in the text (line 670) it is described as “same event.” Please standardize the terminology.

Reference
- Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. Sam Wiseman, Alexander M. Rush, Jason Weston, and Stuart M. Shieber. ACL 2015.
