Summary and contributions
This paper addresses within-document event coreference (“event linking”) without relying on external semantic resources. The authors propose a two-stage neural model: (a) a CNN-based encoder that produces a representation for each event mention from its sentence and local trigger context; (b) a similarity module that combines two mention representations with pairwise features to output a coreference score. The model is trained with gold triggers and evaluated on ACE 2005 and a larger ACE++ collection. It reports state-of-the-art results (notably higher BLANC F1) and includes ablations, clustering strategy comparisons, and an error analysis.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that sentence-level neural representations can substitute for external semantic resources in event coreference while achieving state-of-the-art performance on ACE.
2) A practical two-stage architecture (mention encoder + distributed similarity with pairwise features) and evidence that, for this model, transitive-closure clustering outperforms BestLink.
3) A useful analysis of data properties (argument sharing, trigger mismatch, and cross-sentence distances) and the scaling effect of ACE++ on recall.

Strengths
- Competitive performance without external semantic resources: The model attains state-of-the-art or on-par results on ACE across several metrics, especially BLANC F1. This supports the claim that external knowledge bases are not strictly necessary for strong within-document event coreference.
- Sound use of sentence-level modeling and ablations: The paper cleanly separates and evaluates feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, and sentential features), showing their complementary contribution. The sentential features meaningfully improve both precision and recall.
- Clear analysis of data characteristics: The quantification of argument overlap, trigger mismatch, and mention distance is compelling and directly motivates modeling decisions and the need for sentence-level representations.
- Insightful clustering comparison: The empirical comparison between transitive closure and BestLink is valuable; the finding that BestLink hurts recall in this setup adds nuance to prior results.
- Error analysis: The manual inspection of false positives/negatives (e.g., pronoun resolution and borderline annotation cases) is informative and identifies concrete directions for future work and integration with entity coreference.

Weaknesses
- Limited architectural justification and baselines: The choice of CNNs for mention encoding is under-motivated. There is no comparison to strong sequence models (e.g., BiLSTMs, CNN+BiLSTM hybrids) or to modern pooling variants. Given modest absolute F1 gains, stronger evidence is needed that CNNs are the right design here (or at least not inferior).
- Novelty positioning is unclear: The paper frames “sentential features” as the key idea, but using CNN-based sentence encoders for classification is established. The paper should explicitly situate the work relative to prior neural or continuous-representation approaches to event coreference (if any) and clarify what is novel beyond applying CNNs to this task with pairwise features.
- Insufficient detail about ACE++: The paper does not explain how ACE++ was constructed (sources, annotation procedure, event types covered, quality control, inter-annotator agreement). Without this, it is difficult to assess domain coverage, generalization claims, and reproducibility. The claim that resource-heavy systems are domain-tied would be stronger if accompanied by evaluations showing such systems degrade on ACE++, or if ACE++ specifically broadens the event-type distribution.
- Evaluation scope: Results rely on gold triggers (and apparently gold types for type-agreement features). While common, the paper would be much stronger with an end-to-end setting using predicted triggers/types to quantify robustness to upstream noise. Additionally, the comparison set in Table 3 omits some related systems and does not provide a uniform evaluation setup, further complicating conclusions.
- Pairwise feature dependence: Table 5 suggests pairwise features remain crucial on ACE-scale data. This raises questions about how much of the gain comes from neural sentential representations versus well-engineered pairwise features. More analysis of which pairwise features drive performance would help interpret the contribution of the neural component.

Questions to authors
- Architecture/baselines:
  - Why CNNs over BiLSTMs or hybrid encoders for mention representation? Can you report a controlled comparison (same parameters and training budget) against BiLSTM encoders?
  - The similarity layer uses a square nonlinearity. Can you provide quantitative comparisons with cosine, ReLU/tanh MLPs, or bilinear scoring to substantiate this choice?
- ACE++ details and availability:
  - How was ACE++ collected and annotated? What event types are included, and how do their distributions compare to ACE? What annotation guidelines and quality controls (IAA) were used?
  - Will ACE++ (and code) be released to enable reproducibility and meaningful future comparisons?
- Evaluation protocol:
  - Are event types for the type-agreement feature gold or predicted? Please clarify. Can you report results with predicted triggers/types to show robustness?
  - Can you expand the comparison set to include additional neural or continuous-space baselines for event coreference, if available, and ideally run them on your splits?
- Feature analysis:
  - Which pairwise features contribute most? Please provide feature importance or ablations within the pairwise set, and describe how this set was selected and what alternatives were tried.
- Integration with entity/coreference tools:
  - Your error analysis highlights pronoun resolution. Have you tried integrating outputs from a standard coreference system (e.g., for bridging pronominal or nominal anaphora) as features or constraints? Even a simple pipeline baseline could contextualize the remaining gap.

Overall, this is a well-written paper with solid empirical results and useful analyses. Addressing the architectural justification, clarifying ACE++ construction and availability, and broadening the evaluation (stronger neural baselines and non-gold settings) would significantly strengthen the contribution and its impact.
