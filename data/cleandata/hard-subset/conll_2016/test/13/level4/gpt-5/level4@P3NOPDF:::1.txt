This paper presents a CNN-based model for event linking/coreference. Event mentions are encoded via word embeddings passed through a convolutional layer with max-pooling; the resulting vector is concatenated with embeddings of neighboring words and combined with a small set of pairwise features. A single-layer neural network produces a similarity score for coreference decisions. The model is evaluated on ACE and an expanded ACE++ dataset, achieving performance comparable to feature-rich baselines.

Strengths
- Clear, modular neural architecture that integrates distributed representations with a compact set of linguistic features, avoiding heavy reliance on hand-crafted external resources.
- Empirical finding that word embeddings alone are insufficient, while modest feature augmentation yields competitive performance, is informative for the field.
- Results on two datasets, including an expanded version, help probe generality.

Concerns about experimental setting
- Use of gold triggers: While the paper explains this choice, it complicates fair comparison, particularly because at least one competing system uses predicted triggers. Reporting results with predicted triggers (or normalizing comparisons) would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Data splits: Different train/test splits across papers hinder comparability. When possible, please adopt widely used splits or provide results on them in addition to your chosen split.

Requests for clarification
- Cross-sentential information: The quantitative evidence that cross-sentential information helps is convincing, but the concluding statement in the second paragraph (lines 65–70) is unclear. Please restate the claim more explicitly and, if possible, add a concrete example.
- Position embeddings: You state that position embeddings are “generated in a way similar to word embeddings.” Please specify: Are they randomly initialized and learned end-to-end? Are they tied to absolute or relative positions? Are they shared across contexts? Why should the same relative position next to different words have the same embedding, and did you consider contextualizing position vectors?
- Left vs. right neighbors (lines 307–311): How exactly are left and right contexts handled? Are separate convolutional filters applied, or is the distinction only reflected in pooling or concatenation? Clarify whether the asymmetry influences the CNN, the pooling step, or only the final concatenation.
- Context window choice: You append embeddings for exactly one word before and after the trigger. Why was this window selected? Did you try broader windows or learned attention over context words, and how did those affect performance?
- Representation v_e (line 330): How is v_e used downstream? Subsequent sections appear to rely on v_sent+lex. Please clarify the roles of these vectors and where each enters the scoring network.
- Pairwise features (Section 3.2): Most features are binary, but how is distance encoded (e.g., binned, normalized scalar, or embedding)? Are these feature embeddings learned jointly or fixed? Precisely how are pairwise features integrated with the neural representations (concatenation vs. separate MLP and then fusion)?

Results and analysis
- Reported scores are often close. Statistical significance testing (e.g., bootstrap or paired randomization) would bolster the comparisons.
- Given the central claim that embeddings alone underperform, an ablation table isolating the contribution of each feature group (neighbor words, position, pairwise features) would be valuable.
- Error analysis highlighting frequent failure modes (e.g., cross-sentence links, lexical variation, argument compatibility) would increase interpretability.

Modeling choices
- The use of a square function as the nonlinearity is intriguing. Is this novel in this context? Please discuss motivation, stability considerations (e.g., gradient growth), and comparisons to common activations (ReLU, tanh). If included, an ablation on activation choice would strengthen the case for this design.
- The approach seems readily adaptable to entity coreference. Demonstrating applicability on an entity coreference benchmark (e.g., OntoNotes) would broaden impact and enable comparison to a larger body of prior work, even if only as a preliminary experiment.

Datasets and reproducibility
- While ACE is public (subject to license), ACE++ is not. Do you plan to release ACE++ or scripts to reproduce it? Absent release, a stronger comparison against feature-rich systems on the public ACE split would help.
- Please provide exact preprocessing steps, tokenization, and any resources used (e.g., pre-trained embeddings, hyperparameters) to facilitate replication.

Related work
- The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of neural coreference models, including Wiseman et al. (2015): “Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution” (ACL 2015). Position your approach relative to antecedent-ranking architectures and discuss similarities/differences in feature integration.

Minor issues
- Line 143: “that” appears redundant.
- Table 6 vs. text (line 670): One baseline is labeled “same type” in the table but “same event” in the text. Please standardize the terminology.
- Tighten notation consistency for v_e vs. v_sent+lex and ensure all symbols are defined at first use.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution that demonstrates a clean neural formulation for event coreference with minimal feature engineering and competitive performance. The work would be notably stronger with: (i) results using predicted triggers or a clearer normalization of comparisons; (ii) standard data splits and/or release of ACE++; (iii) statistical significance tests and targeted ablations; and (iv) clarifications on representation design and feature integration. Addressing these points would enhance both rigor and reproducibility, and would make the paper more impactful.
