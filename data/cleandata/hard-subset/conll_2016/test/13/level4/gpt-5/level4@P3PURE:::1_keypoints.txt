- Paper models event linking with CNN-based mention representations plus surrounding-word embeddings and pairwise features, producing coreference scores comparable to prior systems.
- Main contribution: a neural event-linking approach combining word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features; embeddings alone are insufficient.
- Concern: authors use gold trigger words rather than predicted triggers, making comparisons to systems using predicted triggers unfair.
- Concern: inconsistent train/test splits across papers; authors should adhere to established splits for comparability.
- Clarification needed: final statement in second paragraph (lines 65–70) about cross-sentential information is unclear.
- Clarification needed: how position embeddings are generated — random, lexicalized, or otherwise — and why same relative position would share embeddings across different words.
- Clarification needed: how left vs. right neighbors are used in representation (lines 307–311) and whether distinction only affects max-pooling.
- Clarification needed: rationale for appending exactly one word before and after the trigger rather than a different context window.
- Clarification needed: how the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is used, since subsequent sections reference only v_sent+lex.
- Clarification needed: how pairwise features are encoded (binary vector?) and handled during training (e.g., distance feature, are features fixed?).
- Suggestion: investigate applying the approach to entity coreference to compare against a larger body of prior work and datasets like OntoNotes.
- Question: is the use of a square function as nonlinearity novel here, and is it likely applicable to other tasks?
- Dataset/reproducibility: ACE++ is not public — authors should release it or at least compare feature-rich systems on ACE++ for meaningful comparisons.
- Results: some performance differences are small; statistical significance testing is needed to strengthen claims.
- Related work: cite neural entity-coreference work such as Wiseman et al. (2015).
- Minor issues: remove redundant “that” (line 143) and make baseline naming consistent (“same type” vs. “same event”, Table 6 vs. text).
