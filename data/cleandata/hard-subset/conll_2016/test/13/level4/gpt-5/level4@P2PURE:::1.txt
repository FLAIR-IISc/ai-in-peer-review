This paper addresses event linking using CNNs. For each event mention, the authors build a vector representation by feeding word embeddings through a CNN followed by max pooling. They then concatenate this representation with embeddings of surrounding context words. In combination with a set of pairwise features, these vectors are passed to a single-layer neural network to produce similarity scores and, ultimately, a coreference score. The model is evaluated on ACE and an expanded ACE++ dataset, achieving performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems.

The main contribution, in my view, is a neural method for event linking that integrates word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features. The finding that embeddings alone are insufficient is notable. Encouragingly, the linguistic features employed are limited and do not rely on hand-engineered external resources.

Experimental setting
- It appears that gold triggers are used rather than predicted ones. While the authors argue this is reasonable, I would still like to see results with predicted triggers. This is especially problematic because one competing system uses predicted triggers, making the comparison uneven.
- The variation in train/test splits across papers is concerning. I recommend adhering to previously used splits where possible to facilitate fair comparison.

Unclear points
- The evidence supporting the need for cross-sentential information is persuasive. However, the final claim in the second paragraph (lines 65–70) is unclear.
- The paper states that position embeddings are generated “similarly to word embeddings.” How exactly? Are they randomly initialized and learned during training? Are they lexicalized in any way? It is not obvious why the same relative position (e.g., +1) should share an embedding regardless of the adjacent word.
- How are left versus right neighbors incorporated into the representation (lines 307–311)? Does this distinction only influence the max-pooling step, or does it affect earlier layers as well?
- You append the embeddings for exactly one word to the left and one word to the right of the trigger. This choice seems arbitrary. Why one word on each side rather than a different window size?
- It is not clear how the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is ultimately used. Subsequent sections appear to rely on v_sent+lex rather than v_e.
- In Section 3.2, how are the pairwise features encoded and used? For the mostly binary features, I assume a binary vector; how is the distance feature handled (e.g., binned, scaled, or embedded)? Are these feature representations fixed or learned during training?

Other issues and suggestions
- Can the approach be extended to entity coreference resolution? This would enable comparisons with more prior work and widely used datasets such as OntoNotes.
- The use of a square activation function is intriguing. Is this novel in this context? Do you expect it to be effective in other tasks?
- Datasets: while one dataset is publicly available, results are also reported on ACE++, which is not. Do you plan to release ACE++? That would facilitate comparisons. At minimum, it would be helpful to include comparisons to the feature-rich systems on this dataset as well.
- Results: several reported numbers are close. Statistical significance testing would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Related work: among neural approaches to (entity) coreference, please consider citing Wiseman et al. (2015).

Minor issues
- Line 143: “that” appears redundant.
- A baseline is referred to as “same type” in Table 6 but “same event” in the text (line 670); please standardize the naming.

Refs
- Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. Sam Wiseman, Alexander M. Rush, Jason Weston, and Stuart M. Shieber. ACL 2015.
