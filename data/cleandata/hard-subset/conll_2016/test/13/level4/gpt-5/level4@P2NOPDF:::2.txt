This paper addresses event entity linking by replacing external semantic resources with sentential representations learned via CNNs. The model has two components: an encoder that builds event mention representations and a pairwise scorer that predicts coreference between event mentions.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written, well organized, and easy to follow.
- The analysis of argument sharing in event coreference on ACE is insightful.
- The investigation of dataset size effects is compelling and motivates the creation of the ACE++ dataset.

However, several major issues require attention:

- Insufficient motivation and analysis for using CNNs. The paper does not justify why CNNs are preferable for mention representation, nor does it compare CNNs against plausible alternatives such as RNNs/biLSTMs or transformers. Given that the reported gains over prior work are modest (approximately 2–3 F1 points across metrics), stronger empirical evidence is needed to support the architectural choice. At minimum, include ablations comparing CNNs to RNN/biLSTM encoders and varying context windows, as well as analysis of training efficiency and robustness.

- Unclear novelty relative to prior neural or continuous-space approaches. Using CNN-derived sentential features for classification is well established. The paper should more explicitly articulate what is new in the context of event linking (e.g., the specific pairing of mention-level and pairwise scoring objectives, feature design, or training regime). Table 3’s baseline selection appears incomplete; please compare against existing continuous-space/neural methods for event linking (or closely related tasks) and clarify why particular baselines were chosen. If such methods are unavailable, position the work more carefully and discuss limitations.

- Lack of details about ACE++. The paper does not explain how ACE++ was collected or annotated. Since a known limitation of ACE is its small and biased event type inventory, it is important to describe ACE++’s event type coverage, domains, annotation guidelines, and label distribution. This information is also necessary to substantiate the claim in Section 5.1 that prior approaches are domain-tied while your approach is not. To support that claim, demonstrate that systems relying on external semantic resources degrade on ACE++ whereas your model remains competitive. Finally, please consider releasing ACE++ to facilitate reproducibility and meaningful future comparisons.

Minor issues and suggestions:
- Report performance without gold references in Table 3 (e.g., using predicted mentions/links), to assess end-to-end applicability.
- Explore integration with a vanilla coreference resolver. In the example around line 687, an off-the-shelf CoreNLP model links “It” to “bombing”; such signals could be leveraged in an event linking baseline or as auxiliary features.
- Given ACE’s limited size, evaluate on additional corpora, especially EventCorefBank, and discuss whether joint modeling of entity and event coreference could improve robustness.
- Table 5 suggests pairwise features are highly effective, indicating that feature engineering still matters at ACE scale. Please report which pairwise features contribute most, how the feature set was selected, and what alternatives you tried. A feature ablation study would be informative.

Additional recommendations to strengthen the paper:
- Provide a more thorough architectural rationale, including theoretical or empirical reasons for preferring CNNs for local sentential context, and discuss the role of context length.
- Include ablations for encoder type (CNN vs. RNN/biLSTM), context window size, pretrained embeddings, and training data scaling; present learning curves if possible.
- Offer an error analysis that distinguishes gains attributable to sentential representations from those due to pairwise features or dataset characteristics.
- Clarify evaluation settings, particularly the use (or not) of gold annotations, and report variance across runs.

Overall assessment:
This is a promising and clearly presented paper with valuable analyses of ACE and dataset size effects. However, the novelty claim is currently under-supported, and the empirical evidence does not yet establish that the proposed architecture is superior to strong alternatives. In addition, ACE++ needs substantially more documentation, and releasing it would greatly benefit the community. Addressing the concerns above—especially stronger baselines, encoder comparisons, feature ablations, and detailed dataset reporting—would significantly improve the submission.
