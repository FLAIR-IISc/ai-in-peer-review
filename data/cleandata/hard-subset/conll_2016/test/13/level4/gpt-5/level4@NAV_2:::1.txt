This paper addresses event coreference with a CNN-based model. For each event mention, the method builds a vector representation by passing word embeddings through a convolutional layer with max pooling, then concatenates this with embeddings of tokens adjacent to the trigger. Combined with a set of pairwise features, a shallow neural layer outputs a similarity vector, which is then converted into a coreference score. Experiments on ACE and a larger ACE++ variant show performance on par with prior systems that rely on extensive semantic features.

In my view, the primary contribution is a neural approach to event linking that blends word embeddings with a modest set of linguistic features. A notable finding is that embeddings alone are insufficient; adding lightweight linguistic signals materially improves results. Importantly, the feature set avoids dependence on manually curated external resources.

Experimental setting
- The experiments appear to use gold triggers rather than predicted ones. While the paper provides a rationale, it would still be valuable to report results with predicted triggers. This is particularly concerning because at least one comparator uses predicted triggers, making the comparison uneven.
- The inconsistency in train/test splits across papers is problematic. Where feasible, please align with previously used splits to facilitate fairer comparisons.

Unclear points
- The statistics motivating cross-sentential reasoning are persuasive; however, the concluding claim in the second paragraph (lines 65–70) is not clear to me.
- Position embeddings are said to be “generated in a way similar to word embeddings.” How exactly? Are they randomly initialized and trained end-to-end? Are they tied to token identities in any way? Why should the same relative position index receive the same embedding regardless of the neighboring word?
- How are left vs. right contexts incorporated into the representation (lines 307–311)? Is the distinction only realized through the piecewise max-pooling step, or elsewhere as well?
- Appending the embeddings of exactly one token to the left and right of the trigger seems ad hoc. Why this window size versus alternatives?
- The role of the event-mention representation ve (line 330) is unclear. Subsequent sections seem to operate on vsent+lex rather than ve—where is ve actually used?
- For the pairwise features in Section 3.2: how are non-binary features such as distance encoded? Are these features treated as constants or are any of their parameters learned during training?

Other issues and suggestions
- Could this approach be adapted to entity coreference? That would allow comparison with a broader literature and datasets such as OntoNotes.
- The choice of a square activation as the similarity nonlinearity is intriguing. Is this novel in this context? Do you anticipate it being useful in other tasks?
- Datasets: only one dataset is public; ACE++ is not. Do you plan to release ACE++? It would aid reproducibility and comparison. At minimum, it would be helpful to include comparisons with feature-rich systems on ACE++ as well.
- Results: several differences are small. Please include statistical significance tests to support claims.
- Related work: consider citing the neural coreference model of Wiseman et al. (2015).

Minor issues
- Line 143: “that” appears superfluous.
- The baseline is labeled “same type” in Table 6 but referred to as “same event” in the text (line 670); please standardize.

Refs
- Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. Sam Wiseman, Alexander M. Rush, Jason Weston, and Stuart M. Shieber. ACL 2015.
