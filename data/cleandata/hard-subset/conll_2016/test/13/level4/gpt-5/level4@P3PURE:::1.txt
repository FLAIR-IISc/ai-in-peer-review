This paper models event linking using CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors construct vector representations by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling. They then concatenate the resulting representations with embeddings of surrounding words. Together with a set of pairwise features, a single-layer neural network produces a similarity vector used to compute a coreference score. The model is evaluated on an ACE dataset and on an expanded version, achieving performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems.

In my view, the main contribution is a neural approach for event linking that combines word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features. It is noteworthy that word embeddings alone are not sufficient for strong performance; the added linguistic features help, yet they are limited in number and do not require manually crafted external resources.

Experimental setting
- It appears that gold trigger words are used rather than predicted ones. Although the authors argue this is reasonable, I would still like to see results with predicted triggers. This is particularly problematic because one of the competing systems uses predicted triggers, making the comparison unfair.
- The use of different train/test splits across papers is worrisome. I encourage the authors to adhere to previously established splits whenever possible.

Points needing clarification
- The evidence indicating that cross-sentential information is necessary is convincing. However, the final statement in the second paragraph (lines 65–70) is unclear.
- Embeddings for positions are described as being generated “in a way similar to word embeddings.” How exactly? Are they randomly initialized? Are they lexicalized? It is not clear why a relative position adjacent to one word should share the same embedding as that same relative position adjacent to a different word.
- How are left vs. right neighbors used to create the representation (lines 307–311)? Does this distinction only affect the max-pooling operation?
- The choice to append the embeddings of exactly one word before and one word after the trigger seems arbitrary. Why one word on each side rather than a different context window?
- It is not clear how the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is used. In subsequent sections, only v_sent+lex appears to be used, not v_e.
- How are pairwise features used in Section 3.2? Most features are binary, so I assume they form a binary vector; what about the distance feature, for example? Are these features fixed during training?

Other issues and suggestions
- Can the approach be applied to entity coreference resolution as well? This would facilitate comparisons to a larger body of prior work and to widely used datasets such as OntoNotes.
- The use of a square function as a nonlinearity is interesting. Is this novel in this context? Do you anticipate applicability to other tasks?
- Datasets: one dataset is publicly available, but ACE++ is not. Do you plan to release it? Release would enable more meaningful comparisons. At minimum, it would be helpful to include comparisons to feature-rich systems on the ACE++ dataset as well.
- Results: some reported differences are small. Statistical significance testing would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Related work: among neural approaches to (entity) coreference resolution, please consider citing Wiseman et al. (2015).

Minor issues
- Line 143: “that” is redundant.
- One baseline is labeled “same type” in Table 6 but referred to as “same event” in the text (line 670). Please make this consistent.

Reference
- Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. Sam Wiseman, Alexander M. Rush, Jason Weston, and Stuart M. Shieber. ACL 2015.
