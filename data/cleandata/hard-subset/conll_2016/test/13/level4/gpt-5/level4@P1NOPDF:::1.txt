This paper models event linking with CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors build vector representations by feeding word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling, then concatenate the resulting vectors with embeddings of words surrounding the mention. Combined with a set of pairwise features, these inputs feed a single-layer neural network to produce a similarity vector and, ultimately, a coreference score. The model is evaluated on ACE and an expanded ACE++ variant, achieving performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems.

The main contribution, in my view, is a neural approach to event linking that integrates word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features. It is notable that embeddings alone are insufficient for strong performance, while the added linguistic features are limited and do not require manually crafted external resources.

Experimental setting
- It appears the experiments use gold trigger words rather than predicted ones. While the authors provide a rationale, I would still like to see results with predicted triggers. This is particularly important because one competing system uses predicted triggers, making the comparison potentially unfair.
- The use of different train/test splits across papers is concerning. I encourage the authors to adopt established splits wherever possible to enable fair comparison.

Unclear points
- The evidence for the need for cross-sentential information is convincing. However, the final statement in the second paragraph (lines 65–70) is unclear.
- Position embeddings are said to be generated “in a way similar to word embeddings.” How exactly? Are they randomly initialized and trained end-to-end? Are they lexicalized or purely relative indices? Why should the same relative position receive the same embedding next to different words?
- How are left vs. right neighbors incorporated into the representation (lines 307–311)? Does this distinction only affect max-pooling, or also the convolutional filters or concatenation?
- Appending the embeddings of one word before and one word after the trigger seems arbitrary. Why this window size, as opposed to a larger or learned window?
- It is unclear how the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is used. Subsequent sections appear to rely only on v{sent+lex}, not v_e.
- How exactly are the pairwise features used in section 3.2? If most are binary, are they concatenated as a binary vector? How is the distance feature handled (e.g., binned, normalized, or embedded)? Are these features fixed inputs, or are any parameters associated with them learned during training?

Other issues and suggestions
- Can the approach extend to entity coreference resolution? This would enable comparison with a larger body of work and widely used datasets such as OntoNotes.
- The use of a square activation function is interesting. Is this novel in this context? Do you see broader applicability to other tasks?
- Datasets: while one dataset is public, ACE++ is not. Do you plan to release it? Public availability would facilitate reproducibility and comparison. At minimum, it would be helpful to compare against feature-rich systems on the same dataset.
- Results: several reported differences are small. Significance testing would strengthen the claims.
- Related work: among neural approaches to (entity) coreference, Wiseman et al. (2015) should be cited.

Minor issues
- Line 143: “that” is redundant.
- One baseline is labeled “same type” in Table 6 but “same event” in the text (line 670); please make the terminology consistent.

Refs
- Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. Sam Wiseman, Alexander M. Rush, Jason Weston, and Stuart M. Shieber. ACL 2015.
