This paper approaches event linking with CNNs. Given event mentions, the authors build vector encodings by passing word embeddings through a CNN followed by max-pooling. They then concatenate these encodings with several contextual word embeddings surrounding the mention. Along with certain pairwise features, they feed these into a single-layer neural network to produce a similarity vector and compute a coreference score. The model is evaluated on an ACE dataset and an expanded ACE++ version, achieving performance comparable to prior feature-rich systems. The main contribution, in my view, is a neural method for event linking that integrates word embeddings with a small set of linguistic features. It is noteworthy that embeddings alone are insufficient for strong performance; fortunately, the added linguistic features are limited and do not rely on hand-crafted external resources.

Experimental setting
- It appears gold trigger words are used rather than predicted ones. While the authors argue this is reasonable, I would still like to see results with predicted triggers. This is especially concerning because one competing system uses predicted triggers, making the comparison unfair.
- The use of different train/test splits across papers is troubling. I encourage the authors to follow established splits whenever possible.

Unclear points
- The evidence suggesting cross-sentential information is needed is convincing. However, the final statement in the second paragraph (lines 65–70) was unclear to me.
- Position embeddings are said to be generated “in a way similar to word embeddings.” How exactly? Are they randomly initialized? Are they lexicalized? It is not obvious why the same relative position next to different words should share an embedding.
- How are left versus right neighbors incorporated into the representation (lines 307–311)? Does this only affect the max-pooling step?
- Appending the embeddings of one token before and one token after the trigger feels arbitrary. Why choose one token on each side rather than another window size?
- It is unclear how the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is used. In later sections only v{sent+lex} seems to appear; how does v_e factor in?
- In Section 3.2, how are pairwise features used? Most are binary—are they encoded as a binary vector? How is the distance feature handled, for instance? Are these features kept fixed during training?

Other issues and suggestions
- Can the approach be applied to entity coreference as well? This would enable comparisons with more prior work and widely used datasets such as OntoNotes.
- The use of a square nonlinearity is intriguing. Is it novel? Do you anticipate applicability to other tasks?
- Datasets: one dataset is public, but results are also reported on ACE++, which is not. Do you plan to release it? This would facilitate comparisons. At minimum, a comparison with feature-rich systems on ACE++ would be useful.
- Results: several reported numbers are very close. Significance testing would strengthen the comparisons.
- Related work: among neural approaches to (entity) coreference, Wiseman et al. (2015) could be cited.

Minor issues
- Line 143: “that” is unnecessary.
- One baseline is labeled “same type” in Table 6 but “same event” in the text (line 670).

Refs
- Learning Anaphoricity and Antecedent Ranking Features for Coreference Resolution. Sam Wiseman, Alexander M. Rush, Jason Weston, and Stuart M. Shieber. ACL 2015.
