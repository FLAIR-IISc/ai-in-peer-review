- Proposes a resource-light neural model for within-document event coreference using CNN-derived sentential representations instead of external semantic resources.  
- Encodes event mentions by combining CNN sentence embeddings with local lexical context and scores mention pairs with additional pairwise features.  
- Evaluates on ACE 2005 and an expanded ACE++ dataset, achieving performance comparable to feature-rich systems.  

- Strength: Resource-independent model matches or exceeds prior systems that rely on external semantic resources, improving portability.  
- Strength: Two-stage architecture (mention representation via CNNs, then pairwise scoring) is well-motivated by data properties.  
- Strength: Comprehensive empirical analysis including ablations, clustering comparisons, and error analysis.  
- Strength: Sentential features significantly boost recall; larger training data (ACE++) further improves generalization.  
- Strength: Useful qualitative error analysis highlighting pronoun/discourse challenges and reproducibility signals (implementation details and ACE split list).  

- Weakness: Main results use gold triggers while some baselines use predicted triggers, undermining fair comparison and end-to-end claims.  
- Weakness: Non-aligned dataset splits complicate direct comparisons to prior work; ACE split choice limits fairness.  
- Weakness: Position-embedding parameterization and training details are unclear.  
- Weakness: Left/right context handling and dual-pooling use are not clearly specified, especially with multiple mentions per sentence.  
- Weakness: Context window choice (one token each side) appears arbitrary without sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: Unclear role of final event-mention vector ve versus vsent+lex in the pairwise scorer.  
- Weakness: Pairwise feature encoding, distance binning/normalization, and learnability are underspecified.  
- Weakness: ACE++ is not publicly available, limiting reproducibility and external benchmarking.  
- Weakness: Many reported differences are small and lack statistical significance testing.  
- Weakness: Related work misses recent neural coreference models (e.g., Wiseman et al., 2015).  

- Q: Can you report results with predicted triggers to assess end-to-end performance and fairness of comparisons?  
- Q: How are relative position embeddings initialized, trained, and shared across contexts?  
- Q: Does left/right pooling affect only max-pooling or earlier layers, and how are multiple mentions per sentence disambiguated?  
- Q: Why include exactly one token of lexical context; were larger windows or attention tried?  
- Q: Which representation (ve or vsent+lex) is fed into the similarity/pairwise scorer?  
- Q: How are binary and distance pairwise features encoded, scaled, and are any parameters learned?  
- Q: Is the squared activation for similarity novel, and how does it compare empirically to ReLU/tanh/cosine beyond reported settings?  
- Q: Will ACE++ or the split/pair lists be released, or can you provide a replication package or run baselines on request?  
- Q: Were statistical significance tests run for key comparisons and ablations?  

- Suggestion: Test the approach on entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes) to broaden impact and comparisons.  
- Request: Clarify the concluding claim about cross-sentential information in the Introduction.
