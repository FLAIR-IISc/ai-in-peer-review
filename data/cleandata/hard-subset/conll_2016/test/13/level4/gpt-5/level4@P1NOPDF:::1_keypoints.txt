- Paper proposes a CNN-based model for event linking that builds mention vectors from word embeddings + CNN+max-pool, concatenates context embeddings, adds pairwise features, and scores coreference; achieves comparable performance to prior feature-rich systems on ACE/ACE++.

- Main contribution: a neural event linking approach combining word embeddings with a small set of simple linguistic features; embeddings alone are insufficient, but added features are limited and resource-light.

- Experimental concern: experiments use gold trigger words rather than predicted triggers, which may give unfair advantage versus competing systems that use predicted triggers.

- Experimental concern: different papers use different train/test splits; authors should adopt established splits for fair comparison.

- Unclear: the final statement about the need for cross-sentential information in lines 65–70 is not clearly explained.

- Unclear: how position embeddings are generated—are they randomly initialized and trained, lexicalized or relative indices, and why should identical relative positions share embeddings across different words?

- Unclear: how left vs. right neighbors are represented—does this affect only max-pooling or also convolutional filters and concatenation?

- Unclear: rationale for using a single-word window (one word before and after) around the trigger—why not larger or learned window sizes?

- Unclear: how the event-mention representation v_e (line 330) is used, since later sections seem to rely only on v_{sent+lex}.

- Unclear: exact treatment of pairwise features in section 3.2—are binary features concatenated, how is distance handled (binned/normalized/embedded), and are any feature parameters learned?

- Suggestion: consider extending the approach to entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes) to enable broader comparisons.

- Question: the use of a square activation function is interesting—is this novel here and does it have broader applicability?

- Dataset issue: ACE++ is not public; authors should release it or at least compare against feature-rich systems on the same public dataset to aid reproducibility.

- Results: many reported differences are small; include statistical significance testing to strengthen claims.

- Related work: cite neural approaches to coreference such as Wiseman et al. (2015).

- Minor: Line 143 has redundant "that."

- Minor: inconsistent terminology—baseline labeled “same type” in Table 6 but “same event” in text (line 670); make consistent.
