Summary and contributions
The paper tackles within-document event coreference by representing each event mention with a CNN over its sentence, augmented with local lexical context (embeddings of the trigger and its immediate neighbors). Pairwise features are concatenated with these representations, passed through a single-layer “similarity” network with a square activation, and scored by logistic regression. The model is evaluated on ACE and an expanded corpus (ACE++), achieving performance comparable to feature-rich systems while avoiding external semantic resources.

Contributions, as I see them:
1) A neural event-linking model that replaces heavy external semantic features with sentential CNN features and lightweight pairwise features, yet remains competitive with prior state-of-the-art.
2) A careful empirical study including ablations over feature classes (pairwise, local, and sentential) and a comparison of clustering strategies, showing which components matter most.
3) A larger ACE++ dataset used for training/evaluation (though not released), with error analysis highlighting remaining challenges.

Strengths
- Competitive results without external knowledge bases: The model attains state-of-the-art or on-par performance with systems that rely on extensive semantic resources. This is a meaningful contribution for portability to low-resource domains.
- Solid ablations and analysis: The feature ablations convincingly demonstrate that word embeddings alone are insufficient; combining sentential features with local lexical and pairwise signals substantially improves performance. The data-driven motivation (e.g., many positive pairs lack argument overlap and often do not share a trigger) is well argued.
- Insightful evaluation of clustering strategies: The comparison between transitive closure and BestLink clarifies why the proposed classifier reduces the need for aggressive downstream pruning and how recall is affected.
- Qualitative error analysis: The discussion of pronoun-related misses and annotation ambiguities provides useful guidance for future work.

Weaknesses
- Use of gold triggers and split inconsistencies weaken comparability: Results are reported with gold triggers; at least one competing system uses predicted triggers, making the comparison unfavorable to that baseline. Moreover, non-standard train/test splits across papers complicate comparisons. Reporting performance with predicted triggers and aligning splits (or releasing the exact split lists) would strengthen claims.
- Clarity gaps in model description:
  • Position embeddings: “Generated similarly to word embeddings” is too vague. Are they randomly initialized, learned from scratch, and independent of token identity? Why should the same relative offset embedding be shared across different anchor words?
  • Left vs. right handling: Please clarify precisely how left/right contexts affect convolution, pooling, and subsequent layers (lines 307–311). Does the split only affect max-pooling, or also convolution windows?
  • Local context window choice: Appending embeddings for exactly one token on each side of the trigger seems ad hoc; motivate this choice or show sensitivity to window size.
  • Use of v_e: After defining v_e (line 330), later equations predominantly reference v_{sent+lex}. Please clarify where v_e is actually used in the pairwise scoring pipeline.
  • Pairwise feature encoding: Most features appear binary, but distance is not. How are these features encoded and normalized? Are they trainable embeddings or fixed inputs? This affects reproducibility.
- Limited novelty beyond adaptation: CNN-based sentential encoders for relation/event tasks are established; the primary novelty seems to be applying them to event coreference with a square activation in the similarity layer. The originality of the square activation and its broader applicability are not convincingly argued.
- Dataset availability and statistical testing: ACE++ is not publicly available; without release, it is hard for others to reproduce or compare. Some reported improvements are small; significance testing would bolster the conclusions. Also, comparing the proposed model with feature-rich systems on ACE++ would be informative.
- Scope: The approach appears directly applicable to entity coreference; evaluating on a standard entity coref benchmark (e.g., OntoNotes) would enable stronger positioning relative to broader coreference literature.

Questions to authors
1) Can you report results with predicted triggers (and the trigger detector used), at least on ACE? This would enable fairer comparisons and practical insights.
2) How are position embeddings initialized and trained? Are they tied only to relative offsets, independent of token identity, and do you use clipping for large distances?
3) Precisely where is v_e used downstream? If vsent+lex is the actual input to the similarity network, what role does v_e play?
4) How are pairwise features encoded and scaled (especially distances)? Are any pairwise features embedded and learned?
5) Did you test larger local windows around the trigger or alternative context representations (e.g., span pooling)? Please provide a sensitivity analysis.
6) Is the square activation in the similarity layer novel in this context? Did you compare against alternatives (e.g., ReLU, tanh, bilinear, cosine) with significance tests?
7) Will you release ACE++ and the exact train/valid/test splits for both datasets?
8) Have you tried the framework for entity coreference (e.g., OntoNotes)? If not, what adaptations would be required?
