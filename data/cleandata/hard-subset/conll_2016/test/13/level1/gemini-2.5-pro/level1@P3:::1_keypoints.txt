- Proposed model: CNN with piecewise max-pooling produces sentential event representations; a feed-forward network scores event-pair coreference using those representations plus a few pairwise features.
- Evaluation: Achieves state-of-the-art on ACE 2005 and on a new larger ACE++ dataset, removing dependence on external semantic KBs.
- Contribution 1: Demonstrates learned sentential features can replace hand-crafted KB-derived features for event coreference, improving portability and domain adaptability.
- Contribution 2: Introduces and evaluates on ACE++, a larger dataset that shows benefits of more training data and provides a resource for future work.
- Contribution 3: Presents thorough empirical evaluation including ablation studies and error analysis, identifying remaining challenges like pronominal event anaphora.
- Strength 1: High impact—addresses dependency on costly, domain-specific knowledge resources and supports a more deployable approach to event extraction.
- Strength 2: Strong empirical results—matches or exceeds prior SOTA despite not using external engineered features, with careful metric justification (BLANC).
- Strength 3: Convincing analyses—ablation, clustering comparisons, and ACE++ results substantiate the role of sentential features and model components.
- Strength 4: Clear presentation and reproducibility—well-explained architecture, diagrams, and released data splits.
- Weakness 1: Limited architectural novelty—the CNN with piecewise pooling is largely adopted from prior relation-classification work.
- Weakness 2: Insufficient analysis of what the learned representations capture and weak justification/comparison for the square activation in the similarity layer.
- Weakness 3: No comparison to alternative sentence encoders (e.g., LSTMs/GRUs) to determine whether CNN is uniquely effective.
- Weakness 4: Shallow incorporation of event arguments—argument compatibility is only captured via a simple overlap feature rather than integrated into representations.
- Question 1: Please provide comparative results and rationale for using the square non-linearity versus standard choices (tanh, ReLU) and explain the desirability of polarity invariance.
- Question 2: Have you tried a more end-to-end similarity computed directly from event vectors (concatenation, element-wise product/difference) to test reliance on hand-crafted pairwise features?
- Question 3: How could the architecture be extended to incorporate wider document context to better handle pronominal event anaphora and other discourse-level phenomena?
