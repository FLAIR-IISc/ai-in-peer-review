- Summary: Proposes a two-stage neural model (CNN + pairwise similarity + logistic layer) for within-document event coreference that forgoes external lexical resources and matches or exceeds prior SOTA on ACE 2005 and a proprietary ACE++ dataset.

- Contribution 1: Demonstrates state-of-the-art event linking without external semantic knowledge bases by using learned sentential features.

- Contribution 2: Introduces a two-part neural architecture: CNN-based sentential encodings for event mentions and a learned similarity function over mention pairs.

- Contribution 3: Provides extensive empirical evaluation and ablation studies (including on a larger dataset) and qualitative error analysis.

- Strength 1: Achieves strong empirical performance (SOTA F1 across multiple metrics) despite not using resource-heavy external features.

- Strength 2: Improves generality and portability by replacing external KB features with learned sentential representations.

- Strength 3: Clear, well-documented methodology with diagrams, hyperparameters, and algorithms that aid reproducibility.

- Strength 4: Thorough evaluation beyond SOTA comparison, including ablation, clustering strategy analysis, data-size impact, and manual error analysis.

- Weakness 1: Architectural novelty is incremental—CNN with position embeddings and max-pooling is adapted from prior relation classification work.

- Weakness 2: Key analyses (ablation, data-size effects) rely on a non-public ACE++ dataset, limiting reproducibility and verification.

- Weakness 3: Comparisons to prior work may be unfair due to differences in splits/preprocessing and unclear use of gold vs. predicted arguments for the argument-overlap feature.

- Weakness 4: Ablation lacks comparison to simpler sentence encoders (e.g., averaged word embeddings), so the specific benefit of the CNN is not isolated.

- Question 1: Is the "overlap in arguments" feature computed from gold-standard annotations or from an automatic argument extraction system?

- Question 2: Have you qualitatively analyzed learned CNN filters or representations to verify the hypothesis that the model focuses on triggers when recall drops?

- Question 3: Does piecewise max-pooling entail processing the sentence once per trigger with left/middle/right segments defined relative to the current trigger?
