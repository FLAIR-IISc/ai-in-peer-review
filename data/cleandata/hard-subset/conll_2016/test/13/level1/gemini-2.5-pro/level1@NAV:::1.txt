**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for event coreference resolution, a task aimed at linking event mentions that refer to the same real-world event within a document. The core idea is to replace the reliance on external knowledge bases (e.g., WordNet), common in prior work, with learned "sentential features" generated by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The model first computes a vector representation for each event mention by processing its containing sentence with a CNN. These representations, augmented with local lexical and pairwise features, are then fed into a second network component that learns a similarity function to produce a coreference score. The primary contribution is the successful demonstration that learned, sentence-level neural features can effectively supplant handcrafted semantic features from external resources, achieving state-of-the-art results on the standard ACE 2005 dataset. A secondary contribution is the creation and use of a larger dataset (ACE++), which, along with a thorough ablation study, provides valuable insights into the model's behavior and the impact of different feature classes.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Impact and Practicality:** The paper compellingly addresses a major limitation of previous event linking systems: their dependency on domain-specific external knowledge bases. By showing that a CNN can learn effective representations from raw text that are competitive with feature-rich systems, the work significantly enhances the portability of event coreference models to new domains where such resources are unavailable. This is a crucial step forward for the practical application of event extraction pipelines.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong. The authors compare their model against multiple state-of-the-art systems on a standard benchmark. Crucially, they conduct a detailed ablation study that clearly isolates the contribution of each component of their model (sentential, local, and pairwise features), validating their design choices. The analysis is further strengthened by comparisons to sensible baselines and an investigation of different clustering strategies.
3.  **Valuable Resource and Error Analysis:** The introduction of the larger ACE++ dataset is a welcome contribution, addressing the data scarcity of the original corpus and enabling more robust model training and evaluation. The error analysis is also insightful, highlighting key remaining challenges for the field, such as pronoun resolution and inherent annotation ambiguities, which provides a clear direction for future work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the application of a CNN to this specific task is effective, the core neural architecture itself (word/position embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling) is heavily borrowed from prior work on sentence-level relation classification, as cited by the authors. The paper could be strengthened by more clearly delineating its architectural innovations or providing a more in-depth justification for why this standard architecture is particularly well-suited for event linking over other alternatives.
2.  **Ambiguity in State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The authors correctly acknowledge that direct comparison with prior work is difficult due to varying data splits and preprocessing pipelines. This makes the claim of achieving SOTA performance slightly less definitive, especially as the proposed model underperforms on the B-CUBED metric and the gains on other metrics are modest.
3.  **Unclear Justification for Similarity Function:** The choice of a `square` activation function in the distributed similarity layer is non-standard. The justification provided—that "invariance under polarity changes is desirable"—is brief and not immediately intuitive. The claim that this choice outperformed alternatives would be much stronger if supported by quantitative results, even if only in an appendix.
