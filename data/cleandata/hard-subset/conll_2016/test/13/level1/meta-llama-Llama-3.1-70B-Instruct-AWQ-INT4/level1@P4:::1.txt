**Summary**

The paper proposes a model for event linking that utilizes sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to resolve coreferences between event mentions in a document. The model consists of two parts: the first part generates a representation for a single event mention using CNNs, and the second part calculates a coreference score for a pair of event mentions based on their representations and pairwise features. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, one of which is publicly available.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel use of sentential features**: The paper proposes a new approach to event linking by utilizing sentential features from CNNs, which is different from traditional methods that rely on external semantic features.
2. **Effective model architecture**: The proposed model architecture, which consists of two parts, is effective in capturing the relationships between event mentions and resolving coreferences.
3. **State-of-the-art performance**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in event linking tasks.

**Strengths**

1. **Robust evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the model on two datasets, including a publicly available one, which demonstrates its effectiveness and generalizability.
2. **Comparison to state-of-the-art systems**: The paper compares the proposed model to state-of-the-art systems, which provides a clear understanding of its strengths and weaknesses.
3. **Error analysis**: The paper provides an error analysis, which helps to identify areas for improvement and provides insights into the challenges of event linking tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited domain coverage**: The paper only evaluates the model on two datasets, which may not be representative of all domains or genres.
2. **Dependence on gold-annotated event mentions**: The model relies on gold-annotated event mentions, which may not be available in all cases.
3. **Limited analysis of feature classes**: The paper only provides a limited analysis of the impact of feature classes on the model's performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the model perform on datasets with different genres or domains?
2. Can the model be adapted to work with automatically generated event mentions instead of gold-annotated ones?
3. How do the sentential features from CNNs compare to other types of neural architectures, such as recursive or recurrent networks, in terms of effectiveness and efficiency?
