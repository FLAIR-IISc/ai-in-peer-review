**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking, a task that involves resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document. The authors design a two-part model that utilizes sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to represent individual event mentions and make coreference decisions. The model does not rely on external semantic features, which are often limited in their domain coverage. The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, and achieve state-of-the-art performance on both.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel use of sentential features for event linking**: The authors propose a new approach to event linking that utilizes sentential features from CNNs, which are more robust and domain-independent compared to traditional external semantic features.
2. **Two-part model architecture**: The authors design a two-part model that first generates event-mention representations and then makes coreference decisions based on these representations.
3. **State-of-the-art performance on ACE and ACE++**: The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets and achieve state-of-the-art performance, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

**Strengths**

1. **Robustness to domain limitations**: The authors' approach does not rely on external semantic features, which are often limited in their domain coverage. This makes their approach more robust and applicable to various domains.
2. **Improved performance on ACE and ACE++**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art performance on both datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Novel use of CNNs for event linking**: The authors propose a new approach to event linking that utilizes CNNs, which are more robust and domain-independent compared to traditional approaches.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited error analysis**: The authors only provide a limited error analysis, which may not be sufficient to understand the limitations of their approach.
2. **Lack of comparison to other neural architectures**: The authors only compare their approach to traditional approaches and do not evaluate the performance of other neural architectures, such as recursive or recurrent neural networks.
3. **Limited evaluation on other datasets**: The authors only evaluate their approach on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, and do not provide results on other datasets, which may limit the generalizability of their approach.

2. How do you plan to evaluate the performance of your approach on other datasets, and what are the potential limitations of your approach in different domains?
3. Can you provide more details on the comparison to other neural architectures, including the performance of recursive or recurrent neural networks on the event linking task?
