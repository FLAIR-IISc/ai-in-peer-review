**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a model for event linking that uses sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to resolve coreferences between event mentions in a document. The model does not rely on external semantic features, instead using a combination of local and sentential features to describe individual event mentions. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, one of which is publicly available.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel use of sentential features**: The paper proposes a new approach to event linking by using sentential features from CNNs, which has not been explored before in this task.
2. **State-of-the-art performance**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Robustness to domain changes**: The model does not rely on external semantic features, making it more robust to domain changes and applicable to a wider range of text types.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of CNNs**: The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using CNNs to generate sentential features for event linking.
2. **Robust evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the model on two datasets, including a comparison with state-of-the-art systems.
3. **Error analysis**: The paper provides an error analysis that sheds light on the limitations of the model and suggests directions for future research.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited generalizability**: The paper only evaluates the model on two datasets, which may not be representative of all possible text types and domains.
2. **Lack of interpretability**: The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the learned sentential features, making it difficult to understand how the model is making its predictions.
3. **Dependence on hyperparameter tuning**: The paper relies heavily on hyperparameter tuning to achieve good performance, which may not be feasible in all scenarios.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the learned sentential features and how they are used by the model to make predictions?
2. How does the model perform on other datasets and domains, and are there any plans to evaluate the model on more diverse text types?
3. Can you discuss the potential limitations of using CNNs for sentential feature generation and whether other neural architectures may be more effective for this task?
