This paper presents a system for event linking that eschews traditional reliance on external knowledge bases, instead leveraging sentential features derived from convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The core idea – to learn event representations directly from context using CNNs – is sound and addresses a significant limitation of prior work. The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, including a publicly available one.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of CNNs to Event Linking:** The primary contribution is the successful application of CNNs to learn event representations directly from sentence context for the event linking task. This is a departure from traditional methods relying heavily on external semantic resources. This approach allows for greater portability and applicability across domains.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Combination:** The model effectively combines sentential features from CNNs with lexical and pairwise features, resulting in a robust and performant system. The explicit consideration of trigger-local features alongside sentence-level ones is a thoughtful design choice.
3. **Empirical Validation and Error Analysis:** The thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies and error analysis, provides valuable insights into the model's strengths and weaknesses, and identifies promising directions for future research.  The identification of pronoun resolution as a key challenge is particularly useful.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Limitation:**  The paper directly tackles the issue of domain dependence in event linking caused by reliance on external knowledge bases.  The proposed solution is highly relevant and valuable to the field.  This is particularly important as deploying IE systems in specialized domains often lacks readily available semantic resources.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on both datasets is a significant accomplishment.  The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of a public dataset is also commendable, facilitating reproducibility and further research.
3. **Well-Designed Model Architecture:** The two-stage architecture – learning event representations followed by a coreference decision – is logically sound and facilitates modularity.  The use of max-pooling to handle multiple event mentions per sentence is a clever design choice.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The manual error analysis goes beyond simply reporting numbers, providing valuable qualitative insights into the types of errors the model makes and identifying specific challenges like pronoun resolution. This depth is often lacking in similar works. The comparison of annotator disagreements with model errors is especially insightful.
5. **Clear and Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN Application:** While the *application* to event linking is novel, the use of CNNs for sentence representation is not. The core CNN architecture itself isn’t groundbreaking and relies on established techniques. The paper doesn't detail significant architectural innovations *within* the CNN component. The novelty lies more in *where* it’s being applied rather than *how*.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons to prior work are provided, the baselines are somewhat dated. The inclusion of more recent, strong baselines leveraging pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) would provide a more rigorous evaluation of the proposed approach and demonstrate its advantages more convincingly.  Given the recent advances in contextualized embeddings, this is a significant omission.
3. **Feature Engineering Still Required:** Despite the goal of reducing reliance on external knowledge, the model still relies on several handcrafted pairwise features. While reasonable, this suggests that a significant portion of the system's performance still depends on careful feature engineering. A deeper exploration of end-to-end learning with minimal feature engineering could further strengthen the approach.
4. **Clustering Strategy Limitation:** The error analysis revealed potential issues in the clustering strategy. The preference for transitive closure over BestLink, despite reported performance improvements in related work, is not fully justified and could be contributing to some errors. A more in-depth analysis of the impact of different clustering strategies would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the CNN-based feature extraction compared to using pre-trained embeddings directly?  Are there scalability concerns for very large documents?
2.  You mention the importance of pronoun resolution as a challenge. Have you considered incorporating specific mechanisms to address this, such as coreference resolution as a pre-processing step or integrating pronoun features directly into the model?
3.  How sensitive is the model performance to the choice of CNN hyperparameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters)? Did you explore a wider range of hyperparameter settings?
4.  The results with ACE++ show a significant drop in precision when solely using sentential and pairwise features (Table 5, row 4). Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made in this configuration, to understand what information is lost when excluding trigger-local lexical features?
