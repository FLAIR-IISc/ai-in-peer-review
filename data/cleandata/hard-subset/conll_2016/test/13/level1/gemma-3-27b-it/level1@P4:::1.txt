This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, avoiding reliance on external knowledge bases. The core idea is to represent event mentions using CNN-derived features from their surrounding context, combined with lexical and pairwise features, and then use these representations for coreference resolution. The authors claim state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, one of which is publicly available, and provide an error analysis to suggest future research directions.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that competitive event linking performance can be achieved *without* relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. This is a valuable contribution, as it broadens the applicability of event linking techniques to domains where such resources are unavailable or limited. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical validation of using CNN-derived sentential features for this task, confirming the potential of these features in capturing relevant contextual information. Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation and error analysis provide a useful benchmark and identify areas for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a practical limitation:** The reliance on external resources is a significant bottleneck for many event linking systems. This work directly addresses this limitation by proposing a viable alternative using solely internal representations learned from the text. This significantly increases the portability and applicability of the approach. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate that competitive performance *is* achievable without these resources.

2. **Solid experimental evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), including comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. The use of standard metrics (BLANC, MUC, B-CUBED) allows for a clear comparison of performance.  The ablation studies (Table 5) providing insights into the importance of each feature class strengthen the analysis.

3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis section is well-executed, providing qualitative examples of the system's failures. The identification of pronoun resolution as a key challenge is insightful. The comparison of model errors to human annotation discrepancies further contextualizes the results, acknowledging inherent ambiguity in the task.

4. **Clear Model Description:** The model architecture is well-described, with a clear explanation of each component (CNN layers, feature concatenation, similarity function). Figure 1 effectively illustrates the two-part architecture. The explanations of design choices are generally logical and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN application:** While the application of CNNs to event linking is a reasonable approach, the specific architecture and methodology are not substantially novel. CNNs have been extensively used for sentence representation in various NLP tasks. The paper doesn't deeply explore or justify the specific CNN architecture choices beyond citing existing work.

2. **Dataset Size:** The ACE dataset is relatively small (599 documents). While ACE++ provides more data, the results on ACE might not generalize well to larger, more diverse datasets. This limitation should be explicitly acknowledged. The impact of dataset size on the observed improvements with ACE++ could be more thoroughly discussed.

3. **Clustering Strategy:** The authors found that a simple transitive closure clustering strategy outperformed the BestLink approach proposed in prior work. While this is a valid finding, the reasons behind this difference are not fully explored. Further investigation into the interaction between the model's confidence scores and the clustering strategy could provide valuable insights.  Why did BestLink *hurt* performance in this setting?

4. **Lack of comparison to more recent methods:** While the paper compares to relevant baselines, the field has progressed rapidly. The inclusion of more recent, strong baseline models, perhaps those leveraging transformers (BERT, etc.) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed method’s competitiveness.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of your model compared to methods relying on extensive external features?  Is there a significant trade-off between performance and computational efficiency?
2.  The error analysis highlights difficulties with pronoun resolution. Have you considered incorporating coreference resolution (entity or event) as a pre-processing step to help address this issue?
3. You found that BestLink performed worse than transitive closure. Can you provide a hypothesis about *why* BestLink degraded performance in your specific setup?  What characteristics of your model’s output might explain this observation?
4.  Given the increasing popularity of transformer-based models, have you experimented with replacing the CNNs with transformer encoders (e.g., BERT) for generating event representations? If so, how did the performance compare?
