## Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

This paper presents a system for event linking that eschews reliance on external knowledge bases, instead leveraging sentential features extracted using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The core idea is to represent event mentions and their contexts with CNNs, combine these representations with lexical and pairwise features, and use a similarity function to determine coreference. The authors report state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, including one publicly available, and provide an error analysis.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that competitive event linking performance can be achieved *without* relying on traditional external semantic features like WordNet or FrameNet. This is a significant step towards more portable and domain-independent event extraction systems. A second notable contribution is the thoughtful architecture combining CNN-based sentential representations with more standard lexical and pairwise features, and the justification for design choices (e.g., dual max-pooling, separate handling of trigger-local features). Finally, the detailed error analysis, which identifies challenges like pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, is a valuable addition and points toward future research directions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Elimination of External Resources:** The strongest argument for acceptance is the success in achieving strong results without relying on potentially unavailable or domain-specific external knowledge bases. This significantly broadens the applicability of the approach.
2. **Well-Motivated Architecture:** The combination of CNNs for sentential features with traditional features is logical and well-explained. The authors justify design choices with preliminary data analysis (e.g., argument overlap, trigger agreement) and insights from related work.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the ACE dataset and competitive performance on ACE++ demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. 
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis is more than just a list of mistakes; it identifies specific challenges and potential avenues for improvement, showcasing a deep understanding of the task.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in CNN Application:** While the *application* to event linking is valuable, the use of CNNs for sentence representation is not inherently novel. The core contribution lies more in the successful integration of this technique within a broader event linking pipeline.
2. **Dataset Size Limitation:** The ACE dataset is relatively small, potentially limiting the generalizability of the results. While ACE++ is used, further evaluation on a larger, more diverse corpus would strengthen the findings.
3. **Clustering Strategy:** The rejection of BestLink clustering, contrary to findings in similar work, warrants further justification. A deeper investigation into *why* transitive closure performs better in their setup would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific reasons why the BestLink clustering strategy performed worse in your experiments compared to previous work? Was it sensitive to hyperparameter tuning?
2.  The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a major challenge. Do you have any initial thoughts on how the model could be extended to better handle this issue (e.g., incorporating coreference resolution at the entity level)?
3.  What is the computational cost of the model, particularly compared to approaches that rely heavily on external features?
