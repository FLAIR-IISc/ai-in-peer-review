## Review for CONLL 2016 Paper: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, aiming to reduce reliance on external knowledge bases. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a system for event linking using CNNs to model intra-sentential event mentions, (2) a model that learns coreference decisions based on generated representations and combined with lexical and pairwise features, and (3) achieving state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, along with an error analysis. I believe the primary contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of solely relying on sentential features derived from CNNs for event linking, achieving competitive results without external semantic resources, and performing a detailed error analysis. The dataset usage and resulting performance improvements also contribute significantly.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on two datasets, including a publicly available one (ACE). This is a significant accomplishment, demonstrating the viability of the proposed approach and its potential to outperform existing methods that heavily rely on external knowledge. The results in Table 3 are compelling and suggest a genuine advancement in the field.

2. **Novelty in Feature Engineering:** The paper successfully utilizes CNNs to extract sentential features for event linking – a relatively novel approach.  The authors effectively argue that reliance on external resources can be a bottleneck, and propose a solution that is more adaptable to different domains. The detailed explanation of the CNN architecture (Section 3.1) and the rationale behind design choices (positional embeddings, max-pooling) are well-presented. 

3. **Comprehensive Error Analysis:**  The error analysis (Section 5) is a strong point of the paper.  Identifying specific error types (pronoun resolution, annotation discrepancies) provides valuable insights into the limitations of the model and suggests clear directions for future research. The discussion of false negatives and their link to challenging linguistic phenomena strengthens the analysis and demonstrates a deep understanding of the task.

4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies (Table 5) to assess the contribution of different feature classes. The comparison to baseline methods (Table 6) and the analysis of different clustering strategies (Table 4) further validate the proposed approach and demonstrate its robustness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter optimization, it lacks sufficient detail about the process. The specific search space and criteria used for selecting the final hyperparameters are not clearly outlined.  Providing more details on this aspect would enhance reproducibility and allow readers to better understand the robustness of the reported results.

2. **Dataset Bias and Generalizability:** The reliance on the ACE dataset, while standard, raises concerns about generalizability. The ACE corpus is relatively small and focused on specific event types. The authors acknowledge this to some extent by using ACE++, but further evaluation on more diverse datasets would strengthen the claims about the model's adaptability to different domains.

3. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Neural Methods:** The related work section doesn't extensively cover recent advancements in neural network architectures for relation/event extraction (e.g., transformers). A comparison with these more recent methods would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach's competitiveness and identify potential areas for improvement. While mentioning recursive and recurrent networks, the discussion lacks depth and a direct comparative analysis.

4. **Clustering Strategy Sensitivity:** The results indicate the clustering strategy (BestLink vs. transitive closure) significantly impacts performance. The justification for the chosen strategy (transitive closure) relies on the observation that the model already weeds out low-confidence links, which feels somewhat post-hoc. Further investigation into why BestLink performs worse on ACE++ would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more details regarding the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and selection criteria?
2. Have you explored the use of more recent neural network architectures, such as transformers, for generating sentential features? If so, how do they compare to the CNN-based approach presented in the paper?
3. Could you elaborate on why BestLink resulted in decreased performance on ACE++ compared to transitive closure?  Were there specific error patterns observed with BestLink that contributed to this outcome?
4. What specific challenges do you foresee in adapting this model to datasets with significantly different event types or domain characteristics than ACE?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 is helpful for visualizing the model architecture.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   The provided code link for the datasets is appreciated.
*   The discussion of related work is reasonable but could be more extensive, particularly regarding recent advancements in neural networks.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a novel and effective approach to event linking. The strong empirical results, comprehensive error analysis, and detailed experimental setup make it a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the identified weaknesses would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
