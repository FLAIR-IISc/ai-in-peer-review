## Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages sentential features extracted via convolutional neural networks, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, including a publicly available one, and provide an error analysis.

**Summary and Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that competitive event linking performance can be achieved *without* relying on external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors achieve this by employing CNNs to encode sentential context surrounding event mentions, combining these representations with lexical and pairwise features, and training a similarity function for coreference scoring. A secondary contribution is the empirical analysis showcasing the impact of different feature combinations and clustering strategies. The error analysis, while relatively brief, highlights challenges related to pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, pointing to directions for future research. Finally, the release of experimental setups (dataset splits) is a valuable contribution to reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Elimination of External Resources:** The core strength of this work is its ability to achieve strong performance without dependence on potentially domain-specific or unavailable external knowledge. This improves the portability and applicability of the system across diverse domains. This is a significant step forward, as many previous approaches were limited by the availability of these resources.
2. **Effective Use of CNNs for Sentential Encoding:** The use of CNNs to capture sentential context is well-motivated and appears to be effective. The architecture, described with sufficient detail, allows for learning representations relevant to event coreference. The inclusion of position embeddings alongside word embeddings is a sensible addition to provide the model with positional information relative to the trigger.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE dataset and competitive performance on the ACE++ dataset. The ablation studies (Table 5) provide valuable insights into the contribution of each feature class.
4. **Thorough Experimentation and Analysis:** The paper includes a reasonably thorough set of experiments, exploring different configurations and feature combinations. The error analysis, while limited in scope, offers some useful insights into the model’s weaknesses. The comparison to several baselines and related work is also appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the CNN Architecture:** While the application of CNNs to this specific task is valuable, the CNN architecture itself is relatively standard. The paper does not introduce any significant innovations in the CNN design itself. The novelty primarily lies in the *application* of existing techniques to the event linking problem, rather than introducing fundamentally new methodologies.
2. **Shallow Error Analysis:** The error analysis, although helpful, is limited to 100 false positives and 100 false negatives. This sample size is relatively small and might not fully capture the spectrum of errors the model makes. Furthermore, the analysis primarily focuses on identifying challenging cases (pronoun resolution, annotator disagreement) but doesn't provide quantitative insights into the *frequency* of different error types.
3. **Lack of Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, it lacks a discussion of the sensitivity of the model’s performance to different hyperparameter settings. Providing some insights into the robustness of the model would increase confidence in the results. The table listing hyperparameter settings (Table 2) is difficult to parse.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The ACE dataset is relatively small. While the authors augment it with ACE++, the limited size and domain specificity of both datasets might restrict the generalizability of the findings. A more diverse evaluation on larger, more representative datasets would strengthen the conclusions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper states that the “square” function is used as the non-linearity in the similarity layer because of its invariance to polarity. Could you elaborate on why you believe polarity invariance is important in this context? Does it relate to the event types being symmetric or asymmetric?
2.  Regarding the error analysis, could you provide more quantitative data about the distribution of error types (e.g., percentage of errors due to pronoun resolution, annotator disagreement, etc.)?
3.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to different word embedding initializations? Did you experiment with different pre-trained embeddings or consider fine-tuning the embeddings during training?
4. You mention potential benefits from incorporating discourse-level information. Have you considered any initial experiments utilizing discourse parsing or rhetorical structure theory to enhance the model?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 2 could be improved for readability.
*   The citation style appears inconsistent in places.
*   The claim of “state-of-the-art” performance should be qualified, considering the limitations of the ACE dataset and the challenges associated with comparing results across different experimental setups.
* Please clarify the implementation details of the transitive closure clustering, particularly how ties are broken when determining which events to merge.
