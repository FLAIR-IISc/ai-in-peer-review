

# Peer Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event coreference resolution (linking) that avoids reliance on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. Instead, it uses convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features from event mentions and their contexts, combined with lexical and pairwise features for coreference decisions. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a system using CNNs to generate sentential features for event mentions, (2) a model that achieves state-of-the-art performance without external semantic features, and (3) a publicly available ACE++ dataset.

From my perspective, the most significant contribution is the demonstration that external semantic resources are unnecessary for high-performance event linking, which addresses a major practical limitation of previous approaches. The second most important contribution is the empirical validation that sentential features from CNNs are sufficient for event linking. The ACE++ dataset is a valuable but less novel contribution.

## Strengths

1. **Practical Significance of Eliminating External Resources**: The paper makes a compelling argument for the practical benefits of eliminating reliance on domain-specific external resources. Previous state-of-the-art systems (e.g., Liu et al., 2014) used "a rich set of 105 semantic features" from external sources, which are expensive to create and have limited cross-domain applicability. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach achieves comparable performance (BLANC F1: 73.31% vs. 70.43%) without these resources, which is a substantial practical contribution for real-world deployment.

2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors conduct ablation studies (Table 5) showing that each feature class contributes positively to performance, and they carefully analyze error patterns (Section 5.3). The comparison against multiple baselines and the inclusion of multiple metrics (BLANC, MUC, B-CUBED) are appropriate given the task's characteristics (high negative link prevalence). Their choice to use gold event mentions (rather than propagated extraction results) is justified to isolate the event linking task.

3. **ACE++ Dataset Contribution**: The creation of ACE++ (1950 documents vs. ACE's 599) is a valuable resource for the community, especially since the ACE 2005 corpus is small. The paper's demonstration that model performance improves with more training data (Table 4) validates the importance of larger datasets for this task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Addressing of Pronoun Resolution Challenge**: The error analysis reveals that a significant portion of false negatives involves pronoun resolution (e.g., "It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me"), which is a fundamental challenge in coreference resolution. The paper acknowledges this but fails to discuss how their model might be extended to handle such cases, which represents a major limitation. Given that pronouns are a common coreference challenge in all coreference tasks, this is a critical gap that should be addressed.

2. **Outdated Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims "state-of-the-art performance" but compares only against systems from 2010-2014 (Liu et al., 2014; Bejan and Harabagiu, 2010). Since then, neural coreference models have advanced significantly (e.g., Lee et al., 2017; Clark and Manning, 2016), and these newer approaches would likely outperform the cited systems. The paper does not compare against more recent neural coreference models, making it difficult to assess the true novelty and competitiveness of their approach.

3. **Inadequate Discussion of Neural Architecture Choices**: The paper uses CNNs for feature extraction but doesn't adequately justify this choice over other neural architectures that have shown promise for coreference tasks (e.g., LSTMs, attention-based models). The related work section (Section 6) cites some neural approaches but doesn't discuss why CNNs were chosen over alternatives, or how their approach would compare to more modern neural architectures.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper identifies pronoun resolution as a major error type (17% of false negatives), which is a well-known challenge in coreference resolution. How would you modify your model to specifically address this limitation, and what additional features or architectural changes would you propose?

2. The paper compares against systems from 2010-2014 but doesn't address more recent neural coreference models (e.g., those using BERT or other contextual embeddings). How would your approach compare to these more recent systems, and would your sentential feature approach still be beneficial in the context of pre-trained language models?

3. The authors use pre-trained Word2Vec embeddings, which are a form of external resource. How does this differ from the external semantic resources they claim to eliminate (e.g., WordNet), and why is this acceptable in their framework?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that external semantic resources are unnecessary for high-performance event linking, with a well-designed model and thorough evaluation. However, the limited discussion of the pronoun resolution challenge and outdated comparison to state-of-the-art are significant weaknesses that need addressing. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address these points, particularly regarding the comparison to more recent neural coreference models and the pronoun resolution limitation. The paper's practical contribution and ACE++ dataset make it a strong candidate for CONLL, but the weaknesses must be resolved to fully establish its significance in the current research landscape.
