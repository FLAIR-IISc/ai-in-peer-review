

# Peer Review for "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to event coreference resolution that eliminates reliance on external semantic resources (WordNet, FrameNet, etc.) by leveraging sentential features extracted via convolutional neural networks. The authors design a two-stage model: first, a CNN generates latent representations of individual event mentions by processing their surrounding sentences; second, these representations are combined with pairwise features to compute coreference scores. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A novel event linking model that achieves state-of-the-art performance without requiring external semantic resources, demonstrating that domain-specific semantic knowledge is not necessary for high-performance event coreference resolution.
2. A detailed analysis showing that sentential features from CNNs are sufficient for capturing event-related context, with empirical evidence demonstrating the contribution of different feature classes.
3. An error analysis that identifies specific, meaningful challenges (particularly pronoun resolution) for future research, providing concrete directions for improvement.

## Strengths

1. **Practical significance of addressing the external resource dependency**: The authors compellingly demonstrate that previous approaches are restricted to domains with available external semantic resources, creating a significant barrier for cross-domain application. Their model eliminates this constraint while maintaining state-of-the-art performance, which is a major practical contribution for real-world information extraction systems. The paper's empirical evidence (23% of event mentions require cross-sentential resolution in ACE 2005) strongly justifies the importance of the problem.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), with ACE++ being publicly available. The ablation study (Table 5) provides convincing evidence that all feature classes contribute meaningfully to performance, and the analysis of clustering strategies (Table 4) shows thoughtful consideration of implementation details. The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art performance" is well-supported by the comparison to multiple previous approaches in Table 3.

3. **Insightful error analysis**: The error analysis goes beyond typical superficial error categorization by identifying specific challenges like pronoun resolution as a key limitation. The examples provided (e.g., "It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me") demonstrate a deep understanding of the problem's nuances. The observation that 17% of false negatives were cases where human annotators made inconsistent decisions adds valuable context to the performance metrics.

4. **Clear model architecture and justification**: The paper provides a well-structured explanation of the model's design choices, particularly the rationale for using sentential features (55.42% of coreferential mentions are more than five sentences apart, making locality-based heuristics ineffective). The architecture diagram (Figure 1) and detailed description of the CNN processing make the approach accessible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inadequate comparison to previous work due to experimental inconsistencies**: The authors acknowledge that "the exact documents in the partitions vary from system to system" and that "published methods follow different strategies regarding preprocessing components," but they don't sufficiently address how these differences affect the comparison. For example, Liu et al. (2014) uses a semantic-role labeler while the authors use gold-annotated arguments, which could significantly impact results. Without a fair comparison under identical experimental conditions, the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is weakened.

2. **Misleading claim about external resource dependency**: The paper claims the model "does not rely on external semantic features," but the authors use pre-trained word embeddings (from Mikolov et al., 2013), which are a form of external semantic resource. This undermines their main contribution, as previous approaches also used pre-trained embeddings. The paper needs to clarify this distinction and better position their contribution as eliminating *domain-specific* external resources rather than *all* external resources.

3. **Insufficient justification for neural approach over simpler methods**: While the authors show that removing features (Table 5) degrades performance, they don't compare against non-neural approaches that might use more sophisticated lexical features. The claim that "neural models for generating latent-feature representations for sentences" are necessary isn't sufficiently justified, especially since they acknowledge that 50.97% of coreferential pairs share no arguments (suggesting lexical features alone might not be sufficient).

4. **Lack of discussion on scalability and domain transfer**: The paper demonstrates performance on ACE++ (a slight extension of ACE), but doesn't address how the model would perform on completely new domains or document types. The claim that "low linking performance, coming from a lack of semantic knowledge about a domain, is evitable" isn't substantiated by evidence of cross-domain performance.

5. **Limited discussion of computational complexity**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of their approach compared to previous methods. Given that the model uses multiple neural network components, this is an important practical consideration that should be mentioned.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you modify your model to handle the pronoun resolution problem identified in your error analysis? What specific changes to the architecture or training procedure would you implement?

2. The paper uses pre-trained word embeddings, which are external semantic resources. How do you distinguish between "acceptable" external resources (like word embeddings) and "unacceptable" ones (like WordNet)? What criteria would you use to determine which external resources are permissible for this task?

3. The ACE++ dataset was created by adding web documents. How would you expect your model to perform on a completely different domain (e.g., medical documents) without additional training data or adaptation?
