

Summary of Paper: The paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event linking (resolving coreferences between event mentions in documents) that relies solely on sentential features generated from convolutional neural networks (CNNs), without requiring external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The model processes event mentions and their context through two CNN-based steps: first generating event-mention representations, then making coreference decisions based on these representations combined with lexical and pairwise features. The authors report state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++), with the latter being publicly available.

Main Contributions (ordered by strength):
1. A novel event linking model that eliminates reliance on external semantic features while achieving state-of-the-art performance.
2. Empirical demonstration that sentential features from CNNs are sufficient for high-quality event linking, without domain-specific knowledge resources.
3. An error analysis identifying specific challenges (e.g., pronoun resolution) that could guide future research.

Strengths:
1. The model's elimination of dependency on external semantic resources represents a significant technical contribution. Previous approaches (Bejan and Harabagiu, 2010; Liu et al., 2014) rely on features from resources like WordNet and FrameNet, which have limited domain coverage and require substantial effort to create. The authors successfully demonstrate that their CNN-based approach achieves comparable or better performance without these resources, making the method more broadly applicable across domains.

2. The comprehensive ablation study (Table 5) provides strong evidence for the contribution of each feature class. Removing sentential features (row 3 vs. row 2) results in significant performance drops, demonstrating their critical role. The study also shows that pairwise features alone (row 1) perform poorly, while adding sentential features (row 3) substantially improves both precision and recall. This careful analysis strengthens the authors' claim about the necessity of sentential features.

3. The paper presents a thorough error analysis (Section 5.3) that goes beyond typical performance metrics. The authors identify specific failure modes, including cases where pronoun resolution to event mentions is required (a known challenging problem), and note that 22% of false positives were plausible to human raters. This analysis provides concrete directions for future research and helps contextualize the model's limitations.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison to prior work (Table 3) suffers from methodological inconsistencies. Different systems use different train/test splits and preprocessing approaches, making direct comparison difficult. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide a clear way to account for these differences in their evaluation. For example, Liu et al. (2014) use gold-annotated event mentions while others use semantic-role labelers, which affects the comparability of results.

2. The paper lacks sufficient detail about the model architecture and hyperparameters to ensure reproducibility. While Table 2 lists some hyperparameters, it doesn't provide enough information about the CNN architecture (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters, hidden layer dimensions), training procedures, or computational resources required. This is particularly problematic for a neural network-based approach where small architectural choices can significantly impact results.

3. The creation of the ACE++ dataset (Section 4) raises concerns about dataset bias. The authors create ACE++ by adding 1,351 web documents to the original ACE 2005 corpus. However, the paper doesn't provide details about the annotation process for these additional documents, nor does it analyze whether they differ significantly from the ACE 2005 documents. This could introduce bias that affects the generalizability of the results.

4. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why their approach outperforms previous methods that use external features. While they claim their model achieves state-of-the-art results without external features, they don't provide a clear analysis of the specific advantages of their sentential features over the semantic features used in prior work. The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a key challenge, but doesn't connect this to the specific strengths of their model.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the quality of the ACE++ web documents compare to the original ACE 2005 documents in terms of annotation consistency and domain characteristics?
2. Can you provide more details about the CNN architecture (e.g., number of filters, filter sizes, hidden layer dimensions) to facilitate reproducibility?
3. In your error analysis, you mention pronoun resolution as a key challenge. How might the model be modified to better handle pronoun-based coreference in event linking?
