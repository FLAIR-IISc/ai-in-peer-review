- Proposal: novel event coreference approach that eliminates external semantic resources by using CNNs to generate sentential features.  
- Model: two-step method—CNNs produce latent representations from mentions and context, then combine with pairwise features for coreference scoring.  
- Results: reports state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++; ACE++ is publicly released.  
- Primary contribution: demonstrates external semantic resources are unnecessary for high-performance event linking, improving domain applicability.  
- Secondary contribution: creation and release of ACE++, a larger dataset (1950 documents) to enhance training.  
- Tertiary contribution: error analysis that flags pronoun resolution as a major challenge.  
- Strength: shows CNN-derived sentential features can replace external resources without performance loss (Table 3).  
- Strength: ablation study (Table 5) rigorously validates the necessity of each feature class.  
- Strength: error analysis identifies clear limitations and directions for future work.  
- Weakness: comparisons to prior work are undermined by inconsistent data splits and preprocessing (e.g., gold arguments vs. not).  
- Weakness: ACE++ creation lacks sufficient annotation detail, raising concerns about consistency with ACE 2005.  
- Weakness: insufficient explanation of how sentential features capture the semantic information provided by external resources.  
- Question: how is domain independence quantified relative to methods that use external resources?  
- Question: what specific annotation guidelines were used for ACE++ to ensure consistency with ACE 2005?  
- Question: can you provide concrete examples where the model outperforms prior approaches despite lacking external semantic features?
