

This paper proposes a novel approach to event coreference resolution that eliminates reliance on external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet) by using convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features for event mentions. The authors present a two-step model: first, CNNs process event mentions and their contexts to generate latent representations; second, these representations are combined with pairwise features for coreference scoring. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on two datasets (ACE 2005 and their newly created ACE++), with ACE++ being publicly available.

The primary contribution is demonstrating that external semantic features are unnecessary for high-performance event linking, significantly broadening the applicability of such systems across domains. The secondary contribution is the creation and release of ACE++, a substantially larger dataset (1950 documents) that enhances the training of event linking models. The tertiary contribution is the insightful error analysis highlighting pronoun resolution as a key challenge.

Strengths: The paper convincingly shows that sentential features from CNNs can replace external semantic resources without performance loss (Table 3), which is a major practical contribution. The ablation study (Table 5) rigorously demonstrates the necessity of each feature class, providing strong evidence for their model design. The error analysis identifies meaningful limitations (e.g., pronoun resolution failures), offering clear directions for future work.

Weaknesses: The comparison to prior work is undermined by inconsistent data splits and preprocessing (e.g., some systems use gold arguments while others don't), making it difficult to fully validate the "state-of-the-art" claim. The ACE++ dataset creation lacks sufficient annotation details, raising concerns about its consistency with the original ACE 2005 corpus. The paper doesn't adequately explain how sentential features capture the same semantic information as external resources, which is crucial for understanding the model's novelty.

Questions to Authors: How do you quantify the domain independence of your approach compared to previous methods that require external resources? What specific annotation guidelines were followed for ACE++ to ensure consistency with ACE 2005? Can you provide examples of event linking scenarios where your model outperforms previous approaches despite the absence of external semantic features?
