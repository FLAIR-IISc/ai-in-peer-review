

# Review for "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary
The paper addresses event coreference resolution (event linking), which determines whether different event mentions in a document refer to the same real-world event. Previous approaches relied heavily on external semantic resources like WordNet and FrameNet, limiting their applicability to domains where such resources exist. The authors propose a model using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features from the context of event mentions, eliminating the need for external semantic features. The model has two components: (1) a CNN-based representation of individual event mentions using their sentence context, and (2) a coreference scoring mechanism that combines these representations with pairwise features. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, with one being publicly available, and provides an error analysis identifying pronoun resolution as a key challenge.

## Main Contributions
1. Demonstrating that high-performance event coreference resolution can be achieved without relying on external semantic resources, which is a significant contribution to the field as it removes a major barrier for applying such systems in new domains.
2. Providing a thorough ablation study showing that each component of the model (sentential features, trigger-local lexical features, pairwise features) contributes meaningfully to the overall performance.
3. Offering a valuable error analysis that identifies pronoun resolution as a major challenge for current approaches, providing clear directions for future research.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a compelling case for the need to move away from external semantic resources. The authors effectively demonstrate that 23% of event mentions in ACE 2005 require cross-sentence resolution, and that 50.97% of coreferential event pairs share no arguments while 47.29% have different trigger words. This strong empirical foundation justifies their approach and highlights a genuine limitation in previous work.

2. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance without using external semantic resources, which is a significant contribution. The results in Table 3 show that the model outperforms Liu et al. (2014) in most metrics (BLANC, MUC, positive links) despite Liu et al.'s use of 105 semantic features. This demonstrates that high performance is possible without the domain restrictions imposed by external resources.

3. The ablation study in Table 5 provides strong evidence for the importance of each feature class. The results clearly show that removing any feature class (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, sentential) leads to performance degradation, supporting the authors' claim that their multi-feature approach is essential for success. This is particularly convincing as the authors show that even removing just the sentential features (while keeping pairwise features) leads to a significant drop in performance.

4. The error analysis is thorough and insightful. The authors identify pronoun resolution as a major challenge (e.g., "It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me."), which is a difficult problem even for human annotators. The fact that 17% of false negatives involve pronoun resolution and that 22% of false positives represent boundary cases where human annotators disagree provides valuable context for interpreting the results and highlights a clear direction for future work.

## Weaknesses
1. The comparison with state-of-the-art systems has significant caveats that undermine the strength of the claim that their model achieves "state-of-the-art" performance. The authors acknowledge that the exact documents in train/test partitions vary across systems, and that different preprocessing strategies are used (e.g., Liu et al. uses a semantic-role labeler while the authors use gold-annotated event mentions). This makes it difficult to definitively claim that their model is better than Liu et al.'s, as the comparison might be affected by these differences rather than the model itself.

2. The paper doesn't provide a clear comparison with other neural network approaches for event coreference resolution. While the authors mention recent neural work for relation/event extraction, they don't compare their CNN-based approach to other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs, recursive networks) that might be better suited for sequential data. This omission is significant given the growing interest in neural approaches for NLP tasks.

3. The ACE++ dataset is created by adding 1351 web documents to ACE 2005, but the paper lacks details about how these web documents were annotated and whether they follow the same annotation guidelines as ACE 2005. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the quality and consistency of the new data, which is crucial for the reported performance gains.

4. The paper doesn't discuss computational efficiency compared to previous approaches. Liu et al. (2014) uses a random forest classifier with 105 semantic features, while the authors' model requires training a neural network. It would be valuable to know whether the authors' model is more or less computationally expensive to train and deploy, especially since resource constraints are often a consideration for real-world applications.

5. The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a major challenge, but the paper doesn't discuss how the model could be extended to address this issue. The authors mention this as a direction for future work, but without some preliminary ideas about how to incorporate pronoun resolution into the model, the error analysis feels somewhat disconnected from the proposed solution.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your model compare to other neural network approaches for event coreference resolution, such as those using RNNs or recursive networks? Have you considered using these architectures instead of CNNs, and if so, why did you choose CNNs?

2. Can you provide more details about the creation of the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, how were the 1351 web documents annotated, and how do you ensure they follow the same annotation guidelines as ACE 2005?

3. The paper claims state-of-the-art performance, but the comparison with Liu et al. (2014) has caveats regarding different train/test splits and preprocessing. How confident are you that the performance difference is due to the model rather than these other factors?

4. The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a key challenge. What are your preliminary thoughts on how to extend the model to better handle pronoun resolution, and how would this integrate with the current architecture?

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that high-performance event coreference resolution can be achieved without external semantic resources, which is a significant step toward more domain-agnostic event processing systems. The model's performance and the thorough error analysis provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach. With some clarification on the dataset creation process and a more comprehensive comparison with other neural approaches, this work would be even stronger.
