Summary
The paper addresses within-document event coreference (event linking) and proposes a two-stage neural model that avoids external knowledge bases. First, a convolutional neural network produces an event-mention representation using sentence-level context with position embeddings and piecewise max-pooling relative to the trigger, augmented by trigger-local lexical embeddings. Second, a pairwise decision component combines two mention representations with hand-designed pairwise features (type compatibility, distance, modality/realis match, and argument overlap) and a squared nonlinearity-based similarity layer, followed by logistic regression. Pairwise decisions are clustered via transitive closure. Experiments on ACE 2005 and an enlarged ACE++ dataset show competitive or state-of-the-art results without external semantic resources. Ablations quantify the contributions of sentential, lexical, and pairwise features; an error analysis highlights limitations such as pronoun-event coreference.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical finding that sentence-level CNN features, combined with simple pairwise cues, can match or surpass prior state-of-the-art event coreference systems that rely on external semantic resources, on ACE 2005, and generalize well with more training data (ACE++).
2) A concrete neural architecture for event-mention representation (piecewise pooling around triggers) and a similarity function using a squared activation that supports multiple similarity facets.
3) A thorough experimental study including ablations, clustering strategy comparison, baselines, and error analysis; plus a new larger dataset (ACE++) used to demonstrate scalability (though release status is unclear).

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated modeling of sentential context: The data analysis shows that many coreferent pairs either lack shared arguments (≈51%) or have different triggers (≈47%), and are often distant (>5 sentences). This supports leveraging sentence-level semantics beyond trigger matching. The CNN with position embeddings and piecewise pooling is a reasonable, well-explained design to encode such features.
- Competitive performance without external KBs: On ACE, the model attains higher BLANC, MUC, and positive-link F1 than Liu et al. (2014), despite using no WordNet/FrameNet/VerbOcean-type features. This demonstrates the viability of KB-free event linking, potentially improving portability across domains.
- Strong ablations and analysis: The study isolates the contributions of pairwise vs. trigger-local lexical vs. sentential features (Table 5), showing that sentential features materially improve both precision and recall. The BestLink vs. transitive-closure comparison (Table 4) is informative: the model’s classifier already filters low-confidence links, so BestLink harms recall. Baselines (all-singletons, one-instance, same-type) are included and interpreted appropriately.
- Attention to evaluation methodology: The paper prioritizes BLANC due to class imbalance, yet reports MUC, B-CUBED, and positive-link metrics for completeness. Pair generation and clustering are precisely specified, and ACE split lists are provided, aiding reproducibility.
- Informative error analysis: The analysis identifies pronoun-event coreference and annotation inconsistencies as major sources of error, which contextualizes the task’s difficulty and frames future work.

Weaknesses
- Unclear use of gold arguments and fairness of comparison: The model includes “argument overlap” and modality/realis features. It is not explicitly stated whether these use gold argument annotations on ACE. The paper notes that some prior work did not use gold arguments. If gold argument features are used here, the comparison may be optimistic and not reflective of end-to-end performance. Results without gold-argument features are needed.
- Limited novelty in the core mechanism: Employing CNN-based sentential encoders for mention representation and a pairwise classifier with standard features is an incremental adaptation from relation/event extraction to coreference. The clustering via transitive closure is standard. The squared activation for similarity is a minor design choice with limited theoretical justification beyond polarity invariance.
- Evaluation caveats and statistical rigor: Although the paper acknowledges split differences on ACE, the SOTA claim rests on non-identical splits and modest gains. No variance over multiple random initializations or significance tests are reported. This limits confidence in the robustness of improvements.
- ACE++ dataset transparency and availability: ACE++ adds 1351 web documents “annotated in an analogous fashion,” but annotation procedures, quality control, inter-annotator agreement, and release status are not described. Improvements driven by additional data are compelling, yet not verifiable without public release or detailed documentation.
- Practicality and scope: The system assumes gold triggers and focuses on within-document linking; it does not evaluate with predicted triggers/arguments or in an end-to-end pipeline, where upstream errors can materially affect linking. Despite many cross-sentence/distant links, the model’s context is sentence-local; document-level discourse structure is not modeled, and pronoun-event coreference remains unaddressed beyond observation.

Questions to Authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- Do the pairwise “argument overlap” and modality/realis features use gold argument/realis annotations on ACE? If yes, can you report performance without any gold argument/realis features to gauge a more realistic setting and ensure fair comparison to Liu et al. (2014)?
- What is the release status of ACE++? Please detail annotation guidelines, annotator training, inter-annotator agreement, domains covered, and whether the data (or at least features/labels) will be released.
- Did you run multiple training seeds and conduct statistical significance testing on the main ACE results? Please provide mean and variance.
- Please provide the final hyperparameters (dw, dp, dc, de, dsim, n, regularization, dropout rates) actually used; Table 2 is referenced but the concrete values are not visible here.
- How are multiple event mentions in the same sentence handled during piecewise pooling when triggers are adjacent or overlapping? Any ambiguity resolution needed?

Additional comments
- An end-to-end evaluation with predicted triggers/arguments would strengthen the practical impact claims.
- Comparing to recurrent encoders (e.g., BiLSTMs) or alternative similarity functions could validate the squared activation choice.
- Reporting runtime/complexity and memory usage would help assess scalability.
