Summary
The paper addresses within-document event coreference (event linking) without relying on external semantic resources. It proposes a two-part neural model: (a) a sentence-level CNN with position embeddings and piecewise max pooling to produce an event-mention representation anchored at the trigger, concatenated with trigger-local lexical features; (b) a pairwise scorer that ingests two mention representations plus engineered pairwise features (event type, distance, modality, and argument overlap) and outputs a coreference score via a squared nonlinearity and logistic classifier. Pairwise links are clustered via transitive closure. The model is evaluated on ACE 2005 and an extended ACE++ collection, reporting state-of-the-art or competitive performance, feature ablations, clustering comparisons, and an error analysis.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that sentential features learned by a CNN can replace external KB-dependent semantic features for event coreference, achieving SOTA/competitive results on ACE.
2) A careful experimental study isolating the impact of sentential, trigger-local lexical, and pairwise features, as well as clustering strategies and training data size, with useful insights for practitioners.
3) Construction of a larger ACE++ dataset and associated analyses (though not publicly released).

Strengths
- Strong empirical results with reduced dependency on external resources. The model matches or exceeds prior work that leverages 100+ semantic features and KBs, especially on BLANC, suggesting the approach is more portable across domains lacking rich resources.
- Clear, modular architecture with sensible design choices. The piecewise pooling around triggers and position embeddings are well motivated and align with successes in relation extraction. The pairwise scorer learns multi-faceted similarity and outperforms cosine/other activations.
- Thorough ablations and analyses. The feature ablation convincingly shows sentential features add substantial value beyond pairwise and trigger-local lexical features. The data-size experiment (ACE → ACE++) quantifies recall gains, and the BestLink vs transitive-closure comparison is informative.
- Useful dataset-characterization and error analysis. Statistics about argument overlap, trigger mismatch, and distance motivate modeling choices. The qualitative error analysis highlights challenging pronominal event anaphora and annotation ambiguities—important for future work.
- Practicality and simplicity. No reliance on costly, domain-limited resources; uses standard embeddings and basic pairwise features; implemented in TensorFlow; method is computationally straightforward (pairwise scoring + simple clustering).

Weaknesses
- Reliance on gold annotations for key features likely inflates performance and complicates comparison. The paper notes that all systems use gold triggers on ACE, but pairwise features include event type agreement, realis, and argument overlap. It appears these are derived from gold labels; if so, the evaluation overestimates real-world performance and is not directly comparable to systems using predicted arguments/types. At minimum, report results with predicted types/arguments (or omit such features) to quantify sensitivity.
- Limited novelty beyond applying a CNN to the mention encoder; heavy use of engineered pairwise features. While replacing external KBs is valuable, the coreference decision still depends on hand-crafted, schema-specific features. The model does not explicitly model argument compatibility within the neural encoder (e.g., no joint encoding of triggers and their arguments), nor document-level context.
- Sentence-local encoding for a document-level problem. Many positive links are >5 sentences apart, yet the encoder is strictly intra-sentential. Gains for long-distance links likely come from non-neural pairwise features (type, modality, distance). An analysis by distance and by trigger/type agreement would clarify where the sentential representation helps.
- Reproducibility and dataset concerns. ACE++ is not public; annotation process, quality control, schema alignment, and IAA are not reported. Without release, results on ACE++ are hard to verify. Some training details are underspecified in the submission (final hyperparameters, regularization, seeds). Also, only a subset of coreference metrics is reported; CEAF and CoNLL F1 would aid comparability.
- Clustering is purely heuristic. Transitive closure can magnify local errors; no global inference or learning-to-cluster is attempted. The conclusion that transitive closure beats BestLink could be threshold-dependent; broader analysis (e.g., learning thresholds or using global objectives) would strengthen the claim.

Questions to authors
- Are event types, realis, and argument spans/roles used in pairwise features gold or predicted? Please report performance with predicted values, and an ablation that removes these features to isolate the contribution of the CNN representation.
- How was ACE++ annotated? Who annotated, with what guidelines, and what inter-annotator agreement was achieved? Will the dataset (or at least splits and annotation tools) be released?
- Provide exact hyperparameters (dw, dp, dc, n, de, dsim), regularization, and early-stopping criteria. How sensitive are results to random seeds?
- Can you report bucketed performance by (i) trigger identity agreement vs mismatch, (ii) type agreement vs mismatch, (iii) argument overlap vs none, and (iv) sentence distance, to substantiate the claim that sentential features capture more than trigger/type cues?
- Why choose the squared activation in the similarity layer over standard element-wise product/absolute difference concatenation followed by an MLP? Any empirical comparisons?
- Please include CEAF_e/phi4 and CoNLL F1; also report cluster properties (average cluster size, singleton rate) after clustering.

Additional comments
- Consider augmenting the encoder with argument-aware representations (e.g., attention to entity mentions, dependency-path encodings) to internalize argument compatibility without gold features.
- Document-level encoders (e.g., hierarchical BiLSTMs or transformers over sentences) could provide context for long-distance linking and pronominal event anaphora.
- Discuss computational cost and candidate pruning; the pairwise strategy is O(n^2) in mentions per document.
