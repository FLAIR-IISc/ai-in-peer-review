Summary: The paper tackles within-document event coreference (“event linking”) without external semantic resources by learning sentential representations with CNNs. Each event mention is encoded from its sentence via piecewise max-pooled convolutions plus trigger-local lexical features; a learned similarity module with pairwise cues (e.g., type agreement, distance, argument overlap) produces a link score, followed by transitive-closure clustering. On ACE, the model attains state-of-the-art BLANC F1 and competitive results on other metrics; on a larger ACE++ variant, ablations show sentential features are crucial. An error analysis highlights hard cases (e.g., pronominal events).

Contributions (as assessed):
1) Empirical demonstration that sentential CNN features, combined with lightweight pairwise cues, can match or exceed prior state-of-the-art event linking that depended on external KBs, improving portability and domain robustness.
2) Systematic empirical analysis: dataset diagnostics motivating design (argument sparsity, trigger mismatch, long-distance links), ablations isolating the value of sentential vs lexical vs pairwise features, and comparison of clustering strategies (transitive closure vs BestLink).
3) A practical, simple architecture (including a learned multi-dimensional similarity with square activation) that is effective and easy to deploy.

Strengths:
- Strong performance without external knowledge: Achieves best BLANC F1 on ACE while eschewing WordNet/FrameNet, suggesting broader applicability where KBs are unavailable.
- Convincing ablations and diagnostics: Table 5 shows performance drops when removing sentential features; analysis of data properties (e.g., 51% positive pairs share no arguments; 47% have different triggers) credibly motivates the modeling choices.
- Sensible clustering study: Contrary to prior reports, BestLink hurts recall here; the comparison is informative and tied to the classifier’s calibration.
- Meaningful error analysis: Identifies pronominal events and annotation inconsistencies as major sources of errors, contextualizing ceiling effects in this task.
- Reproducibility details: Clear training setup, hyperparameters, and published splits for ACE; use of pre-trained embeddings.

Weaknesses:
- Reliance on gold triggers (and likely gold arguments for pairwise features) limits ecological validity; no end-to-end assessment with predicted mentions.
- Comparability caveats: Different ACE splits and preprocessing across papers make “state-of-the-art” claims less definitive; no statistical significance tests.
- Context limitation: Encoding only the sentence of each mention may miss discourse cues critical for long-distance links noted in the analysis.
- Simple transitive-closure clustering can propagate erroneous links and over-merge; no global consistency or joint inference.
- ACE++ release/annotation details are unclear, limiting verification and broader impact.

Questions to Authors:
- Do pairwise features use gold arguments? How does performance change with predicted triggers/arguments?
- Will ACE++ be released, and what are its annotation guidelines/quality controls?
- Did you test significance across runs/splits? How sensitive are results to thresholding and to the square activation choice?
- Can the model incorporate multi-sentence context (e.g., document encoders) without external resources, and does that help on long-distance cases?
