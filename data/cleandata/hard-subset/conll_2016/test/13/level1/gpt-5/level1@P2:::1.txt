Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses within-document event coreference (“event linking”) and proposes a neural mention-pair model that replaces external semantic resources with sentential features learned by CNNs. The architecture builds an event-mention representation from the sentence (with word and relative-position embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling, and a hidden layer), augments two mention representations with pairwise features (type compatibility, distance, modality/realis, argument overlap), and scores coreference via a learned squared-similarity layer and logistic regression. Pairwise decisions are clustered via transitive closure. Experiments on ACE2005 and an expanded ACE++ dataset show state-of-the-art or on-par results, and ablations analyze the contribution of sentential features. An error analysis highlights challenges (pronoun resolution, annotation inconsistencies).

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength
1) Demonstration that CNN-based sentential features, without external KB resources (WordNet/FrameNet), can match state-of-the-art event linking on ACE. This is significant because prior best systems rely on many external semantic features with limited domain portability.
2) A clear ablation showing that sentential features add complementary signal beyond trigger-local lexical and pairwise features for event coreference.
3) An empirical comparison of clustering strategies (transitive closure vs BestLink) in this neural setting, with evidence that the classifier is strong enough to render BestLink unnecessary.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance without external semantic resources: The model achieves comparable or better F1 than Liu et al. (2014) on BLANC, MUC, and positive-link metrics while eschewing WordNet/FrameNet-like resources. This supports the core claim of domain portability and reduces the engineering burden of integrating KB-dependent features. The ablation results further substantiate that the learned sentence features are doing real work.

- Solid and transparent modeling choices motivated by data analysis: The paper analyzes ACE++ to show that (a) many coreferential pairs have different triggers and (b) many share no arguments, and that pairs are often far apart. These findings justify modeling sentence-level context (not just trigger identity or argument overlap) and favor a robust mention representation. The piecewise pooling around the trigger is a reasonable adaptation from relation extraction to handle multiple mentions per sentence.

- Careful ablation and baseline coverage: The ablations isolate contributions of pairwise vs trigger-local vs sentential features and show sizable gains when adding sentential features. They also present informative naive baselines (all-singletons, all-positives, same-type) to calibrate difficulty and class imbalance. The BestLink vs transitive-closure experiment offers a concrete take-away for clustering with modern mention-pair scores.

- Useful error analysis and discussion of limits: The qualitative analysis identifies pronoun-based event anaphora and annotation ambiguity as major error sources. This acknowledges that some misses require discourse modeling beyond sentence features and that inter-annotator variance bounds achievable performance.

- Implementation details and training protocol: The paper provides TensorFlow implementation, initialization, optimization choices, and a structured hyperparameter search (random + line search), improving reproducibility relative to many contemporaneous NN works.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on gold annotations for several pairwise features weakens the “resource-free” claim and applicability: The pairwise feature set (type compatibility, modality/realis, argument overlap) appears to require gold event types and ACE attributes and possibly gold arguments. This substantially simplifies the task and obscures the true benefit when upstream predictions are noisy. The claim of avoiding “external semantic features” is fair, but the reliance on gold event structure is a strong assumption. End-to-end robustness (with predicted triggers/types/arguments) is not evaluated.

- ACE++ dataset is insufficiently described and (apparently) not publicly released: The additional 1351 web documents are “annotated in an analogous fashion,” but annotation process, guidelines, annotator expertise, inter-annotator agreement, and availability are not detailed. Since ACE++ drives important conclusions (e.g., recall improvements, ablations), lack of transparency and public access limits impact and reproducibility.

- Limited comparison set and mismatched settings on ACE: The main comparison to Liu et al. (2014) is complicated by different splits and by different preprocessing choices across papers (gold mentions/attributes vs predicted). The paper acknowledges this, but without a controlled re-implementation or a stronger set of controlled baselines (e.g., a feature-rich non-neural model without external KBs), it is hard to isolate the contribution of sentential features.

- Modeling scope is still local; no document-level/global inference: The model scores mention pairs and clusters via transitive closure. This can propagate local errors and offers no global constraints (e.g., entity/event agreement, mutual exclusivity, or cluster-level attributes). While BestLink is tested, more principled global inference (ILP/CCM/spectral or neural structured prediction) could address well-known pitfalls like over- or under-merging, especially given the long-distance pairs highlighted in the analysis.

- Ablation/justification gaps: The choice of square activation for similarity is interesting but under-explained. The paper claims it outperforms alternatives, but quantitative ablations (e.g., cosine, ReLU/tanh, bilinear) are not reported. Likewise, the piecewise pooling and hidden layer design choices would benefit from ablation numbers. Statistical significance testing is not reported.

Questions to Authors
- Which pairwise features use gold annotations? Specifically, are event type, modality/realis, and argument overlap gold? If so, can you report performance with predicted types/attributes/arguments to assess end-to-end robustness?

- Will ACE++ be released? If yes, please provide annotation guidelines, annotator information, and inter-annotator agreement. If not, can you at least release train/dev/test splits, statistics per type, and a subset for reproducibility?

- Could you provide quantitative ablations for the similarity layer activation (square vs cosine/bilinear/ReLU/tanh) and for piecewise pooling vs standard pooling? Also, report variance over multiple random seeds and significance tests.

- How sensitive is performance to the CNN window size, position embeddings, and fine-tuning of word embeddings? A small study would help others re-use your architecture.

- Do you have any results with predicted triggers and arguments (e.g., standard ACE pipelines) to estimate end-to-end gains/losses relative to KB-heavy systems?

Overall assessment
Novelty is moderate but appropriate: adapting sentence-level CNN representations to event coreference is a timely, well-motivated contribution in 2016. The empirical evidence that such sentential features can match state-of-the-art without external KBs is valuable. However, dependence on gold event structure and the non-public ACE++ dataset limit practical impact and reproducibility. With clarifications and additional experiments on predicted inputs and clearer dataset release, this would be a strong accept; as is, I lean positive but cautious.
