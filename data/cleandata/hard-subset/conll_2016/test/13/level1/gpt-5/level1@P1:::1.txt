Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses within-document event coreference (event linking) and proposes a two-stage neural model that (i) builds position-aware sentence-level representations of individual event mentions using a CNN with piecewise max pooling and trigger-local lexical cues, and (ii) scores mention pairs via a learned distributed similarity function combined with simple pairwise features, followed by clustering via transitive closure. The approach aims to avoid reliance on external semantic resources while achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance. Experiments on ACE 2005 and a larger in-house ACE++ corpus show competitive or better results versus prior systems; ablations and an error analysis are provided.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical demonstration that sentence-level CNN representations, combined with light pairwise features, can achieve SOTA-level within-document event coreference without external knowledge bases (WordNet, FrameNet, etc.), on ACE 2005.
- A clear, simple two-step architecture for event mention encoding and pairwise similarity (including the use of position embeddings, piecewise pooling, and a squared nonlinearity for similarity), with ablations showing the contributions of sentential, lexical, and pairwise features, and an analysis of clustering strategy (transitive closure vs BestLink).
- A larger dataset (ACE++) used for training/analysis and a useful error analysis highlighting challenging phenomena (e.g., pronoun-based event references, annotation inconsistencies).

Strengths
- Strong results with minimal external resources: The model achieves competitive or better BLANC/MUC/positive-link F1 on ACE compared to Liu et al. (2014) and others while avoiding heavy reliance on external semantic resources. This supports the paper’s central claim that sentential features can substitute for curated knowledge sources, improving portability to new domains.
- Careful model design grounded in task properties: The use of position embeddings relative to the trigger and piecewise max pooling elegantly focuses on context relevant to the trigger, accommodates multiple triggers per sentence, and is well-motivated by the dataset analysis (e.g., many positive pairs differ in triggers or lack explicit arguments). The squared nonlinearity for distributed similarity is a simple, sensible choice that works well empirically.
- Thorough ablations and analysis: The feature ablations convincingly show that sentential features provide substantial gains beyond trigger-local and pairwise signals; the BestLink vs transitive-closure comparison is informative and shows the model’s pairwise scorer already filters low-confidence links, making BestLink counterproductive for recall. The dataset-size analysis (ACE vs ACE++) demonstrates improved generalization/recall with more data.
- Transparent experimental setup: The paper details training (TensorFlow, word2vec initialization, Adam, validation-based early selection), hyperparameter search strategy, and evaluation metrics. The publication of ACE split IDs is helpful for comparability.
- Insightful error analysis: The identification of pronoun-based event references as a major source of false negatives and the observation of annotation inconsistencies provide useful guidance for future work (e.g., discourse modeling, better guidelines).

Weaknesses
- Use of gold annotations and potentially optimistic features: The system assumes gold event triggers, and pairwise features include argument overlap and modality. It is unclear whether arguments/modality are gold or predicted. If gold, the reported numbers may overestimate practical performance and reduce comparability with works using predicted arguments/SRL. A variant without gold argument/modality features (or with predicted ones) would better support the paper’s core claim of avoiding external resources.
- Comparability and metrics: The ACE comparison is weakened by differing train/test splits and preprocessing across papers (acknowledged by authors). The paper emphasizes BLANC (reasonable), but omits CEAF and the CoNLL average (MUC/B3/CEAF), which are widely used in coreference; including them would ease comparison to prior work.
- Limited methodological novelty: The architecture adapts established CNN sentence encoders from relation/event extraction to coreference with relatively standard pairwise features and clustering. The novelty lies mainly in application and validation rather than new modeling ideas. Stronger baselines (e.g., neural pairwise encoders without CNN sentence context, or learned bilinear/cosine similarity with calibration) would help isolate gains.
- ACE++ details and availability: The paper introduces ACE++ but provides few details about annotation guidelines, quality control (inter-annotator agreement), and availability. Since many conclusions (e.g., recall gains) rely on ACE++, clarity on its construction and release plans is important.
- Clustering and calibration: The clustering threshold is fixed at 0.5 and the method uses simple transitive closure. There is no globally trained clustering objective or calibration per document, which could hurt precision/recall trade-offs, particularly with class imbalance. No analysis of threshold sensitivity or alternative learned clustering is provided.
- Class imbalance handling: The data is highly imbalanced (over 90% negative links), yet the training regime does not describe class weighting, negative sampling, or decision-threshold tuning. This could affect both training stability and metric optimization.

Questions to authors
- Do the pairwise features “argument overlap” and “modality agreement” rely on gold annotations? If so, can you report performance without gold arguments/modality, or with predicted arguments, to assess realism and portability?
- Which dataset is “publicly available” in your claim—ACE (licensed via LDC) or ACE++? Will ACE++ be released? Please provide annotation guidelines, annotator training, IAA statistics, and domain composition.
- How sensitive are results to the 0.5 clustering threshold? Did you tune this on validation sets? Have you tried a learned/global clustering approach (e.g., agglomerative with learned linkage, or structured training)?
- How did you handle class imbalance during training (loss weighting, sampling, threshold selection)? Could you report PR curves or threshold-optimized F1 on validation?
- What is the impact of removing the argument-overlap feature altogether? Similarly, ablate modality and “antecedent in first sentence” to quantify their contributions.
- Can you provide CEAF and CoNLL average scores on ACE to facilitate comparison?

Additional comments
- Reporting performance with predicted triggers (or end-to-end pipeline) would strengthen practical relevance.
- The “square” activation is simple and effective; a brief comparison to absolute value or bilinear similarity would be informative.
- A qualitative analysis of when sentential features overcome differing triggers and missing arguments would add interpretability.
