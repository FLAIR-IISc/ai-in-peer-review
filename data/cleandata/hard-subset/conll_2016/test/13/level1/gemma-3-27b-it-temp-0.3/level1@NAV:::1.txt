## Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

This paper presents a system for event linking that eschews traditional reliance on external knowledge bases, instead leveraging sentential features extracted via convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The core idea is to represent event mentions using CNN-derived embeddings of their surrounding context, combined with lexical and pairwise features, to determine coreference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, including one publicly available, and provide an error analysis.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of CNNs to generate effective sentential representations for event mentions in the context of coreference resolution. While the use of CNNs for relation/event extraction is not entirely novel (as acknowledged by the authors), applying this approach specifically to *event linking* and demonstrating its effectiveness *without* external semantic features is a valuable contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough experimental evaluation, including a comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods and a detailed error analysis that points to future research directions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Application:** The paper successfully adapts a promising technique (CNNs for sentential features) to a specific and challenging NLP task (event linking) where it hasn't been extensively explored, particularly in a setting that avoids external resources.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, including a public one, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The performance gains are not marginal and demonstrate a clear benefit of the method.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a detailed experimental setup, including ablation studies (Table 5) that clearly demonstrate the contribution of each feature class. This level of detail strengthens the validity of the findings.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis is particularly valuable. Identifying the challenges related to pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement provides concrete directions for future work and demonstrates a deep understanding of the task’s complexities.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of CNN Architecture:** The paper lacks a detailed justification for the specific CNN architecture choices (filter sizes, number of filters, etc.). A more in-depth discussion of these design decisions and their impact on performance would be beneficial.
2. **Clustering Strategy:** The choice of transitive closure for clustering, despite being outperformed by BestLink in some experiments, seems somewhat arbitrary. A more thorough justification for this choice is needed.
3. **Dataset Limitations:** While the inclusion of ACE++ is commendable, the datasets remain relatively small. Evaluating the model on larger and more diverse datasets would further strengthen the claims of generalizability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the specific CNN architecture choices (filter sizes, number of filters, etc.)? Were any other architectures explored, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Given that BestLink outperformed transitive closure in some experiments, why was transitive closure ultimately chosen for the final evaluation?
3.  The error analysis highlights challenges with pronoun resolution. Have you considered incorporating explicit pronoun resolution mechanisms into the model?
