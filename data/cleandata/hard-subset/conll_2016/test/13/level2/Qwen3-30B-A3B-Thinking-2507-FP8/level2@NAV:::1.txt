

This paper proposes a neural network model for event linking that leverages sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The main contributions are: (1) a novel approach to event linking using CNN-generated sentential features, (2) state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE and ACE++) without external semantic features, and (3) a thorough error analysis identifying pronoun resolution as a key limitation.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for the practical utility of their approach by demonstrating that it achieves competitive performance without domain-specific external resources. The ablation study (Table 5) convincingly shows the contribution of each feature class, with sentential features providing the most significant improvement. The error analysis (Section 6.2) is particularly valuable, revealing that pronoun resolution represents a fundamental challenge in event linking that even human annotators struggle with, providing meaningful direction for future work.

Weaknesses: The comparison to state-of-the-art baselines (Table 3) lacks consistency in dataset partitioning, which undermines the strength of their performance claims. While they acknowledge the issue, they don't provide a more robust comparison methodology. The paper also fails to adequately contrast with recent neural approaches to event extraction (e.g., those using RNNs or attention mechanisms), making it difficult to assess the novelty of their CNN-based feature generation. Additionally, the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is somewhat weakened by the lack of comparison to the most recent neural approaches to event linking.

Questions: How would your model perform on datasets with significantly more event mentions per document? Have you considered how your approach might be adapted for cross-document event linking, which would require handling longer-range dependencies?

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the event linking literature with practical implications for domain-agnostic event processing systems. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the comparative methodology concerns and better contextualizing the approach within recent neural network advances for event extraction. The work demonstrates clear value in reducing reliance on external semantic resources while maintaining competitive performance.
