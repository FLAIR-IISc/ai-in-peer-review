Summary and contributions
The paper addresses within-document event coreference (“event linking”). It proposes a two-part neural architecture: (a) a sentence-level encoder using CNNs with positional embeddings and piecewise max-pooling to produce an event-mention representation that combines sentential and trigger-local lexical features; (b) a learned similarity module that, together with simple pairwise features (type agreement, distance, modality, argument overlap), predicts whether two mentions corefer. Pairwise decisions are clustered via transitive closure. Experiments on ACE 2005 and an extended ACE++ dataset show state-of-the-art performance without using external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet). Ablations highlight the contribution of sentential features, and an error analysis identifies hard cases (notably pronominal links).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Demonstration that CNN-derived sentential features can match or exceed prior SOTA for event linking without external semantic resources.
2) A practical architecture and analysis (including ablations and clustering choices) clarifying the utility of sentential versus pairwise/lexical features for this task.
3) A larger ACE++ dataset and empirical insights (data properties, error analysis) that motivate future research directions.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results with fewer external dependencies: The model achieves competitive or better BLANC/MUC/positive-link F1 than Liu et al. (2014) while avoiding hand-engineered semantic resources. This is a meaningful advance in portability and applicability across domains where WordNet/FrameNet coverage is limited.
- Clear, well-motivated architecture: The decomposition into mention encoding and pairwise similarity is intuitive, and the use of positional embeddings and piecewise pooling to focus on trigger-local and broader sentential cues aligns with recent advances in relation extraction. The square nonlinearity for similarity is a simple but sensible design choice and is empirically justified.
- Thorough ablations and design comparisons: The paper systematically probes the impact of sentential and trigger-local features, pairwise features, clustering strategy (transitive closure vs. BestLink), and dataset size. Results convincingly show sentential features substantially improve both precision and recall, and that more training data boosts recall.
- Useful analysis of data characteristics: Quantifying argument-sharing, trigger mismatches, and long-distance links provides compelling motivation for sentential modeling and de-emphasizes reliance on argument overlap and trigger identity.
- Error analysis with actionable insights: The identification of pronoun-driven failures and borderline annotation cases suggests concrete next steps (discourse modeling, better handling of anaphoric “it”).

Weaknesses
- Comparability and use of gold annotations: While the paper notes that all compared systems use gold triggers, it remains unclear whether pairwise features (type agreement, modality, argument overlap) use gold labels. If type and modality are gold (as they are part of ACE annotations) and argument overlap is also gold, this may inflate performance and diminishes the claim of being pipeline-robust. Please clarify exactly which labels are gold vs. predicted at test time. The fairness of the SOTA comparison hinges on this.
- ACE++ dataset details and availability: ACE++ adds 1,351 web documents “annotated in an analogous fashion,” but the paper does not provide annotation guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, or a public release. Without these, the magnitude of the recall gains and the generality of conclusions are harder to assess or reproduce. The statement that “one dataset is publicly available” is ambiguous given ACE’s licensing; please specify which dataset is public and provide release details (or a timeline).
- Limited baselines and significance testing: Although naive baselines and ablations are included, the paper lacks a strong non-neural or simpler neural baseline trained on the same features (e.g., logistic regression/random forest over pairwise + bag-of-words context; or a BiLSTM/FFNN without CNN piecewise pooling). No significance tests are reported for improvements over Liu et al. (2014) or across ablations. Given small test sets and split differences, significance testing would strengthen claims.
- Metric coverage and standardization: The paper emphasizes BLANC (arguably reasonable), but omits CEAF scores, which are standard in coreference evaluation and capture different aspects than MUC/B3. Given non-identical ACE splits across prior work, reporting CEAF (and average CoNLL score) would facilitate more robust comparison.
- Clustering and global consistency: The approach learns pairwise scores independently and enforces consistency only via transitive closure. While the study finds BestLink hurts recall, global models (e.g., agglomerative or ILP/CRF-based) may better control error propagation and singleton bias. Some analysis of cluster-level errors (over-/under-merging trends) would help.

Questions to authors
- Which features are gold vs. predicted at test time? Specifically: event type (coarse/fine), modality, and argument roles/overlap. If any are predicted, which tools and with what accuracy? If gold, how does performance change with predicted annotations?
- For ACE++: What are the annotation guidelines, domains, annotator training, and IAA? Will ACE++ be released (and under what license)? If not, can you release only the document IDs and a conversion script?
- Thresholding and calibration: Is the 0.5 logistic threshold fixed across datasets/experiments, or tuned on dev? Did you try global threshold optimization for BLANC? Any calibration analysis?
- Significance: Are improvements over Liu et al. (2014) and over your own ablations statistically significant (e.g., via bootstrap resampling)?
- Representation details: Did you compare CNN sentential features to BiLSTM encoders or to PCNN variants with explicit trigger-region masking? Any per-type performance breakdowns?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include CEAF metrics and, if possible, report results on a commonly used ACE split or provide your exact splits for reproducibility. Consider releasing code and trained models.
- Add a “no external features but with predicted triggers/types” setting to quantify end-to-end robustness; even a small drop would be informative for real-world use.
- Expand the error analysis with category counts (e.g., pronominal anaphora, lexical inference, temporal mismatch) and examples where transitive closure propagates errors.
- Consider exploring lightweight global clustering (e.g., best-first with merge constraints or learned linkage penalties) to balance precision and recall without heavy feature engineering.

Overall, this is a solid, timely paper with clear empirical value. With clarifications on gold/predicted features, dataset availability, and stronger baselines/metrics, the work would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
