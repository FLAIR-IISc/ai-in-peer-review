Summary and contributions
This paper addresses within-document event coreference (“event linking”) and proposes a neural model that avoids reliance on external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet). The model has two parts: (a) a CNN-based encoder that produces a sentential representation of each event mention, augmented with trigger-local lexical features; and (b) a pairwise scorer that combines the two mention encodings with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance, realis match, argument overlap) to produce a coreference score via a learned distributed similarity (square activation) followed by logistic regression. Pairwise decisions are turned into clusters via transitive closure. The system achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on ACE 2005 and on an extended dataset (ACE++). Ablations show the utility of sentential features; an error analysis highlights issues such as pronominal references and annotation inconsistencies.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A resource-lean, CNN-based sentential representation for event mentions applied to event coreference, yielding competitive SOTA performance without external semantic KBs.
2) An empirical analysis demonstrating the importance of sentential context for event linking (dataset statistics and ablations), and a careful comparison of clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink).
3) A practical evaluation on ACE and a larger ACE++ variant with ablation studies and qualitative error analysis.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance without external semantic resources: On ACE, the model achieves BLANC F1 73.31, exceeding Liu et al. (2014) on BLANC/MUC/positive-links and competitive on B3. On ACE++, the model attains BLANC F1 76.90. This is a meaningful result given many prior systems’ dependence on expensive or domain-limited resources.
- Clear evidence for the value of sentential features: The paper motivates the design with ACE++ statistics (e.g., 50.97% of coreferent pairs share no arguments; 47.29% have different triggers; 55.42% are >5 sentences apart), and corroborates with ablations. For example, pairwise-only features yield BLANC F1 56.69, adding trigger-local lexical features yields 64.12, and adding sentential features lifts to 76.90.
- Thoughtful modeling choices and analysis: The two-part architecture with left/right piecewise pooling targets multiple triggers per sentence; the square activation for distributed similarity is justified and empirically superior to alternatives considered. The comparison of clustering strategies is informative: while BestLink increases precision, it hurts recall and overall F1 for this model, suggesting the classifier already filters low-confidence links.
- Useful error analysis: The manual review of 100 FPs and 100 FNs identifies systematic challenges (e.g., pronoun references to events, boundary cases with annotation variability), providing guidance for future work (e.g., discourse modeling).

Weaknesses
- Reliance on gold annotations during evaluation is under-specified and may overstate practical performance: The model uses pairwise features such as event type agreement, realis match, and argument overlap. It is unclear whether type and realis are gold or predicted; argument overlap likely relies on gold arguments. The paper argues not to rely on external semantic resources, but depends on upstream gold labels. This limits claims about end-to-end applicability and comparability to systems using predicted inputs.
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural IE work: While applying CNN-based sentential features to event coreference is worthwhile, many components (CNN encoders with position embeddings, piecewise pooling, distributed similarity) are adaptations of established approaches in relation/event extraction. The main methodological novelty is the application and engineering of these features to coreference, rather than a new coreference model.
- Comparison and reproducibility caveats: Cross-paper comparisons on ACE are confounded by differing splits and preprocessing; significance testing is absent. For ACE++, details on annotation quality, availability, and inter-annotator agreement are missing. Code, trained models, and precise hyperparameters (beyond high-level description) are not provided, making reproduction difficult.
- Potential overfitting and regularization details: Training for 2000 epochs on small data without clear regularization choices raises concerns. Although dropout/L2 were explored, the final chosen settings and their impact are not fully reported.
- Evaluation scope: The paper focuses on within-document linking with gold triggers. No results with predicted triggers/arguments, no end-to-end pipeline evaluation, and no cross-document setting, which limits the assessment of robustness and portability.

Questions to authors
1) Which upstream annotations are gold vs. predicted at test time? Specifically: event types, realis, and arguments used for “overlap in arguments.” If any are gold, can you report results with predicted labels to quantify the impact?
2) What is the full set of hyperparameters selected (dw, dp, dc, de, dsim, filter widths, pooling choices, dropout/L2 values), and can you provide validation curves? Did you observe overfitting; how was early stopping applied?
3) For ACE++, what is the annotation protocol, IAA, and availability? Will ACE++ (or a subset) be released to support reproducibility?
4) Did you conduct statistical significance tests for the ACE comparisons (e.g., bootstrap over documents) and for ablations? If so, please report; if not, can you add them?
5) How sensitive is performance to the choice of similarity activation (square) and to the presence/absence of each pairwise feature (e.g., type, realis)? An ablation over pairwise features would help.

Additional comments
- Consider reporting performance with BestLink and transitive closure under a fixed operating point (e.g., threshold tuned on validation only) and including precision-recall curves.
- An experiment with predicted triggers/arguments from a standard ACE extractor would strengthen practical relevance.
- Exploring alternative encoders (biLSTM/attention) and joint modeling with entity coreference or argument prediction could address some of the error patterns (e.g., pronominal events).
- Releasing code, model configurations, and the ACE split you used (you partially provide doc lists) would greatly aid adoption.

Overall, this is a solid, timely paper with strong empirical results and clear analysis. Addressing the gold vs. predicted inputs and reproducibility concerns would significantly improve its impact.
