## Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, aiming to reduce reliance on external knowledge bases. The authors achieve state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, one of which is publicly available, and provide an error analysis to guide future research.

**Summary & Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is a competitive event linking system that effectively utilizes CNNs for sentential feature extraction, demonstrably achieving strong performance *without* relying on traditional external semantic features (WordNet, FrameNet, etc.).  A secondary, and significant, contribution is the empirical demonstration that such a CNN-based approach *can* perform competitively with methods reliant on external resources, potentially broadening the applicability of event linking to lower-resource domains. A third contribution is a detailed error analysis, pointing towards challenging cases like pronoun resolution and highlighting potential areas for improvement.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance:** The reported state-of-the-art performance on ACE and ACE++ is a significant achievement.  The results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparative evaluation against existing literature (Table 3) provides a useful benchmark.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** While CNNs are increasingly used in NLP, their application specifically for sentential feature extraction *for event linking* is a worthwhile contribution.  The authors correctly identify a gap in the field, where reliance on expensive and domain-specific external resources has been a limiting factor.  The proposal to bypass this requirement is well-motivated.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a series of ablation studies (Table 5) that convincingly demonstrate the contribution of each feature class (pairwise, lexical, sentential). This detailed analysis strengthens the claims about the importance of the proposed approach.  The exploration of different clustering strategies (BestLink vs. transitive closure) also adds to the robustness of the evaluation.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The manual error analysis is a particularly valuable aspect of the paper.  Identifying key error types, such as pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, provides concrete directions for future work.  The examples provided help illustrate the challenges inherent in the task.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The model architecture is explained with a helpful diagram (Figure 1), and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section, while present, feels somewhat cursory.  There is a missed opportunity to more thoroughly position the work within the broader context of event coreference resolution and neural network-based relation extraction.  Specifically, the paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of prior work using different neural network architectures (e.g., LSTMs, transformers) for similar tasks.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The ACE dataset, while a standard benchmark, is relatively small.  The addition of ACE++ helps, but the conclusions might benefit from evaluation on larger and more diverse datasets. The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but a more explicit discussion of the potential limitations related to dataset size would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While the authors mention a hyperparameter search, the details are limited. Providing more information about the search space and the criteria used for selecting the final hyperparameters would increase the reproducibility of the results.
4. **Justification for Design Choices:** Some design choices, such as the specific architecture of the CNN (filter sizes, number of filters) and the use of the square function in the similarity calculation, are not fully justified. While the authors mention intuition behind some choices, a more systematic exploration or justification would be helpful.
5. **Pronoun Resolution – Known Issue, Limited Discussion:** The error analysis correctly identifies pronoun resolution as a significant challenge, but the discussion remains largely descriptive. The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging existing work on pronoun resolution in the context of event linking and outlining potential approaches for addressing this issue.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the CNN-based feature extraction compared to using external knowledge bases?  A comparison of training/inference time would be useful.
2.  Were any alternative neural network architectures (e.g., LSTMs, transformers) explored for sentential feature extraction? If so, could you briefly describe the results and explain why the CNN architecture was ultimately chosen?
3.  Regarding the error analysis, how consistent were the annotations across different annotators for the examples identified as potential annotation disagreements?
4.  The paper mentions that the BestLink clustering strategy did not improve performance. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the cases where BestLink made incorrect decisions compared to the transitive closure approach?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the ACEvalid/ACEtest document list (in Section 4) is helpful.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of relying solely on sentential features, particularly in scenarios where event descriptions are ambiguous or incomplete.
*   The formatting in the tables could be improved for better readability. For example, aligning decimal points would be helpful.
