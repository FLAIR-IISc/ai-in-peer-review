## Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

This paper presents a novel approach to event linking, a crucial task for document-level information extraction. The authors address the limitation of existing systems relying on domain-specific knowledge bases by proposing a model that utilizes sentential features derived from convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The core idea – leveraging contextual information encoded by CNNs – is well-motivated and aligns with recent trends in applying neural networks to relation and event extraction.

The primary contribution of this work is a competitive event linking system that achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets *without* relying on external semantic features. A second, supporting contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation, including ablation studies demonstrating the importance of different feature components (pairwise, lexical, and sentential). Finally, the included error analysis provides valuable insights into potential areas for future improvement, particularly highlighting challenges with pronoun resolution.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear presentation, comprehensive experimentation, and strong baseline comparison. The ablation studies are particularly helpful in understanding the contribution of each component of the proposed model. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work and clearly articulate the advantages of their approach. The inclusion of error analysis demonstrates a critical engagement with the results.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the performance is strong, the gains over existing methods are not substantial. The authors could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the computational complexity of their approach, especially concerning the CNN component. Furthermore, the paper lacks a discussion of potential biases within the datasets, which could influence the model’s performance. Although the error analysis is helpful, it could be expanded to include more examples and a more systematic categorization of error types.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide some details on the computational cost (training and inference time) of your model compared to the baseline methods?
2.  Have you investigated potential biases in the datasets (ACE and ACE++) that might affect the model’s performance?
3.  You mention challenges with pronoun resolution in the error analysis. Have you considered incorporating specific features or techniques to address this issue, such as coreference resolution on the entities involved?



Overall, this is a solid paper with a promising approach to event linking. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the contribution and impact of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
