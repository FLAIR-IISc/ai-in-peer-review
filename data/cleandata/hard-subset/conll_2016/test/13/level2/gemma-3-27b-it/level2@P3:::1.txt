This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages sentential features derived from convolutional neural networks, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The core contribution lies in adapting recent advances in neural network-based feature extraction for relation and event extraction to the specific task of event coreference resolution. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, and a detailed error analysis. A third, related contribution, is the public release of a dataset (ACE++).

Strengths:

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE dataset and competitive results on the newly introduced ACE++ dataset. The performance gains, particularly on ACE++, demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed approach. The inclusion of a second, larger dataset is a valuable contribution to the community.
2. **Novel Application of CNNs:** The adaptation of CNNs to generate sentential features specifically for event linking represents a meaningful contribution. While CNNs have been used for relation extraction, applying them directly to event coreference, without relying on external knowledge bases, is a novel direction. The architecture presented, focusing on intra-sentential event modeling and subsequent coreference decisions, is well-motivated.
3. **Comprehensive Error Analysis:** The included error analysis provides valuable insights into the limitations of the model and identifies areas for future research. Recognizing the difficulty of pronoun resolution and highlighting annotation inconsistencies are particularly insightful observations.  This analysis goes beyond simply reporting numbers and demonstrates a clear understanding of the challenges inherent in the task.
4. **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The paper provides a clear rationale for the design choices made, linking them to observations about the datasets (e.g., the high proportion of non-overlapping argument sets, the distances between coreferential event mentions).  This connection between data analysis and model design strengthens the work.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Comparison to Recent Work:** While the paper compares to several relevant systems, the field has moved quickly. A more thorough comparison to more recent neural network-based approaches to event linking (beyond those cited) would strengthen the claim of state-of-the-art performance.  Specifically, methods utilizing transformers or more sophisticated attention mechanisms should be considered.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The description of hyperparameter tuning is relatively brief. Providing more details about the search space, the optimization strategy, and the computational resources used would increase reproducibility and allow for a better assessment of the robustness of the results.
3. **Clustering Strategy Impact:** The results in Table 4 show a performance *decrease* when using the BestLink clustering strategy, contrary to previous findings in the literature (Liu et al., 2014). This discrepancy is noted but not deeply investigated. A more thorough analysis of why BestLink is detrimental in this particular setup would be beneficial. The paper mentions it "weeds out many low confidence links," but doesn't explain why this is *harmful* in this context.
4. **Feature Ablation Study Interpretation:** The interpretation of the feature ablation study (Table 5) could be clearer. The increase in precision when using only pairwise and sentential features is attributed to the model focusing on trigger words. However, this interpretation relies on an assumption about how the model learns. A more detailed analysis of the learned weights or attention patterns would strengthen this claim.

Questions to Authors:

1. Could you elaborate on the reasons why the BestLink clustering strategy performed worse than the transitive closure method in your experiments? What specific characteristics of your model or the dataset might explain this difference?
2.  Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter search process, including the specific ranges explored for each hyperparameter and the computational resources used?
3.  Could you provide some examples from the error analysis demonstrating the types of pronoun resolution failures encountered by the model? This would help to illustrate the challenges more concretely.



Additional Comments:

* The writing is generally clear and concise.
* The figures and tables are well-organized and contribute to the understanding of the paper.
* The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, though, as noted above, a more up-to-date comparison to recent approaches would be desirable.
* The publicly releasing of the ACE++ dataset is highly appreciated.
