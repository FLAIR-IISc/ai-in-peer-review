This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, avoiding reliance on external knowledge bases. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, and the paper includes an error analysis.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Effective Use of CNNs for Sentential Features:** The primary contribution is demonstrating that CNNs can effectively capture sentential features for event linking, achieving competitive performance without the need for external semantic resources. This is particularly valuable for domains where such resources are limited or unavailable.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), comparing the proposed model to existing state-of-the-art methods. The inclusion of ACE++ is a significant contribution as it provides a larger, more challenging dataset for evaluation.
3.  **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis offers valuable insights into the model's strengths and weaknesses. Identifying challenges like pronoun resolution provides a clear direction for future research.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Performance:** The model outperforms existing approaches on the ACE dataset and demonstrates competitive results on the larger ACE++ dataset, establishing its effectiveness.
2.  **Novelty in Feature Engineering:** Replacing reliance on external knowledge bases with learned sentential features from CNNs represents a novel approach to event linking. This is a valuable contribution as it enhances the portability and adaptability of the model to different domains.
3.  **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its evaluation. The architecture is clearly explained with the help of the figure.
4.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed error analysis reveals specific challenges the model faces, guiding future improvements. Identifying issues with pronoun resolution is particularly helpful.
5.  **Thorough Comparison to Baselines & Ablation Studies:** The comparison to naive baselines and the ablation studies clearly demonstrate the impact of the individual components of the proposed model.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, the details are scarce. Providing more details about the tuning process, search space, and justification for chosen parameters would increase the reproducibility and understanding of the results.
2.  **Dataset Bias:** The ACE dataset is relatively small, and the ACE++ dataset, while larger, is still limited in size and domain coverage. The model's generalization ability to significantly different domains remains uncertain.
3.  **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches:** The comparison with state-of-the-art focuses on older methods. Including comparisons to more recent neural network-based approaches for event linking (if any exist) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
4.  **Clustering Strategy:** The choice of transitive closure clustering seems suboptimal, as noted in the paper itself by referencing Liu et al. (2014). The authors acknowledge BestLink outperforms on their original system, but their results show the opposite, which warrants further investigation. The reason for this discrepancy should be discussed.
5.  **Over-reliance on Gold Event Mentions:** Although acknowledged, the reliance on gold event mentions for evaluation potentially masks issues related to event trigger identification, which is often a challenging subtask.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and justification for the chosen parameters?
2.  You mention that BestLink performed worse in your experiments than transitive closure, despite prior work showing the opposite. Could you elaborate on potential reasons for this discrepancy?
3.  How might the model's performance be affected by errors in event trigger identification? Have you considered evaluating the model end-to-end with a separate event trigger identification component?
4.  Could you comment on the computational cost of your model compared to the baseline methods?
5.  The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a key challenge. Have you considered incorporating any specific mechanisms to address this issue, such as attention mechanisms or coreference models?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of related work, particularly recent advances in neural network-based event linking.
*   Consider including a table summarizing the dataset statistics (e.g., number of events, average sentence length) for better clarity.
*   The notation could be more consistent throughout the paper. For instance, clarifying the meaning of symbols like 'dc' and 'dw' in the text would be helpful.
