**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach for event linking, a task that involves resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document. The authors design a model that uses sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to represent event mentions, without relying on external semantic features. The model consists of two parts: the first part generates a representation for a single event mention, and the second part computes a coreference score for a pair of event mentions. The authors evaluate their model on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, and achieve state-of-the-art performance.

**Contributions**

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. A novel approach for event linking that uses sentential features from CNNs to represent event mentions.
2. A two-part model that generates a representation for a single event mention and computes a coreference score for a pair of event mentions.
3. State-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE and ACE++.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach for event linking that does not rely on external semantic features, making it more robust and applicable to different domains.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, and achieve state-of-the-art performance.
3. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the model's performance, including an error analysis that highlights the challenges of the task.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes that the event mentions are already recognized and their semantic types are classified, which may not always be the case in real-world applications.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art models, making it difficult to assess the relative performance of their approach.
3. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the model handles cases where the event mentions are not in the same sentence or paragraph.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the model handle cases where the event mentions are not in the same sentence or paragraph?
2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art models, including their strengths and weaknesses?
3. How do you plan to extend the model to handle more complex cases, such as joint trigger identification and coreference resolution?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed approach. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model and highlight the challenges of the task. However, there are some areas that need further clarification and improvement. Overall, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing and event linking.
