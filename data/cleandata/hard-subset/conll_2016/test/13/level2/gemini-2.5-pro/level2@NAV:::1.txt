**Summary of the paper**
This paper proposes a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The central idea is to replace traditional, resource-heavy feature engineering with learned "sentential features" derived from a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The model operates in two stages: first, a CNN processes the full sentence containing an event mention to generate a dense vector representation. This representation is augmented with local lexical features. Second, a pair of these event representations, along with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement), is fed into another neural network that computes a final coreference score.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the successful demonstration that a neural model can achieve state-of-the-art performance in event linking without relying on external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet. This significantly improves the model's portability to new domains where such resources are unavailable.
2.  A secondary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation, including a well-designed ablation study that quantifies the performance impact of pairwise, trigger-local, and the novel CNN-derived sentential features.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Impact and Generalizability:** The paper addresses a critical limitation of prior systems: their dependency on external semantic resources. By learning features directly from text, the proposed method is more scalable and applicable to low-resource domains, which is a significant step forward for the field.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, outperforming a strong baseline that uses over 100 hand-engineered semantic features. This provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of learning sentential representations.
3.  **Thorough Analysis:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation. The ablation study clearly demonstrates the importance of each feature component, validating the architectural choices. Furthermore, the error analysis offers valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task, such as pronoun resolution, which can guide future research.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the application to event linking is novel, the core CNN architecture for generating sentence representations is largely adapted from prior work in relation classification. The paper could better position its specific architectural contributions relative to these existing models.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Alternative Architectures:** The paper motivates the use of CNNs but does not discuss or compare against other prominent neural architectures for sentence encoding, such as LSTMs or GRUs. An argument for why CNNs are particularly suited for this task over recurrent models would strengthen the work.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on the ACE++ Dataset:** The paper introduces a new, larger dataset (ACE++), which is a welcome contribution. However, it lacks crucial details about the source of the additional documents, the annotation guidelines, and inter-annotator agreement. This information is necessary to fully assess the quality of the dataset and the reliability of the results reported on it.
