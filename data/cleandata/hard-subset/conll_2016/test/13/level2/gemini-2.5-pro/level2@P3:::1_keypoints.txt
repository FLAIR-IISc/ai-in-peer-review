- Proposes a neural within-document event coreference model that learns sentential representations to reduce reliance on external lexical-semantic resources.  
- Uses a CNN with piecewise max-pooling to encode event mentions and a feed-forward similarity network on pair representations, with clustering via transitive closure.  
- Evaluated on ACE 2005 and a newly introduced larger ACE++ dataset, showing competitive or improved performance.  
- Main contribution: effective event coreference without external knowledge bases, improving portability across domains.  
- Secondary contribution: thorough empirical evaluation including ACE++ introduction, ablation studies, and error analysis.  
- Strength: high portability and reduced need for handcrafted semantic features or external resources.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance comparable to or exceeding prior state-of-the-art on ACE 2005.  
- Strength: comprehensive analysis (ablation, larger-data experiments, qualitative error analysis) highlighting feature contributions and failure modes.  
- Weakness: limited architectural novelty—the CNN design closely follows prior relation-extraction work rather than introducing a new architecture.  
- Weakness: unclear description of how the two event-mention representations are combined for the similarity function, hurting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: insufficient details about the ACE++ dataset creation, annotation process, and inter-annotator agreement.  
- Question: please clarify the exact composition of the input to the similarity network (how ve representations for both mentions and pairwise features are combined).  
- Question: please provide more information on ACE++ sources, annotation procedures, inter-annotator agreement, and plans for public release.
