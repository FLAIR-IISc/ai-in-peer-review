## Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

This paper presents a novel approach to event linking, a crucial task for document-level information extraction, by leveraging sentential features derived from convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The key contribution lies in moving away from reliance on external knowledge bases, a significant limitation of prior work, and instead utilizing learned representations of event mentions within their sentential context. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, including a publicly available one, and provide a thoughtful error analysis.

The strength of this work is its practical relevance and strong empirical results. The demonstrated ability to achieve competitive performance *without* external semantic features is a significant advantage, broadening the applicability of event linking systems to domains where such resources are unavailable or costly to create. The detailed ablation studies (Table 5) clearly demonstrate the contribution of each feature class, and the error analysis provides valuable insights into remaining challenges, particularly regarding pronoun resolution. The model design, while not radically new in its components (CNNs, logistic regression), is effectively combined and tailored to the specific task of event linking.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The novelty of using CNNs for sentential features in this context feels incremental. While the authors acknowledge related work utilizing similar features for relation/event extraction, a more thorough discussion of *how* this work differs and builds upon those approaches would strengthen the contribution. The experimental setup, while comprehensive, lacks a comparison to simpler baselines beyond the "all singletons" and "same type" baselines (Table 6). Including a baseline relying solely on event type matching, for example, would provide a more direct comparison. The discussion of the clustering strategy (BestLink vs. transitive closure) feels somewhat contradictory, initially stating BestLink is expected to perform better based on related work, then showing it performs worse in their experiments. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific differences between your approach to utilizing sentential features with CNNs and the existing work you cite (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015)?
2.  Why did BestLink perform worse than transitive closure in your experiments, and what does this suggest about the characteristics of your model’s output scores?
3.  The error analysis highlights challenges with pronoun resolution. Have you considered incorporating coreference resolution techniques *before* event linking to potentially address this issue?



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of event linking. The strong empirical results and practical focus make it a valuable addition to the conference. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version.
