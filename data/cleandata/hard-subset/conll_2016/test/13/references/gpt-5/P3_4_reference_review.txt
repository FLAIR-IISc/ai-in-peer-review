Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles within-document event coreference (event linking). It proposes a two-stage neural model: (a) a CNN-based encoder that builds a mention representation from the full sentence using word and position embeddings with piecewise max-pooling around the trigger, concatenated with trigger-local embeddings; (b) a pairwise scorer that combines the two mention vectors with several hand-crafted pairwise features (e.g., type agreement, distance, modality/realis match, argument overlap), passes them through a “squared” similarity layer, and predicts a coreference score. Clustering is by transitive closure of positive links (vs. BestLink). The model uses no external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet). Experiments on ACE and an extended ACE++ dataset show competitive or better BLANC/MUC F1 vs prior work; ablations show gains from sentential features; an error analysis highlights pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that sentence-level neural features (CNN with position embeddings and piecewise pooling) can achieve state-of-the-art event linking without external semantic resources.
2) Empirical analysis clarifying the utility of various feature classes (pairwise vs trigger-local vs sentential) and clustering strategies (transitive closure vs BestLink).
3) A larger ACE++ dataset used for training/validation and a careful evaluation with BLANC and other coref metrics, plus an informative error analysis.

Strengths
- Solid empirical results with reduced dependency on external resources: The model matches or surpasses strong feature-rich baselines on ACE (e.g., higher BLANC/MUC F1 than Liu et al. 2014) while deliberately avoiding WordNet/FrameNet. This is a meaningful advance for portability across domains where such resources are not available.

- Careful architectural choices for mention encoding: The use of position embeddings and piecewise max-pooling centered on the trigger is well-motivated for handling multiple mentions per sentence and for emphasizing context relative to the trigger. The ablations convincingly show that sentential features add substantial value over pairwise and trigger-local features alone.

- Thorough experimental design and analysis: The paper reports multiple metrics (including BLANC, which is well-suited for skewed coref distributions), compares clustering strategies, studies data scaling effects (ACE → ACE++), and provides informative ablations. The error analysis is thoughtful, acknowledging genuine challenges (event pronominals) and annotation ambiguity.

- Practicality: The approach uses only standard NLP preprocessing (tokenization/positional indexing) and pretrained word embeddings; no heavy syntactic/semantic resources are required, improving applicability and reproducibility relative to prior pipelines.

- Interpretability of pairwise features: Including interpretable features (type agreement, distance, realis, argument overlap) facilitates understanding model behavior and enables targeted analysis.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on gold annotations limits practical significance: The experiments are conducted with gold event triggers, and pairwise features include event type agreement, modality/realis match, and argument overlap. It is not clearly stated whether arguments and realis are also gold. If so, performance likely overestimates real-world utility; if predicted, details (systems, accuracy, error propagation) are missing. An end-to-end evaluation or at least reporting with predicted triggers/types/arguments is crucial.

- Limited novelty in the scoring/clustering stage and minimal global reasoning: The pairwise scorer is relatively simple (element-wise square nonlinearity followed by logistic regression), and clustering is transitive closure. No global inference (e.g., joint clustering, consistency constraints, or collective features) is explored. Given the long-distance nature of many links, a stronger global model could matter. Comparisons to standard matching architectures (bilinear, element-wise product/abs-diff with MLP) are missing.

- Under-specified ACE++ resource and reproducibility: ACE++ is central for hyperparameter tuning and for the main ablations, but it is not public, and annotation details (guidelines, annotator expertise, IAA, coverage, event types) are not described. Without releasing the data or more documentation, reproducibility and transferability are limited.

- Unclear extent of external supervision and preprocessing: While the paper emphasizes no use of external semantic resources, the approach still uses pretrained word embeddings and possibly relies on gold event attributes (type, realis, arguments). The exact sources for pairwise attributes, and whether they are predicted or gold, should be clearly specified. If arguments are gold, that is a strong oracle.

- Analysis granularity: Results are not broken down by event type, mention distance buckets, or argument availability (e.g., pairs with no shared arguments), despite the motivating data analysis. Such breakdowns would clarify where the sentential CNN helps most and where errors concentrate.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold event arguments and gold realis/modality labels when computing pairwise features (argument overlap, modality match)? If not, what systems produce them and with what accuracy? Please report performance with predicted triggers/types/arguments to assess end-to-end applicability.

- What are the exact representations of pairwise features (one-hot, embeddings, scalars) and how are they normalized? Is the distance bagging a categorical histogram? Please detail.

- Why choose element-wise square as the similarity nonlinearity? Did you compare against common alternatives (cosine, bilinear, element-wise product/abs-diff + MLP)? Please provide comparative results.

- Can you provide per-type and distance-based performance (e.g., coreferent pairs >5 sentences apart), and performance on pairs with/without shared arguments, to substantiate the claim that sentential features capture context beyond arguments?

- For ACE++: who annotated it, under which guidelines, and with what IAA? Will you release the corpus or at least the event linking annotations and scripts?

- Thresholding and calibration: Is the 0.5 threshold fixed or tuned on validation per dataset? Did you try per-document calibration or class weighting to handle imbalance?

Additional comments
- Consider experimenting with global inference (e.g., best-first or ILP with transitivity/compatibility constraints) or learning-to-cluster approaches; your pairwise scores could seed stronger clusterers.

- Given the prominence of pronoun-based event mentions in errors, incorporating coreferential anaphora cues (e.g., event nominal/pronominal detection, discourse connectives) and cross-sentential encoders (BiLSTM/Transformer with segment-level context) may help.

- Please clarify whether syntactic parses were used at any point (they are listed as “no longer expensive” but not used here), and whether their inclusion offers gains.
