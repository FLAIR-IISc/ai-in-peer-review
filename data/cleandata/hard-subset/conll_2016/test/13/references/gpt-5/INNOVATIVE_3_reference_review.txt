Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles within-document event coreference (“event linking”) and proposes to learn event-mention representations from sentence context via a CNN with position embeddings and piecewise max pooling around the trigger. Pairwise coreference decisions are then made by combining two learned mention vectors with standard pairwise features and a squared nonlinearity similarity layer, followed by logistic regression; clusters are formed via transitive closure. The key claim is that sentential features can obviate the need for external semantic resources (WordNet/FrameNet) while maintaining or improving state-of-the-art performance.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Demonstration that sentence-level neural representations of event mentions suffice to reach SOTA on ACE without external knowledge resources, improving portability across domains.
2) A simple, effective architecture for event coreference that combines CNN-based mention encoding with a light pairwise module and straightforward clustering, supported by ablations on feature classes, clustering strategies, and data size (ACE vs. ACE++).
3) Creation of an expanded ACE++ dataset and an informative empirical analysis (distance, trigger/argument overlap) that motivates modeling choices and guides future work.

Strengths
- Novelty/value of idea: Using sentential CNN features to replace external semantic resources in event linking is a bold and timely direction. The work convincingly shows that learned sentence representations can stand in for costly, domain-limited resources, which is important for portability.
- Empirical results: Strong and competitive performance on ACE (BLANC/MUC/positive-link F1 improvements over a strong prior system) and improved recall with more data (ACE++). The method’s performance with naive transitive closure outperforms BestLink in their setup, suggesting robust pairwise scoring.
- Careful ablations and diagnostics: The feature-class ablations (pairwise vs. +lexical vs. +sentential) clearly demonstrate the additive value of sentential features. The analysis of dataset properties (long-distance links, low argument overlap, differing triggers) is compelling and well-aligned with the model design.
- Simplicity/practicality: The architecture is simple, fast, and easy to port—no reliance on WordNet/FrameNet/VerbOcean/DBpedia/Geonames. This lowers barriers to application in new domains and languages.
- Error analysis: The qualitative discussion (e.g., pronoun-based event anaphora, annotation ambiguity) is thoughtful and identifies genuinely hard phenomena and annotation inconsistencies, providing useful guidance for follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: While the application to event coreference is fresh, the core components (CNN sentence encoder with position embeddings, piecewise pooling, pairwise scoring) are largely standard in relation/event extraction. The squared similarity activation is a minor tweak with limited theoretical or empirical justification (only a brief claim of outperforming alternatives; details are sparse).
- Reliance on gold annotations: The evaluation uses gold triggers (and the text suggests possible use of gold arguments via “argument overlap”). This makes the reported numbers optimistic and weakens claims about practical deployment. There’s no end-to-end evaluation to quantify sensitivity to trigger/argument errors.
- Reproducibility and resource release: It is unclear whether ACE++ will be released, and no code release is mentioned. Without public availability of the expanded dataset or implementation details beyond a hyperparameter table, reproducibility is limited.
- Comparative baselines and significance: The comparison set is heterogeneous (different splits/preprocessing), and no statistical significance testing is reported. There are no comparisons to modern neural coreference baselines (even mention-pair BERT-like encoders were not available at the time but stronger sentence encoders or RNNs/Tree models are discussed only in related work). More direct ablations against, e.g., cosine similarity or alternative encoders (RNN) would strengthen the case.
- Clustering and global reasoning: The clustering is simple transitive closure; while effective here, it can propagate early mistakes and lacks global consistency constraints. Given the long-distance nature of many links, a more principled global model could matter, and at least an oracle/upper bound or stronger alternative would help.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold arguments for the “argument overlap” pairwise feature? If not, what system produced arguments, and how accurate are they? Please clarify the exact supervision used for all non-gold features.
- Can you report end-to-end performance using predicted triggers (and arguments if applicable) to quantify robustness? Even a small-scale experiment on ACE would be helpful.
- Will you release the ACE++ annotations and/or your code? If not, can you at least release detailed preprocessing pipelines, splits, and trained models?
- Can you provide more details on the similarity layer ablation: which activations (cosine, bilinear, tanh/ReLU MLP, absolute value) were tried, with numbers? Also, what is the observed effect of dsim?
- How are positives/negatives sampled during training (all antecedents vs. nearest-positive Soon-style)? You mention two strategies; could you include quantitative results comparing them and the class-imbalance handling (loss weighting, sampling)?
- Given the prevalence of long-distance links, did you consider document-level context in the mention encoder (e.g., adding preceding sentence representations)? Any preliminary observations?

Overall recommendation
I am positively inclined. The central idea—replacing external semantic resources with learned sentential features for event coreference—is a meaningful and forward-looking contribution, and the empirical results plus ablations are solid. The method is not groundbreaking architecturally and relies on gold supervision, but given the conceptual shift and practical value, I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the gold-argument issue and improving reproducibility.
