Summary and main contributions
This paper addresses within-document event coreference (“event linking”) and proposes a two-stage neural model that (i) builds event-mention representations using sentence-level convolutional neural networks with piecewise max pooling around the trigger, augmented with trigger-local lexical features, and (ii) scores pairwise coreference decisions by combining these representations with lightweight pairwise features (type compatibility, discourse distance, modality/realis match, and argument overlap), followed by clustering via transitive closure. The model avoids external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet) and reports state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 and a larger in-house ACE++ dataset. Ablations and an error analysis are provided.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A resource-light event coreference model that leverages sentential CNN features to achieve competitive or better performance than prior systems that rely on rich external semantics.
2) An empirical study with thorough ablations demonstrating the contribution of sentential features over pairwise and trigger-local features, and an analysis of clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink).
3) Dataset analysis and error analysis that motivate the architectural choices (e.g., many positive pairs lack shared arguments and differ in triggers; long-distance links common) and highlight remaining challenges (e.g., pronominal/event anaphora).

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated modeling of sentential context: The paper convincingly motivates why sentence-level context is crucial (e.g., 50.97% of coreferent pairs share no arguments; 47.29% have different triggers; many links are long-distance). The CNN with position embeddings and piecewise pooling is a principled way to capture such context while remaining sentence-local and computationally tractable.
- Strong, resource-lean performance: On ACE, the model matches or exceeds prior state-of-the-art on BLANC, MUC, and positive-links, despite using no external semantic resources. This is important for portability across domains with limited lexical resources. On ACE++, the model further improves recall, showing benefits from more data.
- Solid experimental design with ablations: The paper systematically ablates feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local, sentential) and clustering strategies. Results clearly attribute gains to sentential features and justify the choice of transitive closure over BestLink in this setting. The pairwise-only baseline confirms that surface/type cues are insufficient.
- Transparent discussion of comparability caveats: The authors acknowledge different train/test splits and preprocessing choices in the literature and motivate their use of BLANC in class-imbalanced settings. This improves the integrity of the claims.
- Useful error analysis: The qualitative analysis identifies event anaphora and borderline annotation decisions as major sources of errors, pointing to future directions (discourse modeling, consistency across similar cases).

Weaknesses
- Evaluation comparability and metric coverage: While caveats are noted, the claim of state-of-the-art performance is weakened by (a) non-identical ACE splits and (b) omission of CEAF-e/m and CoNLL F1, which are standard in coreference evaluation alongside MUC and B3. Including CEAF and CoNLL scores would strengthen comparability and help assess over-merging/under-linking behavior more robustly than BLANC alone.
- Reliance on gold annotations beyond triggers is unclear: Pairwise features include event type compatibility, modality/realis match, and argument overlap. It is not made explicit whether these attributes are gold or predicted. If gold, the task becomes substantially easier and comparability to systems that predict these attributes is compromised. This needs clarification and, ideally, an evaluation with predicted types/modality/arguments.
- Limited realism of the setup: Using gold triggers (and possibly other gold attributes) sidesteps upstream errors. While this isolates the coreference component, it limits real-world relevance. A pipeline evaluation (predicted triggers and types) or at least a sensitivity analysis to upstream noise would add practical value.
- Reproducibility and dataset availability: ACE++ is not publicly available. Hyperparameters are tuned primarily on ACE++, and final settings are reused for ACE; however, without releasing code/models and an equivalent public dataset split, replicability is limited. Publishing code and detailed preprocessing, and reporting complete ACE document IDs for all splits, would mitigate this.
- Modeling scope: The model considers only sentence-local encoders per mention and pairwise decisions with post-hoc transitive closure. This ignores useful cross-sentential discourse signals and global cluster-level consistency during inference. Some competitors explore joint or global inference. Even a lightweight global feature or neural clustering objective might improve consistency.

Questions to authors
- What supervision is used for event type, modality/realis, and arguments in the pairwise features? Are these gold ACE annotations or predictions? If gold, can you report results with predicted attributes to assess robustness?
- Why omit CEAF and CoNLL F1? Can you provide these metrics on your ACE split to facilitate comparison with coreference literature?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of encoder? Did you compare the CNN to a BiLSTM encoder or CNN+attention? Any variance across random seeds?
- What is the average number of mentions per document and the O(n^2) pair count at inference? Any pruning strategies, and what is the runtime?
- Can you release code and trained models, and provide precise train/dev/test document lists (including for ACE++)?

Additional comments
- Reporting statistical significance for key comparisons (e.g., vs. Liu et al., 2014) would strengthen claims.
- The square activation for similarity is interesting; ablation vs. ReLU/tanh/cosine is briefly mentioned—please include quantitative results.
- Consider a lightweight document-level context (e.g., learned mention priors, section position embeddings) to help with long-distance and pronominal links.
- The error analysis could quantify the proportion of pronominal cases and ambiguous annotations over a larger sample for more robust conclusions.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed paper with clear motivation, a sensible neural architecture, and competitive results without external knowledge. Clarifying gold vs. predicted attributes and adding standard metrics would significantly strengthen the empirical claims.
