Summary and main contributions
- This paper addresses within-document event coreference (event linking). The core idea is to learn sentential representations of individual event mentions with a CNN (piecewise max pooling around the trigger), concatenate these with trigger-local embeddings, and combine the resulting per-mention vectors with pairwise features to score coreference. Pairwise decisions are clustered via transitive closure.
- The authors claim state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 without relying on external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet), and ablations show that sentential features materially improve performance. They also analyze model errors and compare clustering strategies (BestLink vs transitive closure). They introduce a larger in-house ACE++ dataset and show gains from more training data.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical event linking system that replaces heavy external semantic resources with CNN-based sentential features while remaining competitive with prior SOTA on ACE.
2) An empirical study demonstrating the utility of sentential features for event coreference via ablations and dataset-size analyses, including a careful look at clustering strategies.
3) An error analysis highlighting current limitations (e.g., pronoun-mediated coreference), which helps position future work.

Strengths
- Empirical effectiveness without external semantic resources: The model achieves competitive or better F1 on BLANC, MUC, and positive-link metrics compared to Liu et al. (2014), despite not using WordNet/FrameNet, etc. This is practically significant for portability to new domains where such resources are unavailable or unreliable.
- Clear architectural design and rationale: The two-stage design (mention representation then pairwise decision) is well motivated by data properties (e.g., many positive pairs have different triggers and share no arguments; long-distance links are common). The piecewise pooling around the trigger is sensible and connects to prior relation-extraction work.
- Informative ablations and analyses: The feature ablations (pairwise vs trigger-local vs sentential) are convincing; sentential features substantially improve F1. The dataset-size study (ACE → ACE++) shows recall gains, supporting the claim that the neural features generalize with more data. The clustering comparison (BestLink vs transitive closure) is useful and somewhat surprising, showing that strong pairwise decisions can obviate downstream filtering.
- Solid error analysis: The qualitative inspection identifies pronoun-mediated links as a key failure mode and highlights annotation ambiguities. This grounds the empirical results and clarifies what the current approach does and does not capture.
- Reasonable evaluation coverage: Results are reported on ACE with multiple coreference metrics (including BLANC), and baselines are included (even if simple). Hyperparameter search is described, and pretraining is standard.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The technical novelty is modest—using a standard CNN sentence encoder (with left/right pooling) and a simple pairwise scorer for event coreference is a straightforward adaptation of existing neural relation/event extraction approaches. There is no new architectural component specific to coreference (e.g., global inference, mention-ranking losses, or joint modeling).
- Use of gold information and unclear realism: The paper states that systems use gold triggers; however, the pairwise features include event type agreement, realis match, and argument overlap. It is unclear whether event types, realis, and arguments are gold or predicted. If gold, the setup is optimistic and weakens downstream applicability; if predicted, the paper should describe the upstream models and their performance. This ambiguity affects comparability and the practical significance of the reported gains.
- Dataset and reproducibility concerns: ACE++ is a substantial extension (1950 docs), but annotation procedures, guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, and availability are not described. Since much of the hyperparameter tuning and ablation evidence comes from ACE++, lack of public availability limits reproducibility and verification of claims.
- Evaluation caveats and missing rigor: The ACE split differs from prior work; there is no statistical significance testing; and one key competing system (Liu et al., 2014) outperforms on B-CUBED. Stronger non-neural baselines (e.g., well-tuned pairwise logistic/linear models with lexical/syntactic features) are not included, making it harder to isolate the gain due to sentential features alone. No per-type analysis or distance-based breakdown is provided to substantiate claims about handling long-distance or varying-type links.
- Limited modeling of discourse/coreference phenomena: The approach is purely local at the pairwise level with transitive closure and does not incorporate global constraints, entity-event interactions, or explicit discourse phenomena (e.g., pronominal/nominal event anaphora). The error analysis itself highlights pronoun resolution as a major gap. This limits the paper’s conceptual advance on the event coreference task.

Questions to authors
- Are event types, realis/modality, and arguments used in pairwise features gold-annotated or predicted? If predicted, please describe the upstream models and report their accuracy; if gold, please justify the choice and discuss expected performance under realistic prediction noise.
- How exactly is “argument overlap” computed (role matching? head-string match? coref-normalized entity arguments?), especially when mentions have missing arguments?
- How is the severe class imbalance (90% negative links) handled during training? Is there any loss reweighting, negative sampling, or threshold tuning beyond default logistic loss?
- Please clarify the ACE and ACE++ preprocessing: tokenization, sentence splitting, parsing (if any), and whether any external NER/coref was used to normalize arguments.
- Can you provide more details about ACE++: source selection, annotation protocol, IAA, and plans for releasing at least labels or scripts? Given its centrality to the ablations, availability would be valuable.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider reporting significance tests and per-type/per-distance breakdowns to better characterize where the gains come from.
- A simple, strong non-neural baseline trained on the same features (minus sentential CNN) would help isolate the contribution of CNN features.
- Discuss the role of pre-trained embeddings (they are an external resource in a sense) and whether domain-specific embeddings change results.
- Exploring light-weight global inference (e.g., best-first clustering with global features, or cluster-level representations) might address some over/under-merging without heavy complexity.
- The paper is generally clear; including the full hyperparameter table and implementation details would further improve reproducibility.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed empirical paper with modest novelty but useful evidence that sentential CNN features can substitute for external semantic resources in event linking, achieving competitive accuracy. Clarifying the use of gold information and improving reproducibility would strengthen the contribution.
