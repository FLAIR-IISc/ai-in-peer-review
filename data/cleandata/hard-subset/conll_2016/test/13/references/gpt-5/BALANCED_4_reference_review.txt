Summary
This paper tackles within-document event coreference (“event linking”). The key idea is to learn event-mention representations from their entire sentence using a CNN with word and position embeddings, piecewise max pooling, and a small MLP, then score mention pairs using (i) the two learned mention embeddings and (ii) a handful of pairwise features (type agreement, distance, modality match, argument overlap). Pairwise scores are thresholded and transitively closed into clusters. The model aims to remove reliance on external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet) while achieving state-of-the-art performance. Experiments on ACE 2005 and an extended collection (ACE++) show competitive BLANC F1 vs. prior work, ablations quantify the impact of sentential features, and an error analysis highlights challenges (e.g., pronominal events).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that sentential features learned by a CNN can support strong event coreference without external semantic resources. On ACE, BLANC F1 73.31 surpasses Liu et al. (2014; 70.43), with on-par results in other metrics given setup differences.

2) Empirical analysis and ablation isolating the contribution of sentence-level features for event linking. On ACE++, using pairwise+local+sentential features reaches BLANC F1 76.90, vs. 64.12 without sentential features and 56.69 with only pairwise features.

3) Practical insights on clustering strategies and data properties. Contrary to earlier reports, BestLink reduces recall substantially in this setup (e.g., BLANC recall 83.21 → 68.86 on ACE++), and dataset analysis motivates cross-sentential modeling (e.g., 55.42% positive pairs >5 sentences apart).

Strengths
1) Clear, well-motivated architectural choice that leverages sentential context. The preliminary data analysis (e.g., 47.29% positive pairs have different triggers; 50.97% share no arguments) strongly motivates modeling beyond local lexical features. The CNN encoder with position embeddings and piecewise pooling is appropriate and computationally simple.

2) Solid empirical gains without external semantic resources. The model matches or exceeds prior state-of-the-art that relies on extensive semantic features (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet) on ACE BLANC F1 (73.31 vs. 70.43), strengthening the claim of portability to domains lacking such resources.

3) Informative ablations and diagnostics. The systematic feature ablations on ACE++ quantify where gains come from (sentential features contribute the largest boost). The clustering comparison (transitive closure vs. BestLink) is carefully analyzed and counters earlier findings, adding useful practical guidance.

4) Error analysis with concrete phenomena. The manual audit of 100 FPs/FNs surfaces pronominal event mentions as a major source of false negatives and highlights annotation ambiguities; this helps chart future improvements and contextualizes the absolute scores.

5) Sensible metric choice and discussion. The use of BLANC (balancing positives and negatives) is justified given the extreme class imbalance; reporting multiple metrics offers a fuller picture.

Weaknesses
1) Unclear reliance on gold annotations beyond triggers, limiting realism. Pairwise features include type agreement, modality match, and argument overlap. It is not explicit whether event types, modalities, and arguments are gold or predicted. If gold, the evaluation inflates performance and undermines claims about practical applicability; if predicted, the paper should specify the upstream systems and their accuracies. This is a central clarity gap.

2) Comparability and reproducibility concerns. Results on ACE are not on a standard split, and comparison systems use varying preprocessing and resources. Statistical significance is not reported. The extended ACE++ corpus is not clearly described in terms of annotation process, quality (IAA), and availability, limiting reproducibility and independent verification.

3) Novelty is incremental. While applying CNN-based sentential features to event coreference is a reasonable adaptation of prior work in event/relation extraction, the coreference modeling is a standard pairwise scoring plus transitive closure; no new learning or inference ideas for coreference are introduced.

4) Limited evaluation scope. The model is only evaluated in a pipeline with gold triggers (and possibly more gold pieces). No end-to-end evaluation with predicted mentions/types is presented, and no cross-document or cross-domain tests are run. Given the stated goal of domain portability, an evaluation on a publicly available cross-document/coreference dataset (e.g., ECB+) or a different domain would strengthen claims.

5) Some missing training and hyperparameter details. Although a hyperparameter search is mentioned and Table 2 is referenced, essential details (final settings, window sizes, dimensions) are not fully presented in this submission excerpt. Without code or full specs, reproduction is difficult.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold or predicted event types, modalities, and arguments when computing pairwise features? Please provide results under both settings if possible.
- If arguments and types are gold at test time, what happens when they are predicted? Can you report end-to-end performance with a standard event extraction pipeline?
- How was ACE++ annotated? Who performed the annotation, with what guidelines and IAA? Will ACE++ (or at least the splits) be released?
- Can you report statistical significance for the ACE improvements vs. Liu et al. (2014), and adopt a standard ACE split to enable apples-to-apples comparison?
- How sensitive are results to the CNN encoder choices (e.g., filter widths, piecewise pooling vs. global pooling) and to pre-trained embeddings?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider adding a stronger modern baseline (e.g., pairwise encoder using a BiLSTM over the sentence or contextual encoders) to isolate the effect of CNNs vs. sentential modeling per se.
- Evaluate clustering with learned or constrained decoding beyond transitive closure and BestLink (e.g., ILP or mention-ranking with cluster-level consistency), especially given the recall-precision trade-offs observed.
- Expand the error analysis to quantify how often pronoun resolution would be needed and whether simple heuristics (e.g., coreference resolution of nominalized/pronominal mentions) could help.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is well-motivated and empirically solid within its stated scope, and the ablations are informative. However, unclear reliance on gold information, non-standard splits, and limited novelty and evaluation breadth weaken the contribution for a conference acceptance. With clarified evaluation (especially predicted settings), standardized comparisons, and broader testing, this work could be a strong submission.
