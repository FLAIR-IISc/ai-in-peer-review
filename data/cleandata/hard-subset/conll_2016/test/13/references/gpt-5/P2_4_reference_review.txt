Summary and contributions
- Contribution 1 (primary): A two-step event coreference model that builds sentence-level event mention representations with a CNN (using word and position embeddings, piecewise max pooling, and a small MLP), and then scores mention pairs with a simple feed-forward similarity function plus standard pairwise features. The key claim is that this avoids external semantic resources (WordNet/FrameNet) while achieving state-of-the-art performance on ACE.
- Contribution 2: An empirical study showing (i) sentential features materially improve coreference performance beyond trigger-local and pairwise features, (ii) more training data (ACE++) mostly boosts recall, and (iii) simple transitive-closure clustering outperforms BestLink for this model. Includes several ablations and naive baselines.
- Contribution 3: Error analysis exposing common failure modes (e.g., pronominal/gapped event mentions) and annotation ambiguities in ACE-style event coreference.

Strengths
- Strong empirical showing without external semantic resources. The paper demonstrates that sentence-level neural representations (CNN with relative positions and piecewise pooling) plus a lightweight pairwise module can match or exceed prior resource-heavy systems on ACE for BLANC, MUC, and positive-link F1. This matters for portability across domains where WordNet/FrameNet features are costly or brittle.
- Careful experimental analysis. The paper goes beyond a single headline number: it analyzes dataset properties (e.g., high rate of missing arguments and non-identical triggers, long-distance links), compares clustering strategies, adds data (ACE++), and runs multiple ablations isolating the value of pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential features. The observed trends (e.g., recall gains with more data; sentential features improving both P/R) are coherent and informative.
- Clear and simple model. The architecture is easy to reproduce and extend: sentence encoders trained end-to-end, explicit feature concatenation, square activation to enforce polarity invariance in similarity, and a straightforward clustering heuristic. Design choices are motivated and, in several cases, empirically backed by ablations/hyperparameter search.
- Error analysis and discussion of annotation ambiguity. The qualitative inspection of false positives/negatives is valuable and appropriately tempers the interpretation of absolute scores, highlighting dataset-intrinsic ambiguity and difficult cross-sentential pronominal references.
- Practicality. The model makes only commonly available inputs (tokenization, POS/parse optional) and avoids curated semantic lexicons, lowering the barrier to deployment.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. The architecture is essentially a standard mention-pair model where mention representations are obtained with a CNN over the sentence and combined with hand-crafted pairwise features. Using sentential CNN features for IE was known; the step to event coreference is incremental. The square activation is a minor tweak. There is no global or joint inference, no learning-to-cluster, and no attempt to model discourse structure beyond distance features.
- Evaluation realism and fairness. Experiments assume gold event triggers (and the pairwise features include type compatibility and argument overlap; it is unclear whether arguments are gold). This setup overstates practical performance and complicates comparison with prior work that used predicted arguments/SRL. The paper notes split and preprocessing differences, but the SOTA claim remains hard to substantiate without matched settings. Also, coreference evaluation typically reports CEAF and CoNLL average; CEAF is missing.
- ACE++ details and reproducibility. The 1,351-document ACE++ extension is described as “annotated in an analogous fashion” but the annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, genre mix, availability, and license are not specified. Without release and quality details, the gains on ACE++ are harder to interpret or reproduce. Code release is not mentioned.
- Baselines and comparisons. While ablations are useful, the paper lacks stronger contemporary baselines: e.g., (i) a purely feature-based feed-forward mention-pair model without CNN sentence encoders, (ii) a BiLSTM or transformer-based sentence encoder, or (iii) a simple cosine similarity over pre-trained embeddings. No statistical significance tests are reported; many deltas are modest and could be within variance.
- Scope vs. identified errors. The largest error source involves pronominal or abstract references to events and broader discourse phenomena. The proposed architecture does not attempt to address these (no document context modeling, no coreference-aware encoders, no joint entity-event modeling). This limits the advance beyond showing that sentence encoders help.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold event arguments and gold event types in computing “argument overlap,” “type compatibility,” and “realis match”? If not, what upstream systems and accuracies are assumed? Please quantify performance with predicted triggers/types/arguments to assess realism.
- Will ACE++ (documents, annotations, and splits) be released? What was the annotation protocol, annotator training, inter-annotator agreement, and quality control? How do its domains/genres compare to ACE?
- Please report CEAF_e and the CoNLL average (MUC, B3, CEAF) to align with common coreference evaluation practice, in addition to BLANC.
- What is performance if you remove all pairwise hand-crafted features (distance, type, realis, arguments) and rely solely on the learned mention representations? Conversely, what if you keep pairwise features but remove the CNN sentence encoder?
- Can you provide variance across multiple random seeds and significance tests for key comparisons (ACE SOTA, clustering strategy, ACE vs. ACE++)?
- Runtime and complexity: number of parameters, average inference time per document, and any pruning strategies for long documents with many mentions.

Overall assessment
This is a solid empirical paper with a clear takeaway: sentential neural features enable competitive event coreference without external semantic resources. The methodological novelty is modest, and the evaluation would be stronger with matched settings, CEAF/CoNLL scores, clarity about gold vs. predicted inputs, and public release of ACE++. With these clarifications and, ideally, an additional setting with predicted triggers/types/arguments, this would make a valuable contribution.
