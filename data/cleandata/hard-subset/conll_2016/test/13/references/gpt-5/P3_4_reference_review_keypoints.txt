1. Paper addresses within-document event coreference with a two-stage neural model: CNN-based sentence encoder plus pairwise scorer with hand-crafted features and transitive closure clustering.  
2. Encoder uses word + position embeddings and piecewise max-pooling around triggers, concatenated with trigger-local embeddings.  
3. Pairwise scorer combines mention vectors with features (type agreement, distance, realis, argument overlap), uses element-wise square similarity, and predicts coreference.  
4. Model avoids external semantic resources (WordNet/FrameNet) and relies on pretrained word embeddings and standard preprocessing.  
5. Claims state-of-the-art or competitive performance on ACE and ACE++ using BLANC/MUC F1 and shows gains from sentential features.  
6. Contribution: shows sentence-level neural features can match prior resource-heavy systems for event linking.  
7. Contribution: empirical analysis of feature classes (pairwise vs trigger-local vs sentential) and clustering strategies (transitive closure vs BestLink).  
8. Contribution: introduces a larger ACE++ dataset for tuning and evaluates with multiple coref metrics plus error analysis.  
9. Strength: strong empirical results reducing dependence on external semantic resources, improving portability.  
10. Strength: well-motivated mention encoding (position embeddings, piecewise pooling) with convincing ablation showing sentential features help.  
11. Strength: thorough experimental design—multiple metrics, clustering comparisons, data-scaling study, and thoughtful error analysis.  
12. Strength: practical and reproducible approach using minimal preprocessing and pretrained embeddings.  
13. Strength: interpretable pairwise features aid understanding and targeted analysis.  
14. Weakness: reliance on gold annotations (triggers, possibly types/realis/arguments) may overestimate real-world performance; end-to-end evaluation missing.  
15. Weakness: limited novelty in scoring/clustering; minimal global reasoning and no comparison to common matching architectures.  
16. Weakness: ACE++ resource under-described and not public, harming reproducibility (missing guidelines, IAA, coverage).  
17. Weakness: unclear extent of external supervision/preprocessing—whether pairwise attributes are predicted or gold is unspecified.  
18. Weakness: analysis lacks granular breakdowns by event type, distance buckets, and argument availability to pinpoint strengths/failures.  
19. Question: Are event arguments and realis/modality labels gold when computing pairwise features; if not, what systems/accuracies were used?  
20. Question: What are exact representations and normalizations for pairwise features (one-hot, embeddings, scalar; distance binning)?  
21. Question: Why use element-wise square similarity and how does it compare to alternatives (cosine, bilinear, product/abs-diff+MLP)?  
22. Question: Please provide per-type and distance-based performance and results for pairs with/without shared arguments to justify claims about sentential features.  
23. Question: For ACE++: who annotated it, what guidelines/IAA, and will the corpus or annotations/scripts be released?  
24. Question: Is the 0.5 threshold fixed or tuned per validation; were calibration or class-weighting strategies tried for imbalance?  
25. Suggestion: Explore global inference or learning-to-cluster methods (ILP, best-first) to improve long-distance linking.  
26. Suggestion: Address pronoun-based event mentions via anaphora cues and cross-sentential encoders (BiLSTM/Transformer, discourse features).  
27. Suggestion: Clarify whether syntactic parses were used and whether including them would provide gains.
