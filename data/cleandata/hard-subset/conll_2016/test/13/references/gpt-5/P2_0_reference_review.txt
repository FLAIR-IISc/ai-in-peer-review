Summary
The paper tackles within-document event coreference (event linking). It proposes a two-step model: (a) represent each event mention with a CNN over the sentential context plus trigger-local lexical embeddings; (b) combine the two mention representations with pairwise features (e.g., type agreement, distance, modality match, argument overlap) and a shallow neural similarity (square nonlinearity) followed by logistic regression. Pairwise decisions are clustered via transitive closure. The model avoids external semantic resources (WordNet, FrameNet), relying on learned sentential features and simple features. Experiments on ACE 2005 and an expanded ACE++ dataset show state-of-the-art or on-par results against prior systems that use richer external features. The paper includes ablations (feature classes, clustering), error analysis, and a motivation based on dataset statistics.

Claimed/main contributions (as I see them)
1) A resource-light event coreference model that uses CNN-based sentential features for mention representations and achieves competitive SOTA on ACE without external semantics. This is the central methodological and empirical contribution.
2) Empirical study on a larger ACE++ dataset with detailed ablations showing the impact of sentential, lexical, and pairwise features, and of clustering strategy (BestLink vs transitive closure).
3) Error analysis and dataset statistics motivating design choices (e.g., many positive pairs lack shared arguments; many positives have different triggers; long-distance links common).

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with reduced feature engineering: The model matches or exceeds prior SOTA on several metrics (BLANC, MUC, positive links) while avoiding external resources. This supports the central claim that sentential neural features can substitute for hand-crafted semantic resources. The comparison to Liu et al. (2014) is particularly compelling given their heavy semantic feature use.
- Solid, well-motivated architecture: The two-stage design is appropriate for event coreference; the CNN sentence encoder with position embeddings and piecewise max-pooling is a reasonable way to capture sentential context around triggers and handle multiple mentions per sentence. The dedicated similarity layer and simple clustering make the approach clear and reproducible conceptually.
- Thorough ablations and analyses: The paper quantifies the contribution of feature classes (pairwise vs lexical vs sentential) and of clustering strategies, and examines dataset properties that motivate the approach. The finding that BestLink hurts recall here (in contrast to prior work) is insightful and grounded in results.
- Sensible evaluation choices and discussion: Reporting BLANC is appropriate given class imbalance. The authors candidly discuss caveats (varying splits, preprocessing differences), which aids honest interpretation.
- Error analysis with concrete phenomena: The inspection of 100 FPs/FNs surfaces pronoun/event anaphora and annotation ambiguities as key pain points, giving actionable directions.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: Applying CNN-based sentential features to event coreference is a relatively straightforward extension of prior CNNs for relation/event extraction. The pairwise decision + clustering paradigm is standard; the square activation for similarity is a modest tweak without deep theoretical grounding. The paper’s novelty lies more in application and empirical validation than in methodological innovation.
- Ambiguity about gold information and fairness of comparison: The paper states gold triggers are used, but pairwise features include “argument overlap.” Are gold arguments used? If so, results are not end-to-end and may not be directly comparable to systems that use predicted arguments; if not, how are arguments obtained? The comparison across different ACE splits and preprocessing pipelines further complicates claims of SOTA. Statistical significance tests are not reported.
- Reproducibility and resource release: While hyperparameter search is described, code, trained models, and (especially) ACE++ are not clearly released. ACE++ is central to the best results and ablations; lack of availability limits impact and reproducibility. Only a list of ACE split documents is provided for ACE, not full experimental artifacts.
- Limited modeling of global constraints: Clustering via transitive closure is simple and can propagate local errors; no global coherence constraints or joint modeling (e.g., entity-event interactions, cluster-level features) are explored. Given that long-distance links are common, more global structure could matter.
- Evaluation scope: Results are limited to within-document ACE/ACE++; no cross-document evaluation, no end-to-end pipeline setting (trigger and argument prediction), and no comparisons to stronger neural encoders (e.g., RNN/attention) are provided. The “square vs cosine” comparison is mentioned but not quantified.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold event arguments when computing “argument overlap” and “realis match,” or are these predicted? Please clarify exactly which gold annotations are assumed at test time and how this aligns with compared systems.
- Will you release ACE++ (or an anonymized subset/annotation schema) and code/trained models to enable replication of Tables 4–6?
- What are the absolute numbers for your comparison of similarity functions (square vs cosine/bilinear)? Any sensitivity to dsim?
- How are cases with multiple triggers in a sentence handled precisely in the piecewise pooling setup? Do overlapping contexts cause leakage between mentions?
- Did you test statistical significance of improvements over Liu et al. (2014) across metrics?

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed, clearly written empirical paper showing that sentential CNN features can deliver strong event coreference performance without external semantic resources. The novelty is moderate, but the ablations and careful evaluation add value. Provided the authors clarify the use of gold information and commit to releasing ACE++/code, I lean toward acceptance as a solid, resource-light baseline and informative study. Without such clarifications/releases, the impact is reduced.
