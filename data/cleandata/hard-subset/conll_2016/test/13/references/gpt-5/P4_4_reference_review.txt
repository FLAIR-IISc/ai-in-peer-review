Summary and main contributions
The paper addresses within-document event coreference (event linking) and proposes a two-step neural architecture: (a) a CNN-based encoder that builds an event-mention representation from the whole sentence using word and position embeddings with piecewise max-pooling plus trigger-local lexical features; (b) a pairwise scoring module that combines two mention representations with a small set of pairwise features (type compatibility, discourse position/distance, realis/modality match, argument overlap), computes a “distributed similarity” via a squared activation, and outputs a coreference score with logistic regression. Clustering is done by transitive closure over positive links. The authors evaluate on ACE 2005 and a larger ACE++ dataset, compare with prior work, conduct ablations of feature classes, analyze clustering strategies, and provide an error analysis.

Contributions (as assessed):
1) Empirical demonstration that sentential CNN features, combined with light pairwise features, can achieve state-of-the-art event coreference performance on ACE without external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet), supported by ablation studies.
2) A practical neural architecture for event-mention encoding and pairwise similarity (including a simple square nonlinearity and piecewise pooling) with analysis of clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) and data properties.
3) A new, larger ACE++ dataset used to study data scaling effects and feature contributions (though availability is unclear).

Strengths
- Competitive performance without external resources: On ACE, the method matches or exceeds prior state of the art in several metrics (notably BLANC F1), while avoiding reliance on WordNet/FrameNet or other domain-specific resources. This supports the claim that sentential features can substitute for many hand-crafted semantic features and improves portability.
- Clear, sound model design with ablations: The architecture is well-motivated and technically sound. Ablations (pairwise-only, +trigger-local, +sentential) show consistent gains from sentential features in both precision and recall, strengthening the causal link between the proposed feature class and observed performance. The left/right piecewise pooling design suitably handles multiple mentions per sentence.
- Thorough empirical analysis: The paper reports dataset statistics (argument sharing, trigger mismatch, distance between coreferent mentions) that motivate modeling choices, compares clustering strategies (showing transitive closure outperforms BestLink under this classifier), examines data scaling (ACE→ACE++ improves recall), and includes baselines and an informative error analysis (pronoun resolution and annotation ambiguity).
- Reproducibility of training protocol: The paper describes implementation details (TensorFlow, Adam), initialization, hyperparameter search strategy, and evaluation metrics, and releases ACE split document lists, improving transparency relative to typical ACE evaluations.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and completeness of comparisons: Results are on gold event triggers (and possibly types/arguments). The paper notes that systems use different ACE splits and preprocessing, which weakens cross-paper comparability. It is unclear whether pairwise features such as argument overlap and event type agreement use gold annotations or predicted components; this significantly affects difficulty and applicability. No experiments with predicted triggers/types are reported, limiting conclusions about end-to-end usefulness.
- Limited novelty in modeling: The core components (CNN sentence encoder with position embeddings and piecewise pooling; pairwise classifier with engineered features) are adaptations of established techniques from relation classification to event coreference. The proposed square activation for similarity is a minor architectural tweak. The contribution is primarily an application and empirical validation rather than a conceptual advance.
- Dataset availability and replicability: ACE++ is central to several findings (notably recall gains and ablations), but its availability and annotation process are not clearly stated. Without public release or detailed guidelines/quality control, replicating results and using ACE++ for future comparisons will be difficult.
- Scope of evaluation: The study focuses on within-document coreference with gold triggers; there is no cross-document setting or joint extraction evaluation, and no statistical significance testing is reported. The clustering is a simple transitive closure; more global inference or learning-to-cluster could further test robustness. Error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a key failure mode, but no quantitative breakdown or controlled experiments (e.g., with coref-resolved mentions) are provided.

Questions to authors
- Data and supervision:
  - Do pairwise features (event type agreement, modality/realis match, argument overlap) rely on gold annotations or predictions? If predicted, which tools and what accuracy? Please quantify performance degradation when using predicted triggers/types/arguments.
  - Is the ACE++ dataset publicly available? If so, where, and what are the annotation guidelines and inter-annotator agreement? If not, can you release at least the document lists and annotation format to facilitate replication?

- Modeling and training:
  - Why choose the square activation for similarity over alternatives (e.g., absolute value, bilinear, cosine, or learned symmetric functions)? Can you provide a small ablation with significance testing across random seeds?
  - How sensitive are results to CNN hyperparameters (filter widths, number of filters) and to using other encoders (e.g., BiLSTM with attention)? Any observed trade-offs?

- Evaluation:
  - Can you report results with predicted triggers/types to assess end-to-end impact? Even a controlled experiment on ACE with a standard trigger detector would be informative.
  - For the argument overlap feature, what proportion of mentions have arguments available, and how often are arguments shared across coreferent pairs? An ablation that removes argument-based pairwise features would help isolate their contribution under realistic settings.
