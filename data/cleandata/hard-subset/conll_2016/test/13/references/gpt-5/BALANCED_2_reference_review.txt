Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses within-document event coreference (“event linking”). It proposes a two-step model: (a) a CNN-based encoder to produce sentential representations for individual event mentions (with piecewise max-pooling around the trigger and small trigger-local lexical features), and (b) a mention-pair classifier that combines the two encodings with a set of pairwise features (type/realis agreement, distance, argument overlap) to score coreference. Pairwise scores are clustered via transitive closure. The authors report state-of-the-art or on-par results on ACE 2005 and introduce a larger ACE++ collection, along with ablations, clustering analyses, and an error analysis.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Demonstration that neural sentential features (via CNNs) can replace external semantic resources for event linking, achieving competitive SOTA on ACE without WordNet/FrameNet/VerbOcean-like features.
2) Empirical study of design choices for event linking: feature ablations (pairwise vs trigger-local vs sentential), clustering strategy (BestLink vs transitive closure), and pair generation strategies.
3) Introduction and experimental use of a larger ACE++ dataset, and an informative error analysis highlighting event anaphora challenges.

Strengths
- Clear motivation grounded in data analysis. The paper quantifies that many positive pairs share no arguments (≈51%), often have different triggers (≈47%), and are frequently far apart (>5 sentences in ≈55%). This motivates modeling sentential context rather than relying heavily on argument overlap or local lexical cues, and it is consistent with the architectural choices.

- Competitive performance without external semantic resources. On ACE, the model exceeds or matches strong prior systems in several metrics (e.g., BLANC F1 73.31 vs 70.43 for Liu et al., 2014; improvements also in MUC F1 and positive-link F1). This is meaningful given the portability concerns of resource-heavy systems.

- Thorough ablation studies. The paper systematically removes feature classes and shows monotonic gains when adding trigger-local and then sentential features over pairwise-only baselines. This supports the claim that learned sentential representations contribute substantially.

- Analysis of clustering strategies and data scale. The finding that transitive closure outperforms BestLink in this setting, contrary to prior reports, is interesting and backed by precision/recall trade-offs. Scaling from ACE to ACE++ increases recall substantially, indicating the approach benefits from more data.

- Useful error analysis. The qualitative inspection surfaces event anaphora/pronominal references as a key failure mode and highlights annotation ambiguity in borderline cases, providing direction for future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling. The architecture is a fairly standard mention-pair model with CNN sentence encodings; CNNs for sentence-level event/relation features are known from prior extraction work, and pairwise classification with simple clustering is conventional in coreference. The “square” activation and dsim vector are minor tweaks; comparisons to other similarity functions are mentioned but not substantiated with numbers.

- Evaluation comparability and realism. The paper uses gold event triggers (and appears to use gold types and possibly gold arguments for pairwise features like argument overlap and realis/type agreement). Prior work sometimes used predicted arguments/SRL. This can inflate performance and complicate comparisons. The ACE splits differ from the literature; no statistical significance tests or variance across runs are reported.

- ACE++ dataset lacks detail and availability. The paper adds 1,351 web documents “annotated in an analogous fashion,” but provides no annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, or clear availability. Without release and quality controls, results on ACE++ are hard to interpret and reproduce, and claims about generalization are weakened.

- Reliance on hand-designed pairwise features. While external semantic resources are avoided, the model still leans on engineered pairwise features (type/realis compatibility, argument overlap, position/distance). Computing argument overlap presupposes reasonably accurate argument extraction or access to gold roles—a strong assumption in open-domain settings—so the claim of not relying on “semantic features” is somewhat overstated.

- Modeling limitations vs task properties. Despite showing that many coreferent mentions are far apart, the model does not encode cross-sentential discourse context beyond the two local sentences and coarse distance features, and lacks global inference beyond transitive closure. No joint modeling with entity coref/argument sharing or global consistency is attempted, which could better address long-distance links and chain-level constraints.

Questions to authors
1) What supervision sources are gold vs predicted at test time? Specifically: event types, realis, and event arguments used for “type compatibility,” “realis match,” and “argument overlap.” Please report results when these are predicted (and the upstream component’s accuracy).

2) ACE++ details: Who annotated, with what guidelines, and what is the inter-annotator agreement? Will you release the data (or at least the annotations) and official splits?

3) Class imbalance and training: Given the extreme skew toward negative pairs, do you use any loss reweighting, downsampling of negatives, or curriculum strategies? What is the positive/negative ratio in training pairs?

4) Variance and significance: How stable are results across random seeds? Please provide mean/std over multiple runs and significance testing vs baselines.

5) Comparisons of similarity functions: Can you include a table comparing square vs ReLU/tanh/cosine/bilinear and common pairwise feature combinations (concat, abs-diff, elementwise product) to substantiate the choice?

6) Metrics: Please add CEAF (φ4) results for completeness; many coref papers report MUC/B3/CEAF/BLANC.

7) Reproducibility: Provide the full set of hyperparameters (Table 2), filter widths, position embedding ranges, and release code if possible.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen the evaluation with predicted triggers, types, realis, and arguments to assess end-to-end utility; report both gold and predicted settings.

- Provide/release ACE++ with detailed annotation documentation and IAA, or alternatively evaluate on a public event-coref dataset with established protocols.

- Explore stronger/global inference (e.g., agglomerative clustering with learned linkage, ILP/greedy transitivity-aware merges) and integrate entity coref/argument-sharing constraints.

- Consider cross-sentence encoders (e.g., document-level context) or mention-context windows to better capture long-distance dependencies identified in the data analysis.

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject. The empirical results are solid and the analysis is useful, but the methodological novelty is limited, and the evaluation setup (use of gold information and non-public data) reduces the strength and comparability of the claims. Addressing the evaluation concerns and releasing resources would significantly strengthen the paper.
