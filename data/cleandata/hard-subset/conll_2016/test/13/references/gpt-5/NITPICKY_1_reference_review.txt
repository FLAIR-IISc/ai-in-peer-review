Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper addresses within-document event coreference (event linking) and proposes a two-step neural model: (a) a CNN-based encoder producing sentential representations of individual event mentions augmented with trigger-local embeddings, and (b) a pairwise scorer that combines two mention representations with simple pairwise features, followed by transitive-closure clustering. The model avoids external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet).
- Empirically, the approach reports competitive results on ACE 2005 and improved recall with more data (ACE++), plus ablations showing contributions of sentential features.
- Error analysis highlights pronoun/event anaphora challenges and some annotation inconsistency.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for using sentential features: dataset analysis (e.g., many coreferent pairs lack argument overlap and have different triggers) convincingly argues for sentence-level context beyond intrinsic event properties.
- Simple, resource-light architecture: avoiding external semantic resources is attractive for portability across domains without expensive knowledge bases.
- Thorough ablations (to a point): removing sentential vs trigger-local vs pairwise features provides evidence that sentential features materially help, and comparison of clustering strategies (BestLink vs transitive closure) is informative.
- Implementation transparency in parts: optimization choices (Adam), use of pre-trained embeddings, and pairing strategy are described; preliminary dataset statistics are helpful.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation comparability and metrics: The ACE split is non-standard and not aligned with prior works; competitors used different splits and preprocessing. The paper does not report CEAF (φ4 or e) nor the CoNLL average, which are standard for coreference; relying on BLANC/MUC/B3 and a “positive links” metric complicates fair SOTA claims. No significance testing is provided; reported gains are small and mixed (e.g., Liu et al. outperform on B3 F1).
- Use of gold information and fairness: It appears that gold triggers are used for all systems, but the paper’s pairwise features include “argument overlap,” “realis match,” and “modality agreement.” It is unclear whether these come from gold annotations or predicted tools; if gold, results overestimate realistic performance and are not directly comparable to systems using predicted arguments/SRL. This is a critical clarity gap that undermines the main claim (state-of-the-art without external features).
- ACE++ dataset: The paper relies on a much larger ACE++ dataset “annotated in an analogous fashion” but provides no annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, or availability. Claims of improved recall hinge on this proprietary resource, which impacts reproducibility and limits the community’s ability to validate results.
- Methodological detail gaps: Many essential hyperparameters and implementation details are missing or relegated to a referenced Table 2 not available here (dw, dp, dc, de, dsim, window size n actually used, dropout/L2 settings, Adam parameters, batch size, class weighting to handle extreme imbalance, max sentence length/padding). Handling of multi-word triggers is unclear; the model seems to assume single-token triggers and only concatenates left/right neighbor embeddings. How “modality/realis” and “argument overlap” are computed (gold vs predicted, which system) is unspecified.
- Pairing and thresholding: The classifier uses a fixed threshold of 0.5 with transitive closure; no calibration/tuning or validation-based threshold selection is reported. The training pair generation includes all antecedent candidates, likely dominated by trivial negatives (e.g., different types) without negative sampling strategy; this may inflate headline precision/recall and limit generalization. BestLink vs transitive-closure conclusions may be dataset/threshold dependent.
- Novelty is incremental: Applying CNN-based sentential features to event coreference is a reasonable extension of prior neural IE work, but the architectural novelty (piecewise pooling around trigger; square activation for similarity) is minor and insufficiently justified with quantitative ablations (e.g., no numbers vs cosine/other activations).

Questions to Authors
- Do you use gold arguments, gold realis/modality, and gold event types for the pairwise features? If not, which tools produce them, and with what accuracy? Please quantify performance gaps when using predicted vs gold features.
- How are multi-word triggers handled? If triggers can be multi-token, what is the representation (head only, average, span pooling)?
- Please provide the exact hyperparameters used (dw, dp, dc, de, dsim, n, dropout rates/L2, Adam η, β1, β2, ε, batch size, max sentence length/padding). Are word embeddings fine-tuned?
- Did you tune the decision threshold on the validation set? If not, how sensitive is BLANC/MUC/B3 to threshold choices? Any calibration?
- For class imbalance, did you use loss weighting or negative sampling? What is the positive:negative ratio during training?
- How are “argument overlap” and “distance (#intermediate event mentions)” computed when mentions are in different sentences or far apart? Are arguments constrained to same sentence mentions only?
- Can you report CEAF and the CoNLL average to facilitate comparison? If not, why?
- ACE++: Who annotated it, following which guidelines? What is IAA? Will it be released? If not, can you at least release the train/valid/test document lists and code?
- Did you restrict training/testing to candidate pairs with matching coarse or fine type? If not, what fraction of negatives are cross-type, and does this make training too easy?

Additional comments
- Many minor writing/formatting issues (typos: “enrichtment,” “wrt.”, inconsistent footnotes/parentheses, missing ε in Adam parameters list; figures/tables referenced but partially informative). Please proofread.
- The “square” non-linearity justification is plausible, but please add a small ablation table with actual numbers vs tanh/ReLU/cosine to support the choice.
- Consider reporting a stronger non-neural baseline (e.g., logistic regression over your pairwise features; TF-IDF cosine with threshold) for ACE and ACE++.
- Claiming “state-of-the-art” should be tempered given metric/reporting differences and missing CEAF/CoNLL scores.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The idea is reasonable and results are promising, but clarity on gold vs predicted features, missing standard metrics, reliance on a non-public dataset, and insufficient detail/ablations make the empirical claims hard to verify. With clarified evaluation, standard metrics, and reproducible settings, this could be a solid contribution.
