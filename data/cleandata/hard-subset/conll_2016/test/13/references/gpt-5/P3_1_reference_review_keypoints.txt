- Paper proposes within-document event coreference using sentence-level CNN (piecewise max-pooling + position embeddings) with trigger-local lexical features, pairwise shallow similarity network, and clustering by transitive closure.
- Claims comparable or better performance than prior work without external lexical-semantic resources, evaluated on ACE 2005 and a larger ACE++ dataset, with ablations and error analysis.
- Achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results (especially BLANC) and shows improved recall with larger ACE++ training data.

- Strength: Clear, sensible modeling—learning mention representations from full sentences is well-motivated and the piecewise CNN with position embeddings focuses context around triggers.
- Strength: Simple square nonlinearity in similarity layer provides polarity-invariant similarity and is an interesting, lightweight tweak.
- Strength: Strong experimental setup—evaluations on ACE and ACE++, multiple metrics including BLANC, ablations isolating feature contributions, and clustering strategy comparisons.
- Strength: Competitive results without heavy semantic resources, improving portability across domains and languages.
- Strength: Useful manual error analysis highlighting pronoun-based event anaphora and annotation inconsistencies as major error sources.
- Strength: Problem relevance—reducing reliance on curated semantic resources is timely and practical.

- Weakness: Unclear supervision realism—paper does not state whether argument roles and event types used in pairwise features are gold at test time, affecting comparability and the “no external resources” claim.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—architecture mainly adapts known components (position-aware CNN, max-pooling, shallow MLP); the contribution is largely empirical/integrative.
- Weakness: Dataset and reproducibility concerns—ACE++ lacks description (protocol, IAA, licensing) and may not be public; ACE splits differ across works, CEAF metrics omitted, and no statistical significance testing reported.
- Weakness: Scope mismatch—motivating analysis cites long-distance coreference, but model encodes only sentence-local information and simple pairwise features, lacking discourse/document-level modeling.
- Weakness: Baselines could be stronger—no comparison to modern contextualized encoders (BiLSTM/attention, pretrained models) and missing CEAF/CoNLL average for standard coref evaluation.
- Weakness: Missing details on hyperparameters, class imbalance handling, and early stopping.

- Question: Do you use gold event arguments and gold event types at test time for pairwise features, and if not, what upstream systems and accuracies are used?
- Question: How are multi-token triggers and multiple mentions per sentence handled in the CNN and position embeddings?
- Question: How is class imbalance handled during training (negative subsampling, class weighting, minibatch positive/negative ratio)?
- Question: Please report CEAF (phi-4), CoNLL average, statistical significance, and clarify ACE split release details and relation to prior work.
- Question: Provide full ACE++ details (source selection, guidelines, IAA, adjudication) and confirm if ACE++ or model code/hyperparameters will be released for reproducibility.
- Question: Share quantitative comparisons for cosine similarity and other similarity baselines (e.g., bilinear/tensor) that were claimed to underperform.

- Suggestion: Evaluate end-to-end with predicted triggers/arguments to assess robustness and practicality.
- Suggestion: Address pronoun-based event anaphora by incorporating document-level context, entity coreference features, or joint modeling with entity coref.
- Suggestion: Clarify training duration relative to dataset size, include dev learning curves, and provide early stopping details to address overfitting concerns.

- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept—solid empirical contribution with good analysis, but needs clarification on supervision, evaluation completeness, and reproducibility.
