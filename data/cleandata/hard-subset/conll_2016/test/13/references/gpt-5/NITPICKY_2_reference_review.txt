Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a two-step, within-document event coreference model: (a) a CNN-based encoder to build sentence-level representations of individual event mentions using piecewise max pooling around the trigger, concatenated with local lexical features; (b) a pairwise scorer that combines the two mention encodings with hand-crafted pairwise features (type/modality agreement, distance, argument overlap), followed by a squared nonlinearity and logistic regression. Transitive-closure clustering produces chains.
- Empirical results on ACE 2005 and a larger, extended dataset (ACE++) show on-par to slightly better performance than prior work without using external semantic resources such as WordNet/FrameNet. Ablations indicate both sentential and local features contribute substantially, and increasing training data improves recall. The paper includes an error analysis.
- The claimed contribution is practical: a reasonably strong, resource-light event linking approach using sentential features, coupled with ablations and some analysis.

Strengths
- Sound empirical showing on ACE with widely reported coreference metrics (BLANC, B-CUBED, MUC, positive links). Despite caveats about splits, the model performs on par or better than Liu et al. (2014) on three of four metrics and uses fewer external resources, supporting the portability claim.
- Careful ablations isolate the impact of feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local, sentential) and the clustering choice (BestLink vs. transitive closure), providing useful insight. The finding that adding ACE++ data materially boosts recall is informative.
- Sensible architectural design choices adapted from relation classification literature (piecewise CNN, position embeddings) applied to event coreference; the pairwise feature set is reasonable and aligns with prior work.
- The error analysis highlights difficult phenomena (pronominal event references, annotation ambiguity), providing some qualitative depth beyond aggregate numbers.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. The core modeling components (piecewise CNN encoders for sentence-level features; mention-pair classification with hand-crafted pairwise features; transitive closure clustering) closely follow prior relation/classification work (e.g., Zeng et al.) and standard coreference paradigms. The “squared” similarity nonlinearity is a minor tweak and lacks rigorous justification; no ablation is shown against standard alternatives (tanh/relu/cosine/bilinear) beyond a brief claim.
- Evaluation comparability and fairness are unclear. The paper states all systems use gold triggers, but is ambiguous about whether pairwise features (argument overlap, modality/type agreement) use gold arguments and gold realis/modality. If gold arguments/modality are used, the setup is unrealistic and may advantage this system over those using predicted arguments/SRL (as noted for competitors). The train/test splits differ from prior work, and no significance testing is reported; improvements are modest and could be within variance. Thresholding is fixed at 0.5 without calibration or ROC/PR analysis.
- ACE++ dataset is insufficiently specified. The paper claims 1351 additional web documents “annotated in an analogous fashion,” but provides no details on annotation guidelines, annotator expertise, inter-annotator agreement, quality control, or domain composition. The dataset does not appear to be publicly released. As a result, conclusions drawn from ACE++ (e.g., recall gains, ablation outcomes) are not independently verifiable.
- Missing or incomplete methodological details hinder reproducibility. Hyperparameters and final chosen settings are said to be in Table 2 (not present here); batch size, loss weighting to handle severe class imbalance, negative sampling strategy, and regularization choices are not clearly specified. The paper trains for 2000 epochs and selects best on dev, but does not report variance across runs or seeds. The choice of transitive closure vs. BestLink uses a fixed threshold and does not analyze tuning sensitivity. Preprocessing details (tokenization, sentence splitting) are absent.
- Scope/mismatch to the problem’s challenges. The model encodes each mention using only its sentence, while the authors’ own statistics highlight that 55% of positive pairs are more than five sentences apart and many involve pronominal descriptions. The approach does not model document-level discourse, anaphora, or cross-sentence context beyond a distance feature, and the error analysis confirms systematic failures here. Given the paper’s motivation (cross-sentential inference is crucial), the modeling choice feels under-ambitious.
- Minor but numerous presentation issues: footnotes and figure references are awkwardly placed; several typos/grammar errors (“evitable”); inconsistent notation; reliance on external word2vec contradicts the framing “no external features” (the authors mean no external semantic resources, but the wording is imprecise).

Questions to authors
- Are argument overlap and modality/type agreement computed from gold ACE annotations or predicted outputs? If predicted, which systems are used and what are their accuracies? Please report results with predicted (not gold) arguments and realis to assess realistic performance.
- How was ACE++ annotated? Who were the annotators, what guidelines were followed, and what was inter-annotator agreement (for triggers, types, arguments, and links)? Will ACE++ be released?
- How was the 0.5 decision threshold chosen? Did you tune it on dev? Please add PR/ROC curves or report sensitivity to threshold and BestLink/transitive-closure configurations.
- Please provide an ablation for the squared nonlinearity vs. tanh/relu/cosine/bilinear, and report the final hyperparameters, batch size, negative/positive sampling strategy, and any class-imbalance handling.
- Can you report results with predicted triggers (end-to-end), or at least with predicted arguments/modality, to better compare with prior work that does not rely on gold arguments?

Overall recommendation
While the paper is competent and the empirical results are reasonable, the novelty is limited, key experimental details (especially around use of gold information and ACE++) are unclear, and reproducibility is weak. Given these issues and the modest gains under non-comparable settings, I lean toward rejection. Strengthening the evaluation (clear, realistic setups; release or thorough documentation of ACE++; ablations for the similarity function; significance testing) and incorporating document-level context would substantially improve the paper.
