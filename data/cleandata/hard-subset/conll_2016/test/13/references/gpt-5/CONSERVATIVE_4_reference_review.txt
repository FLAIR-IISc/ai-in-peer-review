Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles within-document event coreference (event linking). It proposes a two-stage model: (a) a CNN-based sentence encoder with position embeddings and piecewise max-pooling to produce an event-mention representation enriched with trigger-local lexical features; (b) a pairwise classifier that combines two mention representations with simple pairwise features (event type compatibility, discourse distance, modality/realis match, and argument overlap), followed by a similarity layer and logistic regression, and transitive-closure clustering.
- Claimed contributions: (1) achieving competitive/state-of-the-art performance on ACE without external semantic resources; (2) demonstrating the utility of sentential features for event coreference via ablations; (3) an expanded ACE++ dataset and analysis of data properties and errors.
- As I see it, the primary contribution is an empirical finding: a relatively simple CNN-based sentential feature approach, combined with basic pairwise features, can match prior results that relied on heavy external resources. Methodological novelty is limited.

Strengths
- Competitive results without external resources. On ACE, the model achieves strong BLANC/MUC F1, roughly on par with or exceeding prior work (e.g., Liu et al., 2014) despite not using WordNet/FrameNet-like features. This is practically useful for domains where such resources are unavailable.
- Sensible and transparent ablations. The paper systematically ablates pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential features, and examines clustering strategies (transitive closure vs BestLink) and data scale (ACE vs ACE++). The findings (sentential features help; more data boosts recall; BestLink harms recall in this model) are informative.
- Clear, simple architecture. The CNN with position embeddings and piecewise pooling is a well-established design; the pairwise model and transitive closure are straightforward. Implementation details (embeddings, optimizer, search over hyperparameters) are reasonably documented, aiding reproducibility.
- Data/property and error analyses. The authors quantify argument sharing, trigger mismatch, and sentence distances between coreferent mentions, providing motivation for sentential features. The error analysis highlights challenging phenomena (pronoun-like event anaphora), and annotator inconsistency on boundary cases.
- Use of BLANC alongside traditional coref metrics. Given class imbalance, reporting BLANC is justified and informative; the inclusion of other metrics helps with comparability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. The core components (CNN + position embeddings + piecewise pooling; mention-pair classification; transitive-closure clustering) are established in relation/event extraction and coreference. The “square” similarity activation is a minor tweak, and quantitative evidence vs standard alternatives (e.g., cosine, tanh/ReLU) is only asserted, not shown. As a contribution, this is more an application/engineering consolidation than a methodological advance.
- Evaluation comparability and completeness. Results on ACE use a non-standard split and gold triggers; it is unclear whether argument overlap uses gold arguments. Comparisons to prior work are therefore hard to interpret. The paper does not report CEAF and CoNLL average F1, which are commonly used for within-document coreference. No significance testing or variance across runs is provided.
- Reliance on gold annotations reduces realism. Using gold triggers (and possibly gold arguments) inflates performance and masks robustness to upstream errors. The claim of “state-of-the-art without external resources” would be stronger with end-to-end results (predicted triggers/arguments) or at least sensitivity analyses to noise in mention detection/argument extraction.
- Dataset concerns. ACE++ appears to be an internal extension; details on annotation procedure, guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, and availability are scant. Without public release, the main public evidence remains ACE with caveated comparisons. The paper states only one dataset is public; this limits verification.
- Clustering remains naïve. Transitive closure on a 0.5 threshold can propagate errors and ignores global constraints (e.g., type/realis compatibility) and cluster-level features. While BestLink is evaluated, more principled global inference or learning-to-cluster approaches are not explored, nor are constraints beyond pairwise features enforced at clustering time.

Questions to authors
- Do your pairwise features use gold arguments? Please clarify precisely which gold annotations are used at training and test time (triggers, argument spans/roles, realis). If gold arguments are used, report results with predicted arguments (and with predicted triggers) to assess practical utility.
- Please report CEAF (phi-4) and CoNLL average F1 on ACE, and provide statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap) and variance across multiple random seeds.
- Can you release ACE++? If not, please provide detailed information about its annotation process, guidelines, annotator expertise, inter-annotator agreement, distributions of event types, and overlap with ACE. Otherwise, the community cannot verify findings beyond ACE.
- Provide quantitative comparisons for the similarity layer: square vs cosine vs standard nonlinearities, with numbers. Also, how sensitive are results to the piecewise pooling design vs a simpler CNN, or to RNN/PCNN alternatives?
- How does performance degrade with predicted mentions? A controlled experiment injecting noise into triggers/arguments or using an upstream extractor would contextualize the gains.
- What constraints are enforced at clustering time (e.g., type/realis compatibility)? Could simple global constraints or a learned clustering objective reduce transitive-closure error propagation?

Additional comments
- Please include full hyperparameter settings used in the final model and regularization choices, plus training time and runtime inference cost.
- Consider reporting the CoNLL metric suite for better comparability and including a strong non-neural baseline trained on the same features to isolate the value of CNN-based sentential features.
