Summary and claimed contributions
The paper tackles within-document event coreference (event linking) and proposes a two-stage neural model: (a) a CNN-based encoder that builds mention-specific sentential representations using word and position embeddings plus trigger-local lexical features, and (b) a pairwise classifier that combines two mention representations with standard pairwise features (type agreement, distance, modality/realis match, argument overlap) and computes a “distributed similarity” via a squared activation followed by logistic regression. Pairwise decisions are clustered by transitive closure. The model avoids external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet) and is evaluated on ACE 2005 and an expanded ACE++ collection. The paper reports state-of-the-art or competitive results (especially BLANC), provides ablations showing the value of sentential features, compares clustering strategies (transitive closure vs BestLink), and includes a brief error analysis.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that sentence-level CNN representations substantially improve event coreference without external semantic resources, achieving competitive performance on ACE.
2) A thorough ablation showing the complementary utility of sentential and trigger-local features alongside standard pairwise features, plus an empirical comparison of clustering strategies.
3) Construction and use of a larger ACE++ dataset to study data scaling effects (yielding recall gains) and support hyperparameter selection.

Strengths
- Clear motivation backed by data analysis: The authors quantify that many positive pairs lack argument overlap and share different triggers, and that many coreferent pairs are far apart, strongly motivating sentential modeling beyond local lexical/argument features.
- Simple, well-engineered architecture with solid baselines/ablations: The separation into mention encoding and pairwise scoring plus ablations (pairwise-only, +local, +sentential) convincingly isolate the contribution of sentential features. The exploration of the “square” nonlinearity for similarity is interesting and pragmatic.
- Competitive results without external semantic resources: Matching or surpassing prior work that used extensive external semantic features supports the paper’s practical claim of portability across domains without costly resources.
- Sensible evaluation choices and analysis: Using BLANC (with class imbalance) is appropriate; reporting multiple metrics is helpful. The BestLink vs transitive-closure comparison is informative and shows the model already controls precision at the classifier stage.
- Useful error analysis: Identifies phenomena (event anaphora/pronominal references) and annotator inconsistency, highlighting realistic bottlenecks.

Weaknesses
- Experimental realism and reliance on gold inputs: It appears that gold event triggers (and possibly event types, realis/modality, and arguments) are used both to define mentions and for pairwise features (type agreement, argument overlap, realis match). This makes the setup optimistic and limits conclusions for end-to-end systems. Please clarify exactly which annotations are gold vs predicted and quantify performance with predicted triggers/types/arguments where feasible.
- Limited novelty in modeling: The use of CNN-based sentential features adapted from relation extraction to mention encoding for coreference is incremental. The pairwise decision with hand-crafted pairwise features and simple transitive-closure clustering is a standard pipeline. There is no global inference or learning to optimize a clustering metric, and no structured training beyond binary classification.
- Reproducibility and dataset concerns: The expanded ACE++ dataset is not public (to my knowledge), and details about its annotation process/quality, inter-annotator agreement, and availability are scant. Gains attributed to ACE++ cannot be independently verified. Code/splits for ACE are also not explicitly released.
- Evaluation breadth: There is no breakdown by distance, shared-argument vs no-argument cases, same-trigger vs different-trigger, or within-sentence vs cross-sentence, which would directly test the claimed benefits of sentential context. Statistical significance testing is not reported. The clustering comparison is limited to two strategies without tuning thresholds per strategy on dev.
- Feature dependence and potential leakage: Several pairwise features (type compatibility, realis, argument overlap) are strong signals. If gold, they may overshadow the contribution of sentential features. If predicted, their accuracy and impact are not reported. The claim of “no external semantic features” is fair, but reliance on rich gold annotations reduces the generality of the findings.

Questions to authors
- Which annotation layers are gold vs predicted at training and test time? Specifically: event trigger spans, event (coarse/fine) types, realis/modality, and event arguments. For arguments, do you use gold entity coreference to compute argument overlap?
- Can you provide results with predicted triggers/types/arguments (e.g., from an ACE extractor) to estimate end-to-end impact?
- How is “modality/realis” obtained and operationalized in the pairwise features? What are the label sets and accuracies if predicted?
- What are the statistics and annotation protocol for ACE++? Who annotated the additional 1351 documents, what was the IAA, and will the dataset (or at least splits and guidelines) be released?
- Can you report breakdowns by (a) sentence distance buckets, (b) same vs different trigger lemma, (c) with vs without shared arguments, to substantiate the sentential-feature gains?
- Did you explore class-imbalance strategies (e.g., cost-sensitive loss, negative subsampling) beyond standard minibatch sampling? How sensitive are results to thresholding and to the square activation vs other nonlinearities?
- Any evidence that the left/right piecewise pooling is superior to standard max-pooling or to PCNN-style three-way pooling around the trigger? Ablation on that choice would strengthen the modeling claim.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider reporting LEA in addition to BLANC/B3/MUC for a more cluster-aware evaluation, and include significance testing.
- Discuss computational efficiency and scaling with document length (pair count), and whether approximate nearest-antecedent pruning impacts performance.
- If code cannot be released, provide detailed hyperparameters and preprocessing steps, and release ACE splits for exact comparability.
- Future work could incorporate global objectives (e.g., easy-first cluster merging, ILP, or neural mention-ranking) and joint reasoning over entity/event coreference to mitigate pronoun/anaphora errors highlighted in the analysis.

Overall recommendation: Borderline accept. The empirical finding—that sentential CNN features can match resource-heavy systems for event linking—is valuable and practically impactful, but the setup’s reliance on gold annotations and limited novelty temper enthusiasm. Addressing the realism and clarity concerns would significantly strengthen the work.
