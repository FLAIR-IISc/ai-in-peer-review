Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper tackles within-document event coreference (event linking). It proposes a two-step neural model: (a) a CNN-based encoder to produce sentential representations of individual event mentions (with position embeddings and trigger-local lexical features), and (b) a pairwise scorer combining these representations with simple pairwise features, followed by clustering via transitive closure. The key claim is to reach state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 without external semantic resources and with additional results on an expanded ACE++ dataset.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) A reasonably strong, simple mention-pair model showing that sentence-level CNN features can substitute for external semantic resources in event linking under gold mention conditions.
  2) An ablation indicating additive benefits of sentential features alongside trigger-local and pairwise features; and a comparison of clustering strategies (transitive closure vs BestLink).
  3) An additional dataset (ACE++) and a brief error analysis.

Strengths
- Sensible architecture grounded in established practice. The system is a straightforward mention-pair approach with a compact CNN encoder and simple pairwise features. This clarity makes the study easy to interpret and extend, and aligns with well-understood coreference modeling paradigms.
- Competitive results under their setup. On ACE, the model achieves BLANC F1 that is comparable or better than prior work cited, despite not using external semantic resources. The ACE++ experiments show that additional training data improves recall, which is consistent and encouraging.
- Useful ablations. The paper evaluates the impact of (i) sentential CNN features, (ii) trigger-local lexical features, and (iii) pairwise features, showing each contributes. The BestLink vs transitive-closure comparison is also informative for practitioners.
- Error analysis. The qualitative analysis highlights key failure modes (e.g., pronoun-triggered events and annotation ambiguity), which is valuable diagnostic information.

Weaknesses
- Unrealistic experimental setting due to extensive gold information. The model relies on gold-annotated event mentions and (by the feature list) appears to use gold event types and argument overlaps. This makes the task substantially easier and departs from realistic pipelines where trigger/type/argument identification is noisy. The claim of obviating external semantic resources is weakened if strong oracle annotations are used instead. At minimum, the paper should report results with predicted triggers, types, and arguments to demonstrate robustness.
- Comparability and evaluation gaps undermine the “state-of-the-art” claim. Reported ACE splits differ from prior work, and preprocessing differs (the paper explicitly notes caveats). Core coreference metrics are incomplete: CEAF and the CoNLL average (MUC, B3, CEAF) are standard; only BLANC, MUC, B3 and a “positive links” metric are reported. Without consistent splits and standard metrics, the strength of the SOTA claim is limited. No statistical significance testing is provided.
- Modest novelty. CNN-based sentential features for event/relation modeling are well established; the model is essentially a conventional mention-pair classifier with max-pooling CNN encoders and transitive-closure clustering. The square activation for similarity is a minor tweak. The main novelty is the application to event coreference; methodologically, the contribution is incremental.
- ACE++ dataset details are insufficient and likely not reproducible. The paper adds 1351 “web documents annotated in an analogous fashion,” but does not provide details on annotation guidelines, annotator training, inter-annotator agreement, domain coverage, or public release. Using a non-public extension to claim stronger results (e.g., recall gains) limits the community value and reproducibility.
- Missing stronger baselines and robustness checks. Baselines include naive strategies and an internal ablation, but there is no reimplementation of a strong feature-based mention-pair baseline (e.g., with lexical/syntactic features) or a non-CNN neural encoder (e.g., BiLSTM). No robustness analysis with noisy upstream predictions, no analysis across event types, distance buckets, or argument availability. Hyperparameter search is described, but no variance or confidence intervals.

Questions to authors
- What gold information is used at training and test time? Specifically: Are event types, realis/modality, and argument roles/mentions gold or predicted? If predicted, what are the upstream models and their accuracies?
- Can you report results with predicted triggers/types/arguments (end-to-end within-document event linking), and quantify the performance drop vs. gold?
- Why omit CEAF and the CoNLL average? Please add these metrics for ACE and ACE++, and, if possible, evaluate on a commonly used ACE split to enable fairer comparison.
- What is the composition of ACE++ (domains, sources), the annotation protocol, IAA scores, and will the dataset be released? If not, can you at least release the train/dev/test document lists and code to ensure reproducibility?
- The similarity layer uses square as the nonlinearity. Can you provide quantitative comparisons to cosine, ReLU/tanh, and bilinear scoring, with statistical tests across multiple seeds?
- How sensitive are results to the clustering threshold and to the choice of BestLink vs transitive closure when calibrated on dev data?

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The model is clear and performs competitively under gold conditions, and the ablations are useful. However, the methodological novelty is limited, the reliance on gold annotations and non-standard splits/metrics weakens the empirical claims, and the ACE++ addition is insufficiently documented/released. Demonstrating robustness with predicted inputs and reporting standard metrics on standard splits would strengthen the case.
