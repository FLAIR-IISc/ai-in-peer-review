Summary
The paper tackles within-document event coreference (“event linking”) by learning event-mention representations from CNN-based sentential features, combined with local lexical features around the trigger, and a small set of pairwise features (type agreement, distance, modality/realis, argument overlap). Coreference decisions are made with a shallow neural similarity layer and thresholded to form clusters via transitive closure. The approach avoids external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet) and claims on-par or state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005, and reports additional experiments and ablations on an extended in-house ACE++ dataset.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: CNN-based sentential features can serve as a viable replacement for external semantic resources for event linking, achieving competitive performance on ACE without WordNet/FrameNet.
2) An ablation and design study: evidence on the contributions of pairwise features, trigger-local vs. sentential CNN features, and clustering strategy (transitive closure vs. BestLink).
3) A descriptive analysis of data properties (argument-sharing, trigger mismatch, sentence distance) motivating the design.

Strengths
- Clear, reasonably thorough empirical study: The paper presents ablations isolating the impact of sentential vs. local vs. pairwise features, and compares two clustering strategies. The error analysis (e.g., pronoun cases) is useful and grounded.
- Competitive performance on ACE with simpler resources: Achieving BLANC F1 comparable to prior work while eschewing costly external semantic resources is practically appealing for portability.
- Sensible modeling choices grounded in prior IE/RC literature: Piecewise CNN over sentential context, simple pairwise scoring, and transitive closure are established and interpretable; analysis of why BestLink hurts recall is informative.
- Data-driven motivation: The statistics on argument-sharing, trigger mismatch, and long-distance links are valuable and support the emphasis on sentential features rather than sole reliance on argument overlaps.
- Reproducibility of ACE split: Publishing the ACE valid/test doc lists is helpful (though see concerns below).

Weaknesses
- Questionable realism of evaluation setup; reliance on gold annotations: The approach appears to use gold triggers and likely gold event types (given “type agreement”) and possibly gold arguments/realis (given “argument overlap” and “modality/realis match”). This substantially weakens conclusions about practical utility. No results are reported with predicted triggers/types/arguments, and thus it is unclear how the model behaves in realistic pipeline settings. The claim of “state-of-the-art” should be tempered accordingly.
- Comparability and baselines: The cross-paper comparisons are hard to interpret due to inconsistent splits and differing preprocessing. Within the paper, the strongest baseline beyond “same type” is missing: a non-neural classifier (e.g., logistic regression or random forest) using exactly the proposed pairwise features (plus naive lexical similarity) would establish how much the CNN sentential features add. Also absent are more standard coreference metrics like CEAF and statistical significance testing.
- Limited novelty: The modeling is largely a straightforward application of CNN-based sentence encoders (already common in relation/event extraction) to event coreference, plus standard pairwise scoring and transitive closure. The method still depends on hand-crafted pairwise features. The novelty is incremental.
- Use of private ACE++ dataset and unclear annotation details: While the extra data improves recall, ACE++ does not seem publicly available and the annotation process is only described as “analogous.” This limits reproducibility and raises questions about annotation quality/consistency and domain match to ACE.
- Gains over simple heuristics are modest: The “same type” baseline is already strong on BLANC recall, and the reported gains in F1, while meaningful, are not overwhelming. Without stronger baselines and significance testing, it’s hard to assess the true incremental value of the CNN features beyond type/lexical cues.
- Missing analysis under distribution shift and robustness: No evaluation on predicted mentions, no robustness to distance, domain, or argument noise, and no calibration analysis. The model may overfit to signals correlated with gold types and local lexical overlap.

Questions to Authors
1) Which annotations are gold vs. predicted? Specifically: event triggers, event types, realis/modality, and arguments used to compute “argument overlap.” If any are predicted, with which systems? Please report performance with predicted annotations end-to-end.
2) Can you provide results with a strong non-neural baseline using your pairwise feature set (and simple lexical similarity features), as well as a neural baseline without CNN sentential features, to quantify the added value of the CNN?
3) Please report CEAF (phi4) alongside MUC, B3, and BLANC, and conduct statistical significance tests for the main ACE results.
4) How sensitive are results to the threshold and to the choice of clustering (BestLink vs. transitive closure) when using predicted mentions? Do the conclusions still hold?
5) What exactly is ACE++? Who annotated it, with what guidelines, and what is inter-annotator agreement? Will the data and code be released to enable reproducibility?
6) Do you have results stratified by distance between mentions, trigger agreement vs. disagreement, and argument presence/absence, ideally compared to a pairwise-feature baseline?
7) The pairwise features include “event type agreement.” If this uses gold types, what happens when types are predicted (with realistic error rates)? Similarly for realis and arguments.
8) Why choose BLANC as the primary metric over the CoNLL average (MUC, B3, CEAF), commonly used in coreference? Can you add CEAF and the CoNLL score for completeness?

Overall, while the study is careful and the idea of replacing external semantic resources with sentential features is appealing, the reliance on gold annotations, limited novelty, lack of stronger baselines and significance tests, and the use of a private dataset constrain the evidential strength. I encourage the authors to add more rigorous, realistic evaluations and baselines.
