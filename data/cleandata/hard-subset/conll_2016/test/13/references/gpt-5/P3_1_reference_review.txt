Summary and main contributions
- The paper tackles within-document event coreference (event linking) by learning event-mention representations from their sentences with a CNN (piecewise max-pooling, position embeddings) augmented by trigger-local lexical features. Pairwise decisions combine the two mention embeddings with simple pairwise features in a shallow similarity network, followed by clustering via transitive closure.
- The model aims to avoid external lexical-semantic resources (WordNet, FrameNet), claiming comparable or better performance than prior work on ACE 2005 and on a larger ACE++ collection. The paper provides ablations to quantify the impact of sentential vs. local vs. pairwise features and an error analysis.
- Empirically, the method achieves state-of-the-art or on-par results on ACE (particularly on BLANC), and shows improved recall when training on the larger ACE++ dataset.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea and sensible architecture: Learning a mention representation from its full sentence is well-motivated by data analysis (many positive pairs have different triggers or no shared arguments). The piecewise CNN with position embeddings is a reasonable choice to focus on context relative to the trigger. The square nonlinearity in the similarity layer is an interesting, simple tweak to achieve polarity-invariant similarities.
- Strong, well-structured experimental section: The paper evaluates on ACE and a larger ACE++ set, reports multiple metrics (including BLANC which is appropriate for imbalanced link distributions), runs ablations isolating feature classes, and compares clustering strategies (BestLink vs. transitive closure). The analysis of how data size affects recall is useful.
- Competitive results without external semantic resources: The approach matches or surpasses prior state-of-the-art relying on heavy semantic resources, which is valuable for portability across domains and languages.
- Useful error analysis: The manual inspection highlights two common pain points—event anaphora via pronouns and annotation inconsistencies—providing context for the limits of the approach and the data.
- Relevance and timeliness: Reducing reliance on curated semantic resources while maintaining performance is a meaningful contribution for practical IE pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and realism of supervision remain unclear. The pairwise features include argument overlap and event type compatibility. It is not stated clearly whether argument roles and types are gold at test time. If gold arguments/types are used, the setup may overestimate practical performance and is not directly comparable to prior work that refrains from gold arguments. If they are predicted, the paper should specify the upstream systems and their performance. This issue directly affects the “no external resources” claim and comparability.
- Limited novelty beyond applying sentential CNN features to event coreference. The architectural components (position-aware piecewise CNN, max-pooling, shallow MLP for pairwise scoring) are adaptations of known relation/event extraction setups. The square activation is minor. The contribution is primarily empirical and integrative rather than methodological.
- Dataset concerns and reproducibility: ACE++ is not described in sufficient detail (annotation protocol, adjudication, inter-annotator agreement, licensing) and appears not to be public, limiting verification. Even on ACE, train/test partitions differ across compared systems; CEAF metrics are omitted; no statistical significance testing is reported. Hyperparameter choices, batch construction under heavy class imbalance, and early stopping details could be clearer.
- Scope mismatch with motivating analysis: The data analysis notes that a majority of coreferent pairs are far apart in the document, but the model only encodes sentence-local information per mention and simple pairwise features. There is no explicit modeling of discourse structure, entity coreference chains, or cross-sentential context beyond lexical overlap/type/realis, which likely contributes to low recall on hard cases (as the error analysis also suggests).
- Baselines could be stronger. While simple baselines and ablations are included, the paper does not compare against modern sentence encoders or contextualized embeddings (e.g., BiLSTM/attention or pretrained encoders), nor provide quantitative comparisons for alternative similarity functions it claims to have tried (e.g., cosine). Including CEAF and the CoNLL average (MUC, B3, CEAF) would better align with coref evaluation practice.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold event arguments and gold event types at test time for computing pairwise features (argument overlap, type compatibility, realis)? If not, what upstream systems are used and with what accuracy? How does performance change when replacing gold with predicted arguments/types?
- What is the precise treatment of multi-token triggers and multiple mentions per sentence in the CNN (single index, head token, average)? How are position embeddings defined in those cases?
- How is class imbalance handled during training? Do you use negative subsampling, class weighting, or calibrated thresholds? What is the positive/negative ratio per minibatch?
- Please report CEAF (phi-4) and the CoNLL average, and, if possible, statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap) on ACE. Also clarify whether your ACE split is released and how it relates to previous work.
- Can you provide more details on ACE++: source selection, annotation guidelines, IAA, adjudication, and whether it will be released? If not, can you release model code and the hyperparameters (Table 2) for reproducibility?
- You state that cosine-based similarity underperformed the square-activated MLP. Could you include those numbers, and perhaps a simple learned bilinear or tensor similarity baseline?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider end-to-end evaluation (predicted triggers and arguments) to quantify robustness. Even a partial study would sharpen claims about practicality.
- Given the prominence of pronoun-based event anaphora in your errors, incorporating document-level context (entity/coref links, discourse cues) or cross-sentence encoders may bring further gains. Joint modeling with entity coref (or using entity coref features) is a promising next step.
- Minor: clarify training duration (“2000 epochs”) relative to dataset size and early stopping; add dev learning curves to alleviate overfitting concerns.

Overall recommendation: borderline accept. The paper offers a solid, well-executed empirical contribution showing that sentential CNN features can yield competitive event coreference without external semantic resources, with good analysis. Clarifying supervision, improving evaluation completeness, and addressing reproducibility would strengthen the work.
