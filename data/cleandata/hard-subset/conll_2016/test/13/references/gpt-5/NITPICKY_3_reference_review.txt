Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper tackles within-document event coreference (“event linking”) by learning sentence-level representations of event mentions with a piecewise CNN and combining them with simple pairwise features, followed by pairwise scoring and transitive-closure clustering.
- It claims competitive, near–state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 without external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet), and introduces a larger ACE++ dataset to improve recall. It also reports ablations of feature classes and a comparison of BestLink vs. transitive closure clustering, and provides an error analysis.

My view of the primary contributions (strongest first):
1) An empirical study showing that CNN-based sentential features can replace external semantic resources for event coreference while remaining competitive on ACE, with ablations and clustering analysis.
2) A simple, modular architecture (mention encoder + pairwise scorer + transitive closure) plus a practical observation that BestLink hurts recall in this setting.
3) An expanded dataset (ACE++) used to demonstrate recall gains (but see concerns below).

Strengths
- Competitive performance without external semantic KBs: The model achieves BLANC F1 comparable to Liu et al. (2014) while forgoing expensive external semantic resources. This is a useful empirical result for portability across domains lacking KB coverage.
- Clear modularity and sensible feature design: The combination of (i) piecewise CNN sentence representations centered on the trigger, (ii) simple pairwise features (type compatibility, distance, modality, argument overlap), and (iii) a lightweight clustering step is straightforward to implement and potentially extensible.
- Ablation and analysis: The paper reports ablations teasing apart the contribution of pairwise vs. trigger-local vs. sentential features, and a small but informative analysis showing that larger training data (ACE++) improves recall. The qualitative error analysis (100 FPs/FNs) is useful, especially highlighting pronoun/event anaphora cases.
- Clustering comparison: The empirical finding that BestLink lowers recall compared to transitive closure for this model is a helpful, reproducible insight for practitioners.
- Data-driven motivation: The preliminary analysis (argument sharing rarity, trigger mismatch rate, long-distance links) helps justify sentence-level features beyond trigger-local information.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The core idea—apply standard sentence-level CNN features (with position embeddings and piecewise pooling) to event coref and combine with pairwise features—is incremental relative to prior CNN-based relation/event extraction and pairwise coreference scoring. The square activation for similarity is a minor architectural tweak without deep analysis.
- Unclear and unrealistic preprocessing assumptions: The paper states gold event-mention triggers are used; it is unclear whether gold types, gold arguments, and/or modality are used for pairwise features (e.g., “argument overlap,” “modality agreement”). If these are gold, the setup is unrealistic and not directly comparable to systems using predicted arguments/types; if predicted, the pipeline and performance of upstream components are not described. This ambiguity materially affects interpretation of results and the “no external resources” claim.
- ACE++ dataset concerns: ACE++ is “annotated in an analogous fashion” but details are missing: annotation protocol, guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, provenance, licensing, and availability. It is unclear whether ACE++ will be released. Using a large, non-public dataset to claim improved recall weakens reproducibility and the strength of the empirical claim.
- Evaluation and comparability issues: 
  - Non-standard or incomplete metrics for coref: CEAF metrics and CoNLL average are missing; BLANC is included (arguably good), but comparability to prior work remains unclear.
  - Different train/test partitions across systems; no attempt to re-run baselines on the same split.
  - No statistical significance, no multiple random seeds, and a fixed 0.5 threshold are used. Reported gains over prior work (e.g., +2.9 BLANC F1 vs. Liu et al.) are modest and could be within variance.
- Methodological/detail gaps and inconsistencies:
  - Hyperparameters (Table 2), Figure 2 (example generation), and several implementation specifics are missing in the submission excerpt; training uses 2000 epochs on a small dataset without clear regularization strategy or early-stopping details; class imbalance handling (positive/negative weighting or sampling) is not described.
  - The claim that the sentential module “degenerates to trigger-local features” in one ablation is speculative; more controlled analyses would help (e.g., probe tasks or attention/feature importance).
  - The pairwise feature “argument overlap” is problematic given known ACE argument incompleteness; robustness analyses are missing.
  - Several citations and formatting issues (e.g., “Ng and Gardent (2002)” appears to be an error; numerous typos; uneven notation; messy figures) detract from clarity.

Questions to authors
- Precisely which annotations are gold vs. predicted at test time? Are event types, arguments (roles and fillers), and modality/realis gold? If predicted, describe the upstream systems and their performance; if gold, please quantify how performance changes with predicted inputs.
- Will ACE++ be released (documents, annotations, splits)? What are the annotation guidelines, IAA, and licensing?
- Please report CEAF (M and/or E) and CoNLL F1, and provide results averaged over multiple random seeds with significance tests. Also, justify the 0.5 threshold or tune it on validation.
- Provide the missing implementation details (full hyperparameters, filter/window sizes, dp/dw/dc/de/dsim, regularization used, OOV handling, tokenization) and clarify handling of multi-word triggers and sentences with multiple mentions.
- Can you compare transitive closure vs. BestLink under calibrated scores (e.g., Platt scaling) and/or tuned thresholds, and provide an analysis of cluster size distributions?

Overall recommendation
While the paper is well-motivated and empirically solid in parts, the methodological novelty is modest and there are substantial clarity, evaluation, and reproducibility concerns (especially around gold vs. predicted inputs and the non-public ACE++). Given CoNLL’s standards, I lean toward rejection unless these issues are convincingly addressed.
