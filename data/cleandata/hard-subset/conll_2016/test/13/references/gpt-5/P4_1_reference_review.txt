Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses within-document event coreference (event linking) and proposes a two-step model: (a) a CNN-based encoder that builds sentence-level representations of individual event mentions using word and position embeddings with piecewise max pooling and a small MLP; (b) a pairwise scorer that combines two mention representations with simple pairwise features (type compatibility, distance, discourse position, realis/modality match, argument overlap), uses a square activation to produce a multi-dimensional similarity, and a logistic layer to score coreference. Pairwise scores are converted to clusters via transitive closure. Experiments on ACE 2005 and an extended ACE++ dataset show performance competitive with or better than prior work, while avoiding external lexical-semantic resources. The paper provides ablation studies, a comparison of clustering strategies, basic baselines, and an error analysis.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical finding: sentence-level CNN representations, combined with light pairwise features and simple clustering, achieve state-of-the-art or competitive event coreference performance on ACE without external semantic resources.

2) Methodology: a clear two-stage architecture for event mention encoding and pairwise scoring (including the square activation for similarity), with systematic ablations that quantify contributions of sentential vs. trigger-local and pairwise features, and of clustering strategies.

3) Data analysis and evaluation: analysis of ACE/ACE++ properties (argument sharing, trigger differences, cross-sentence distances), feature ablations, and error analysis highlighting challenging phenomena (e.g., pronoun anaphora) and annotation inconsistencies.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results without external resources: On ACE, the model attains higher BLANC, MUC, and positive-link F1 than a prior state-of-the-art system (Liu et al., 2014) that uses a rich set of external semantic features, demonstrating that sentential neural features can replace expensive resources while maintaining or improving quality.

- Thorough experimental analysis: The paper includes ablations isolating the impact of pairwise vs. trigger-local vs. sentential features (showing monotonic gains when adding lexical and sentential features), and a comparison of clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) with clear precision/recall trade-offs. The additional ACE++ training data yields a substantial recall gain, indicating scalability with data.

- Clear problem motivation via corpus statistics: The analysis that many positive pairs share no arguments (≈51%), often have different triggers (≈47%), and are far apart (>5 sentences in ≈55%) motivates using sentence-level context rather than relying on argument overlap or lexical identity.

- Sound model design and presentation: The architecture builds on established CNN sentence encoders with position embeddings and piecewise pooling, and integrates standard pairwise features. Equations and pseudo-code make the approach reproducible at a high level.

- Useful error analysis: Manual inspection highlights difficult cases (event anaphora by pronouns) and annotation inconsistencies, putting performance numbers in context and delineating where future work is needed.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Using CNN-based sentential features for encoding mentions and pairwise scoring with transitive closure clustering is a relatively incremental extension of prior neural IE work (CNNs for event/relation extraction). The square activation for similarity is a minor twist; the overall architecture and training are standard.

- Comparability and fairness of evaluation are imperfect: Different ACE splits and preprocessing across compared systems are acknowledged, weakening direct claims of state-of-the-art. It is also unclear whether this work uses gold event arguments for the “argument overlap” feature; competitors may rely on predicted arguments, which would make comparisons less balanced.

- Reproducibility and resource release: The ACE++ dataset (1351 additional documents “annotated in an analogous fashion”) is not clearly described or released. Without annotation guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, domain composition, or public access, it is difficult to assess data quality or replicate the gains from additional training data.

- Practicality constraints: Experiments use gold triggers (and possibly gold arguments and realis), which limits conclusions about end-to-end applicability. No results are provided with system-detected triggers/arguments, and the model’s robustness to upstream errors is unknown.

- Missing implementation details: Some aspects are under-specified for full reproducibility, e.g., exact encoding of pairwise features (binning, bagging schemes), whether arguments/realis are gold or predicted, negative sampling/imbalance handling beyond logistic loss, parameter counts, runtime, and variance across random seeds.

Questions to authors
- Did you use gold event arguments and gold realis/modality labels for pairwise features, or predicted ones? Please provide results with predicted arguments/realis to assess practical performance.

- Will ACE++ be released? If so, please provide annotation guidelines, domains covered, inter-annotator agreement, and a train/dev/test split to enable comparison.

- Can you clarify the exact ACE split used and provide results for baselines/competitors on that split, or re-run your model on a commonly used split?

- Please detail the encoding of pairwise features (e.g., how distance and argument overlap are represented), and whether any thresholds or bins were tuned on the test set.

- The square activation reportedly outperforms alternatives; can you include ablation numbers for other activations (e.g., ReLU, tanh) and cosine similarity?

- Do you have end-to-end results with system-extracted triggers (and possibly arguments) to quantify pipeline performance degradation?

- What is the variability across runs (e.g., mean±std over multiple seeds) and the computational footprint (training time, parameters)?
