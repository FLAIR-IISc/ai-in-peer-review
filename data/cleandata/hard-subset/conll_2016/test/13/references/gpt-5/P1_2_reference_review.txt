Summary
The paper tackles within-document event coreference (event linking) and proposes a two-step neural model: (a) a CNN-based encoder that builds an event-mention representation using sentential context with piecewise max-pooling relative to the trigger, augmented with local lexical cues (trigger and neighbors), and (b) a pairwise classifier that combines two mention representations with simple pairwise features (type compatibility, distance, discourse position, realis match, argument overlap) to score coreference. Pairwise scores are converted to clusters via thresholding and transitive closure. The model deliberately avoids external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet) and aims to match prior systems’ performance. Experiments on ACE 2005 and an extended corpus ACE++ (ACE + 1351 additional web documents) show on-par or better BLANC/MUC F1 than prior state-of-the-art, with detailed ablations, clustering-strategy analysis, and an error analysis.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that sentential representations from a CNN, combined with lightweight pairwise features, can achieve state-of-the-art or competitive event coreference performance on ACE without external semantic resources.
2) Empirical analysis of design choices: ablations of feature classes (pairwise vs. trigger-local vs. sentential), clustering strategies (BestLink vs. transitive closure), data scale (ACE vs. ACE++), and naive baselines, with BLANC as the primary metric.
3) Construction and use of an expanded ACE++ dataset and reporting of dataset-driven observations (e.g., high proportion of pairs with no shared arguments and different triggers; long-distance links), which motivate the sentential features.

Strengths
- Strong empirical showing with minimal external resources: The model attains higher BLANC and MUC F1 than Liu et al. (2014) on ACE while dispensing with heavy semantic resources. This is a meaningful practical contribution for domains lacking curated knowledge bases.
- Careful ablation and analysis: The paper rigorously disentangles the impact of feature classes, showing that sentential features yield substantial gains over pairwise and trigger-local features alone. The BestLink vs. transitive-closure comparison is informative and shows surprising differences relative to prior work.
- Sensible metric choice and dataset analysis: Emphasis on BLANC (balanced for negative links) is appropriate given the skew toward singleton mentions, and the paper’s preliminary data analysis (argument overlap, trigger mismatch, long-distance phenomena) is insightful and well-used to justify architectural choices.
- Error analysis with qualitative insights: The discussion of challenging phenomena (e.g., pronominal references to events) and annotation ambiguity highlights real limitations and contextualizes the reported scores.
- Simple, reproducible architecture: The piecewise CNN with basic pairwise features and logistic scoring is straightforward, and the square activation for “distributed similarity” is a neat, lightweight way to capture polarity-invariant similarity.

Weaknesses
- Use of gold annotations reduces realism: The system assumes gold event triggers and (apparently) gold event types and arguments (used in pairwise features, e.g., argument overlap, type compatibility, realis). This inflates performance and limits conclusions about end-to-end usefulness. An evaluation with predicted triggers/types/arguments is needed to assess robustness.
- Limited novelty: The core architectural elements (piecewise CNN sentence encoding; concatenation with local lexical features; pairwise classification) follow prior relation/event extraction work. Applying them to event coreference is valuable but incremental; the “square” nonlinearity is a minor tweak. There is no global, structured, or joint inference beyond thresholding + transitive closure.
- ACE++ dataset clarity and availability: The paper relies heavily on ACE++ to demonstrate improved recall, but the annotation provenance, guidelines alignment with ACE, inter-annotator agreement, and release status are not clearly specified. Without public availability or detailed quality controls, the generality of conclusions is harder to assess.
- Comparability caveats remain significant: Results on ACE are compared to prior work with different train/test splits and preprocessing; while the paper acknowledges this, the claim of state-of-the-art would be stronger with standardized splits and additional metrics (e.g., CEAF/CoNLL average) commonly used in coreference.
- Thresholding and clustering choices are under-specified: The classification threshold is fixed at 0.5, and there is no calibration or development-set tuning reported. More robust clustering (e.g., learned thresholds, global objectives, or constrained optimization) could improve consistency and would better test the utility of the learned similarity.

Questions to Authors
- Do you use gold event types and gold argument spans/roles in the pairwise features (type compatibility, realis, argument overlap)? If so, can you report performance with predicted triggers/types/arguments to quantify the model’s end-to-end robustness?
- How was ACE++ annotated (annotators, guidelines, QA), and will it be released? What is inter-annotator agreement on coreference links and event types?
- How are multi-word triggers handled in the piecewise pooling and local lexical features? Is the “trigger” embedding the head token only?
- Was the 0.5 decision threshold tuned on a validation set? If not, does tuning improve BLANC F1 or the precision–recall trade-off?
- Can you report CEAF and CoNLL scores to facilitate comparison with broader coreference literature?

Additional comments
- Consider an ablation removing gold arguments/types (or replacing with predicted ones) to quantify reliance on these features.
- A per-distance performance breakdown (e.g., links across >5 sentences) would illustrate strength on long-range linking.
- Minor: clarify the size of negative vs. positive examples used per minibatch and whether class balancing or loss weighting was applied.
- Releasing code and trained models would substantially enhance reproducibility.
- Some figures are hard to parse in text form; please ensure clarity of the CNN architecture depiction and notation consistency.
