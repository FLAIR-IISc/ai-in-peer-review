Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles within-document event coreference (event linking). It proposes a two-part neural model: (a) a CNN encoder that builds a mention representation from the entire sentence using word and position embeddings with piecewise max-pooling and local trigger embeddings; (b) a pairwise scorer that combines two mention representations with standard pairwise features (type compatibility, distance, modality, argument overlap) and a squared-activation similarity layer, followed by logistic regression. Clustering is done by transitive closure over links above a threshold.
- The authors emphasize avoiding external semantic resources (WordNet/FrameNet) while achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005; they also construct a larger ACE++ dataset and provide ablations on feature classes and clustering strategies, plus an error analysis.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength):
1) Empirical finding: sentential CNN features for individual mentions, combined with simple pairwise features, match or exceed prior state of the art in event coreference without external semantic resources. This reduces domain-dependence and simplifies deployment.
2) Careful empirical analysis: ablations (pairwise vs local vs sentential features), clustering strategies (BestLink vs transitive closure), dataset size effects (ACE vs ACE++), baselines, and a useful error analysis revealing pronoun-driven misses and annotation ambiguity.
3) Dataset scaling and statistics: construction of a larger ACE++ collection (though release is unclear) and informative statistics motivating design (argument sparsity, diverse triggers, long-distance links).

Strengths
- Relevance and clarity of motivation: The authors quantify argument sparsity (∼51% positive pairs share no arguments), lexical variability (∼47% with different triggers), and long-distance links (∼55% >5 sentences apart). This justifies sentence-level semantic encoding and not over-relying on argument matching. The model design maps well to these observations.
- Competitive results without external semantics: Achieving on-par or better BLANC/MUC F1 than a strong semantic-rich baseline (Liu et al., 2014) while dropping WordNet/FrameNet-style features is practically important for cross-domain applicability. The feature ablations convincingly show sentential features add substantial gains.
- Thorough ablation and analysis: The paper systematically studies (i) the impact of training on ACE++ vs ACE (noting recall gains), (ii) clustering strategy (showing BestLink reduces recall for their setting), and (iii) feature classes (pairwise alone is weak; sentential features are critical). The error analysis is thoughtful and highlights genuine difficulties (pronominal references, borderline annotation decisions).
- Method is simple and reproducible in concept: The architecture is straightforward (CNN encoder + shallow pairwise head) and well-specified (position embeddings, piecewise pooling, squared similarity). The squared activation rationale for polarity invariance is reasonable and empirically motivated.
- Evaluation choices and discussion: The emphasis on BLANC to mitigate skew toward negative links is appropriate for this task; the paper also reports other metrics for completeness and is transparent about split caveats.

Weaknesses
- Realism of evaluation and reliance on gold annotations: All results use gold event mentions (triggers); it is unclear whether argument overlap uses gold arguments (the text implies it may). This inflates performance relative to end-to-end pipelines. Given the claim of domain robustness, an evaluation with predicted triggers/arguments or joint extraction would better support practical impact.
- Limited novelty: Using CNN-based sentence encoders and pairwise neural scoring is a direct adaptation of existing relation/event extraction ideas to coreference; pairwise features and transitive-closure clustering are standard. The main novelty is empirical rather than methodological. This is acceptable if the empirical contributions are strong, but should be framed accordingly.
- Dataset and reproducibility concerns: ACE++ creation is a substantial effort, but the paper does not clearly state whether ACE++ annotations, code, and preprocessing will be released. Only a list of ACE splits is provided. Without released resources, it is hard for the community to verify or build on these results.
- Comparability of results: The comparison to prior work is confounded by different document splits and varying usage of gold vs predicted arguments. While the paper acknowledges this, stronger baselines (e.g., modern BiLSTM/Transformer encoders, CEAF metrics) or a controlled reimplementation would strengthen claims.
- Limited global modeling: The approach uses pairwise decisions with transitive closure. It neither enforces global consistency during learning nor leverages document-level discourse structure, which the authors identify as important (e.g., pronoun resolution). Exploring global inference or joint modeling would likely help the challenging cases highlighted in the error analysis.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold event arguments for the “argument overlap” pairwise feature? If not, what extractor is used and with what performance? If yes, can you report results without gold arguments to approximate end-to-end settings?
- Can you provide results with predicted triggers (e.g., from an ACE-style trigger detector) to quantify robustness end-to-end?
- Will ACE++ (documents, annotations), code, and trained models be released? If not, what prevents release, and can you at least release derived features or scripts?
- How sensitive are results to the encoder choice? Have you compared the CNN encoder to a BiLSTM or CNN+BiLSTM hybrid under the same setup?
- How is the decision threshold (0.5) selected? Did you tune it on validation for BLANC F1, and if so, does that change the precision-recall tradeoff materially?
- Do you have any evidence of cross-domain generalization (e.g., train on ACE, test on ACE++ or vice versa without fine-tuning)?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid empirical paper with clear motivation, a simple and effective model, and thorough analyses. The methodological novelty is limited, and the reliance on gold annotations limits practical conclusions, but the demonstration that sentential features plus lightweight pairwise signals can match semantic-resource-heavy systems is valuable. With clarifications on gold arguments and a commitment to resource release and/or end-to-end evaluation, this would be a strong accept; as is, it is a moderate accept on empirical merit and relevance.
