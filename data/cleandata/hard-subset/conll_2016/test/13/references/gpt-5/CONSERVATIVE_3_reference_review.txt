Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a two-step event coreference model: (a) a CNN-based encoder that produces sentential representations of individual event mentions (trigger-local + piecewise-pooled sentence context with position embeddings), and (b) a pairwise scorer that combines two mention representations with simple pairwise features and a squared nonlinearity, followed by logistic regression and transitive-closure clustering.
- Empirically, the model is reported to achieve state-of-the-art or on-par results on ACE 2005 (using gold triggers), without relying on external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet). Ablations indicate sentential features contribute substantially; analysis compares BestLink vs transitive closure.
- The authors introduce an expanded ACE++ dataset (ACE 2005 + 1351 additional web documents “annotated in an analogous fashion”), and provide some error analysis.

Strengths
- Sensible, practical modeling choice: Using sentential features via CNNs for event linking is well-motivated by prior success in extraction/relation tasks. The architecture is clear, straightforward to implement, and avoids heavy reliance on domain-limited lexical-semantic resources.
- Competitive results on ACE with a relatively lean feature set: Despite using only local/sentential features and simple pairwise cues, performance is comparable to or better than prior work under BLANC, MUC, and positive links (with caveats about splits and supervision acknowledged).
- Ablation studies are informative: The experiments separating pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential features are useful and indicate sentential features materially improve recall; they also analyze clustering strategies (transitive closure vs BestLink) in their setting.
- Data-driven motivation: The preliminary statistics (argument overlap scarcity, trigger mismatch frequency, cross-sentence distances) support the choice to model sentence context beyond trigger-only or argument-only features.
- Some qualitative analysis: The error analysis highlights hard phenomena (e.g., pronominal reference to events) and annotation boundary cases, which is valuable context for interpreting scores.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation realism and supervision assumptions: The model uses gold event triggers (and appears to leverage event type compatibility, modality/realis match, and argument overlap as pairwise features). It is unclear whether event types, modality/realis, and arguments are gold or predicted. If any are gold, the evaluation substantially overestimates practical performance. Even if not gold, the paper does not present an end-to-end pipeline evaluation with predicted triggers/types/arguments, which limits the practical significance of the reported gains.
- Comparability and baselines: Results are compared to prior work on different ACE splits with heterogeneous preprocessing and supervision, making “state of the art” claims tenuous. Strong baselines re-implemented on the same split (e.g., a feature-rich non-neural classifier; or a simpler neural baseline without CNN sentence modeling) are missing. No statistical significance testing or variance across multiple random seeds is reported.
- Novelty is incremental: CNN-based sentential encoding with piecewise pooling is directly borrowed from relation/event extraction literature; pairwise coreference classification and transitive closure are standard. The squared nonlinearity for similarity is a minor tweak. The main technical contribution is the application and engineering of these components to event linking; methodological novelty is limited.
- ACE++ dataset concerns: The additional 1351 documents are “annotated in an analogous fashion,” but details on annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, and public availability are not provided. If ACE++ is not publicly released, reproducibility and the claimed gains from more data cannot be independently verified.
- Experimental reporting gaps: Key choices (negative sampling/imbalance handling, threshold selection beyond default 0.5, number of runs, regularization) are not fully detailed. Training for 2000 epochs on small data without reporting overfitting controls and variance raises concerns. Metrics omit CEAF/CoNLL F1 (common in coreference), which would aid comparability.

Questions to authors
- Supervision details: Are event types, modality/realis, and arguments used in the pairwise features gold-annotated or predicted? If predicted, with which models and what accuracies? Please report performance when using predicted triggers/types/arguments to reflect end-to-end usage.
- ACE++: Who performed the annotation, under what guidelines, and what is the inter-annotator agreement? Will the dataset (or at least the additional annotations) be released to enable replication?
- Baselines and significance: Can you provide results for (i) a strong non-neural baseline (e.g., logistic regression/random forest on the same local/pairwise features) and (ii) a neural baseline without sentential CNNs (e.g., trigger-only/context bag-of-words), all on your exact splits? Also report mean±std over multiple random seeds and statistical tests vs. baselines.
- Metrics: Please include CEAF and CoNLL-style average (MUC, B3, CEAF) to align with coreference evaluation practice, alongside BLANC.
- Thresholding and class imbalance: How is the 0.5 threshold selected? What is the positive/negative pair ratio during training and testing? Did you calibrate scores or use cost-sensitive training or sampling strategies?
- Generalization: Have you evaluated on a different event coref benchmark (e.g., ECB+) to test cross-domain robustness? If not, can you at least report cross-domain from ACE to ACE++ and vice versa?

Overall, the paper presents a reasonable and practically appealing application of established CNN-based sentential features to event linking with competitive results, but the methodological novelty is modest and the evaluation leaves important questions about realism, comparability, and reproducibility. Addressing the supervision realism (predicted vs gold), adding stronger in-split baselines and significance analyses, clarifying ACE++ and releasing resources would substantially strengthen the submission.
