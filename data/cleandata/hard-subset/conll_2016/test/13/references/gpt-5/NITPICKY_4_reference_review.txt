Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper tackles within-document event coreference (“event linking”) and proposes a two-stage model: (a) a CNN-based sentence encoder with position embeddings and piecewise max pooling to build mention representations; (b) a pairwise scorer that concatenates two mention vectors with simple pairwise features and computes a “distributed similarity” via an element-wise square nonlinearity, followed by logistic regression and simple clustering (transitive closure).
- Empirically, the model achieves competitive performance on ACE 2005 relative to prior work, while avoiding external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet/FrameNet). The authors also introduce a larger ACE++ dataset (ACE + 1351 additional web documents), conduct ablations over feature classes, compare clustering strategies, and provide a small error analysis.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) Empirical finding that sentence-level CNN features, combined with lightweight pairwise features, can match prior art on ACE without external semantic lexicons.
2) An expanded dataset (ACE++) and accompanying analyses/ablations illustrating impacts of feature classes and clustering strategies.
3) Minor modeling tweak: an element-wise square activation for a similarity layer that the authors claim works better than alternatives.

Strengths
- Competitive results without external semantic resources: On ACE, the model’s BLANC/MUC/positive-link F1 scores are on par with or better than Liu et al. (2014) despite not using WordNet/FrameNet-style features. This supports the paper’s main practical claim (less domain dependence).
- Sensible motivation and diagnostics: The authors quantify key data properties (argument sharing rarity, different triggers, long mention distances), which clearly motivate moving beyond purely trigger/argument features to sentence-level representations.
- Ablations are informative: The feature ablations (pairwise vs. local lexical vs. sentential) show consistent gains from adding sentential features and help disentangle where improvements originate. The BestLink vs. transitive-closure comparison is also helpful.
- Error analysis: The manual inspection of 100 FP/FN examples surfaces real challenges (pronoun-like event anaphora, annotation ambiguity/borderline cases), providing useful guidance for future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The architecture is essentially a straightforward adaptation of prior CNN sentence encoders (e.g., PCNN with position embeddings) to event coreference, plus a simple pairwise MLP. The “square” nonlinearity is a very small tweak. There is no global/structured inference, no joint modeling with entities, and no document-level context beyond two per-mention sentence encodings.
- Fairness and clarity of evaluation setup: The paper states all systems use gold triggers, but it remains unclear whether argument overlap features are derived from gold arguments or predicted ones. If gold arguments are used here while competitors use SRL or predicted arguments (as suggested for Liu et al., 2014, and Bejan & Harabagiu, 2010), the comparison is not apples-to-apples and may overstate the advantage. Please specify exactly which gold annotations are used (triggers only? arguments? entities?) at train/test and, if gold arguments are used, re-run comparisons without them or provide a controlled ablation.
- Metrics and comparability: The paper emphasizes BLANC (reasonable), but omits CEAF and the standard CoNLL F1 (average of MUC/B3/CEAF), reducing comparability to prior work. No statistical significance testing is reported. The fixed 0.5 threshold is not tuned/validated, and it is unclear whether model scores are calibrated. Given varied splits across papers, stronger care with metrics/significance is needed before claiming “state-of-the-art.”
- ACE++ dataset: The new dataset is insufficiently documented. There are no details on annotation guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, domains, event types, or quality control; it also appears not to be publicly released (contradicting the “one dataset is publicly available” claim if ACE++ is the contribution). As a result, the generality and utility of ACE++ are hard to assess, and conclusions derived from it are difficult to reproduce.
- Missing methodological detail and controls: Important training details are underspecified: class imbalance handling (over 90% negative links—was any reweighting, focal loss, or negative subsampling used?), batch size, early stopping criteria, regularization actually used (dropout/L2), exact hyperparameters (Table 2 referred to but not provided here), and sensitivity to key design choices (e.g., similarity activation, threshold). Training for 2000 epochs without clear regularization and without discussing convergence/overfitting on such imbalanced data is concerning. The decision to use sentence-only encoders for mentions in a task with many cross-sentential dependencies is also limiting; no experiments assess whether adding lightweight document/paragraph context helps.

Questions to authors
- What exact gold annotations are used at train and test? Triggers only, or also gold arguments/entities? If argument overlap uses gold arguments, please quantify the performance drop when using predicted arguments (or removing that feature) to enable a fair comparison to Liu et al. (2014).
- Please report CEAF (φ4) and the CoNLL average, and provide significance testing (e.g., bootstrap) on ACE.
- How is the 0.5 threshold chosen? Did you tune it on dev? Are the scores calibrated across documents? Please report sensitivity to threshold.
- How did you handle the class imbalance in pair generation? Were negatives subsampled or losses reweighted? What was the ratio of positive to negative pairs during training?
- ACE++: Will you release the data? Provide annotation guidelines, IAA, domain distribution, event types covered, and any differences from ACE. If not released, please temper claims based on it.
- Can you provide ablations isolating the impact of the “square” activation vs. ReLU/tanh/cosine, and quantify the difference?
- Did you try adding limited document context (e.g., sentence window around mentions) to the CNN encoder? Given that many coreferent pairs are far apart, sentence-only mention encodings seem restrictive.

Overall, while the empirical results are promising, the methodological novelty is modest and the evaluation clarity/comparability and dataset transparency are insufficient. I would require clarifications and additional controlled experiments (especially around gold arguments, metrics, and thresholding) before considering acceptance.
