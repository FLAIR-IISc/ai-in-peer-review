Summary and key contributions
- The paper addresses within-document event coreference (event linking) and proposes a two-step model: (a) a CNN-based encoder that builds sentential representations of individual event mentions augmented with trigger-local lexical features, and (b) a pairwise scorer that combines the two mention representations with simple pairwise features (type agreement, distance, modality, argument overlap) and a “distributed similarity” layer (elementwise square) followed by logistic regression. Clustering is done via transitive closure.
- Main contributions (as I see them):
  1) Demonstration that sentence-level CNN features learned for event mentions can replace external semantic resources for event linking, achieving state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 across several coreference metrics.
  2) A careful empirical study: ablations of feature classes (pairwise, lexical, sentential), analysis of clustering strategies (BestLink vs transitive closure), and an error analysis highlighting common failure modes and annotation inconsistencies.
  3) Construction of an extended ACE++ dataset to study data scale effects and showing gains in recall.

Strengths
- Clear methodological framing and motivation: The paper convincingly argues for sentential features as a way to reduce reliance on external resources (WordNet, FrameNet), and supports this with ablations showing sentential information materially improves both precision and recall over pairwise-only and lexical-only variants.
- Solid empirical results: On ACE, the model is competitive or better than prior work across BLANC, MUC, and positive-links F1, despite not using external semantic resources. The ACE++ experiments demonstrate that more training data yields substantial recall improvements, consistent with the hypothesis that neural sentential features generalize with data.
- Thoughtful analysis of clustering: The comparison of transitive-closure vs BestLink reveals that BestLink’s precision boost is outweighed by recall loss for this model, suggesting the classifier already suppresses low-confidence links. This is informative for the community given prior claims favoring BestLink.
- Useful feature ablations: The table disentangles the contribution of pairwise, lexical, and sentential features and highlights that all three contribute; notably, sentential features are most impactful, aligning with the central claim.
- Error analysis with concrete phenomena: The manual study identifies pronoun-mediated event references and annotation inconsistency as major sources of error, giving actionable directions (better discourse modeling; handling underspecified contexts).

Weaknesses
- Fairness and realism of supervision: The evaluation appears to rely on gold triggers and (at least for some pairwise features) gold event types and possibly gold arguments (argument overlap). The paper acknowledges competitors differ in their use of gold arguments vs SRL outputs. Using gold event types/arguments substantially simplifies linking and limits applicability in realistic pipelines. The paper should clearly state which gold annotations are used at test time, quantify performance with predicted types/arguments, and discuss the impact on deployment.
- Limited novelty in modeling: The architectural components (piecewise CNN over sentences; concatenation with local lexical features; pairwise features; pairwise scoring with simple nonlinearity) are standard adaptations from relation classification and mention-pair coreference. The “square” activation is a minor twist; no substantive theoretical or empirical justification is provided beyond a brief claim of outperformance without quantitative evidence. The contribution is primarily empirical rather than methodological.
- Dataset and reproducibility concerns: The augmented ACE++ dataset is not publicly available (as far as stated), yet it underpins key claims about data scaling and model superiority. Without releasing the additional documents/annotations or at least providing detailed statistics and selection criteria, the community cannot reproduce those results. Moreover, comparisons on ACE use different splits across papers; while the authors note this, they do not provide standardized re-runs or statistical significance to substantiate superiority claims.
- Scope of baselines: The comparison omits more global/coreference-aware models (e.g., cluster-ranking, entity-event joint models) and stronger neural mention-ranking baselines. The BestLink and pairwise-only baselines are informative, but a global inference baseline (e.g., ILP/graph partitioning/spectral clustering) with the same pairwise scores would strengthen the case.
- Missing implementation and evaluation details: Hyperparameters are said to be in a table (not included here); quantitative comparisons of the “square” nonlinearity vs ReLU/tanh/cosine are not reported; no statistical significance tests are provided. Runtime/efficiency and the O(n^2) pairing cost are not discussed, nor is performance as a function of document length/mention density.

Questions to authors
- What supervision is gold vs predicted at test time? Specifically: event triggers, event types (coarse/fine), realis/modality, and arguments used for “argument overlap.” If any are predicted, which systems are used and what is their accuracy? If gold, can you report results with predicted annotations to assess realistic performance?
- How many of the pairwise positive examples in ACE/ACE++ share event type and/or arguments? Ablations suggest type agreement is a strong signal—how much does the model rely on it vs the sentential encoder?
- Can you provide quantitative comparisons for the “square” similarity vs alternative activations and cosine similarity? How sensitive is performance to dsim?
- Is ACE++ publicly available or releasable? If not, can you provide stratified performance on ACE-only training to isolate gains from architecture vs data size?
- Did you evaluate statistical significance of improvements on ACE across metrics? If so, please report.

Additional comments
- The CNN encodes only the sentence; for cross-sentential cues beyond pairwise features (e.g., discourse structure, coref chains of entities), the model may be capacity-limited. Incorporating document context (e.g., hierarchical encoders) could target the documented pronoun-mediated failures.
- Given the high rate of long-distance links, it would be valuable to report performance by sentence distance buckets.
- Consider evaluating a simple global clustering objective (e.g., best-first with threshold, correlation clustering) fed by your pairwise scores to examine precision-recall trade-offs more comprehensively.
- Please clarify training example generation: all antecedent pairs vs Soon-style nearest-positive—helpful that you tried both; could you share the relative class imbalance and negative sampling strategy (if any)?

Overall recommendation: Borderline accept. The paper’s main value lies in a careful empirical demonstration that CNN-derived sentential features can match prior resource-heavy systems for event linking, with ablations and analyses that will be useful to practitioners. However, concerns about reliance on gold annotations, limited novelty, and reproducibility with ACE++ should be addressed in the response.
