

# Peer Review: "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary

This paper addresses event linking (event coreference resolution), the task of resolving coreferences between recognized event mentions in a document. The authors argue that previous approaches either rely on external semantic features (like WordNet or FrameNet) with limited domain coverage or show low performance. They propose a novel system that uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, eliminating the need for external semantic resources. Their model has two parts: (1) a CNN-based model that generates representations for individual event mentions using sentential features, and (2) a model that combines these representations with pairwise features to determine coreference. The authors evaluate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset and a new dataset they created (ACE++), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic features.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel approach to event linking using sentential features from CNNs**: The authors present a fundamentally new approach to event linking that eliminates the need for external semantic features (WordNet, FrameNet), which have limited domain coverage. This is a significant contribution because it makes the approach more generalizable across different domains.

2. **Empirical justification for event linking importance**: Through analysis of the ACE 2005 corpus, the authors demonstrate that approximately 23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level with respect to information in other mentions of the same event instance, justifying the importance of event linking as a task.

3. **State-of-the-art performance without external semantic features**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art performance on event linking without relying on external semantic resources, while previous state-of-the-art systems (Liu et al., 2014) required a "rich set of 105 semantic features" that are domain-specific.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Generalizable Approach**: The authors make a compelling case for eliminating reliance on external semantic features, which is a significant limitation of previous work. Their approach using sentential features from CNNs is well-motivated by recent literature on event extraction and provides a more generalizable solution that can be applied across different domains without requiring specialized external resources. The paper effectively demonstrates that high performance in event linking is achievable without external semantic knowledge, which is a substantial contribution to the field.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, outperforming previous methods in key metrics (BLANC, MUC, positive links). Most importantly, they achieve this without the need for external semantic features that previous state-of-the-art systems relied on. Their ablation study (Table 5) thoroughly demonstrates the contribution of each feature class, showing that sentential features are particularly important for performance, which validates their core hypothesis.

3. **Comprehensive Ablation Study**: The authors conduct a thorough analysis of their model's components, including the impact of different feature classes and clustering strategies. Their finding that BestLink clustering (recommended by Liu et al., 2014) actually harms performance on their model (Table 4) is particularly valuable, as it demonstrates that their model's classification step already effectively filters out low-confidence links, making downstream filtering unnecessary. This provides important insights into the behavior of neural models for event linking.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Neural Approaches**: The paper compares primarily with older methods (Liu et al., 2014; Bejan and Harabagiu, 2010) but fails to compare with more recent neural approaches to event linking. The paper mentions other neural approaches for relation classification (Socher et al., 2012; Hashimoto et al., 2013), but doesn't incorporate them into their evaluation. This is a significant weakness because it makes it difficult to assess whether their approach is truly state-of-the-art compared to the latest methods in the field. For instance, the recent work by Chen et al. (2015) on relation extraction with neural networks could provide a more relevant baseline.

2. **Limited Analysis of Why Sentential Features Work**: While the authors argue that sentential features are effective for event linking, they don't provide a detailed analysis of *why* these features work well. For example, they could have provided examples of specific aspects of the sentential features that are most indicative of coreference, or conducted a more in-depth analysis of the CNN's learned features. This would strengthen their argument for the importance of sentential features and provide deeper insights into the model's behavior.

3. **Insufficient Details on Dataset Construction**: The authors created a new dataset (ACE++) by adding 1351 web documents to ACE 2005. However, they provide no details about how these web documents were annotated, how they compare to ACE 2005 in terms of domain coverage, or whether the annotation quality is comparable. This makes it difficult to assess the validity of ACE++ as a benchmark dataset. Without this information, it's hard to determine if the performance improvements on ACE++ (Table 4) are due to better data or a better model.

4. **Superficial Error Analysis**: The error analysis (Section 5.3) is somewhat limited. The authors identify two main error types (pronoun resolution and boundary cases) but don't provide a detailed analysis of these errors or how they could be addressed. A more in-depth analysis would help identify the limitations of the approach and guide future work. For example, they could have categorized errors by the specific types of event mentions or by the distance between coreferential mentions.

5. **Lack of Computational Efficiency Discussion**: The authors don't discuss the computational efficiency of their approach compared to previous methods. Given that they're using CNNs, which can be computationally intensive, it's important to know if their approach is feasible for real-world applications. This is particularly relevant for event linking in large-scale document collections.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance, but they don't compare with more recent neural approaches to event linking. Could they provide a comparison with these approaches (e.g., Chen et al., 2015; Zeng et al., 2015) to better establish their method's relative performance?

2. The authors created ACE++ by adding 1351 web documents to ACE 2005. Could they provide more details about how these web documents were annotated, how they compare to ACE 2005 in terms of domain coverage and annotation quality, and whether the annotation guidelines were the same as for ACE 2005?

3. The authors state that "55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart." Given this long-distance coreference, could they elaborate on how their sentential features are effective for handling such cases, and whether they've analyzed the model's performance on these long-distance pairs specifically?

## Additional Comments

- The paper mentions that "50.97% of coreferential event-mentions pairs share no arguments," which is an interesting finding. Could the authors provide a few examples of such cases to help readers understand why arguments are often not shared between coreferential mentions?

- The authors state that "55.42% are more than five sentences apart," which suggests that locality-based heuristics would not work well. However, the paper doesn't discuss how their model specifically handles these long-distance cases. It would be valuable to know if the sentential features are effective for these cases.

- The paper states that they "beat Liu et al. (2014)'s system in terms of F1 score on BLANC, MUC, and positive-links," but they don't provide the exact numbers for Liu et al. on these metrics. It would be helpful to include these numbers for better context.

- The authors mention that they "use the square function as the network's non-linearity is backed by the intuition that for measuring similarity, an invariance under polarity changes is desirable." Could they provide evidence that the square function is indeed better than other activation functions for this task?

## Overall Recommendation

Accept. The paper presents a novel approach to event linking that eliminates reliance on external semantic features while achieving state-of-the-art performance. The work is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that high-performance event linking can be achieved without domain-specific external resources. While there are some weaknesses (particularly regarding comparison with recent neural approaches and dataset details), these do not undermine the core contribution of the paper. The work is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance at CONLL.
