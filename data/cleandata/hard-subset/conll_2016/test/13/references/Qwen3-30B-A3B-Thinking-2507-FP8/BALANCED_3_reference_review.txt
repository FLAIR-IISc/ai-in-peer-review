

# CONLL Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event linking (event coreference resolution) that avoids reliance on external semantic features (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet) that have limited cross-domain availability. The authors design a two-step system: (1) a CNN-based model for generating representations of individual event mentions using sentential features, and (2) a pairwise coreference decision model that combines these representations with standard pairwise features.

**Main Contributions (ordered by strength):**

1. **Demonstration that state-of-the-art event linking performance can be achieved without external semantic features**: The authors successfully show that their neural approach achieves competitive results without relying on expensive, domain-limited resources like WordNet or FrameNet, making the approach more generally applicable across domains.

2. **Thorough empirical analysis of feature importance**: The paper provides a detailed ablation study examining the impact of different feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, and sentential features), offering valuable insights into what aspects of the model contribute most to performance.

3. **Comprehensive error analysis identifying specific challenges**: The authors conduct a detailed manual analysis of false positives and false negatives, revealing that pronoun resolution and boundary cases in annotation are significant challenges for current models.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of sentential features to event linking**: The paper makes a strong contribution by applying the recently proposed "sentential features" approach (previously used for relation extraction) to event linking. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this approach can achieve state-of-the-art performance without external semantic resources. This is particularly valuable as previous work on event linking has heavily relied on these limited-domain resources, making systems difficult to port across domains.

2. **Rigorous evaluation and comparison**: The paper provides a thorough comparison with previous state-of-the-art systems, acknowledging caveats in the comparison (e.g., different train/test splits, preprocessing choices). The authors demonstrate clear performance improvements over Liu et al. (2014) in most metrics (BLANC, MUC, positive links), while noting the only area where their approach is slightly worse (B-CUBED). This balanced comparison shows a high level of scholarly rigor.

3. **Insightful ablation study**: The paper's analysis of feature classes (Table 5) is particularly strong, showing that all feature classes contribute positively to performance. The authors also demonstrate the impact of dataset size (Table 4), showing that additional training data significantly improves recall while maintaining reasonable precision, which is a valuable practical insight.

4. **Meaningful error analysis**: The error analysis (Section 5.3) goes beyond typical error analysis by identifying specific challenges like pronoun resolution and boundary cases in annotation. This analysis not only explains the model's limitations but also provides clear directions for future work (e.g., "investigate if the model can benefit from more fine-grained information about the discourse structure").

5. **Practical contribution with ACE++ dataset**: By creating ACE++, a larger dataset that extends ACE 2005 with 1351 additional web documents, the authors provide a more robust testbed for event linking research, addressing the limitation of the small ACE 2005 corpus (599 documents).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core neural architecture**: While the application to event linking is novel, the core architecture (CNN for sentence representation followed by pairwise classification) is not particularly innovative. Similar architectures have been used extensively in relation extraction (Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015). The paper does not sufficiently distinguish their approach from these prior works, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of their contribution.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent neural approaches for event linking**: The paper compares primarily to older state-of-the-art systems (e.g., Liu et al., 2014), but doesn't compare to more recent neural approaches that might have addressed similar challenges. For example, the paper could have compared with approaches like those in Zhou et al. (2016) or Zhang and Wallace (2015), which might have used similar neural architectures but for event linking.

3. **Unclear integration with full event extraction pipeline**: The paper focuses solely on event linking but doesn't discuss how their model would integrate into a complete event extraction pipeline. In practice, event linking would typically be applied after event extraction has already identified triggers and arguments. The paper doesn't address how their model would handle errors from the event extraction component, which is a significant practical consideration.

4. **Ambiguous evaluation metrics and dataset splits**: The paper acknowledges that ACE splits vary across systems, but doesn't provide a detailed explanation of their specific splits or how they compare to other systems. This weakens the strength of their comparison claims, as it's unclear whether the performance differences are due to the model or differences in dataset partitioning.

5. **Limited discussion of computational requirements**: The paper mentions using TensorFlow but provides no details about training time, inference time, or resource requirements. For a practical system, these are important considerations that would help assess the real-world applicability of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model specifically differ from Chen et al. (2015), which also used CNNs for relation extraction with sentential features? What specific adaptations were made to apply this to event linking?

2. The paper states that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart. How does your model specifically handle such long-distance coreference, and what features are most important for this task?

3. Have you compared your model to more recent neural approaches for event linking (e.g., those using attention mechanisms or transformer-based architectures) that might also avoid external features? If not, why not?

4. The ACE++ dataset includes 1351 additional web documents. How were these documents annotated, and how does their annotation quality compare to the ACE 2005 corpus? Were they annotated by the same team or different annotators?

5. In the error analysis, you mention pronoun resolution as a significant challenge. Have you considered incorporating discourse parsing or other discourse-level features to specifically address this issue?

## Recommendation

**Decision: Weak Accept**

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that state-of-the-art event linking performance can be achieved without relying on external semantic features. The empirical results are strong, the analysis is thorough, and the error analysis provides meaningful insights for future work.

However, the paper's core neural architecture is not particularly novel, and it would benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent neural approaches for event linking. The limited discussion of how the model would integrate into a full event extraction pipeline also weakens the practical contribution.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend addressing the questions above, particularly by providing a more detailed comparison with recent neural approaches for event linking and clarifying the integration with event extraction pipelines. With these improvements, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field.
