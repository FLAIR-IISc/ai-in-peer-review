

# Peer Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event linking (coreference resolution of event mentions) that eliminates reliance on external semantic resources. The authors present a two-step model: (1) a CNN-based architecture that generates event mention representations using sentential features (full sentence context), and (2) a pairwise classification model that determines coreference between event mentions using these representations alongside standard pairwise features. The key contributions are:

1. Demonstrating state-of-the-art event linking performance without requiring external semantic resources (WordNet, FrameNet, etc.), which have limited domain coverage and are expensive to maintain.
2. Providing empirical evidence that sentential features from neural networks are sufficient for high-quality event linking, eliminating the need for complex external semantic feature engineering.
3. Creating and releasing ACE++, a new dataset combining ACE 2005 with additional web documents annotated in the same style, which significantly improves performance (from 73.31% to 76.90% F1 on BLANC).

## Strengths

1. **Practical significance of eliminating external resources**: The paper makes a compelling argument about the limitations of existing approaches that rely on external semantic resources. As the authors note, these resources are domain-limited and expensive to maintain, making them impractical for many real-world applications. Their demonstration that high performance can be achieved without these resources is a significant practical contribution that addresses a genuine pain point in the field.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on both ACE 2005 and the new ACE++ dataset. The improvements over Liu et al. (2014) in BLANC, MUC, and positive links metrics are statistically significant (73.31% vs 70.43% F1 on BLANC), which is particularly impressive given that Liu et al.'s approach used 105 semantic features. This demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach.

3. **Rigorous feature ablation studies**: The authors provide thorough ablation studies (Table 5) that clearly demonstrate the contribution of each feature class to the model's performance. The finding that removing sentential features (row 3 vs 4) leads to a substantial drop in recall (83.21% to 70.71%) provides strong evidence for the importance of their key innovation.

4. **Useful error analysis**: The error analysis reveals important insights about the limitations of current approaches, particularly the difficulty with pronoun resolution (e.g., "It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me"). The authors correctly identify this as a fundamental challenge in the field, not just a flaw in their model.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inadequate comparison to prior work**: The paper claims state-of-the-art performance, but the comparison is undermined by inconsistent experimental setups. As the authors themselves note, "the exact documents in the partitions vary from system to system" and "published methods follow different strategies regarding preprocessing components." This makes it difficult to assess whether the performance gains are truly due to their method or differences in experimental setup.

2. **ACE++ dataset quality concerns**: The paper doesn't sufficiently address the quality of the additional web documents in ACE++. Without details on how these were annotated (e.g., were they annotated by the same team using the same guidelines as ACE 2005?), it's hard to assess whether ACE++ represents a valid extension of the original corpus. The claim that "the additional training data" improves performance (Table 4) may simply reflect better data rather than a better model.

3. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any information about the computational resources required to train or deploy their model. Given that neural networks are typically more computationally intensive than traditional machine learning approaches, this is a significant omission for practitioners considering adoption.

4. **Overstated novelty**: The CNN architecture used is largely standard for NLP tasks (similar to Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). The paper doesn't clearly articulate what's novel about their specific implementation beyond applying these techniques to event linking. The claim of "state-of-the-art performance" is supported, but the novelty of the approach itself is not sufficiently established.

## Questions to Authors

1. How was ACE++ created? Specifically, what quality control measures were taken to ensure the additional web documents were annotated consistently with ACE 2005?

2. How do you address the fact that previous systems like Liu et al. (2014) used different preprocessing (e.g., semantic-role labelers) that could affect the comparison? Could you provide a fairer comparison using the same preprocessing pipeline?

3. What are the computational requirements (training time, inference time, memory) for your model compared to previous approaches?

## Additional Comments

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that high-quality event linking can be achieved without external semantic resources. The error analysis is particularly strong, as it identifies a fundamental challenge in the field (pronoun resolution) rather than just listing model weaknesses. The authors should clarify the limitations of their approach regarding this challenge in the conclusion.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the claims about novelty and the comparison to prior work need to be more carefully qualified to ensure they're fully supported by the evidence. The use of ACE++ as a new benchmark is a significant contribution that will benefit future research in this area.
