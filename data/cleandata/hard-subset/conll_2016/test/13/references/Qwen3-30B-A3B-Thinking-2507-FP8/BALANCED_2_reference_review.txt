

# Review for "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a new approach to event linking (event coreference resolution) that uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions. The authors argue that previous approaches rely on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, which have limited domain coverage. Their model first generates representations for individual event mentions using CNNs that capture sentential features, then combines these representations with pairwise features for coreference decisions. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets without relying on external semantic features.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel neural approach to event linking without external semantic resources:** The paper presents a system that achieves state-of-the-art results for event linking without requiring external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, which have limited domain coverage. This is a significant contribution as it removes a major barrier to deploying event linking systems across domains.

2. **Comprehensive data analysis and dataset extension:** The authors provide a thorough analysis of the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating that 23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level and that coreferential event mentions often share no arguments (50.97%) or have different trigger words (47.29%). They also create a new, larger dataset (ACE++) by adding 1351 web documents annotated in an analogous fashion to ACE 2005, which addresses a key limitation of previous work.

3. **Empirical validation of sentential features for event linking:** The paper provides strong evidence that sentential features (generated via CNNs) are valuable for event linking, as shown through ablation studies where removing these features significantly degrades performance.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical justification for the approach:** The paper provides a compelling argument that previous approaches for event linking suffer from reliance on external semantic resources with limited domain coverage. The authors support this with a thorough analysis of the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating that coreferential event mentions often share no arguments or have different triggers (50.97% and 47.29% respectively), making intrinsic semantic features insufficient. This analysis is well-executed and directly motivates their approach.

2. **Clear experimental design and thorough evaluation:** The authors conduct comprehensive experiments including comparisons to state-of-the-art systems, ablation studies on feature classes, and analysis of clustering strategies. Their results show a clear performance boost when using sentential features (from 62.24/76.23/64.12 to 73.22/83.21/76.90 on ACE++), and they provide a useful error analysis highlighting the difficulty of pronoun resolution. The creation of the ACE++ dataset addresses a key limitation of previous work (small ACE 2005 dataset).

3. **Practical contribution to the field:** The paper demonstrates that state-of-the-art performance can be achieved without relying on external semantic resources, which is a significant practical contribution. This makes the approach more deployable across domains where resources like WordNet or FrameNet are unavailable or incomplete. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of their approach compared to previous work that uses "a rich set of 105 semantic features."

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to recent neural approaches:** The paper compares to systems from 2010-2014 (Liu et al. 2014, Bejan and Harabagiu 2010), but fails to compare with more recent neural approaches for event linking. The field has advanced significantly since then, with transformer-based models (BERT, etc.) becoming the standard for many NLP tasks. The authors should compare with recent neural approaches to establish the true novelty of their contribution.

2. **Insufficient justification for ACE++ dataset creation:** The authors create ACE++ by adding 1351 web documents annotated in an analogous fashion to ACE 2005, but they don't provide details about the annotation process, quality control measures, or how these documents were selected. Without knowing how the annotations were created, it's difficult to assess the quality of the new dataset or trust the results on ACE++. This is a significant limitation for reproducibility.

3. **Lack of analysis of computational requirements:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed model compared to previous approaches. For a practical system, this would be an important consideration, especially given that neural models can be computationally expensive. The authors should provide details about training time, inference speed, and resource requirements.

4. **The model architecture isn't sufficiently novel:** While the use of CNNs for sentential features is presented as novel, the overall two-step architecture (first generate event representations, then make coreference decisions) is similar to previous work. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their model differs from existing neural approaches to event linking, making it hard to assess the true novelty.

5. **Limited error analysis:** The error analysis is limited to 100 false positives and 100 false negatives. A more thorough analysis would be valuable to understand specific failure modes. The paper mentions pronoun resolution as a significant challenge but doesn't explore how this could be addressed in future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why didn't you compare your approach to recent neural approaches for event linking, such as those using BERT or other transformer-based models? How would your model compare to these approaches, and what are the key differences in performance?

2. Can you provide more details about the ACE++ dataset creation process? Specifically, how were the 1351 web documents selected, and how were the event annotations created? Were they done by the same annotators as ACE 2005, or were new annotators used? What quality control measures were implemented?

3. The paper states that the model "achieves state-of-the-art performance," but the comparison to Liu et al. (2014) is limited by different data splits and preprocessing. How much of the performance difference can be attributed to the model architecture versus these implementation differences?

4. The paper mentions that "the model does not rely on external semantic features," but doesn't provide evidence that it generalizes well to domains without these resources. Can you provide examples or experiments showing that your model performs well in a domain without external semantic resources?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a strong case for the value of sentential features in event linking. The authors have done a good job of demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results without external resources. However, the lack of comparison to more recent neural approaches and insufficient details about the ACE++ dataset creation are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed.

## Recommendation

**Reject** (with strong possibility of acceptance after major revisions)

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by showing that state-of-the-art event linking can be achieved without external semantic resources. However, the lack of comparison to recent neural approaches and insufficient details about the ACE++ dataset creation are serious concerns that need to be addressed. If the authors can provide these details and compare with recent approaches, this paper could be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the weaknesses outweigh the strengths, but the paper has potential for improvement.
