

# Peer Review: "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary

This paper addresses event coreference resolution (event linking), which involves resolving coreferences between recognized event mentions in a document. The authors propose a novel approach that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, eliminating the need for external semantic features like WordNet or FrameNet. Their model has two components: (1) a CNN-based module that generates representations for individual event mentions by incorporating the surrounding sentence context, and (2) a module that combines these representations with pairwise features to make coreference decisions. The authors evaluate their approach on ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++ dataset, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance without external semantic resources.

## Main Contributions

1. **Demonstration that external semantic features are not necessary for high-performing event coreference resolution**: The authors show that a CNN-based approach using sentential features can achieve state-of-the-art results without relying on external resources like WordNet or FrameNet, which have limited domain coverage.

2. **Systematic analysis of feature contributions**: The paper provides a detailed empirical analysis of the impact of different feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, and sentential features) on model performance, revealing that all contribute positively to the overall results.

3. **Comparison of clustering strategies**: The authors compare different strategies for generating event clusters from pairwise coreference decisions (transitive closure vs. BestLink), providing empirical evidence that transitive closure performs better for their model, contrary to previous work.

## Strengths

1. **Significant methodological contribution**: The paper makes a compelling case that event coreference resolution can be performed effectively without external semantic resources. This is a major contribution because previous approaches heavily relied on these resources, which are expensive to create and have limited domain coverage. The authors demonstrate that their CNN-based approach using sentential features achieves comparable or better performance than previous methods that use these external resources, making their approach more generalizable across domains.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation across multiple datasets (ACE and ACE++), with detailed analysis of feature contributions, dataset size impact, and clustering strategies. The error analysis is particularly valuable, revealing important insights about the challenges of event coreference resolution, including the difficulty of pronoun resolution and annotation disagreements.

3. **Clear model design and rationale**: The authors provide a well-structured explanation of their model architecture and the rationale behind each design decision. The two-step approach (event representation learning followed by coreference decision making) is logically sound and well-motivated by the data analysis presented in Section 3. The paper also clearly explains the limitations of previous approaches that relied on external features.

## Weaknesses

1. **Unfair comparison to previous work**: The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is undermined by the fact that comparisons with previous work are not entirely fair. The authors acknowledge that different systems use different data splits and preprocessing, which affects the comparability of results. For instance, they note that Liu et al. (2014) use gold-annotated event mentions with a semantic-role labeler, while their system uses gold-annotated event mentions but not gold arguments. This inconsistency makes it difficult to determine whether the performance difference is due to the model or the preprocessing.

2. **Lack of detail about ACE++ dataset**: The paper introduces ACE++, a new dataset created by adding 1351 web documents to the ACE 2005 corpus, but provides insufficient details about how these additional documents were annotated. Were they annotated by the same team as ACE 2005? Were they annotated using the same guidelines? Without this information, it's difficult to assess the quality of the ACE++ dataset and how it compares to the original ACE 2005 corpus.

3. **Inadequate discussion of limitations**: The paper identifies challenges like pronoun resolution as a source of error, but doesn't provide enough detail about how these limitations might be addressed in future work. The error analysis mentions that "17 false negatives would involve the resolution of a pronoun to a previous event mention," but doesn't elaborate on why their model fails at this task or how future models might overcome this limitation. This weakens the paper's contribution to guiding future research.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that "the exact documents in the partitions vary from system to system" for the ACE dataset. Could you provide the specific document splits used in your experiments so that other researchers can reproduce your results and make more meaningful comparisons with previous work?

2. The ACE++ dataset is introduced as a new resource, but the paper lacks details about how the additional 1351 web documents were annotated. Could you provide more information about the annotation process, including the guidelines used and the annotators' expertise?

3. In Section 5.1, you note that "the model already weeds out many low confidence links in the classification step," which makes BestLink clustering unnecessary. Could you provide more insight into why your model is able to do this better than previous approaches, and whether this is a general property of CNN-based approaches to coreference resolution?

## Additional Comments

The paper has a clear structure and presents a well-motivated approach to event coreference resolution. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the error analysis provides valuable insights. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the fair comparison with previous work and the detailed description of the ACE++ dataset.

The authors' claim that "low linking performance, coming from a lack of semantic knowledge about a domain, is evitable" is an important contribution to the field, but would be more convincing with a more rigorous comparison to previous work that accounts for differences in preprocessing and data splits.

The paper's contribution to demonstrating that external semantic features are not necessary for high-performing event coreference resolution is significant and should be highlighted more strongly in the discussion.
