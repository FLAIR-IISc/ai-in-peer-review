

# Peer Review: "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary

This paper proposes a neural network approach for event coreference resolution (event linking) that leverages sentential features generated by convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors argue that previous approaches to event linking rely heavily on external semantic resources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet), which are expensive to create and have limited domain coverage. Their model consists of two steps: (1) a CNN-based model that generates representations of individual event mentions by processing the full sentence context around the event trigger, and (2) a model that combines these representations with pairwise features to make coreference decisions. The approach is evaluated on ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++ dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results without external semantic features. The paper also provides a thorough error analysis, identifying specific challenges like pronoun resolution.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Demonstration that neural sentential features can replace external semantic resources for event linking**: The paper convincingly shows that a CNN-based approach can achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on expensive external semantic resources, addressing a significant limitation in the field.

2. **Comprehensive analysis of feature contributions**: The ablation studies provide clear evidence of how different feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local, sentential) contribute to performance, which is valuable for understanding what aspects of context are most important for event linking.

3. **Creation of ACE++ dataset**: The extension of the ACE 2005 corpus with 1351 additional web documents annotated in a similar fashion creates a valuable resource for future research in event linking.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and problem statement**: The paper effectively establishes the importance of event linking by providing concrete statistics from ACE 2005 (23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level) and citing relevant prior work. The argument against relying on external semantic resources is well-supported by the limited domain coverage and high cost of these resources.

2. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper includes comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art systems, ablation studies of different feature classes, and a detailed error analysis. The results demonstrate that the model significantly outperforms previous approaches that rely on external resources (e.g., Liu et al., 2014) while achieving comparable performance to those systems without needing the same resource dependencies.

3. **Insightful error analysis**: The error analysis identifies specific challenges (e.g., pronoun resolution, ambiguous cases) that remain difficult even for the best systems. The authors provide concrete examples of cases where human annotators disagree, which puts performance metrics in context and demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the task's difficulty.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent neural coreference approaches**: The paper compares with Liu et al. (2014) and other prior work, but doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches to coreference resolution (e.g., Lee et al., 2017; Joshi et al., 2017; Clark and Manning, 2016). These approaches have achieved state-of-the-art results on general coreference resolution and could provide a stronger baseline for the novelty of the authors' approach. The absence of these comparisons weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.

2. **Insufficient details about ACE++ dataset creation**: The paper introduces ACE++ as a valuable extension of ACE 2005, but provides minimal details about the annotation process for the additional 1351 web documents. Without information about annotation guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, and quality control, it's difficult to assess whether ACE++ is truly comparable to ACE 2005. This is particularly concerning since the model's performance on ACE++ is presented as a key contribution.

3. **Limited discussion of architectural choices**: The paper uses CNNs for generating sentential features but doesn't provide a thorough justification for why CNNs are better suited for this task than other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs, LSTMs, Transformers). The authors briefly mention that "more types of neural architectures lend themselves to the generation of sentential features" but don't provide any comparative analysis or ablation study of these alternatives.

4. **Lack of computational efficiency analysis**: The paper focuses on performance metrics but doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the proposed model. For real-world applications, scalability is crucial, yet the paper provides no information about training time, inference time, or memory requirements, which would be important for assessing the model's practicality.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to recent neural coreference resolution approaches (e.g., Lee et al., 2017; Joshi et al., 2017) that have achieved state-of-the-art results on general coreference resolution? Have you considered adapting these approaches to the event coreference resolution task, and if so, why would your CNN-based approach be preferable?

2. Could you provide more detailed information about the ACE++ dataset creation process? Specifically:
   - What annotation guidelines were used for the additional web documents?
   - How many annotators were involved, and what was the inter-annotator agreement score?
   - How were the web documents selected, and what was the process for ensuring annotation quality?

3. The paper mentions that "sentential features from event-/relation-mention representations" have shown promising results in recent literature. Could you elaborate on how the specific CNN architecture used in this paper differs from or improves upon the approaches cited in the literature (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; etc.) for the event linking task?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper, but with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses identified above in the camera-ready version. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that event linking can be performed effectively without external semantic resources, which is a significant practical advantage. The thorough evaluation and insightful error analysis strengthen the paper's contribution. However, the lack of comparison with more recent neural approaches and insufficient details about the ACE++ dataset creation process are notable weaknesses that should be addressed before publication.

The paper's strengths in motivation, experimental design, and error analysis outweigh the weaknesses, particularly since the authors have already demonstrated strong results. With the suggested clarifications, this paper would make a meaningful contribution to the field of event coreference resolution.
