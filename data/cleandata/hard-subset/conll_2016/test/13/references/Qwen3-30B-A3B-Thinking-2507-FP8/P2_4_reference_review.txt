

# Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event linking (event coreference resolution) that leverages sentential features from convolutional neural networks. The authors argue that previous approaches rely heavily on external semantic resources (WordNet, FrameNet) with limited cross-domain availability, and they demonstrate that their CNN-based model using sentential features can achieve state-of-the-art performance without these resources. The model consists of two steps: (1) generating representations for individual event mentions by processing the full sentence context with CNNs, and (2) making coreference decisions based on these representations plus pairwise features.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel neural network model for event linking that eliminates reliance on external semantic resources
2. Comprehensive analysis of feature importance through ablation studies
3. State-of-the-art performance on event linking without external semantic features
4. Detailed error analysis identifying key challenges (e.g., pronoun resolution)

## Strengths

**1. Novel approach to eliminating external semantic resources:** The paper makes a compelling case that previous event linking systems rely on expensive-to-create semantic resources (WordNet, FrameNet) with limited domain coverage. The authors successfully demonstrate that their CNN-based approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without these resources, which significantly broadens the practical applicability of event linking systems to new domains. This is a substantial contribution to the field, as it removes a major barrier to applying event linking in new domains.

**2. Strong empirical evidence with thorough evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), comparing against multiple baselines and performing detailed ablation studies. Their analysis shows that their model's performance improves significantly with additional training data (ACE++), and they provide clear evidence that all feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local, sentential) contribute positively to model performance. The error analysis is particularly valuable, identifying that 17% of false negatives involve pronoun resolution, a known challenging problem in coreference resolution.

**3. Well-motivated model design based on corpus analysis:** The paper demonstrates strong justification for their architectural choices through careful analysis of the ACE corpus. They show that 50.97% of coreferential event pairs share no arguments, and 55.42% are more than five sentences apart, justifying their use of sentential features rather than relying solely on intrinsic event properties or locality-based heuristics. This data-driven design approach significantly strengthens their contribution.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited comparison to recent neural coreference approaches:** While the paper compares to Liu et al. (2014) and others, it fails to compare with more recent neural coreference resolution methods (e.g., Lee et al. 2017, Clark and Manning 2016) that have achieved state-of-the-art results in coreference resolution more broadly. This omission weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance, as the paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach compares to the current best practices in neural coreference resolution.

**2. Insufficient analysis of pronoun resolution challenges:** The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a key challenge but doesn't provide specific insights into why the model fails on these cases or how the model could be improved to handle them. For example, the paper doesn't discuss whether the model's sentential features could be augmented with discourse structure information (as suggested in the conclusion) or how the model might be adapted to better handle pronouns.

**3. Limited discussion of ACE++ dataset construction:** The paper creates a new dataset (ACE++) by adding 1351 web documents to ACE 2005, but provides minimal details about the annotation process or domain characteristics of these additional documents. Without this information, it's difficult to determine whether the performance gains from ACE++ are due to the model's inherent capabilities or simply better domain coverage. The paper should clarify how ACE++ differs from ACE in terms of domain distribution.

**4. Reproducibility concerns:** While the paper mentions using TensorFlow, it lacks sufficient detail about hyperparameters, training procedures, and implementation specifics. The authors mention they used specific hyperparameters but don't provide them in the paper or a link to code. This makes it difficult for others to reproduce their results, which is particularly problematic given the complexity of neural network models.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to recent neural coreference resolution approaches that use end-to-end neural architectures (e.g., Lee et al. 2017, Clark and Manning 2016)? Would it be beneficial to incorporate these more recent architectural innovations?

2. The paper identifies pronoun resolution as a key challenge, but doesn't discuss potential architectural modifications to address this. Could you elaborate on how incorporating discourse structure information (as mentioned in your conclusion) might improve performance on these cases?

3. Could you provide more details about the construction of the ACE++ dataset, particularly regarding the domain characteristics of the additional 1351 web documents? How do these compare to the ACE 2005 documents in terms of domain coverage and annotation quality?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that event linking can be effectively performed without relying on external semantic resources. The model design is well-motivated by corpus analysis, and the empirical results are compelling. While the paper has some limitations in comparing to recent work and providing implementation details, the core contribution is significant and well-supported. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.

The paper's main strength is its practical contribution: it shows that event linking can be made more accessible to new domains without the need for expensive semantic resources. This is a substantial step forward for the field, and the authors provide strong empirical evidence for their claim. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the event coreference resolution literature.
