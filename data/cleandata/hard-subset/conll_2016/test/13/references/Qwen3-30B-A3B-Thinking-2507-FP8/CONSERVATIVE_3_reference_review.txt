

# Review: "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step neural network approach for event coreference resolution (event linking) that uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions. The authors claim their method achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, which previous approaches used. The model first generates representations for individual event mentions using CNNs that process the full sentence context, then combines these representations with pairwise features to make coreference decisions.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A CNN-based architecture for generating sentential features for event mentions without external semantic resources
2. An empirical demonstration that sentential features alone can achieve competitive performance on event linking
3. A detailed analysis of feature contributions and error patterns

## Strengths
1. **Clear and well-structured model design**: The paper provides a thorough technical description of the two-part architecture, with detailed explanations of how the CNN processes sentence context to generate event representations. The design choices (e.g., relative position embeddings, piecewise max-pooling) are well-motivated by the problem characteristics.

2. **Comprehensive ablation studies**: The authors effectively demonstrate the contribution of different feature classes through ablation experiments (Table 5), showing that both sentential features and trigger-local lexical features are necessary for optimal performance. This provides strong evidence for their feature engineering decisions.

3. **Realistic assessment of limitations**: The error analysis (Section 5.3) identifies specific challenges like pronoun resolution and ambiguous cases where even human annotators disagree. This provides valuable context for interpreting the model's performance and avoids overclaiming.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient comparison to recent state-of-the-art neural approaches**: The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance but only compares to older systems (Liu et al. 2014, Bejan and Harabagiu 2010, Sangeetha and Arock 2012). The most relevant recent work (e.g., Zhang and Wallace 2015, Chen et al. 2015, Zeng et al. 2015) that uses similar neural approaches for event extraction/coreference is not properly compared to. The improvement over Liu et al. (2014) is marginal (73.31 vs 70.43 F1 on BLANC), and Liu's system uses 105 semantic features while the authors' system uses a neural approach. The paper fails to demonstrate how their method advances beyond the current neural network-based approaches in the literature.

2. **Unjustified novelty claim**: The authors claim their work is novel because it avoids external semantic resources, but this is not a significant contribution given that many recent neural approaches (Zeng et al. 2014; Nguyen and Grishman 2015; etc.) already avoid these resources. The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how their specific approach differs from these prior neural methods or why their particular CNN architecture is superior.

3. **Limited validation on diverse datasets**: The paper uses ACE 2005 and a derived ACE++ dataset, but doesn't provide enough analysis of model generalization to different domains or text types. The ACE++ dataset is simply ACE 2005 plus web documents, which doesn't represent a significant domain shift. Without evaluation on more diverse datasets (e.g., biomedical, legal, or social media texts), the claim that their approach "does not depend on resources with restricted domain availability" is unsubstantiated.

4. **Questionable "state-of-the-art" claim**: The paper claims to achieve "state-of-the-art" performance, but the comparison is limited to older systems. The improvement over Liu et al. (2014) is modest (only 2.88 F1 points on BLANC), and Liu's system uses a more complex feature set. Without comparison to more recent neural approaches, the claim is not well-supported. The paper should either demonstrate significant improvement over the current state-of-the-art or temper their claim.

5. **Lack of comparison to simpler baselines**: While the paper includes naive baselines (Table 6), it doesn't compare to simpler neural baselines that might be expected to perform well on this task. For example, a simple bi-LSTM or attention-based model might serve as a stronger baseline than the naive approaches used.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your model compare to the neural approaches of Zhang and Wallace (2015) and Chen et al. (2015) for event coreference resolution, which also use neural networks without external semantic resources? These papers would provide a more relevant comparison for your "state-of-the-art" claim.

2. The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance, but the improvement over Liu et al. (2014) is marginal (2.88 F1 points on BLANC). Given that Liu's system uses 105 semantic features while your system uses a neural approach, is the improvement significant enough to justify the "state-of-the-art" claim, or is it simply a trade-off between feature engineering complexity and model complexity?

3. The ACE++ dataset is created by adding web documents to ACE 2005. How does your model perform on a dataset from a completely different domain (e.g., biomedical or legal texts) to demonstrate the generalizability of your approach?

## Overall Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper presents a well-structured neural approach to event linking with reasonable performance, it fails to adequately demonstrate its novelty or superiority over recent neural approaches in the literature. The claimed "state-of-the-art" performance is not sufficiently validated against the most relevant recent work, and the novelty claim is questionable given that similar neural approaches have already been proposed. The paper lacks the rigorous validation against established baselines that would be expected for a CONLL submission. The marginal improvement over existing systems does not justify the novelty claims, and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the authors' assertion that their approach is a significant advancement in the field.
