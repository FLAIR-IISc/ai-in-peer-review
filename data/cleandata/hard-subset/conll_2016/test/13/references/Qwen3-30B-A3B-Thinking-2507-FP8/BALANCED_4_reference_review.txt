

# Review for "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of event linking (event coreference resolution), which involves resolving coreferences between event mentions in a document. The authors propose a neural network-based approach that uses sentential features from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to model event mentions without relying on external semantic features like WordNet or FrameNet. The model has a two-step architecture: first, it generates representations for individual event mentions using CNNs that incorporate sentential features; second, it makes coreference decisions between pairs of event mentions using these representations along with standard pairwise features. The authors evaluate their model on ACE 2005 and a new dataset they created called ACE++, achieving state-of-the-art results.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel neural network-based approach for event linking that does not rely on external semantic features, which are typically used in previous work but have limited domain coverage.
2. An analysis of the properties of coreferential event mentions (e.g., 50.97% share no arguments, 47.29% have different trigger words, 55.42% are more than five sentences apart) that motivates the need for sentential features.
3. Empirical evidence that sentential features from CNNs can effectively capture the necessary information for event linking, achieving state-of-the-art performance without external semantic resources.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to reducing dependency on external resources:** The paper makes a strong case for the importance of event linking (23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level) and demonstrates that high performance can be achieved without relying on expensive, domain-limited external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. This is a meaningful contribution to the field as it makes event processing more practical for new domains without requiring extensive resource development.

2. **Thorough empirical evaluation and ablation studies:** The authors conduct comprehensive experiments, including ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the contribution of each feature class (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, and sentential features) to the overall performance. The results show that adding these features progressively improves performance, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach. The comparison to multiple baselines (including naive baselines) and the analysis of the impact of dataset size are particularly well-executed.

3. **Insightful error analysis:** The error analysis is thorough and identifies specific challenges in event linking, such as pronoun resolution (e.g., "It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me.") and boundary cases where human annotators disagree. This provides valuable insights into the limitations of current approaches and directions for future work, which is crucial for advancing the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty compared to recent literature:** The paper claims to use "sentential features from convolutional neural networks" for event linking, but similar approaches have been extensively explored in recent literature for relation extraction (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; Chen et al., 2015; dos Santos et al., 2015; Zeng et al., 2015). The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its specific model architecture or application to event linking from these prior works. The authors acknowledge using sentential features but don't clearly articulate how their approach differs or why it's particularly effective for event linking.

2. **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods:** The paper compares primarily to Liu et al. (2014), which is from 2014, but doesn't compare to more recent approaches that might have surpassed this work. Given that the paper is from 2016, it would be expected to compare to newer methods that might have emerged since then. The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art" performance may not be valid in the current landscape of event coreference resolution.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations:** While the error analysis identifies specific challenges (e.g., pronoun resolution), the paper doesn't fully address the limitations of their approach. For example, the paper doesn't discuss how their model specifically fails in pronoun resolution cases or how they might address this limitation. The computational cost and scalability of their approach compared to previous methods are also not discussed, which is an important practical consideration for real-world applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach specifically differ from the work of Zeng et al. (2015) or Chen et al. (2015) who also use CNNs for relation/event extraction? What are the specific architectural modifications or insights that make your approach effective for event linking?

2. Have you compared your approach to more recent state-of-the-art methods for event coreference resolution (post-2014)? If not, why not, and how would you expect your method to perform against these newer approaches?

3. The paper mentions that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart. How does your model handle such long-distance coreference, and what are the limitations of your approach in these cases?

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper has several strengths, including a strong empirical evaluation and insightful error analysis, the lack of clear novelty compared to recent literature and the limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods are significant weaknesses that prevent it from meeting CONLL's high standards.

## Constructive Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify novelty:** The authors should more clearly articulate how their specific model architecture or application to event linking differs from prior work on using CNNs for relation/event extraction. This could be done by providing a detailed comparison table of their approach versus the most relevant prior work.

2. **Update comparison to state-of-the-art:** The authors should compare their approach to more recent state-of-the-art methods for event coreference resolution (post-2014) to validate their claim of "state-of-the-art" performance. If they haven't done this, they should acknowledge this limitation in the paper.

3. **Expand discussion of limitations:** The authors should provide a more thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding long-distance coreference and pronoun resolution. They should also discuss computational efficiency and scalability, as these are important practical considerations.

4. **Consider more recent architectures:** Given the rapid advancement in neural network architectures for NLP, the authors might want to explore whether their approach could be enhanced by more recent architectures (e.g., transformers) or whether their CNN-based approach has specific advantages that would still be relevant.

5. **Address the pronoun resolution challenge:** The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a significant challenge. The authors should discuss specific ways their model could be extended or modified to better handle this challenge, as it represents a major gap in current event linking systems.

With these improvements, the paper could make a stronger contribution to the field and potentially be suitable for publication at CONLL.
