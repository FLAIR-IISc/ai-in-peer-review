

# Peer Review: "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper addresses event linking (event coreference resolution), which resolves coreferences between recognized event mentions in documents. The authors demonstrate that approximately 23% of event mentions in the ACE 2005 corpus are incomplete on the argument level, making event linking an important task. They propose a CNN-based approach that generates sentential features for event mentions without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, which previous approaches have used. The model has two components: a CNN for generating event mention representations from surrounding sentences, and a pairwise classifier for coreference decisions.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel approach to event linking that eliminates the need for external semantic resources while achieving state-of-the-art performance.
2. A thorough analysis of event mention characteristics (e.g., 50.97% of coreferential pairs share no arguments, 55.42% are more than five sentences apart) that justifies their approach.
3. Comprehensive evaluation showing the impact of different feature classes and thorough error analysis.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical justification for the problem**: The authors provide compelling evidence from ACE 2005 corpus analysis that event linking is indeed a significant problem (23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level), which strengthens the paper's motivation. This analysis is more rigorous than the cited prior work (Swampillai & Stevenson, 2010; Ji & Grishman, 2011).

2. **Well-designed evaluation framework**: The ablation studies (Table 5) clearly demonstrate the contribution of each feature class to the model's performance. The authors also provide a valuable error analysis that identifies specific limitations (e.g., pronoun resolution challenges) and shows that many errors stem from ambiguous annotations rather than model shortcomings.

3. **Practical contribution**: By eliminating the need for external semantic resources, the authors address a significant practical barrier to deploying event linking systems in new domains. This is particularly valuable for applications where resources like WordNet or FrameNet have limited coverage.

4. **Thorough comparison with prior work**: The comparison with Liu et al. (2014) is well-justified, and the authors correctly acknowledge the trade-offs between their resource-light approach and the more feature-rich approach of prior work. Their claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is supported by their results on ACE++.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable "state-of-the-art" claim**: The comparison with Liu et al. (2014) is problematic because Liu et al. used a "rich set of 105 semantic features," while the authors' model does not. This makes the comparison unfair, as the authors' model has a fundamental advantage in terms of resource requirements. The paper should clarify that they achieve comparable performance *without* external resources, rather than claiming "state-of-the-art" performance in a more general sense.

2. **Limited handling of discourse-level phenomena**: The error analysis reveals that a significant portion of false negatives involves pronoun resolution ("It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me."), which is a core aspect of event coreference resolution. The authors' model appears to have fundamental limitations in handling discourse-level coreference, which is a major weakness for an event linking system.

3. **Insufficient justification for ACE++ dataset**: The authors create ACE++ by adding 1351 web documents to ACE 2005, but they don't provide sufficient justification for why these documents are suitable for event linking or how they were annotated. Without evidence that ACE++ is a valid extension of ACE 2005, the claims about the benefits of larger training data (Table 4) are questionable.

4. **Lack of novelty in model architecture**: While the application to event linking is novel, the CNN-based architecture for sentence representation followed by a pairwise classifier is quite standard in the coreference resolution literature. The authors don't clearly articulate what is novel about their specific architecture compared to recent neural approaches to coreference.

## Questions to Authors

1. How exactly does your model compare to Liu et al. (2014) in terms of the exact evaluation setup (e.g., train/test splits, gold event mentions)? The authors acknowledge caveats in the comparison, but this is crucial to the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.

2. Given that pronoun resolution is a major limitation (as shown in the error analysis), what specific strategies do you propose to address this in future work beyond the vague mention of "discourse structure"?

3. Could you provide more details about the annotation process of the additional documents in ACE++ to ensure consistency with ACE 2005 and justify their inclusion in the evaluation?

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to event linking by eliminating the need for external semantic resources. The empirical analysis is strong, and the approach is practical for real-world applications. However, the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is problematic due to the resource disparity with prior work, and the model's limitations in handling discourse-level phenomena are significant. The paper would be strengthened by a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between resource requirements and performance, and by providing more justification for the ACE++ dataset.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about the "state-of-the-art" claim and to provide more detail about the ACE++ dataset.
