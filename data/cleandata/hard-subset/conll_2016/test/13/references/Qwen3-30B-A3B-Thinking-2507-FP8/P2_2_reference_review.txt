

# Review for CONLL

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event linking (event coreference resolution), which is the task of determining whether different mentions of events in a document refer to the same real-world event. The authors argue that previous approaches rely heavily on external semantic resources like WordNet and FrameNet, which have limited domain coverage. Their approach instead uses "sentential features" generated by convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to represent event mentions, without relying on external semantic resources. The model has two parts: (1) a CNN-based representation of individual event mentions using their surrounding sentence context, and (2) a classifier that makes coreference decisions based on the representations of two event mentions and some pairwise features.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel neural approach for event linking that avoids reliance on external semantic resources**: The paper presents a significant contribution by demonstrating that state-of-the-art event linking performance can be achieved without external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, which have limited domain coverage. The authors show that their CNN-based sentential features are sufficient for high performance.

2. **Thorough empirical evaluation with ablation studies**: The paper provides comprehensive experimental results on two datasets (ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++), with detailed ablation studies that demonstrate the contribution of each feature class to overall performance.

3. **Insightful error analysis**: The paper includes a detailed error analysis of 200 examples (100 false positives and 100 false negatives), which reveals specific challenges like pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, providing valuable insights for future work.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and problem statement**: The paper effectively demonstrates the practical importance of event linking by analyzing the ACE 2005 corpus and showing that approximately 23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level. This analysis provides a compelling justification for the work. The paper also clearly identifies the limitations of previous approaches that rely on external semantic resources with restricted domain coverage.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper includes a thorough comparison to previous state-of-the-art methods (Liu et al., 2014) and shows that the proposed model achieves comparable or better performance on key metrics (BLANC F1: 73.31% vs. 70.43%). The ablation studies (Table 5) provide convincing evidence that each feature class contributes positively to the model's performance, with the full model (including sentential features) outperforming variants that omit specific feature classes.

3. **Insightful error analysis**: The error analysis is one of the strongest aspects of the paper. By examining 100 false positives and 100 false negatives, the authors identify specific challenges like pronoun resolution ("It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me.") and highlight cases where annotator disagreement makes the coreference decision ambiguous. This analysis provides valuable context for interpreting the model's performance and suggests concrete directions for future work.

4. **Clear justification for architectural choices**: The paper provides strong justification for the use of CNNs for generating sentential features, noting that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart, making locality-based heuristics ineffective. This analysis directly supports the choice of neural architectures that can capture long-range dependencies.

5. **Practical implications**: The paper demonstrates that the model can generalize better with additional training data (ACE++ vs. ACE), showing a significant recall improvement (75.16% → 83.21%). This is valuable for practitioners who might have access to additional domain-specific data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated claim of "state-of-the-art" performance**: The paper claims to achieve "state-of-the-art" performance, but the comparison in Table 3 shows that the model outperforms Liu et al. (2014) only on some metrics (BLANC, MUC, positive links) while performing worse on others (B-CUBED). The authors themselves acknowledge that "it seems justified to assess that our model performs in general on-par with their state-of-the-art system." This overstatement weakens the paper's contribution claim.

2. **Limited comparison to recent neural approaches**: The paper compares to Liu et al. (2014), which is not the most recent work in the field. The paper cites some neural approaches (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; etc.), but doesn't directly compare to them in the evaluation. Since the field has advanced significantly since 2014, this comparison is insufficient to claim state-of-the-art performance.

3. **Lack of comparison to alternative neural architectures**: The paper focuses on CNNs but doesn't compare to other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs, LSTMs, transformers) that could potentially be used for the same task. The related work section mentions that "more types of neural architectures lend themselves to the generation of sentential features," but the paper doesn't evaluate these alternatives, missing an opportunity to demonstrate why CNNs are particularly well-suited for this task.

4. **Insufficient detail about the ACE++ dataset**: The paper introduces ACE++, a new dataset created by adding 1351 web documents to ACE 2005, but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of this new dataset. It doesn't discuss how the additional documents differ from ACE 2005 in terms of domain, language, or annotation style, making it difficult to assess whether the performance gains on ACE++ are due to more training data or better domain coverage.

5. **Limited analysis of hyperparameters**: While the paper describes the hyperparameters used, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how different parameter choices affect performance. For example, it doesn't discuss how the number of filters (dc), convolution window size (n), or dimensionality of representations (dw, dp, etc.) impact the model's performance, limiting reproducibility and understanding of the model's behavior.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to more recent neural network approaches for event linking (e.g., those using transformers or attention mechanisms) that have been published since Liu et al. (2014)?

2. Why did you choose CNNs over other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs or transformers) for generating sentential features? Have you tried any of these alternatives, and if so, how did they perform?

3. In Table 3, your model outperforms Liu et al. (2014) on BLANC F1 (73.31% vs. 70.43%) but performs worse on B-CUBED (75.16% vs. 70.01%). Can you provide more insight into why your model might be better at some metrics but worse at others? Is there a fundamental difference in how these metrics evaluate coreference resolution?

4. The paper mentions that "55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart." How does this compare to other coreference resolution tasks (e.g., entity coreference)? Does this difference impact the choice of model architecture?

5. The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a significant challenge. Have you considered incorporating discourse-aware features (e.g., pronoun resolution models) to address this specific challenge?

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that state-of-the-art event linking performance can be achieved without relying on external semantic resources. The thorough experimental evaluation, insightful error analysis, and clear motivation provide strong evidence for the approach. However, the paper should revise its claims about "state-of-the-art" performance to be more accurate and provide a more comprehensive comparison to recent neural approaches. Addressing the questions above would strengthen the paper further.
