

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for event linking (event coreference resolution) that uses sentential features instead of external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art results without relying on external knowledge sources. The paper includes an analysis of feature classes, a comparison of clustering strategies, and an error analysis. They create a new dataset (ACE++) by adding 1351 web documents to the ACE 2005 corpus.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A CNN-based approach for event linking using sentential features (rather than external semantic resources)
2. An empirical analysis of the impact of different feature classes on event linking performance
3. A comparison of clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) for event linking

## Strengths
1. **Novel application of sentential features to event linking**: The paper demonstrates that sentential features from CNNs can be effectively used for event coreference resolution without external semantic resources, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

2. **Thorough ablation study**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of different feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, sentential features), which helps understand what aspects of the model are most important for performance.

3. **Creation of a new dataset (ACE++)**: The addition of 1351 web documents to ACE 2005 creates a larger, more diverse dataset for event linking research, which is a significant contribution to the community.

4. **Clear comparison of clustering strategies**: The paper challenges previous assumptions about the best clustering strategy for event linking by showing that transitive closure outperforms BestLink in their model.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty in core methodology**: The model architecture is nearly identical to previous work cited (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; Chen et al., 2015; dos Santos et al., 2015; Zeng et al., 2015). The paper fails to clearly articulate how this is different from prior work in a meaningful way. The "contribution" appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing approaches to a new task rather than a novel methodological contribution.

2. **Inconsistent and incomplete dataset description**: The paper claims to create ACE++ by adding "1351 more web documents annotated in an analogous fashion" but provides no details on:
   - How the web documents were annotated (who annotated them, what guidelines were used)
   - Whether inter-annotator agreement was measured
   - How the annotation guidelines for the web documents compare to ACE 2005 guidelines
   - This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess the validity of the ACE++ dataset.

3. **Inconsistent and flawed experimental comparisons**: The paper claims to outperform Liu et al. (2014), but the comparison is inconsistent with their own findings. The paper states Liu et al. (2014) uses "a manifold of external feature sources" with "a rich set of 105 semantic features," but then in Table 4, the authors show that their model using BestLink (the strategy Liu et al. used) performs worse than transitive closure. This undermines the comparison, as the paper's own results contradict their claim about Liu et al.'s approach.

4. **Inconsistent terminology and formatting**: The paper uses inconsistent capitalization ("ACE++" vs. "ACE++"), inconsistent use of "event mentions" vs. "event instances," and inconsistent citation styles (some with "et al.", some with full author lists). These minor formatting issues reflect a lack of attention to detail that undermines the paper's professionalism.

5. **Lack of methodological reproducibility details**: The paper states it uses TensorFlow v0.6 (released in 2015), which is extremely outdated. The paper appears to be recent, making this inconsistency problematic for reproducibility. Additionally, the paper mentions "180 points" of random sampling from the hyperparameter space but fails to provide the exact hyperparameter ranges or random seed used, making it difficult to reproduce the results.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to use TensorFlow v0.6, but this version is very old (released in 2015). Why was this specific version chosen instead of a more recent version? Can the authors confirm that the results are reproducible with current TensorFlow versions?

2. The paper states that ACE++ was created by adding "1351 more web documents annotated in an analogous fashion" to ACE 2005. Please provide details on the annotation process for these web documents, including the guidelines used, the annotators, and inter-annotator agreement measures.

3. The paper uses a threshold of 0.5 for coreference decisions in Figure 3 (line 4: "score(mi,mj) > 0.5"). Why was this specific threshold chosen, and how does it compare to the optimal threshold found during validation?

4. The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results but doesn't provide statistical significance tests. Can the authors provide p-values for their performance improvements over previous work?

5. The paper states that "the model can generalize much better using this additional training data" (Table 4, rows 1 vs 3), but the precision decreases from 75.16% to 73.22% when using ACE++ instead of ACE. How does the paper explain this apparent contradiction?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper presents a reasonable application of sentential features to event linking, the numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies significantly undermine its quality. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance due to the following reasons:

1. The core methodology lacks sufficient novelty - it's a straightforward adaptation of existing neural network approaches to event linking rather than a genuinely novel contribution.

2. The dataset description is incomplete and inconsistent, making it difficult to assess the validity of the new ACE++ dataset.

3. The experimental comparison is flawed and inconsistent with the authors' own results.

4. The paper contains numerous minor formatting and consistency issues (inconsistent capitalization, terminology, citation style) that reflect a lack of attention to detail.

5. The lack of reproducibility details (TensorFlow version, hyperparameter ranges) is concerning for a computational linguistics paper.

While the paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field, the cumulative effect of these minor flaws is substantial enough to warrant rejection under the stringent standards of CoNLL. The paper could be improved significantly with more attention to detail, clearer articulation of novelty, and more complete methodological descriptions.
