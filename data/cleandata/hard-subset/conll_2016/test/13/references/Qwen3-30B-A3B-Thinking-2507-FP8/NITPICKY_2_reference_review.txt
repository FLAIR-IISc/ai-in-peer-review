

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event coreference resolution (event linking) that uses sentential features generated by convolutional neural networks, aiming to eliminate the need for external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE 2005 and a new ACE++ dataset) without relying on external semantic features.

My assessment of the main contributions (in order of strength as I see them):

1. The proposed model architecture that combines sentential features with pairwise features for event coreference resolution
2. The creation of a new dataset (ACE++) by augmenting ACE 2005 with additional web documents
3. The empirical evidence showing that sentential features are valuable for event coreference resolution

## Strengths

1. **The model design is well-motivated and well-structured**: The two-step architecture (first learning event representations, then making coreference decisions) is logically sound and well-explained. The authors provide a good rationale for why sentential features are important for event linking, particularly given their analysis showing 50.97% of coreferential pairs share no arguments and 55.42% of coreferential mentions are more than five sentences apart.

2. **The empirical evaluation is comprehensive**: The authors compare their model against multiple state-of-the-art systems on ACE 2005 and conduct ablation studies to analyze the impact of different feature classes. The error analysis provides valuable insights into the limitations of the current approach.

3. **The ACE++ dataset is a useful contribution**: The creation of this larger dataset with additional web documents provides a more robust testbed for event linking research, addressing the limitation of the small ACE 2005 corpus (599 documents).

## Weaknesses

1. **Multiple inconsistent and ambiguous technical details**: The paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies that undermine the reproducibility of the work. For example:
   - Inconsistent formatting of equations (e.g., "vi:i+n−1t" in Equation 1 should be "v_{i:i+n-1}^t")
   - Ambiguous descriptions of key parameters (e.g., "dc = 3 and n = 2" in Figure 1 without justification)
   - Inconsistent use of terms like "event mention" vs "event mentions" and "coreference" vs "coreferential"
   - The paper claims "50.97 % of coreferential event-mentions pairs share no arguments" but doesn't clarify whether this refers to all pairs or only coreferential pairs (which is logically confusing)

2. **Inadequate explanation of critical design choices**: The authors make numerous assumptions without justification:
   - They use TensorFlow v0.6 (2015) but don't explain why they're not using a more recent version
   - They state "the model can generalize much better using this additional training data" but provide no statistical evidence for this claim
   - They claim "the model achieves state-of-the-art performance" without providing statistical significance testing to support this claim
   - They don't explain why they chose to use "BestLink" clustering strategy in some experiments but not others

3. **Numerous minor but pervasive formatting and consistency issues**: These are the kind of details that would make a perfectionist reviewer reject the paper:
   - Inconsistent spacing around percentages (e.g., "50.97 %" vs "50.97%")
   - Inconsistent capitalization ("Event linking" vs "event linking")
   - Inconsistent dataset naming ("ACE 2005" vs "ACE 2005 corpus")
   - The ACE++ dataset is never properly defined (what does the "++" signify?)
   - The paper states "The corpus consists of merely 599 documents" - the use of "merely" is subjective and unprofessional
   - The paper refers to "MUC 6 dataset" (should be "MUC-6" with a hyphen)
   - The example in Section 2 has a period after "Lloyds.1" that should be a footnote marker

4. **Inconsistent and misleading claims about the state-of-the-art**: The paper claims to achieve "state-of-the-art performance" but:
   - Doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with all relevant prior work
   - Doesn't explain why they don't include Chen et al. (2009) in their comparison despite their results being on ACE
   - Makes a claim that "their approach is strongly tied to domains where these semantic features are available" but doesn't provide evidence for this claim
   - The comparison to Liu et al. (2014) is problematic because they used different preprocessing methods

5. **Insufficient explanation of the evaluation metrics**: The paper claims BLANC is the most valid metric but doesn't provide enough detail about why it's better than other metrics. The statement "BLANC has the highest validity, as it balances the impact of positive and negative event-mention links in a document" is too vague without explaining how it achieves this balance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "55.42% are more than five sentences apart" but doesn't clarify what "are" refers to (coreferential event mentions or all event mentions). Please clarify.

2. In Table 4, you show "Section 3" as a model variant but don't explain what "Section 3" refers to. Please clarify this notation.

3. You claim "the model can generalize much better using this additional training data" but provide no statistical evidence. Please provide statistical significance testing for this claim.

4. You state that "the model achieves state-of-the-art performance" but don't provide statistical significance testing. Please provide p-values or confidence intervals for your main claims.

5. The paper uses TensorFlow v0.6 (2015) but doesn't explain why you're not using a more recent version. Please explain this choice.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a reasonable approach with some empirical evidence supporting its claims, the numerous minor but pervasive technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and ambiguous descriptions significantly undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of a paper with such a high number of minor flaws that impact the reproducibility and clarity of the work. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmitting.

The paper's core contribution is potentially valuable, but the presentation is marred by numerous technical inconsistencies that make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. In a field where reproducibility is critical, these minor details matter greatly. A paper with such a high number of minor flaws should not be accepted, even if the core idea is promising.
