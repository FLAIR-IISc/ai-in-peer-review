

# Peer Review: "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event linking (event coreference resolution) that uses sentential features generated by convolutional neural networks, eliminating the need for external semantic features like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets without relying on domain-specific external resources. 

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. Demonstrating that external semantic features (WordNet, FrameNet) are not necessary for state-of-the-art event linking performance, with significant practical implications for deploying event linking systems across different domains.
2. Providing a thorough data analysis showing that 50.97% of coreferential event mentions share no arguments and 47.29% have different trigger words, justifying the need for sentential features.
3. Presenting a well-designed two-step model architecture that learns event representations using CNNs and then makes coreference decisions based on these representations.

## Strengths

1. **Significant practical contribution**: The paper makes a strong case that external semantic features (which are expensive to create and have limited domain coverage) are not necessary for state-of-the-art event linking performance. This is a major practical contribution with clear implications for real-world deployment of event linking systems across different domains. The authors successfully demonstrate that their model achieves better results than previous systems that rely on these resources.

2. **Thorough data analysis**: The paper provides compelling evidence supporting their approach through careful analysis of the ACE 2005 corpus. The finding that 50.97% of coreferential event mentions share no arguments and 47.29% have different trigger words is crucial because it directly motivates the need for sentential features rather than relying on intrinsic semantic features. This analysis is well-executed and serves as strong justification for their methodological approach.

3. **Clear ablation study**: The ablation study (Table 5) provides convincing evidence for the importance of each feature class in the model. The results show that removing any feature class leads to a performance drop, demonstrating that each component contributes meaningfully to the overall model. This is a strong empirical validation of their design choices.

4. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper compares against multiple previous approaches using multiple metrics (BLANC, MUC, B-CUBED, positive links), providing a thorough assessment of their model's performance. The results consistently show improvements over previous methods, strengthening the paper's claims. The inclusion of both ACE and ACE++ datasets further demonstrates the model's robustness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Unfair comparison to Liu et al. (2014)**: The paper claims to outperform Liu et al. (2014) in terms of F1 score on BLANC, MUC, and positive links, but Liu et al. used a different clustering strategy (BestLink) which the authors themselves acknowledge outperforms their transitive closure approach. Table 4 shows that their model with BestLink clustering performs worse than their transitive closure approach (71.09 vs. 76.90 F1 on ACE++). This makes the comparison to Liu et al. less convincing, as the authors' own results suggest that Liu et al.'s clustering strategy is superior.

2. **Insufficient implementation details**: The paper lacks sufficient detail about the model implementation, particularly regarding hyperparameters. While they mention using random sampling and line search to optimize hyperparameters, they don't provide the full set of hyperparameters used in the final model. This makes it difficult for other researchers to reproduce their results, which is a significant issue in the machine learning community.

3. **Limited error analysis**: The error analysis is limited to only 100 examples of false positives and false negatives (50 each). This is a small sample size to draw meaningful conclusions about the model's weaknesses. While the examples provided (like pronoun resolution cases) are interesting, they don't fully illustrate the most challenging cases, and the analysis doesn't provide specific insights on how to address these weaknesses.

4. **Inconsistent evaluation protocols**: The paper acknowledges that "the exact documents in the partitions vary from system to system" and that "published methods follow different strategies regarding preprocessing components." This inconsistency in evaluation protocols makes it difficult to fully assess the significance of the claimed improvements. For instance, the paper notes that Liu et al. (2014) use gold-annotated event mentions while Bejan and Harabagiu (2010) and Liu et al. (2014) use a semantic-role labeler instead of gold-argument information. This inconsistency undermines the validity of the comparison.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art performance on ACE, but Liu et al. (2014) used a different clustering strategy (BestLink) that outperforms the transitive closure approach used in this paper. Could you clarify how your model would perform with the BestLink clustering strategy, and whether this would close the performance gap with Liu et al.?

2. The paper mentions using a 9:1 train/test split for ACE, but the exact documents in the partitions vary from system to system. Could you provide the exact train/test split used in your experiments to facilitate reproducibility?

3. In Table 5, the model with only pairwise features and sentential features (row 4) shows higher precision but lower recall than the full model. Could you elaborate on why this occurs, and whether this suggests a fundamental limitation in the model's design?

4. The error analysis shows that 17 false negatives were cases where annotators made different annotation decisions. Could you provide more details about these cases, and whether the disagreement between annotators was systematic or random?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to the event linking literature. The two-step architecture is clearly explained, and the motivation for using sentential features is well-supported by the data analysis. However, the weaknesses noted above should be addressed before publication to strengthen the paper's claims and ensure reproducibility. The authors should also consider providing more details about their implementation, including the exact hyperparameters used and the train/test splits for the ACE dataset.
