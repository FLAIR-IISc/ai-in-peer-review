

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event linking (coreference resolution for event mentions) that uses "sentential features" derived from convolutional neural networks, without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors claim to achieve state-of-the-art performance without external semantic features. The paper presents a two-step model: (1) generating representations for individual event mentions using CNNs with sentential features, and (2) learning coreference decisions between pairs of event mentions using these representations combined with pairwise features. The authors demonstrate their approach on ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++ dataset.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural network model for event linking that uses sentential features from CNNs without external semantic resources
2. Creation and validation of the ACE++ dataset by augmenting ACE 2005 with additional web documents
3. A comprehensive analysis of feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, and sentential features) and their impact on model performance

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a valid and important point that external semantic resources like WordNet and FrameNet are expensive to create and have limited domain coverage, and it's valuable to demonstrate that a neural network approach can achieve comparable performance without them. This addresses a real limitation in current event linking systems.

2. The ablation study in Table 5 provides clear evidence that all three feature classes (pairwise features, trigger-local lexical features, and sentential features) positively contribute to model performance. This is a useful contribution to understanding what aspects of the model are most important.

3. The error analysis is thoughtful and provides valuable insights into the limitations of the model, particularly regarding pronoun resolution and boundary cases in event coreference. The examples of ambiguous annotations highlight an important challenge in the task that is often overlooked.

## Weaknesses
1. **Inadequate description of the ACE++ dataset**: The paper states "we create a second dataset that encompasses these documents and additionally contains 1351 more web documents annotated in an analogous fashion" but provides no details on how these web documents were annotated (who did the annotation, what guidelines were followed, how long it took, etc.). This is a major omission for a dataset that forms the basis of the main experimental results. Without this information, the validity of the ACE++ dataset is questionable, and the results cannot be properly evaluated or reproduced.

2. **Grammatical and technical inconsistencies**: The paper contains multiple grammatical errors and technical inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism and credibility. For example:
   - "55.42% are more than five sentences apart" (should be "55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart")
   - Inconsistent citation format (sometimes "Liu et al., 2014" and sometimes "Liu et al. (2014)")
   - The paper claims to "beat Liu et al. (2014)" on F1 score but Table 3 shows Liu et al. having higher B-CUBED scores than their model, creating confusion for readers
   - The paper mentions "pre-trained embeddings of (Mikolov et al., 2013)" without specifying which embeddings or their dimensionality, which is critical for reproducibility

3. **Inaccurate technical claims**: The paper states "The use of the square function as the network's non-linearity is backed by the intuition that for measuring similarity, an invariance under polarity changes is desirable." This is factually incorrect - the square function does not provide invariance under polarity changes; it merely makes the output positive. Invariance under polarity changes would be achieved by absolute value or similar. This technical inaccuracy raises questions about the authors' understanding of the model.

4. **Lack of comparison to recent work**: The paper compares primarily to Liu et al. (2014) but does not compare to more recent state-of-the-art approaches that might have emerged since 2014. This is particularly problematic given the rapid evolution of neural network approaches in NLP. The claim of "state-of-the-art performance" is therefore questionable without more recent comparisons.

5. **Confusing presentation of results**: The paper's Table 4 refers to "Section 3" as the model, but "Section 3" is the entire "Model design" section, which includes both parts of the model. This is confusing and misleading. Additionally, the paper states "We further split dev 9:1 into a training (train) and validation (valid) partition" which results in an unusual 81% train, 9% dev, 10% test split, which is not standard and makes it difficult to compare results to other work.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details on how the additional 1351 web documents for ACE++ were annotated? Specifically, who performed the annotation, what annotation guidelines were followed, and how long did the annotation process take?

2. The paper claims "The use of the square function as the network's non-linearity is backed by the intuition that for measuring similarity, an invariance under polarity changes is desirable." This is factually incorrect. Could you clarify what the correct intuition was for using the square function, and why it was chosen over other activation functions like ReLU or sigmoid?

3. The paper states "We beat Liu et al. (2014) in terms of F1 score on BLANC, MUC, and positive-links," but Table 3 shows Liu et al. (2014) having higher B-CUBED scores than the authors' model. Could you clarify this apparent contradiction in the paper's claims and results?

4. Could you specify which pre-trained embeddings from Mikolov et al. (2013) were used (e.g., word2vec, GloVe, dimensionality) to ensure reproducibility of your results?

5. The paper mentions "We implemented our model using the TensorFlow framework (Abadi et al., 2015, v0.6)" but TensorFlow v0.6 was released in 2015. Is this a typo, or is the paper using an outdated framework version? If it's a typo, what version of TensorFlow was actually used?

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a reasonable approach to event linking without external semantic features, the numerous methodological inconsistencies, grammatical errors, and critical omissions in the description of the ACE++ dataset undermine the credibility of the work. The technical inaccuracies (particularly regarding the square function) and the lack of comparison to more recent state-of-the-art approaches further diminish the contribution. The paper's flaws are too significant to overlook, even if the core idea has some merit. A more rigorous presentation with proper attention to detail would be required for this work to be considered for publication.
