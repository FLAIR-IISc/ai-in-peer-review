

# Review for "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper addresses event coreference resolution (event linking), which involves determining whether different mentions of events in a document refer to the same real-world event. The authors argue that current approaches often rely on external semantic resources like WordNet and FrameNet, which have limited domain coverage. They propose a novel approach that uses "sentential features" generated by convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to represent event mentions, without relying on external semantic features. The model first generates representations for individual event mentions using a CNN that processes the entire sentence containing the event trigger, then combines these representations with pairwise features to make coreference decisions.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Demonstrating state-of-the-art performance without external semantic resources**: The authors show that their CNN-based approach achieves competitive results on event coreference resolution without relying on expensive-to-create semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet.
2. **Systematic analysis of feature contributions**: The paper provides a thorough ablation study demonstrating the importance of different feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, and sentential features) for the task.
3. **Creation of a larger dataset (ACE++)**: The authors expand the ACE 2005 corpus by adding 1351 web documents, creating a more substantial dataset for event linking research.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling motivation for the problem**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence that event linking is a significant task, with the authors' analysis showing that approximately 23% of event mentions in ACE 2005 are incomplete on the argument level. This makes a convincing case for the importance of the task.

2. **Clear demonstration of the value of sentential features**: The paper effectively demonstrates that sentential features (from neural network processing of entire sentences) are valuable for event coreference resolution, showing that removing these features leads to significant performance drops (from 76.90% to 64.12% BLANC F1). This is a valuable contribution to the field.

3. **Strong empirical results with proper evaluation**: The authors compare their approach to relevant baselines and state-of-the-art systems, using appropriate metrics (including BLANC, which they correctly identify as most suitable for this imbalanced task). Their model achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE dataset and shows significant improvement with the larger ACE++ dataset.

4. **Insightful error analysis**: The error analysis identifies specific challenges (like pronoun resolution and boundary cases in annotation) that are difficult even for human annotators, providing a realistic perspective on the task's difficulty.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty compared to recent work**: While the paper claims to be the first to use sentential features for event coreference resolution, the approach is very similar to recent work on relation extraction using similar features. The paper cites Zeng et al. (2014), Nguyen and Grishman (2015), and others who used these features for event extraction, but doesn't clearly articulate how applying these features to the coreference task is novel. The main contribution appears to be the application of existing techniques to a new task, rather than a new technique.

2. **Insufficient comparison to recent neural coreference approaches**: The paper only compares to Liu et al. (2014) and Bejan and Harabagiu (2010), but fails to compare with more recent neural coreference resolution models. For example, the authors mention "more types of neural architectures" like recurrent networks, but don't compare to these approaches for event coreference. This weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.

3. **Superficial error analysis**: While the paper provides an error analysis, it's somewhat superficial. The authors note that pronoun resolution is a significant challenge but don't provide concrete examples or discuss how the model could be improved to handle these cases. The analysis doesn't go deep enough to suggest concrete improvements or explain why the model fails on these cases.

4. **Lack of computational complexity discussion**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational requirements of their model compared to previous approaches, which is important for practical applications. Given the use of CNNs and training on larger datasets, this would be a valuable addition to the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper cites several works that use sentential features for event extraction (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; etc.), but doesn't clearly articulate how applying these features to coreference resolution is novel. Could you elaborate on what makes this application to event coreference resolution distinct from previous work?

2. The paper states that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart, which suggests that locality-based heuristics wouldn't work well. However, the model uses a CNN that processes local windows of words. How does the model handle coreferential mentions that are far apart in the document, given the local nature of CNNs?

3. The paper mentions that they outperform Liu et al. (2014) on BLANC F1 but underperform on B-CUBED. Can you explain why the model performs better on some metrics but worse on others, and whether this suggests any limitations of the proposed approach?

4. The paper mentions that they "plan to test the model on more datasets and task variations" in the future. Have you considered applying your approach to the EventCorefBank dataset or the ProcessBank dataset mentioned in related work, which are specifically designed for event coreference resolution?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a well-executed study that demonstrates the effectiveness of sentential features for event coreference resolution without relying on external semantic resources. The methodology is sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the results are competitive with state-of-the-art approaches. However, the novelty claim is somewhat overstated, and the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons to recent neural approaches to coreference resolution. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
