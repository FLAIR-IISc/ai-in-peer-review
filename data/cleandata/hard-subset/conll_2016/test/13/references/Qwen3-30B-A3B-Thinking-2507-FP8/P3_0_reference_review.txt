

# Peer Review: "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event coreference resolution (event linking) that utilizes sentential features generated by convolutional neural networks (CNNs) without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors argue that previous approaches either used domain-limited external semantic features or showed lower performance. Their system has two components: (1) a CNN that generates representations for individual event mentions by processing their full sentence context, and (2) a classifier that makes coreference decisions based on pairwise features and the generated event representations.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. **Demonstration that state-of-the-art event linking performance can be achieved without external semantic resources** - This is a significant contribution as it removes a major barrier to applying event linking systems in new domains.
2. **Creation and validation of the ACE++ dataset** - The authors extend the standard ACE 2005 corpus with 1351 additional web documents, creating a larger dataset that improves model performance.
3. **Comprehensive analysis of data properties and model behavior** - The paper provides valuable insights into the characteristics of event coreference (e.g., 55.42% of coreferential mentions are more than five sentences apart) and a detailed error analysis that identifies pronoun resolution as a key challenge.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evidence for the claim that external semantic features are not necessary** - The paper provides thorough experimental evidence that their CNN-based approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources. Their ablation studies (Table 5) clearly demonstrate that the sentential features generated by the CNN are sufficient for good performance. This is a significant contribution to the field as it addresses a major practical limitation of previous approaches.

2. **Thorough and insightful data analysis** - The authors provide a detailed analysis of the ACE 2005 corpus (23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level) and the properties of coreferential event mentions (50.97% share no arguments, 55.42% are more than five sentences apart). This analysis is not only valuable for justifying their approach but also provides important insights for future research in event linking.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation and ablation studies** - The paper includes multiple metrics (BLANC, MUC, B-CUBED, positive links), compares against multiple strong baselines, and provides detailed ablation studies showing the contribution of each feature class. The comparison between transitive closure and BestLink clustering strategies (Table 4) is particularly insightful and demonstrates careful consideration of the problem.

4. **Insightful error analysis** - The manual analysis of 100 false positives and 100 false negatives is valuable. The identification of pronoun resolution as a key challenge (e.g., "It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me.") is particularly important as it points to a fundamental limitation of the current approach. The observation that many errors are due to ambiguous annotations (17 cases where false negatives matched analogous false positives) provides important context for evaluating performance.

5. **Practical contribution with ACE++ dataset** - The creation of the ACE++ dataset (1950 documents vs. ACE's 599) is a valuable resource for future research. The paper demonstrates that increasing the training data size significantly improves recall (from 75.16% to 83.21%), which is an important finding for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the approach** - While the application to event linking is novel, the overall architecture (two-step process with CNN for sentence representation followed by pairwise classification) is very similar to recent work on relation extraction (Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this is different from or better than existing approaches to relation extraction, which also use sentential features. The authors claim their model "does not rely on external semantic features," but many of the cited prior works (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015) also don't use external features, so this isn't a major novelty.

2. **Inadequate description of ACE++ dataset creation** - The paper states that ACE++ "additionally contains 1351 more web documents annotated in an analogous fashion," but provides no details about the annotation process. How were these documents selected? What annotation guidelines were followed? How was inter-annotator agreement measured? Without this information, it's difficult to assess the quality of the ACE++ dataset or whether it's truly comparable to ACE 2005. This is a significant concern for the validity of the experiments.

3. **Overstated claims about eliminating external resources** - The paper claims their model "does not rely on external semantic features," but they use pre-trained word embeddings (Word2Vec) which are external resources. While these are common in NLP, this is not entirely accurate. The authors should clarify this distinction, as it undermines their main claim about eliminating external resources.

4. **Lack of comparison to recent neural approaches** - The paper compares to Liu et al. (2014) as the state-of-the-art, but doesn't compare to more recent neural approaches to event extraction or coreference resolution. For example, there's no comparison to approaches using transformers or more advanced neural architectures for coreference resolution (like those used in recent coreference resolution systems). This limits the significance of their results.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations and generalizability** - While the error analysis identifies some limitations (pronoun resolution), the paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach. How well does the model generalize to domains outside of news articles? How would it handle cross-document event linking (which the authors mention as a future direction)? The paper doesn't explain why their current approach might not work well for these scenarios, which is important for understanding the scope of their contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their model "does not rely on external semantic features," but they use pre-trained word embeddings (Word2Vec). Could you clarify how you distinguish between "external semantic features" (which you claim to avoid) and pre-trained word embeddings (which you use)? What specific external resources would you consider "semantic features" versus just "word representations"?

2. How were the 1351 additional web documents in ACE++ annotated? What annotation guidelines were followed, and how was inter-annotator agreement measured? Were the annotators trained on the ACE 2005 guidelines, or were there any modifications to the annotation scheme for these new documents?

3. The paper mentions that their model outperforms Liu et al. (2014) on BLANC, MUC, and positive-links, but performs worse on B-CUBED. Could you explain why this might be the case? What does this difference in performance metrics tell us about the strengths and weaknesses of your model?

4. In the error analysis, you identify pronoun resolution as a key challenge. Have you considered incorporating discourse parsing or other techniques specifically designed for pronoun resolution into your model? How would you expect this to affect performance?

5. The paper states that "55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart." How does this compare to the typical sentence length in news articles? Does this suggest that the event linking task is inherently difficult due to the long-range dependencies in news text?
