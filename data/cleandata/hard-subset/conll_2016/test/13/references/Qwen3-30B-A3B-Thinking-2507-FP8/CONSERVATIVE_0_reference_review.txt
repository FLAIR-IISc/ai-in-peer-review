

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes a CNN-based approach for event coreference resolution (event linking) that uses sentential features rather than external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors argue that event linking is important because 23% of event mentions in ACE 2005 are incomplete on the argument level. Their model has two components: a CNN for generating event mention representations from the surrounding sentence, and a pairwise classifier that combines these representations with standard pairwise features for coreference decisions. They claim state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 and a new dataset (ACE++) they created.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A CNN-based approach for event linking that claims to avoid reliance on external semantic resources
2. A new dataset (ACE++) created by augmenting ACE 2005 with web documents
3. A thorough analysis of feature classes and their impact on model performance

## Strengths

1. **Thorough empirical evaluation**: The authors provide a comprehensive comparison with prior state-of-the-art systems on ACE 2005, including detailed ablation studies on feature classes. Their analysis of the ACE 2005 dataset showing 23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level provides useful context for the importance of event linking.

2. **Careful error analysis**: The manual investigation of 100 false positives and 100 false negatives provides valuable insights into the limitations of current approaches, particularly highlighting the difficulty of pronoun resolution and boundary cases in annotation.

3. **Clear demonstration of dataset impact**: The comparison between ACE and ACE++ (Table 4) convincingly shows that increasing training data improves recall, which is a valuable insight for future work in this area.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient evidence for novelty**: The paper claims to use "sentential features from event-/relation-mention representations" but cites several prior works (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; Chen et al., 2015; dos Santos et al., 2015; Zeng et al., 2015) that have already applied similar approaches to relation/event extraction. The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how their specific application to event coreference resolution is novel or significantly different from prior work. This is a critical weakness as the paper's main claim of novelty is not adequately supported.

2. **Inadequate comparison to fair baselines**: The paper claims to achieve "state-of-the-art" performance but makes an apples-to-oranges comparison with Liu et al. (2014), which uses a rich set of 105 semantic features. The authors' model does not use external semantic features, while Liu et al.'s approach relies on them. The paper doesn't show how their model would perform if it were to incorporate some of these external features (which would be a fairer comparison), nor does it compare to simpler baselines that might achieve similar performance without the complexity of a CNN.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper provides empirical evidence that their approach works, but fails to provide a clear theoretical explanation for why sentential features from CNNs are better suited for event coreference than previous approaches. The authors mention that 50.97% of coreferential pairs share no arguments, but don't explain how their approach specifically addresses this challenge better than prior work.

4. **Misleading claim about "no external semantic features"**: The paper claims to not rely on external semantic features, but their model uses pre-trained word embeddings (from Mikolov et al., 2013), which are a form of external semantic resource. This undermines their main selling point and creates confusion about what constitutes an "external semantic feature" in their framework.

5. **Questionable clustering strategy**: The paper states that their approach outperforms Liu et al.'s BestLink strategy, but their Table 4 shows that BestLink actually improves precision (at the cost of recall) when used with their model. The authors claim BestLink is "counter-productive" for their model, but the evidence suggests it could be useful in practice as a post-processing step to improve precision.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper cites several prior works that have explored sentential features for relation/event extraction. How does your specific application to event coreference resolution differ from these prior approaches, and what specific novelty does your method bring?

2. You claim to avoid "external semantic features" but use pre-trained word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013). How do you distinguish between "external semantic features" and "pre-trained word embeddings" in your framework? If word embeddings are allowed, why can't external semantic resources like WordNet also be used?

3. Your ablation study (Table 5, row 4) shows that using just pairwise features and sentential features actually increases precision but decreases recall. Can you provide a theoretical explanation for this counter-intuitive result?

4. You state that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart, suggesting locality-based approaches won't work. How does your CNN-based approach, which processes only the surrounding sentence of each event mention, handle cases where coreferential mentions are more than five sentences apart?

## Recommendation

I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper as it stands. While the empirical evaluation is thorough, the paper lacks sufficient evidence for its main claim of novelty and makes misleading claims about avoiding external semantic features. The approach appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing neural network approaches to a new task, with insufficient justification for why this adaptation would be superior to prior work. The paper would need to substantially revise its claims of novelty, provide a more careful comparison with prior work, and clarify the distinction between "external semantic features" and pre-trained embeddings before it could be considered for publication at CoNLL.

As a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods and rigorous validation, I find the paper's claims of novelty and state-of-the-art performance unsubstantiated by the evidence provided. The paper would benefit from a more critical examination of prior work and a clearer demonstration of what makes their approach truly novel.
