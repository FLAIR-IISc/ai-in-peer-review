

# Review for CONLL: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to event coreference resolution (event linking) that uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, eliminating the need for external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors argue that existing approaches either rely on these expensive-to-create external resources with limited domain coverage or show low performance. Their model first generates representations for individual event mentions using a CNN that processes the surrounding sentence, then uses these representations along with pairwise features to make coreference decisions. The approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without relying on external semantic features.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel application of neural-generated sentential features for event coreference resolution without external semantic resources**: This is the most significant contribution. The paper demonstrates that CNN-based sentential features can achieve state-of-the-art performance for event linking without requiring domain-specific external semantic resources, which have traditionally been a major component of event coreference systems.

2. **Empirical validation of sentential features' effectiveness for event linking**: The authors provide thorough empirical evidence that neural network-generated sentential features are highly effective for event coreference resolution, with detailed ablation studies showing how different feature classes contribute to performance.

3. **Insightful analysis of feature classes and clustering strategies**: The paper provides valuable insights into which features are most important for event linking and how different clustering strategies affect performance, going beyond simple performance metrics to provide meaningful guidance for future research.

## Strengths

1. **Bold conceptual shift**: The paper's most compelling strength is its bold approach of eliminating the need for external semantic resources in event coreference resolution. This is a significant conceptual shift from the established practice in the field (as evidenced by the fact that Liu et al. (2014) use "105 semantic features" while the authors achieve comparable performance without any external semantic features). This is exactly the kind of innovative thinking I value as a reviewer.

2. **Strong empirical results with significant implications**: The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without external semantic resources, which is a major achievement. Their model outperforms Liu et al. (2014) in terms of F1 score on BLANC, MUC, and positive-links metrics while eliminating the need for domain-specific semantic resources. This has important practical implications for making event processing systems more portable across domains.

3. **Meaningful analysis of feature importance**: The ablation study (Table 5) provides valuable insights into which aspects of the model contribute most to performance. The finding that removing sentential features has the largest negative impact (reducing F1 from 76.90 to 64.12) is particularly compelling evidence of the importance of this novel component.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core technique**: While the application to event coreference resolution is novel, the core technique of using CNNs for sentence representation is not particularly novel in the NLP community. Many papers have used CNNs for sentence representations (e.g., Kim, 2014; Zeng et al., 2014). The paper could better articulate why this specific application to event coreference resolution is novel and significant beyond just the technical implementation.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent neural coreference models**: The paper compares with older approaches but doesn't compare with recent neural coreference resolution models based on transformers or other state-of-the-art architectures. For example, the paper doesn't mention how their approach would compare with models like those based on BERT, which have achieved state-of-the-art results for many coreference resolution tasks. This omission weakens the claim that their approach achieves "state-of-the-art" performance.

3. **Insufficient explanation of why sentential features work**: While the paper shows that sentential features are effective, it doesn't deeply explain *why* they work well for event coreference resolution. A more detailed analysis of specific examples where sentential features help (and where they don't) would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their approach is novel for event coreference resolution, but previous work has used sentential features for event extraction. Could you clarify exactly what makes your application to event coreference resolution novel compared to previous work on event extraction?

2. The paper mentions that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart. How does your model handle coreferential mentions that are far apart in the document, given that it only processes the sentence containing the event mention? This is particularly important since the model doesn't have direct access to the entire document context.

3. Given that recent coreference resolution systems have achieved state-of-the-art results using transformer-based architectures (e.g., BERT), could you discuss how your approach would compare to such models, and whether you believe your CNN-based sentential features would be beneficial in a transformer-based framework?

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that event coreference resolution can be effectively performed without relying on external semantic resources, which have traditionally been a major component of such systems. The authors' bold approach to eliminate these resources while maintaining state-of-the-art performance is exactly the kind of innovative work I prioritize as a reviewer. While the paper has some weaknesses regarding novelty claims and comparison with recent neural approaches, the core contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance. The empirical validation of their approach is strong, and the analysis of feature classes provides valuable insights for future research. The paper's conceptual shift from resource-dependent to resource-independent event linking is a meaningful contribution to the field.
