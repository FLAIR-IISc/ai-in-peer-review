

# Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses event linking (event coreference resolution), a task that resolves coreferences between recognized event mentions within a document. The authors argue that a significant portion of event mentions (23% in ACE 2005) are incomplete on the argument level, requiring cross-sentential inference. Previous approaches relied heavily on external semantic resources (WordNet, FrameNet) which have limited domain coverage. The authors propose a novel CNN-based approach that generates sentential features for event mentions without requiring these external resources. Their two-step model first learns representations for individual event mentions using CNNs that process the surrounding sentence, then combines these representations with pairwise features for coreference decisions. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 and a larger dataset they created (ACE++).

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Demonstrating that event linking can be done effectively without external semantic resources**: This is the primary contribution, showing that CNN-generated sentential features can replace traditional external semantic resources while maintaining state-of-the-art performance.

2. **Systematic feature ablation study**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of how different feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, sentential) contribute to model performance, which is valuable for understanding the model's behavior.

3. **Creation and evaluation of ACE++ dataset**: The authors create a larger dataset by adding 1351 web documents to ACE 2005, enabling more robust evaluation and demonstrating the model's scalability.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and problem definition**: The paper compellingly demonstrates the importance of event linking by analyzing the ACE 2005 corpus and showing that 23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level. This makes a clear case for why the task matters.

2. **Novel contribution with practical impact**: The key contribution—demonstrating that event linking can be performed without external semantic resources—has significant practical value. This removes a major barrier for applying event linking across domains where resources like WordNet or FrameNet are unavailable or limited.

3. **Rigorous evaluation and error analysis**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including comparison to previous approaches, ablation studies of feature classes, and a detailed error analysis. The error analysis is particularly strong as it identifies specific challenges (like pronoun resolution) that even expert annotators find difficult, providing valuable direction for future work.

4. **Clear technical presentation**: The model architecture is well-explained with a helpful figure (Figure 1) and the implementation details are sufficiently described for reproducibility. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of the trade-offs between different clustering strategies.

5. **Strong empirical results**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on ACE without using external semantic features, and the performance improves significantly on the larger ACE++ dataset. The results are statistically significant and well-documented.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison with more recent neural approaches**: The paper compares primarily with Liu et al. (2014), but there have been significant advances in neural coreference resolution since then (e.g., Clark and Manning, 2016; Lee et al., 2017). The paper would be much stronger if it compared with these more recent approaches to establish a more accurate position in the current state-of-the-art.

2. **Lack of discussion about computational efficiency**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity or training time of their model compared to previous approaches. This is a significant omission for a practical system, especially since neural network approaches can be computationally intensive.

3. **Inadequate details about ACE++ dataset creation**: The paper mentions adding 1351 web documents to create ACE++, but doesn't provide sufficient details about the annotation process or how consistency with ACE 2005 guidelines was maintained. This makes it difficult to assess the validity of the ACE++ dataset for comparison purposes.

4. **Overstatement of "state-of-the-art" claim**: While the model performs well on ACE, the paper's claim of "state-of-the-art" is somewhat overstated given the caveats about differing experimental setups across systems. The comparison with Liu et al. (2014) has specific limitations (e.g., different preprocessing, different document splits) that aren't fully acknowledged.

5. **Limited discussion of model limitations**: The paper identifies specific error cases (pronoun resolution), but doesn't thoroughly discuss the model's broader limitations. For example, how does the model handle events with highly complex discourse structures or extremely long-distance dependencies beyond the 5+ sentence distance mentioned?

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results without external semantic features, but how does your model compare to more recent neural approaches to event linking (e.g., Clark and Manning, 2016; Lee et al., 2017) that also don't rely on external resources? Have you considered these approaches in your comparison?

2. Could you provide more details about how the ACE++ dataset was created, particularly how the additional 1351 web documents were annotated to ensure consistency with ACE 2005 guidelines? How did you handle cases where the web documents had different linguistic characteristics than the ACE 2005 news articles?

3. The error analysis reveals that pronoun resolution is a significant challenge. Have you considered incorporating discourse parsing or other discourse-level features to address this specific challenge, and if so, what were the results?

4. The paper mentions that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart. How does your model specifically handle such long-distance coreference, and are there specific cases where it struggles with this? Could you provide a few examples from your error analysis?

5. The paper states that using the transitive closure clustering strategy (instead of BestLink) works better for your model. Why do you think this is the case, and what does this say about the quality of the confidence scores generated by your model compared to Liu et al.'s approach?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that event linking can be effectively performed without external semantic resources, which addresses a significant limitation of previous approaches. The model design is sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the error analysis provides meaningful insights. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing with more recent neural approaches, providing more details about the ACE++ dataset creation, and addressing the computational efficiency of the model.

The primary weakness is the lack of comparison with more recent neural coreference resolution approaches, which makes it difficult to fully assess the paper's position in the current state-of-the-art. The other weaknesses, while important, are secondary to this critical gap.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with the understanding that the authors should address the questions above, particularly the comparison with more recent approaches, in the author response period).
