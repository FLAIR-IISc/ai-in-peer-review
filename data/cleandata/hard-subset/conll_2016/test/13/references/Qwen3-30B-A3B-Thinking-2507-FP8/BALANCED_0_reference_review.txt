

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to event linking (event coreference resolution) using convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features, eliminating the need for external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors argue that previous approaches rely on expensive, domain-limited semantic features, while their method leverages neural networks to capture sentential context for event mentions. The model operates in two steps: first, it generates representations for individual event mentions using a CNN that processes the surrounding sentence; second, it uses these representations along with pairwise features to make coreference decisions. The authors evaluate their approach on ACE 2005 and a new dataset (ACE++) they created, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance without external semantic resources.

My assessment of the main contributions (ordered by strength):
1. **Demonstration that event linking can be effectively performed without external semantic resources**: The paper successfully shows that neural models can generate sufficient sentential features for event linking, eliminating the need for expensive domain-specific resources. This is a significant practical contribution to the field.
2. **Thorough ablation studies showing the contribution of different feature classes**: The authors provide convincing evidence that both sentential features and pairwise features are necessary for optimal performance, with clear quantitative evidence of how removing each component affects results.
3. **Error analysis identifying specific challenges for future work**: The manual error analysis reveals meaningful insights, particularly about the limitations of the approach for pronoun resolution cases, which provides concrete directions for future research.

## Strengths

1. **Practical significance of eliminating external resources**: The paper effectively addresses a meaningful limitation of previous event linking approaches. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on domain-specific semantic resources, which is a major practical advantage for real-world deployment. This addresses a clear gap in the literature where previous approaches require expensive external resources that may not be available in many domains. Their results on ACE++ (76.90% BLANC F1) outperform previous work that relies on such resources, providing strong empirical evidence for their approach.

2. **Thorough ablation studies and feature analysis**: The paper provides exceptionally clear ablation studies that demonstrate the contribution of each component in the model. Table 5 is particularly strong, showing that removing any of the feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local, or sentential) degrades performance, and that the combination of all three is necessary for optimal results. This level of analysis is rare in the literature and significantly strengthens the paper's claims.

3. **Well-motivated error analysis**: The error analysis is one of the paper's strongest points. By manually examining 100 false positives and 100 false negatives, the authors identify specific challenges, particularly the difficulty with pronoun resolution ("It's crazy that we're bombing Iraq. It sickens me"), which is a well-known challenge in coreference resolution. This analysis goes beyond typical error analysis by identifying specific failure modes and providing concrete examples, making it valuable for future work.

4. **Clear comparison to previous work with appropriate metrics**: The authors carefully select BLANC as their primary metric, recognizing that it's more appropriate for their task than other metrics (like MUC or B-CUBED) given the high imbalance between positive and negative links in event linking. Their comparison to previous work is fair, acknowledging the limitations of the comparisons (different train/test splits, different preprocessing) while still making a compelling case for their approach's superiority.

5. **Practical dataset creation and validation**: The authors create a new dataset (ACE++) by adding 1351 web documents to the ACE 2005 corpus. This provides a more substantial dataset for evaluation (1950 documents vs. ACE's 599), which is important for neural network approaches that require larger training data. The paper's ablation studies on this larger dataset (Table 4) show clear benefits of increased training data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited evaluation of generalization to unseen domains**: While the paper demonstrates strong performance on ACE 2005 and ACE++, it doesn't provide any evidence that the model would generalize to domains where the training data (ACE++-style) isn't available. The authors claim their approach "does not depend on resources with restricted domain availability," but they don't test this claim. A crucial question remains: how would the model perform on a domain without the ACE++-style training data? The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a cross-domain evaluation.

2. **Insufficient detail about ACE++ dataset creation**: The paper states that ACE++ "encompasses these documents and additionally contains 1351 more web documents annotated in an analogous fashion," but provides no details about how the web documents were selected, how the annotation was performed (including quality control procedures), or whether the annotation guidelines were the same as for ACE 2005. This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess the quality and consistency of the ACE++ dataset, which is crucial for the evaluation.

3. **Inadequate addressing of the pronoun resolution limitation**: The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a significant challenge (with 17 false negatives involving pronoun resolution), yet the paper doesn't discuss how this limitation could be addressed. The authors mention "investigating if the model can benefit from more fine-grained information about the discourse structure" in the conclusion, but this is vague. A more concrete discussion of how to address this specific limitation would strengthen the paper's contribution.

4. **Lack of comparison to recent neural approaches**: The paper compares to previous work (Liu et al., 2014; Bejan and Harabagiu, 2010), but doesn't compare to more recent neural approaches to event linking. For example, the paper doesn't discuss how their approach compares to recent work that uses attention mechanisms or graph neural networks for coreference resolution. This is a significant omission given the rapid progress in neural approaches to coreference resolution.

5. **Overstated novelty of the approach**: The paper's architecture (two-step processing of event mentions followed by coreference decisions) builds on well-established patterns in coreference resolution literature. The primary novelty is the use of sentential features from CNNs, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from previous work that has used similar neural approaches for relation extraction (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015). The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of how their approach differs from these related works.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "our model achieves state-of-the-art performance" on ACE 2005. However, the comparison in Table 3 has caveats about different train/test splits and preprocessing. Could you provide more details about the exact documents used in the train/test splits for your model compared to previous work (Liu et al., 2014) and how this affects the comparison?

2. The ACE++ dataset is a key component of your evaluation, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about how the additional 1351 web documents were selected and annotated. Could you clarify the annotation guidelines used for the web documents and how the quality of these annotations was ensured?

3. The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a significant challenge. How do you plan to address this limitation in future work, and do you have any specific ideas for incorporating discourse structure information into the model to help with this issue?

4. The paper mentions that "the generation of sentential features from other types of neural networks seems promising" for future work. Could you elaborate on which specific neural architectures (beyond CNNs) you're considering, and why they might be particularly well-suited for this task?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that event linking can be effectively performed without external semantic resources, which addresses a significant practical limitation in the field. The thorough ablation studies and error analysis are particularly strong, providing clear evidence for the contributions.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses, particularly the lack of evaluation on unseen domains and insufficient detail about the ACE++ dataset creation. These limitations prevent the paper from being a strong candidate for acceptance at a top-tier conference. The authors could significantly strengthen their submission by addressing these weaknesses, particularly by providing evidence of their model's generalization capabilities and more details about their dataset.

With appropriate revisions addressing these concerns, this paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field.
