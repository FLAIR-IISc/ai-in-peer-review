

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of event linking (event coreference resolution), which aims to determine whether different mentions of events in a document refer to the same underlying real-world event. The authors argue that a significant portion of event mentions (approximately 23% in ACE 2005) require cross-sentential inference, making event linking an important task.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them (ordered by strength):

1. **Demonstration that high-quality event linking can be achieved without external semantic resources**: The paper provides compelling evidence that a neural model using only sentential features (without relying on external resources like WordNet or FrameNet) can achieve state-of-the-art performance. This addresses a significant limitation of previous approaches that required domain-specific semantic resources.

2. **Thorough empirical analysis of feature classes for event linking**: The paper provides a detailed ablation study showing how different feature classes (pairwise features, trigger-local lexical features, sentential features) contribute to performance, with clear evidence that sentential features are particularly valuable.

3. **Creation of a new dataset (ACE++) and analysis of dataset size impact**: The authors created ACE++, a larger dataset by adding 1351 web documents to ACE 2005, and demonstrated that model performance improves significantly with more training data.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach that eliminates reliance on external semantic resources**: This is the paper's strongest contribution. Previous state-of-the-art systems for event linking (like Liu et al., 2014) relied on a "rich set of 105 semantic features" derived from external resources like WordNet and FrameNet. The authors convincingly demonstrate that these resources are unnecessary for achieving high performance. This makes their approach more generally applicable across domains, as they no longer need to develop or obtain domain-specific semantic resources. The paper's claim that "low linking performance, coming from a lack of semantic knowledge about a domain, is evitable" is well-supported by their results.

2. **Strong empirical results and thorough ablation study**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without using external resources, outperforming Liu et al. (2014) on most metrics. The ablation study (Table 5) is particularly strong, systematically demonstrating how each feature class contributes to performance. The analysis shows that adding sentential features (which are generated by the CNN) provides the largest improvement over using only pairwise or trigger-local features, supporting the paper's core thesis.

3. **Insightful error analysis**: The error analysis (Section 5.3) provides valuable context for the results. The authors identify that a significant portion of false negatives involve pronoun resolution (a well-known challenging problem in coreference resolution), and they demonstrate that many errors are boundary cases where even human annotators disagree. This helps contextualize the results and sets realistic expectations for the task's difficulty.

4. **Practical contribution with ACE++ dataset**: The creation of ACE++, a larger dataset with 1,351 additional web documents, is a valuable resource for future research. The paper demonstrates that model performance improves significantly with more training data (Table 4), which is an important practical insight.

5. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from problem definition to model design to evaluation. The figures (particularly Figure 1) clearly illustrate the model architecture, and the experimental setup is detailed enough to be reproducible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to recent neural coreference resolution approaches**: The paper compares to systems from 2010-2014 but does not compare to more recent neural approaches to coreference resolution (e.g., those using BERT or other transformer-based architectures). Given that neural approaches have advanced significantly since 2014, this is a significant omission. For example, the recent work by Lee et al. (2018) or Clark and Manning (2016) on coreference resolution would be relevant comparisons. Without these comparisons, the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is questionable.

2. **Unclear comparison methodology**: The paper compares to previous work but doesn't clearly address differences in experimental setup. For instance, Liu et al. (2014) used a "best-link" clustering strategy while the authors use transitive closure, and the paper doesn't clarify whether this difference contributes to the performance gap. The authors also don't specify whether they used gold event mentions (as they claim) versus system-generated event mentions, which would significantly impact results. This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess whether the performance gains are due to the model or the experimental setup.

3. **Limited analysis of why sentential features work well**: While the paper demonstrates that sentential features improve performance, it doesn't deeply analyze *why* they work well. For example, it doesn't explore which specific aspects of the sentence context are most important for event linking, or how the CNN captures these aspects. This limits the paper's theoretical contribution and makes it harder to generalize the findings.

4. **Lack of discussion on computational efficiency**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the model compared to previous approaches. For a practical system, training and inference time are important considerations, especially for larger datasets. Without this information, it's unclear whether the performance gains justify the computational cost.

5. **Limited evidence of domain transferability**: While the paper claims their approach doesn't require external resources, it doesn't provide evidence that the model can be effectively transferred to new domains without retraining. The ACE++ dataset is still in the news domain, so it's unclear how well the model would perform on other domains (e.g., biomedical or legal documents) without additional training data.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims state-of-the-art performance but doesn't compare to recent neural coreference resolution approaches (e.g., those using BERT or other transformers). How would your model perform compared to these more recent approaches, and why didn't you include these comparisons?

2. The paper mentions that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart, yet the model uses a CNN with a small window size (n=2). Why didn't you explore using a model that can better capture long-range dependencies (e.g., RNNs, Transformers, or attention mechanisms)?

3. The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a major challenge. Have you considered incorporating discourse information or using a model that explicitly handles pronouns (e.g., by modeling the discourse structure)?

4. The paper shows that ACE++ leads to better performance (Table 4), but it's unclear how much of this improvement is due to the additional data versus the model architecture. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the data contribution versus architectural contribution?

5. The paper mentions that the model "does not rely on external semantic features," but doesn't provide evidence of how well it transfers to new domains without retraining. Could you discuss the potential for domain adaptation or transfer learning with your model?

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that high-quality event linking can be achieved without external semantic resources, which addresses a significant limitation of previous work. The empirical results are strong, and the ablation study is thorough.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses, particularly the lack of comparison to more recent neural approaches and unclear comparison methodology. These issues undermine the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance. The authors should address these concerns in the revision, particularly by comparing to recent neural coreference resolution approaches and clarifying their experimental setup.

If the authors can address these concerns in a revision, this paper would make a solid contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a strong paper that could be even better with some additional comparisons and clarifications.
