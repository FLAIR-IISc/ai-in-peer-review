- Summary: Proposes a two-step CNN-based event linking model using sentential features to represent mentions and pairwise features for coreference, claiming strong performance without external semantic resources.

- Contribution 1: Novel neural model that eliminates reliance on external resources (WordNet, FrameNet) for event linking.

- Contribution 2: Comprehensive ablation studies analyzing the importance of feature classes.

- Contribution 3: Claims state-of-the-art performance on event linking without external semantic features.

- Contribution 4: Detailed error analysis highlighting key challenges such as pronoun resolution.

- Strength 1: Demonstrates practical applicability across domains by removing dependency on costly semantic resources.

- Strength 2: Strong empirical evaluation on ACE and ACE++ with baselines and ablations, showing gains with more training data.

- Strength 3: Model design is data-driven and well-motivated by corpus analysis (many coreferent pairs lack shared arguments or are far apart).

- Weakness 1: Lacks comparison to recent end-to-end neural coreference methods (e.g., Lee et al. 2017, Clark & Manning 2016), weakening SOTA claim.

- Weakness 2: Error analysis flags pronoun resolution but lacks diagnostic insight or proposed concrete fixes.

- Weakness 3: Insufficient documentation of ACE++ construction and domain characteristics, raising questions about dataset-induced gains.

- Weakness 4: Reproducibility concerns due to missing hyperparameters, training details, and absence of linked code.

- Question 1: How does the model compare to recent end-to-end neural coreference architectures and could their innovations be incorporated?

- Question 2: How might incorporating discourse structure or other modifications concretely address pronoun resolution failures?

- Question 3: Please provide more details on ACE++ annotation, domain distribution, and how it differs from ACE 2005.

- Overall assessment: Valuable, well-motivated contribution demonstrating event linking without external semantics; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address comparisons, dataset details, and reproducibility.
