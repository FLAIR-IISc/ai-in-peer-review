

# Peer Review: "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event coreference resolution (event linking) that generates sentential features using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) instead of relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art performance without requiring domain-specific semantic resources, which would significantly improve cross-domain applicability. The model has two parts: (1) a CNN-based architecture that generates representations for individual event mentions by processing the entire sentence context, and (2) a pairwise classifier that combines these representations with standard pairwise features to determine coreference. The authors evaluate their model on ACE 2005 and a derived ACE++ dataset, reporting competitive results compared to previous work.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel application of CNN-generated sentential features for event coreference resolution, demonstrating that external semantic resources are not necessary for state-of-the-art performance
2. A thorough ablation study showing the contribution of each feature class to model performance
3. Empirical evidence that increasing training data size significantly improves recall for event linking

## Strengths

### 1. Addressing a significant gap in event extraction pipelines
The paper effectively demonstrates the importance of event linking as a task, providing compelling statistics (23% of event mentions are incomplete on the argument level) that justify the need for this work. Previous approaches have relied on external semantic resources with limited cross-domain availability, creating a significant barrier for practical application. The authors correctly identify this as a meaningful gap in the literature that their work addresses.

### 2. Strong empirical results without external resources
The paper's main contribution is well-supported by empirical evidence: the model achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset without using any external semantic resources. The ablation study (Table 5) clearly shows that each feature class contributes positively to performance, and the comparison to Liu et al. (2014) demonstrates that the model can match or exceed performance without the "rich set of 105 semantic features" that their approach relies on. This is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications.

### 3. Comprehensive analysis of feature importance
The ablation study (Table 5) is particularly strong, showing how each feature class contributes to the model's performance. The finding that removing sentential features causes the largest performance drop (from 76.90 to 64.12 F1) provides compelling evidence for the value of the proposed approach. The authors also correctly identify that the model's ability to handle cases with different trigger words (47.29% of positive pairs) is enabled by the sentential features.

### 4. Practical implications for cross-domain application
The paper correctly emphasizes that avoiding external semantic resources is not just an academic point but has significant practical implications. The authors demonstrate that the model can generalize better with more training data (ACE++), which is more feasible than creating new domain-specific semantic resources. This addresses a real pain point in the field.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Insufficient novelty compared to recent neural approaches
While the paper correctly cites recent work (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; etc.) as motivation, it fails to clearly distinguish its contribution from these prior works. The approach of using CNNs to generate sentential features for relation/event extraction has been explored in the literature, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain what's novel about its specific application to event linking. The authors don't discuss how their model differs from the recent neural approaches they cite, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of their contribution.

### 2. Limited evaluation of cross-domain capability
The paper claims that their model doesn't require domain-specific resources, yet the evaluation is limited to ACE 2005 and ACE++ (web documents annotated similarly to ACE). There's no evaluation on a completely different domain to demonstrate true cross-domain applicability. Without this, the claim that the model can be "more easily applied to new domains" remains unproven. The paper would be significantly stronger with a cross-domain evaluation showing performance on a domain different from ACE.

### 3. Inadequate comparison with recent state-of-the-art
The paper compares to Liu et al. (2014), but fails to compare with more recent neural approaches that might have emerged since their work. For example, the paper doesn't discuss how their model compares to neural approaches that use attention mechanisms or more sophisticated architectures for coreference resolution. The paper mentions that "recent literature proposes a new kind of feature class" but doesn't position their work relative to the most recent neural approaches.

### 4. Superficial error analysis
The error analysis is limited to 100 false positives and 100 false negatives, which is insufficient for a thorough understanding of the model's limitations. The paper identifies some patterns (like pronoun resolution issues), but doesn't provide enough detail to understand *why* these cases are problematic for their specific model architecture. For example, they mention pronoun resolution as a hard problem, but don't explain how the model's architecture specifically fails at this task, which would be valuable for future work.

### 5. Questionable interpretation of the clustering strategy results
The paper claims that BestLink (the clustering strategy recommended by Liu et al., 2014) performs worse than their transitive closure approach, but the reasoning is questionable. The paper states that "their model already weeds out many low confidence links in the classification step, which makes a downstream filtering unnecessary." However, the paper doesn't provide evidence that their model's confidence scores are more reliable than those of Liu et al.'s system. The comparison between clustering strategies is based on the authors' own model, not on a fair comparison between methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art performance without external resources, but the comparison to Liu et al. (2014) is complicated by differences in dataset splits and preprocessing. Could the authors provide more details about how they handled these differences to ensure a fair comparison?

2. The paper uses ACE++ as an extended dataset, but it's derived from ACE 2005 with additional web documents annotated similarly. How would the model perform on a completely different domain with no prior annotation (e.g., medical or legal documents) where no external resources are available?

3. The paper cites several recent neural approaches for relation extraction (Zeng et al., 2015; dos Santos et al., 2015), but doesn't compare to these specifically for event linking. How does the proposed model differ from these approaches, and why would they be less effective for event linking than for relation extraction?

## Additional Comments

The paper has a strong technical foundation and provides valuable empirical evidence about the importance of sentential features for event linking. The model architecture is well-motivated and the ablation study is thorough. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly the lack of clear novelty compared to recent neural approaches and the limited evaluation of cross-domain performance.

The authors make a compelling case for the importance of event linking as a task, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their approach specifically addresses the challenges of event coreference resolution (e.g., the high percentage of coreferential pairs with different trigger words and no shared arguments) compared to previous work.

The paper would also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between their approach and recent neural approaches for coreference resolution (e.g., those using attention mechanisms or more sophisticated architectures). This would help position their work more clearly in the current landscape of neural approaches to coreference resolution.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field, but it could be significantly improved with more rigorous comparison to recent work and more thorough evaluation of cross-domain applicability.
