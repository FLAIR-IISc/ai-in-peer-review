

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a two-step neural network model for event coreference resolution (event linking) that uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions. The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic features like WordNet or FrameNet, which previous approaches used. The model first generates representations for individual event mentions using CNNs that incorporate sentential context, then uses these representations along with pairwise features to make coreference decisions.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Empirical demonstration that sentential features from CNNs can be successfully applied to event linking** - The paper provides evidence that this approach achieves competitive performance on standard event linking benchmarks.
2. **Thorough feature ablation study** - The authors systematically analyze the contribution of different feature classes to model performance.
3. **Analysis of the impact of dataset size and clustering strategy** - The paper demonstrates how model performance improves with more training data and evaluates different clustering strategies.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evaluation**: The paper provides a comprehensive comparison against several state-of-the-art baselines on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), with thorough ablation studies that demonstrate the contribution of different feature classes. The results show clear improvements over previous work on key metrics like BLANC F1.

2. **Practical contribution**: The paper demonstrates that high-performance event linking can be achieved without relying on external semantic resources, which is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications where such resources may not be available or applicable across domains.

3. **Detailed error analysis**: The authors conducted a thorough manual analysis of 200 errors (100 false positives and 100 false negatives), which provides valuable insights into the model's limitations and the difficulty of the task itself.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach**: This is the most significant weakness. The paper's core technique—using CNNs to generate sentential features for event mentions—is not new. The authors cite several papers (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; Chen et al., 2015; dos Santos et al., 2015; Zeng et al., 2015) that have already applied similar approaches to relation/event extraction. The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from these prior works, making the "new" claim questionable. The authors are not proposing a fundamentally novel method but rather applying an existing technique to a new task.

2. **Insufficient comparison to relevant baselines**: The paper compares against Liu et al. (2014), Bejan and Harabagiu (2010), and Sangeetha and Arock (2012), but does not compare against more recent neural network-based approaches that might have already applied similar techniques to event linking. For example, the paper doesn't mention or compare to Li et al. (2015) or Xu et al. (2015), which use different neural architectures for relation classification and might provide more relevant baselines.

3. **Misleading claim about external resources**: The paper claims their approach "does not rely on external semantic features," but they use pre-trained Word2Vec embeddings, which is a form of external resource. This is a common practice in NLP, but the paper's claim is misleading as it implies they don't use any external resources at all, which is not the case. The authors should clarify this distinction.

4. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the reported performance differences. Without this, it's difficult to determine if the performance differences they report are meaningful or just due to random variation in the data splits.

5. **Inadequate validation against established baselines**: The authors acknowledge that "the exact documents in the partitions vary from system to system" and that "published methods follow different strategies regarding preprocessing components." However, they don't address these issues in their comparison, making the "state-of-the-art" claim questionable. A fair comparison would require using the same train/test split and preprocessing pipeline as previous works.

## Questions to Authors

1. You cite several papers that have used CNNs for relation/event extraction (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; etc.), but you don't compare your approach to these specific methods applied to event linking. How does your approach differ from these methods, and why do you believe your results are better than what would be achieved by applying these methods directly to event linking?

2. You claim your approach "does not rely on external semantic features," but you use pre-trained Word2Vec embeddings. How is this different from using external semantic features like WordNet or FrameNet, and why is this distinction important for the practical application of your model? If you consider Word2Vec embeddings as external resources, then your claim is inaccurate.

3. The paper states that you use a 9:1 train/test split for ACE, but different studies use different document splits. Can you provide the exact train/test split you used for comparison with Liu et al. (2014) and others, so that we can verify that your comparison is fair?

4. The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance, but doesn't provide statistical significance testing. Can you provide p-values or confidence intervals for the performance differences between your model and the baselines?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-executed empirical study that demonstrates the effectiveness of using CNN-generated sentential features for event linking. However, the core approach is not novel, and the paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from prior work that has already applied similar techniques to related tasks. The authors' claim that their approach "does not rely on external semantic features" is misleading, as they use pre-trained word embeddings. The paper lacks sufficient validation against relevant baselines and doesn't provide statistical significance testing for their results.

While the empirical results are promising, the lack of novelty and the misleading claims about external resources significantly weaken the contribution. For a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological rigor and established approaches, this paper falls short of the expected standards for a conference submission. The authors should either substantially revise their claims to reflect the true novelty (or lack thereof) of their approach or provide a more thorough comparison with relevant prior work that directly applies similar techniques to event linking.

I recommend rejection due to lack of novelty and misleading claims about the external resource dependency. The paper would be significantly stronger if it properly positioned itself against existing work and provided a more accurate assessment of its contributions.
