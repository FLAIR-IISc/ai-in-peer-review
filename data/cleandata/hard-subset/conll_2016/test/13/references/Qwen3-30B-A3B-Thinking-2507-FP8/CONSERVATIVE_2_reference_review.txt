

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary
The paper proposes a neural network-based approach for event linking (resolving coreferences between event mentions in documents) using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions. The authors claim their approach eliminates the need for external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet while achieving state-of-the-art performance. The model consists of two parts: (1) a CNN that generates representations for individual event mentions by processing the surrounding sentence, and (2) a classifier that makes coreference decisions based on these representations plus pairwise features.

## Main Contributions
1. The paper presents a neural network architecture for event linking that uses sentential features generated by CNNs rather than external semantic resources.
2. The authors provide empirical evidence that their approach achieves competitive performance without relying on domain-specific external resources.
3. The paper includes an error analysis identifying challenges with pronoun resolution and boundary cases in event linking.

## Strengths
1. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper conducts comprehensive experiments on two datasets (ACE and ACE++) and compares against multiple state-of-the-art approaches. The authors provide detailed analysis of how different feature classes contribute to model performance, which is valuable for understanding the model's behavior.

2. **Clear presentation of the problem and approach**: The paper provides a well-structured problem definition, motivation, and model description. The architecture is clearly illustrated in Figure 1, and the paper explains the rationale for their design choices, including the importance of sentential features based on their dataset analysis.

3. **Practical implications**: The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach can achieve good performance without relying on external semantic resources, which addresses a significant limitation of previous work. This is particularly valuable for real-world applications where external resources may not be available or domain-specific.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of fundamental novelty**: The paper claims to use "sentential features" from CNNs, but this approach has been extensively explored in previous work (Zeng et al., 2014; Nguyen and Grishman, 2015; Chen et al., 2015; dos Santos et al., 2015; Zeng et al., 2015). The authors do not clearly differentiate their approach from these prior works, and their contribution appears to be a straightforward application of existing methods to event linking rather than a novel methodological contribution. The claimed novelty is minimal given the prior literature.

2. **Insufficient validation against appropriate baselines**: The paper compares against Liu et al. (2014), which uses a "rich set of 105 semantic features," but does not compare against simpler, more established baselines that don't rely on external resources. The marginal performance improvement (BLANC F1: 73.31 vs. 70.43) is not statistically significant, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that their approach is robust or generalizable across domains. The authors claim to "remove the need for extensive external semantic features," but don't demonstrate that their model is more practical or effective than simpler approaches that might work without external resources.

3. **Incomplete error analysis and failure to address fundamental challenges**: The error analysis reveals that a significant portion of false negatives involve pronoun resolution, which is a well-known difficult problem in coreference resolution. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation but don't propose any specific improvements to address this fundamental challenge. The paper claims their approach "removes the need for extensive external semantic features," but doesn't provide evidence that their model handles the most challenging cases better than previous approaches.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you clarify how your approach differs from the prior work by Zeng et al. (2015), which also uses CNNs to generate sentential features for relation extraction? What specific modifications did you make to adapt this approach to event linking?

2. The paper claims that the model "achieves state-of-the-art performance," but the performance improvement over Liu et al. (2014) is marginal (BLANC F1: 73.31 vs. 70.43). Could you provide statistical significance testing for this improvement, and discuss whether this marginal gain is worth the added complexity of the neural network approach?

3. Given that 17% of false negatives involve pronoun resolution (which is a known difficult problem), how does your approach specifically address this challenge compared to previous work? The paper doesn't provide any specific mechanisms to handle pronouns, which is a significant limitation for event linking.

4. The paper claims that their model "does not depend on resources with restricted domain availability," but doesn't provide evidence that their model generalizes well to new domains. Could you discuss how your model would perform on a completely new domain without any additional training data or domain adaptation?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents a well-structured and thoroughly evaluated application of existing neural network techniques to event linking. However, the lack of fundamental novelty, insufficient validation against appropriate baselines, and incomplete addressing of fundamental challenges in event linking are significant weaknesses. While the paper makes a useful contribution to the field by demonstrating that neural networks can achieve good performance without external resources, the marginal performance gains and lack of clear methodological innovation make it difficult to justify as a significant contribution to the state-of-the-art. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to simpler baselines that don't rely on external resources, a clearer differentiation from prior work, and a more detailed discussion of how the approach addresses the fundamental challenges in event linking.

**Recommendation: Reject**
