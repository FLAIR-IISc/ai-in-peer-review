

# Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach for event coreference resolution (event linking) that utilizes sentential features generated from convolutional neural networks. The authors argue that previous approaches either rely on external semantic features (like WordNet or FrameNet) that are not broadly applicable across domains, or have low performance. Their model consists of two parts: (1) a CNN-based system for generating event-mention representations from full sentence context, and (2) a coreference decision module that combines these representations with pairwise features. The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Novel application of sentential features to event linking**: The primary contribution is applying a recent technique (using CNNs to generate sentential features for event extraction) to the specific task of event coreference resolution. This is a bold application of an existing technique to a new problem domain.

2. **Demonstration that external semantic features are not necessary for state-of-the-art performance**: The authors show that they can achieve competitive results without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, making their approach more broadly applicable across domains.

3. **Ablation study on feature contributions**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of how different feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local lexical, sentential) contribute to the model's performance, which is valuable for understanding the model's behavior.

## Strengths

1. **Significant novelty in application**: The core contribution - applying CNN-generated sentential features to event coreference resolution - is genuinely novel. While sentential features have been used for event extraction, this is the first application to event linking. This aligns perfectly with the conference's emphasis on novel approaches. The paper demonstrates that a technique from one NLP subtask (event extraction) can be successfully transferred to another (event coreference resolution), which is a bold and promising direction.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the ACE dataset, outperforming previous approaches on several key metrics (BLANC, MUC, and positive links). The ablation study (Table 5) clearly shows that each component of their model contributes positively to performance, providing strong evidence for their approach.

3. **Practical contribution to domain generalization**: The paper makes a compelling case that their approach doesn't require external semantic resources, which addresses a real limitation of previous work. This makes their approach more practical for deployment in new domains without requiring additional semantic resources, which is a significant practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient distinction from prior work**: The paper doesn't clearly differentiate its approach from Liu et al. (2014), which also uses neural networks for event coreference resolution. While Liu et al. uses "105 semantic features," the authors don't explain how their CNN-based approach fundamentally differs in its feature generation mechanism. This lack of clear distinction weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The authors only evaluate on ACE and ACE++, both news corpora. To truly demonstrate that external semantic features aren't necessary, they should test their approach on a domain where such resources are particularly scarce (e.g., biomedical or social media text), which would provide stronger evidence for their claim.

3. **Questionable "state-of-the-art" claim**: The authors claim state-of-the-art performance, but Liu et al. (2014) outperforms them on B-CUBED (70.43 vs. 73.31), which is a significant metric in coreference resolution. The paper doesn't adequately address this trade-off, making the "state-of-the-art" claim somewhat questionable.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach specifically differ from Liu et al. (2014), which also uses a neural network approach for event coreference resolution? The authors mention that Liu et al. "use a 'rich set of 105 semantic features'" but don't clearly explain how your approach is different.

2. The paper claims that external semantic features aren't necessary, but you only evaluate on ACE and ACE++. Can you demonstrate that your approach works well on a domain where external semantic resources are particularly scarce (e.g., biomedical text)?

3. The authors mention that "sentential features" have been used for event extraction, but don't clearly explain how these features differ from those used in previous event linking work. What specific aspect of the sentence context is captured by your CNN-based approach that previous approaches missed?

## Overall Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper presents a novel application of a recent technique (CNN-generated sentential features) to a new problem domain (event coreference resolution), which aligns perfectly with the conference's preference for bold, novel approaches. The authors provide strong empirical evidence that their approach achieves competitive results without relying on external semantic resources, which is a significant contribution to the field.

While there are some weaknesses in the evaluation scope and insufficient distinction from Liu et al. (2014), the novelty of the approach is substantial enough to outweigh these concerns. The paper introduces a new direction for event linking research that could have broader implications for other NLP tasks.

The authors have made a clear contribution by applying a technique from one NLP subtask to another, demonstrating that external semantic resources aren't necessary for state-of-the-art performance in event linking. This is a significant conceptual contribution that advances the field in a meaningful way.
