### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The core idea is to learn rich representations of individual event mentions by applying a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) over the entire sentence in which an event appears. This generates "sentential features" that capture wider context. These learned representations, combined with local trigger-word features and standard pairwise features, are then used to train a classifier that predicts whether two event mentions are coreferential.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Primary Contribution:** A compelling demonstration that sentential features learned via a CNN can effectively substitute for external, hand-crafted semantic resources (like WordNet or FrameNet) in event linking. The model achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without relying on these knowledge bases, which is a significant step towards building more portable and domain-independent event processing systems.
2.  **Secondary Contribution:** A thorough empirical evaluation and analysis. The authors test their model on two datasets (one of which is a newly created, larger corpus), conduct a detailed ablation study that clearly shows the impact of each feature component (pairwise, local, and sentential), and provide an insightful error analysis that highlights remaining challenges for the task.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Practical Significance:** The paper effectively addresses a well-known limitation of many previous information extraction systems: their heavy reliance on external knowledge sources that are costly to create and often have limited domain coverage. By showing that latent features learned from raw text can achieve comparable performance, the paper presents a more practical and generalizable approach to event linking.
2.  **Solid Empirical Results:** The model achieves performance on par with or exceeding previous SOTA systems on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark. As shown in Table 3, the proposed model obtains a higher BLANC F1 score than the strong system of Liu et al. (2014), which notably relies on 105 engineered semantic features. This result strongly supports the central claim that learned sentential features can replace manually engineered ones.
3.  **Excellent Ablation Study:** The feature ablation in Table 5 is a key strength. It methodically demonstrates the contribution of each component. The progression from using only pairwise features (row 1), to adding local features (row 2), and finally adding the sentential features (row 3) shows a clear and significant performance boost at each step. This provides convincing evidence that the CNN-based sentential features are indeed crucial for the model's success.
4.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The qualitative error analysis in Section 5.2 provides valuable insights. The authors identify hard cases like event pronominalization ("It sickens me") as a major source of false negatives, which points to clear directions for future research. Furthermore, their observation about annotation inconsistencies and plausible system errors helps to contextualize the performance metrics and underscores the inherent ambiguity of the task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The model architecture itself is a fairly direct application of existing techniques. The CNN structure for generating sentence representations (word+position embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling) is highly similar to models proposed for relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015), which the authors acknowledge. While the application to event linking is effective, the core technical contribution lies more in the successful adaptation and evaluation of this architecture rather than in methodological innovation.
2.  **Ambiguity in SOTA Comparison:** The authors are commendably transparent about the caveats in their comparison to prior work (Section 5.1), namely the variance in train/test splits and the use of gold vs. predicted upstream information (e.g., arguments). However, these caveats do weaken the claim of definitively outperforming the state of the art. The results should be interpreted as "competitively on-par with SOTA" rather than a clear advancement, especially given the differing experimental conditions.
3.  **Lack of Detail on ACE++ Dataset:** A significant portion of the analysis (impact of data size, ablation study, baselines) is performed on the newly introduced ACE++ dataset. While the use of a larger dataset is a strength, the paper provides very little information about it beyond basic statistics. Critical details are missing: What was the source of the 1351 additional documents? What annotation guidelines were used? What was the inter-annotator agreement? Is the dataset, or at least the list of document IDs and annotations, going to be released? Without this information, the results on ACE++ are not fully reproducible or verifiable.
4.  **Underdeveloped Clustering Analysis:** The paper experiments with two clustering strategies (transitive closure and BestLink) and finds that simple transitive closure works better, contradicting findings from Liu et al. (2014). The explanation provided—that the model already filters low-confidence links—is plausible but not deeply investigated. A more detailed analysis of the score distributions or a comparison with other common clustering algorithms could have strengthened this part of the evaluation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide more details on the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, can you elaborate on the annotation process, the source of the documents, and whether you plan to make the annotations and document list publicly available to ensure the reproducibility of your experiments?
2.  The CNN architecture is very similar to prior work in relation classification. Could you elaborate on any specific modifications or design choices that were critical for adapting this architecture from classifying entity pairs within a single sentence to linking event mentions that can be many sentences apart? Was the piecewise max-pooling (splitting the sentence around the trigger) particularly important?
3.  Regarding the clustering results, you found that BestLink hurt performance, unlike in Liu et al. (2014). Do you have any further analysis on why this might be? For example, is the output distribution of your classifier's scores fundamentally different (e.g., more polarized towards 0 and 1) than what might be expected from a random forest classifier, making an additional filtering step like BestLink redundant or harmful?
