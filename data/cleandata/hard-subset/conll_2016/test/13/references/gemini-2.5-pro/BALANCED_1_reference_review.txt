### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The model operates in two stages: first, it generates a vector representation for each event mention using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that captures features from the entire sentence, combined with local features around the event trigger. Second, it takes pairs of these event representations, augments them with standard pairwise features (e.g., type agreement, distance), and feeds them into a classifier to produce a coreference score. A key aspect of the work is achieving state-of-the-art results without relying on external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet. The model is evaluated on the standard ACE 2005 corpus and a new, larger corpus (ACE++) created by the authors.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A strong empirical demonstration that learned sentential features can effectively substitute for external knowledge resources in event linking.** The primary contribution is showing that a neural model can achieve state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark without hand-engineered semantic features, thereby offering a more portable and less resource-dependent solution.
2.  **The introduction of a new, larger dataset (ACE++) for the task.** By significantly increasing the amount of annotated data available, this resource has the potential to facilitate the development of more robust, data-driven models for event coreference and serve as a valuable benchmark for future research.
3.  **A comprehensive set of experiments and analyses.** The paper includes a thorough ablation study justifying the model's feature components, a comparison of clustering strategies, and a qualitative error analysis that provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task, such as pronominal event coreference.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Advance in Feature Engineering:** The paper’s central claim—that learned neural features can replace complex, resource-heavy semantic features—is well-supported and impactful. By achieving performance on par with or exceeding systems like Liu et al. (2014) which use a "rich set of 105 semantic features," the authors make a compelling case for the power of representation learning in this domain. This result is important as it lowers the barrier to applying event linking systems to new domains where resources like WordNet may be unavailable or incomplete.

2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength. The authors evaluate on the standard ACE 2005 dataset for comparability and on their new, larger ACE++ dataset to demonstrate scalability. The ablation study in Table 5 is particularly effective, clearly isolating the contribution of pairwise, local lexical, and sentential features and showing that all three are necessary for optimal performance. The comparison to multiple baselines and the analysis of different clustering strategies further strengthen the paper's conclusions.

3.  **Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, and the model architecture is explained clearly with a helpful diagram. The authors provide key hyperparameters in Table 2 and a link to their data splits for the ACE 2005 corpus, which are commendable practices that facilitate reproducibility and future comparisons.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core neural architecture, a CNN with piecewise max-pooling for sentence representation, is heavily borrowed from prior work on relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). While its application to event linking is novel and effective, the paper does not sufficiently motivate this specific choice over other possible neural architectures (e.g., RNNs/LSTMs, which were also prevalent for sentence modeling). The contribution is therefore primarily empirical rather than methodological.

2.  **Ambiguity in Comparison to Prior Work:** The authors correctly note that direct comparison with previous systems is difficult due to differences in data splits and preprocessing (e.g., using gold vs. predicted event arguments). This is a significant caveat. Using gold arguments, as this paper does, provides a much cleaner input signal than the system-predicted arguments used by some competitors. This difference makes it hard to definitively claim state-of-the-art performance, as the reported improvements may be partially attributable to this easier setting rather than the model's superiority alone.

3.  **Insufficient Justification for Key Design Choices:** The paper introduces a non-standard `square` activation function in its similarity layer (Eq. 4), justifying it with a brief intuition about "invariance under polarity changes." However, the empirical claim that this "outperformed model variants with different activation functions" is not supported by any results in the paper. An ablation comparing `square` to standard functions like ReLU or tanh would be necessary to validate this interesting but unusual design choice.

4.  **Lack of Detail on the ACE++ Dataset:** While the creation of ACE++ is a potential strength, the paper provides almost no details about its construction. Key information is missing, such as the source of the new documents, the annotation process and guidelines, and the inter-annotator agreement. Furthermore, it is not stated whether this valuable dataset will be publicly released. Without this information and access, the results on ACE++ are less verifiable, and its contribution to the community is limited.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide more details on the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what was the source of the 1351 additional documents, what were the annotation guidelines, and what was the inter-annotator agreement? Crucially, do you plan to make this dataset publicly available?
2.  Your model is evaluated using gold-standard event arguments, whereas some key baselines use system-predicted arguments. How would you expect your model to perform in a more realistic end-to-end pipeline where errors from an upstream argument extraction module are propagated? Does the reliance on sentential context make your model more or less robust to such errors compared to models relying on argument overlap?
3.  Regarding the `square` function in your similarity layer, could you provide quantitative results from the ablation you mentioned, comparing its performance to more standard activation functions (e.g., ReLU, tanh)? This would significantly strengthen the motivation for this novel component.
