### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a neural network model for event coreference resolution. The central idea is to use a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to generate a dense vector representation for each event mention based on its sentential context. This "sentential feature" vector is then combined with local lexical features and standard pairwise features to predict whether two event mentions are coreferent. The authors claim that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  The application of a standard CNN architecture to generate sentence-level features for the task of event linking, demonstrating that this can achieve performance comparable to systems using extensive hand-engineered features and external knowledge bases.
2.  An ablation study analyzing the relative importance of the learned sentential features compared to traditional local and pairwise features.
3.  An evaluation on a larger, proprietary dataset ("ACE++") to show the model's performance scales with more training data.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Goal:** The paper's primary motivation—to reduce the dependency on external, domain-specific semantic resources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet)—is a valid and important research direction. Systems that can learn effective representations directly from text are more portable and adaptable to new domains, which is a significant practical advantage.

2.  **Clear Model Description:** The paper provides a clear and well-structured description of the model architecture. The two-part design, separating event mention representation learning from the pairwise coreference decision, is logical and easy to follow. The inclusion of diagrams aids in understanding the data flow.

3.  **Thorough Ablation Study:** The ablation analysis presented in Table 5 is a definite strength. It systematically evaluates the contribution of each feature class (pairwise, local, and sentential), providing convincing evidence that the CNN-generated sentential features are indeed providing a significant performance boost over the more traditional feature sets. This analysis successfully demonstrates the utility of the proposed feature type within the authors' framework.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Marginal and Unconvincing State-of-the-Art Claim:** The central claim of achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance is not well-supported. The comparison to Liu et al. (2014) in Table 3 shows a very minor improvement in BLANC F1 score (73.31 vs. 70.43) while performing worse on the B-CUBED metric (88.26 vs. 89.38). The authors themselves acknowledge crucial caveats, namely different data splits and preprocessing pipelines (e.g., use of gold vs. predicted arguments in prior work). Given these inconsistencies, a direct and fair comparison is impossible, and the claim of outperforming previous work is tenuous at best. The presented results suggest the model is "on-par" with, not superior to, established methods. This marginal, inconsistent improvement does not provide a compelling reason to favor the complexity and opacity of a neural model over a well-understood feature-rich classifier.

2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The proposed model is a rather straightforward combination of existing techniques. The use of a CNN with piecewise max-pooling for sentence representation has been well-established in prior work on sentence classification and relation extraction (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015), which the authors cite. The contribution here appears to be the application of this known technique to a new task, rather than a novel methodological advance in either neural architectures or coreference resolution. The work feels more like a successful engineering application than fundamental research.

3.  **Non-Reproducible Key Experiments:** A substantial portion of the paper's analysis, including the impact of data size (Table 4), the feature ablations (Table 5), and baseline comparisons (Table 6), is conducted on the proprietary ACE++ dataset. Since this dataset is not publicly available, these results are entirely non-reproducible. While the insights may be interesting, they cannot be verified or built upon by the community, significantly diminishing their scientific value. Claims derived from this dataset should be treated with skepticism.

4.  **Superficial Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides little insight specific to the proposed model. Identifying pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies as sources of error is not new; these are well-known challenges for the task itself. The analysis fails to investigate *why* the CNN-based sentential features succeed or fail in specific cases. What kinds of semantic nuances are they capturing that lexical overlap misses? Where do they generate confusingly similar representations for distinct events? A deeper analysis connecting the model's behavior to its architecture would have been much more valuable than a general discussion of the task's difficulties.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your results indicate that simple transitive closure outperforms the BestLink strategy, which contradicts the findings of Liu et al. (2014). This is an interesting divergence. Does this imply that your model's output scores are better calibrated for a simple threshold, or is there another property of your learned representations that makes BestLink less effective?

2.  Given the inconsistent and marginal performance gains over Liu et al. (2014), could you elaborate on the practical argument for adopting your approach? For many common domains like newswire, resources like WordNet are readily available. In such a scenario, what is the compelling advantage of your resource-agnostic model if it does not offer a decisive performance improvement?

3.  The ablation in Table 5 (row 4), which removes local features, shows a large and counter-intuitive boost in precision. Your explanation is that "the sentential features degrade to trigger-local features." This is a strong claim. Could you provide more direct evidence for this phenomenon, perhaps through an analysis of the learned filter weights or attention mechanisms? Without further proof, this reads as post-hoc speculation.
