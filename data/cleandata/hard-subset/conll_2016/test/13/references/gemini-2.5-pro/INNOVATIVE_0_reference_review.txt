### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The core idea is to leverage sentence-level features learned by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to create rich representations of event mentions. These representations, combined with local trigger information and standard pairwise features, are then used in a second network to predict a coreference score between two event mentions.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **A significant conceptual shift for event linking:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that learned, sentence-level representations can effectively replace the complex, hand-engineered, and resource-dependent semantic features (e.g., from WordNet, FrameNet) that dominated previous state-of-the-art systems. This is a bold and important move towards more data-driven and domain-agnostic models for this task.
2.  **A strong, competitive model:** The paper proposes a solid and well-motivated neural architecture that achieves state-of-the-art results on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, validating the central hypothesis of the work.
3.  **Insightful analysis:** The paper provides a valuable ablation study that quantifies the impact of the proposed sentential features, as well as an error analysis that identifies key challenges for the field, such as pronoun resolution and dataset ambiguities.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Boldness of the Approach:** The paper's most significant strength is its departure from the prevailing paradigm of feature engineering for event linking. The decision to eschew external knowledge bases like WordNet in favor of features learned directly from sentential context is a bold one. This work successfully argues that the semantics needed for coreference can be captured implicitly in distributed representations, a non-obvious and impactful finding for this specific task. This represents exactly the kind of forward-looking research that moves the field forward.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The approach is not just conceptually appealing; it is backed by strong results. Achieving on-par or superior performance compared to systems like Liu et al. (2014), which use a "rich set of 105 semantic features", is a powerful validation of the proposed method. This demonstrates convincingly that the reliance on external resources is not a prerequisite for high performance, and that neural models can effectively learn the necessary semantic relationships.
3.  **Generality and Portability:** By eliminating the dependency on curated lexical-semantic resources, the proposed model is inherently more portable to new domains and languages. This is a major practical advantage over previous systems that would require significant adaptation or the existence of similar high-cost resources in a new target setting. The model's architecture is clean and relies only on word embeddings and annotated data, making it a much more generalizable solution.
4.  **Thorough Ablation and Error Analysis:** The authors do an excellent job of dissecting their model's performance. The ablation study in Table 5 clearly shows that the sentential features are the key driver of the performance boost over more basic models. Furthermore, the error analysis is insightful, correctly identifying hard discourse-level phenomena (e.g., event anaphora with pronouns like "it" or "this") as a key remaining challenge. This thoughtful analysis helps contextualize the results and provides clear directions for future research.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Architectural Novelty:** While the application of this methodology to event linking is novel and impactful, the core neural architecture is not. The CNN with piecewise max-pooling is a well-established technique for sentence-level representation learning in other NLP tasks, particularly relation extraction, as the authors acknowledge. Therefore, the main technical contribution is in the successful adaptation and application of existing methods, rather than the development of a fundamentally new neural architecture.
2.  **Ambiguity in State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The authors are transparent about the caveats in their comparison to prior work (e.g., different data splits, use of gold vs. predicted components). However, this does make it difficult to make a definitive claim of superiority. While the results are clearly very competitive, the lack of a perfectly controlled comparison slightly weakens the strength of the SOTA claim. For instance, re-implementing a key baseline within their exact experimental framework would have made the comparison more compelling. I consider this a minor flaw, as the overall performance is clearly in the SOTA range, but it's a point of methodological softness.
3.  **Limited Exploration of Alternative Representations:** The paper focuses exclusively on a CNN to generate sentential features. At the time of this research, recurrent architectures like LSTMs were also becoming prominent for capturing sequential information. A discussion or comparison with an RNN-based encoder would have been valuable, as RNNs might theoretically be better suited to capture longer-range dependencies within the sentence that could be relevant for event semantics.

### **Questions to Authors**

The use of the `square` function as the non-linearity in the similarity layer is an interesting choice. The paper justifies this with the intuition of achieving "invariance under polarity changes." Could you elaborate on this? Did you empirically compare this against more standard activation functions like `relu` or `tanh` for that specific layer, and if so, what was the quantitative difference in performance?
