### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network-based model for within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The model operates in two stages. First, it uses a convolutional neural network (CNN) with piecewise max-pooling to generate a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention, capturing both local features around the trigger word and broader sentential context. Second, it pairs these event mention representations, augments them with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type match, distance), and feeds them into a final classifier to produce a coreference score. The authors evaluate their system on the ACE 2005 dataset and an extended version they created (ACE++), demonstrating that their approach achieves performance competitive with the state of the art without relying on external knowledge resources like WordNet or FrameNet.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Demonstration of Effectiveness:** The primary contribution is empirically showing that learned sentential features from a CNN can effectively replace hand-engineered features derived from external lexical-semantic resources for the task of event linking. The model achieves competitive performance with a state-of-the-art system that uses 105 such features, highlighting a path toward more portable and less resource-dependent event processing systems.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Analysis:** The paper provides a strong and comprehensive evaluation of the proposed model. This includes an ablation study that clearly demonstrates the positive contribution of each feature class (pairwise, local, and sentential), an analysis of different clustering strategies, and a qualitative error analysis that identifies key remaining challenges for the task, such as pronoun resolution.
3.  **Creation of a Larger Dataset (ACE++):** The authors have created and used a significantly larger dataset by augmenting the standard ACE 2005 corpus. This new resource allows for more robust training and evaluation, and the results show a clear performance benefit from the additional data.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Clear Limitation of Prior Work:** The paper is well-motivated by the goal of reducing dependency on external, domain-specific knowledge bases (KBs) like WordNet and FrameNet. By successfully replacing features from these KBs with learned sentential representations, the work presents a significant step towards more adaptable and general-purpose event linking systems. This is a strong and well-supported argument for acceptance.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength. The authors compare their model to multiple strong baselines and prior state-of-the-art systems on a standard benchmark. The inclusion of an ablation study (Table 5) is particularly valuable, as it rigorously validates the architectural choices by showing that each component—pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential features—is necessary for optimal performance. The comparison of clustering strategies also adds to the paper's thoroughness.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper provides useful analysis beyond just reporting performance metrics. The initial data analysis in Section 3, which finds that over 50% of coreferent pairs share no arguments and over 47% have different triggers, effectively motivates the need for context-aware sentential features. Furthermore, the final error analysis provides a qualitative look at the model's failures, correctly identifying hard NLP problems like event pronoun resolution and highlighting inherent annotation ambiguities, which puts the performance numbers into a useful context.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The model architecture is well-explained and complemented by an excellent diagram (Figure 1) that cleanly separates the two main parts of the model (representation learning and coreference scoring). The authors are also transparent about experimental details, such as providing a link to their specific data splits for ACE, which aids reproducibility.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core neural architecture—a CNN with piecewise max-pooling over concatenated word and position embeddings—is not novel. As the authors themselves note, this approach is heavily inspired by and very similar to prior work in relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). The main novelty lies in the *application* of this architecture to event linking and the demonstration of its effectiveness in replacing external KBs, rather than in the development of a new neural method. This is acceptable for a venue like CONLL, but the contribution should be seen primarily as empirical and applicational.
2.  **Overstated "State-of-the-Art" Claim:** The claim of achieving "state-of-the-art performance" is slightly overstated. In Table 3, while the proposed model outperforms Liu et al. (2014) on the BLANC F1 score (73.31 vs. 70.43), it performs slightly worse on the B-CUBED F1 score (88.26 vs. 89.38). A more accurate description would be "competitive with the state of the art" or "achieves a new state of the art on the BLANC metric". While the authors commendably mention the caveats of comparison (different splits/preprocessing), the main claim in the text could be tempered for greater accuracy.
3.  **Lack of Detail on the ACE++ Dataset:** The introduction of the larger ACE++ dataset is a potential strength, but the paper provides almost no information about it. Key details are missing: What is the source of the 1351 additional web documents? What were the annotation guidelines and process? What was the inter-annotator agreement? Will this dataset be made publicly available? Without these details, the results on ACE++ are not fully reproducible, and its value as a contribution to the community is diminished.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The ACE++ dataset is a significant part of your experiments and a potentially valuable resource. Could you please provide more details on its creation, including the source of the documents, the annotation process and guidelines, and whether you have plans to release it to the research community?
2.  Your model's performance on ACE is competitive but not uniformly superior to Liu et al. (2014) across all metrics (e.g., B-CUBED). Could you elaborate on why you consider BLANC the most representative metric for this task, which would strengthen your claim of achieving state-of-the-art results?
3.  The use of the `square` activation function in the similarity layer is an interesting choice. The justification provided is brief ("invariance under polarity changes"). Did you experiment with other functions that provide similar invariance, such as the absolute value function, or other standard non-linearities? A brief comment on this comparison would be insightful.
