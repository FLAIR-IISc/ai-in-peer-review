### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel neural network-based approach for within-document event linking. The core of the model is a two-stage process: first, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is used to generate a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention by encoding its sentential context. This learned representation is then combined with local lexical features. Second, pairs of these event representations, augmented with a small set of standard pairwise features, are fed into another neural network layer to predict a coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 dataset and a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++), demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and compelling application of neural sentence modeling to event coreference.** The paper successfully reframes a problem traditionally dominated by complex feature engineering into a representation learning task. This is a bold and significant conceptual shift that demonstrates a new and promising direction for event processing.
2.  **Strong empirical evidence that learned features can replace external knowledge bases.** By achieving state-of-the-art results without any external lexical-semantic resources, the paper challenges the long-held assumption that such resources are indispensable for high-performance event linking. This greatly enhances the potential applicability of such systems to new, low-resource domains.
3.  **The introduction of a new, larger dataset for the task.** The creation and use of the ACE++ corpus is a valuable contribution that can help the community train more robust models and drive further research.

### Strengths

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty:** The paper's primary strength lies in its bold new direction. At a time when event coreference systems were defined by their ever-growing lists of hand-crafted semantic features, this work makes a compelling case for a representation learning paradigm. The idea of using a CNN to distill rich, task-relevant contextual information directly from text is powerful. It replaces brittle, resource-dependent features with flexible, learned ones. This is exactly the kind of innovative thinking that pushes the boundaries of the field.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Validation:** The model's performance is a strong validation of its central hypothesis. Achieving results on par with, and in some metrics exceeding, heavily engineered systems like Liu et al. (2014) is a remarkable feat. The key takeaway is not just a new state-of-the-art number, but the *how*: this performance was reached by *removing* a major dependency (external KBs), not adding a new one. This makes the result far more significant than a simple incremental improvement.
3.  **Thorough Ablation and Error Analysis:** The authors provide a convincing ablation study (Table 5) that clearly delineates the contribution of each feature type, confirming the critical importance of the learned sentential features. The analysis is insightful, exploring the impact of data size and clustering strategies, and the error analysis provides a clear-eyed view of remaining challenges (e.g., pronoun resolution) and data artifacts (annotation inconsistencies). This adds a layer of depth and credibility to the work.
4.  **Increased Domain Portability:** A direct and important consequence of the proposed approach is its enhanced portability. Systems dependent on WordNet, FrameNet, or DBpedia are difficult to adapt to specialized domains (e.g., biomedical, financial) where such resources are incomplete or non-existent. By learning features from the text itself, this model presents a much more general and adaptable solution for event linking.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the *application* of a CNN to this problem is novel, the CNN architecture itself is a straightforward implementation of models from prior work in related tasks like relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014). The work would have been even stronger if it had explored or at least discussed why this specific architecture is well-suited for event linking compared to alternatives like RNNs/LSTMs, which might be better at capturing longer-range dependencies within the sentence.
2.  **Somewhat Ad-Hoc Design Choices:** Certain design choices lack deep justification. For example, the use of the `square` activation function in the similarity layer is motivated by a brief and rather vague intuition about "invariance under polarity changes." A more rigorous explanation or an empirical comparison against other standard similarity functions (e.g., a bilinear model, simple concatenation followed by ReLU) would have made the architectural design feel less arbitrary.
3.  **Simplistic Clustering:** The final clustering is performed via a simple transitive closure over pairs exceeding a threshold. While the authors show this outperforms BestLink for their model, it is still a very basic method. Given that the model produces graded scores, a more sophisticated clustering algorithm could potentially leverage this information more effectively. This aspect of the system feels underdeveloped compared to the novel representation learning component.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The error analysis highlights the difficulty of resolving pronominal references to events (e.g., "It sickens me."). This seems to be a case where single-sentence context is insufficient. Have you considered extending your model to incorporate broader document-level context, perhaps through a hierarchical architecture, to address these particularly hard cases?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the creation of the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what was the source of the new documents, what were the annotation guidelines, and what was the inter-annotator agreement? This information is important for the community to understand the nature and quality of this new resource.
3.  Regarding the similarity layer, could you elaborate on the choice of the `square` activation function? Did you experiment with other non-linearities or more standard similarity computations, and if so, how did they perform in comparison?
