### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The authors propose a two-step neural model that first learns a dense representation for individual event mentions and then uses these representations for a pairwise coreference decision. The core of the approach is a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that generates "sentential features" by processing the entire sentence containing an event mention. This is combined with local features (trigger word and its context) and standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance). The authors' main claim is that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without relying on external, domain-specific semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, which were essential for previous top-performing systems.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **Effective Application of Learned Sentential Features to Event Coreference:** The primary contribution is demonstrating that CNN-based sentential features, which had shown promise in related tasks like relation extraction, are highly effective for event coreference. The work successfully shows that these automatically learned features can serve as a powerful substitute for extensive, hand-crafted semantic features derived from external knowledge bases, thereby creating a more portable and less resource-dependent system.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation and Analysis:** The paper presents a strong empirical case for its model. It not only achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark but also introduces a larger dataset (ACE++) to show the model's ability to benefit from more training data. Crucially, the ablation study (Table 5) provides clear evidence of the significant positive impact of the sentential features, justifying the central architectural choice.
3.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The qualitative analysis of errors provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task. It highlights difficult linguistic phenomena like pronominal event anaphora and points to inconsistencies in the gold-standard annotation, which helps contextualize the performance of all systems and suggests directions for future work.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong and Well-Justified Results:** The model achieves performance on par with or exceeding the previous state-of-the-art on ACE 2005 (Table 3). The key strength here is not just the score itself, but the *how*: it achieves this without the "rich set of 105 semantic features" used by the main competitor (Liu et al., 2014). This is a compelling argument for the power of learned representations and significantly improves the practicality and domain-portability of event linking systems.
2.  **Excellent Motivation and Model Design:** The paper does a great job motivating its design choices. The introduction clearly frames the problem of dependency on external resources. The preliminary data analysis (Section 3), which finds that many coreferent pairs share no arguments or triggers, provides a strong rationale for incorporating wider sentential context, which the CNN is designed to capture. The model architecture itself is logical and clearly explained with a helpful diagram (Figure 1).
3.  **Rigorous Ablation Study:** The ablation experiments presented in Table 5 are a major strength. They systematically dissect the model to quantify the contribution of each feature class (pairwise, local-lexical, and sentential). The results convincingly demonstrate that while all components contribute, the addition of sentential features provides the largest performance gains, robustly supporting the paper's central thesis.
4.  **Creation and Use of a Larger Dataset:** By creating and experimenting on the larger ACE++ dataset, the authors demonstrate that their model's performance scales with data availability. The significant jump in recall when moving from ACE to ACE++ (Table 4) suggests the model is not overfitting and is capable of learning more general patterns from more examples, which is a desirable property for neural models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core CNN architecture (word/position embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling) is heavily borrowed from prior work in relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015), which the authors acknowledge. While the *application* to event linking is novel and effective, the paper does not introduce a new neural architecture or technique. The primary contribution is therefore one of successful integration and application rather than fundamental methodological innovation.
2.  **Ambiguity Regarding the ACE++ Dataset:** The paper introduces and uses the ACE++ dataset to great effect, but its status and creation process are unclear. The paper states that it contains 1351 additional "web documents annotated in an analogous fashion," but provides no details on the annotation process, guidelines, or inter-annotator agreement. Furthermore, it is not clear if the dataset itself will be released for public use (only the document splits are mentioned). Without this information, the results on ACE++ are not fully reproducible, and the potential contribution of this new resource to the community is diminished.
3.  **Simple Clustering Method:** The paper settles on a simple transitive closure of positive pairwise links for clustering. While the authors show this works better than the "BestLink" strategy in their experiments, this is a relatively basic approach. The paper could have been strengthened by exploring or at least discussing more sophisticated clustering algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering) that can leverage the continuous scores from the classifier more effectively than a hard threshold (0.5).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the status of the ACE++ dataset? Do you plan to release the full annotations? Could you provide more details about the annotation process, such as the source of the web documents, the annotation guidelines, and any inter-annotator agreement metrics you calculated?
2.  Your choice of a CNN architecture is well-motivated by prior work. Did you experiment with or consider other neural architectures for generating the sentential representations, such as recurrent neural networks (LSTMs/GRUs), which might be better suited for capturing sequential information and longer-range dependencies within a sentence?
3.  The use of a `square` activation function in the similarity layer (Equation 4) is an interesting choice. Could you provide a bit more intuition or perhaps a brief quantitative comparison against more standard activations like `relu` or `tanh` in that specific layer? This could provide insight into the nature of the similarity space the model learns.
