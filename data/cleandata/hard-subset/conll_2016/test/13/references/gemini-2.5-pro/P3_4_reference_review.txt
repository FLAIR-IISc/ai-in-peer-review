### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of within-document event coreference resolution, also known as event linking. The authors propose a two-stage neural network model that first learns a dense representation for individual event mentions and then uses these representations, along with pairwise features, to classify whether two mentions are coreferent. The core novelty lies in using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to generate "sentential features" from the full sentence containing an event mention, thereby capturing wider context. The authors claim this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without relying on external, domain-specific semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, which have limited the portability of previous systems.

As I see them, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **An effective application of neural sentence representation to event linking:** While the CNN architecture itself is adapted from prior work in relation extraction, this paper is one of the first to successfully demonstrate that such sentential features can replace complex, hand-engineered features from external knowledge bases for the distinct task of event coreference, achieving comparable state-of-the-art results.
2.  **A new, larger dataset (ACE++) for the task:** The authors augment the standard, relatively small ACE 2005 corpus with over 1300 additional documents. This is a valuable contribution to the community, enabling more robust model training and evaluation. The paper effectively uses this dataset to show how their model's performance, particularly recall, improves with more data.
3.  **Thorough experimental analysis and ablation studies:** The evaluation is comprehensive, not only comparing against prior work but also providing detailed ablation studies that justify the inclusion of each feature class (pairwise, local, sentential). The insightful error analysis identifies key remaining challenges like event-referring pronoun resolution and highlights the inherent ambiguity of the task.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High-impact methodological shift:** The most significant strength is the successful demonstration that latent sentential features can substitute for curated external knowledge resources. Previous state-of-the-art systems (e.g., Liu et al., 2014) relied on a large suite of features derived from resources like WordNet, VerbOcean, and DBpedia. Such reliance creates a major bottleneck for adapting systems to new domains where these resources may not exist or be relevant. By achieving on-par performance using only pre-trained word embeddings and features derivable from the text itself, this work presents a much more portable and generalizable approach to event linking.

2.  **Strong empirical validation:** The paper's claims are backed by a robust and well-designed set of experiments. The authors use multiple, appropriate coreference metrics (rightly highlighting BLANC), compare against several strong baselines and prior systems, and conduct an excellent ablation study (Table 5) that clearly teases apart the contribution of each component of their model. The finding that all three feature classes (pairwise, local, sentential) are complementary and necessary for peak performance is a convincing result.

3.  **Insightful problem and error analysis:** The paper is well-motivated by a preliminary data analysis (Section 3) that highlights why simple features are insufficient (e.g., many coreferent pairs have different triggers and no shared arguments). This justifies their choice to incorporate broader sentence context. Furthermore, the manual error analysis in Section 5.2 is excellent. It moves beyond simple categorization to identify challenging linguistic phenomena (pronoun resolution for events) and, crucially, annotation ambiguity. This provides a nuanced perspective on the results and points toward important avenues for future research, while also contextualizing the performance ceiling for this task.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is clearly explained and visualized in Figure 1. The authors provide sufficient detail about their hyperparameter tuning, training setup, and even provide a link to their data splits, which strengthens the reproducibility of their work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Architectural novelty is limited:** While the *application* is novel, the core CNN architecture for generating sentential features (word+position embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling) is heavily borrowed from prior work on relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014, 2015; Chen et al., 2015). The authors acknowledge this inspiration, but it means the work's primary contribution is in engineering and evaluation rather than fundamental model innovation. This is not a fatal flaw, but it does temper the novelty of the proposed method itself.

2.  **The "on-par" SOTA claim is slightly weak:** The comparison to Liu et al. (2014) on ACE shows the proposed model winning on some metrics (BLANC, MUC) but losing on another (B-CUBED). While the overall performance is clearly competitive, it doesn't represent a decisive new state-of-the-art. The authors are commendably transparent about the caveats of such comparisons (different data splits, use of gold arguments). However, the use of gold-standard event mentions and arguments throughout the experiments means the reported performance is an upper bound and may not reflect performance in a realistic end-to-end system where errors from upstream components would propagate.

3.  **Lack of comparison to alternative neural architectures:** The paper's data analysis shows that over half of coreferent event mentions are more than five sentences apart, suggesting long-range dependencies are important. Given this, the choice of a CNN, which operates on local windows, over a recurrent architecture (e.g., LSTM/GRU) is not explicitly justified. RNNs are often considered superior for capturing sentence-level semantics and longer-range context. A comparison against even a simple LSTM-based sentence encoder would have strengthened the paper by either confirming the sufficiency of the CNN or providing a more complete picture of the neural modeling landscape for this task.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The use of the `square` activation function in the similarity layer is an interesting and non-standard choice. You state it "outperformed model variants," which is a key empirical claim. Could you provide quantitative results from your hyperparameter search comparing `square` to more common alternatives like ReLU, tanh, or even a simple similarity metric like a trained bilinear model (dot product) or cosine similarity on the final event representations?

2.  Your data analysis indicates that long-distance links between event mentions are common. This seems to motivate architectures capable of handling long-range dependencies. Could you elaborate on your decision to use a CNN-based encoder instead of an RNN-based one (e.g., LSTM)? Did you experiment with any recurrent models, and if so, how did they compare? A brief discussion of this architectural choice would be valuable.
