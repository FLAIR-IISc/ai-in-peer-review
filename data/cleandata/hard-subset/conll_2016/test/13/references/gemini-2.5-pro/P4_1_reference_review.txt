### Summary

This paper proposes a neural network-based model for within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The core idea is to replace the reliance on handcrafted, external semantic features (e.g., from WordNet or FrameNet) with learned "sentential features" derived from a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The model operates in two stages. First, it generates a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention by applying a CNN over the mention's sentence, using piecewise max-pooling around the event trigger. This sentential representation is concatenated with local lexical features (embeddings of the trigger and its context). Second, a pairwise classifier takes the representations of two event mentions, combines them with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance), and computes a coreference score. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++), achieving performance comparable to or exceeding state-of-the-art systems that depend on extensive external knowledge sources.

### Contributions

1.  **Novel Application of Learned Sentential Features for Event Linking:** The primary contribution is the successful application of CNN-based sentence representations to the task of event coreference resolution. The work demonstrates that features learned directly from the sentential context of an event mention can effectively substitute for traditional, handcrafted semantic features derived from external knowledge bases. This significantly improves the model's portability across different domains.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance without External Knowledge:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that a neural approach can achieve state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark (ACE 2005) without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet, FrameNet, or DBpedia. This is a significant finding, as it lowers the barrier to building high-performance event linking systems.
3.  **Extensive Empirical Analysis and a New Dataset:** The work is supported by a thorough evaluation, including ablation studies that validate the contribution of each feature component (local, sentential, and pairwise). The authors also introduce and evaluate their model on ACE++, a new, larger dataset, which demonstrates the model's ability to benefit from more training data and provides a more challenging testbed for future work.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Technical Soundness:** The paper is well-motivated by the need to move away from feature engineering and reliance on external, domain-specific resources. The proposed solution—using a CNN to learn sentential features—is a technically sound and well-justified approach to this problem. The model architecture, while building on existing components, is thoughtfully designed for the task by incorporating trigger-centric piecewise max-pooling and combining multiple feature types (lexical, sentential, pairwise).
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental validation is a key strength. The authors compare their model against multiple strong baselines on the standard ACE 2005 dataset, showing on-par or superior performance. The inclusion of the larger ACE++ dataset demonstrates the model's scalability and generalization capabilities. Crucially, the ablation study presented in Table 5 provides clear and convincing evidence that the novel sentential features provide a substantial performance boost over models using only local and pairwise features, directly supporting the central claim of the paper.
3.  **Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The model architecture is explained in detail and effectively visualized in Figure 1. The authors provide a comprehensive list of hyperparameters (Table 2) and specify their data splits for the standard ACE dataset, which are important steps towards ensuring reproducibility—a practice that is often overlooked.
4.  **Insightful Data and Error Analysis:** The paper includes valuable analysis that strengthens its conclusions. The initial data analysis in Section 3 provides a strong motivation for using sentential features by highlighting that a large percentage of coreferential event pairs do not share arguments or trigger words. Furthermore, the manual error analysis in Section 5.2 offers important insights into the remaining challenges of the task, such as pronoun resolution and annotation ambiguity, providing a nuanced perspective on the system's performance and identifying clear directions for future research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Gold-Standard Information:** The experiments are conducted using gold-annotated event triggers and arguments. While this is a common practice to isolate the performance of the coreference module, it does not reflect a realistic, end-to-end system where these inputs would be predicted and thus noisy. The paper does not assess the model's robustness to errors from upstream components, which limits the conclusions about its practical applicability. The comparison with prior work is also complicated by this, as some competing systems used predicted arguments.
2.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core technical component, a CNN with piecewise max-pooling to generate a sentence representation, is heavily inspired by and directly adapted from prior work in relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). The authors acknowledge this lineage. While the *application* to event linking is novel and effective, the contribution in terms of a new neural network architecture is limited. The paper's strength lies more in the successful adaptation and empirical demonstration for a new task rather than in fundamental methodological innovation.
3.  **Status of the ACE++ Dataset:** A significant part of the evaluation and a claimed contribution rests on the new ACE++ dataset. However, the paper does not specify whether this dataset will be made publicly available. If the dataset remains proprietary, its value to the research community is severely limited, as the results presented on it are not verifiable or reproducible by other researchers, and it cannot serve as a benchmark for future work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your experiments rely on gold event mentions and arguments. How do you expect the model's performance to degrade in a realistic end-to-end setting with predicted inputs? Have you performed any preliminary experiments to assess the model's sensitivity to upstream errors in trigger or argument identification?
2.  The pairwise classification approach has a quadratic complexity with respect to the number of event mentions in a document. Could you comment on the model's inference time and scalability for very long documents? Did you consider any antecedent pruning heuristics to improve efficiency?
3.  The ACE++ dataset appears to be a valuable resource for future research in this area. Do you have plans to release this dataset to the public?
