### Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

**Summary of the submission:**
This paper proposes a two-stage model for within-document event coreference resolution. The first stage uses a convolutional neural network (CNN) with piecewise max-pooling to generate a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention, capturing features from the entire sentence. The second stage takes pairs of these event mention representations, combines them with a set of standard pairwise features (e.g., type agreement, distance), and feeds them into a final classifier to produce a coreference score. The primary claim is that this approach, which relies on learned "sentential features" instead of external knowledge bases like WordNet, achieves performance comparable to the state-of-the-art on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Contributions:**
1.  An empirical study demonstrating that a standard CNN architecture, used to generate sentential features, can replace the need for extensive, hand-crafted semantic features from external resources (like WordNet or FrameNet) in the task of event linking, while achieving competitive performance.
2.  A thorough ablation study that isolates the contribution of different feature types (pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential), providing evidence for the effectiveness of the learned sentential representations.
3.  The introduction and use of a larger, augmented dataset (ACE++) to demonstrate the model's ability to generalize with more data.

**Strengths:**
1.  The primary strength of this work lies in its attempt to reduce the dependency on external, domain-specific lexical-semantic resources. Previous state-of-the-art systems for this task often rely on a large set of features derived from resources like WordNet, VerbOcean, and DBpedia. The proposed model's ability to achieve competitive results without these resources is a significant practical contribution, as it suggests a path toward more portable and less resource-intensive event processing systems.
2.  The paper presents a solid set of experiments, including a detailed ablation study (Table 5). This analysis clearly demonstrates that each component of the proposed feature set (pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential) contributes positively to the final performance. This rigorous breakdown provides convincing evidence for the utility of the learned sentential features.
3.  The inclusion of an error analysis provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task, such as the difficulty of resolving event pronouns. This demonstrates a thoughtful engagement with the problem beyond simply reporting metrics and highlights important directions for future work.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The claim of achieving "state-of-the-art performance" is not strongly supported. The authors themselves acknowledge two significant caveats in their comparison to prior work (Table 3): differing train/test splits of the ACE dataset and, more critically, differences in the experimental setup (e.g., use of gold arguments vs. system-predicted arguments). These inconsistencies, particularly the use of gold-standard information where competitors use system outputs, make a direct comparison with systems like Liu et al. (2014) problematic. An "on-par" F1 score under these more favorable conditions does not constitute a convincing claim of advancing the state of the art. A more rigorous comparison would require running the baselines on the exact same data split and with identical preprocessing, or at a minimum, acknowledging that the comparison is not apples-to-apples and tempering the claims accordingly.
2.  The methodological novelty of the proposed model is limited. The CNN architecture for learning sentence representations—using word and position embeddings, convolution, and piecewise max-pooling—is a direct and now standard application of techniques established in prior work for relation classification and event extraction (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015), which the authors cite. The contribution here is not a new model architecture but rather the application of an existing one to the event coreference task as a feature generator. While this is a valid contribution, the paper frames it as a novel model design rather than an application study.
3.  The experiments rely heavily on a new, non-standard dataset called "ACE++". While using more data is beneficial, the paper provides insufficient information about the creation and properties of this dataset. Key details regarding the annotation process, annotator guidelines, and inter-annotator agreement are missing. Without this information, it is difficult to assess the quality of the dataset and, consequently, the reliability of the results reported on it (Tables 4, 5, and 6). Furthermore, the lack of public availability for this dataset harms the reproducibility of a significant portion of the paper's experiments.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Given the noted discrepancies in data splits and preprocessing pipelines, can you provide a stronger justification for your claim of achieving on-par performance with the state-of-the-art? Have you considered re-running the most competitive baseline (Liu et al., 2014) on your specific data split and with gold arguments to enable a direct, controlled comparison?
2.  Could you provide more details on the creation of the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what was the source of the additional 1351 documents, what were the annotation guidelines, who performed the annotation, and what was the inter-annotator agreement? Will this dataset be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility?
3.  The "distributed similarity" layer uses a `square` activation function, which is unconventional. The paper states this "outperformed model variants" but provides no further justification or empirical support. Could you elaborate on the intuition behind this choice and provide ablation results comparing it to more standard similarity computations, such as a multi-layer perceptron with ReLU activation or a simple cosine/dot-product similarity?
