### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of within-document event coreference resolution, also known as event linking. The authors propose a two-part neural network model. The first part uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to generate a dense vector representation for each individual event mention. This representation is learned from the entire sentence containing the mention, combining sentential features derived from piecewise max-pooling over convolutional filters with trigger-local lexical features. The second part of the model takes the representations of two event mentions, combines them with a set of standard, hand-crafted pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance), and feeds them into a final classification layer to produce a coreference score. The model is trained to minimize logistic loss, and final event clusters are formed by taking the transitive closure of all pairs classified as coreferential. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 dataset and a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++), demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective event linking model that does not require external semantic resources.** The main contribution is the demonstration that a neural model leveraging learned sentential features can achieve a performance level on par with, or exceeding, previous state-of-the-art systems that depend heavily on hand-crafted features derived from external knowledge bases. This significantly improves the portability and domain-independence of event linking systems.
2.  **A thorough empirical analysis of neural features for event linking.** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation that includes an ablation study clearly isolating the benefit of the CNN-based sentential features over and above traditional local and pairwise features. Furthermore, the introduction and use of a larger dataset (ACE++) demonstrates the model's ability to generalize better with more data, and a detailed error analysis offers valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance without External Knowledge.** The most significant strength of this work is that it achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 benchmark while deliberately avoiding external semantic resources. Previous leading systems, such as Liu et al. (2014), rely on a large set of features from resources like WordNet, FrameNet, and DBpedia. By replacing this dependency with learned sentential features, the proposed method presents a more generalizable and self-contained solution, which is a valuable direction for the field. The results in Table 3, showing competitive F1 scores across BLANC, MUC, and other metrics, strongly support this claim.

2.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Model Design.** The two-part architecture is logical and well-motivated by an analysis of the dataset properties. The authors correctly identify that many coreferential event pairs lack overlapping arguments or trigger words, justifying the need for richer sentential context. The use of a CNN with piecewise max-pooling to capture features from the left and right context of the trigger is a proven technique for related tasks, and its application here is technically sound. The combination of these learned representations with established pairwise features is a pragmatic design choice that leverages the strengths of both paradigms.

3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation and Analysis.** The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors not only compare against prior work on a standard dataset but also demonstrate the model's scalability and improved generalization by training on a larger, newly introduced corpus (ACE++). The ablation study presented in Table 5 provides clear evidence for the contribution of each feature component (pairwise, local, and sentential), empirically validating the paper's central hypothesis. The inclusion of an error analysis that identifies challenging cases like pronominal anaphora and annotation ambiguity adds depth to the results and provides a useful guide for future research.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation.** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The model architecture is effectively communicated through both text and a diagram (Figure 1). The motivation, experimental setup, and results are presented transparently, making the work accessible and its contributions clear.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty.** While the *application* of a CNN to event linking is novel, the core CNN architecture for learning sentence representations (word/position embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling) is largely adopted from prior work in relation extraction (Zeng et al., 2014) and event detection (Chen et al., 2015). The paper's primary contribution is therefore in engineering and integration rather than the development of a fundamentally new neural architecture. The model learns representations for each event mention independently and only combines them at the final classification stage, lacking a more sophisticated mechanism (e.g., attention) to model the interaction between the sentential contexts of the two candidate mentions.

2.  **Weakened Comparability with State-of-the-Art.** The authors are transparent about two caveats in their comparison to prior work, but these caveats significantly complicate the interpretation of the results. First, the use of different train/test splits of the ACE dataset across papers makes direct comparison difficult. Second, and more critically, the proposed model uses gold-annotated event arguments for its pairwise features, whereas competing systems like Liu et al. (2014) use automatically predicted arguments. Access to gold arguments provides a substantial advantage and means the comparison is not on an equal footing. This makes it difficult to definitively claim that the proposed model itself is superior, as the performance gains could be partially attributable to cleaner input features.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on ACE++ Dataset.** The paper introduces a new, larger dataset (ACE++) which is used for several key experiments, including the analysis of data size impact and feature ablations. However, the details provided about the 1351 additional documents are sparse. Information regarding the source of these documents, the annotation process, inter-annotator agreement, and how closely the new annotations conform to the original ACE guidelines is missing. Without these details, it is difficult to assess the quality and consistency of this new resource and, consequently, the reliability of the experiments that depend on it.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The model's pairwise comparison module uses a `square` activation function in its hidden layer (Equation 4), which is an unconventional choice. The paper justifies this with an intuition about "invariance under polarity changes." Could you elaborate on this intuition? More importantly, could you provide a quantitative comparison in your ablation studies of the `square` function against more standard non-linearities like ReLU or tanh to empirically justify this design decision?

2.  The error analysis identifies pronominal anaphora (e.g., event mentions triggered by "it" or "this") as a major source of false negatives. How are these pronominal triggers handled by the model? Are they represented by standard word embeddings, and if so, does the model learn a useful representation for them, or does it primarily rely on the surrounding sentential context in these cases?

3.  Could you provide more details regarding the creation of the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what was the source of the 1351 additional web documents, who performed the annotation, and what steps were taken to ensure the annotation quality and style were consistent with the original ACE 2005 corpus? This information is crucial for the community to understand and potentially use this resource.
