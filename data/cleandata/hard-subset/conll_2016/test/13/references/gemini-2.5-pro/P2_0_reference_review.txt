### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural model for within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The core of the approach is a two-stage process. First, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with piecewise max-pooling is used to generate a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention, capturing both local features around the trigger word and broader sentential context. Second, these representations for a pair of event mentions are concatenated with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type match, distance) and fed into another neural network layer to produce a coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the standard ACE 2005 dataset and a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++), demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Primary Contribution:** A successful demonstration that learned, dense "sentential features" from a neural network can effectively replace hand-crafted, external semantic features for the task of event linking. The paper shows that this approach achieves a state-of-the-art level of performance, thereby lowering the barrier to applying event linking systems to new domains where resources like FrameNet may not be available.
2.  **Secondary Contribution:** A thorough empirical evaluation that provides valuable insights. This includes a clear ablation study isolating the impact of different feature classes (pairwise, local, and sentential), an analysis of clustering strategies, and an insightful qualitative error analysis that highlights key remaining challenges for the task, such as pronominal anaphora and annotation ambiguity.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It clearly identifies a critical limitation of previous state-of-the-art systems: their dependence on external, domain-specific knowledge sources. The entire paper is framed around solving this specific problem, and the proposed model is a direct and elegant solution. This clear focus makes the paper's contribution easy to understand and appreciate.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental section is a major strength. The authors are not content with simply reporting a new top score.
    *   The ablation study (Table 5) is particularly compelling, as it systematically breaks down the model's components and proves that the sentential features (row 3 vs. row 2) provide a significant performance boost over and above traditional lexical and pairwise features.
    *   The comparison of clustering strategies (Table 4) yields an interesting result that contrasts with prior work, suggesting their model's classifier is more precise.
    *   The inclusion of simple but important baselines (Table 6) properly contextualizes the model's performance and demonstrates that the task is non-trivial.

3.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The manual error analysis is excellent. It moves beyond quantitative scores to provide a qualitative understanding of the model's failures. Identifying difficult cases like pronominal event anaphora ("It sickens me") and highlighting instances of potential annotation inconsistency provides valuable direction for future research and helps contextualize the absolute performance numbers of any system on this task.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, and the model architecture is explained clearly with a helpful diagram (Figure 1). The authors provide sufficient detail on their hyperparameter tuning process and model specifics (Table 2) to facilitate replication. While the new dataset's status is a minor weakness (see below), providing the document splits for the standard ACE dataset is a good practice that aids comparability.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core technical components of the model, particularly the CNN architecture for sentence representation (convolution followed by piecewise max-pooling), are heavily borrowed from prior work on relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; 2015), which the authors rightly cite. While the *application* of this technique to event linking is novel and effective, the paper does not introduce a new neural architecture. Its main contribution is therefore more empirical and applicational than a fundamental advance in neural network design.

2.  **Ambiguous Status of the ACE++ Dataset:** The introduction of a new, larger dataset (ACE++) is a potentially significant contribution, as data scarcity is a common problem. However, the paper only provides a link to the list of test documents, not the full annotations. Without a full public release of the annotated data, the strong results on ACE++ are not reproducible, and the dataset itself cannot be considered a full contribution to the community. This limits the long-term impact of a substantial part of the paper's evaluation.

3.  **The "Distributed Similarity" Layer Is Under-justified:** The authors propose a similarity layer with a `square` activation function, arguing it provides "invariance under polarity changes." This intuition is not fully explained, and the claim that it outperformed other variants (like cosine similarity or standard activations like ReLU) is stated without supporting evidence or ablation results in the paper. While this is a minor point, strengthening the justification for this non-standard choice would improve the paper's technical depth.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more justification for the choice of the `square` non-linearity in the similarity layer (Eq. 4)? Specifically, could you elaborate on the "invariance under polarity" intuition and provide quantitative results from the experiments you mention that compared this setup to models using more standard activation functions (e.g., ReLU, tanh) or a cosine-similarity based comparison?

2.  The creation of the ACE++ dataset is a valuable effort. Are there plans to make the full annotations publicly available? Doing so would be a significant service to the research community and would make your strong results on this dataset fully reproducible and buildable upon.

3.  Your error analysis correctly identifies event pronominal anaphora as a major source of false negatives. Given your model's architecture, do you have any thoughts on how it could be extended to better handle these cases? For example, could information from an entity coreference system be incorporated to help resolve the antecedent of pronouns like "it" or "this" before the sentence is encoded by the CNN?
