**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a novel approach for within-document event coreference resolution. The central idea is to use a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to generate rich, "sentential" feature representations for individual event mentions. These learned representations are then combined with trigger-local lexical features and standard pairwise features to train a classifier that predicts coreference between pairs of event mentions. The authors claim state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without relying on any external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A New Paradigm for Event Linking:** The primary contribution is the successful application of learned sentential representations to the task of event coreference. This marks a significant and bold departure from prior work that relied heavily on handcrafted, resource-intensive semantic features. By demonstrating that a neural model can learn the necessary semantics directly from the sentential context, the paper opens a promising new research direction for this task.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance without External Knowledge:** A strong secondary contribution is demonstrating that this new approach can achieve performance on par with, and on some metrics exceeding, previous state-of-the-art systems that are dependent on a multitude of external knowledge bases. This is a crucial result, as it suggests a path toward building high-performance, domain-portable event linking systems.
3.  **Valuable Ablation and Error Analysis:** The paper provides a convincing ablation study that clearly isolates the contribution of the novel sentential features. Furthermore, the error analysis provides useful insights into the remaining challenges of the task, such as pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies, which is valuable for the community.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The paper's core idea is its greatest strength. At a time when many systems were piling on more and more external resources (e.g., Liu et al.'s 105 semantic features), this work takes a bold step in the opposite direction. It hypothesizes that the necessary information for coreference can be learned from the raw text and its local context. Proving this hypothesis with a relatively straightforward CNN architecture is a significant conceptual advance for the field. It correctly identifies the trend of using neural networks for feature representation seen in related IE tasks and successfully ports it to event coreference. This is exactly the kind of high-impact, paradigm-shifting work I look for.
2.  **Strong Empirical Justification:** The results strongly support the central claim. The model achieves competitive performance on the ACE dataset against heavily feature-engineered systems. The +3.6 F1 point gain on BLANC on the larger ACE++ dataset when moving from local features (Table 5, row 2) to the full model (row 3) provides clear evidence for the power of the learned sentential features. This isn't just an incremental improvement; it's a demonstration that a fundamentally different approach works, and works well.
3.  **Addresses a Major Practical Limitation:** The reliance on external resources like WordNet, FrameNet, and DBpedia has long been an Achilles' heel for NLP systems, limiting their applicability to new domains or low-resource languages. By eliminating this dependency, the proposed model is far more practical and adaptable. This practical contribution should not be understated.
4.  **Thoughtful Experimental Design:** The authors have gone beyond a simple SOTA comparison. The creation of the larger ACE++ dataset to show the model's ability to leverage more data, the comparison of clustering strategies, and the detailed ablation study all add significant weight to the paper's conclusions.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the *application* of a CNN to this problem is novel, the CNN architecture itself (word/position embeddings -> convolution -> piecewise max-pooling) is a direct and acknowledged adaptation of architectures proposed for related tasks like relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014). The work is more of a "proof of concept" for a new application area than a fundamental advance in neural network design. However, given the strength of the conceptual contribution, I consider this a minor weakness.
2.  **Ambiguity in the "State-of-the-Art" Claim:** The authors are commendably transparent about the caveats in their comparison to prior work (different data splits, use of gold vs. system arguments). However, this transparency also slightly weakens the SOTA claim. For instance, Liu et al. (2014) still outperform this model on the B-CUBED metric. While I believe BLANC is a more suitable metric, the results do not show unambiguous dominance. The claim should perhaps be softened to "highly competitive with the state of the art."
3.  **Insufficient Detail on the ACE++ Corpus:** The creation of a larger dataset is a strength, but the paper provides almost no detail about the 1351 additional documents. What is their source/domain? What was the annotation process and what were the inter-annotator agreement scores? Without this information, it is difficult to fully assess the validity and generality of the results reported on ACE++. Releasing the data and providing these details would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The use of the `square` function as the non-linearity in the similarity layer (Eq. 4) is an interesting and non-standard choice. The intuition about "invariance under polarity changes" is noted, but could you elaborate on this? Did you experiment with more common activation functions like ReLU or tanh in this layer, and if so, how did they compare?
2.  Your error analysis correctly identifies pronoun resolution as a major source of false negatives (e.g., "It sickens me."). This seems to require a level of discourse understanding beyond the sentence level. Do you have thoughts on how your CNN-based model, which is fundamentally sentence-bound, could be extended to capture this type of longer-range anaphoric link to an event?
3.  Regarding the ACE++ dataset, could you provide more details on the source of the 1351 additional documents and the annotation procedure? Are you planning to make the document list and annotations for this new data publicly available?
