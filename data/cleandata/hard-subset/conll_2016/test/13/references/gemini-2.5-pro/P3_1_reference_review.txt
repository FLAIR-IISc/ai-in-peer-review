### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of within-document event coreference resolution, also known as event linking. The authors propose a novel neural network model that avoids reliance on external, domain-specific semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, which have limited portability. The model operates in two stages. First, it generates a fixed-size vector representation for each individual event mention by combining local lexical features (trigger and its neighbors) with sentential features learned by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) applied over the entire sentence containing the mention. Second, it takes pairs of these event representations, augments them with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type compatibility, distance), and feeds them into a final classifier to produce a coreference score. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset and conduct a thorough analysis on an extended, larger dataset (ACE++) to investigate the impact of different feature classes, data size, and clustering strategies.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Application of CNNs for Event Representation in Coreference:** The primary contribution is the successful adaptation and application of a CNN-based sentence encoder to generate representations for event mentions specifically for the task of coreference resolution. While CNNs have been used for event/relation extraction, this work demonstrates their effectiveness in capturing the necessary contextual semantics to replace hand-crafted features derived from external knowledge bases for event linking.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance without External Semantic Resources:** The paper presents a system that achieves state-of-the-art results on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark. The key significance of this result is that it is achieved without leveraging external lexical-semantic resources (e.g., WordNet), making the approach more generalizable and portable to new domains or languages where such resources are scarce.

3.  **Comprehensive Empirical Analysis:** The authors provide a strong empirical evaluation beyond a single SOTA result. They introduce a larger dataset (ACE++) to show the model's ability to benefit from more training data. The detailed ablation study (Table 5) effectively teases apart the contributions of the pairwise, local lexical, and learned sentential features, confirming that the sentential features are a critical component of the model's success. The error analysis also provides valuable qualitative insights for future research.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Sound Methodology:** The model design is elegant and well-justified. The motivation to replace brittle, resource-heavy features with learned distributed representations is compelling and aligns with modern trends in NLP. The hybrid architecture, which combines the strengths of learned sentential features with proven trigger-local and pairwise features, is a very sensible approach. The use of piecewise max-pooling is an appropriate choice adapted from prior work to handle sentences containing multiple event mentions.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Results:** The system achieves competitive, state-of-the-art performance on a widely used benchmark (ACE 2005). The comparison against strong prior systems, particularly Liu et al. (2014) which relies on 105 engineered semantic features, highlights the power of the proposed approach. The performance gains observed when moving from the ACE to the larger ACE++ dataset further validate the model's capacity to learn from data.

3.  **High Portability and Reduced Engineering Effort:** A major strength of this work is its practical implication. By obviating the need for external knowledge bases, the system is far easier to deploy in new domains. This significantly lowers the barrier to building high-performance event processing pipelines, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation and Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting headline metrics. The ablation study provides clear evidence for the utility of each component of the model's input representation. The analysis of different clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) and the comparison to simple but strong baselines provide important context for the results. The error analysis is also insightful, identifying hard challenges like pronominal event anaphora and highlighting annotation ambiguity, which helps to ground the performance numbers realistically.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the application is novel, the core CNN architecture (embeddings -> convolution -> piecewise max-pooling) is largely adopted from prior work in relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). The paper is transparent about this, but it means the technical novelty lies more in the successful adaptation and integration into a coreference system rather than in the neural architecture itself.

2.  **Dependence on a Non-Public Dataset for Key Analyses:** A significant portion of the analysis, including the ablation study and the impact of data size, is conducted on the proprietary ACE++ dataset. While the creation of a larger dataset is a valuable effort, its unavailability hinders reproducibility and prevents the community from verifying these specific findings or building directly upon them. The paper's strongest analytical claims rest on this inaccessible data.

3.  **Lack of Broader Architectural Comparison:** The work focuses exclusively on a CNN to generate sentential features. The related work section mentions recurrent and recursive models, which are often better suited for capturing sequential information and longer-range dependencies. Given the paper's own finding that over half of coreferential links span more than five sentences, a discussion of why a CNN with its limited receptive field is sufficient, or a comparison to an LSTM/GRU-based encoder, would have significantly strengthened the paper's conclusions about sentential feature generation.

4.  **Implicit Assumption of a Sentence-Level Problem:** The model generates representations from single sentences and then compares them pairwise. This largely treats event linking as a sentence-pair classification problem, with discourse structure only crudely captured by a distance feature. This approach may miss document-level narrative cues that are important for resolving complex coreference chains. The transitive closure is applied as a post-processing step, rather than being integrated into a global or joint inference model.

### Questions to Authors

1.  **Clarification on Argument Features:** The pairwise features include "Overlap in arguments." How is this feature computed? Does it rely on gold-standard argument annotations from the dataset? If so, this could inflate performance and limit the model's utility in a real-world setting where arguments must be extracted by an upstream system. Please clarify the source of the argument information used at both training and test time.

2.  **Rationale for CNN over RNN:** Your analysis indicates that many coreferential links are long-distance. This seems to favor a model that can capture document-level context, like a hierarchical RNN. Why did you choose a sentence-level CNN architecture, and do you have any insights into why it performs so well despite the prevalence of long-distance links? Is it primarily capturing semantic similarity of the event's local context rather than specific discourse relationships?

3.  **Details on the ACE++ Dataset:** Could you provide more information about the 1351 web documents added to create ACE++? Specifically, what was the source of these documents, what was the annotation protocol, and what was the inter-annotator agreement? This information is critical for the community to assess the validity and difficulty of the results reported on this extended dataset.
