### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The model operates in two stages. First, it generates a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that processes the entire sentence containing the event trigger. This representation combines learned sentential features with local lexical features around the trigger. In the second stage, these representations for a pair of event mentions are concatenated with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance) and fed into another network to produce a coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 corpus and an expanded version (ACE++), demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Empirical Demonstration of Sufficiency:** The primary contribution is a strong empirical demonstration that learned, sentence-level representations from a CNN can effectively substitute for expensive, handcrafted features from external knowledge bases (like WordNet) for the task of event linking. The paper shows that this approach can achieve a state-of-the-art performance level, a significant result for improving the portability and reducing the complexity of event processing pipelines.
2.  **Thorough Ablation and Error Analysis:** The paper provides a valuable and detailed analysis of the model's performance. The ablation study (Table 5) clearly isolates and quantifies the contribution of the different feature classes (pairwise, local, and sentential), confirming that the sentential features are critical to the model's success. Furthermore, the qualitative error analysis provides important insights into the remaining challenges of the task, such as pronominal event anaphora and annotation inconsistencies.
3.  **New Dataset Resource (ACE++):** The authors have created and utilized a larger dataset, ACE++, by annotating an additional 1351 documents in the style of ACE 2005. This is a useful contribution to the community, as it provides more training data for a data-hungry task and allows for more robust model development and evaluation.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Results:** The paper is motivated by a clear and important goal: reducing the dependency of event coreference systems on external, domain-specific semantic resources. The results convincingly support their thesis. Achieving on-par or better performance than a system like Liu et al. (2014), which uses a "rich set of 105 semantic features," is a significant achievement and a strong argument for the power of learned representations in this context.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is very thorough. The authors compare against multiple state-of-the-art systems, use multiple standard coreference metrics (BLANC, MUC, B-CUBED), and conduct experiments on two datasets. The inclusion of an ablation study is a major strength, as it provides clear evidence for the impact of the novel component of their model (the sentential features). The analysis of different clustering strategies (Table 4) and comparison to simple baselines (Table 6) further strengthen the evaluation.
3.  **Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, and the model architecture is explained clearly, aided by a helpful diagram (Figure 1). The authors provide sufficient detail about their hyperparameter settings (Table 2) and training procedure. Crucially, they also provide a link to their data splits, which is a commendable step towards reproducibility.
4.  **Honest and Insightful Discussion:** The authors are transparent about the limitations of their comparison to prior work, noting the caveats of differing data splits and preprocessing pipelines. The error analysis is also insightful, highlighting not just model failures but also fundamental difficulties and ambiguities in the task itself (e.g., annotation inconsistencies), which puts the performance of all systems into a more realistic perspective.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the application and empirical results are strong, the core methodology itself is a fairly direct application of existing techniques. The use of a CNN with piecewise max-pooling to generate sentence representations for relation/event extraction tasks was well-established by prior work, which the authors cite (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). The two-stage architecture of generating mention representations and then performing pairwise classification is also a standard paradigm. The novelty lies in successfully applying this to event coreference and showing it can replace external resources, making the contribution more empirical than methodological.
2.  **Unfair Comparison due to Gold Inputs:** A significant weakness in the SOTA comparison (Table 3) is the use of gold-annotated event mentions and arguments. The paper notes that competitor systems like Liu et al. (2014) use system-predicted arguments from an SRL tool. Using gold inputs simplifies the task considerably and makes the comparison to systems operating in a more realistic pipeline setting not truly apples-to-apples. The reported performance is therefore likely an upper bound and may not reflect performance in a real-world, end-to-end system.
3.  **Limited Discourse-Level Modeling:** The model generates a representation for each event mention based only on its local sentence. All discourse-level reasoning is handled by the simple pairwise features (e.g., sentence distance) in the second stage. Given that the paper's own analysis shows over half of coreferent pairs are more than five sentences apart, it seems that a sentence-level CNN might not be the optimal architecture. The model has no mechanism to incorporate broader document context into the event mention representations themselves. Architectures like RNNs or Transformers might be better suited for this, but the paper does not justify the choice of CNNs over these alternatives.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the comparison to prior work: Could you estimate or conduct an experiment to show how much of a performance drop your system experiences when using system-predicted event arguments instead of gold arguments? This would provide a much fairer comparison to systems like Liu et al. (2014) and give a more realistic picture of the model's utility in a full IE pipeline.
2.  Could you elaborate on the choice of a CNN architecture? Given that event coreference is a discourse-level task and many coreferent mentions are far apart, did you consider recurrent architectures (e.g., BiLSTM) that might be better at capturing sequential context? Why did you decide on the sentence-level CNN approach?
