### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of within-document event coreference resolution, also known as event linking. The authors propose a two-stage neural network model. In the first stage, a convolutional neural network (CNN) is used to generate a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention, incorporating features from the entire sentence in which the mention appears. In the second stage, these representations for a pair of event mentions are combined with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type compatibility, distance) and fed into a classifier to produce a coreference score. The final event clusters are formed by taking the transitive closure of all pairs with a score above 0.5.

From my perspective, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A successful demonstration that learned, sentence-level neural features can replace external knowledge bases for event linking.** The paper's main achievement is showing that a model without features from resources like WordNet or FrameNet can achieve state-of-the-art performance. This is a significant result, as it suggests a path toward building more portable and less resource-dependent event processing systems.
2.  **A thorough empirical evaluation and analysis.** The authors conduct a strong set of experiments, including ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed sentential features, a comparison of clustering strategies, and an analysis on a new, larger dataset (ACE++) to show the impact of training data size.
3.  **The creation of a larger, ACE-style annotated dataset (ACE++).** By annotating an additional 1351 documents, the authors provide a more robust testbed for their model and show that its performance improves with more data, which is a valuable finding for the community.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated problem and approach.** The paper clearly articulates a key limitation of prior state-of-the-art systems: their heavy reliance on external, domain-specific semantic resources. The proposed solution—using a CNN to learn latent sentential features—is an elegant and effective way to address this limitation. The preliminary data analysis (e.g., showing that many coreferential pairs have different triggers or no argument overlap) provides strong justification for looking beyond local and intrinsic features.
2.  **Strong and convincing experimental results.** The model achieves performance on par with or exceeding the previous state-of-the-art on the standard ACE 2005 dataset. Crucially, it does so without the "rich set of 105 semantic features" used by the competing system of Liu et al. (2014). The ablation study in Table 5 is particularly effective, clearly showing that each feature class (pairwise, local, and sentential) contributes positively to the final performance. The experiments on the larger ACE++ dataset further strengthen the paper's claims by demonstrating the model's ability to generalize and benefit from more training data.
3.  **Clarity and potential for reproducibility.** The model architecture is explained clearly, aided by a well-designed figure (Figure 1). The authors are transparent about their experimental setup, providing details on hyperparameters (Table 2) and even publishing the document lists for their data splits. This commitment to transparency is commendable and aids in the reproducibility of their work.
4.  **Insightful error analysis.** The manual analysis of false positives and false negatives provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task. Identifying pronominal event anaphora as a major source of error and highlighting annotation inconsistencies in the dataset are useful observations that can guide future research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty in the neural architecture.** The core CNN architecture (word/position embeddings -> convolution -> piecewise max-pooling) is a well-established method for sentence-level representation learning, heavily inspired by prior work on relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015), which the authors appropriately cite. The primary novelty lies in its successful *application* to the event linking task, rather than in the model architecture itself.
2.  **The SOTA comparison has some caveats.** The authors are commendably upfront about this, but it's worth noting that the comparison to previous work is not perfectly controlled due to variations in train/test splits and preprocessing pipelines (e.g., use of gold vs. system-predicted arguments). While the results are strong enough to support the claim of being "on-par with the state-of-the-art," the small differences in data and setup slightly temper the strength of this claim.
3.  **Lack of exploration of alternative neural encoders.** The paper focuses exclusively on a CNN for generating sentential features. Given the sequential nature of language, it would have been interesting to see a comparison or at least a discussion of why a CNN was preferred over recurrent architectures like LSTMs or GRUs, which are also commonly used for sentence encoding and might be better suited to capture longer-range dependencies within a sentence.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The use of the `square` function as the non-linearity in the similarity layer (Equation 4) is an interesting and non-standard choice. While the intuition about "invariance under polarity changes" is noted, could you provide more justification for this? Did you experiment with more common activation functions like ReLU or tanh in this layer, and if so, how did they perform in comparison?
2.  The ACE++ dataset is a great asset for this work. Are there any plans to make this dataset publicly available? Releasing it would be a significant contribution to the community, enabling more robust evaluation and development of event linking systems.
3.  Could you elaborate on the choice of a CNN over a recurrent architecture (like an LSTM) for encoding the sentence representation? Did you hypothesize that the local feature patterns captured by convolutions would be more important for this task than the sequential state captured by an RNN, or was this choice motivated by other factors like computational efficiency?
