**Paper:** Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary**
This paper proposes a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The model operates in two stages. First, it generates a representation for each event mention using a convolutional neural network (CNN) to capture sentential context, combined with local features around the event trigger. Second, it pairs these representations, augments them with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance), and feeds them into a feed-forward network to produce a coreference score. The primary claim is that this approach achieves performance comparable to the state-of-the-art on the ACE 2005 dataset without relying on external, hand-crafted semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors also introduce and evaluate on a larger, proprietary dataset (ACE++).

**Contributions**
1.  The main contribution is an empirical demonstration that a model using learned sentential features from a CNN can achieve competitive performance on the event linking task, removing the dependency on external knowledge bases that limits the portability of many existing systems.
2.  The paper presents a thorough ablation study that validates the contribution of different feature components (sentential, local lexical, and pairwise) to the model's overall performance.
3.  The authors introduce a larger dataset, ACE++, for training and evaluation, highlighting the benefit of more data for this task.

**Strengths**
1.  **Competitive Performance without External Resources:** The most significant strength of this work is its demonstration that a model relying on learned sentential features can achieve performance comparable to state-of-the-art systems that are heavily engineered with numerous external semantic features (e.g., Liu et al., 2014). This is a valuable finding, as it suggests a path toward more portable and less resource-intensive event processing systems. The ability to perform on-par with feature-rich models is a strong argument for the validity of the approach.
2.  **Methodological Soundness in Evaluation:** The authors conduct a commendable evaluation. The ablation study in Table 5 clearly demonstrates the incremental value of each feature class, confirming that the sentential features from the CNN are indeed critical to the model's success. The inclusion of simple but informative baselines in Table 6 provides useful context for the model's performance. The error analysis is also a welcome addition, providing qualitative insights into the remaining challenges of the task.
3.  **Data-driven Motivation:** The model design is well-motivated by a preliminary analysis of the data (Section 3). For instance, the findings that a high percentage of coreferential pairs share no arguments or have different triggers effectively justify the need for richer sentential context beyond simple lexical or argument overlap features. This grounding in data properties strengthens the rationale for the proposed architecture.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The primary weakness of this work is the limited novelty of the proposed neural architecture. The model is a straightforward application of a piecewise max-pooling CNN, an architecture that was already well-established for sentence-level tasks like relation classification at the time of this work (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). While applying this established technique to event linking is a valid contribution, the paper presents it as a new model design without sufficiently acknowledging that the core components are directly borrowed from prior work in a related task. The contribution is thus more of an application and empirical result than a new methodology.
2.  **Overstated State-of-the-Art Claim:** The claim of achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on ACE is not entirely substantiated and should be moderated. As shown in Table 3, while the model outperforms Liu et al. (2014) on BLANC and MUC F1 scores, it underperforms on the B-CUBED metric. The authors themselves rightly point out the caveats of such comparisons due to different data splits and preprocessing pipelines. Given these inconsistencies, a more accurate characterization would be that the model achieves "competitive" or "on-par" performance, rather than definitively setting a new SOTA.
3.  **Lack of Justification for Architectural Choices:** The paper focuses exclusively on a CNN for generating sentential features. While effective, there is no justification provided for this choice over other established neural architectures for sentence representation, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTMs/GRUs), which are often considered more natural for sequential data. A comparison or even a brief discussion of why a CNN was preferred would have strengthened the paper's claims about its suitability for encoding event context. Similarly, the use of the `square` activation function in the similarity layer is an unconventional choice justified only by a brief "intuition." A more rigorous justification or comparison against standard activations (e.g., ReLU, tanh) or a simple dot product would be more convincing.
4.  **Issues with the ACE++ Dataset:** While the use of more data is a strength, the introduction of the proprietary ACE++ dataset presents a problem for reproducibility. The paper provides no details on the annotation process, guidelines, or inter-annotator agreement for the 1351 new documents. Without this information, it is difficult to assess the quality of the dataset. Furthermore, since the dataset is not made public, the results on it cannot be reproduced or built upon by other researchers, limiting its value to the community.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please clarify the novel aspects of your model's architecture in comparison to the CNNs used for relation extraction by Zeng et al. (2014, 2015) and Chen et al. (2015), which you cite? The core mechanism of piecewise max-pooling over convolutional features appears very similar.
2.  What was the rationale for selecting a CNN over an RNN/LSTM-based encoder for modeling the sentence context? Did you perform any preliminary experiments with recurrent models, and if so, how did they compare?
3.  Can you provide more details regarding the annotation of the new documents in the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what were the annotation guidelines, what was the source of the documents, and what was the inter-annotator agreement? Are there any plans to release this dataset?
