### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The approach learns vector representations for individual event mentions using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that processes the entire sentence in which an event trigger appears. These learned "sentential features" are combined with local lexical features (from the trigger and its immediate context) and a set of standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance). A classifier then uses this combined representation to produce a coreference score for a pair of event mentions. The authors claim that this method, which does not rely on external knowledge resources like WordNet, achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical study demonstrating that a standard CNN architecture can be effectively applied to the task of event coreference resolution, achieving performance competitive with established methods that rely on extensive feature engineering and external semantic resources.
2.  A useful ablation study that isolates and quantifies the impact of the learned sentential features, showing their significant contribution to the model's overall performance.
3.  An insightful error analysis that highlights key challenges in the task, such as pronominal event anaphora and annotation ambiguities, which provides valuable context for the results.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem Formulation:** The paper correctly identifies a key limitation of many previous event coreference systems: their heavy reliance on external, domain-specific lexical-semantic resources. The goal of replacing this dependency with features learned directly from text is a practically important and well-motivated direction for research, as it promises greater portability and adaptability.
2.  **Thorough Ablation and Component Analysis:** The experiments on the ACE++ dataset are a strong point of the paper. The ablation study in Table 5 clearly demonstrates that each component of the feature set (pairwise, local, and sentential) contributes positively to the final performance. This provides convincing evidence for the authors' central claim that the learned sentential features are a valuable addition to the model. Similarly, the analysis of different clustering strategies provides useful insights for practitioners.
3.  **Solid Empirical Results:** The model achieves strong performance on the ACE 2005 benchmark. While the "state-of-the-art" claim is debatable (see Weaknesses), the system is clearly competitive with prior work. The fact that it does so without external resources makes this result noteworthy and suggests that learned representations can effectively capture some of the semantic information previously supplied by resources like WordNet.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The proposed neural architecture is a direct application of established techniques. The CNN model, with its use of word and position embeddings, convolution layers, and piecewise max-pooling, is heavily borrowed from prior work in relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014) and other sentence-level tasks. While the application to event coreference is new, the paper does not introduce any significant methodological innovation. The contribution is primarily one of engineering and empirical validation rather than a fundamental advance in modeling.
2.  **Overstated "State-of-the-Art" Claim:** The claim of achieving state-of-the-art performance is not robustly supported by the evidence presented. The comparison to Liu et al. (2014) shows mixed results: the proposed model has a higher BLANC F1 score but a lower B-CUBED F1 score. More importantly, the authors rightly acknowledge that the experimental conditions (data splits, use of gold vs. predicted arguments) are not identical, which fundamentally undermines a direct comparison. A more cautious and accurate claim would be that the model achieves performance that is "on-par" or "competitive with" the state-of-the-art, which is still a strong result given its lack of external resources.
3.  **Insufficient Detail and Lack of Availability of the ACE++ Dataset:** A significant portion of the analysis, including the crucial ablation study, is performed on the newly introduced ACE++ dataset. However, the paper provides almost no details about the construction of this dataset—the source of the documents, the annotation process, and quality control measures (like inter-annotator agreement) are all missing. This makes it impossible to fully assess the validity of the results obtained on this data. As the dataset is not made publicly available, this part of the work is not reproducible and its value to the research community is limited.
4.  **Weak Justification for Architectural Choices:** Certain design decisions in the model are not well-motivated. For example, the use of a `square` activation function in the similarity layer is justified by a vague intuition about "invariance under polarity changes." It is not clear what this means in the context of vector similarity, and no empirical comparison is provided to justify this unconventional choice over more standard approaches like a multi-layer perceptron with ReLU activations or other similarity functions (e.g., element-wise product). Rigorous justification for such choices is essential for a method to be considered methodologically sound.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Given that your model underperforms Liu et al. (2014) on the B-CUBED metric and that the experimental setups are not directly comparable, could you please clarify the basis for your state-of-the-art claim? Would you agree that "competitive with the state-of-the-art" is a more precise characterization?
2.  The ACE++ dataset is central to your ablation study and analysis of data size. Could you provide more details regarding its creation, including the source of the web documents, the annotation guidelines used, and the resulting inter-annotator agreement? Are there plans to release this resource to the community?
3.  Could you provide an empirical comparison justifying the use of the `square` activation function in your similarity layer (Eq. 4) against more conventional alternatives, such as a standard MLP layer with ReLU/tanh or a learned bilinear model?
