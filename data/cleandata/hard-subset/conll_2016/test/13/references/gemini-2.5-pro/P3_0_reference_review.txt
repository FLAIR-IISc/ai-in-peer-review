### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The central idea is to learn rich, latent representations of event mentions by applying a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to the full sentence in which an event appears. This learned "sentential" representation is then combined with local features (from the event trigger) and a set of standard pairwise features (e.g., type agreement, distance) to score the coreference likelihood of a pair of event mentions. The authors evaluate their model on the standard ACE 2005 dataset and a new, larger dataset they call ACE++, demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external, domain-specific knowledge bases like WordNet.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of learned sentential features for event coreference.** This work successfully adapts a CNN architecture, popular at the time for relation extraction, to the task of event coreference, showing that it can replace the need for hand-crafted semantic features from external resources while achieving state-of-the-art results. This is a significant contribution, highlighting a path toward more portable and less resource-dependent information extraction systems.
2.  **A thorough and insightful experimental analysis.** Beyond the headline results, the paper provides a valuable ablation study that clearly isolates the contribution of the sentential features. The analysis of clustering strategies, the impact of dataset size, and the detailed manual error analysis provide important insights into the model's behavior and the remaining challenges of the task.
3.  **The creation of a larger dataset (ACE++) for the task.** The standard ACE 2005 corpus is quite small for training modern neural networks. By creating and experimenting with a larger dataset, the authors not only demonstrate their model's ability to generalize better with more data but also provide a resource that, if released, could be valuable for future research.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The proposed two-stage model is well-designed and logical. The first stage focuses on building a high-quality representation of individual event mentions by combining global (sentential) and local (trigger-level) information. The second stage then uses these rich representations for the pairwise comparison task. The use of piecewise max-pooling to handle multiple events in a sentence is a sensible adaptation from prior work.
2.  **Impressive Performance without External Resources:** The paper's primary claim—that it achieves state-of-the-art performance without external knowledge bases—is well-supported by the results in Table 3. Matching or exceeding the performance of systems like Liu et al. (2014), which uses 105 engineered semantic features, is a powerful result. This significantly enhances the model's practical appeal, as it can be more easily applied to new domains where resources like FrameNet or domain-specific ontologies are unavailable.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written and easy to understand. Figure 1 provides a clear illustration of the model architecture, and the authors provide sufficient detail on hyperparameters and the training regime to facilitate reproduction. The decision to publish the list of documents used for their ACE data splits is commendable and a good step towards ensuring fair future comparisons.
4.  **Excellent Analysis:** The ablation study in Table 5 is particularly strong, convincingly showing that each feature class (pairwise, local, and sentential) contributes positively to the final performance. The error analysis is also insightful, correctly identifying notoriously difficult cases like pronominal event anaphora and highlighting the inherent ambiguity and annotation inconsistencies in the task, which helps to contextualize the performance of all systems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core CNN architecture for generating sentence representations (word/position embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling) is directly adopted from prior work on relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014, 2015). While its application to event coreference is novel and impactful, the contribution in terms of neural network architecture is incremental. The paper would be stronger if it discussed why this specific architecture is superior for this task compared to contemporary alternatives like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs/LSTMs).
2.  **Uncertainty in State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The authors are transparent about the caveats in their comparison to prior work, namely differing data splits and, more importantly, the use of gold-standard vs. system-predicted arguments. Using gold arguments gives the proposed model a significant advantage, as it operates on a cleaner input representation than systems like Liu et al. (2014) which must handle noise from upstream components like semantic role labeling. This makes the claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art less definitive, as it is not a direct "apples-to-apples" comparison.
3.  **Lack of Detail on the ACE++ Dataset:** While creating a larger dataset is a potential strength, the paper provides almost no information about it. Key details are missing, such as the source of the documents, the annotation process and guidelines, inter-annotator agreement scores, and most importantly, whether the dataset will be released. Without this information and a public release, the dataset's contribution to the community is minimal, and the results on it are not reproducible by others.
4.  **No Qualitative Analysis of Learned Features:** The model is presented as a black box that achieves good performance. A major opportunity is missed to provide insight into *what* the CNN learns. For instance, an analysis of the convolutional filters could reveal whether the model is learning to recognize specific lexico-syntactic patterns indicative of coreference (e.g., reporting verbs, nominalizations, causal links). Such an analysis would significantly deepen the reader's understanding of why the model works and what kind of linguistic knowledge it is implicitly capturing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  You acknowledge that your model uses gold arguments while some competitors use predicted ones. How do you expect your model's performance to degrade in a more realistic end-to-end pipeline that uses system-predicted arguments? Is there any aspect of your model that might make it more or less robust to such input noise compared to a feature-based random forest model?
2.  The use of a `square` activation in the similarity layer is an unconventional and interesting choice. You state it outperformed other variants. Could you please elaborate on what other activation functions (e.g., ReLU, tanh) or similarity computations (e.g., a learned bilinear model, element-wise product followed by a linear layer) you experimented with?
3.  Could you provide more information on the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what was the source of the additional 1351 documents and what was the annotation protocol? Do you have plans to make this valuable resource publicly available?
