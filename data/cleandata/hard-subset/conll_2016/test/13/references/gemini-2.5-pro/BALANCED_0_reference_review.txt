### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event linking (coreference resolution). The core idea is to learn rich representations of individual event mentions by applying a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) over the entire sentence in which an event appears. This learned "sentential" representation is combined with traditional local features (from the event trigger) and pairwise features (comparing two event mentions). This combined representation is then used to train a classifier that predicts whether two event mentions are coreferent. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 dataset and a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++), demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A strong empirical demonstration that learned sentential features can effectively replace features from external knowledge bases for event linking.** By achieving state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark without hand-engineered semantic features, the paper makes a compelling case for more portable, data-driven approaches to this task.
2.  **A thorough experimental analysis and ablation study.** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to multiple baselines and prior work, analysis of different clustering strategies, and a detailed ablation study that clearly quantifies the impact of each feature class (pairwise, local, and sentential).
3.  **The introduction of a larger dataset (ACE++) for the task.** Although details are sparse, the use of this larger dataset provides evidence for the model's ability to benefit from more training data and offers a potentially valuable resource for future work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Achieving State-of-the-Art Performance without External Resources:** The paper's primary strength is its core result. By matching or exceeding the performance of prior systems that rely on a "rich set of 105 semantic features" from resources like WordNet, DBpedia, and VerbOcean, the authors successfully demonstrate a path towards building high-performance event linking systems that are more domain-independent and easier to deploy. This is a significant step forward for the field.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is very thorough. The ablation study in Table 5 is particularly effective, clearly showing that each component of the model (pairwise, local-lexical, and sentential features) contributes positively to the final performance. The comparison of clustering strategies (Table 4) and the inclusion of strong baselines (Table 6) further strengthen the paper's conclusions. This level of analysis provides convincing support for the authors' claims and model design.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Well-Designed Model:** The paper is well-motivated, citing data statistics (e.g., 47% of coreferent pairs have different triggers, 51% share no arguments) to justify the need for richer, sentence-level context beyond the trigger word and its arguments. The two-part architecture is intuitive: first, build a good representation of a single event mention, and second, compare two such representations. The use of piecewise max-pooling is a sensible way to handle multiple events within a single sentence.
4.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights that go beyond simply categorizing mistakes. The identification of pronoun resolution as a major source of false negatives points to a clear and challenging direction for future research. Furthermore, the observation of potential annotation inconsistencies highlights the inherent ambiguity and difficulty of the task, providing important context for interpreting the reported performance metrics.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the application of a CNN to this specific task is novel and the results are excellent, the core neural architecture itself (word/position embeddings -> convolution -> piecewise max-pooling) is a well-established technique adapted directly from prior work on relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). The paper's main contribution is therefore more empirical and application-focused than a fundamental advance in neural network architectures.
2.  **Ambiguity in State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The authors rightly acknowledge caveats in their comparison to prior work, particularly regarding preprocessing. The use of gold-annotated arguments and triggers in this work, while justifiable for isolating the coreference task, complicates a direct comparison with systems like Liu et al. (2014) which may have operated on system-predicted inputs. This difference in experimental setup makes the "state-of-the-art" claim less definitive. A stronger claim would require running the model on the same system-level inputs as the competitors.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on the ACE++ Dataset:** The introduction of the ACE++ dataset is a potential contribution, but the paper lacks crucial details about its creation. Information about the source of the 1351 additional web documents, the annotation guidelines, the annotation process (e.g., single vs. double annotation), and inter-annotator agreement is missing. Without this information, it is difficult for the community to assess the quality and nature of this new resource. Furthermore, the paper does not clarify if the full dataset annotations will be released, which would limit its impact.
4.  **Lack of Justification for Architectural Choices:** The paper settles on a CNN architecture without discussing or comparing it to other plausible alternatives for encoding sentences, such as LSTMs or other recurrent models, which are often favored for capturing sequential information. While the chosen architecture works well, the paper would be stronger if it briefly justified this choice over alternatives, even if just through reasoning or reference to prior work in related tasks. Similarly, the choice of the `square` activation function in the similarity layer is intriguing but under-explained; a brief comparison to other options (e.g., on the validation set) would have been beneficial.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the comparison in Table 3, could you clarify the inputs used by Liu et al. (2014)? Specifically, did their system use gold-standard event triggers and arguments, or were these produced by an upstream system? This is critical for assessing the fairness of the comparison.
2.  Could you provide more details about the creation and annotation of the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what was the source of the additional documents and what was the annotation protocol? Do you plan to make the full annotations for this dataset publicly available?
3.  The use of the `square` function as the non-linearity for the similarity layer is an interesting choice. You state it outperformed other variants. Could you briefly elaborate on what other functions were tested (e.g., ReLU, a simple concatenation followed by a standard hidden layer, or a direct cosine similarity between event representations) and perhaps provide a sense of the performance difference on the validation set?
