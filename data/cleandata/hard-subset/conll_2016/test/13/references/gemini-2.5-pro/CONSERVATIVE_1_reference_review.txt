**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The model operates in two stages. First, it employs a convolutional neural network (CNN) with piecewise max-pooling to generate a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention, intended to capture salient information from the surrounding sentence. This sentential representation is combined with local lexical features from the event trigger. Second, for any pair of event mentions, their respective representations are concatenated with a set of standard pairwise features and fed into a classifier to produce a coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 corpus and a larger, newly created dataset (ACE++), claiming state-of-the-art performance without reliance on external semantic resources like WordNet.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that a fairly standard CNN architecture, which requires no external knowledge bases, can achieve performance competitive with prior feature-engineered systems on the ACE 2005 event linking task.
2.  A useful ablation study that isolates the contributions of the sentential, local lexical, and pairwise feature components, confirming that the CNN-derived features provide a significant performance boost over more traditional features alone.

**Strengths**

1.  **Independence from External Resources:** The most compelling aspect of this work is its departure from models that depend heavily on curated, domain-specific semantic resources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet, VerbOcean). By learning features directly from the text, the proposed method offers a more portable and scalable solution for event linking, which is a significant practical advantage over systems that are tethered to specific knowledge bases.

2.  **Methodical Ablation Analysis:** The authors provide a clear and convincing ablation study in Table 5. This analysis systematically dissects the model to show the impact of each feature class (pairwise, local, sentential). The results strongly support their central hypothesis: that learned sentential features from the CNN are a critical component for achieving high performance and complement the other feature types. This adds methodological rigor to the paper's claims.

3.  **Solid Baseline Comparisons:** The paper includes several simple but important baselines (Table 6), such as predicting all singletons or linking all mentions of the same event type. Comparing against these helps to properly contextualize the model's performance and demonstrates that the task is non-trivial and that the learned model is capturing meaningful coreference patterns beyond simple heuristics.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Overstated and Unfair State-of-the-Art Claim:** The claim of achieving state-of-the-art performance on the ACE dataset is not well-supported due to significant differences in experimental settings compared to prior work. The authors acknowledge using gold-annotated event arguments, whereas key comparison systems like Liu et al. (2014) use system-generated arguments. Using gold inputs makes the task considerably easier and invalidates a direct comparison of scores. The reported performance gains are marginal and could easily be artifacts of this experimental mismatch. A more rigorous comparison would require running the model on system-produced output to demonstrate its robustness in a realistic pipeline.

2.  **Lack of Architectural Novelty:** The neural architecture itself is a direct application of established CNN models from the relation classification literature (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014). The use of word and position embeddings followed by a convolution and piecewise max-pooling layer is a standard technique. While applying existing architectures to new problems is a valid contribution, the paper presents the model as a novel design without sufficiently acknowledging its direct predecessors. The contribution feels more empirical than methodological, demonstrating that a known technique works for a related task, rather than proposing a new technique.

3.  **Insufficient Description of the ACE++ Dataset:** A substantial part of the evaluation and analysis is performed on a new dataset, "ACE++". However, the paper provides virtually no details about the additional 1351 documents. Critical information regarding the source of the texts, the annotation protocol, quality control measures, and inter-annotator agreement is completely absent. Without these details, the results on ACE++ are not verifiable or reproducible, and the dataset itself cannot be considered a useful contribution to the community in its current form. This is a major methodological flaw.

4.  **Poorly Motivated Design Choices:** Certain model design choices lack rigorous justification. For instance, the use of a `square` activation function in the similarity layer is motivated by a brief "intuition" and the claim that it worked better in experiments. This is insufficient. For an unconventional choice, a quantitative comparison against standard alternatives (e.g., a simple feed-forward layer with ReLU, or cosine similarity between the event vectors) should have been included to properly validate its effectiveness.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given that your key comparison (Liu et al., 2014) does not use gold arguments, can you provide results for your model using system-generated arguments to enable a fair, apples-to-apples comparison? How much does performance degrade, and does your model still outperform the baseline in that setting?

2.  Could you please provide more details on the creation of the ACE++ dataset? Specifically, what was the source of the new documents, what annotation guidelines were used, and what was the measured inter-annotator agreement? This information is essential for assessing the validity of your experiments on this dataset.

3.  The `square` activation function is an unusual choice. Could you provide results from your hyperparameter search or an ablation study that compares it directly to more standard activation functions (e.g., ReLU, tanh) or similarity metrics (e.g., cosine similarity, a learned bilinear model) for the pairwise scoring component?
