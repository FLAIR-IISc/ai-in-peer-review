**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The central idea is to learn rich representations of event mentions by leveraging the entire sentence in which they appear, thereby reducing the reliance on external knowledge resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The model consists of two parts: first, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) generates a "sentential feature" vector for each event mention, which is combined with local lexical features. Second, a classifier takes pairs of these event representations, augments them with standard pairwise features (e.g., type agreement, distance), and predicts a coreference score. The authors demonstrate that this approach achieves performance on par with the state-of-the-art on the ACE 2005 dataset, despite not using the external semantic features common to competing methods. They also introduce a larger dataset, ACE++, to conduct a thorough ablation study and error analysis.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **An effective and resource-light neural model for event linking:** The paper successfully adapts the now-common paradigm of using CNNs for sentence representation to the task of event coreference. The primary contribution is demonstrating that these learned sentential features can effectively replace the need for external, domain-specific resources, achieving competitive performance with a more portable and self-contained model.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation and analysis:** The work is supported by a strong set of experiments. Beyond reporting state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark, the authors conduct a detailed ablation study on a larger dataset (ACE++) that clearly isolates the contribution of each component of their model (sentential, local, and pairwise features). The included error analysis provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Achieving SOTA-level performance without external resources:** The strongest argument for this paper is its core result: it matches the performance of complex systems that depend heavily on hand-crafted features and external knowledge bases (e.g., Liu et al., 2014). By showing that learned features can serve as a substitute, the paper makes a valuable contribution towards building more robust and domain-adaptable NLP systems. This is a significant practical advantage.
2.  **Thorough and convincing experimental methodology:** The evaluation is well-designed and supports the paper's claims effectively. The authors compare against relevant prior work on the standard ACE 2005 dataset and are transparent about potential inconsistencies in evaluation setups. The creation and use of the larger ACE++ dataset for deeper analysis is a major strength, allowing for a clear ablation study (Table 5) and demonstrating that the model benefits from more training data (Table 4).
3.  **Clear presentation and insightful error analysis:** The paper is well-written, and the model architecture is explained clearly with helpful diagrams. The analysis is particularly strong. The ablation study gives a clear understanding of what drives the model's performance. Furthermore, the manual error analysis is insightful, highlighting difficult cases like pronoun resolution and pointing to potential annotation ambiguities. This helps contextualize the model's performance and provides clear directions for future research.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited novelty in the core neural architecture:** While the application to event linking is effective, the neural architecture itself is not novel. The CNN-based sentence encoder with position embeddings and piecewise max-pooling (Model part (a)) is a standard design borrowed directly from prior work on relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014, 2015). The main novelty is in the successful application to this new task, rather than in the development of a new neural technique.
2.  **Unclear contribution of the "distributed similarity" layer:** The paper proposes a similarity layer using a `square` activation function (Eq. 4), motivated by a desire for "invariance under polarity changes." This is a non-standard choice, and the motivation is not fully explained. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed justification or an ablation study comparing this design to more common alternatives (e.g., a standard `relu` or `tanh` activation, or a different method for combining the two event vectors). Without this, it is difficult to assess the importance of this specific architectural decision.
3.  **Lack of comparison to alternative neural encoders:** The paper is motivated by the general utility of "sentential features" but only explores one method for generating them (a CNN). The related work section acknowledges other prominent architectures like RNNs/LSTMs, which are also powerful sentence encoders. A discussion of why a CNN was chosen over these alternatives, or ideally a comparative experiment, would provide a more complete assessment of neural approaches for this task and better situate the contribution.
4.  **Experiments are based on gold-standard inputs:** The experiments are conducted using gold-annotated event mentions (triggers and arguments). This is a common and reasonable methodology for isolating the performance of the coreference component. However, in a real-world scenario, the model would operate on the output of an upstream event extraction system. The paper would be more complete if it included a discussion of the model's expected robustness to noise from predicted event mentions, as performance can degrade significantly in a full pipeline setting.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for using the `square` activation function in the similarity layer? Did you experiment with other methods for comparing the two event representations (e.g., element-wise product followed by a standard MLP, or using the absolute difference) before the final classifier?
2.  Your error analysis correctly identifies pronoun resolution as a key source of errors (e.g., "It sickens me"). Since your model processes the full sentence, do you have any insight into why the CNN architecture fails to capture these links? Does it suggest a limitation in the model's ability to represent discourse-level phenomena?
3.  The ACE++ dataset appears to be a valuable resource for this task. Do you plan to make the document lists and annotations for the newly added documents publicly available to facilitate replication and future research?
