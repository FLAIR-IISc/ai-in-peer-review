### Summary

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The central idea is to learn rich representations of event mentions by considering their full sentential context, thereby reducing the dependency on external, domain-specific semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The model operates in two stages. First, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with piecewise max-pooling is used to generate a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention, capturing both trigger-local lexical features and broader sentential features. Second, these learned representations for a pair of event mentions are combined with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance) and fed into another neural layer to compute a coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the standard ACE 2005 dataset and a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++). The results show that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming previous systems that rely on extensive hand-crafted semantic features.

### Contributions

1.  **Demonstration of effective feature learning for event linking:** The primary contribution is the empirical demonstration that learned sentential features from a CNN can successfully replace a large suite of external semantic features for the task of event linking. By achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark without resources like WordNet, the work provides strong evidence for the portability and effectiveness of neural representation learning in this specific task.
2.  **Creation and analysis of a new, larger dataset:** The paper introduces ACE++, a significant extension of the ACE 2005 corpus. Experiments on this dataset show that the model's performance, particularly recall, improves substantially with more training data. This new resource and the accompanying analysis highlight the data-hungry nature of such models and provide a more robust evaluation environment.
3.  **A comprehensive ablation study and error analysis:** The paper includes a detailed ablation study that methodically evaluates the contribution of different feature classes (pairwise, local, and sentential), confirming that all components are beneficial. Furthermore, the error analysis identifies key remaining challenges, such as pronominal event anaphora and annotation ambiguity, offering valuable directions for future work.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong empirical results without external knowledge bases:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 benchmark, outperforming a strong prior system by Liu et al. (2014) on several key metrics (BLANC, MUC). This is particularly impressive because the proposed model deliberately avoids external semantic resources, in contrast to prior work which relied on a "rich set of 105 semantic features." This result makes a compelling case for the model's improved portability and generalizability to new domains where such resources may not be available.
2.  **Clear and well-motivated model design:** The two-stage architecture is intuitive and clearly explained. The first stage focuses on building a high-quality representation of a single event mention, while the second stage focuses on the comparison task. The authors provide strong motivation for using sentential features by analyzing the dataset, noting that a high percentage of coreferential pairs share no arguments or have different trigger words, making sentence-level context crucial. The use of established techniques like piecewise max-pooling is appropriate for the task.
3.  **Thorough evaluation and analysis:** The paper's evaluation is comprehensive. Beyond the main SOTA comparison, it includes an insightful ablation study (Table 5) that quantifies the impact of each feature component. The comparison of clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) and the analysis of the effect of increased training data (ACE vs. ACE++) provide valuable insights into the model's behavior. The error analysis is also a strength, as it moves beyond quantitative metrics to provide a qualitative understanding of the model's failures, pointing to challenging linguistic phenomena and potential annotation inconsistencies.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited architectural novelty:** While the application of CNNs to event linking is novel and impactful, the core CNN architecture itself (word and position embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling) is heavily based on prior work in relation extraction (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014, 2015; Chen et al., 2015). The paper acknowledges its inspiration from this literature, but the submission's contribution lies more in the successful adaptation and evaluation of this architecture for a new task rather than in proposing a novel neural architecture.
2.  **Lack of clarity in the pairwise comparison mechanism:** Section 3.2 and Figure 1(b) are ambiguous about how the representations of the two event mentions are combined before being fed to the similarity layer. The text mentions "the concatenation of these vectors (vsent+lex+pairw)", but it is unclear how the two distinct `vsent+lex` vectors (one for each mention) are integrated into a single input stream. Is it a simple concatenation of the two vectors, or is an element-wise product or difference used to model their interaction before concatenation with pairwise features? This detail is critical for understanding the model and for reproducibility.
3.  **Reproducibility issues due to proprietary dataset:** A significant portion of the analysis, including the ablation study and the demonstration of performance gains from more data, is conducted on the new ACE++ dataset. While the authors are commended for releasing the document IDs for their ACE splits, the ACE++ annotations themselves are not made publicly available. This prevents the community from verifying the results on this larger dataset and from using it as a benchmark for future research, limiting the long-term impact of this part of the work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact mechanism for combining the representations of two event mentions in the second part of your model (Section 3.2)? Specifically, how are the two `vsent+lex` vectors and the `vpairw` vector combined to form the input to the similarity layer `Wsim`?
2.  Your error analysis correctly identifies pronominal anaphora (e.g., "It sickens me") as a major challenge. Given that your CNN operates only on the sentence containing the trigger word ("sickens"), how can the model be expected to resolve the pronoun "It" to a preceding event? Does your model learn any implicit cross-sentence context, or is this type of coreference fundamentally beyond the scope of the current sentence-level architecture?
