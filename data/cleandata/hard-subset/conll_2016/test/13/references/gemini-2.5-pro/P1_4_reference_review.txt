### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The central thesis is that rich, sentence-level features learned by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) can replace the need for extensive, hand-crafted semantic features from external knowledge bases (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet) while maintaining state-of-the-art performance. The proposed model operates in two stages: first, it uses a CNN with piecewise max-pooling to generate a dense vector representation for each event mention based on its sentential context. Second, it combines the representations of a pair of event mentions with standard pairwise features (e.g., type agreement, distance) and feeds them into another neural network to compute a coreference score.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A strong empirical demonstration that learned sentential features can effectively substitute for external structured knowledge bases in event linking.** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset without relying on resources like WordNet, thereby improving the portability of event linking systems to new domains.
2.  **The creation and use of a larger dataset (ACE++) for event linking.** By supplementing the standard ACE corpus, the authors provide a more robust training and evaluation environment, showing that their model benefits significantly from more data. The public release of the data splits is a valuable contribution to the community.
3.  **A thorough set of experiments and analyses.** The paper includes a valuable ablation study that quantifies the contribution of different feature classes (sentential, local, pairwise), a comparison of clustering strategies, and a qualitative error analysis that provides insights into the remaining challenges of the task.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Well-Defined Problem.** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It clearly identifies a key limitation of prior work—the heavy reliance on external, domain-specific semantic resources—and proposes a direct and compelling solution using neural networks to learn features from context. This clear narrative makes the paper easy to follow and the contribution significant.
2.  **Strong State-of-the-Art Results.** The primary claim of the paper is well-supported by the results. The model achieves performance on par with or exceeding the previous state-of-the-art system by Liu et al. (2014) on the ACE 2005 benchmark (Table 3). Achieving this without the "rich set of 105 semantic features" used by the competitor is a significant achievement and convincingly validates the authors' approach.
3.  **Comprehensive Ablation Study.** The analysis in Table 5 is a major strength. It systematically dissects the model's architecture, demonstrating that all three feature groups (pairwise, trigger-local, and sentential) contribute positively to the final performance. This analysis provides strong evidence that the learned sentential features are not merely replicating local trigger information but are capturing additional, useful contextual signals.
4.  **Valuable Analysis of Clustering and Data Size.** The paper provides useful insights beyond the main model. The comparison in Table 4 shows that more training data (via ACE++) leads to a substantial jump in recall, highlighting the data-hungry nature of such models. Furthermore, the finding that simple transitive closure outperforms the "BestLink" strategy for their model is an interesting result that contrasts with previous work, suggesting their classifier produces more calibrated scores.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty.** While the application is effective, the core neural architecture is not novel. The CNN model for generating sentence representations (word/position embeddings -> convolution -> piecewise max-pooling) is a standard architecture widely used in prior work on relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015), which the authors rightly cite. The main contribution is therefore the successful application and engineering of these known components for event linking, rather than a new model architecture. The paper could be strengthened by more explicitly framing its contribution this way.
2.  **The Claim of Avoiding "External Features" is Nuanced.** The paper's central claim is that it avoids external semantic features. However, the model is initialized with pre-trained word embeddings from Mikolov et al. (2013). These embeddings are a powerful source of semantic knowledge derived from a massive external corpus. This weakens the claim of resource independence. The authors should clarify that their method avoids *structured* external resources like ontologies (WordNet, FrameNet), which require significant manual effort and have limited domain coverage, but not *unstructured* resources like large text corpora used for pre-training.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on the ACE++ Dataset.** The introduction of the ACE++ dataset is a good contribution, but the paper provides very few details about its creation. Information about the source of the 1351 additional documents, the annotation process and guidelines, and, most importantly, the inter-annotator agreement (IAA) is missing. Without these details, it is difficult for reviewers to assess the quality and reliability of the dataset and, by extension, the results reported on it.
4.  **Potential for Unfair Hyperparameter Tuning.** The hyperparameter tuning was performed on the ACE++ validation set. The paper is unclear whether these same hyperparameters were then used for the experiments on the much smaller, original ACE dataset. Using hyperparameters optimized on a large, potentially out-of-domain dataset for a small, in-domain task could lead to suboptimal performance and may affect the fairness of the SOTA comparison on ACE 2005. This detail should be clarified.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify your definition of "external features"? Does your claim specifically refer to the avoidance of structured knowledge bases like WordNet, as opposed to knowledge derived from large, unstructured text corpora via pre-trained word embeddings?
2.  Could you provide more details regarding the creation of the ACE++ corpus? Specifically, what was the source and domain of the additional web documents, what annotation process was followed, and what was the resulting inter-annotator agreement?
3.  For the main SOTA comparison on ACE 2005 (Table 3), were the hyperparameters tuned on ACE++ (as described in Section 4) used directly, or was a separate tuning process performed using only the original ACE training/validation data?
