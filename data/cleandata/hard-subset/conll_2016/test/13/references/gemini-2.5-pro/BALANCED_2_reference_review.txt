### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a neural network-based model for within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The central idea is to learn rich representations of event mentions by applying a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) over the entire sentence in which an event trigger appears. This learned "sentential" representation is combined with trigger-local lexical features to form a vector for each event mention. In a second step, a pairwise classifier takes the representations of two event mentions, combines them with a set of standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance), and predicts a coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 dataset and a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++), demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A strong empirical demonstration that learned sentential features can effectively replace hand-crafted semantic features for event linking.** The paper successfully shows that a CNN-based model can achieve state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark, removing the dependency on external, domain-specific resources. This is a significant practical contribution that improves the portability and applicability of event linking systems.
2.  **A thorough experimental analysis, including a new dataset and insightful ablation studies.** The introduction of the larger ACE++ dataset, along with ablation experiments that clearly isolate the impact of sentential, local, and pairwise features, provides convincing evidence for the model's design. The error analysis further offers valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance without External Resources:** The paper's strongest argument is its empirical success. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark, outperforming a feature-rich system (Liu et al., 2014) on the BLANC metric. Crucially, it does so without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet, FrameNet, or DBpedia, which are a major bottleneck for porting IE systems to new domains. This is a compelling result that highlights the power of learning representations directly from text.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Principled Model Design:** The model's architecture is clearly motivated by both prior work in related IE tasks and a data analysis that highlights the need for features beyond trigger and argument overlap. The two-stage approach—first learning mention representations, then making a pairwise decision—is logical. The use of a CNN with piecewise max-pooling to capture salient n-grams relative to the event trigger is a sensible and proven technique. The final model, which combines the strengths of learned sentential features with robust local and pairwise features, is well-designed and effective.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Evaluation:** The evaluation is a significant strength. Beyond the SOTA comparison on ACE, the authors perform several valuable experiments on their larger ACE++ dataset.
    *   The ablation study in Table 5 is particularly effective, clearly demonstrating that all three feature components (pairwise, local, and sentential) contribute positively to the final performance, with the sentential features providing a major boost.
    *   The comparison between transitive closure and the BestLink clustering strategy provides useful practical insights.
    *   The qualitative error analysis is excellent, identifying challenging linguistic phenomena like event-anaphora resolution via pronouns ("It sickens me") and highlighting potential annotation inconsistencies, which helps to contextualize the performance numbers and points toward important directions for future research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the application to event linking is effective, the core model architecture itself has limited novelty. The CNN for sentence representation, including word/position embeddings and piecewise max-pooling, is heavily inspired by and very similar to models from prior work on relation classification and event extraction (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly discussed how its architecture differs from these predecessors and why this specific configuration is particularly advantageous for event linking as opposed to other tasks. As it stands, the contribution feels more empirical than methodological.

2.  **Unsubstantiated "Distributed Similarity" Layer:** The paper introduces a "distributed similarity" layer that uses a `square` activation function, claiming this provides "invariance under polarity changes" and outperformed other methods in experiments. This claim is not supported by any results in the paper. The intuition behind "polarity invariance" is not explained clearly, and it is not obvious why a squared activation would be superior to, for example, a standard ReLU or a simple cosine similarity baseline. Without an ablation study or a more rigorous justification, this component feels ad-hoc and its contribution to the model's success is unclear.

3.  **Lack of Detail on the New ACE++ Dataset:** The introduction of the ACE++ dataset is a potentially valuable contribution, but the paper provides insufficient detail about its creation. It is described only as ACE plus 1351 "web documents annotated in an analogous fashion." For the community to trust the results based on this data and potentially use it in the future, more information is essential: What was the source of the documents? What were the annotation guidelines and process? What was the inter-annotator agreement? Without these details, the quality of the dataset and the validity of the experiments conducted on it are difficult to assess.

4.  **Caveats in the State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The authors are transparent about the fact that their comparison to prior work is not perfectly apples-to-apples, due to different data splits and, importantly, different inputs (gold vs. system-predicted arguments). For instance, Liu et al. (2014) use a more realistic setting with predicted arguments, making their task harder. While the use of gold arguments is a reasonable choice to isolate the coreference task, this makes the claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art slightly less conclusive. A discussion of how performance might be affected in a true end-to-end setting would strengthen the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the "distributed similarity" layer? Specifically, could you clarify the intuition for using the `square` activation function and provide the results of the ablation experiments you mention that compared it against alternatives like cosine similarity or other activation functions? This would help to quantify its actual contribution.
2.  Could you provide more details on the creation of the ACE++ dataset? Information regarding the source of the additional documents, the annotation protocol, and the inter-annotator agreement would be very helpful for assessing the quality of this new resource and the reproducibility of your results.
3.  Given that your model uses gold arguments while some key prior work (e.g., Liu et al., 2014) uses system-predicted ones, do you have any insights or preliminary results on how your model's performance might degrade in a more realistic end-to-end pipeline? Does the reliance on sentence-level semantics make the model more or less robust to errors in argument identification compared to models that rely more heavily on argument-based features?
