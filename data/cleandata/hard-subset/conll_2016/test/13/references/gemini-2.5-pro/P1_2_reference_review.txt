### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of within-document event coreference resolution, also known as event linking. The authors propose a two-part neural network model that first learns a representation for individual event mentions and then uses these representations to make a pairwise coreference decision. The core idea is to leverage a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to generate "sentential features" from the full sentence containing an event mention, thereby capturing contextual information without relying on external lexical-semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The model combines these learned sentential features with local lexical features and standard pairwise features. Experiments on the ACE 2005 corpus and a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++) show that this approach achieves performance on par with or exceeding previous state-of-the-art systems that heavily depend on such external resources.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A successful demonstration of replacing external knowledge with learned features for event linking.** The primary contribution is showing that a relatively standard CNN architecture can effectively learn sentential representations that serve as a substitute for hand-engineered semantic features from external knowledge bases. Achieving state-of-the-art results with a more portable, resource-agnostic model is a significant step forward for the field.
2.  **Creation of a larger dataset (ACE++) for the task.** The authors augment the standard, relatively small ACE 2005 corpus with additional annotated documents. This new dataset is a valuable contribution, as it enables more robust training and evaluation of data-hungry neural models and facilitates future research in this area.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis.** The paper includes a strong set of experiments, including an insightful ablation study that clearly isolates and validates the contribution of the proposed sentential features. The error analysis is also useful, highlighting challenging remaining problems like pronominal event anaphora and annotation inconsistencies.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and impactful problem framing.** The paper clearly motivates the need for event linking systems that are not dependent on external, domain-specific resources like WordNet. This is a practical and important goal, as it makes the resulting system far more portable to new domains where such resources may not exist. The results strongly support their central claim that this is achievable.
2.  **Strong and thorough experimental evaluation.** The evaluation is a key strength of this work. The authors compare their model to several state-of-the-art systems on the standard ACE benchmark. The ablation study presented in Table 5 is particularly convincing, as it systematically breaks down the model's components and demonstrates that each feature class (pairwise, local, and sentential) contributes positively to the final performance, with the sentential features providing the largest boost. The inclusion of experiments on the larger ACE++ dataset also shows the model's ability to benefit from more training data.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility.** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained clearly, aided by a helpful diagram in Figure 1. The authors provide sufficient detail about their hyperparameters (Table 2) and experimental setup, including a link to their data splits, which is commendable and enhances the reproducibility of their work.
4.  **Insightful Data and Error Analysis.** The preliminary analysis of the ACE++ dataset in Section 3 provides strong motivation for the use of sentential features by highlighting that a large percentage of coreferent pairs do not share arguments or trigger words. Furthermore, the manual error analysis in Section 5.2 offers valuable insights into the limitations of the current model and the inherent difficulties of the task, such as resolving pronouns that refer to entire events and dealing with annotation ambiguities. This points to clear avenues for future work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited architectural novelty.** While the application of the model to event linking is novel and effective, the CNN architecture itself is a fairly standard implementation of techniques previously used for relation extraction and sentence classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015), which the authors cite. The contribution lies more in the successful transfer and integration of this architecture for a new task rather than in the development of a new neural architecture. The paper could be strengthened by more explicitly positioning itself as such.
2.  **Slightly weakened claim of state-of-the-art performance due to experimental differences.** The authors are transparent about two caveats in their comparison to prior work: differing train/test splits and the use of gold-standard vs. system-predicted arguments. While their performance is clearly competitive, these differences make a direct, definitive claim of superiority difficult. For instance, Liu et al. (2014) achieve a better B-CUBED score, while this paper wins on BLANC and MUC. A more nuanced conclusion of "achieving competitive SOTA performance without external resources" seems more appropriate than a hard claim of outperforming all prior work. The reliance on gold event mentions and arguments is an oracle setting, and performance in a real-world, end-to-end pipeline remains an open question.
3.  **Lack of justification for the choice of CNN over other architectures.** The paper focuses exclusively on a CNN for generating sentential features. By 2015, Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTMs, GRUs) were also a very popular and powerful choice for sentence modeling, arguably better suited for capturing long-range dependencies. The related work section briefly mentions them, but the paper would benefit from a short discussion justifying the choice of a CNN over recurrent alternatives for this specific task.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential impact of using gold-annotated arguments compared to the system-level arguments used by competitors like Liu et al. (2014)? Does having access to perfect argument information provide a significant advantage for your argument overlap features, and how might performance degrade in a more realistic end-to-end setting where arguments are predicted with errors?
2.  The ACE++ dataset seems like a valuable resource for the community. Are there plans to make the annotations publicly available? This would greatly facilitate future research and allow for direct and fair comparisons against your work.
3.  The error analysis points to pronominal anaphora (e.g., "It sickens me") as a key challenge. Do you have thoughts on how your current architecture could be extended to handle such cases? Would it require a different type of modeling altogether, perhaps one that explicitly models discourse structure or takes a wider document context into account beyond the immediate sentence?
