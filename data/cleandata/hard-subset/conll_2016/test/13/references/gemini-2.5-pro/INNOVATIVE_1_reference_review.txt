### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution. The core of the approach is a two-stage process: first, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is used to learn a dense vector representation for each event mention, capturing features from the entire sentence. Second, these learned representations are paired and combined with a small set of standard pairwise features (e.g., event type match, distance) to make a final coreference decision via a logistic regression classifier. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 dataset and a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++), demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on external, manually curated semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **A Methodological Shift for Event Linking**: The primary contribution is the successful demonstration that learned, data-driven sentential features can serve as a powerful and sufficient replacement for the complex, handcrafted semantic features derived from external knowledge bases that were predominant in prior state-of-the-art systems. This is a significant conceptual advance that makes event linking systems far more portable to new domains.
2.  **A New, Larger Dataset (ACE++)**: The authors have created and utilized a new dataset that is substantially larger than the standard ACE 2005 corpus. This is a valuable contribution to the community, as the paper shows that more data leads to significant performance improvements, particularly for representation-learning models like the one proposed.
3.  **A Solid and Well-Analyzed Model**: The paper proposes a clean and effective neural architecture and provides a thorough set of experiments and analyses, including ablation studies on feature sets and a manual error analysis that offers valuable insights into the remaining challenges of the task.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and High Potential Impact**: The paper's most compelling aspect is its bold departure from the feature-engineering-heavy paradigm. By showing that a model can learn the necessary semantic context directly from text and achieve state-of-the-art results, the work provides a clear path away from the brittle and domain-specific systems that rely on external resources like WordNet. This focus on learned representations over engineered features is precisely the kind of forward-looking research that pushes the field in a new and promising direction.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support for the Central Claim**: The experiments provide convincing evidence for the model's effectiveness. The comparison to Liu et al. (2014) is particularly powerful; achieving comparable or better performance while explicitly avoiding their "rich set of 105 semantic features" is a major result. Furthermore, the ablation study in Table 5 is excellent, clearly demonstrating that the novel sentential features provide a substantial boost over and above the local and pairwise features (e.g., the F1 jump from 64.12% to 76.90% on BLANC).
3.  **Thoughtful Analysis and New Resource**: The paper does not simply present a model and a number. The analysis of dataset size (Table 4) reinforces the value of their new ACE++ dataset, a contribution that will hopefully benefit future work. The error analysis is also insightful, correctly identifying notoriously difficult phenomena like pronominal anaphora and, importantly, highlighting the inherent ambiguity and annotation inconsistencies in the task. This contextualizes the results and points to fundamental challenges beyond model architecture.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas where it could be strengthened. I view these as minor points that do not detract from the significance of the core contribution.

1.  **Limited Architectural Innovation**: The CNN architecture itself (embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling) is a direct and faithful application of models previously proposed for the related task of relation classification. While its application to event linking is novel and effective, the paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of *why* this architecture is particularly suited for event coreference. For instance, is there an intuition for why piecewise pooling around the trigger is as effective for coreference as it is for argument extraction? The architectural contribution feels more like a successful technology transfer than a novel design.
2.  **Slightly Overstated "No External Resources" Claim**: The model initializes its word embeddings with pre-trained word2vec vectors. While these are fundamentally different from curated knowledge bases like FrameNet, they represent a massive source of external information learned from web-scale corpora. The central claim would be more precise if framed as replacing *manually-curated lexical-semantic resources* with *unsupervised, corpus-derived representations*. This is still a very strong and important claim, but the current phrasing is a bit imprecise. An experiment with randomly initialized embeddings trained only on the task data would have helped to quantify the knowledge contributed by word2vec.
3.  **Comparison to SOTA is Indirect**: The authors rightly acknowledge the caveats of comparing to published numbers on potentially different data splits and with different pre-processing pipelines. The claims would be unassailable if the authors had re-implemented a strong feature-based baseline on their own splits for a direct, controlled comparison. While I understand this is a significant undertaking, its absence means the SOTA comparison relies on a degree of faith that the experimental differences are minor. Given the strength of the results, I am willing to accept this, but a direct comparison would have been ideal.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The `square` function in the similarity layer is an interesting choice. The intuition about "invariance under polarity changes" is noted, but could you elaborate? How did this compare empirically to other common element-wise non-linearities (like `relu`, `tanh`) or to other similarity functions like a learned bilinear model?
2.  Your error analysis suggests that a large fraction of errors (up to ~20% of FPs and ~17% of FNs) are either plausible misclassifications or stem from annotation inconsistencies. This is a fascinating finding. Does this suggest that we are approaching the ceiling of what is achievable on this task with current annotation schemes, and that future gains may depend as much on refining the task definition or data quality as on improving model architectures?
