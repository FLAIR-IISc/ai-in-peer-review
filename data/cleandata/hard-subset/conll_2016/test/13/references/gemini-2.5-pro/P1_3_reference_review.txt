### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for within-document event coreference resolution (event linking). The model operates in two stages. First, it generates a fixed-size vector representation for each event mention by using a convolutional neural network (CNN) to capture sentential context, which is then combined with trigger-local lexical features. Second, it takes a pair of these event mention representations, augments them with standard pairwise features (e.g., event type agreement, distance), and feeds them into a final classifier to produce a coreference score. The authors evaluate their model on the ACE 2005 dataset and a larger, newly created dataset (ACE++). The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **An effective event linking system that eliminates the need for external knowledge bases.** The primary contribution is demonstrating that a model using only features derivable from the text itself (lexical, syntactic, and neurally-generated sentential features) can achieve state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark. This is a significant result, as it moves towards more portable and less domain-dependent event processing systems compared to prior work that relied heavily on resources like WordNet or FrameNet.
2.  **A thorough empirical analysis of feature contributions and model design choices.** The paper includes a valuable ablation study that clearly isolates the impact of pairwise, trigger-local, and the novel sentential features, confirming that all three components are beneficial. The analysis is further strengthened by experiments on a larger dataset (ACE++), which demonstrates the model's ability to generalize better with more data, and an investigation into different clustering strategies.
3.  **A qualitative error analysis that highlights key remaining challenges.** The manual inspection of errors provides useful insights into the limitations of the current model and the inherent difficulties of the task, such as resolving pronominal event anaphora and handling cases of annotation inconsistency.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance without External Resources:** The model achieves performance on par with or exceeding previous state-of-the-art systems on the ACE 2005 dataset. Crucially, it does so without relying on handcrafted semantic features from external knowledge bases. This successfully validates the central hypothesis of the paper: that neurally-derived sentential features can effectively replace the need for such external resources, making the approach more robust and adaptable to new domains.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Sound Model Design:** The architecture is logically structured and well-motivated by both the authors' preliminary data analysis and trends in related NLP tasks. The analysis showing that a high percentage of coreferential pairs share no arguments or trigger words provides a strong justification for incorporating richer sentential context. The use of a CNN with piecewise max-pooling is a standard and effective technique for sentence representation that is sensibly adapted here for event triggers.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments. The ablation study in Table 5 provides convincing evidence for the utility of the proposed sentential features. The comparison between transitive closure and BestLink clustering offers a practical insight for future work. Furthermore, the inclusion of results on the larger ACE++ dataset demonstrates the scalability of the approach and its positive response to more training data.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written, and the model architecture is explained well with a helpful diagram (Figure 1). While the new dataset (ACE++) is not released, the authors improve reproducibility on the standard ACE benchmark by publishing the exact document splits they used for their experiments, a commendable practice that is often overlooked.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core CNN architecture (word + position embeddings, convolution, piecewise max-pooling) is heavily inspired by, and very similar to, prior work in relation classification (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). While applying this architecture to event linking is a valid contribution, the paper presents it as a novel design without sufficiently situating it in the context of this closely related work. The contribution is more in the application and system integration rather than in the neural network design itself.
2.  **Ambiguous Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors acknowledge that their comparison to prior work is complicated by differing train/test splits and preprocessing pipelines (e.g., use of gold vs. predicted event arguments). This makes the claim of achieving SOTA performance less conclusive. While using gold arguments is a valid experimental choice to isolate the coreference task, it also overestimates performance in a realistic, end-to-end setting. A more detailed discussion of the potential impact of these differences would strengthen the paper's claims.
3.  **Insufficient Details on the ACE++ Dataset:** A significant portion of the analysis relies on the new ACE++ dataset. However, the paper provides minimal details about the 1351 additional documents: their source, domain, annotation process, annotation guidelines, and inter-annotator agreement are not described. This lack of information makes it difficult to fully assess the quality of the resource and the validity of the experiments conducted on it. Since the dataset is not made public, these details are crucial for the community to understand and trust the results.
4.  **Unclear Justification for Model Details:** The motivation for using a `square` activation function in the similarity layer is based on a vague "intuition" about "invariance under polarity changes." This is not clearly explained. The paper states that this setup outperformed others, but provides no empirical data or further explanation to support this claim, leaving it as an unsubstantiated design choice.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the rationale for using the `square` activation function in the similarity layer? What specific "polarity changes" are you referring to? Could you provide the results from your experiments with other activation functions (e.g., ReLU, tanh) that are mentioned in the paper to have been outperformed?
2.  Regarding the ACE++ dataset, could you provide more details about the source and genre of the 1351 additional web documents? What was the annotation procedure, and what were the inter-annotator agreement scores for the new event and coreference annotations?
3.  Your model learns representations for individual event mentions and then compares them. Have you considered or experimented with an architecture that processes the pair of event mentions and their sentences jointly (e.g., with a cross-sentence attention mechanism)? Such a model might be better at capturing the subtle relational cues needed for coreference, especially for the difficult cases identified in your error analysis.
