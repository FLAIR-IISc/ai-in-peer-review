**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of within-document event coreference resolution. The authors propose a two-stage model that first uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to learn a dense vector representation for individual event mentions based on their full sentential context. In the second stage, these learned representations are combined with standard pairwise features to train a classifier that predicts whether two event mentions are coreferent. The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of learned, sentence-level neural features to event linking, demonstrating that this approach can achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on the external, hand-curated semantic resources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet) that have been central to previous top-performing systems. A secondary contribution is the creation and use of a larger annotated dataset, ACE++, which provides further evidence for the model's effectiveness and serves as a valuable resource.

**Strengths**

1.  **A Bold and Promising New Direction:** The paper's most significant strength is its central thesis: that learned representations of sentential context can effectively replace the need for extensive feature engineering and external knowledge bases for event coreference. This is a bold claim that challenges the dominant paradigm in the field at the time. By showing that their relatively simple, resource-agnostic model can compete with and even exceed the performance of complex, feature-rich systems, the authors make a compelling case for the power of representation learning. This work opens up a new and important research direction, paving the way for more portable and scalable event processing systems.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The claims are well-supported by strong empirical evidence. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, which is impressive given its deliberate avoidance of the external semantic features used by its main competitors. Furthermore, the experiments on the larger ACE++ dataset show a significant boost in recall, demonstrating the model's ability to generalize and learn effectively from more data, which is a hallmark of successful neural approaches.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The authors provide more than just a single performance number. The ablation study in Table 5 is particularly effective, clearly isolating the contribution of each component (pairwise, local-lexical, and sentential features) and confirming that the novel sentential features are indeed critical to the model's success. The error analysis is also insightful, identifying hard remaining challenges like event pronominalization and highlighting potential annotation inconsistencies, which helps to contextualize the performance and provides clear avenues for future work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the *application* of CNNs to event linking is novel and impactful, the CNN architecture itself is a straightforward adaptation of models proposed in very recent work for relation extraction (e.g., Zeng et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). The paper presents the model clearly, but it could better position its architectural choices with respect to this prior work to more precisely delineate its specific design contributions beyond the novel application. However, given the strength of the core idea, this lack of deep architectural innovation is a minor concern.

2.  **Ambiguities in State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The authors are transparent about the caveats in their comparison to prior work, noting the differences in data splits and the use of gold versus predicted arguments. This is commendable, but it does slightly weaken the claim of definitively achieving state-of-the-art performance. While the results are clearly very strong and competitive, the comparison is not perfectly controlled. A re-implementation of a feature-based baseline on their exact experimental setup would have made the comparison iron-clad, but the current results are still compelling enough to support the paper's main conclusion.

3.  **Insufficient Justification for the Similarity Function:** The paper proposes using a squared activation function for its "distributed similarity" layer and notes empirically that it outperforms other choices. The intuition provided—"invariance under polarity changes"—is plausible but very brief. It is not immediately obvious why this specific non-linearity is superior to other standard options (e.g., ReLU) or more established similarity metrics (e.g., a learned bilinear mapping). A deeper analysis or justification for this architectural choice would strengthen the paper's methodological contribution.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more intuition or a more detailed analysis of the "distributed similarity" layer? Why do you believe the squared activation function works so well here compared to cosine similarity or a standard hidden layer? Have you analyzed the weights of `Wsim` to understand the different "similarity facets" it learns?

2.  Your error analysis correctly identifies event pronominalization (e.g., "It sickens me") as a major challenge. Given that your CNN-based features are derived from a single sentence, how do you see this approach evolving to handle such discourse-level phenomena? Do the learned features offer any inherent advantage or disadvantage for this specific problem compared to traditional feature sets?
