This paper tackles event linking, a crucial task for comprehensive information extraction. The core contribution, as I see it, is the demonstration that strong performance can be achieved *without* relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, a common bottleneck in many IE systems. Instead, the authors effectively leverage convolutional neural networks to extract sentential features, creating a self-contained system. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental validation on both the ACE 2005 and a larger, constructed dataset (ACE++), showing consistent improvements and providing insightful error analysis. Finally, the ablation studies, while not groundbreaking, offer useful information about the relative importance of different feature sets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The explicit focus on replacing external knowledge with learned sentential representations is a genuinely interesting and promising direction. Many IE systems are hampered by the limitations of curated resources; this work presents a viable alternative, opening the door to more adaptable and domain-independent systems. This is the strongest aspect of the paper.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The system achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE dataset and demonstrates good generalization to a larger dataset. The gains over existing methods, even if modest, are significant given the reliance on learned features rather than hand-engineered ones.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes ablation studies that shed light on the contribution of different feature types.  The detailed error analysis, particularly the identification of challenges related to pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies, is valuable and highlights areas for future research.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the model architecture, experimental setup, and results. The figures are helpful in visualizing the process.
5. **Dataset Creation:** The creation of the ACE++ dataset provides a valuable resource for the research community, addressing the limited size of the original ACE corpus.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Incrementalism:** While the *idea* of relying on learned sentential features is novel, the method itself – CNNs for feature extraction followed by logistic regression – feels somewhat conventional. It doesn't push the boundaries of neural network architectures or learning techniques. It’s a solid application of existing tools, but lacks a truly innovative methodological leap.
2. **Limited Error Analysis Depth:** While the error analysis identifies *what* errors the system makes, it doesn’t delve deeply into *why*. For instance, the pronoun resolution issue is noted, but there’s no discussion of potential strategies to address it, beyond suggesting further research into discourse structure.
3. **Clustering Strategy Sensitivity:** The results show that the choice of clustering strategy (transitive closure vs. BestLink) significantly impacts performance. The paper provides a reasonable explanation for why BestLink underperforms in this case, but it raises questions about the robustness of the system to different downstream processing steps.  More investigation into alternative clustering algorithms could be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to baselines, while present, feels a little weak.  A more direct comparison to a strong, publicly available baseline system trained on the same data would strengthen the claims of superiority.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention pronoun resolution as a significant source of error. Could you provide specific examples of the types of pronoun resolutions that are most challenging for your system?
2.  The architecture utilizes a relatively standard CNN. Have you experimented with more recent architectures, such as transformers, for extracting sentential features? If not, why?
3.  The ACE++ dataset is a valuable addition. Could you detail the annotation process for ACE++ and assess inter-annotator agreement?
4.  The choice between transitive closure and BestLink seems crucial.  Were any other clustering approaches explored?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the list of documents in ACEvalid/ACEtest is helpful.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   Consider briefly discussing the computational cost of the model, particularly the CNN component.




Overall, this is a solid and worthwhile contribution. While the methodology is not revolutionary, the core idea of circumventing reliance on external semantic resources is compelling and the experimental results are convincing. I lean towards **accepting** the paper, valuing the novelty of the approach and the potential impact of the findings. The identified weaknesses are not critical and could be addressed in future work.
