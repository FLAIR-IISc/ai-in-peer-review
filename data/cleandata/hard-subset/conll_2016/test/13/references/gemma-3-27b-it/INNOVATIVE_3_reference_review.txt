This paper tackles the important problem of event linking, aiming to resolve coreference between event mentions across sentences. The authors propose a system leveraging convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features, avoiding reliance on external semantic resources like WordNet and FrameNet. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the demonstration that strong performance on event linking *can* be achieved without these traditionally essential, but often domain-specific, resources. A secondary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation on two datasets (ACE and a larger ACE++), coupled with an insightful error analysis. Finally, the exploration of different clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) provides a useful comparative analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Resource Independence:** The core idea – achieving competitive event linking performance *without* relying on external lexical resources – is genuinely novel and significant. Many prior systems are hampered by the availability and coverage limitations of resources like WordNet. This work presents a compelling alternative.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The system achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on both datasets, convincingly demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The improvement on ACE++ is particularly encouraging, suggesting scalability.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis is insightful and goes beyond simply listing error types. Identifying the prevalence of pronoun resolution difficulties and highlighting discrepancies with human annotation reveal important challenges and avenues for future research. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem.
4. **Well-Designed Experiments:** The systematic ablation studies (Table 5) clearly illustrate the contribution of each feature class, providing valuable insights into the model’s behavior and informing future design choices.
5. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The inclusion of diagrams (Figure 1) aids in understanding the model architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Incrementalism:** While the core idea is novel, the underlying methodology (CNNs for feature extraction, logistic regression for classification) feels somewhat conventional. The paper doesn't introduce a breakthrough in *how* neural networks are applied; rather, it demonstrates a successful application to a specific task *with a specific goal* (resource independence). This isn’t necessarily a flaw, but limits the overall impact.
2. **Limited Exploration of Alternatives:** The paper focuses heavily on CNNs. A brief exploration of alternative neural architectures for sentential feature extraction (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) could strengthen the argument that CNNs are a particularly suitable choice.
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The ACE dataset is relatively small, which potentially limits the generalizability of the results. While the ACE++ dataset is larger, it is still constrained by its origin. Evaluating on more diverse datasets would bolster the claims of robustness.
4. **Over-reliance on Gold Event Mentions:** The use of gold event mentions simplifies the task and may mask difficulties in real-world scenarios where event extraction is imperfect. Investigating performance with automatically extracted event mentions would provide a more realistic assessment.
5. **Clustering Strategy Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of performance to the clustering strategy (BestLink vs. transitive closure) suggests the coreference decisions themselves are somewhat fragile. Further investigation into more robust clustering methods might be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the proposed system compared to methods relying on external semantic resources? A discussion of efficiency would be valuable.
2. You mention pronoun resolution as a major source of error. Have you considered incorporating specific features or techniques to address this challenge?
3. How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the word embeddings used? Did you experiment with different pre-trained embeddings or fine-tuning the embeddings during training?
4. The analysis suggests discrepancies between human annotation and model predictions. Could you provide more examples of such cases and discuss the potential reasons for these disagreements?



**Additional Comments:**

The citation style is slightly inconsistent (e.g., some entries include page numbers, others do not). Please review and standardize. The link to the ACEvalid/ACEtest document list is useful.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor methodological shortcomings, I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. The core idea of achieving strong performance without relying on external semantic resources is genuinely novel and the empirical results are convincing. The thorough evaluation and insightful error analysis further strengthen the paper's impact. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above. The novelty and potential for broader impact outweigh the methodological conservatism.
