## Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for event linking (coreference resolution between event mentions) that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event representations. The core idea is to move away from reliance on external semantic resources (WordNet, FrameNet) by encoding contextual information using CNNs. The system achieves state-of-the-art results on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), and the authors provide an error analysis.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates competitive performance on event linking, establishing a solid baseline for future work. Achieving state-of-the-art results on a challenging task is a significant accomplishment.
2. **Effective Use of Sentential Features:** The successful application of CNNs to generate sentential features for event representations is a key strength. This demonstrates the utility of learning contextualized event embeddings without relying on explicit semantic resources.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights into the remaining challenges in event linking, highlighting difficulties with pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement. This helps to point the community towards fruitful avenues for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation using two datasets, including a newly created, larger dataset (ACE++). The use of standard evaluation metrics and comparison to existing state-of-the-art systems provides a strong basis for assessing the performance of the proposed approach.  The ablation studies exploring the contributions of different feature sets are also well-executed.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Design:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind the design choices, emphasizing the limitations of relying on external knowledge sources and the potential benefits of learning contextualized representations.  The rationale for each component of the model is explained well.
3. **Practical and Domain-Independent Approach:**  The avoidance of external semantic resources makes the approach potentially more adaptable to different domains and languages. This is a significant advantage over methods that rely on domain-specific knowledge bases.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis is a strong point, going beyond simply reporting performance numbers to provide a deeper understanding of the model’s weaknesses. Identifying pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies as key error sources is particularly valuable.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide a link to the dataset split, aiding in reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** While the application of CNNs to event linking is sound, the core CNN architecture itself is not particularly novel.  The CNN setup (convolution, max-pooling) is a relatively standard approach in NLP. The paper’s primary contribution lies in *applying* this architecture effectively to the specific task of event linking, rather than introducing a fundamentally new technique.
2. **Insufficient Discussion of Alternative Architectures:** The paper mentions other neural architectures (RNNs, recursive networks) in the related work section but does not explore their potential benefits in the context of this task. A brief comparison, even if only in terms of computational cost or complexity, would strengthen the justification for the chosen CNN architecture.
3. **Clustering Strategy Limitations:** The chosen clustering strategy (transitive closure) seemingly underperforms compared to BestLink on ACE++, despite the reported findings in Liu et al. (2014). The reasons for this discrepancy are not fully explored, and further investigation into the optimal clustering method could improve performance.  The discussion feels a little dismissive of BestLink’s potential.
4. **Sensitivity to Annotation Quality:** The error analysis highlights the impact of annotation inconsistencies. While acknowledging this is important, the paper doesn’t explicitly address how the system might be made more robust to noisy annotations.  Exploring techniques like training with noisy labels could be beneficial.
5. **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While avoiding external resources is a benefit, the computational cost of CNNs (especially with larger vocabularies and document lengths) isn't fully addressed. A discussion of scalability concerns, particularly for very large datasets, would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Architectural Choices:** Why was a CNN specifically chosen over other neural network architectures (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) for generating sentential features? What were the trade-offs considered?
2. **Clustering Strategy:**  Can you elaborate on why the transitive closure strategy performed better in your experiments than BestLink, despite the findings reported by Liu et al. (2014)? Are there specific characteristics of your model or datasets that might explain this discrepancy?
3. **Robustness to Noise:** How might the system be made more robust to annotation errors? Have you considered techniques like training with noisy labels or incorporating confidence estimates into the coreference decisions?
4. **Scalability:**  What are the computational limitations of the current approach when applied to very large documents or datasets? Have you explored techniques for improving scalability, such as using smaller filter sizes or distributed training?
5. **Pronoun Resolution:**  Given that pronoun resolution is a significant source of error, do you have any preliminary ideas on how to integrate pronoun resolution techniques into the event linking pipeline?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
* The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
* The figures and tables are clear and informative.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. While the methodological novelty is somewhat limited, the empirical results are solid, the error analysis is insightful, and the approach is practical and domain-independent. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
