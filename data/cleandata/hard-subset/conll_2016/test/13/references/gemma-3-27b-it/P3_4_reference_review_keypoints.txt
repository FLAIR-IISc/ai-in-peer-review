- Summary: Proposes CNN-based sentential features for event linking to avoid external semantic resources, achieving SOTA on ACE and ACE++ with error analysis.  
- Contribution: Strong empirical performance establishing a solid baseline for event linking.  
- Contribution: Effective use of CNNs to learn contextualized event embeddings without external semantic resources.  
- Contribution: Detailed error analysis highlighting pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement.  
- Strength: Thorough experimental setup using two datasets (including ACE++), standard metrics, and ablation studies.  
- Strength: Clear, well-motivated model design and rationale for components.  
- Strength: Domain- and language-independent approach by avoiding external knowledge bases.  
- Strength: Insightful error analysis identifying key failure modes (pronouns, annotation inconsistencies).  
- Strength: Reproducibility aided by providing dataset splits.  
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—standard CNN architecture rather than a new technique.  
- Weakness: Insufficient exploration or empirical comparison with alternative architectures (RNNs, Transformers).  
- Weakness: Clustering strategy (transitive closure) underperforms on ACE++; reasons for discrepancy with prior work are not fully analyzed.  
- Weakness: Paper doesn’t address robustness to noisy or inconsistent annotations and potential mitigation strategies.  
- Weakness: Limited discussion of scalability and computational costs for large vocabularies/documents.  
- Question: Why choose CNNs over other architectures (RNNs, Transformers) and what trade-offs were considered?  
- Question: Why did transitive closure outperform BestLink in these experiments despite prior findings?  
- Question: How can the system be made more robust to annotation noise (e.g., noisy-label training, confidence estimates)?  
- Question: What are the computational limitations for very large datasets and possible scalability improvements?  
- Question: How might pronoun resolution techniques be integrated to reduce a major source of errors?  
- Additional: Paper is well-written, with comprehensive related work and clear figures/tables.  
- Overall: Strong, practical contribution with solid results and valuable analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
