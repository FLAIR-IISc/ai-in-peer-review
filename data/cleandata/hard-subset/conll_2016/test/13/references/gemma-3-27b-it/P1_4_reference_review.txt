## Peer Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for event linking (coreference resolution for event mentions) that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features representing individual event mentions. The system combines these CNN-derived representations with pairwise features for a coreference decision, avoiding reliance on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the ACE 2005 and a larger, newly created ACE++ dataset, alongside an error analysis to guide future work.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Sentential Feature Learning for Event Linking:** The core contribution is showing that CNNs can effectively capture sentence-level context to represent event mentions, enabling accurate event linking *without* relying on potentially limited external knowledge bases. This is valuable, particularly for domain adaptation scenarios.
2. **Creation and Release of the ACE++ Dataset:** The authors created and are making available a larger dataset (ACE++) for event linking. This dataset will be a valuable resource for the community, encouraging further research in this area.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation and Error Analysis:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the model on two datasets, alongside a detailed error analysis, offering insights into the challenges of event linking and potential areas for improvement.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive, and in some cases state-of-the-art, results on both ACE 2005 and ACE++. This provides concrete evidence that the proposed approach is effective.  The authors clearly present and compare their results to established baselines.
2. **Avoidance of External Resources:** The reliance on internal, learned features (sentential representations) is a significant advantage.  It makes the system more portable and less dependent on the availability and quality of external knowledge bases. This addresses a limitation identified in the introduction regarding prior work.
3. **Detailed Model Description:**  The paper provides a clear and well-structured description of the model architecture and training process, including the motivations behind specific design choices (e.g., the use of convolutional layers, pooling, and relative position embeddings). The inclusion of the equations aids in understanding the implementation.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis identifies key challenges such as pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies, providing valuable directions for future research.  Acknowledging the subjective nature of annotation and its impact on performance is commendable.
5. **Dataset Contribution:** The release of the ACE++ dataset is a substantial contribution to the community. Larger datasets are crucial for training robust and generalizable models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the CNN Architecture:** While the *application* of CNNs for event linking with sentential features is valuable, the CNN architecture itself is fairly standard and doesn't present a novel architectural contribution. The core innovation lies in the feature engineering and application to event linking, rather than the CNN itself.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Relation Extraction Models:** The related work section, while comprehensive, doesn’t sufficiently acknowledge or compare to more recent advances in neural relation extraction, which also utilize similar sentence-level representation learning techniques (e.g., transformers).  A discussion of how this approach differs or complements those techniques would be beneficial.
3. **Sensitivity to Clustering Strategy:** The results highlight sensitivity to the clustering strategy (BestLink vs. transitive closure). While the authors acknowledge this, the reasons for the observed performance differences aren't fully explored. Further investigation into the impact of different clustering algorithms is warranted.  The chosen transitive closure method appears suboptimal given the results.
4. **Ablation Study Could Be More Granular:** While the ablation study (Table 5) is helpful, it would be stronger with a more granular analysis. For example, exploring the impact of different convolutional filter sizes or pooling strategies could provide further insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Novelty of Architecture:** Given the relatively standard CNN architecture, could you elaborate on the specific design choices that were particularly crucial for the performance gains in event linking?
2. **Comparison to Transformer Models:** How do you anticipate your approach would compare to more recent transformer-based models for relation/event extraction, and what advantages might your approach offer in terms of computational efficiency or robustness?
3. **Clustering Strategy:** Why does BestLink perform worse than transitive closure in your experiments, and what properties of your model or dataset might explain this difference?  Would exploring more sophisticated clustering methods (e.g., graph-based clustering) be beneficial?
4. **Pronoun Resolution:** Given that pronoun resolution is a significant source of error, are there plans to integrate explicit pronoun resolution mechanisms into the model, or to explore data augmentation techniques to improve performance on these cases?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
* The inclusion of the document list for the ACEvalid/ACEtest splits is excellent for reproducibility.
* The code and data should be made available to the community to further facilitate research.




Overall, this is a solid contribution that demonstrates the effectiveness of sentential feature learning for event linking. While the CNN architecture itself isn't novel, the application to this task, coupled with the creation of a new dataset and comprehensive evaluation, makes this a worthwhile submission. Addressing the weaknesses and responding to the questions raised would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
