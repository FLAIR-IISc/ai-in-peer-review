## Peer Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for event linking (coreference resolution of event mentions) that leverages sentential features extracted using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The core idea is to move away from reliance on external semantic resources (like WordNet or FrameNet) which can be domain-specific and costly to maintain, and instead learn event representations directly from the surrounding text. The system operates in two stages: first, representing individual event mentions using CNNs on the sentences they appear in; second, using these representations and pairwise features for coreference decision making via logistic regression. The authors report state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE and a newly created ACE++) and provide an error analysis.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Event Linking without Extensive External Semantic Features:** The paper successfully demonstrates competitive event linking performance relying primarily on learned sentential features, showcasing a viable alternative to approaches heavily dependent on knowledge bases.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of Sentential Features for Event Linking:**  The thorough experimental setup, including ablation studies on feature classes, provides valuable insights into the contribution of sentential features and their interaction with other features for this specific task. 
3. **Creation and Release of the ACE++ Dataset:** The authors contribute a larger dataset (ACE++) which expands the available resources for event linking research. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results on ACE and ACE++ are competitive with existing state-of-the-art methods, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. While the margin isn't overwhelming, the removal of reliance on external resources is a significant advantage.
2. **Well-Defined and Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for using sentential features (avoiding costly external resources, capturing contextual information) and provides a detailed explanation of the model architecture and training procedure.  The initial analysis of ACE data to highlight the importance of cross-sentential information is also well done.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The ablation studies (Table 5) offer compelling evidence for the usefulness of each feature class, and the comparison with baseline models (Table 6) helps contextualize the performance gains.  The investigation of different clustering strategies is also valuable.
4. **Comprehensive Error Analysis:** The error analysis, although limited in scale (100 FPs/FNs), reveals key challenges in event linking (pronoun resolution, annotation inconsistency) and offers directions for future research.
5. **Publicly Available Dataset:** The release of ACE++ is a valuable contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Core Method:** While the application to event linking is valuable, the core CNN architecture for sentence representation isn’t particularly novel. The use of CNNs for sentence encoding is now quite common in NLP.  The primary novelty lies in applying this technique effectively to the specific task of event linking and demonstrating its competitiveness *without* external resources.
2. **Sensitivity to Clustering Strategy:** The performance dip when switching to BestLink clustering (Tables 4) suggests the model is sensitive to the choice of clustering algorithm. This warrants further investigation and perhaps the development of a more robust clustering component.  The authors’ explanation feels slightly dismissive; a deeper analysis of *why* BestLink hurts performance would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches:** While the paper compares to older methods, it doesn’t engage with more recent neural approaches to event linking or coreference resolution, such as those utilizing transformers (e.g., BERT) or more advanced attention mechanisms.  A comparison to these could better position the contribution of this work.
4. **Dataset Bias and Annotation Quality:** The error analysis highlights potential annotation inconsistencies, suggesting the quality of the datasets (especially ACE++) might influence the results. Further investigation into inter-annotator agreement could strengthen the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Clustering Sensitivity:** Can you elaborate on why BestLink clustering degrades performance? What specific types of errors does it introduce?
2. **Transformer Comparison:** Have you experimented with replacing the CNN with a transformer-based sentence encoder (e.g., using pre-trained BERT embeddings)? If so, how did it affect performance? If not, why not?
3. **Annotation Quality:** What is the inter-annotator agreement on ACE++? Are there specific types of events or scenarios where annotation disagreements are more frequent?
4. **Pronoun Resolution:** Given the challenges identified in the error analysis regarding pronoun resolution, have you considered incorporating explicit pronoun resolution techniques into your system?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   Figure 1 is helpful for understanding the model architecture.
*   The dataset statistics in Table 1 are useful.
*   The link to the dataset documents in footnote 2 is appreciated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. While the core method isn't groundbreaking, the successful application of CNNs for learning event representations *without* relying on external resources, the thorough evaluation, and the release of the ACE++ dataset make this a worthwhile publication. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
