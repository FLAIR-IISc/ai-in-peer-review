This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, aiming to avoid reliance on external semantic resources. The core idea is to represent event mentions using CNNs processing the surrounding sentence, and then use these representations, along with pairwise features, to determine coreference. The authors report state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE and ACE++) and provide an error analysis.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Use of Sentential Features:** The paper demonstrates a successful application of CNNs to generate sentential features for event linking, achieving competitive results without relying on external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet. This is a significant contribution, as it addresses a key limitation of prior work and promotes domain portability.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art (or competitive) performance on both the ACE and ACE++ datasets, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of ACE++ is valuable, providing results on a larger and more recent dataset.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights into the model’s weaknesses, specifically identifying challenges related to pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement. This helps guide future research directions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** While CNNs are increasingly used in NLP, applying them specifically to generate sentential features for event linking, and demonstrating its effectiveness, is a valuable contribution. The avoidance of external resources is a noteworthy advantage.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, including comparisons to several baselines and state-of-the-art systems, and reporting results on two datasets. The ablation studies in Table 5 effectively demonstrate the importance of each feature class.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model architecture, experimental setup, and results. The figures are helpful in visualizing the model’s components.
4. **Practical Relevance:** Event linking is a crucial task in information extraction with real-world applications, and improving its performance is directly beneficial.
5. **Detailed Motivation:** The introduction effectively motivates the task and explains the limitations of prior work, clearly establishing the need for the proposed approach. The numbers presented regarding the percentage of incomplete event mentions are compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the authors mention hyperparameter optimization, the details are somewhat sparse. A more detailed explanation of the search space, optimization strategy, and sensitivity to different hyperparameters would strengthen the paper. What was the effect of different embedding sizes, filter numbers, or window sizes?
2. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The baselines used, while standard, could be strengthened. For example, a simpler model utilizing pre-trained sentence embeddings (e.g., BERT, Sentence-BERT) as sentential features, combined with the pairwise features, could provide a more informative comparison point.  This would better isolate the contribution of the CNN architecture.
3. **Potential for Overfitting:** The ACE dataset is relatively small. While the authors address this by creating ACE++, more discussion on regularization techniques and their impact on generalization would be beneficial. The results on ACE++ are much stronger than on ACE, suggesting some potential for overfitting to the larger dataset.
4. **Clustering Strategy:** The discussion around the clustering strategy (transitive closure vs. BestLink) is somewhat confusing. The results seem to contradict previous work (Liu et al., 2014), but the explanation for this discrepancy is not fully convincing. A more in-depth analysis of why BestLink performs worse in this setting would be helpful.
5. **Error Analysis Depth:** While the error analysis is a strength, it could be further deepened.  Categorizing the errors more systematically (e.g., by event type, distance between mentions) might reveal more specific patterns and guide targeted improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what search space was explored, which optimization algorithm was used, and how sensitive were the results to different hyperparameter settings?
2. How would the performance of your model compare to a simpler baseline using pre-trained sentence embeddings (e.g., BERT) as sentential features, combined with the pairwise features?
3. Could you elaborate on why the BestLink clustering strategy performed worse in your experiments than the transitive closure approach, given that previous work (Liu et al., 2014) suggests the opposite?
4. Did you investigate the impact of different regularization techniques (e.g., dropout, L2 regularization) on the model’s performance and generalization ability, particularly on the smaller ACE dataset?
5. Could you provide a more detailed categorization of the errors identified in the error analysis, perhaps grouped by event type or distance between mentions?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "Figure 2" appears to be correct, but it would be helpful to explicitly state in the caption of Figure 2 what constitutes a "pair" of event mentions (e.g., all preceding mentions).
*   The link provided for the list of documents in ACEvalid/ACEtest is helpful.
*  Minor: "Section 3" used repetitively to refer to the model; consider replacing with "the model" for better flow.
* The writing is generally good, but there are instances where sentence structure could be improved for clarity.

**Overall:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. The approach is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results are promising. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the questions and suggestions raised in this review.
