## Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for event linking (coreference resolution) that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features representing event mentions. The system eschews external semantic resources (WordNet, FrameNet) and relies on a combination of local (trigger word) and sentential features, along with standard pairwise features, for the coreference decision. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art (or competitive) performance on two datasets (ACE and a larger, newly created ACE++) and provide an error analysis.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Effective Sentential Feature Learning for Event Linking:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that CNNs can learn useful sentential representations for event mentions, achieving strong performance *without* relying on potentially brittle and domain-specific external knowledge sources. This is a significant practical contribution.
2. **Creation and Release of the ACE++ Dataset:**  The authors’ contribution of a larger event linking dataset (ACE++) is valuable to the community, providing a more robust benchmark for future research. 
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The included error analysis provides useful insights into the remaining challenges in event linking, particularly the difficulty of pronoun resolution and inherent ambiguity in annotation.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on the ACE dataset, and demonstrably improves performance on the larger ACE++ dataset. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.  The careful ablation studies (Table 5) clearly show the contribution of each feature set.
2. **Novelty in Feature Engineering:**  While CNNs are becoming common in NLP, applying them specifically to *generate sentential features for event linking*, without relying on external resources, is a novel and worthwhile contribution. This contrasts with prior work heavily reliant on Lexical resources.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation, including a well-defined train/dev/test split, multiple baselines, and an ablation study of feature components. The hyperparameter tuning process is also reasonably well-described.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis is particularly strong. Identifying the challenges of pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies are crucial for guiding future research.  The examples provided are illustrative and valuable.
5. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model architecture, experimental setup, and results. The inclusion of figures helps to clarify the process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Core Methodology:** The core CNN architecture and training procedure are fairly standard.  The primary innovation lies in the *application* of these techniques to event linking with a specific focus on sentential features, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic approach. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but it reduces the overall impact.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:**  While comparisons to previous work are provided, several more recent and potentially stronger baselines utilizing transformer-based architectures (BERT, etc.) are missing.  It's unclear how the proposed model would perform against these more powerful models.  A comparison would strengthen the claims of state-of-the-art performance.
3. **Sensitivity to Clustering Strategy:** The results show a significant performance difference based on the clustering strategy (BestLink vs. transitive closure). The authors claim the model already filters low confidence links, making BestLink detrimental. However, this result feels somewhat counterintuitive and needs further investigation.  Why does the transitive closure lead to a better outcome in this particular case? More discussion on this is needed.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of the CNN model compared to simpler baselines. This is an important consideration for practical applications.  



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Transformer Baselines:**  How do you anticipate your model would perform compared to recent transformer-based models (e.g., BERT-based systems) applied to event linking?  Would you be willing to add a comparison to such a baseline?
2. **Clustering Strategy:** Could you elaborate on why you believe the transitive closure clustering method performs better than BestLink in your setting?  Perhaps provide additional examples where BestLink leads to incorrect clustering decisions?
3. **Computational Cost:** Can you provide an estimate of the training and inference time for your model, and compare it to the computational cost of the simpler baseline models (e.g., Liu et al., 2014)?
4. **Pronoun Resolution:** Given the identified challenges with pronoun resolution, are there any specific architectural changes or features you are considering to address this issue in future work?

**Additional Comments:**

*  It would be helpful to include a link to the ACE++ dataset in the camera-ready version.
*  The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive, especially regarding recent advances in neural relation/event extraction.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. The empirical results are strong, the methodology is sound, and the error analysis is insightful.  While the methodological novelty is somewhat limited, the practical impact of demonstrating effective sentential feature learning and the release of the ACE++ dataset are significant.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
