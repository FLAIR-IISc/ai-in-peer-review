This paper proposes a system for event linking leveraging convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The primary contribution, as presented, is achieving state-of-the-art performance on two event linking datasets without using knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet. A secondary contribution lies in the error analysis identifying challenges, particularly pronoun resolution. I would argue the most significant contribution is the demonstration that competitive performance *can* be achieved without extensive external lexical resources, a practical benefit for low-resource languages or specialized domains.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Resource Independence:** The avoidance of external semantic features is a genuine strength. Many event extraction and linking systems are heavily reliant on resources like WordNet which have limited coverage and require significant maintenance. This work demonstrates a viable alternative, which is valuable for broader applicability.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), providing a relatively comprehensive assessment. The inclusion of a second, larger dataset is commendable. The comparisons to existing state-of-the-art methods, while acknowledging caveats in comparisons, are reasonable.
3. **Detailed Model Description:** The architecture of the convolutional neural network and the feature engineering process are described with sufficient detail, allowing for reproducibility. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful in understanding the model's flow.
4. **Thoughtful Error Analysis:** The error analysis, while limited in scale (100 false positives/negatives), is insightful. Identifying pronoun resolution as a major challenge is important and points towards promising avenues for future research. The acknowledgment of annotator disagreement further contextualizes the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Innovation:** While achieving state-of-the-art results is positive, the core methodology—using CNNs to generate sentential features—builds heavily on established work (Zeng et al., 2014, and others cited). The novelty lies in *applying* this technique to event linking, but the foundational innovation is limited. The paper doesn’t present a fundamentally new architectural approach.
2. **Limited Ablation Study:** While Table 5 presents an ablation study of feature classes, it feels incomplete. The individual removal of features doesn’t fully dissect the interplay between them. A more systematic ablation, perhaps exploring different combinations, would be more informative. For example, is the improvement from sentential features dependent on the presence of lexical features?
3. **Dataset Bias and Generalizability:** The ACE corpus is a relatively small and well-studied dataset. While ACE++ expands the size, the authors don’t provide compelling evidence that the model will generalize well to different domains or text types. The performance on ACE++ doesn't dramatically surpass ACE, which raises concerns about the robustness of the gains.
4. **Clustering Strategy:** The decision to favor the transitive closure clustering strategy over BestLink, despite Liu et al. (2014) finding BestLink superior, feels insufficiently justified. The explanation provided (model already weeds out low-confidence links) is somewhat circular. More rigorous experimentation and analysis of the clustering process are needed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the pre-processing steps applied to the ACE++ corpus? Were any adaptations made to the annotation scheme or event types compared to ACE?
2. The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a key challenge. Have you considered incorporating explicit coreference resolution techniques *before* event linking, potentially as a pre-processing step?
3.  You mention that the model is not domain-specific. However, the datasets used (ACE/ACE++) are relatively news-oriented. What steps would be necessary to adapt the model to a substantially different domain, such as biomedical text or social media?
4. The paper refers to the use of Adam optimizer but does not specify the learning rate and other optimization parameters. Providing this information is critical for reproducibility.




**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are convoluted and could benefit from clarification.
* It would be helpful to include a discussion of computational costs (training time, inference time) for the proposed model.
* The reference to “square” as an activation function (line 338) is unclear and potentially misleading. It seems to refer to squaring the output of the preceding layer.
