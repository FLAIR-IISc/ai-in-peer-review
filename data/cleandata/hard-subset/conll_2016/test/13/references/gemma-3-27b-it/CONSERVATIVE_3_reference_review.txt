This paper proposes a system for event linking that utilizes convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The primary claimed contribution is achieving state-of-the-art performance on two event linking datasets without using resources like WordNet or FrameNet. A secondary contribution is an error analysis identifying areas for future research. As I see it, the most significant contribution lies in the thorough experimental evaluation and comparison against established baselines, demonstrating the viability of a CNN-based approach for event linking, even if the underlying architectural innovations are incremental.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Experimental Validation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), comparing the proposed model to several previously published methods. The results consistently demonstrate competitive, and in some cases superior, performance. This rigorous evaluation is a significant strength, particularly in a field where new methods often lack sufficient empirical grounding.
2. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the importance of event linking, supported by analysis of existing corpora demonstrating the frequency of cross-sentential dependencies. This effectively establishes the relevance and practical need for improved event linking techniques.
3. **Well-Established Methodology:** The core methodology – employing CNNs for feature extraction and a logistic regression classifier for coreference decisions – builds upon established techniques in natural language processing. While not radically novel, this pragmatic approach lends credibility to the results and facilitates reproducibility.
4. **Useful Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights into the limitations of the model, highlighting challenges such as pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement. This identifies promising directions for future work and demonstrates a critical self-assessment of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Innovation:** While the performance is competitive, the proposed approach doesn’t represent a substantial departure from existing methods. The core idea of using sentential features with CNNs has been explored in other relation/event extraction tasks (as acknowledged by the authors), and the application to event linking feels like a logical extension rather than a breakthrough. The novelty lies more in the specific application and thorough evaluation than in a fundamentally new technique.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Detail:** The description of hyperparameter tuning is somewhat brief. The authors mention a combined random sampling and line search strategy, but lack specifics about the ranges explored or the criteria used for selection. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the reported results and potentially undermines reproducibility.
3. **Limited Analysis of Feature Importance:** While the paper demonstrates the overall impact of different feature classes (pairwise, lexical, sentential), it lacks a more granular analysis of which specific features contribute most significantly to performance. A more detailed investigation of feature weights could provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and guide future development.
4. **Dataset Bias:** The ACE corpus, while widely used, is relatively small and may not be fully representative of real-world text. The reported improvements on ACE++ are encouraging, but further evaluation on more diverse datasets is needed to establish the generalizability of the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process, including the ranges explored for key parameters and the evaluation metric used for selection?
2. Can you share the relative importance of different features within the sentential feature representation (e.g., which convolutional filters are most active)?
3. Have you experimented with different neural network architectures beyond CNNs for generating sentential features (e.g., LSTMs, Transformers)? If so, what were the results?
4. Could you discuss the computational cost of your model compared to the baseline methods? Is there a significant trade-off between performance and efficiency?
