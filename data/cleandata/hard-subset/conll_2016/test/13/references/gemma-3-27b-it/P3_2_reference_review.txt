## Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for event linking (coreference resolution of event mentions) that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential feature representations of event mentions, circumventing the need for extensive external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The system combines these learned representations with pairwise features and a logistic regression classifier to determine coreference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive performance on two datasets (ACE and a newly created ACE++), and include an error analysis.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of effective event linking *without* reliance on external lexical resources:** This is the strongest contribution.  The paper successfully shows that strong performance in event linking can be achieved purely through learned representations from text, addressing a key limitation of many prior approaches.
2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on two datasets, including ablation studies examining the contribution of different feature sets. This provides a convincing picture of the model's strengths and weaknesses.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The inclusion of a manual error analysis, acknowledging challenges like pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, adds valuable insight and suggests directions for future work.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Feature Engineering:**  The core idea of utilizing CNNs to generate sentential representations for event linking is a valuable contribution.  While CNNs are widely used in NLP, applying them specifically to learn contextualized event representations for coreference resolution is a noteworthy approach, particularly as it avoids brittle external knowledge sources.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art (or very close to it) performance on ACE, and demonstrating improvements on the larger ACE++ dataset, establishes the effectiveness of the proposed method. The performance gains are significant enough to warrant attention.
3. **Well-Structured and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.  The model architecture is clearly explained with the help of the accompanying figures, and the experimental setup is detailed.  This facilitates reproducibility.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis reveals challenges in the task that go beyond model limitations – specifically, the inherent ambiguity and subjectivity in event coreference annotation.  Identifying pronoun resolution as a key failure point is particularly useful.
5. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of different feature classes (pairwise, trigger-local, sentential) are crucial for understanding the model's behavior and the importance of each component.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Architectural Exploration:** While the CNN architecture is well-explained, the paper lacks a deep exploration of alternative neural architectures (e.g., RNNs, Transformers). Given the success of Transformers in capturing long-range dependencies, it’s unclear why they weren’t considered.  The justification for using CNNs (handling multiple event mentions per sentence) seems weak, as other architectures could likely address this as well.
2. **Over-reliance on ACE:** The ACE dataset is relatively small. While the introduction of ACE++ is appreciated, the paper would benefit from evaluation on a more diverse and larger dataset to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach. The results on ACE++ are encouraging, but a more substantial testbed is needed.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Recent End-to-End Models:** The related work section mentions various event extraction and coreference models, but it doesn't adequately address more recent end-to-end neural models that jointly perform event extraction and linking.  Comparing against these would better contextualize the contribution of this work.
4. **Suboptimal Clustering Strategy:** The finding that BestLink performs *worse* than the transitive closure method is surprising, given previous literature.  The explanation provided (model already filters low-confidence links) is plausible but requires more investigation.  A more detailed analysis of why BestLink fails in this specific context would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Architectural Choices:**  Why were CNNs specifically chosen over other neural architectures like RNNs or Transformers? Have you experimented with these alternatives, and if so, what were the results?
2. **Generalizability:**  Do you have plans to evaluate the model on other datasets beyond ACE and ACE++? If so, which datasets are you considering and what challenges do you anticipate?
3. **Pronoun Resolution:**  Given that pronoun resolution is a major source of error, have you considered incorporating specific features or techniques to address this challenge?  (e.g., coreference resolution for entities within the sentence).
4. **BestLink Performance:** Could you provide more detailed results analyzing the behavior of BestLink? For example, what types of errors does BestLink introduce compared to the transitive closure method?  Perhaps an analysis of the confidence scores assigned by the model.
5. **End-to-End Comparison:**  Could you discuss the challenges of comparing your model to end-to-end event extraction and linking systems, and why a direct comparison wasn't included in the evaluation?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper’s notation is generally clear, but some equations (e.g., Equation 2) could benefit from more explicit explanation of the variables.
* The link to the list of ACEvalid/ACEtest documents is helpful for reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. The demonstrated ability to achieve strong performance without relying on external resources is particularly significant. While there are areas for improvement (architectural exploration, dataset diversity, comparison to end-to-end models), the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions and concerns raised above.
