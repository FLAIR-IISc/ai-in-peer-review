This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, aiming to achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The core idea is to represent event mentions using both local (trigger word and neighbors) and sentential features, combined with pairwise features for coreference decision-making. The authors claim to achieve state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE and ACE++) and provide an error analysis to identify areas for future work.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of a competitive event linking system that effectively utilizes CNN-derived sentential features, eliminating the need for potentially domain-specific and costly external semantic resources. A secondary, though valuable, contribution lies in the empirical analysis comparing the performance impact of different feature combinations and clustering strategies (BestLink vs. transitive closure). Finally, the thorough error analysis provides useful insights into the challenges of event linking, particularly regarding pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves competitive results on the ACE and ACE++ datasets, matching or surpassing previous state-of-the-art systems, particularly considering its independence from external knowledge bases. This is a significant accomplishment, demonstrating the effectiveness of the chosen approach. The detailed results in Table 3 and subsequent ablation studies (Table 5) convincingly support this claim.
2. **Effective Use of CNNs for Sentential Features:** The architecture effectively leverages CNNs to capture contextual information around event mentions. The design choices, such as separate pooling for left and right context and the inclusion of trigger-local features, are well-motivated and contribute to the model's performance.  The clear explanation and visualization in Figure 1 aid understanding.
3. **Comprehensive Ablation Study:** The ablation study (Table 5) clearly demonstrates the contribution of each feature class (pairwise, lexical, sentential) to the overall performance. This provides valuable insights into the model's behavior and confirms the importance of all components, particularly the sentential features.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The authors conducted a manual error analysis on a sample of false positives and false negatives. The insights gained from this analysis, specifically highlighting the difficulties with pronoun resolution and the presence of annotation inconsistencies, are valuable for guiding future research. Recognizing the inherent ambiguity in the task and acknowledging limitations is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of CNNs to event linking is sensible, the overall architecture is relatively standard. The CNN itself utilizes common techniques (convolution, max-pooling) and the subsequent coreference decision-making relies on a logistic regression classifier. The novelty primarily lies in the combination of these elements for the specific task of event linking with a focus on sentential features, rather than introducing a fundamentally new architectural component.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The ACE corpus, while widely used, is relatively small. The authors address this by creating ACE++, but the increased size alone may not fully address potential biases or limitations of the data. While the performance gains on ACE++ are encouraging, further evaluation on more diverse datasets is necessary to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.
3. **Sensitivity to Clustering Strategy:** The contrast between the results obtained using transitive closure and BestLink (Table 4) indicates a sensitivity to the clustering strategy. The authors' justification for preferring transitive closure is reasonable, but the performance difference warrants further investigation. A more robust clustering approach might be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to existing state-of-the-art systems is provided, some of these systems are relatively old. Including comparisons to more recent neural network-based approaches, even if they utilize external knowledge sources, would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed model’s competitiveness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the annotators sometimes disagreed on whether event mentions were coreferential. Could you provide more details on the nature of these disagreements, and how they might affect the evaluation of different systems?
2.  The error analysis highlights the difficulty of resolving pronouns. Have you considered incorporating any specific mechanisms for pronoun resolution into your model, such as coreference resolution modules or attention mechanisms?
3.  Regarding the sensitivity to the clustering strategy, did you experiment with other clustering algorithms besides BestLink and transitive closure? If so, what were the results?
4.  While you avoid external knowledge bases, were any pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec) used? If so, how might the choice of embedding model affect the results?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The figures are helpful in illustrating the model architecture and experimental setup.
* The inclusion of both ACE and ACE++ datasets is a positive aspect.
* Providing the link to the ACEvalid/ACEtest document list is a good practice.
