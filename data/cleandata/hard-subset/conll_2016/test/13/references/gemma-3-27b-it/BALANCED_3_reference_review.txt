This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, avoiding reliance on external semantic resources. The authors claim to achieve state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, and provide an error analysis to identify directions for future work. The primary contribution is the demonstration of strong event linking performance using solely local and sentential features learned via CNNs, eliminating the need for expensive and potentially limited external knowledge sources. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical analysis of error cases, highlighting challenges like pronoun resolution. Finally, the creation and use of the ACE++ dataset represents a valuable addition to the field, providing a larger training corpus.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance on both ACE and the newly introduced ACE++ dataset, as shown in Table 3. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The performance gains on ACE++, particularly the improvement in recall, are encouraging and suggest the benefits of a larger training dataset.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of utilizing CNNs to learn sentential features for event linking, and achieving competitive performance *without* external resources, is a significant strength. This distinguishes the work from many previous approaches that heavily rely on resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The focus on learning representations from the text itself is a valuable direction.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies (Table 5) to assess the contribution of different feature classes. This helps to understand the importance of each component of the model and provides insights into the learned representations.  The comparison against various baselines (Table 6) is also well-executed and contextualizes the performance of the proposed system.
4. **Useful Dataset Contribution:** The release of the ACE++ dataset is a valuable contribution to the community.  Providing a larger dataset facilitates further research and development in event linking and related tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in CNN Architecture:** The CNN architecture itself is relatively standard and lacks significant novelty. While effective, the core innovation lies in the *application* of CNNs to this specific task and feature engineering (sentential features), rather than a novel CNN design.  The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of why this specific architecture was chosen and how it is particularly well-suited to event linking.
2. **Error Analysis Depth:** While the error analysis is a positive aspect, it could be more in-depth. Identifying pronoun resolution as a major challenge is useful, but a more detailed categorization of error types and a quantitative breakdown would provide stronger insights. Furthermore, providing concrete examples alongside the analysis would make it easier to understand the specific difficulties the model faces.
3. **Clustering Strategy Sensitivity:** The results demonstrate sensitivity to the clustering strategy (Table 4), with BestLink leading to decreased recall. While the authors explain this, it raises questions about the robustness of the overall system. A more robust clustering approach or further investigation into why BestLink performs poorly in this setting would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Neural Models:** The "Related Work" section references several recent neural network architectures (recursive, recurrent networks) for relation classification, but doesn’t compare against implementations of these on event linking. Including such a comparison would strengthen the positioning of the work within the current landscape of neural NLP.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper states that annotators disagreed on the coreference link for some examples. Could you provide more details on the inter-annotator agreement for the ACE++ dataset, and how these disagreements might influence the evaluation results?
2.  You mention that the model struggles with pronoun resolution. Have you considered incorporating coreference resolution *before* event linking, to address this challenge?
3.  Regarding the sensitivity to the clustering strategy, can you elaborate on the characteristics of the errors introduced by using BestLink? Are there specific types of event pairs that are more prone to being misclassified when using this strategy?
4.  Given the success of the sentential features, have you explored incorporating attention mechanisms within the CNN to focus on the most relevant parts of the sentence for event linking?

**Additional Comments:**

* The figure captions are somewhat sparse and could benefit from more detail explaining the specific components and data flow.
* The motivation for choosing specific hyperparameter settings could be elaborated upon.
* Consider providing a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the model.
