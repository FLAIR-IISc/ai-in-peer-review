## Peer Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for event linking (coreference resolution) that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions. The system eschews reliance on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet, instead relying on a combination of local, sentential, and pairwise features. The authors report state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE 2005 and a larger, newly created dataset, ACE++. They also include an error analysis to identify future research directions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Competitive Performance Without External Resources:** The paper successfully demonstrates that strong event linking performance can be achieved without relying on expensive and domain-specific external knowledge bases. This is a significant practical contribution, making the approach more portable and scalable.
2. **Empirical Evaluation on a Larger Dataset (ACE++):** The introduction of the ACE++ dataset provides a more substantial testbed for event linking research, potentially leading to more robust and generalizable models.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:**  The manual error analysis offers valuable insights into the challenges of event linking, highlighting specific error types (pronoun resolution, annotation disagreement) that warrant further investigation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:** The system achieves competitive results on both ACE 2005 and ACE++, surpassing previous state-of-the-art on some metrics. This suggests the proposed approach is effective and a viable alternative to methods relying on external resources.
2. **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The paper clearly articulates the motivations behind the model’s architecture, particularly the use of CNNs for sentential feature extraction and the avoidance of external knowledge bases. The analysis of the datasets (percentage of coreferential pairs sharing no arguments, distances between mentions) justifies the design decisions effectively.
3. **Thorough Evaluation and Ablation Studies:** The authors conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of different feature classes (pairwise, local, sentential). This demonstrates a good understanding of the model’s inner workings and the importance of each component.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The figures and tables are helpful in understanding the model architecture and experimental results.
5. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis is particularly strong, pinpointing specific limitations of the model and highlighting the challenges inherent in the task, such as pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement.  This goes beyond simply reporting numbers and provides valuable direction for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** The use of CNNs for sentence representation is not particularly novel in the context of information extraction. While the application to event linking is valuable, the core technical contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The novelty lies more in the combination of techniques and the successful application to this specific task without external resources.
2. **Dataset Details & Public Availability:** While the creation of ACE++ is a positive contribution, the paper doesn’t explicitly state if and when ACE++ will be publicly available.  Making the dataset accessible to the research community is crucial for reproducibility and further advancements.
3. **Clustering Strategy Sensitivity:** The results show sensitivity to the clustering strategy (transitive closure vs. BestLink). The authors adequately acknowledge this, but the reasons for the observed difference aren’t fully explored.  A deeper understanding of why BestLink performs poorly in this specific setup would be beneficial.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While the authors mention a hyperparameter search, the details are somewhat limited.  Providing more specifics about the search space and optimization process would improve reproducibility.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Availability:** Will the ACE++ dataset be made publicly available? If so, when?
2. **BestLink Performance:** Can you elaborate on why the BestLink clustering strategy performed worse than transitive closure in your experiments? Do you have any hypotheses about the interaction between your model and this strategy?
3. **Pronoun Resolution:** The error analysis highlights pronoun resolution as a significant challenge.  Have you considered any specific techniques to address this issue, such as incorporating coreference features from a separate entity coreference system?
4. **Hyperparameter Search:** Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter search space and optimization process? For example, what search strategy (e.g., random search, grid search) was used, and how many configurations were evaluated?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The references section is comprehensive.
*   The choice of evaluation metrics is appropriate for the task.
*   Consider providing a qualitative example of a successful and unsuccessful linking decision, showcasing the strengths and weaknesses of the model.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. While the core method isn’t highly novel, the demonstration of competitive performance without relying on external resources, the creation of a larger dataset, and the insightful error analysis make this a worthwhile submission. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
