This paper presents a system for event linking leveraging convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features for event mentions, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The primary claimed contribution is achieving state-of-the-art performance on two event linking datasets without utilizing resources like WordNet or FrameNet. A secondary contribution is an error analysis identifying areas for future work. I would characterize the main contribution as a reasonably competent application of established CNN techniques to a specific task, rather than a significant methodological advancement. The empirical results are solid, but the innovation is incremental.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Performance:** The model achieves competitive results on the ACE and ACE++ datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness. The comparison to existing state-of-the-art systems, while noting caveats regarding experimental setup differences, is generally favorable. This indicates a practical value to the approach.
2. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of event linking and provides supporting statistics highlighting the prevalence of cross-sentential dependencies in event information. The motivation for reducing reliance on external resources is also well-stated.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The paper details the datasets used, model architecture, hyperparameters, and training procedure. The ablation studies (Table 5) examining the impact of different feature classes are particularly valuable, providing insights into the model's behavior.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis, while limited in scope (100 false positives, 100 false negatives), reveals challenging cases, such as pronoun resolution, and identifies potential directions for future research. The acknowledgement of annotation inconsistencies is a sign of careful analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core technical approach – using CNNs to generate sentential representations – is not novel. This technique has been extensively used in relation extraction and similar NLP tasks. The paper doesn’t introduce any fundamentally new architectural components or learning algorithms. It's an application of existing techniques, not a breakthrough.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to previous work is incomplete. While acknowledging differences in setup, the paper doesn’t thoroughly address these discrepancies. Furthermore, the comparison lacks a strong, easily reproducible baseline. A simpler, non-neural baseline (e.g., a feature-based model with a linear classifier) would provide a clearer indication of the benefits of the proposed approach.
3. **Over-Reliance on ACE Dataset:** The reliance on the relatively small ACE dataset (599 documents) raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. The ACE++ dataset is a step in the right direction, but its creation and properties aren't discussed in sufficient detail. Is it representative of broader text domains?
4. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the paper mentions a search over hyperparameters, the specifics of this process are not detailed. It's unclear how the final hyperparameter settings were chosen and whether the search was comprehensive enough. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed description of the ACE++ dataset, including its source and any potential biases it may introduce?
2. What was the specific search strategy used for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., grid search, random search, Bayesian optimization)?
3. Could you include a comparison to a simpler, non-neural baseline (e.g., a logistic regression model with hand-crafted features) on both datasets to better isolate the contribution of the CNN-based approach?
4. The error analysis highlights difficulties with pronoun resolution. Have you considered incorporating specific features or techniques to address this challenge?

**Additional Comments:**

The figure captions are often very verbose and interrupt the flow of reading.
The references section is extensive, but a few are quite dated (e.g., Swampillai and Stevenson, 2010). It would be beneficial to focus on more recent and relevant work.
