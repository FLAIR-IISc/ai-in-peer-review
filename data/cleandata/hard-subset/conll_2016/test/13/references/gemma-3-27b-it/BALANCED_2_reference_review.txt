This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, aiming to achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources. The core idea is to represent event mentions using both local (trigger word and neighboring words) and sentential features, combined with standard pairwise features for coreference decisions. The system is evaluated on the ACE 2005 and a larger ACE++ dataset.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Use of Sentential Features for Event Linking:** The primary contribution lies in demonstrating that CNNs can effectively capture sentential context to improve event linking performance, achieving competitive results *without* relying on external knowledge bases like WordNet or FrameNet. This is a valuable result, as it offers a path towards more portable and domain-independent event linking systems.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the ACE 2005 dataset and presents solid results on the larger ACE++ dataset, indicating the robustness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies on feature classes are well-conducted and insightful.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The thorough error analysis, highlighting challenges like pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, provides valuable insights for future research directions. This level of analysis strengthens the paper and contributes to a better understanding of the task itself.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of event linking and motivates the need for methods that don’t depend on potentially unavailable or limited external resources. The statistical support for the importance of event linking using ACE data is helpful.
2. **Well-Designed Model:** The two-step architecture – generating event representations using CNNs and then making coreference decisions – is logically sound and effectively implemented. The explanations for design choices (e.g., dual pooling, relative position embeddings) are clear and well-justified.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons to state-of-the-art systems, ablation studies to assess the contribution of different feature classes, and an evaluation on two datasets. The comparison of different clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) is also insightful.
4. **Thorough Error Analysis:** As mentioned above, the error analysis is a significant strength. Identifying specific error cases, like pronoun resolution, provides concrete guidance for future work. Recognizing the impact of annotator disagreement is also commendable.
5. **Reproducibility:** Providing the list of documents used for the dev/test splits enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN Architecture:** While the *application* of CNNs to event linking is a reasonable approach, the CNN architecture itself isn't particularly novel. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of *why* this specific CNN architecture is well-suited for capturing the relevant information for event linking, relative to other possible neural network architectures (e.g., RNNs).
2. **Dependence on Gold Event Mentions:** The reliance on gold event mentions limits the practical applicability of the system. Event linking is often part of a larger information extraction pipeline, where event *detection* is also performed automatically and subject to errors. The paper should acknowledge this limitation and potentially discuss how the system might be adapted to handle imperfect event mentions.
3. **Feature Engineering:** While avoiding external knowledge bases is a strength, the pairwise features still require significant feature engineering.  A discussion of the rationale behind the chosen pairwise features and potential avenues for automating this process would be valuable.  Are there more learnable approaches to capture these pairwise relationships?
4. **Lack of Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of training and deploying the model, which could be a concern for large-scale applications.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was a CNN chosen over other neural network architectures like RNNs or Transformers for capturing sentential features? Did you experiment with other architectures, and if so, what were the results?
2.  How sensitive is the model performance to errors in event mention detection?  Have you considered any techniques to make the system more robust to imperfect event mentions?
3.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind the chosen pairwise features? Are there any plans to explore learnable approaches for capturing pairwise relationships between event mentions?
4.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time, memory usage) of the model, and how does it scale with the size of the input document?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "Section 3" throughout the error analysis is a bit redundant.
*   The formatting of Table 5 could be improved for readability.
*   The discussion of related work is somewhat limited.  A more comprehensive overview of recent advances in event linking and coreference resolution would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. The proposed approach is effective, the experiments are comprehensive, and the error analysis is insightful. While the novelty of the CNN architecture is limited and the reliance on gold event mentions is a practical concern, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses and questions raised above. The paper has the potential to stimulate further research in this important area.
