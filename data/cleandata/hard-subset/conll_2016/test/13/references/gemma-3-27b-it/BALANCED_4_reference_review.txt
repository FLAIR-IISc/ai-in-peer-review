This paper addresses the important task of event linking, aiming to resolve coreferences between event mentions within a document. The authors propose a system leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, combined with pairwise features for coreference determination. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the problem, proposed method, experimental setup, and results.

**Summary of the Paper & Contributions:**

The paper tackles event linking, motivated by the observation that a significant portion of event information requires cross-sentential reasoning. The authors present a two-step model: (1) representing individual event mentions using CNNs to integrate sentential features, and (2) learning coreference decisions based on these representations and pairwise features. The key contribution lies in demonstrating that strong performance on event linking can be achieved *without* relying on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of errors made by the system.  A second, less prominent contribution is the empirical comparison of different clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) for resolving coreference decisions. Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets (ACE and a larger, newly created ACE++) and ablation studies are a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance & Novelty in Feature Engineering:** The authors achieve competitive, and in some cases superior, performance on the ACE dataset without relying on external semantic resources. This is a significant strength, as these resources can be domain-specific and expensive to acquire and maintain. The use of CNNs to generate sentential features is a sensible and well-motivated approach.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies to assess the contribution of different feature classes. The inclusion of the larger ACE++ dataset is particularly valuable, as it provides a more robust test of the model's generalization ability.  The analysis of the impact of different clustering strategies is also insightful.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, clearly explaining the problem, the proposed method, and the experimental setup. The figures and tables are informative and easy to understand. The motivation for design choices is well articulated.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights into the limitations of the current approach and suggests directions for future research. Identifying the difficulty of pronoun resolution and the impact of annotation inconsistencies are particularly useful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Architecture:** While the application to event linking is valuable, the core architecture (CNNs for sentence representation, followed by logistic regression) is fairly standard and lacks significant novelty.  The authors acknowledge this, but the contribution relies heavily on the specific application and the avoidance of external resources.
2. **Dataset Bias & Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The ACE dataset is relatively small and may not fully represent the diversity of real-world text. While the ACE++ dataset is larger, both datasets focus on a specific type of event (terrorism, movement, death).  The generalization ability of the model to other event types and domains remains unclear.  The ACE corpus is also known to have annotation inconsistencies, as highlighted in the error analysis, which can artificially limit performance.
3. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Neural Models:** The comparison to related work is somewhat dated. More recent advances in neural networks, such as transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa), could potentially offer significant improvements in performance and haven’t been explored.  The authors briefly mention other relevant approaches in the related work section, but do not compare against them experimentally.
4. **Clustering Strategy Sensitivity:** The observed difference in performance between transitive closure and BestLink clustering, with BestLink performing worse than expected, requires further investigation. The authors provide a possible explanation, but a more thorough analysis of the specific cases where BestLink fails would be beneficial.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Transformer-based Models:** Have you experimented with using pre-trained transformer models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) to generate the event representations?  If not, can you comment on the potential benefits and challenges of doing so?
2. **Annotation Consistency:** Given the identified annotation inconsistencies in ACE/ACE++, how might these inconsistencies be addressed to improve the reliability of the evaluation?  Could a more robust evaluation metric be used that is less sensitive to these inconsistencies?
3. **Pronoun Resolution:**  The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a significant challenge. What strategies might be employed to specifically address this issue, such as incorporating coreference features or using a dedicated pronoun resolution module?
4. **Domain Generalization:**  How do you anticipate the model performing on datasets with different event types or from different domains?  Are there specific modifications that might be necessary to improve domain generalization?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of URLs for datasets (e.g., "https://git.io/vwEEP") is helpful.
*   The detailed discussion of hyperparameters and the training process is commendable.
*   Figure 1 provides a clear visual overview of the model architecture.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. The authors demonstrate that strong performance can be achieved without relying on external semantic resources, and the comprehensive experimental evaluation and insightful error analysis are commendable. While the core architecture lacks significant novelty, the application to event linking and the thorough analysis justify acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions raised above and potentially include a brief discussion of the potential benefits of using more recent neural models.
