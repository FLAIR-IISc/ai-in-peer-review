This paper tackles event linking, a crucial but often overlooked component of information extraction. The core idea – leveraging sentential features via CNNs to bypass reliance on external semantic resources – is genuinely interesting and represents the paper's primary contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough experimental validation on two datasets, demonstrating competitive performance. Finally, the detailed error analysis, while not groundbreaking, adds valuable insights into the challenges of event coreference.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The central premise – replacing traditional, often domain-specific, semantic features (WordNet, FrameNet) with learned sentential representations – is a bold and compelling move. This has the potential to significantly broaden the applicability of event linking systems to new domains without extensive feature engineering. This is a substantial shift in thinking.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art (or competitive) performance on both ACE and ACE++ is a clear positive. While the gains aren’t massive, demonstrating that a purely learned representation can perform *at least* as well as systems heavily reliant on hand-crafted features is significant.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting results. The ablation studies (Table 5) dissecting the contribution of different feature sets are valuable, and the comparison of clustering strategies (Table 4) demonstrates a thoughtful approach to evaluation.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis, identifying common failure modes (especially pronoun resolution), isn't just a recitation of mistakes. It grounds the limitations in the inherent difficulties of the task and suggests concrete directions for future work.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the model is described in detail, and the experiments are presented in a straightforward manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Nature of CNN Application:** While the *idea* of using CNNs for sentential features is novel in this context, the CNN architecture itself is fairly standard. The paper doesn't explore particularly innovative CNN configurations or address challenges specific to event linking – it's a relatively 'off-the-shelf' application. The novelty resides more in *where* it’s being applied than *how*.
2. **Limited Analysis of Negative Results:** The ablation study provides insights into feature importance, but doesn't delve deeply into *why* certain features are less effective. A more nuanced analysis of these results would be beneficial. For example, why does adding pairwise features *increase* precision at the cost of recall?
3. **Dataset Limitations:** ACE is a relatively small dataset. While ACE++ is a welcome addition, the authors acknowledge the potential for bias in the annotation process (Table 6). Further validation on larger, more diverse datasets would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
4. **Dependence on Gold Event Mentions:** The reliance on gold event mentions simplifies the task considerably. Real-world applications require robust event *extraction* as a prerequisite, and errors in event detection will inevitably propagate to the linking stage. Evaluating the system end-to-end with a full IE pipeline would provide a more realistic assessment of its performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. You mention the challenges of pronoun resolution in the error analysis. Have you experimented with incorporating coreference resolution for entities *before* event linking, potentially providing additional context for resolving pronoun references to events?
2. The model seems sensitive to the clustering strategy (BestLink vs. transitive closure). Could you elaborate on the trade-offs between precision and recall in this context, and why your system doesn’t benefit from BestLink as observed in previous work?
3. While the sentential features remove reliance on external knowledge bases, they require substantial training data. How do you envision adapting this approach to low-resource scenarios where labeled data is scarce? Have you considered transfer learning or data augmentation techniques?
4. Could you comment on the computational cost of the convolutional layers compared to feature engineering approaches? Is the increased computational complexity a significant drawback?

**Additional Comments:**

* The list of documents used for the ACEvalid/ACEtest splits should be readily available online – this is excellent practice.
* The related work section is comprehensive, but could benefit from a more focused discussion of recent advances in neural relation extraction, as this field is closely related to event linking.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a promising approach. While not revolutionary in its technical details, the shift away from hand-crafted features and towards learned representations is a significant step forward. I lean towards acceptance, believing the novelty of the core idea and the strong empirical results outweigh the relatively minor weaknesses. The authors should address the questions above during the rebuttal period to further strengthen their claims.
