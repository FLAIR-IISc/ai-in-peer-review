This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, avoiding reliance on external semantic resources like WordNet or FrameNet. The core idea is to represent event mentions using both local features and the semantics of the surrounding sentence, combining these with pairwise features for coreference decisions. The authors claim state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE and ACE++) and provide an error analysis to suggest future research directions.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that competitive event linking performance can be achieved *without* relying on extensive external semantic features. While the use of CNNs for feature extraction isn’t novel in itself, its application to event linking and the resulting performance, coupled with the avoidance of external resources, is a significant achievement. A secondary contribution is the detailed experimental analysis on the ACE and ACE++ datasets, including the impact of different feature combinations and a thoughtful error analysis. The error analysis provides useful insights into remaining challenges and potential avenues for improvement, specifically highlighting difficulties with pronoun resolution. A third contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental setup and clear reporting of results, enabling comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance & Resource Independence:** The reported state-of-the-art performance on ACE, without relying on external knowledge bases, is a key strength. This addresses a common limitation of previous approaches – their dependence on resources that may not be available for all domains. The achieved F1 score on the BLANC metric is competitive and suggests the method is robust.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides a well-structured experimental section. The ablation studies (Table 5) are particularly valuable, clearly demonstrating the contribution of each feature class (pairwise, local, sentential) to the overall performance. This analysis provides useful insights for future model development.
3. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis is a significant strength. Identifying the difficulties with pronoun resolution and instances of annotator disagreement provides concrete directions for future work and highlights the inherent complexities of the task. It's valuable that the authors acknowledge the limitations of the gold standard data.
4. **Clear Model Description:** The model architecture is described clearly, with a helpful figure illustrating the two-step process and the flow of information. The mathematical formulation of the key components (convolution, pooling, similarity calculation) is also presented effectively.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors make the list of documents in the validation/test splits available, which aids reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the combination of CNNs and event linking is effective, the individual components (CNNs, sentential features) are not novel in themselves. The primary novelty lies in the *application* of these techniques to event linking and demonstrating competitive performance without external resources, which, while valuable, is an incremental advancement.
2. **Limited Comparison Depth:** The comparison with prior work (Table 3) is somewhat limited by the variations in experimental setups (e.g., different train/test splits, use of gold argument information). A more direct comparison, controlling for these factors, would strengthen the claim of state-of-the-art performance.
3. **Clustering Strategy Sensitivity:** The observation that BestLink clustering performs *worse* than transitive closure is counterintuitive, given findings in related work (Liu et al., 2014). While the authors offer a plausible explanation, further investigation into this discrepancy would be beneficial. It warrants a deeper dive into why BestLink fails to improve performance in this specific context.
4. **Hyperparameter Optimization Detail:** While the authors mention a combined strategy of random sampling and line search for hyperparameter optimization, more details about the specific hyperparameter ranges explored and the criteria used for selection would increase transparency and reproducibility.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a significant challenge. Have you considered incorporating coreference resolution techniques specifically designed for pronouns as a pre-processing step, or as additional features in your model?
2.  The results show that BestLink clustering performs worse than transitive closure. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors that BestLink introduces, compared to transitive closure, and why you believe this is happening in your specific setting?
3.  You mention the potential for fine-grained discourse information to improve performance. Could you elaborate on what types of discourse features you envision incorporating and how they might address the identified challenges?
4. The paper highlights a noticeable disagreement in annotation, could you provide more details on the types of scenarios where annotators disagree?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation (e.g., the use of subscripts like 'i:i+n−1') is occasionally dense and could be simplified for clarity.
*   Consider adding a discussion of computational cost and scalability of the proposed method.
*   The reference to "Araki and Mitamura (2015)" appears multiple times. Consider whether this reference is consistently the most relevant one in each context.
