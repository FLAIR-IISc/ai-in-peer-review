## Peer Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for event linking (coreference resolution of event mentions) that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event representations. The model eschews reliance on external semantic resources like WordNet and FrameNet, instead combining local and sentential features with pairwise features for a coreference decision. The authors report state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE and a newly created ACE++) and provide an error analysis.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Event Linking without External Lexical Resources:** The paper successfully demonstrates competitive performance on event linking using solely learned representations from CNNs and readily available data, bypassing the need for potentially domain-specific and costly external knowledge bases. This is a practical and valuable result.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation and Analysis:** The thorough experimental setup with two datasets, ablation studies regarding feature combinations, and insightful error analysis contribute significantly to understanding the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.
3. **Dataset Extension (ACE++):** The creation and release of the ACE++ dataset expands the available resources for event linking research, which is beneficial to the community.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive results on the ACE dataset, matching or exceeding previous state-of-the-art systems, and shows consistent improvement on the larger ACE++ dataset. This validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Well-Motivated Design:** The design choices are clearly motivated by observations about the challenges of event linking – the frequent lack of shared arguments between coreferential mentions and the importance of sentential context. This grounding in empirical analysis strengthens the work.
3. **Detailed Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the importance of each feature class (pairwise, lexical, and sentential) and provide valuable insights into the model's behaviour. These results are well-presented and contribute to a deeper understanding of the system.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis, despite being limited in sample size, identifies key challenges like pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, providing directions for future research.
5. **Clear and Thorough Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written, logically organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. Figures and tables are clear and helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Method:** While the application to event linking is valuable, the underlying methodology (using CNNs for sentence representation and logistic regression for classification) is not particularly novel. The core innovation lies in *applying* these well-established techniques effectively to this specific task, rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach.  This diminishes the overall impact.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons to previous work are included, the baselines are somewhat dated.  More recent and competitive baselines, especially those leveraging pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) for event representation, would provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the model’s performance.  Simply stating that external resources are avoided doesn’t necessarily justify not comparing against a strong current model.
3. **Sensitivity to Clustering Strategy:** The paper acknowledges the negative impact of BestLink clustering on recall. The explanation offered feels somewhat weak. A deeper investigation of *why* BestLink degrades performance is needed and potentially an exploration of alternative clustering strategies (beyond transitive closure).
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of training and inference.  Given the use of CNNs and potential for long sequences, a discussion of efficiency would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Pre-trained Language Models:** Have you considered incorporating pre-trained language models (like BERT or RoBERTa) into your model, either for generating word embeddings or directly as sentence encoders? How do you anticipate such additions would impact performance and computational cost?
2. **Clustering Strategy:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* BestLink clustering negatively impacts recall?  Are there specific types of event coreference errors that are exacerbated by this strategy?  Have you explored other clustering algorithms beyond transitive closure and BestLink?
3. **Pronoun Resolution:** Given the identification of pronoun resolution as a major source of error, do you have any preliminary ideas on how to incorporate information specifically targeted at resolving pronominal references within the event linking framework?
4. **Computational Efficiency:** Can you provide a rough estimate of the training and inference time for your model on the ACE++ dataset?  How does the model scale with document length?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the ACEvalid/ACEtest document lists is appreciated.
*   Consider including a table summarizing the hyperparameter settings used in the final model.
*  A brief discussion of the limitations of ACE and ACE++ in terms of domain coverage or event types would be useful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. While the core methodology lacks significant novelty, the successful application to this task, comprehensive evaluation, and insightful analysis warrant acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly incorporating stronger baselines and exploring alternative clustering strategies, would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
