This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The primary claimed contributions are: 1) a novel system design integrating CNNs for intra-sentential event modeling and pairwise features for coreference; 2) achieving state-of-the-art performance on two datasets; and 3) an error analysis identifying future research directions.

I believe the primary contribution of this work lies in the detailed engineering and comprehensive evaluation of a CNN-based approach to event linking, rather than a fundamentally novel architectural idea. While CNNs for sentence representation are not new, their application specifically to event linking, coupled with the systematic experimentation and ablation studies, provides a valuable empirical baseline. The datasets used and the thoroughness of the evaluation are also strengths.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a wide range of experiments, including ablation studies (Table 5) examining the contribution of different feature classes, and comparisons against existing state-of-the-art methods (Table 3). This demonstrates a strong commitment to empirical validation.
2. **Detailed Model Description:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the model architecture (Figure 1) and training process. The inclusion of mathematical formulations (equations 1-5) enhances reproducibility.
3. **Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights into the challenges of event linking, particularly the difficulty of resolving pronoun references and the inherent ambiguity in annotation guidelines. This section demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the limitations of the approach.
4. **Performance on ACE++:** The presented results on the ACE++ dataset, while not drastically surpassing prior work, establish a strong baseline for future research on a larger and more representative corpus. The creation and use of ACE++ itself is a positive contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Architecture:** The core architectural component—using CNNs for sentence representation—is not novel. The application to event linking is a valuable extension, but doesn't constitute a breakthrough in model design. The authors should temper their claim of a "novel system design".
2. **Over-Reliance on ACE Dataset:** The initial comparison to state-of-the-art is largely on the relatively small ACE 2005 dataset. While ACE++ is introduced, the conclusions drawn from the ACE experiments should be presented with greater caution. The performance gains on ACE++ are more convincing, but the context of its size relative to other potential datasets is missing.
3. **Suboptimal Clustering Strategy:** The authors acknowledge that their transitive closure clustering strategy performs worse than BestLink in prior work (Liu et al., 2014). Despite this, they continue to use transitive closure, claiming recall benefits. This seems counterintuitive and requires further justification. The explanation provided isn’t compelling, and the impact of this decision on overall performance should be more thoroughly investigated.
4. **Insufficient Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The description of hyperparameter optimization is brief. While a "combined strategy of random sampling and line search" is mentioned, the specific ranges explored and the justification for the chosen hyperparameters are lacking. This impacts reproducibility. Table 2, listing the final settings, is missing from the submission.
5. **Minor Formatting and Consistency Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are inconsistencies in the presentation of tables and figures. The captioning is sometimes ambiguous. Equation (2) is not clearly explained in the text. The use of the term “trigger-local” is vague and could be clarified.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the ranges explored for each parameter and the rationale behind the chosen settings? Please include Table 2 in the revised submission.
2.  Why did you choose to use transitive closure clustering despite the findings of Liu et al. (2014) suggesting that BestLink performs better for event linking? What specific analysis led you to the conclusion that transitive closure is more suitable for your model?
3.  The error analysis highlights the difficulty of pronoun resolution. Are there any specific architectural modifications or feature additions you are considering to address this challenge?
4.  The paper mentions the lack of completeness of EventCorefBank. How does the coverage of event types in ACE and ACE++ compare to other potential datasets? Would expanding to a more diverse dataset affect the performance of your model?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to “Figure 2” in Section 3.3 is missing the figure itself.
*   The link provided in footnote 2 is broken.
*  Clarify the meaning of "trigger-local" features. Is it just the trigger word and its immediate neighbours?

Overall, this paper presents a solid and well-executed study. However, the claims of novelty are overstated, and the weaknesses regarding the clustering strategy and hyperparameter tuning need to be addressed. A revised version addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution. I lean towards rejection due to the aforementioned issues, but would reconsider with substantial revisions.
