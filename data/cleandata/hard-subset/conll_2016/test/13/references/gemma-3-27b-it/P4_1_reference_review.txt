This paper addresses the task of event linking, aiming to resolve coreferences between event mentions within a document. The authors propose a system leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, combined with pairwise features for coreference decision making. The key contribution is the application of CNNs for sentential feature extraction within an event linking system, achieving state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE and ACE++) without relying on external semantic resources. A secondary contribution is the detailed error analysis, identifying challenges like pronoun resolution and highlighting potential areas for improvement. A third, though less impactful, contribution is the empirical investigation of the impact of various feature combinations and clustering strategies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE dataset and competitive performance on the larger ACE++ dataset. This is a significant accomplishment, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach and the value of sentential features. The comparison to existing methods provides a solid basis for evaluating the contribution.
2. **Novel Application of CNNs:** Utilizing CNNs to learn sentential features specifically for event linking represents a novel application within the field. While CNNs are increasingly used in NLP, their tailored application to generate contextualized event representations is a noteworthy contribution. The justification for employing CNNs, based on recent trends in relation/event extraction, is well-articulated.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation & Analysis:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation, considering different feature combinations and clustering strategies. The error analysis is particularly valuable, providing insights into the limitations of the model and suggesting directions for future work. Identifying pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies as key error sources demonstrates a good understanding of the task's challenges.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a logical flow from introduction to experimental results. The model architecture is explained in detail, supported by a helpful diagram (Figure 1). The motivation for design choices is also well presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Method:** The core methodology – using CNNs for feature extraction – is not entirely novel in NLP. The specific application to event linking *is* novel, but the underlying CNN architecture and training procedure are relatively standard. The paper would benefit from further highlighting *how* the CNN is adapted and optimized for this particular task, rather than simply stating that it's applied.
2. **Dataset Size & Generalizability:** While the ACE++ dataset is a valuable addition, the overall dataset sizes remain relatively small. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other domains or datasets with different characteristics. Further evaluation on more diverse datasets would strengthen the claims.
3. **Performance Gains – Incremental rather than Transformative:** The performance improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods are incremental rather than a substantial leap. While achieving SOTA is a positive outcome, the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a paradigm shift in event linking performance. The gains could be, in part, attributable to better hyperparameter tuning or experimental setup, rather than solely the proposed method.
4. **Insufficient Discussion of Pronoun Resolution:** While the error analysis correctly identifies pronoun resolution as a major challenge, the paper lacks a discussion of potential solutions or future work specifically addressing this issue. Providing some preliminary ideas for tackling pronoun-related errors would significantly strengthen the contribution.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific choices made in the CNN architecture (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters) and the rationale behind them?
2. Beyond the mentioned error analysis, did you investigate the impact of specific pairwise features on the model's performance? Were any features found to be particularly informative or redundant?
3.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding types or training embeddings from scratch?
4. The ACE++ dataset seems to improve performance, but can you provide more detail about the characteristics of the additional web documents and how they differ from the original ACE corpus?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper references “Figure 2” in the text, but there doesn't seem to be a Figure 2.
*   The mention of “Section 3” repeatedly referring to the model design is slightly redundant and could be streamlined.
*   Providing ablation studies with different pooling mechanisms (max-pooling vs. average-pooling) could give additional insight into their respective benefits.
