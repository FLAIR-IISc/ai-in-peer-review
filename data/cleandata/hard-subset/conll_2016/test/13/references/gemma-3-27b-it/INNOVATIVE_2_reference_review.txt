This paper presents a system for event linking leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions. The core idea is to move away from reliance on external semantic resources like WordNet and FrameNet, instead encoding event context directly from the surrounding text. The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on two datasets, ACE and ACE++, and provide an error analysis.

The primary contribution is the demonstration that strong event linking performance *can* be achieved without heavy reliance on traditional external knowledge sources. The shift towards purely data-driven, neural representations of event context is a valuable direction. A secondary, though significant, contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation on both the standard ACE dataset and a larger, newly created ACE++ dataset. The error analysis, while not deeply insightful, is a helpful attempt to pinpoint remaining challenges.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The deliberate avoidance of external semantic features in favor of learned sentential representations is genuinely interesting. While CNNs are common in NLP, their application to *event linking* specifically using this sentential feature engineering is a relatively fresh angle. This focus on learning contextual representations is crucial, as dependence on curated resources limits scalability and domain adaptation.
2. **Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on ACE and demonstrating competitive results on the larger ACE++ dataset provides strong evidence for the viability of the approach. The performance gains over existing systems – even if incremental – validate the core hypothesis.
3. **Dataset Contribution:** The creation and release of the ACE++ dataset is a positive addition to the community. A larger, more diverse dataset will facilitate further research in event linking and enable more robust model evaluation.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the model architecture and experimental setup. The motivation for design choices is well articulated, and the inclusion of diagrams (Figure 1) aids understanding.
5. **Thoughtful Error Analysis:** The error analysis, despite its limitations, demonstrates an attempt to understand the weaknesses of the system and provides direction for future work (specifically, challenges with pronoun resolution).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the approach is *interesting*, the novelty isn't groundbreaking. CNNs for sentence representation are well-established. The key contribution is the application to event linking and the deliberate exclusion of external resources, but this feels more like a strategic choice than a fundamentally new technique. The core CNN architecture itself isn't pushing any boundaries.
2. **Limited Ablation Study:** While Table 5 attempts an ablation study, it doesn’t dissect the impact of individual CNN parameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters) or architectural choices. A more granular analysis of the CNN components would provide a deeper understanding of what's driving the performance.
3. **Clustering Strategy:** The choice of transitive closure for event clustering seems somewhat arbitrary. The authors acknowledge that BestLink performed better on some metrics in previous work, but dismiss it based on their results. A more comprehensive comparison, potentially with other clustering strategies, would be beneficial.
4. **Error Analysis Depth:** The error analysis provides a high-level overview of common errors, but lacks detailed examples or quantitative analysis of error types. For instance, quantifying the prevalence of pronoun resolution failures would strengthen the argument.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Comparing against simpler, yet competitive baselines (e.g., a simple cosine similarity between event type embeddings and argument overlap) more explicitly would better contextualize the performance gains.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific hyperparameter tuning process used? You mention random sampling and line search, but more details on the search space and optimization criteria would be helpful.
2.  The paper briefly mentions other neural architectures (RNNs, Recursive Networks). Did you experiment with any of these, and if so, why did you choose CNNs over them?
3.  You dismiss BestLink based on your experiments. Could you provide the specific performance numbers for BestLink on ACE++ (including precision, recall, and F1) to justify this decision?
4.  What is the computational cost of your model, both in terms of training time and inference time, compared to some of the feature-based baseline methods?



**Additional Comments:**

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a worthwhile contribution to the field. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the practical results and the creation of a new dataset justify acceptance.
