## Review for "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid, well-executed approach to event linking leveraging convolutional neural networks for sentential feature extraction. While not groundbreaking in terms of novel algorithmic ideas, the strong empirical results on two datasets, coupled with a thorough analysis, make a compelling case for acceptance. The focus on removing reliance on external knowledge resources is a valuable contribution, particularly given the domain-specificity issues with such resources.

**Summary of Paper & Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art (or competitive) performance on two event linking datasets (ACE and ACE++), validating the proposed approach.
2. **Effective Use of Sentential Features:** The successful integration of sentential features, derived via CNNs, for event linking is a significant practical contribution. This shows the power of learning contextual representations without relying on hand-crafted features or external knowledge bases.
3. **Comprehensive Analysis:**  The paper includes a useful error analysis, identifying common failure cases (pronoun resolution) and providing insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the model. This enhances the value of the work beyond simply reporting performance numbers.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline and Competitive Results:** The paper clearly establishes a competitive baseline by comparing against previous state-of-the-art systems on the standard ACE dataset. Achieving competitive or improved performance demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The additional evaluation on the larger ACE++ dataset further strengthens this claim.
2. **Practicality and Portability:**  The avoidance of external semantic resources is a major strength.  Many existing event linking systems rely on resources like WordNet or FrameNet, limiting their applicability to domains where these resources are less comprehensive or unavailable. This work offers a more adaptable solution.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors provide a detailed description of their experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies (Table 5). This transparency allows for reproducibility and facilitates further research.  The consideration of different clustering strategies (BestLink vs. transitive closure) and their impact is also commendable.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis goes beyond simply stating performance numbers.  Identifying specific error types (pronoun resolution, annotation discrepancies) offers valuable direction for future research and improvements.  Acknowledging the inherent ambiguity in the task itself (due to annotation disagreements) demonstrates critical self-awareness.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The architecture (Figure 1) is well-illustrated, and the problem definition is effectively contextualized.




**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** The use of CNNs for sentence representation is well-established in NLP.  While the application to event linking is effective, the core architectural components lack significant novelty. The paper’s contribution lies more in the *application* and *evaluation* of these components within the specific context of event linking, rather than a breakthrough in model design.
2. **Dependence on Gold Event Mentions:** While acknowledged, the reliance on gold event mentions may inflate performance. Event extraction (trigger identification and argument classification) is a challenging task in itself. The performance of the linking system is contingent on accurate initial event mention detection. Evaluating the end-to-end pipeline with a full information extraction system would provide a more realistic assessment.
3. **Pronoun Resolution Challenge Remains:** The error analysis highlights the difficulty of resolving pronoun references to previous event mentions. While identified as a weakness, the paper doesn’t propose any specific solutions or future work to address this limitation.  Exploring techniques specifically designed for coreference resolution involving pronouns would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:**  The paper doesn’t provide any details on the computational resources required for training and inference.  Given the use of CNNs and large datasets, this information would be valuable for assessing the practical feasibility of the approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **End-to-End Evaluation:** Have you considered evaluating your event linking system in an end-to-end pipeline, integrating it with a separately trained event extraction system? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
2. **Pronoun Resolution Strategies:**  Are you planning to explore specific techniques for addressing the pronoun resolution challenge identified in your error analysis?  Could you elaborate on potential approaches?
3. **Computational Resources:** Could you provide an estimate of the computational resources (e.g., GPU hours, memory requirements) required for training and inference with your model on the ACE++ dataset?
4. **Error Analysis Granularity:** Could you provide more specific details on the types of event triggers where the system struggles most frequently?  Is there a particular semantic class of events that presents a greater challenge?



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is extensive and well-maintained.
* The provided link to the ACEvalid/ACEtest document lists is helpful for reproducibility.



**Recommendation:**  **Accept** with minor revisions.  The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of event linking, and the identified weaknesses are not severe enough to warrant rejection. Addressing the questions above in the author response period would further strengthen the paper.
