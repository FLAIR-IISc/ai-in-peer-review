## Review of "Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks"

This paper presents a system for event linking that utilizes convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate event representations based on sentential features, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a system for event linking employing CNNs for intra-sentential event representation, (2) a coreference model leveraging these representations combined with pairwise features, and (3) achieving state-of-the-art performance on two datasets with an accompanying error analysis. 

However, while the paper presents a functional system and a detailed experimental setup, the contributions feel incremental rather than groundbreaking. The core idea of using sentential features is not novel, building upon established work, and the specific CNN architecture and feature combinations appear relatively standard. The primary contribution resides in the thorough experimental evaluation and the analysis of errors, providing a solid benchmark and insights into the challenges of event linking.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper demonstrates a rigorous experimental setup, evaluating the model on two datasets (ACE and ACE++) with a well-defined train/test split. The ablation studies (Table 5) are particularly valuable, offering a clear understanding of the contribution of each feature class. This allows for a fair comparison with existing methods and provides insights into the model's behavior.
2. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis section provides valuable qualitative insights into the types of errors the model makes, identifying challenging cases like pronoun resolution and inconsistencies in annotation. This highlights areas for future research and demonstrates a thoughtful understanding of the limitations of the approach.
3. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a clear and detailed description of the model architecture, training process, and experimental setup. This allows for easy reproducibility and understanding of the work.
4. **Solid Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to existing state-of-the-art systems, while acknowledging caveats in experimental settings, demonstrates competitive performance. The inclusion of a discussion regarding the potential influence of varying setups and annotation inconsistencies adds to the credibility of the evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Method:** The core technical contribution – utilizing CNNs to encode sentential features for event linking – feels evolutionary rather than revolutionary. The CNN architecture is fairly standard, and the use of sentential features is already established in the literature. The paper doesn't present a significantly new way of leveraging these established techniques.
2. **Overly Detailed Feature Engineering:** The paper spends considerable space detailing feature engineering (e.g., pairwise features, trigger-local features). While thorough, this level of detail feels somewhat at odds with the stated goal of reducing reliance on manually engineered features. A more streamlined or automatically learned feature representation might have been more compelling.
3. **Inconsistent Justification for Design Choices:** Some design choices appear arbitrary or lack strong justification. For example, the choice of ReLU activation in the convolutional layer (Equation 1) and the square function in the similarity calculation (Equation 4) are presented with limited explanation. A deeper exploration of alternative activation functions or similarity measures could strengthen the work.
4. **Minor Formatting and Typographical Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, varying spacing around equations). Although minor, these issues detract from the overall polish of the manuscript. Additionally, some of the table captions are incomplete or unclear.
5. **Overclaiming State-of-the-Art:** The claim of achieving "state-of-the-art performance" should be tempered. While the results are competitive, the performance gain over existing methods is not substantial, and the caveats surrounding dataset variations and experimental setups are acknowledged. 



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the ReLU activation function and the square function in Equations 1 and 4, respectively? Were alternative functions considered, and if so, what were the results?
2.  The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a significant challenge. Have you considered incorporating techniques specifically designed for coreference resolution involving pronouns (e.g., using pronoun-specific features or incorporating a separate pronoun resolution module)?
3.  The paper mentions exploring different clustering strategies (BestLink vs. transitive closure). Could you provide the statistical significance of the observed differences in performance between these strategies?
4.  Given the observation that over 50% of coreferential event mentions share no arguments, did you investigate methods to better capture semantic similarity beyond argument overlap?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please review the paper carefully for typographical errors and formatting inconsistencies.
*   In Table 1, clarify what ACE++ consists of (e.g., "ACE 2005 plus 1351 web documents").
*   The link to the ACEvalid/ACEtest document list is broken: https://git.io/vwEEP. Please provide a valid link.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper is well-written and presents a thorough experimental evaluation, the lack of substantial novelty and the presence of minor flaws, including inconsistencies and a somewhat overstated claim of state-of-the-art performance, outweigh the strengths. Addressing the weaknesses and responding thoughtfully to the questions would significantly improve the paper, but as it stands, it does not represent a significant contribution to the field.
