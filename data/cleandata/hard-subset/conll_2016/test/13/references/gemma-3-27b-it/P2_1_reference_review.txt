## Review of “Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks”

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid and well-executed approach to event linking, leveraging recent advancements in neural networks, specifically convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for feature extraction. The work achieves strong results on established datasets and offers a valuable contribution to the field. While not groundbreaking in terms of novel algorithmic ideas, the successful application of sentential features and the thorough experimentation make this a worthwhile contribution for acceptance.

**Summary & Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of Sentential Features:** The primary contribution is the successful integration of sentential features, derived via CNNs, into an event linking system. This demonstrates the effectiveness of these features for capturing contextual information crucial for resolving event coreference without relying heavily on external knowledge sources.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the ACE 2005 and ACE++ datasets, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Thorough Error Analysis:** The paper includes a detailed error analysis that identifies key challenges in event linking, such as pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies, providing valuable insights for future research.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of event linking and motivates the use of sentential features as a way to overcome limitations of previous approaches (reliance on external resources, low performance). The justification for design choices, like the CNN architecture and the two-step process, is well-explained.
2. **Strong Experimental Evaluation:**  The experiments are comprehensive, including comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and ablation studies examining the contribution of different feature classes. The use of two datasets (ACE and ACE++) adds to the robustness of the findings.  The analysis of clustering strategies (BestLink vs. transitive closure) is also insightful.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis goes beyond simply reporting numbers. The authors delve into the *types* of errors the system makes, offering valuable qualitative insights and highlighting areas for improvement (e.g., handling pronoun resolution, addressing annotation ambiguity). This demonstrates a deep understanding of the challenges in event linking.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide details regarding hyperparameter settings and data splits, enhancing the reproducibility of their work. (mention of published test/valid splits is excellent)
5. **Practical Significance:** By reducing reliance on external knowledge sources, the proposed method has the potential to be more easily applicable to new domains and languages where such resources are limited.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Core Method:** The use of CNNs for feature extraction and the overall architecture (two-step process, pairwise features) are not entirely novel. The key innovation lies in the application to event linking and the specific implementation of sentential features, but the underlying concepts are well-established.  This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but it limits the potential impact of the work.
2. **Dependence on Gold Event Mentions:**  While the authors acknowledge this, the reliance on gold event mentions simplifies the task.  A more realistic evaluation would involve integrating the event linking system with a potentially noisy event extraction module.  This would better reflect real-world applications.
3. **Dataset Size Limitations:** The ACE 2005 dataset is relatively small. While ACE++ mitigates this somewhat, the results might not generalize well to significantly different datasets with different characteristics.  Exploring performance on larger, more diverse datasets would be valuable.
4. **Analysis of Pronoun Resolution is Limited:** While the error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a major challenge, it doesn't delve into potential solutions or strategies to address this specific issue. Further exploration of techniques for handling coreference involving pronouns would strengthen the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the use of pre-trained word embeddings. Did you experiment with fine-tuning these embeddings during training, and if so, what impact did it have on performance?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of your approach, particularly the CNN-based feature extraction step? How does it compare to methods relying on hand-crafted features?
3.  You found BestLink performed worse than transitive closure for your model. Do you have any hypotheses about why this difference exists compared to Liu et al.’s findings? Could it be related to the specific characteristics of your features or model architecture?
4.  Given the challenges with pronoun resolution identified in the error analysis, do you have any plans to integrate techniques specifically designed to handle pronominal coreference into your system?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The inclusion of related work is adequate and demonstrates a good understanding of the field.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameter settings used in the experiments for easier reproducibility.




**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**  This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of event linking. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (particularly exploring the limitations of using gold event mentions and delving further into pronoun resolution) would further strengthen the work. The strengths of the paper—strong empirical results, thorough error analysis, and a clear presentation—outweigh the limitations.
