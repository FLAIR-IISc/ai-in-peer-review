This paper presents a system for event linking that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions. The core idea is to avoid reliance on external semantic resources by learning event representations directly from the surrounding context. The primary claimed contributions are: (1) a CNN-based system for event linking utilizing sentential features; (2) achieving state-of-the-art performance on two datasets; and (3) an error analysis identifying directions for future research.

**Contributions (as perceived by the reviewer):**

The most significant contribution is the demonstration of competitive event linking performance *without* relying on extensive external semantic features like WordNet or FrameNet. While the CNN architecture itself is not novel, applying it specifically to event linking with a focus on sentential features, and achieving strong results, is a worthwhile contribution. The empirical evaluation, including comparison to existing state-of-the-art systems, constitutes a second, notable contribution. Finally, the error analysis provides valuable insights into the challenges of event linking and highlights areas for future investigation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive performance on the ACE and ACE++ datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods provides a clear benchmark and validates the approach's viability. The detailed results in Table 3 and Table 4 are helpful for understanding the performance across different metrics and datasets.
2. **Elimination of External Resources:**  The avoidance of external semantic resources is a significant strength. This makes the system more portable and applicable to domains where these resources are unavailable or incomplete. This is well motivated in the introduction, citing the limitations of prior work.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The paper details a thorough experimental setup, including dataset splits, hyperparameter tuning, and ablation studies.  The ablation studies (Table 5) effectively demonstrate the contribution of each feature class.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis is a valuable addition. Identifying common error cases, such as pronoun resolution, provides concrete directions for future work. The comparison of model errors with human annotation discrepancies is particularly insightful.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is clearly explained with the help of Figure 1, and the problem definition is concise and understandable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methodology:** The use of CNNs for sentence-level feature extraction is not novel in itself. The core novelty lies in the application to event linking and the focus on sentential features, which, while valuable, is incremental. The paper would benefit from more clearly positioning its contribution relative to broader trends in neural network applications to NLP.
2. **Dataset Size Limitations:** The ACE dataset is relatively small, which might limit the generalizability of the results. While ACE++ is introduced to address this, the improvement in performance could be even more substantial with larger datasets.
3. **Clustering Strategy Impact:** The results regarding the clustering strategy (BestLink vs. transitive closure) are somewhat contradictory to findings in related work (Liu et al., 2014). This discrepancy requires further investigation and a more detailed explanation within the paper. Why did BestLink *hurt* performance in this case?
4. **Lack of Discussion on Computational Cost:** The paper does not provide any information on the computational cost of the model, which could be a concern for real-world applications.  Training and inference time, as well as memory requirements, should be discussed.
5. **Pronoun Resolution as a Recurring Error:** While the identification of pronoun resolution as a key error source is valuable, the paper doesn’t delve into potential solutions or related work addressing this challenge within the context of event linking.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why the BestLink clustering strategy performed worse than transitive closure in your experiments, given that previous work (Liu et al., 2014) suggests the opposite? What specific characteristics of your model or dataset might explain this difference?
2.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time, memory usage) of your model, and how does it compare to other state-of-the-art event linking systems?
3.  Have you considered incorporating techniques specifically designed to address pronoun resolution in the context of event linking? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
4. The error analysis shows disagreement with human annotators in some cases. Could you provide more details on the nature of these disagreements and how they might inform future annotation guidelines or model development?
