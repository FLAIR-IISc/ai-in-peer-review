This paper presents a system for event linking that utilizes convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, avoiding reliance on external semantic resources. The system achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets (ACE and ACE++). The primary contribution is the demonstration that strong event linking performance can be achieved *without* extensive external semantic features, relying instead on learned sentential representations. A secondary contribution is the creation and release of the ACE++ dataset, which expands upon the existing ACE corpus. Finally, the error analysis provides valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and directions for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance & State-of-the-Art Results:** The paper demonstrates competitive performance on the ACE dataset and establishes a new state-of-the-art on the larger ACE++ dataset. This is a significant accomplishment, suggesting the proposed approach is effective. The quantitative results are clearly presented and compared to existing work.
2. **Novelty in Feature Engineering:** The core idea of leveraging CNNs for sentential feature extraction is a valuable contribution. In a field often reliant on handcrafted features or expensive external resources, this approach offers a promising alternative. The motivation for this approach – avoiding the cost and domain limitations of resources like WordNet and FrameNet – is well-articulated.
3. **Creation of ACE++ Dataset:** The introduction of the ACE++ dataset is a significant contribution to the research community. Expanding the ACE corpus provides a larger and potentially more robust benchmark for evaluating event linking systems.  The dataset’s characteristics are clearly described, and its availability will facilitate further research.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis is insightful and goes beyond simply reporting numbers. Identifying specific error types, such as difficulties with pronoun resolution and inconsistencies in human annotation, highlights areas where the approach struggles and suggests avenues for future improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Ablation Study Results:** While Table 5 presents ablation results, the discussion of these results is somewhat limited. The observation that removing sentential features significantly impacts recall, but leads to a precision *increase* is intriguing, but not fully explored. A deeper dive into *why* this happens would strengthen the paper. Is the model overfitting to local features? Is the sentential representation somehow introducing noise?
2. **Over-Reliance on ACE Corpus Family:** The evaluation is almost exclusively focused on the ACE corpus and its extension. While this provides a consistent benchmark, it’s unclear how well the approach would generalize to other domains or corpora with different event schemas or annotation styles. Testing on a more diverse set of datasets would increase confidence in the robustness of the method.
3. **Clustering Strategy Choice:** The paper finds that BestLink *decreases* performance compared to transitive closure, which is counter to previous work (Liu et al., 2014). While the authors present a plausible explanation, a more thorough investigation of *why* this discrepancy exists would be beneficial. Was the difference in performance statistically significant? Are there specific characteristics of ACE++ that contribute to this result?
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Neural Models:** While the paper cites relevant prior work, the comparison to state-of-the-art is somewhat dated. Since the publication of some of the cited works, more advanced neural models (e.g., Transformers) have emerged. A comparison against these more recent approaches would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s competitiveness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the ablation study results, specifically regarding the impact of removing sentential features on both precision and recall? What hypotheses do you have for *why* precision increases when sentential features are removed?
2. Could you discuss the potential limitations of evaluating solely on the ACE corpus family and outline any plans for evaluating the model on more diverse datasets?
3. Was the difference in performance between transitive closure and BestLink statistically significant? Could you elaborate on potential reasons why ACE++ might exhibit different behavior compared to the findings of Liu et al. (2014)?
4. Have you experimented with more recent neural architectures, such as Transformers, for generating sentential features? If so, how did their performance compare to the CNN-based approach presented in the paper?



**Additional Comments:**

* The figure captions are generally clear and informative.
* The writing quality is good overall, although some sentences could be more concise.
* Consider adding a section discussing the computational cost of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. The method achieves strong performance, avoids reliance on expensive external resources, and introduces a useful new dataset. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
