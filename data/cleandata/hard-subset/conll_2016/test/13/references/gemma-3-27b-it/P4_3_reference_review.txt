This paper proposes a system for event linking leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event mentions, aiming to achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on external semantic resources. The core contribution lies in the application of CNNs to encode sentential context for event coreference resolution, combined with pairwise features for making coreference decisions. A secondary contribution is the empirical evaluation on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), demonstrating competitive performance and providing an error analysis. A third, less substantial contribution, is the empirical comparison of different clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) for generating event clusters.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves competitive performance on the ACE dataset, reaching state-of-the-art results according to the reported metrics. The experiments on the larger ACE++ dataset further demonstrate the model's potential and generalizability. This is a significant finding, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Novel Application of CNNs:** While CNNs are increasingly used in NLP, their application to generate sentential features specifically for *event linking* is a novel contribution. The use of CNNs to capture contextual information surrounding event mentions, combined with pairwise features, represents a well-motivated and effective approach.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation and Error Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed experimental setup, including ablation studies to assess the contribution of different feature classes. The error analysis, while limited in scope (100 false positives and 100 false negatives), provides valuable insights into the model’s weaknesses and potential directions for future work, such as handling pronoun resolution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Architecture:** The overall architecture (CNNs followed by pairwise feature combination and logistic regression) is relatively standard in the field of relation/event extraction. The novelty primarily resides in the specific application to event linking and the focus on sentential features. The core algorithmic innovations are incremental rather than transformative.
2. **Dataset Size Limitations:** While ACE++ is introduced, both datasets remain relatively small, limiting the statistical significance of some findings. The ACE dataset, in particular, is a well-studied benchmark, and achieving SOTA performance on such a dataset doesn’t necessarily translate to significant progress in the field.
3. **Clustering Strategy Impact:** The performance difference between transitive closure and BestLink clustering is substantial, yet the justification for the observed result feels incomplete. The authors state BestLink’s lack of benefit might be due to the model already filtering low-confidence links, but this requires further investigation. A deeper analysis of the types of errors each clustering strategy makes would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Models:** The related work section, while extensive, could benefit from a more thorough discussion of recent neural network-based approaches to event linking or coreference resolution. A comparison against more recent and sophisticated models (e.g., those utilizing transformers) would provide a better understanding of the model's relative strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a key challenge. Could you elaborate on the features currently used by the model that are intended to address pronoun resolution, and what modifications could be made to improve performance in this area?
2. The paper states that the use of gold-annotated event mentions is reasonable to mitigate error propagation. However, this also introduces a potential bias. How sensitive are the results to errors in the event mention extraction stage? Did you consider conducting experiments with automatically extracted event mentions?
3. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the range of values explored for each parameter and the criteria used to select the final configuration?
4. You mention that the positive links are highly imbalanced. Has the loss function been adjusted to address the class imbalance? If so, how?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is extensive and well-organized.
*   The figures and tables are clear and informative.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections could benefit from further clarification (e.g., the description of the convolution layer).
*   The code availability would enhance the reproducibility of the results.
