This paper presents a system for event linking utilizing convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features for event mention representations, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The authors claim contributions in: 1) a novel system design incorporating CNNs for event representation and pairwise features for coreference; 2) achieving state-of-the-art performance on two datasets; and 3) providing an error analysis to guide future research.

I believe the primary contribution is the systematic application of CNNs to generate sentential features specifically *for* event linking, alongside standard pairwise features. While the individual components (CNNs, pairwise features) are not novel, their integration within the event linking context is a reasonable approach. The empirical results, while claiming SOTA, are marginal and require significant scrutiny (see weaknesses). The error analysis is a standard practice and doesn't constitute a major contribution in itself.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of event linking, providing supporting evidence from related tasks and analyses of existing corpora. The motivation for reducing reliance on external knowledge sources is also well-stated.
2. **Sound Architectural Design:** The two-stage architecture – generating event representations with CNNs followed by pairwise coreference scoring – is logically structured and reasonably well-explained. The use of max-pooling and the consideration of relative position embeddings are sensible design choices.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct experiments on two datasets, including a newly created one (ACE++), and perform ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of different feature classes.  The reporting of results across multiple metrics (BLANC, MUC, etc.) is commendable.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis:**  The inclusion of a manual error analysis, identifying common failure modes (pronoun resolution, annotation disagreement), demonstrates a thoughtful approach to understanding the limitations of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Marginal Performance Gains & Questionable SOTA Claim:** The performance improvements over existing state-of-the-art systems (as reported in Table 3) are small. The claim of achieving “state-of-the-art performance” feels overstated, particularly considering the caveats the authors themselves acknowledge regarding variations in experimental setups across different publications. A more nuanced description of performance relative to the baseline would be more appropriate.
2. **Lack of Novelty in Core Components:** The use of CNNs and pairwise features, while effective, are not groundbreaking.  The paper doesn't demonstrate significant architectural innovations *within* the CNN component itself. The novelty lies primarily in the *application* of existing techniques to this specific task.
3. **Insufficient Justification for Hyperparameter Choices:** The description of the hyperparameter tuning process is vague. Saying they started from "previously reported values" and used a combination of random sampling and line search isn’t detailed enough. A more thorough explanation of the hyperparameter search strategy is needed. The final hyperparameter settings should be presented in a more organized fashion (Table 2 is helpful, but incomplete).
4. **Inconsistent Clustering Strategy Evaluation:** The authors experiment with both transitive closure and BestLink clustering strategies, but the justification for preferring transitive closure is weak. They state BestLink hurt recall, but the overall F1 score decreased. A more robust comparison, perhaps with error analysis focused on the differences in clustering output, is required.
5. **Formatting & Typographical Errors:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors. For example, inconsistencies in the use of boldface, occasional awkward phrasing, and the use of multiple bullet points within a single bullet point. While minor, these detract from the overall polish and credibility of the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed account of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space, the number of configurations evaluated, and the criteria used to select the final hyperparameters?
2.  The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a major challenge. Have you considered incorporating specific features or techniques to address this issue, such as coreference resolution for pronouns as a pre-processing step?
3.  Why did you choose to use the square function as the activation function in the similarity layer (Equation 4)? What empirical evidence supports this choice over other common activation functions?
4. The model’s performance on ACE++ seems significantly better. Is this attributable to the larger dataset size, or are there other differences between ACE and ACE++ that might explain the performance gap?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the ACEvalid/ACEtest document list is helpful.
*   The references section is comprehensive.
*   Please proofread the paper carefully to eliminate formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors.



Overall, this paper presents a solid, but not exceptional, contribution to the field of event linking. While the approach is sound and the experiments are comprehensive, the lack of significant novelty and the marginal performance gains raise concerns. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing stronger justification for their performance claims and providing more detail regarding their experimental methodology.
