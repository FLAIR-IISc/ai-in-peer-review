This paper tackles the important problem of event linking, aiming to resolve coreferences between event mentions across sentences. The core contribution, as I see it, is the application of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features for event representations, *specifically* within the context of event coreference. While the use of CNNs for feature extraction isn’t novel in NLP, applying it to event linking, bypassing reliance on often domain-specific external knowledge sources like WordNet and FrameNet, is a worthwhile direction. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets, demonstrating competitive performance and a reasonable error analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Approach:** The central idea – leveraging CNNs to encode sentential context for event coreference – is refreshing. Event linking has long been hampered by the need for extensive, and often brittle, external resources. This work demonstrably shows a path toward a more self-contained, data-driven approach. The combination of local and sentential features, coupled with pairwise features, is a sensible design choice.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on at least one dataset (and competitive performance on the other) is significant. While the gains aren’t massive, the fact that the model performs *as well as* or *better than* systems relying on more elaborate feature engineering is compelling.
3. **Detailed Model Description:** The paper provides a clear and comprehensive description of the model architecture, including the motivations behind design choices (e.g., the use of max-pooling, the dual pooling for left/right context). The diagrams are particularly helpful for understanding the flow of information.
4. **Thoughtful Error Analysis:** The error analysis section isn’t just a list of failures. The authors identify specific challenges (pronoun resolution, annotation disagreements) and relate them back to potential future research directions. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem.
5. **Clear Motivation & Problem Framing:** The introduction effectively argues for the importance of event linking and clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches, setting the stage for the proposed solution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Advancement:** While the approach is novel in its *combination* of techniques, the individual components (CNNs, pairwise features) are well-established. The paper doesn’t present a groundbreaking architectural innovation. The improvements are evolutionary rather than revolutionary.
2. **Limited Ablation Study:** While Table 5 shows the impact of removing feature classes, the ablation isn’t exhaustive. It would be valuable to see the effect of varying CNN parameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters) or exploring different neural network architectures (e.g., RNNs) more systematically.
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The ACE 2005 dataset is relatively small, and ACE++ is an extension built on it. The generalizability of the findings to other domains and event types is unclear. The authors acknowledge this to a degree, but it remains a concern.
4. **Clustering Strategy Sensitivity:** The experiments with different clustering strategies (transitive closure vs. BestLink) reveal a significant performance difference, suggesting the model's output is sensitive to the post-processing step. This indicates potential instability or a need for a more robust clustering method.
5. **Superficial Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section is quite lengthy but doesn’t offer particularly *critical* engagement with prior research. It lists many relevant papers but doesn’t deeply analyze their strengths and weaknesses in relation to the current work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper states that 55.42% of coreferential event mentions are more than five sentences apart. How does the CNN architecture, particularly the fixed-window size, handle such long-range dependencies? Is there an inherent limitation in capturing relationships across very distant sentences?
2. You mention the difficulty of resolving pronouns. Have you considered incorporating coreference resolution *before* event linking as a pre-processing step, even if it adds another layer of potential error?
3. Could you elaborate on why you chose a square function in the final layer for calculating similarity? Is there a specific theoretical justification for this choice beyond the desired invariance to polarity?
4. The performance gain from adding sentential features appears substantial (Table 5). Does this suggest that current event extraction systems often fail to capture crucial contextual information needed for accurate linking?

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While it doesn't represent a paradigm shift, it offers a valuable contribution to the field of event linking by proposing a viable alternative to reliance on external knowledge sources. The novelty lies in the application of CNNs to this specific task, and the empirical results are encouraging. The weaknesses are not fatal and are largely addressable in future work. The clarity of the presentation and the insightful error analysis further strengthen the case for acceptance. I prioritize novel directions even with incremental improvements, and this paper fits that criteria.
