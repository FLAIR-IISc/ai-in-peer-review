## Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for event linking (coreference resolution between event mentions) that utilizes convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The system employs a two-step architecture: first, CNNs model intra-sentential event mentions using sentential features; second, coreference decisions are made based on these representations and pairwise features. The authors report state-of-the-art results on two datasets (ACE and a larger ACE++).  They also include an error analysis to identify areas for future work.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of effective event linking *without* extensive external semantic features:** The paper successfully shows that competitive performance can be achieved using only local and sentential features derived via CNNs, bypassing the need for potentially domain-limited resources like WordNet or FrameNet. This is valuable for portability and scalability.
2. **Detailed empirical analysis of feature contributions:** The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of different feature sets (pairwise, trigger-local, sentential) to the overall performance, providing insights into the model's decision-making process.
3. **Comprehensive error analysis:** The manual error analysis, identifying challenges like pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, contributes to a nuanced understanding of the difficulties inherent in the task and directs future research.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The model achieves competitive performance on standard benchmarks (ACE) and demonstrates improvement on a larger, newly created dataset (ACE++). This provides solid evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach.
2. **Well-motivated design:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches relying on external resources and justifies the adoption of sentential features based on observed data characteristics (high proportion of coreferential pairs without argument overlap, distant mentions). The rationale behind the CNN architecture is well-explained.
3. **Thorough experimentation:**  The authors explore multiple design choices (different clustering strategies, feature combinations) and present a robust evaluation using various metrics. The ablation studies are particularly insightful.
4. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the methodology and understand the results. The diagrams (Figure 1) are helpful in visualizing the model architecture.
5. **Realistic error analysis**: The error analysis doesn't just report types of errors but connects them to inherent difficulties in the task (pronoun resolution) and even potential annotator ambiguity, which demonstrates a thoughtful understanding of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in the method itself:** While the application of CNNs for sentential features in event linking is solid, the core method – utilizing CNNs for feature extraction and a logistic regression classifier – is not groundbreaking. The novelty lies more in the *application* of established techniques to this specific task and the demonstration of effectiveness without external resources.
2. **Dataset details are somewhat lacking:**  While the authors create and utilize ACE++, more details about its annotation process and characteristics beyond the table in Section 4 would be beneficial.  How was the annotation quality controlled?  What is the distribution of event types in ACE++?
3. **Clustering strategy sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the clustering strategy employed (BestLink vs. transitive closure). The explanation for why BestLink *didn’t* work as expected is insightful, but warrants further investigation.  Why does the model already sufficiently weed out low-confidence links?
4. **Lack of comparison to more recent neural approaches:** The comparison to related work (Table 3) is somewhat dated. Including comparisons to more recent approaches utilizing transformers or other advanced neural architectures would strengthen the paper.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more details regarding the creation of the ACE++ dataset, specifically concerning the annotation process and quality control measures?  What is the distribution of event types in this dataset compared to ACE?
2.  You mention that your model already effectively filters low-confidence links, explaining the inferior performance of BestLink. Could you elaborate on *why* the model is so effective at this filtering?  Are there specific features or architectural components contributing to this behavior?
3.  Have you experimented with more recent neural architectures (e.g., transformers) for generating sentential features? If so, how did their performance compare to the CNN-based approach?  If not, could you discuss the potential benefits and challenges of exploring such architectures?
4.  The error analysis identifies pronoun resolution as a significant challenge.  Have you considered incorporating coreference resolution techniques specifically designed for pronouns into the pipeline?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the ACEvalid/ACEtest document list is appreciated.
*   The references section is comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper. While the method doesn’t represent a radical departure from existing techniques, the authors demonstrate its effectiveness for event linking, particularly in scenarios where external semantic resources are limited. The thorough experimentation, insightful analysis, and clear writing make this a valuable contribution to the field.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
