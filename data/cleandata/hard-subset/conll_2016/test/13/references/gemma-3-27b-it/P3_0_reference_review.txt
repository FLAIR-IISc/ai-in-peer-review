## Review: Event Linking with Sentential Features from Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a system for event linking (coreference resolution of event mentions) that leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features representing event mentions. The core idea is to avoid reliance on extensive external semantic resources (like WordNet and FrameNet) by learning rich representations directly from text. The system consists of two main stages: first, a CNN encodes individual event mentions and their surrounding context; second, a logistic regression classifier determines coreference links based on these representations and pairwise features. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, ACE 2005 and a newly created ACE++ dataset, along with an error analysis.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Sentential Feature Learning for Event Linking:** The paper successfully applies CNNs to learn effective representations of event mentions from surrounding sentence context, achieving competitive results *without* relying on expensive external knowledge bases. This is a valuable contribution, demonstrating a viable path towards more portable and scalable event linking systems.
2. **Creation and Release of the ACE++ Dataset:** The introduction of ACE++, a larger version of the ACE 2005 dataset, is a significant contribution.  It provides a more challenging benchmark for event linking research and facilitates the development of more robust models.
3. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation & Error Analysis:**  The paper provides a well-structured experimental setup, comparing the proposed model against several baselines and ablation studies.  The error analysis, while limited, offers insights into the remaining challenges in event linking, particularly the handling of pronoun references.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance and Competitive Results:**  The reported results on both ACE 2005 and ACE++ are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed approach. While the comparison is nuanced due to variations in experimental setup, the improvement over prior work using similar resources is encouraging.
2. **Well-Motivated Design Choices:**  The authors provide a clear rationale for their design decisions, particularly the use of CNNs and sentential features. They effectively build upon recent work in relation/event extraction and convincingly argue for the benefits of avoiding reliance on external knowledge bases. The initial data analysis highlighting the prevalence of coreferential pairs with no shared arguments or trigger words is particularly insightful.
3. **Solid Experimental Methodology:**  The experimental setup is well-defined, including a clear description of the datasets, hyperparameter tuning process, and evaluation metrics.  The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of different feature components (sentential vs. lexical vs. pairwise).
4. **Useful Error Analysis:** The initial error analysis, pointing to difficulties with pronoun resolution and annotator disagreement, provides valuable direction for future research.  Identifying these specific challenges is crucial for advancing the field.
5. **Publicly Available Dataset:** The release of ACE++ is a valuable resource for the research community and will undoubtedly foster further progress in event linking.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core CNN Architecture:** The CNN architecture itself is fairly standard and lacks substantial novelty. While the *application* of CNNs to event linking with sentential features is a worthwhile contribution, the architectural innovation is minimal.  The authors don't delve into exploration of more sophisticated neural network architectures (e.g., transformers, recurrent networks) that might capture longer-range dependencies more effectively.
2. **Lack of Deep Analysis of Sentence Feature Importance:** While the ablation studies demonstrate that sentential features are important, the paper lacks a deeper investigation into *which* parts of the sentence are most informative for event linking.  Techniques like attention mechanisms or feature importance analysis could reveal valuable insights into the model's behavior and potentially lead to further performance improvements.
3. **Suboptimal Clustering Strategy:** The paper acknowledges the performance drawbacks of the transitive closure clustering strategy but still employs it. The comparison with BestLink, albeit briefly discussed, suggests it isn’t optimal for this system. The reasons why BestLink performed worse here could benefit from further investigation.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational resources required for training and inference. Given the use of CNNs and relatively large datasets, providing this information would be beneficial for researchers considering adopting this approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Pronoun Resolution:** You identify pronoun resolution as a major source of error. Have you experimented with incorporating explicit pronoun resolution techniques (even simple heuristics) into your system?  If so, what were the results?
2. **Attention Mechanisms:** Would incorporating attention mechanisms into the CNN architecture, to focus on the most relevant parts of the sentence, improve performance?  What is your hypothesis and have you explored this?
3. **BestLink vs. Transitive Closure:** Can you elaborate on why BestLink performed worse in your experiments compared to the findings of Liu et al. (2014)? What characteristics of your system might explain this difference?
4. **Dataset Details:** Could you provide more details about the annotation process for ACE++? Were the same annotators used as for ACE 2005, and what steps were taken to ensure consistency between the two datasets?
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While you mention hyperparameter tuning, can you briefly describe the sensitivity of your model's performance to key hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimension, filter size)?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections (e.g., Section 6, Related Work) feel slightly rushed.
* The citations are comprehensive and well-integrated into the text.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used in your experiments for easy reference.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event linking. While the core CNN architecture is not particularly novel, the successful application of sentential feature learning and the release of the ACE++ dataset are significant achievements.  The thorough experimental evaluation and insightful error analysis further strengthen the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above and responding to the questions posed.
