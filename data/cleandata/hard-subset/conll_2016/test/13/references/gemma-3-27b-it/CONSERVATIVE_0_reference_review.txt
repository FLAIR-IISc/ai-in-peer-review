This paper proposes a system for event linking that utilizes convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to generate sentential features, aiming to reduce reliance on external semantic resources. The primary claimed contribution is achieving state-of-the-art performance on two event linking datasets without the use of resources like WordNet or FrameNet. A secondary contribution lies in the detailed error analysis, identifying challenges and directions for future research. A final, less substantial claim is the design of the overall system architecture itself.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The reported results on ACE and ACE++ are competitive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Achieving state-of-the-art (or near it) performance is a significant positive, even if the margin isn't dramatic. This suggests the method is viable and worthy of further investigation.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The introduction effectively establishes the importance of event linking by citing previous work and providing supporting statistics regarding information incompleteness. The problem definition is well-articulated and easily understood.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors provide a reasonably detailed description of the experimental setup, including dataset splits, hyperparameter tuning, and baseline comparisons. The inclusion of both ACE and a larger, newly created dataset (ACE++) is a positive aspect, allowing for a broader evaluation.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis section is a strength. Identifying specific failure cases, such as pronoun resolution and inconsistencies in annotation, provides valuable insights and directs future research efforts. The recognition of annotation disagreements is particularly commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the results are good, the core novelty of this work is questionable. Utilizing CNNs to generate sentential features is now a relatively standard practice in NLP, and applying this to event linking doesn’t represent a substantial breakthrough. The paper essentially applies an established technique to a specific task, rather than proposing a fundamentally new methodology. The authors acknowledge this by framing it as removing the *need* for external resources, rather than a new theoretical advance.
2. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to other state-of-the-art systems is somewhat limited. While some relevant baselines are included, it's unclear whether these represent the absolute strongest approaches available, particularly given the active research in event coreference. A more comprehensive comparison, including recent neural models, is needed to firmly establish the superiority of the proposed method. Specifically, the lack of comparison to more recent transformer-based architectures is a significant omission.
3. **Over-Reliance on ACE Dataset:** While ACE++ is a welcome addition, the performance gains on it are not drastically different from ACE. The reliance on these specific datasets raises concerns about generalizability. A more diverse set of datasets, representing different domains and event types, would strengthen the conclusions. The authors do acknowledge the limitations of ACE in the related work section.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study Detail:** The ablation study presented in Table 5 is helpful, but lacks depth. While it shows the contribution of different feature sets, it doesn’t provide much insight into *why* certain features are more important than others. A more granular ablation study, examining the effect of different CNN configurations or hyperparameters, would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational resources required to train and run your model, particularly on the larger ACE++ dataset? This information is important for reproducibility and accessibility.
2.  You mention that pronoun resolution is a major source of error. Have you considered incorporating explicit pronoun resolution techniques into your system, and if so, why were they not included?
3.  The BestLink clustering strategy decreased recall. Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of the types of event pairs that were incorrectly filtered out by BestLink?
4. What specific hyperparameter search strategy was employed beyond the “random sampling and line search” mentioned in Section 4? Were any automated hyperparameter optimization techniques (e.g., Bayesian optimization) explored?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figure captions could be more descriptive.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, but some sections (e.g., Related Work) could be more concise.
*   The authors should clarify why they chose a square function as the non-linearity in the similarity layer, beyond just stating an intuition.
