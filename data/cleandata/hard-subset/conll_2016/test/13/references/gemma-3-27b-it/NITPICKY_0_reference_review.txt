The paper proposes a system for event linking based on convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features for event mentions. The core idea is to avoid reliance on external semantic resources by leveraging contextual information from the surrounding sentence. The authors claim contributions in designing such a system, achieving state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, and providing an error analysis. However, the primary contribution appears to be a competent engineering effort applying existing techniques (CNNs, sentential features) to a specific task, rather than a novel algorithmic breakthrough. The datasets used, while valuable, are relatively small, and the performance gains over existing methods are incremental.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Engineering & Implementation:** The authors present a well-implemented system utilizing established neural network techniques. The model architecture is clearly described, and the experimental setup appears thorough. The use of TensorFlow is a practical choice.
2. **Competent Performance:** The system achieves competitive results on the ACE and ACE++ datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness. While not a dramatic leap forward, achieving state-of-the-art results is a positive indication.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The error analysis provides valuable insights into the model's limitations and areas for potential improvement. Specifically, the identification of pronoun resolution as a key challenge is insightful.
4. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper provides a well-defined explanation of the event linking problem and its importance, contextualized within the broader field of information extraction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of using sentential features with CNNs is not novel. This approach has been explored in related tasks (relation extraction) and the authors do not present any significant algorithmic innovations beyond adapting existing techniques to event linking. The combination of components feels incremental rather than transformative.
2. **Small Dataset Size & Generalizability:**  The ACE 2005 dataset is small, and even the ACE++ dataset remains limited. The reported performance may not generalize well to larger, more diverse corpora. The reliance on these datasets raises concerns about the robustness and scalability of the proposed approach.
3. **Insufficient Justification for Architectural Choices:** While the architecture is described, the rationale behind specific design choices (e.g., the number of filters in the convolution layer, the size of the hidden layers) is often lacking. The paper states they started from "previously reported hyperparameter values" without sufficiently detailing the search process or the impact of different settings. The choice of ReLU and the squared activation function in the similarity layer seem arbitrary without deeper justification.
4. **Overstated Claims of Avoiding External Resources:** The reliance on pre-trained word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013) constitutes a form of external knowledge. While the authors avoid explicit knowledge bases like WordNet, they still leverage pre-existing linguistic information. This nuance should be acknowledged.
5. **Inconsistent Clustering Evaluation:** The contrasting results of using transitive closure versus BestLink for clustering, and the authors' preference for transitive closure despite Liu et al. (2014)'s findings, is not fully explained. A more rigorous comparison of different clustering strategies is warranted.
6. **Formatting and Style Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g. spacing after punctuation), and the writing style could benefit from a more concise and precise approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what search strategy was used (e.g., grid search, random search), and how were the final hyperparameters selected?
2.  The paper states the model "does not rely on external semantic features." Please clarify your stance on pre-trained word embeddings. Do you consider these to be external resources, and if so, how does your approach differ from methods that explicitly use knowledge bases like WordNet?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the squared activation function in the similarity layer? What alternatives were considered, and why was this function chosen over others?
4.  Given Liu et al. (2014)'s findings on BestLink, could you further investigate and explain why transitive closure performed better in your experiments?
5.  Regarding the error analysis, can you provide more specific examples of false negatives related to pronoun resolution, and discuss potential strategies for addressing this challenge?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive but could be formatted more consistently.
*   The figure captions could be more descriptive.
*   A more detailed discussion of computational resources (e.g., training time, hardware specifications) would be helpful.
*   Minor grammatical errors throughout.



Overall, this paper presents a competent but incremental contribution to the field of event linking. While the results are solid, the lack of novelty and the limited dataset size raise concerns about its broader impact. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper. Given these issues, I lean towards **rejection**.
