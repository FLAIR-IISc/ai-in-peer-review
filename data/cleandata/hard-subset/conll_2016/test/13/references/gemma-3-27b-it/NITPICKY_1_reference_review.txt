This paper proposes a system for event linking, leveraging convolutional neural networks to generate sentential features for event mentions, combined with pairwise features for coreference resolution. The authors claim contributions in designing this system, achieving state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, and providing an error analysis. I believe the primary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation, demonstrating a competitive system *despite* relying solely on learned features and avoiding external knowledge resources. The secondary contribution is the detailed error analysis, which highlights challenges in the task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Experimental Validation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets (ACE and ACE++), comparing the proposed model against several baselines and state-of-the-art systems. The reported results demonstrate competitive performance, establishing the effectiveness of the approach. The ablation studies (Table 5) provide valuable insights into the contribution of different feature sets.
2. **Comprehensive Error Analysis:** The error analysis, though limited in scale (100 false positives, 100 false negatives), offers a nuanced understanding of the system's weaknesses. Identifying challenges related to pronoun resolution and annotation inconsistencies is particularly valuable. This analysis suggests promising avenues for future research.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The architecture of the model is clearly explained, and Figure 1 provides a helpful visual representation. The motivation for design choices is also reasonably well articulated.
4. **Avoidance of External Resources:** The deliberate choice to eschew external knowledge resources (WordNet, FrameNet) is noteworthy. This makes the system potentially more portable and applicable to domains where such resources are unavailable or incomplete.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the performance is competitive, the technical innovations are incremental. The use of CNNs for feature extraction and pairwise features for coreference is not entirely novel, and the specific architecture details appear fairly standard. The combination of these techniques, while effective, doesn't represent a breakthrough.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Reporting:** The paper mentions a “combined strategy of random sampling and line search” for hyperparameter optimization but lacks sufficient detail. The specific ranges explored and the criteria used for selection are not fully specified. Furthermore, reporting only the final hyperparameters (Table 2) without indicating the search space or the computational cost of the optimization process is a deficiency.
3. **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** Several design choices, such as the specific dimensions of the embedding spaces (dw, dp, dc, de, dsim) and the choice of ReLU and tanh activation functions, are not adequately justified. The authors state motivations (“backed by intuition”), but a more rigorous justification based on prior work or theoretical considerations would be preferable.
4. **Clustering Strategy Inconsistency:** The paper initially mentions experimenting with both transitive closure *and* BestLink clustering strategies. However, the results presented primarily focus on transitive closure. The authors claim BestLink performed worse, but the justification lacks sufficient detail. A more thorough comparison, including a statistical significance test, would be beneficial.
5. **Minor Formatting & Presentation Issues:** There are minor inconsistencies in formatting, such as the variable naming convention (e.g., sometimes using subscripts, sometimes not). The image quality of Figure 1 is noticeably low, and the font size is small, hindering readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter search space and optimization process? Specifically, what ranges were explored for each hyperparameter, and what metric was used to guide the search?
2.  Can you elaborate on the results obtained with the BestLink clustering strategy? What were the specific performance differences compared to transitive closure, and was a statistical significance test performed?
3.  The paper mentions discrepancies between the model's predictions and human annotations (in the error analysis). Can you provide more specific examples of these discrepancies, and discuss potential reasons for these disagreements?
4.  Given the observation that 50.97% of coreferential event mention pairs share no arguments, did you experiment with features specifically designed to capture relationships between non-overlapping arguments?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent.
*   The link to the list of documents in ACEvalid/ACEtest is helpful but should be integrated directly into the text as a footnote.
*   Consider adding a discussion of limitations of the ACE datasets (e.g., its limited domain coverage) and how these limitations might affect the generalizability of the findings.
*   The sentence "Fortunately, recent work has explored the use of a new feature class..." feels somewhat dismissive of earlier work using sentential features, even if not with the same architectural choices. Rephrasing could improve the tone.



Despite the promising results, the incremental novelty and the lack of rigorous justification for some design choices raise concerns. I tentatively recommend **rejection**, contingent upon a significantly revised manuscript that addresses the weaknesses outlined above. Addressing the questions posed to the authors is crucial for demonstrating the robustness and significance of the work.
