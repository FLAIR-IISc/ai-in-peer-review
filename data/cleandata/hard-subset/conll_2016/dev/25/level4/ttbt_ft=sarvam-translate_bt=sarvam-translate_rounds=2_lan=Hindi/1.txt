This paper presents an approach to word meaning that includes temporal information (past, present, future, or temporary). They model the problem using a graph-based semi-supervised classification algorithm that allows combining data without labeling item-specific information – such as the presence of certain temporal indicators – and the structure of the wordnet – the sense relations between the senses. They perform a full annotation of the wordnet based on a pre-labeled training data set and using the remaining part of the wordnet as unlabeled data. Specifically, they take advantage of the structure of the label set by breaking the task into binary formulations (temporal vs. non-temporal), then perform better grained tagging (past, present, or future) using the data labeled as temporal. To internally evaluate their approach, they annotate a subset of the senses in the wordnet using crowd-sourcing. They compare their system with the results obtained by a state-of-the-art time tagger (Stanford's STime), which uses a heuristic as a backup strategy, and also from previous tasks. They achieve an increase of approximately 11% in accuracy, and show that their approach allows better performance compared to previous systems using only 400 labeled data. Finally, they evaluate their resource on an existing task (TempEval-3) and show an increase of approximately 10% in F1 at 4 labels.

The paper is well-structured and generally clear, the approach seems solid and well-justified. This work has given rise to the development of a resource with fine grained temporal information at the word meaning level that will be made available and can be used to improve various NLP tasks. I have some comments, especially about the settings of the experiments.

I think more information should be given on the work done in the external evaluation section. It may be useful to understand what the system is trying to predict (the features describe "unit pairs" but it is not clear what these pairs are) and what the features are (in particular, what are the unit features? What is the POS for a pair, is it one dimension or two? Is the lemma automatically derived?). The sentence describing the label is confusing, I am not sure what "event to document creation time" and "event to same sentence event" mean, are they considered such pairs? Are they relations (as they are described as relations at the beginning of page 8)? I find the footnotes about the 14 relations to be unclear: why should other relations be ignored, what makes the mapping very "complex"? Also, are the scores macro or micro averages? Finally, the ablation study indicates a possible overabundance with a fairly close score between the lexicon and the unit, any clues about this behavior?

I also have some questions about the use of SVMs. For external evaluation, the authors say that they optimized the parameters of the algorithm: what are these parameters? And since an SVM is also used within the MINT framework, is it optimized and how? Finally, if it is a lib SVM library that is used (Veka wrapper), I think a reference to lib SVM should be included.

Other comments:
- It would be interesting to have the number of examples per label in the gold data, the figures are given for coarse grained labels (127 temporal vs. 271 non-temporal), but not for fine grained.
- It would also be good to have an idea of the number of ambiguous words at the temporal level, such as "present".
- The caption in Table 3 says that the results presented are "significantly better" but no significance test is indicated, nor any p-value.
