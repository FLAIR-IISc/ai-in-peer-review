This paper presents a method for adding temporal information (past, present, future, or non-temporal) to tag vocabulary. They model the problem using a graph-based semi-supervised classification algorithm, which allows them to combine some temporal indicators in the vocabulary (such as the presence of some temporal indicators in the vocabulary) and the structure of the wordnet – the semantic relationship between the wordnets – and consider unlabeled data. They create a complete annotation of the wordnet using a set of labeled training data from a previous work and the rest of the wordnet as unlabeled data. Specifically, they break the task into a binary formula (temporal vs. non-temporal) and exploit the structure of the label set, then use the labeled data as temporal to perform more fine-grained tagging (past, present, or future). To evaluate their method implicitly, they create an annotation of a subset of the wordnet's wordnets using crowd-sourcing. They use a heuristic as a backup technique and compare their system's results with those obtained by a sophisticated time tagger (SUTime from Stanford). They achieved an improvement of approximately 11% in accuracy and show that their method performs better than previous systems using only 400 labeled data. Finally, they evaluated their resource on an existing task (TempEval-3) and showed an improvement of approximately 10% in 4 labels, F1.

This paper is well-developed and generally clear, the method seems correct and well-justified. This work has helped create a resource with fine temporal information at the vocabulary level that will be made available and can be used to improve various NLP tasks. I have a few comments, especially regarding the test settings.

I think more information should be given about the task performed in the external evaluation section. An example might be useful to understand what the system is trying to predict (the features describe "entity pairs" but what these pairs are, this was not clarified earlier) and what the features are (especially, what are the entity attributes? What is the POS of a pair, is it one-dimensional or two-dimensional? Are the lemas automatically found?). The sentence describing the used labels is confusing, I don't understand what "event to document creation time" and "event to same sentence event" mean, are these the types of pairs that are considered? Are these relationships (as described as a relationship at the beginning of p.8)? The footnote about the 14 relationships seems unclear to me: why ignore the other relationships, what makes a mapping very "complex"? Also, are the scores macro or micro averages? Finally, the ablation study indicates a possible redundancy due to the fairly close scores between Lexica and Entity, is there any formula regarding this behavior? I also have some questions regarding the use of SVM. For external evaluation, the authors said that they have optimized the algorithm's parameters: what are these parameters? And since an SVM is also used within the Mincut structure, is it optimized and how? Finally, if the Lib SVM library is used (Weka wrapper), I think a reference to Lib SVM should be included.

Other comments:
- It would be good if there were an example number for each label in the gold data, the numbers are given for coarse-grained labels (127 temporal vs. 271 non-temporal), but not for fine-grained labels.
- It would be good to have an idea about the number of ambiguous words at the temporal level, such as "present".
- The caption of Table 3 states that the presented results are "significantly better", but no significance test is indicated, no p-value either.
