This paper presents a method for adding temporal information (past, present, future, or non-temporal) to the tag vocabulary. They model the problem using a graph-based semi-supervised classification algorithm, which allows them to consider some temporal indices in their vocabulary (such as the presence of some temporal indices in the vocabulary), the structure of the wordnet – semantic relationships between wordnets – and unlabeled data. They create a complete annotation of the wordnet using a set of labeled training data from previous work and the remaining wordnets as unlabeled data. Specifically, they divide the task into a binary formula (temporal vs. non-temporal) and exploit the structure of the label set, then use the labeled data as temporal to perform more fine-grained tagging (past, present, or future). To evaluate their method implicitly, they create an annotation of a subset of the wordnets using crowd-sourcing. They use a heuristic as a backup technique and compare the results of their system with the results obtained by a state-of-the-art time tagger (SUTime from Stanford). They achieved an improvement of approximately 11% in accuracy and showed that their method performed better than previous systems using only 400 labeled data. Finally, they evaluated their resource on an existing task (TempEval-3) and showed an improvement of approximately 10% in 4 labels, F1.

This paper is well-developed and generally clear, the method seems correct and well-justified. This work has helped to create a resource with fine temporal information at the vocabulary level that will be made available and can be used to improve various NLP tasks. I have a few comments, especially regarding the test settings.

I think more information about the task performed in the external evaluation section should be given. An example might be useful to understand what the system is trying to predict (the features describe "entity pairs" but what these pairs are, this was not previously clarified) and what the features are (in particular, what are the entity features? What is the POS of a pair, is it one-dimensional or two-dimensional? Are the lemas automatically found?). The sentence describing the used labels is confusing, I don't understand what "event to document creation time" and "event to same sentence event" mean, are these types of pairs considered? These relationships (such as described as a relationship at the beginning of p.8)? The footnote about 14 relationships seems unclear to me: why are other relationships ignored, what makes a mapping very "complex"? Also, are the scores macro or micro averages? Finally, the ablation study indicates a possible redundancy due to roughly close scores between the lexica and the entities, is there any reference to this behavior? I also have some questions regarding the use of SVMs. For external evaluation, the authors said that they have optimized the algorithm's parameters: what are these parameters? And since an SVM is also used within the mincut structure, is it optimized and how? Finally, if the Lib SVM library is used (Weka wrapper), I think a reference to Lib SVM should be included.

Other comments:
- It would be good if there was an example number for each label in the gold data, the numbers are given for coarse-grained labels (127 temporal vs. 271 non-temporal), but not for fine-grained labels.
- It would be good to have an idea about the number of ambiguous words at the temporal level, such as "present".
- The caption in Table 3 states that the presented results are "significantly better", but no significance test is indicated, nor is there a p-value.
