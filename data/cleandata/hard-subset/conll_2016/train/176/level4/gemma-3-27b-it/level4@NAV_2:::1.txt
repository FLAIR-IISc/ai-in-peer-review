This paper proposes a novel framework for unifying various sentence pair scoring tasks – including Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Paraphrasing – under a common task of understanding semantic relations between sentences. It further investigates the potential for learning generally-applicable neural network models across this family of tasks. This is an exciting and potentially impactful approach worthy of presentation and further investigation.

The primary contribution of this work is the formalization of a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across diverse tasks, coupled with a comprehensive empirical evaluation. A second key contribution lies in the introduction of new datasets – yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop – designed to address shortcomings in existing benchmarks, particularly regarding dataset size and quality. Finally, the initial demonstration of effective cross-task transfer learning, particularly leveraging a model pre-trained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, provides preliminary evidence supporting the feasibility of learning task-independent semantic representations. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Unified Framework & Evaluation:** The proposal to treat diverse sentence pair scoring tasks as instances of a general semantic relation understanding problem is a significant conceptual step. The comprehensive evaluation across multiple tasks provides a valuable resource for the community and facilitates more meaningful model comparisons than typically seen in task-specific research. The inclusion of IR baselines offers a strong point of comparison, and the statistical rigor through reporting confidence intervals is commendable.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop addresses clear limitations in existing datasets, such as small size and noisy evaluation metrics. The motivation behind creating these datasets is well-articulated, and their characteristics (as shown in Fig. 1) suggest they will be valuable resources for future research.
3. **Positive Transfer Learning Results:** Demonstrating that a model pre-trained on one task (Ubuntu Dialogue) can effectively transfer to others is a crucial step towards the goal of task-independent semantic comprehension. The observed performance gains highlight the potential for leveraging large datasets to improve generalization. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incomplete Results & Limited Discussion:** While the paper presents a large number of experimental results, the discussion of these results is superficial. The tables require completion, and a more in-depth analysis of the observed performance variations across tasks is necessary to draw meaningful conclusions.  The interpretation of *why* certain models succeed or fail on specific tasks is currently lacking.
2. **Unfulfilled Promise of Novel Dataset:** The abstract mentions the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, but the paper’s contribution feels more focused on improving existing datasets. This discrepancy needs to be addressed; either a novel dataset should be more prominently presented, or the abstract should be revised to accurately reflect the paper’s scope.
3. **Task Heterogeneity & Unified Framework Validity:** The degree to which these tasks truly form a unified whole is questionable. A deeper investigation into task-specific challenges and the limitations of a single model across all tasks is needed.  It is unclear whether performance variations are attributable to model deficiencies or inherent differences between the tasks themselves.
4. **Lack of Illustrative Examples:** The paper would benefit from including example sentence pairs from each dataset, showcasing both prototypical cases and instances that pose challenges for classification. This would provide a more concrete understanding of the tasks and the difficulties involved.
5. **Ambiguity Regarding Transfer Learning:** The rationale behind the success of the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is unclear. Is it due to the inherent properties of the Ubuntu Dialogue task or the dataset’s size? Further analysis is required to determine the generalizability of this finding.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, focusing on the reasons behind performance differences across tasks and models?
2. Please clarify the status of the new dataset mentioned in the abstract. If a novel dataset has not been substantially developed, could the abstract be revised to accurately reflect the paper's focus?
3. What specific experiments could be conducted to disentangle the effects of task characteristics and model limitations on overall performance?
4. Can you provide illustrative examples of challenging sentence pairs from each dataset, highlighting the difficulties faced by the models?
5. What investigations were undertaken to determine why the Ubu. RNN model transfers so effectively? Is this result specific to the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, or is it a more general phenomenon?
