This paper introduces a novel approach by framing several sentence pair scoring tasks – including Answer Set Scoring, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Paraphrase Detection – as instances of a broader semantic relation understanding problem. The authors then explore the feasibility of developing generally applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this a compelling and promising direction, suitable for presentation at CoNLL and deserving of continued research.

However, the paper currently feels incomplete and would benefit significantly from revisions before final publication. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the following points:

1. The initial results table requires completion.
2. The written discussion of results is disproportionately brief given the extensive number of individual findings. A more in-depth interpretation and analysis of these results are necessary.
3. The abstract mentions a new, more challenging dataset, which is not presented in the current manuscript. This discrepancy must be resolved – either by including the dataset or revising the abstract accordingly.
4. Performance varies considerably across different tasks. The authors should investigate the reasons behind these variations, exploring why the models succeed on some tasks while failing on others. Even if definitive answers are elusive, a discussion of whether these differences stem from modeling limitations or inherent task dissimilarities, and whether a unified approach is truly appropriate, would be valuable.
5. Please provide illustrative examples of the datasets utilized, showcasing both representative sentence pairs and those that present classification challenges.
6. While the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is suggested for new tasks, it’s unclear whether this recommendation is due to the model’s generalizability, the nature of the task itself, or the dataset size. Further investigation is needed to clarify this point.

Despite these areas for improvement, the overall approach is exciting and has the potential to stimulate fruitful discussion. I would be pleased to see a more polished version of this work published.

I acknowledge that other reviewers may hold different perspectives.

Regarding minor language issues:

* The terms "weigh" and "weighed" appear to be used incorrectly in contexts requiring "weight" and "weighted."
* There are several instances of "sentene" instead of "sentence."
* The meaning of "interpunction" is unclear.
* One instance reads "world overlap" when it should be "word overlap."
