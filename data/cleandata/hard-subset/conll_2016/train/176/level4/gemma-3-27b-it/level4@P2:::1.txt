This paper proposes a unified framework for approaching a variety of sentence pair scoring tasks – including Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE), and Paraphrasing – by recasting them as instances of a general semantic relation understanding problem. It further investigates the potential for learning broadly applicable neural network models across these tasks. This is a promising direction that merits presentation at CoNLL and further research.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the explicit framing of diverse sentence pair tasks within a single, unified framework. This allows for comparative evaluation and exploration of cross-task transfer learning, which is a significant step towards more generalizable semantic understanding models. A secondary contribution is the introduction of two new datasets, yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, designed to address limitations in existing resources for Answer Sentence Selection. Finally, the paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation of various neural network architectures on these tasks, establishing a strong baseline for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Framework:** The paper's central premise – unifying disparate tasks under a common framework – is a strong contribution. This enables systematic comparisons and promotes the development of models with broader applicability, addressing a fragmentation issue in the field. The shift from task-specific models to a more generalized approach is a valuable perspective.
2. **Dataset Contribution:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 is a significant benefit to the research community. The authors identify and address shortcomings in existing datasets, such as small size, uneven sentence pair distribution, and unreliable validation splits, leading to a more robust evaluation environment. The accompanying dataset addresses these concerns and provides a valuable resource for researchers.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The thorough empirical evaluation, encompassing a range of models and datasets, provides a strong baseline and facilitates informed comparisons. The inclusion of both neural and non-neural baselines is particularly valuable for contextualizing the performance of the proposed models.  The reporting of confidence intervals, rather than single point estimates, demonstrates a commitment to rigorous scientific reporting.
4. **Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** The successful transfer learning results using the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset (Ubu. RNN) provide initial evidence supporting the feasibility of learning general semantic representations. This highlights the potential for leveraging data from one task to improve performance on others, a key goal of the unified framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incomplete Presentation:** The paper feels somewhat unfinished. Key results tables are incomplete, and the discussion of results lacks sufficient depth. Given the breadth of experiments, a more detailed analysis and interpretation of the findings are crucial.
2. **Unfulfilled Promise of a New Dataset:** The abstract mentions a new, more challenging dataset but the paper doesn’t deliver a clear presentation of its characteristics or analysis. This discrepancy needs to be addressed; either the promised dataset needs to be fully presented, or the abstract needs to be revised.
3. **Limited Task-Specific Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates performance across multiple tasks, it lacks a deep dive into why models succeed or fail on specific tasks. Further investigation into task-specific challenges and model limitations would enhance the paper’s insights and inform future research.
4. **Ambiguous Transfer Learning Interpretation:** The recommendation to use the Ubu. RNN model for new tasks is not sufficiently justified. It’s unclear whether the success is due to the task’s inherent generality or the dataset’s size. Addressing this question is critical for understanding the effectiveness of transfer learning in this context.
5. **Minor Technical Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are inconsistencies in terminology ("weigh" vs. "weight") and several spelling errors ("sentene"). The term "interpunction" is also unclear and should be clarified. There's also a "world overlap" instead of "word overlap".

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the results, specifically addressing the performance variations across different tasks? What are the key factors contributing to the successes and failures of your models on each task?
2. Please clarify the characteristics of the "new, more challenging dataset" mentioned in the abstract. If it’s not fully presented, please explain why and revise the abstract accordingly.
3. Can you elaborate on the rationale behind recommending the Ubu. RNN model for new tasks? What experiments did you conduct to determine whether the success is due to the task's nature or the dataset’s size?
4. Would you consider adding example instances of the various datasets used (both prototypical and challenging cases) to aid in understanding the nature of the tasks?
