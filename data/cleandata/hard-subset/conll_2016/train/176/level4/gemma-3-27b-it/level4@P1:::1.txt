This paper proposes a novel framework for treating various sentence pair scoring tasks – such as Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE), and Paraphrasing – as instances of a more general task: understanding semantic relations between two sentences. Furthermore, the authors investigate the potential of learning generally-applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this to be an exciting proposal that is worthy of presentation at CoNLL and further discussion and investigation.

The primary concern with the paper is that it feels somewhat incomplete. Acceptance for publication should be contingent on addressing several points in a revised version:

1.  The first results table requires completion.
2.  Given the extensive number of results presented, the written discussion of these results is disproportionately short. A much more thorough interpretation and discussion of the findings is needed.
3.  The abstract promises the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, but the paper does not fully deliver on this. This discrepancy needs to be resolved.
4.  The results demonstrate considerable variation across different tasks. Investigation into *why* the models succeed or fail on specific tasks would be beneficial. Even if definitive answers are elusive, the authors’ perspective on whether this variation reflects inherent modeling limitations or task dissimilarity would be valuable. Does grouping these tasks into a unified framework ultimately prove effective?
5.  Please include illustrative examples of the various datasets used, showcasing both prototypical sentence pairs and those that pose challenges for classification.
6.  The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks, but is this due to the inherent nature of the task (is it more generalizable) or the size of the dataset? How can this be determined?

Despite its unfinished state, this paper presents an exciting approach that could stimulate significant discussion, and I would welcome its publication *in a more polished form*. I acknowledge that this assessment may differ from those of other reviewers.

Regarding minor linguistic points:

*   "Weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used where “weight” and “weighted” are more appropriate.
*   There are several misspellings of “sentence” (appearing as “sentene”).
*   The meaning of "interpunction" is unclear.
*   One instance of "world overlap" should be "word overlap."



Contributions: 
The most significant contribution of this work is the framing of diverse sentence pair scoring tasks under a unified framework, encouraging a shift towards more generalizable models and evaluation methodologies. Furthermore, the introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset represents a valuable contribution to the field, addressing limitations found in existing datasets for Answer Sentence Selection. Finally, the systematic evaluation of various models across multiple tasks and the demonstration of effective cross-task transfer learning provide useful insights for future research.

Strengths:
1. **Unified Framework:** The proposal to view diverse sentence pair scoring tasks as instances of a common underlying problem is a strong point. This framing promotes the development of more generalizable models, rather than task-specific solutions. It has the potential to streamline research and enable more meaningful comparisons.
2. **New Dataset:** The introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset is a significant contribution. The authors clearly articulate the shortcomings of existing datasets, particularly the wang dataset, and demonstrate a clear need for a larger, more balanced alternative. The methodology for creating this new dataset, leveraging the YodaQA system, is well-described.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation of a range of both neural and non-neural models across multiple tasks. This systematic approach allows for a clear comparison of different approaches and a better understanding of their strengths and weaknesses. The inclusion of confidence intervals in the reported results is particularly commendable, addressing the inherent randomness in neural model performance. 

Weaknesses:
1. **Incomplete Results & Discussion:** A significant weakness is the incomplete results table and the corresponding lack of in-depth discussion. While the authors present a wealth of data, the analysis feels superficial. The impact of various modeling choices and the reasons for performance differences across tasks are not sufficiently explored.
2. **Dataset Delivery:** The abstract promises a new challenging dataset but the delivered dataset is not described in sufficient detail to justify this claim. Its novelty is limited to its size and origin, and the authors do not convincingly demonstrate its superior difficulty or unique properties.
3. **Transfer Learning Explanation:** While the positive results of transfer learning are encouraging, the authors do not adequately address *why* the Ubu. RNN model proves particularly effective. Is it the nature of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, its size, or some other factor? Further investigation and discussion are needed to understand the underlying mechanisms driving these results.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the results, explaining the observed performance differences between models and across tasks?
2. Please clarify the novelty and challenge level of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset compared to existing alternatives. What specific characteristics make it more suitable for evaluating sentence pair scoring models?
3. Could you elaborate on the reasons for the success of the Ubu. RNN model in transfer learning? Have you conducted experiments to isolate the influence of dataset size and task characteristics?
4. Could you provide example instances from each of the datasets used, demonstrating both easy and difficult cases for the models?



Additional Comments:
The review mentions minor grammatical errors – these are relatively unimportant but should be addressed during the final polishing of the paper. The term "interpunction" should be checked for correct spelling and meaning.
