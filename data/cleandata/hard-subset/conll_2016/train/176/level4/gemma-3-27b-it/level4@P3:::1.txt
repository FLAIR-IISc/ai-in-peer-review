This paper proposes a novel framing of several sentence pair scoring tasks – including Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE), and Paraphrasing – as instances of a more general task: understanding semantic relations between sentences. The core idea is to investigate learning generally-applicable neural network models for this family of tasks, pursuing a unified approach instead of task-specific solutions. This is an exciting proposal with the potential to drive significant discussion and further research.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its attempt to unify diverse sentence pair scoring tasks under a single framework and establish a standardized evaluation methodology. While the concept of applying a unified model to multiple tasks is not entirely novel, the paper's comprehensive approach – encompassing dataset analysis, baseline comparisons, and the introduction of new datasets – represents a valuable step towards a more coherent research landscape. A secondary contribution is the introduction of two new datasets, yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, designed to address limitations in existing benchmarks. Finally, the initial exploration of cross-task transfer learning provides preliminary evidence supporting the feasibility of learning general semantic representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Unified Framework & Benchmarking:** The paper’s most significant strength is its attempt to consolidate a fragmented research area. By framing diverse tasks as instances of a common “sentence pair scoring” problem (f2), the authors provide a clear rationale for comparative evaluation. The rigorous benchmarking against both neural and non-neural baselines, alongside a detailed discussion of evaluation metrics (including the introduction of MRR for next utterance ranking), is particularly commendable and establishes a strong foundation for future work.
2. **Dataset Contribution:** The introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop datasets addresses clear deficiencies in existing benchmarks. The authors convincingly demonstrate the shortcomings of the widely used wang dataset and offer a more robust alternative, potentially leading to more reliable and reproducible research results. The detailed discussion of dataset characteristics (Fig. 1) is helpful and informative.
3. **Initial Transfer Learning Results:** The demonstration of positive transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset (Ubu. RNN) provides encouraging evidence for the feasibility of learning general semantic representations. This result suggests that models can indeed acquire knowledge from one task that benefits performance on others, bolstering the central hypothesis of the paper.
4. **Comprehensive Model Overview:** The paper provides a detailed overview of various neural network architectures – including RNNs, CNNs, and attention-based models – and their application to the sentence pair scoring task. This broad coverage is valuable for researchers entering the field and provides a useful reference point for future model development.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incomplete Results & Limited Discussion:** The paper presents a substantial amount of experimental data, but the discussion of the results feels disproportionately brief. Many tables lack complete entries (e.g., the initially mentioned missing results table), and the authors do not fully explore the implications of the observed performance variations across different tasks. A more in-depth analysis is crucial to draw meaningful conclusions.
2. **Dataset Promise Unfulfilled:** The abstract mentions the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, but this promise feels partially unfulfilled. While the introduced datasets are valuable contributions, they don’t appear to fundamentally represent a significant leap in difficulty or complexity compared to existing benchmarks. The authors need to clarify this point.
3. **Task Heterogeneity & Unified Modeling:** The paper acknowledges performance variations across tasks, raising the question of whether these tasks are truly amenable to a unified modeling approach. The authors should explicitly address this concern and provide a more nuanced discussion of the potential limitations of their framework. Is the observed performance variation due to modeling limitations, inherent task differences, or a combination of both?
4. **Lack of Error Analysis & Illustrative Examples:** The paper would benefit significantly from including examples of sentence pairs that pose challenges for the models, as well as a detailed error analysis. Providing illustrative instances of successful and failed classifications would offer valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.
5. **Minor Technical Issues:** The paper contains several minor language errors, including inconsistent use of "weigh/weighed" vs. "weight/weighted," spelling mistakes ("sentene"), and unclear terminology ("interpunction," "world overlap"). While these issues are relatively minor, they detract from the overall polish of the manuscript.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the factors contributing to performance variations across different tasks? Specifically, what characteristics of each task might explain why certain models perform better or worse?
2.  Regarding the claim of a new, more challenging dataset, can you elaborate on the intended level of difficulty and how the introduced datasets achieve this?
3.  You mention the importance of dataset size for transfer learning. Could you provide further experiments investigating the interplay between dataset size and transfer learning performance? Specifically, how does the size of the source dataset (e.g., Ubuntu Dialogue) affect the gains observed on target tasks?
4.  Could you provide some example sentence pairs (both prototypical and challenging) from the new datasets to illustrate the nature of the tasks and the types of semantic relationships being assessed?

**Additional Comments:**

* The authors should ensure consistency in terminology and proofread the manuscript carefully to eliminate minor language errors.
* The URL for the dataset-sts framework was redacted in the provided text. Please ensure this is included in the final submission.



Despite its current limitations, this paper presents a promising and potentially impactful contribution to the field of semantic sentence pair scoring. Addressing the identified weaknesses and responding to the questions raised above would significantly strengthen the manuscript and enhance its value to the research community. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors will address these points in a revised version.
