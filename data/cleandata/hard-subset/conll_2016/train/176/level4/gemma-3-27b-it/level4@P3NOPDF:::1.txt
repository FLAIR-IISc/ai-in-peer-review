This paper presents a novel and, to my knowledge, original approach to sentence pair scoring tasks – Answer Set Scoring, Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE), Paraphrase Identification, and others – by framing them as instances of a more general problem: understanding semantic relations between sentences. The authors further investigate the potential of learning generally-applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this a promising and exciting proposal, worthy of presentation at CoNLL and further research.

However, my primary concern is that the paper feels incomplete in its current form. Acceptance should be contingent upon significant revisions to address several key issues outlined below.

Firstly, Table 1 requires completion to fully present the results. Secondly, given the breadth of reported results across multiple tasks, the corresponding discussion is surprisingly brief. A much more thorough interpretation and analysis of the findings are necessary. The authors should delve deeper into *why* the models perform as they do.

Thirdly, the abstract promises the introduction of a new, more challenging dataset, a promise that the paper does not currently fulfill. This discrepancy needs to be rectified, either by delivering the dataset or by revising the abstract to accurately reflect the paper’s scope.

Fourthly, the results demonstrate considerable variation in performance across different tasks. The authors should investigate the reasons behind these discrepancies. What factors contribute to the models' success on some tasks and their failure on others? Even if definitive answers are elusive, a discussion of the authors’ hypotheses – concerning whether these variations stem from modeling limitations or inherent differences between the tasks themselves – would be valuable. A crucial question to address is whether framing these tasks as a unified whole is, in fact, a fruitful approach.

Fifthly, the inclusion of example instances from each dataset would significantly enhance the paper. Providing both prototypical sentence pairs and examples that prove challenging for classification will help readers better understand the nuances of the tasks and the model’s behavior. 

Finally, the paper recommends the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model for application to new tasks. However, it is unclear whether this recommendation is driven by the model’s ability to generalize to a more fundamental underlying task, or simply by the size of the dataset it was trained on. The authors should address this point, and suggest ways to determine which factor is more influential.

Despite these shortcomings, the core idea presented in this paper is compelling and has the potential to stimulate valuable discussion within the field. I would be pleased to see this work published, provided the aforementioned issues are addressed in a revised version.

I acknowledge that my assessment may differ from that of other reviewers.

Regarding minor linguistic points:

* The terms "weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used where "weight" and "weighted" are more appropriate.
* Several instances of "sentene" appear throughout the text; these should be corrected to "sentence."
* The term "interpunction" is unclear and requires clarification (likely a typo for "punctuation").
* One instance uses "world overlap" instead of the intended "word overlap."
