This paper proposes a novel approach – to the best of my knowledge – by framing several sentence pair scoring tasks (such as Answer Set Scoring, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Paraphrase Identification, among others) as instances of a more general task: understanding the semantic relations between two sentences. Furthermore, the authors investigate the potential of learning generally-applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this to be an exciting proposal, worthy of presentation at CoNLL and further discussion and investigation.

My main concern with the paper is that it feels incomplete in its current form. I recommend acceptance for publication only with the proviso that several updates are made before the final version. Specifically:

1. The first results table needs to be completed.
2. Given the substantial number of individual results presented, the written discussion is disproportionately short. Much more interpretation and discussion of these results is needed.
3. The abstract promises the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, which the paper does not currently deliver. This discrepancy needs to be addressed.
4. The results vary considerably across different tasks. The authors should investigate *why* the models succeed for some tasks but fail for others. Even if definitive answers are elusive, their insights into whether these variations stem from modeling limitations or inherent dissimilarities between the tasks would be valuable. Does grouping these tasks into a unified framework prove effective?
5. Please include illustrative examples of the various datasets used, showcasing both prototypical sentence pairs and those that present challenges for classification.
6. The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks, but it's unclear whether this recommendation is due to the inherent generality of the task itself or simply the size of the dataset. How can this question be resolved?

Despite its unpolished nature, this is an exciting approach that has the potential to generate significant discussion, and I would be happy to see it published in a more complete form. 

I recognize that this assessment may not be shared by all reviewers.

Some minor linguistic points:

* “Weigh” and “weighed” are consistently used in contexts requiring “weight” and “weighted.”
* There are several misspellings of “sentence” (appearing as “sentene”).
* The term “interpunction” is unclear; is this intended to be “punctuation”?
* One instance uses “world overlap” instead of “word overlap.”
