This paper introduces a novel approach – to the best of my knowledge – by framing several sentence pair scoring tasks, such as Answer Set Scoring, Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE), and Paraphrase Identification, as instances of a broader task: understanding semantic relations between sentences. Furthermore, the authors explore the feasibility of developing generally applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this a promising proposal deserving of presentation at CoNLL and further research.

However, the paper currently feels incomplete and should only be accepted conditionally, contingent upon substantial revisions before the final version. Specifically:

1.  The first results table requires completion.
2.  Given the extensive number of results presented, the accompanying discussion is insufficient. A more in-depth interpretation and analysis of the findings are needed.
3.  The abstract claims the introduction of a new, more challenging dataset, but this claim is not substantiated within the paper. This discrepancy must be addressed.
4.  The model’s performance exhibits considerable variation across different tasks. The authors should investigate the reasons for these discrepancies – why the models succeed in some cases and fail in others. Even without definitive answers, their insights into whether these variations stem from modeling limitations or inherent task dissimilarities would be valuable. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand whether grouping these tasks under a unified framework is justified.
5.  Please include illustrative examples from each dataset, showcasing both representative sentence pairs and those that prove particularly challenging for classification.
6.  The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks, but it is unclear whether this recommendation is based on the data’s characteristics (i.e., its generality) or the dataset’s size. Clarification on this point, and a method for determining the relevant factor, would be beneficial.

Despite these shortcomings, the paper presents an exciting approach with the potential to stimulate significant discussion, and I would welcome its publication in a more polished form. 

I acknowledge that my assessment may differ from those of other reviewers.

Regarding minor language issues:

*   The words "weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used where "weight" and "weighted" are more appropriate.
*   Several instances of “sentence” are misspelled as “sentene.”
*   The term “interpunction” is unclear and should be verified.
*   One instance uses “world overlap” instead of the correct term, “word overlap.”
