

This paper proposes a novel (to my knowledge) approach to treating multiple sentence pair scoring tasks (e.g., Answer Set Scoring, RTE, Paraphrasing) as instances of a broader task: understanding semantic relations between two sentences. It further investigates the potential of learning generally applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this to be an exciting proposal worthy of presentation at CoNLL and further discussion and investigation.

The main issue I have is that the paper feels unfinished. It should be accepted for publication only pending the following revisions for the final version:
1.  Complete the first results table.
2.  The written discussion of results is excessively brief given the large number of individual results; significantly more interpretation and analysis of the results is required.
3.  The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset, which the paper fails to deliver; this inconsistency must be resolved.
4.  Results vary substantially across tasks; an investigation into why models succeed or fail on specific tasks is needed. Even without definitive answers, the authors should address whether task dissimilarity or modeling limitations explain this variation, and whether grouping these tasks into a unified framework is valid.
5.  Include example instances from all datasets used, featuring both prototypical sentence pairs and problematic pairs for classification.
6.  The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks; clarify whether this is due to the nature of the data (a more general task) or dataset size, and how this could be determined.

Despite its current unpolished state, this is an exciting approach with significant potential to generate valuable discussion, and I would be happy to see it published in a more finished form.

Minor language points:
*   Consistently use "weight" and "weighted" instead of "weigh" and "weighed".
*   Correct multiple misspellings of "sentence" as "sentene".
*   Clarify the term "interpunction".
*   Correct "world overlap" to "word overlap" in one instance.
