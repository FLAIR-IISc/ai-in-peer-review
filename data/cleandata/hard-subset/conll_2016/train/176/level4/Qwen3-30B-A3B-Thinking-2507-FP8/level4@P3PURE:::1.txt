

This paper proposes treating a number of sentence pair scoring tasks (e.g., Answer Set Scoring, RTE, Paraphrasing) as instances of a more general task: understanding semantic relations between two sentences. Furthermore, it investigates the potential of learning generally applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. This is an exciting proposal worthy of presentation at CoNLL and further discussion and investigation.

The main issue with the paper is that it feels unfinished. It should be accepted for publication only subject to the following revisions for the final version:

1.  **Complete the first results table:** The current table lacks essential entries.
2.  **Expand the results discussion:** The written discussion of the large number of individual results is excessively brief. Much more interpretation and analysis of the results is urgently needed.
3.  **Address dataset promise:** The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset, but the paper does not deliver this. This incongruity must be resolved.
4.  **Analyze task-specific performance:** Results vary significantly across tasks. Investigation into *why* models succeed or fail on specific tasks is required. Even without definitive answers, the authors should discuss whether the variation stems from modeling limitations or inherent dissimilarities between the tasks. Does grouping them into a unified whole truly work?
5.  **Include representative examples:** Provide example instances from each dataset, including both prototypical sentence pairs and problematic pairs for classification.
6.  **Clarify Ubu.RNN justification:** The Ubu.RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks. Is this due to the nature of the data (indicating a more general task) or the size of the datasets? The authors should address how to determine the answer to this question.

Despite its current unpolished state, this is an exciting approach with significant potential to generate interesting discussion. I would welcome its publication in a more polished form.

Minor language points:
*   "Weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used where "weight" and "weighted" are required.
*   Several instances of "sentene" instead of "sentence".
*   "Interpunction" is unclear; likely a typo for "punctuation".
*   "World overlap" instead of "word overlap".
