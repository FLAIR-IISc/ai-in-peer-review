

This paper presents an exciting and potentially significant proposal: framing multiple sentence pair scoring tasks (Answer Set Scoring, RTE, Paraphrasing, etc.) as instances of a unified semantic relation understanding task, and investigating the feasibility of general-purpose neural models for this family. The core idea is compelling and merits presentation at CoNLL, as it opens avenues for broader theoretical insights and practical model transfer. However, the current manuscript requires substantial revision before acceptance.

The primary concern is that the paper feels incomplete and underdeveloped. While the conceptual contribution is strong, the execution lacks the rigor and depth expected for publication. Specific revisions are mandatory:

1.  **Complete the Results Table:** The first results table is incomplete and must be finalized with all reported metrics.
2.  **Substantially Expand Results Discussion:** The written discussion of results is disproportionately brief given the volume of data presented. A detailed, nuanced analysis is required, interpreting *why* models succeed or fail on specific tasks and how results compare across the task family. Merely listing scores is insufficient.
3.  **Resolve Abstract-Dataset Incongruity:** The abstract explicitly promises a new, more challenging dataset, yet the paper does not deliver one. This discrepancy must be resolved—either by including the promised dataset, revising the abstract to accurately reflect the datasets used, or clearly explaining the omission.
4.  **Address Task Variability:** Results vary significantly across tasks. The authors must provide a systematic analysis (even if preliminary) exploring *why* models generalize well to some tasks but struggle with others. Is this due to inherent differences in the tasks themselves, limitations of the model architecture, or dataset characteristics? A clear discussion of whether the tasks truly form a coherent "unified whole" for modeling is essential.
5.  **Include Representative Examples:** Provide concrete examples from each dataset used, showcasing both typical, correctly classified sentence pairs *and* challenging cases where classification fails. This is crucial for understanding model behavior and task nuances.
6.  **Clarify Ubu.RNN Transfer Rationale:** The paper recommends Ubu.RNN for transfer learning on new tasks. The authors must clarify *why* this model is preferred—is it due to the nature of the semantic relation tasks themselves, or primarily because of the dataset sizes used? A brief analysis addressing this distinction is needed.

The paper's potential is clear, but its current form does not adequately demonstrate it. The proposed framework is promising, yet the lack of thorough results analysis, incomplete reporting, and unresolved claims undermine its contribution. Addressing the points above would transform this into a strong, publishable contribution. Without these revisions, the paper cannot be accepted in its current state.

Minor language issues also require correction:
*   Replace "weigh" with "weight" (e.g., "weight the evidence").
*   Correct consistent misspellings of "sentence" (e.g., "sentene").
*   Replace "interpunction" with "punctuation".
*   Correct "world overlap" to "word overlap".

This work addresses a meaningful gap in NLP, and the core proposal is worthy of dissemination. However, its current presentation is unpolished and lacks the necessary depth. The authors must deliver a significantly revised version addressing all points above to merit publication at CoNLL. I recommend acceptance contingent upon these substantial revisions.
