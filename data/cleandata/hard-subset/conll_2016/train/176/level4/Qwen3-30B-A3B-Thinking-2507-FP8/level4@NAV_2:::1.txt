

Summary:
This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, treating various NLP problems (Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment, Paraphrasing, etc.) as instances of a single semantic relationship understanding task. The authors investigate the potential of learning general-purpose neural network models applicable across this family of tasks.

Main Contributions:
1. A novel unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks that treats multiple NLP problems under a single semantic relationship understanding paradigm.
2. Introduction of new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address critical limitations of existing datasets, including small size, uneven candidate distributions, and noisy performance measurements.
3. Demonstration of transfer learning success through the Ubu.RNN model, showing that a model trained on one task (Ubuntu Dialogue Dataset) can be effectively adapted to other tasks, suggesting the feasibility of generic task-independent text comprehension models.

Strengths:
1. The unified framework represents a significant conceptual contribution that could help consolidate fragmented research in the field. By treating various sentence pair scoring tasks under a single framework, the work provides a clearer research direction and methodology for the community.
2. The new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) address critical limitations of existing datasets, including small size, uneven candidate distributions, and noisy performance measurements. The baseline performances on these datasets are notably lower, suggesting more room for future model improvements.
3. The transfer learning results with the Ubu.RNN model are promising and demonstrate the potential of cross-task transfer learning. The consistent performance of Ubu.RNN across multiple tasks suggests that a quest for generic neural models capable of task-independent text comprehension is becoming a meaningful pursuit.

Weaknesses:
1. The first results table is incomplete, which hinders the ability to fully assess model performances across tasks. This is particularly problematic given the large number of experiments conducted.
2. The written discussion of results is insufficiently detailed. The paper lacks adequate interpretation of why certain models succeed or fail across different tasks, which is crucial for understanding the broader implications of the work.
3. The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset but the paper does not fully deliver on this promise. The paper introduces datasets but doesn't clearly articulate how they represent a significant advancement in challenge or difficulty compared to existing datasets.
4. The paper does not sufficiently investigate why models perform differently across tasks. A deeper analysis of model failures and successes across the various tasks would strengthen the contribution and help determine whether the unified framework is truly effective.
5. The recommendation of the Ubu.RNN model as the primary model for new tasks lacks justification. It's unclear whether this model's success is due to the nature of the data (making it a more general task) or the size of the dataset (Ubuntu Dialogue being larger), which is a critical question for the field.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide example instances of the various datasets used (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop, Ubuntu Dialogue, etc.), including both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose challenges for classification?
2. Please clarify whether the Ubu.RNN model's success is due to the nature of the data (making it a more general task) or rather the size of the dataset (Ubuntu Dialogue being larger). How can we determine which factor is more significant?

Minor language issues:
- Consistent use of "weigh" instead of "weight" in contexts requiring the latter
- Several misspellings of "sentence" as "sentene"
- "interpunction" should be "punctuation"
- "world overlap" should be "word overlap"
