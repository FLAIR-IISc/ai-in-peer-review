

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks (e.g., Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment, Next Utterance Ranking), arguing that these can be treated as instances of a single semantic comprehension problem. The authors' key contributions are: (1) a novel software framework (dataset-sts) enabling cross-task evaluation and model reuse; (2) a new, larger Answer Sentence Selection dataset (yodaqa/large2470) addressing limitations in existing datasets; and (3) evidence that transfer learning across tasks can improve performance, suggesting the feasibility of universal sentence comprehension models. The authors' strongest contribution is the framework, which provides a standardized evaluation methodology and open-source implementation, while the dataset and transfer learning results are secondary but significant.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel evaluation framework**: The authors' dataset-sts framework is a major contribution. It standardizes model training and evaluation across diverse tasks, addressing a critical fragmentation in the literature. The open-source implementation (available on GitHub) and rigorous statistical reporting (with 95% confidence intervals) significantly improve reproducibility and comparability of results. This framework establishes a new standard for future work in sentence pair scoring.  

2. **High-quality dataset creation**: The yodaqa/large2470 dataset effectively addresses key limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets (e.g., small candidate sets, unstable metrics). Its construction—using curated questions and YodaQA-retrieved sentences—creates a more realistic and challenging benchmark than Wang et al.'s dataset. The dataset's larger scale (2470 questions vs. Wang's 2042) and improved characteristics will enable more meaningful model comparisons.  

3. **Transfer learning evidence**: The paper provides compelling evidence that models trained on one task (Ubuntu Dialogue) transfer effectively to others (e.g., Answer Sentence Selection), outperforming task-specific baselines. This supports the core thesis that a unified semantic comprehension model is feasible, a significant step toward the authors' stated goal of "universal f2-type measures." The Ubu. RNN model consistently ranks as top performer across tasks, demonstrating practical utility.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Incomplete results and insufficient analysis**: The paper's primary weakness is its lack of thorough results discussion. The first results table (Table 2) is incomplete, and the written analysis (Section 4.4) is too brief to justify the claims about model performance. The authors fail to address *why* models succeed or fail across tasks (e.g., why RNNs outperform CNNs on Ubuntu but not on smaller datasets), which is critical for evaluating their unified framework.  

2. **Misaligned abstract and content**: The abstract promises a "new, more challenging dataset," but the paper fails to deliver a *new* dataset (yodaqa/large2470 is derived from existing sources with additional curation, not novel collection). This creates confusion about the dataset's novelty and undermines the paper's contribution. The authors should clarify this in the final version.  

3. **Lack of dataset examples**: The paper omits concrete examples of sentence pairs from all datasets, including problematic cases where models fail. Such examples would help readers understand the task challenges and model limitations. This omission weakens the paper's utility as a benchmark.  

4. **Unclear justification for Ubu. RNN recommendation**: The authors recommend Ubu. RNN as the primary model for new tasks but do not clarify whether its success stems from the model's architecture or the Ubuntu dataset's size. Without this distinction, readers cannot determine whether transfer learning is universally beneficial or dataset-dependent.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The abstract claims a "new, more challenging dataset," but yodaqa/large2470 is derived from existing sources. How does this dataset differ from prior work (e.g., Wang, WikiQA) in *novelty* and *challenging characteristics*? Please clarify in the final version.  

2. The paper shows significant performance variations across tasks (e.g., strong on Ubuntu, weak on SNLI). Is this due to task dissimilarity (e.g., dialogue vs. entailment) or model limitations? Could the authors provide a qualitative analysis of model failures?  

3. The Ubu. RNN model's success is attributed to transfer learning. Is this due to the model's architecture, the Ubuntu dataset's size, or both? Can the authors test whether a smaller dataset (e.g., Wang) would yield similar transfer gains?  

**Additional Comments**  
- Correct consistent grammatical errors: "weigh" → "weight," "sentene" → "sentence," "interpunction" → "punctuation," "world overlap" → "word overlap."  
- The paper's core idea is promising but requires substantial polishing. The framework and dataset are strong contributions, but the current presentation lacks depth in results interpretation and clarity on dataset novelty.  
- With the requested revisions (particularly completing results, adding examples, and clarifying dataset novelty), this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.
