This paper proposes, to my knowledge, a novel step: reframing a range of sentence-pair scoring tasks (e.g., Answer Set Scoring, RTE, Paraphrasing, among others) as instances of a broader task of modeling semantic relations between two sentences. The authors further explore whether generally applicable neural network models can be learned across this family of tasks. I find this an exciting proposal that is well worth presentation at CoNLL and further discussion and investigation.

However, the paper currently feels unfinished. I would support acceptance only on the condition that the final version addresses the following points:
1) The first results table needs to be completed.
2) Given the large number of individual results, the written discussion is far too brief. Much more interpretation and analysis of the results is needed.
3) The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset, but the paper does not seem to deliver it. This inconsistency should be resolved.
4) Performance varies considerably across tasks. Please investigate how and why the models fail on some tasks and succeed on others. Even if a definitive answer is not possible, it would be valuable to present the authors’ view on whether these differences stem from modeling limitations or from genuine dissimilarities among the tasks. Does it truly make sense to group them into a unified framework?
5) Please include example instances from the various datasets used, including both prototypical sentence pairs and cases that are difficult to classify.
6) The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks. Is this recommendation driven by the nature of the task (i.e., greater generality) or by the dataset size? How can we empirically distinguish between these explanations?

Despite its unpolished state, this is an exciting approach that could stimulate valuable discussion. I would be happy to see it published in a more finished form. I recognize that other reviewers may not share this view.

Minor language issues:
- “weigh” and “weighed” are consistently used where “weight” and “weighted” are appropriate.
- There are several misspellings of “sentence” (as “sentene”).
- What is meant by “interpunction”?
- One instance of “world overlap” should be “word overlap.”
