Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper argues for a unified view of sentence-pair problems (e.g., answer sentence selection, RTE, paraphrasing, semantic similarity, next-utterance ranking) as instances of a general sentence pair scoring function. It proposes task-agnostic neural architectures with lightweight task-specific adapters.
- It presents a shared evaluation framework with standardized baselines, metrics, and statistical reporting across multiple datasets, and introduces new datasets for answer selection.
- It demonstrates cross-task transfer learning, showing that a model pretrained on Ubuntu Dialogs (Ubu. RNN) can be adapted to other tasks with strong results.

Main strengths
- Clear unifying perspective: Framing diverse tasks under a single f2-style scoring function is timely and valuable, encouraging reusable models and systematic comparison.
- Solid empirical framework: The paper evaluates multiple neural architectures and IR baselines across several tasks/datasets, reports averages over 16 runs, and includes confidence intervals—addressing common reproducibility and variance issues in this area.
- Practical tools: The open-source dataset-sts framework (with KeraSTS) lowers the barrier to replicating and extending the experiments.
- Transfer learning evidence: The finding that Ubu. RNN transfers strongly to other tasks is compelling and suggests a promising direction for universal sentence comprehension models.

Main weaknesses (major issues to address for acceptance)
- Incomplete presentation: The first results table is unfinished. Given the number of experiments, missing entries undermine the empirical contribution and make it hard to assess the claims.
- Insufficient analysis of results: The written discussion is too brief relative to the breadth of experiments. The paper needs deeper interpretation—e.g., why particular models win/lose per task, sensitivity to dataset characteristics (size, label noise, candidate set size), and when attention helps.
- Dataset promise vs. delivery: The abstract claims a new, more challenging answer selection dataset; however, the paper does not clearly present it (e.g., construction details, splits, licenses, descriptive statistics, and examples). This mismatch should be resolved with a complete dataset description and release details.
- Variability across tasks unexplained: Performance varies substantially between tasks. The paper should analyze whether gaps stem from model limitations (e.g., capacity, architecture choices, lack of token-level supervision) or task dissimilarity (e.g., dialog vs. encyclopedic text, domain-specific jargon, candidate set size). Even qualitative insights would help justify the “unified” framing.
- Lack of illustrative examples: Readers need concrete example pairs (both prototypical and challenging) from each dataset to understand the phenomena the models capture and where they fail.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- New dataset(s): Please provide a full description of the newly introduced datasets (e.g., yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop): data acquisition, cleaning/denoising, labeling procedure, overlap with existing datasets (especially wang), train/dev/test splits, statistics (pair counts, candidate set size distributions), and license/availability. Include example instances.
- Missing table entries: When will the incomplete results be added? Will you provide per-task confidence intervals everywhere to support claims of significance?
- Cross-task variability: What empirical evidence do you have that performance gaps are due to modeling vs. task divergence? Can you correlate model wins/losses with specific dataset properties (size, length, vocabulary, candidate pool size, overlap features)?
- Ubu. RNN transfer: Is its advantage due to the nature of dialog modeling (more general semantic coverage) or simply to pretraining scale? Have you controlled for pretraining size by, e.g., pretraining on a different large corpus or subsampling Ubuntu? What happens if you pretrain the same architecture on large answer selection data?
- Attention and overlap features: You note strong effects of attention and word overlap features in IR-like tasks. Can you quantify how much each contributes, and whether token-level supervision (e.g., marking answer spans) would further bridge gaps?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Expand the results discussion: Organize per-task analyses with error typologies, ablations (e.g., remove BM25 signal, remove overlap flags), and model-specific observations (CNN vs. RNN vs. RNN-CNN vs. attention) to substantiate the insights claimed in Section 4.4.
- Clarify evaluation protocols: Given the high variance on wang, make explicit your tuning strategy and justify selecting model checkpoints. Consider reporting test performance distributions or box plots to contextualize variability.
- Release artifacts: Ensure that all code, pretrained models, and datasets (with documented preprocessing) are accessible to support the paper’s reproducibility argument.

Minor language and style issues
- Use “weight/weighted” instead of “weigh/weighed” where appropriate.
- Correct misspellings of “sentence” (currently appears as “sentene” in places).
- Replace “interpunction” with “punctuation.”
- Fix “world overlap” to “word overlap.”

Overall assessment
This is an exciting and timely proposal with useful tools and promising transfer learning results. However, the paper currently feels unfinished. I would support acceptance contingent on completing the missing results, substantially expanding the analysis, and properly documenting the newly introduced datasets with examples.
