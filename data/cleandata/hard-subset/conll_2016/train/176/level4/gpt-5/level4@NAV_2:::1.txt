This paper argues for treating several sentence-pair scoring problems (e.g., answer sentence selection, RTE, paraphrasing, among others) as instances of a broader task: modeling semantic relationships between two sentences. It also examines whether broadly applicable neural models can be trained to handle this family of tasks. I find the premise compelling and suitable for presentation at CoNLL, meriting further discussion and follow-up work.

My primary concern is that the submission feels incomplete. I would support acceptance only contingent upon the following revisions being made for the camera-ready version:
1 - The initial results table must be finalized.
2 - Given the breadth of reported results, the narrative analysis is far too brief. Substantially more interpretation and discussion of the findings is needed.
3 - The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset, but the body of the paper does not clearly deliver on this promise. This discrepancy should be resolved.
4 - Performance varies considerably across tasks. Please investigate and discuss why the models succeed on some tasks and fail on others—whether this reflects modeling limitations versus fundamental differences among tasks. To what extent is it valid to unify them?
5 - Include illustrative examples from each dataset—both typical sentence pairs and hard cases that challenge the classifiers.
6 - The Ubu. RNN transfer model is recommended for new tasks. Is this due to the generality of the underlying task or primarily the dataset’s scale? How might we disentangle these factors?

Despite its rough edges, the approach is exciting and could stimulate valuable discussion. I would be pleased to see it published—provided it appears in a more finished state. I acknowledge that other reviewers may view this differently.

Minor language notes:
- “weigh”/“weighed” are repeatedly used where “weight”/“weighted” are appropriate
