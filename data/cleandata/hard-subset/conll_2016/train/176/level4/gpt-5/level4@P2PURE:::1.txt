This paper proposes, to my knowledge for the first time, reframing several sentence-pair scoring tasks (e.g., Answer Set Scoring, RTE, Paraphrasing, among others) as instances of a broader task: understanding semantic relations between two sentences. It further explores whether generally applicable neural network models can be learned for this family of tasks. I find this an exciting idea that merits presentation at CoNLL and further discussion and investigation.

My main concern is that the paper feels unfinished. I would recommend acceptance only on the condition that the final version addresses the following:

1 - Complete the first results table.
2 - Given the large number of individual results, the written discussion is far too brief. Substantially more interpretation and analysis of the results is needed.
3 - The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset, which the paper does not appear to provide. This discrepancy needs to be resolved.
4 - Results vary considerably across tasks. Please investigate and discuss why the models fail on some tasks and succeed on others. Even if no definitive answer emerges, it would be valuable to hear the authors’ view on whether this reflects modeling limitations or genuine dissimilarities between tasks. Does it really make sense to group them into a unified whole?
5 - Include example instances from the datasets used, both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that are difficult to classify.
6 - The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks. Is this because of the nature of the data (i.e., a more general task) or due to the dataset’s size? How can we determine which factor is responsible?

Despite its unpolished state, this is an exciting approach that could generate valuable discussion, and I would be happy to see it published in a more finished form. I recognize that other reviewers may not share this view.

Minor language points:
- “weigh” and “weighed” are consistently used where “weight” and “weighted” are required.
- There are several misspellings of “sentence” (as “sentene”).
- What is “interpunction”?
- One instance of “world overlap” instead of “word overlap.”
