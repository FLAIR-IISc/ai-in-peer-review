Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes treating multiple sentence-pair tasks—such as Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE), Paraphrasing, Next Utterance Ranking, and Semantic Textual Similarity—as instances of a unified “sentence pair scoring” problem (an f2-type function). The authors argue for task-independent neural models with thin task-specific adapters.
- As I see it, the primary contributions are:
  1) A unifying experimental framework and evaluation methodology that run shared neural architectures across diverse f2 tasks, with stronger statistical reporting (multiple runs with confidence intervals) and comparisons to IR baselines.
  2) Broad cross-task empirical results and an initial demonstration of transfer learning (Ubu. RNN) that suggests the feasibility of reusable, task-agnostic sentence encoders.
  3) An open-source framework (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS) that promotes reproducibility and reuse.
- The abstract also claims new, more challenging datasets; however, the current manuscript does not clearly deliver or document them, which needs resolution.

Strengths
- Timely unification: Framing diverse problems under a single sentence-pair scoring paradigm is compelling and useful for the community. It encourages comparable evaluation and reusable modeling.
- Breadth of evaluation: Running a range of neural architectures (CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, attention) across multiple tasks and benchmarking against strong IR baselines is valuable. The finding that CNNs perform better on smaller datasets while RNNs benefit from larger ones aligns with prior observations and is useful guidance.
- Methodological rigor: Averaging over multiple random seeds and reporting confidence intervals addresses a well-known issue in current practice, where single-run numbers are often unstable and not statistically distinguishable.
- Transfer learning signal: The Ubu. RNN model pretrained on Ubuntu dialogue and finetuned on other tasks often outperforms task-specific training, providing promising evidence that a general sentence understanding model is attainable.
- Open-source infrastructure: Releasing the framework and adapters lowers barriers to entry, helping the community push towards comparable, extensible research.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/unfinished presentation: At least one results table is missing/unfinished. Given the paper’s empirical focus, such omissions are critical and must be fixed before publication.
- Insufficient analysis and interpretation: Despite many reported numbers, the narrative discussion is very brief. The paper needs substantially more interpretation of the cross-task trends, ablations where possible, and concrete takeaways for model and dataset design.
- Dataset claims vs. delivery: The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset. The manuscript does not clearly present, document, or release such a dataset (e.g., detailed statistics, splits, examples, licensing), creating an inconsistency that must be addressed.
- Limited error analysis and cross-task insight: The results vary substantially across tasks. The paper does not analyze why models succeed or fail in particular settings, nor whether differences are due to modeling capacity, task dissimilarity, or dataset artifacts. This analysis is essential to assess whether a unified approach is justified beyond convenience.
- Lack of qualitative examples: The paper would benefit from illustrative examples for each dataset (both prototypical pairs and hard/edge cases), helping readers understand what the models are learning—and where they break.
- Ambiguity around transfer learning gains: The recommendation of Ubu. RNN for new tasks is intriguing, but it is unclear whether improvements stem from the nature of the Ubuntu task (dialogue, technical domain, long contexts) or simply the size of the dataset. Without controls, the practical guidance is underspecified.

Questions to authors
- Dataset delivery: Which new datasets are actually introduced in this paper? Please provide clear documentation: construction process, statistics, splits, licensing, and representative examples. If the abstract overstates the contribution, revise accordingly.
- Missing results: Please complete the first results table and ensure all reported experiments are fully documented.
- Cross-task variance: What are the main failure modes by task (e.g., entity-heavy queries vs. compositional reasoning vs. discourse coherence)? Can you provide an error taxonomy or case studies that reveal where particular architectures (CNN/RNN/attention) help or hurt?
- Unification validity: To what extent do the evaluated tasks share the same underlying f2 phenomenon? Are there measurable divergences (e.g., overlap sensitivity, length effects, domain mismatch) that indicate when task-independent models are less appropriate?
- Ubu. RNN transfer: Can you disentangle the effect of pretraining dataset size from its nature? For example:
  - Pretrain on a large non-dialogue corpus of comparable size and compare.
  - Subsample Ubuntu to match smaller datasets and observe scaling behavior.
  - Report learning curves to show whether gains persist at matched data budgets.
- Statistical reporting: You highlight instability on wang; will you commit to reporting confidence intervals for all tasks and models in the final version? If not, please justify exceptions.

Additional comments (minor)
- Consistent word choice: Use “weight”/“weighted,” not “weigh”/“weighed,” when referring to features or combinations.
- Typos: Several instances of “sentene” should be “sentence.”
- Terminology: “Interpunction” is nonstandard; consider “punctuation.”
- Typo: “world overlap” should be “word overlap.”

Overall evaluation
This is an exciting and timely direction with meaningful methodological contributions, broad empirical coverage, and promising transfer-learning results. However, the submission currently feels unfinished. I would be happy to see it published, provided the authors:
- Complete missing tables,
- Substantially expand the analysis and discussion,
- Clarify and deliver the promised dataset contribution,
- Add qualitative examples and error analysis,
- Address the ambiguity around the Ubu. RNN transfer gains.

With these revisions, the paper would spark valuable discussion at CoNLL and provide a solid foundation for unified sentence-pair research.
