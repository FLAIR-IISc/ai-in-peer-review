This paper proposes, to my knowledge for the first time, treating several sentence-pair scoring tasks (e.g., Answer Set Scoring, RTE, Paraphrasing, among others) as instances of a broader problem: modeling semantic relations between two sentences. It also explores whether generally applicable neural architectures can be learned across this family of tasks. I find this an exciting and valuable direction, well suited to CoNLL and worthy of further discussion and investigation.

My main concern is that the paper feels unfinished. I would recommend acceptance only if the following issues are addressed in the final version:

1 - The first results table is incomplete and should be finalized.

2 - Given the large number of reported results, the written discussion is far too brief. Substantially more interpretation and analysis are needed to help readers understand the patterns, implications, and limitations of the findings.

3 - The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset, but the paper does not appear to deliver it. This discrepancy must be resolved—either by providing the dataset and its details or by revising the abstract.

4 - Performance varies considerably across tasks. Please investigate and discuss how and why the models fail on some tasks and succeed on others. Even if no definitive conclusion is reached, it would be valuable to hear the authors’ view on whether the variability arises from modeling choices or from substantive dissimilarities among the tasks. Does it truly make sense to group them within a single unified framework?

5 - Please include example instances from the datasets used—both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that are challenging for the models—to ground the discussion and illustrate error modes.

6 - The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks. Is this recommendation driven by the nature of the data (i.e., a more general task) or by the size of the dataset? How can we determine which factor is responsible? Clarifying this would meaningfully guide practitioners.

Despite its unpolished state, this is an exciting approach that could stimulate valuable discussion. I would be happy to see it published—provided it appears in a more finished form. I recognize that other reviewers may disagree.

Minor language points:
- “weigh”/“weighed” are consistently used where “weight”/“weighted” are required.
- There are multiple misspellings of “sentence” (as “sentene”).
- Please clarify what is meant by “interpunction.”
- One instance reads “world overlap” instead of “word overlap.”
