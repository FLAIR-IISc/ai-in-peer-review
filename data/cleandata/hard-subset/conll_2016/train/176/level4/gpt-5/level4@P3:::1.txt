Summary and Contributions
This paper argues for a unified view of sentence-pair scoring tasks (e.g., answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, STS, paraphrasing) as instances of learning a general function f2(s0, s1) capturing semantic relatedness. Within this framing, the authors:
1) Propose a unified evaluation framework and open-source toolkit (dataset-sts, PySTS, KeraSTS) that standardizes datasets, task adapters, and models, and reports performance with statistical rigor (multiple runs and confidence intervals).
2) Introduce new datasets for answer selection and property selection (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), designed to be larger and more realistic than commonly used alternatives, along with IR baselines for anchoring neural results.
3) Present cross-task transfer learning evidence (pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue followed by fine-tuning) that yields consistent gains, suggesting potential for task-independent sentence comprehension models.

Strengths
1) Unifying framework and tooling that improve comparability and reproducibility: The paper provides a coherent umbrella for diverse sentence-pair tasks, with a modular codebase and standardized adapters. Reporting results over 16 runs with 95% CIs directly addresses the well-known instability of single-run neural evaluations (e.g., Wang MRR variance). This has clear community value.
2) Breadth of empirical study across tasks and architectures: The paper evaluates CNNs, RNNs (GRU), RNN-CNN hybrids, and a simple attention mechanism across answer selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, and STS. This broad comparison helps situate model choices by dataset size and complexity (e.g., CNNs on smaller datasets vs. RNNs on larger).
3) Strong baselines and realistic IR considerations: Including BM25 and TF-IDF, using overlap features, and candidate pruning reflect real IR practices and provide meaningful baselines that many neural papers omit. The finding that IR baselines remain competitive is important.
4) New datasets likely to raise the bar: The proposed large2470 dataset appears more challenging (larger candidate sets per question, lower baseline performance), which should better differentiate models and reduce overfitting to small, uneven test sets like Wang.
5) Demonstrated transfer learning benefits: The Ubuntu-pretrained RNN substantially outperforms task-specific RNNs when fine-tuned on other tasks, giving credible evidence that cross-task representations are beneficial and feasible.

Weaknesses
1) The paper feels unfinished: (a) a key results table is incomplete; (b) the discussion is disproportionately brief given the number of experiments; (c) several promised elements in the abstract (e.g., full presentation of the new dataset) are not adequately delivered in the text. These must be addressed for camera-ready.
2) Limited analysis of why performance varies across tasks: The paper shows significant variation but offers little error analysis or diagnostic insights (e.g., where attention helps, failure modes for RNN-CNN, impact of sentence length, OOVs, NE-heavy cases). Without this, it is hard to assess whether a truly “unified” approach is appropriate vs. task-specific tailoring.
3) Ambiguity about the source of transfer learning gains: It is unclear whether the superiority of the Ubu. RNN stems primarily from the size of the Ubuntu dataset, the nature of the task (dialogue context predicting next utterance), or the architecture/training regime. This weakens the claim of task-independent “comprehension.”
4) Missing qualitative examples: The paper would benefit from representative examples across datasets (both easy and hard cases), illustrating the role of overlap features, attention, and model behavior. This is essential for understanding the models’ strengths and limitations.
5) Writing and clarity issues: Numerous typos and terminology issues (e.g., “weigh/weighed” vs. “weight/weighted,” “sentene,” “interpunction,” “world overlap”) detract from readability. Some dataset caveats (e.g., potential overlap between Wang and yodaqa) are mentioned but deserve explicit experimental safeguards and reporting.

Questions to Authors
- Dataset presentation: The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset. Please provide a clear, self-contained description and statistics (splits, candidate set sizes, labeling procedure, overlap with existing datasets), including release details.
- Data overlap and leakage: You note possible overlap between Wang and yodaqa. What procedures ensured no train/test leakage when models are trained on one and evaluated on the other? Please quantify any overlap and report sensitivity analyses.
- Failure analysis: Where do models fail vs. succeed across tasks? For example, do attention models help more with long contexts or entity-heavy questions? Provide per-category breakdowns (length, NE density, lexical overlap, paraphrastic variation).
- Transfer learning: How much of the Ubu. RNN’s advantage is attributable to pretraining data size vs. task characteristics? Did you try pretraining on other large tasks (e.g., SNLI) and compare? Any scaling curves relating source task size to target gains?
- Role of IR features: Given the strong influence of BM25 and overlap features (used in pruning and as inputs), how much of the final gains are due to these signals vs. learned sentence representations? Please ablate overlap features and BM25 pruning.
- Statistical reporting: You emphasize variance on Wang. Will you commit to reporting multiple-run averages and CIs for all tasks in the final version, and provide seeds and scripts to reproduce splits and runs?

Additional Comments on Writing
- Replace “weigh/weighed” with “weight/weighted.”
- Correct misspellings of “sentence” (currently “sentene” in places).
- Replace “interpunction” with “punctuation.”
- Fix “world overlap” to “word overlap.”

Overall Assessment
I find the unified framing, the open-source framework with statistically sound evaluation, and the transfer learning results to be timely and valuable. However, the paper in its current form appears incomplete and under-discussed. I would support acceptance conditional on: completing all results, expanding the analysis (including qualitative examples and ablations), clarifying the new dataset presentation, and addressing the writing issues.
