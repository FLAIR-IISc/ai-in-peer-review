Summary
The paper proposes treating a range of sentence-pair scoring tasks—such as answer sentence scoring/selection, recognizing textual entailment (RTE), and paraphrase identification—as instances of a broader task: modeling semantic relations between two sentences. The authors further explore whether a single, generally applicable neural architecture can be learned and transferred across tasks. This unifying perspective is timely and appealing, and the empirical exploration across multiple datasets has the potential to spark valuable discussion.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling motivation for unifying several related tasks under a semantic relation framework.
- Ambitious empirical scope, with results across a diverse set of benchmarks.
- Initial transfer learning findings are promising and practically relevant for low-resource tasks.
- If realized fully, the work could serve as a foundation for reusable sentence-pair encoders.

Main Concerns
The submission feels unfinished in its current form. The core idea is strong, but the presentation, analysis, and completeness of results do not yet match the ambition of the proposal. I would support acceptance provided the authors commit to substantial revisions for the camera-ready version.

Required Revisions for the Final Version
1) Complete the first results table. The paper should present a full set of results for all proposed models and baselines, including clear descriptions of metrics and splits used.

2) Substantially expand the analysis of results. Given the breadth of experiments, the write-up of findings is too brief. Please:
- Summarize cross-task trends (where the unified model helps or hurts).
- Discuss variance across datasets and provide statistical significance where appropriate.
- Contrast with strong baselines and prior SOTA, noting where improvements are meaningful versus marginal.

3) Resolve the abstract–content mismatch regarding the “new, more challenging dataset.” If a new dataset is indeed introduced, it must be described in sufficient detail (construction, size, domain, annotation, splits) and properly evaluated. If not, the abstract should be revised to avoid overpromising.

4) Provide cross-task error analysis. Results vary substantially by task. Please investigate:
- Common failure modes (e.g., lexical overlap traps, negation, coreference, reasoning over world knowledge).
- Cases of success that reveal what the model captures well.
- Whether deficiencies are primarily modeling issues (e.g., architecture capacity, lack of alignment mechanisms) or reflect genuine task heterogeneity that resists unification. Even a qualitative position, supported by examples, would be valuable.

5) Include representative examples from each dataset. Show both prototypical sentence pairs and challenging cases that lead to misclassification. This will ground the discussion and help readers interpret model behavior.

6) Clarify the recommendation of the Ubu. RNN transfer model. Is its advantage due to task generality or simply the size/domain of the training data? To disentangle these factors, please consider:
- Downsampling the source data to match other datasets and reporting the effect on transfer.
- Pretraining on alternative large corpora (if available) or multi-task mixtures to test for domain-driven gains.
- Reporting learning curves for transfer to quantify data efficiency and saturation.

7) Improve experimental detail and reproducibility. Please document:
- Dataset statistics (train/dev/test sizes, class balance), preprocessing, and tokenization.
- Model hyperparameters, training schedules, early stopping criteria, and regularization.
- Evaluation protocols, including whether development data is used for model selection and how test metrics are computed.
- Any ensembling or post-processing.
- Ideally, release code and pretrained models.

Additional Suggestions
- Discuss whether a single shared encoder with task-specific heads suffices, or whether lightweight task adaptation (e.g., adversarial domain training, adapters) might bridge performance gaps.
- Consider ablations (e.g., with/without attention or alignment modules) to identify which components drive cross-task transfer.
- Unify notation and define all acronyms on first use.

Recommendation
Borderline accept, contingent on the above revisions. The central idea is exciting and well-motivated, and the work could stimulate useful discussion at CoNLL. However, to realize its potential, the paper needs a more complete results presentation, deeper analysis, and clearer alignment between claims and evidence.

Minor Language and Style Issues
- Replace “weigh/weighed” with “weight/weighted” in the relevant statistical contexts.
- Correct misspellings of “sentence” (appears as “sentene” in several places).
- “Interpunction” should be “punctuation.”
- Correct “world overlap” to “word overlap.”
- Perform a careful proofreading pass for grammar, capitalization of dataset names, and consistent terminology.
