This paper proposes a novel framework for treating a number of sentence-pair scoring tasks—including Answer Set Scoring, RTE, and Paraphrasing—as instances of a more general task of understanding semantic relations. The authors further investigate the potential of learning generally-applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this to be an exciting and compelling proposal that is worthy of both presentation at CoNLL and further investigation by the community. The direction of unifying these related tasks under a single modeling paradigm is timely and has significant potential.

However, the main weakness of the paper in its current form is that it feels preliminary and unfinished. While the core idea is strong, the execution and presentation require substantial revision. My recommendation for acceptance is conditional on the authors addressing a number of major issues for the final camera-ready version.

**Major Points for Revision:**

1.  **Incomplete Results and Discussion:** A primary concern is the presentation and discussion of the empirical results. The first results table is incomplete, which is a critical omission that hinders a full evaluation of the work. Furthermore, the accompanying written discussion of the results is disproportionately brief, especially given the large number of individual experiments presented. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a much more in-depth analysis that interprets these results, discusses the observed trends, and provides insights into what the outcomes mean for the proposed framework.

2.  **Discrepancy with Abstract:** There is a significant incongruity between the abstract, which promises the presentation of a new and more challenging dataset, and the body of the paper, which does not appear to deliver on this claim. This discrepancy must be resolved. Either the dataset should be properly introduced and utilized in the experiments, or the claim should be removed from the abstract.

3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Error Investigation:** The models exhibit considerable performance variance across the different tasks. A deeper analysis is sorely needed to understand the reasons behind this variance. Why do the proposed models succeed on some tasks but fail on others? This investigation is crucial for validating the paper's central hypothesis. Does the variable performance point to a need for better modeling, or does it suggest that some tasks are fundamentally too dissimilar to be grouped into a unified whole? An error analysis, supported by concrete examples, would be invaluable here.

4.  **Need for Qualitative Examples:** The paper would be much clearer and more impactful if it included example instances from the various datasets used. Presenting both prototypical sentence pairs where the models succeed and more challenging pairs that pose problems for classification would provide readers with a more intuitive understanding of the tasks and the models' limitations.

5.  **Justification for Transfer Learning Claims:** The authors recommend the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model for new tasks. It is unclear whether this recommendation is based on the general nature of its training data or simply the large size of the dataset. The authors should discuss this question more thoroughly. How can we disentangle these two factors? A more rigorous justification for this claim is needed to guide future work in this area.

**Minor Points:**

There are several minor but consistent language and spelling errors that should be corrected in the final version:
*   The words "weigh" and "weighed" are frequently used in contexts that require "weight" and "weighted" (e.g., in the context of weighted averages or model weights).
*   There are several misspellings of "sentence" as "sentene".
*   The term "interpunction" is used; this should likely be "punctuation".
*   There is one instance of "world overlap" that should be "word overlap".

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this paper presents a highly promising and thought-provoking research direction. The idea of a unified framework for semantic relation tasks is a valuable contribution that could inspire significant follow-up work and is well-suited for discussion within the CoNLL community. However, the current manuscript is not yet in a publishable state. I would be enthusiastic about seeing this paper published, but my recommendation is contingent on a thorough revision that completes the results, provides a substantive discussion and analysis, and resolves the inconsistencies in its claims. The core idea is excellent, and with these improvements, it would be a strong contribution to the conference.
