This paper introduces the novel approach of unifying several sentence-pair scoring tasks—such as Answer Set Scoring, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Paraphrase Identification—under the general framework of understanding semantic relations between sentences. The authors then investigate the feasibility of training a single, generally-applicable neural network model for this entire family of tasks. I find this to be a compelling and timely proposal, worthy of presentation at CoNLL and likely to stimulate valuable discussion and future research.

My primary concern with the manuscript in its current state is that it feels preliminary and incomplete. I would recommend conditional acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the following major points in the camera-ready version:

1.  The first table of results is incomplete and must be finalized.
2.  The paper presents a large number of experimental results, but the accompanying discussion is disproportionately brief. A more thorough interpretation and analysis of these results is essential.
3.  The abstract promises the introduction of a new, more challenging dataset, but this dataset is not presented in the body of the paper. This discrepancy must be resolved.
4.  The model's performance varies significantly across the different tasks. The paper would be substantially strengthened by an analysis of these differences. An investigation into why the models succeed on some tasks but fail on others would be highly valuable. Even without a definitive conclusion, a discussion of the authors' perspective—whether the variance stems from modeling limitations or from fundamental dissimilarities between the tasks themselves—would be insightful. This analysis is crucial for evaluating the core hypothesis that these tasks can be effectively unified.
5.  To improve the reader's understanding, the paper should include illustrative examples from the various datasets. These should feature not only prototypical sentence pairs but also challenging instances that are difficult for the models to classify correctly.
6.  The authors recommend the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model for new tasks. It is unclear whether this recommendation is based on the general nature of the Ubu. dataset or simply its larger size. The authors should clarify the reasoning behind this recommendation and, if possible, suggest how one might disentangle these two factors.

In summary, while the paper is currently unpolished, its core idea is exciting and has the potential to generate significant and fruitful discussion within the community. I would be pleased to see it published, provided it is revised into a more complete and finished state.

Minor Points on Language and Typos:
*   The words "weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used where "weight" and "weighted" are required.
*   "sentence" is misspelled as "sentene" in several places.
*   The term "interpunction" is used; "punctuation" is likely the intended word.
*   There is one instance of "world overlap" that should be "word overlap".
