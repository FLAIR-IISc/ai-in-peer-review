This paper presents a novel and promising approach to a family of sentence-pair scoring tasks, including Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Paraphrase Detection. The central thesis is that these diverse tasks can be treated as specific instances of a more general problem: modeling the semantic relationship between two sentences. To this end, the authors investigate the potential of learning unified neural network models that can be applied across this family of tasks.

The proposal to unify these disparate tasks under a single modeling framework is a significant contribution. This perspective is thought-provoking and has the potential to foster the development of more generalizable and robust models of sentence understanding. The core idea is exciting, worthy of presentation at CoNLL, and is likely to generate valuable discussion within the community.

However, despite the exciting premise, the paper in its current form feels premature and incomplete. The manuscript reads more like a preliminary research report than a finished paper ready for publication. I would recommend acceptance only on the condition that the authors commit to addressing a number of significant issues for the camera-ready version.

My major concerns are as follows:

1.  **Incomplete Results and Insufficient Analysis:** The primary results table is incomplete, which is a major omission. More importantly, the written discussion of the results is far too brief. Given the large number of experiments conducted across multiple datasets, a much deeper interpretation and analysis are required to help the reader understand the findings and their implications.

2.  **Lack of Error Analysis and Deeper Insight:** The model performance varies significantly across the different tasks. The paper would be substantially improved by an investigation into why the models succeed on some tasks but falter on others. Is this a limitation of the model architecture, or does it suggest that some tasks are fundamentally too dissimilar to be unified under this framework? A qualitative error analysis, including a discussion of the authors' perspective on these questions, would greatly strengthen the paper, even if a definitive answer is not yet available.

3.  **Discrepancy in Contributions:** The abstract promises the introduction of a new, more challenging dataset, yet this dataset is not presented or described in the body of the paper. This incongruity must be resolved. The authors should either include the dataset and a thorough description of it or revise the abstract to accurately reflect the paper's contributions.

4.  **Need for Concrete Examples:** To improve clarity and provide better intuition for the tasks and models, the paper would benefit significantly from including concrete examples from the various datasets. Illustrating both prototypical sentence pairs and challenging or misclassified examples would give readers a much better grasp of the problem space and the models' behavior.

5.  **Unclear Justification for Transfer Learning Choice:** The paper recommends the Ubuntu RNN transfer learning model for new tasks, but the reasoning behind this recommendation is underdeveloped. Is its effectiveness due to the general nature of the source task's data, or is it simply a product of the large size of the Ubuntu dataset? The authors should discuss this question and, if possible, suggest how one might disentangle these factors.

In summary, this paper introduces a compelling and valuable research direction. The central idea is innovative and has the potential to be highly impactful. However, the execution in the current manuscript is insufficient for a top-tier conference publication. While I am enthusiastic about the work's potential, my recommendation for acceptance is conditional on the authors addressing the significant issues outlined above. If thoroughly revised, this paper would be an excellent and stimulating contribution to the CoNLL program.

**Minor Points and Typos:**

*   The words "weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used where "weight" and "weighted" are grammatically required.
*   There are several misspellings of "sentence" (e.g., "sentene").
*   The term "interpunction" is used; the intended word is likely "punctuation."
*   There is at least one instance of "world overlap" that should be "word overlap."
