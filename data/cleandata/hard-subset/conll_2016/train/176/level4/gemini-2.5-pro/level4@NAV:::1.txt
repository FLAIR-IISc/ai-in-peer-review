This paper introduces a novel and compelling perspective: framing various sentence pair scoring tasks, such as Answer Set Scoring, RTE, and Paraphrasing, as instances of a single, more general task of understanding semantic relations. The authors proceed to investigate the potential for creating broadly-applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I consider this a very promising research direction, deserving of presentation at CoNLL and worthy of stimulating further investigation from the community.

However, my primary reservation is that the manuscript feels premature in its current state. I would recommend acceptance only on the condition that significant revisions are made for the camera-ready version. Specifically, the following points must be addressed:

1.  The main results table requires completion.
2.  Given the large quantity of individual results reported, the accompanying written analysis is critically brief. The paper is in strong need of a much more detailed interpretation and discussion of the findings.
3.  The abstract promises the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, which does not appear to be delivered in the paper itself. This discrepancy needs to be rectified.
4.  Performance varies considerably across the different tasks. An investigation into why the models succeed on some tasks and fail on others would greatly strengthen the paper. Even a thoughtful discussion from the authors on whether this is a matter of modeling deficiencies or fundamental dissimilarities between the tasks would be valuable. Does the evidence truly support grouping them into a unified whole?
5.  The paper would benefit greatly from the inclusion of example instances from the various datasets used. This should include both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that prove challenging for the models to classify correctly.
6.  The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is put forward as the recommended choice for new tasks. Is this recommendation due to the inherent nature of its training data (i.e., it represents a more general task), or is it simply a consequence of the dataset's larger size? How might one distinguish between these two possibilities?

Despite the paper's currently unpolished state, the underlying approach is exciting and is likely to generate significant and interesting discussion. For this reason, I would be pleased to see it published in a more finished form. I do acknowledge that other reviewers may not share this conditional perspective.

Some minor points on language:
*   The terms "weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used in contexts that require "weight" and "weighted."
*   There are several instances where "sentence" is misspelled as "sentene."
*   The meaning of the word "interpunction" is unclear.
*   There is one instance of "world overlap" which should likely be "word overlap."
