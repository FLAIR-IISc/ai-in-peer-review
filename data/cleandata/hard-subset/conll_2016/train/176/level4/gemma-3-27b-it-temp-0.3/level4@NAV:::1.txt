This paper introduces a novel and, to my knowledge, original approach by framing several sentence pair scoring tasks – including Answer Set Scoring, Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE), and Paraphrase Identification – as instances of a broader problem: understanding semantic relations between sentences. The subsequent investigation into learning generalizable neural network models for this family of tasks is a promising direction, and I believe this work is suitable for presentation at CoNLL and merits further exploration within the community.

However, the paper currently feels incomplete and should only be accepted contingent upon substantial revisions before the final version. Specifically:

1. The first results table requires completion.
2. The written discussion of the results is disproportionately brief given the volume of data presented; a more thorough interpretation and analysis of the findings is crucial.
3. The abstract mentions a new, more challenging dataset, which is not demonstrably presented within the paper. This discrepancy must be addressed.
4. The performance of the models exhibits considerable variation across different tasks. The authors should investigate the reasons behind these discrepancies – identifying factors contributing to both successes and failures. Even without definitive answers, a discussion of whether these variations point to limitations in the modeling approach or inherent dissimilarities between the tasks would be valuable. Does this unified framing truly capture the underlying commonalities?
5. Please include illustrative examples from each dataset used, showcasing both representative sentence pairs and those that prove particularly challenging for classification.
6. While the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks, it is unclear whether this recommendation stems from the inherent generality of the task itself or the size of the dataset used for training. Further analysis is needed to disentangle these factors.

Despite these areas for improvement, the core idea presented is compelling and has the potential to stimulate significant discussion. I would welcome its publication in a more polished and comprehensive form. 

I acknowledge that this assessment may differ from those of other reviewers.

Finally, several minor linguistic issues should be addressed:

* The words "weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used where "weight" and "weighted" are more appropriate.
* There are multiple instances of the word "sentence" being misspelled as "sentene."
* The term "interpunction" is unclear and should be clarified.
* One instance uses "world overlap" instead of the correct term, "word overlap."
