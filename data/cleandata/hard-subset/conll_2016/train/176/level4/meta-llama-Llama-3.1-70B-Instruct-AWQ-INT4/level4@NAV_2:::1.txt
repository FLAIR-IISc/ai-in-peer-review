This paper proposes a novel approach by treating various sentence pair scoring tasks as instances of a more general task of understanding semantic relations between two sentences. Furthermore, it investigates the potential of learning generally-applicable neural network models for the family of tasks. The proposal is exciting and worthy of presentation and further discussion.

The main concern with the paper is that it feels unfinished. To be considered for publication, several updates should be made for the final version:

1. The first results table needs to be completed.
2. The written discussion of results is too brief. More interpretation and discussion of the results are necessary.
3. The abstract promises the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, which the paper does not seem to deliver. This incongruity needs to be resolved.
4. The results vary significantly across different tasks. An investigation into how and why the models fail for some tasks and succeed for others would be beneficial. Even if no solid answer is found, the authors' position on whether this is a question of modeling or dissimilarity between tasks would be interesting to hear.
5. Example instances of the various datasets used, including prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose problems for classification, should be included.
6. The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks, but it is unclear whether this is due to the nature of the data or the size of the dataset. Further clarification on this point would be helpful.

Despite the paper's unpolished nature, it presents an exciting approach that could generate interesting discussion. However, it would be beneficial to see it published in a more finished form.

Minor points about language:

* "Weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used in contexts that rather require "weight" and "weighted."
* There are several misspellings of "sentence" (as "sentene").
* The term "interpunction" is unclear.
* One instance of "world overlap" instead of "word overlap" is present.

Summary:
The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks and explores the potential of learning general neural network models. While the approach is exciting, the paper feels unfinished and requires several updates, including completing the results table, expanding the discussion of results, and addressing the promised new dataset. Minor language corrections are also necessary.

Contributions:

1. Proposal of a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks.
2. Exploration of the potential of learning general neural network models for the family of tasks.
3. Introduction of a new dataset (although this needs to be clarified).

Strengths:

1. The paper presents a novel and exciting approach to sentence pair scoring tasks.
2. The authors investigate the potential of learning general neural network models, which could lead to significant advancements in the field.
3. The paper includes a comprehensive review of related work and datasets.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper feels unfinished and requires several updates.
2. The discussion of results is too brief and lacks interpretation.
3. The promised new dataset is not clearly presented.
4. The language contains minor errors and unclear terms.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the new dataset and how it differs from existing datasets?
2. How do you plan to address the variability in results across different tasks?
3. Can you clarify the relationship between the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model and the nature of the data or dataset size?
4. How do you plan to expand the discussion of results to provide more interpretation and insights?
