This paper proposes a novel approach to treating various sentence pair scoring tasks, such as Answer Set Scoring, RTE, and Paraphrasing, as instances of a more general task of understanding semantic relations between two sentences. The authors also explore the potential of learning generally-applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this proposal exciting and worthy of presentation at CoNLL, as well as further discussion and investigation.

However, my primary concern with the paper is that it feels unfinished. To be considered for publication, I believe it is essential that the authors address the following issues:

1. The first results table should be completed to provide a comprehensive overview of the experimental results.
2. Given the large number of individual results presented, the written discussion of these results is insufficient. A more in-depth interpretation and analysis of the results are necessary to facilitate a better understanding of the findings.
3. The abstract promises the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, which is not delivered in the paper. This discrepancy needs to be resolved, either by including the dataset or revising the abstract to accurately reflect the content.
4. The results exhibit significant variation across different tasks. It would be beneficial to investigate the reasons behind the models' failures and successes in various tasks. Even if no conclusive answer is found, the authors' perspective on whether this is a modeling issue or a result of dissimilarity between tasks would be valuable. Does grouping these tasks into a unified whole truly work?
5. Including example instances of the various datasets used, encompassing both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose problems for classification, would enhance the paper's clarity and readability.
6. The Ubu RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks, but it is unclear whether this recommendation is due to the nature of the data (i.e., being a more general task) or the size of the dataset. Further analysis is needed to determine the underlying reasons for this recommendation.

Despite the paper's unpolished nature, the proposed approach has the potential to generate interesting discussions. I would be pleased to see it published in a more refined form.

I acknowledge that my view may not be shared by other reviewers.

Some minor points regarding language:

* The authors consistently use "weigh" and "weighed" in contexts where "weight" and "weighted" would be more appropriate.
* There are several instances of "sentene" instead of "sentence."
* The term "interpunction" is unclear; please define or replace it.
* One instance of "world overlap" should be corrected to "word overlap."
