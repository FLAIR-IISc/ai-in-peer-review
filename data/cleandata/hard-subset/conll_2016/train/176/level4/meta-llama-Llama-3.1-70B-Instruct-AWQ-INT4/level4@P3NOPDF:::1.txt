This paper proposes a novel approach by treating various sentence pair scoring tasks, such as Answer Set Scoring, Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE), and Paraphrasing, as instances of a more general task of understanding semantic relations between two sentences. The authors also investigate the potential of learning generally applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this proposal exciting and worthy of presentation at CoNLL, as well as further discussion and investigation.

However, my main concern with the paper is that it feels unfinished. To be considered for publication, I believe the authors should address several issues in the final version. Firstly, the first results table should be completed to provide a comprehensive overview of the experiments. Secondly, given the large number of individual results, the written discussion of results is insufficient and requires more interpretation and analysis. The authors should delve deeper into the results, exploring the implications and significance of their findings.

Thirdly, the abstract mentions the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, which is not delivered in the paper. This discrepancy needs to be resolved, and the authors should either provide the new dataset or revise the abstract to accurately reflect the content of the paper.

Fourthly, the results vary significantly across different tasks, and it would be beneficial to investigate the reasons behind these discrepancies. The authors should analyze how and why the models fail for some tasks and succeed for others, and discuss whether this is due to limitations in the modeling approach or inherent differences between the tasks. Even if no definitive answer is found, it would be interesting to hear the authors' perspective on this issue and whether they believe grouping these tasks into a unified whole is a viable approach.

Additionally, including example instances of the various datasets used, including both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose problems for classification, would enhance the clarity and effectiveness of the paper. Furthermore, the authors recommend the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model for new tasks, but it is unclear whether this is due to the nature of the data or the size of the dataset. The authors should provide more insight into this recommendation and discuss how to determine the answer to this question.

Despite the paper's unpolished nature, the approach is exciting and has the potential to generate interesting discussions. I would be happy to see it published in a more finished form. However, I acknowledge that this view may not be shared by other reviewers.

On a minor note, there are some language issues that need attention. The authors consistently use "weigh" and "weighed" in contexts where "weight" and "weighted" are more appropriate. There are also several misspellings of "sentence" as "sentene." The term "interpunction" is unclear, and the authors should define it or replace it with a more familiar term. Finally, there is an instance of "world overlap" instead of "word overlap" that should be corrected.
