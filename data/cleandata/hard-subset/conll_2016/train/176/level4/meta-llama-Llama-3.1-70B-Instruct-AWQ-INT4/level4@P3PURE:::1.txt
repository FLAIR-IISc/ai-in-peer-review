This paper proposes a novel approach, to my knowledge, by treating various sentence pair scoring tasks (e.g., Answer Set Scoring, RTE, Paraphrasing, among others) as instances of a more general task of understanding semantic relations between two sentences. Furthermore, the authors investigate the potential of learning generally applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this proposal exciting and worthy of both presentation at CoNLL and further discussion and investigation.

The main issue I have with the paper is that it feels unfinished. I believe it should be accepted for publication only with the proviso that several updates will be made for the final version. Specifically:

1. The first results table needs to be completed to provide a comprehensive overview of the experiments.
2. Given the large number of individual results, the written discussion of results is overly concise. More interpretation and discussion of the results are necessary to help readers understand the implications of the findings.
3. The abstract promises the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, which the paper does not seem to deliver. This incongruity needs to be resolved to maintain consistency between the abstract and the content.
4. The results vary significantly across different tasks, and it would be beneficial to investigate how and why the models fail for some tasks and succeed for others. Even if no definitive answer is found, it would be interesting to hear the authors' perspective on whether this is a question of modeling or dissimilarity between the tasks. Does grouping them into a unified whole truly work?
5. Including example instances of the various datasets used, including both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose problems for classification, would enhance the paper's clarity and readability.
6. The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks, but it is unclear whether this is due to the nature of the data (i.e., being a more general task) or the size of the dataset. Further analysis is needed to determine the answer to this question.

Despite the paper's unpolished nature, the approach is exciting and has the potential to generate interesting discussions. I would be happy to see it published in a more finished form.

I do recognize that this view may not be shared by other reviewers.

Some minor points regarding language:

* "Weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used in contexts that require "weight" and "weighted" instead.
* There are several misspellings of "sentence" (as "sentene").
* The term "interpunction" is unclear; please provide a definition or replace it with a more familiar term.
* One instance of "world overlap" should be corrected to "word overlap."
