This paper proposes a novel approach by treating various sentence pair scoring tasks (e.g., Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment, Paraphrasing, among others) as instances of a more general task of understanding semantic relations between two sentences. Furthermore, the authors investigate the potential of learning generally applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this proposal exciting and worthy of both presentation at CoNLL and further discussion and investigation.

The main issue I have with the paper is that it feels unfinished. To be considered for publication, I recommend that the authors make several updates for the final version:

1. The first results table needs to be completed.
2. Given the large number of individual results, the written discussion of results is insufficient. More interpretation and discussion of the results are necessary.
3. The abstract promises the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, which the paper does not seem to deliver. This incongruity needs to be resolved.
4. The results vary significantly across different tasks. It would be beneficial to investigate how and why the models fail for some tasks and succeed for others. Even if no solid answer is found, it would be interesting to hear the authors' position regarding whether this is a question of modeling or rather dissimilarity between the tasks. Does it really work to group them into a unified whole?
5. Please include example instances of the various datasets used, including both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose problems for classification.
6. The Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks, but it is unclear whether this is due to the nature of the data (is it a more general task) or rather the size of the dataset. How can we determine an answer to that question?

Despite the unpolished nature of the paper, it presents an exciting approach that could generate much interesting discussion, and I would be happy to see it published in a more finished form.

Some minor points about language:

* "Weigh" and "weighed" are consistently used in contexts that rather require "weight" and "weighted."
* There are several misspellings of "sentence" (as "sentene").
* The term "interpunction" is unclear; please clarify or replace it.
* There is one instance of "world overlap" instead of "word overlap."

Summary of the Paper:
The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, aiming to develop generally applicable neural network models. The authors investigate the potential of learning universal models for this family of tasks and present a platform for aggregate benchmarking of these models across multiple datasets.

Main Contributions:

1. The authors propose a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, which can be applied to various tasks such as Answer Sentence Selection, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Paraphrasing.
2. They investigate the potential of learning generally applicable neural network models for this family of tasks.
3. The authors present a platform for aggregate benchmarking of these models across multiple datasets.

Strengths:

1. The paper presents an exciting approach that could generate much interesting discussion.
2. The authors propose a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, which can be applied to various tasks.
3. The paper investigates the potential of learning generally applicable neural network models for this family of tasks.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper feels unfinished, with several updates needed for the final version.
2. The results vary significantly across different tasks, and more investigation is needed to understand how and why the models fail or succeed.
3. The paper lacks example instances of the various datasets used, including both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose problems for classification.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more interpretation and discussion of the results, especially regarding the varying performance across different tasks?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of dissimilarity between tasks, and do you believe it is possible to group them into a unified whole?
3. Can you provide example instances of the various datasets used, including both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose problems for classification?
4. How can we determine whether the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks due to the nature of the data or rather the size of the dataset?
