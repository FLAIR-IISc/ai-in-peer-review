This paper proposes a novel approach by treating various sentence pair scoring tasks, such as Answer Set Scoring, RTE, and Paraphrasing, as instances of a more general task of understanding semantic relations between two sentences. Furthermore, the authors investigate the potential of learning generally-applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this proposal exciting and worthy of presentation at CoNLL, as well as further discussion and investigation.

However, my primary concern with the paper is that it feels incomplete. To be considered for publication, I suggest that the authors address the following issues for the final version:

1. The first results table should be completed to provide a comprehensive overview of the experiments.
2. Given the large number of individual results, the written discussion is insufficient. A more in-depth interpretation and analysis of the results are necessary.
3. The abstract mentions the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, which is not delivered in the paper. This discrepancy needs to be resolved.
4. The results exhibit significant variability across different tasks. It would be beneficial to investigate the reasons behind the models' failures and successes in various tasks. Even if no conclusive answer is found, the authors' perspective on whether this is a modeling issue or a result of task dissimilarities would be valuable. Does grouping these tasks into a unified whole truly work?
5. Including example instances of the various datasets used, such as prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose challenges for classification, would enhance the paper's clarity.
6. The Ubu RNN transfer learning model is recommended for new tasks, but it is unclear whether this is due to the nature of the data or the size of the dataset. Further analysis is required to determine the answer to this question.

Despite the paper's unpolished nature, its approach has the potential to generate interesting discussions, and I would be happy to see it published in a more refined form. I acknowledge that this view may not be shared by other reviewers.

Some minor points regarding language:

* The paper consistently uses "weigh" and "weighed" in contexts that require "weight" and "weighted."
* There are several misspellings of "sentence" as "sentene."
* The term "interpunction" is unclear; please provide a definition or replace it with a more familiar term.
* One instance of "world overlap" should be corrected to "word overlap."
