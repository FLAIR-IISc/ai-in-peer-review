This paper proposes a novel approach by treating various sentence pair scoring tasks, such as Answer Set Scoring, RTE, and Paraphrasing, as instances of a more general task of understanding semantic relations between two sentences. The authors also investigate the potential of learning generally-applicable neural network models for this family of tasks. I find this proposal exciting and worthy of presentation at CoNLL, as well as further discussion and investigation.

However, the paper feels unfinished and requires several updates before it can be accepted for publication. Specifically, the following revisions are necessary:

1. The first results table should be completed to provide a comprehensive overview of the experiments.
2. Given the large number of individual results, the written discussion of results is too brief and lacks sufficient interpretation and analysis. The authors should provide more in-depth discussion and explanation of the results.
3. The abstract promises the presentation of a new, more challenging dataset, which is not delivered in the paper. This inconsistency needs to be resolved, either by including the new dataset or revising the abstract.
4. The results vary significantly across different tasks, and the authors should investigate the reasons behind these variations. Specifically, they should analyze why the models fail for some tasks and succeed for others, and discuss whether this is due to modeling issues or dissimilarities between the tasks. Even if no conclusive answer is found, the authors' perspective on this issue would be valuable.
5. The paper should include example instances of the various datasets used, including both prototypical sentence pairs and pairs that pose challenges for classification. This would help readers better understand the tasks and the models' performance.
6. The authors recommend the Ubu. RNN transfer learning model for new tasks, but it is unclear whether this is due to the nature of the data or the size of the dataset. The authors should provide more insight into this question and discuss how to determine the answer.

Despite the paper's unpolished nature, its approach is exciting and has the potential to generate interesting discussions. I would be happy to see it published in a more finished form. However, I acknowledge that this view may not be shared by other reviewers.

Some minor points regarding language usage:

* The paper consistently uses "weigh" and "weighed" in contexts that require "weight" and "weighted."
* There are several misspellings of "sentence" as "sentene."
* The term "interpunction" is unclear and should be defined or replaced with a more common term.
* The paper contains an instance of "world overlap" instead of "word overlap."
