Summary and main contributions
This paper argues for a unified view of “sentence pair scoring” tasks (f2) and presents an empirical framework that evaluates a set of neural architectures across multiple datasets/tasks: answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, RTE (SNLI, SICK), and STS. The work also introduces two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 for answer sentence selection and wqmprop for property selection), releases an open-source toolkit (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS), and advocates improved experimental methodology (multiple random seeds with confidence intervals). Finally, the paper demonstrates transfer learning across tasks by pretraining on Ubuntu dialogues and fine-tuning on other tasks.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Methodology and resources: a unified evaluation framework with multi-seed reporting and CIs, plus new datasets that better reflect IR-like answer selection characteristics.
2) Cross-task empirical study: a careful, multi-task comparison of standard architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, GRU RNN, RNN-CNN, attention) with a consistent scoring setup, and preliminary transfer learning evidence.
3) Datasets: release of yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop to address weaknesses of widely used answer selection data.

Strengths
- Methodological rigor and transparency. Averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs is a valuable corrective to the field’s reliance on single-seed results, especially for unstable small datasets like wang. The paper clearly documents overfitting behavior and variance, which is actionable for future work.
- Unification and reproducibility. Implementing multiple models and tasks in a single open-source framework with shared components (embeddings, scorer modules, loss functions) is useful to the community and lowers barriers to fair comparisons.
- Realistic IR framing and strong baselines. Including TF-IDF/BM25 and overlap features, and discussing their strength, grounds the neural results in practical IR reality; this is often missing in neural-only studies.
- New datasets that likely increase headroom. The proposed large2470 has more candidates per question and appears harder than wang, which should make progress more meaningful. The paper also rightly cautions about instability and small dev/test issues in wang.
- Transfer learning signal. The “Ubu. RNN” pretraining on Ubuntu and fine-tuning improves downstream performance notably, suggesting shared f2 structure. The training details (no dropout during retraining, balanced labels, RMSprop) are helpful.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; weak SOTA standing. The architectures (avg/DAN/CNN/GRU, RNN-CNN, simple attention) are standard and borrowed from prior work. The paper does not introduce new modeling ideas nor achieve SOTA on canonical NLI/STS benchmarks. As such, the empirical unification is the main novelty, but the central claim of a task-independent “universal” f2 model remains largely aspirational.
- Evaluation confounds via BM25 pruning and engineered features. For answer selection the authors (i) add BM25 as an input feature and (ii) prune candidates to top-20 by BM25 before scoring. This risks (a) inflating performance through a strong non-neural filter, (b) suppressing true positives excluded by the filter, and (c) making comparisons to prior literature (that may not prune) unfair. The “universal” model claim is also diluted by task-specific overlaps, capitalization/number flags, and BM25 inputs. Ablations quantifying the effect of these choices are needed.
- Dataset and leakage concerns. The new yodaqa/large2470 shares ancestry with wang and may overlap across train/test splits; the paper warns against mixing but does not quantify overlap or enforce de-duplication. Labels for yodaqa appear to be derived automatically by answer string matching in passages; there is no detailed analysis of noise rates or calibration. Similarly, wqmprop labels are derived from KB paths with limited human validation. Without noise analysis and leakage control, it is hard to trust cross-dataset conclusions.
- Ambiguities in task setup and metrics. It is unclear how the 3-way RTE loss is implemented; the text only specifies binary cross-entropy for “binary tasks” and Pearson’s r for STS. Please clarify the multiclass objective and evaluation metrics used for SNLI and SICK entailment. The paper states models “lag behind SOTA” on RTE/STS but does not provide side-by-side comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., decomposable attention, ESIM, InferSent) or more recent dialog models; context is needed.
- Incomplete ablations and generality claims. The transfer learning result is compelling but limited to one source (Ubuntu) and one architecture configuration. What is transferred (embeddings vs encoder vs scorer) and what matters? How much of the gain comes simply from more data vs domain alignment? The claim of task-independence would be strengthened by multi-source pretraining, joint multitask training, and ablations of overlap features and BM25 inputs.

Questions to authors
- Do you apply BM25 top-20 pruning at evaluation time as well as during training? What fraction of gold answer-bearing sentences are filtered out by this step on dev/test? Please report recall@20(BM25) and ablations without pruning and without using BM25 as an input feature.
- How exactly do you handle the 3-way classification in SNLI/SICK-RTE (loss, output layer), and what are the reported metrics? Please provide side-by-side comparisons to strong published baselines on these tasks to calibrate the “lag behind SOTA” statement.
- Can you quantify and eliminate train/dev/test overlaps between wang and yodaqa/large2470? If overlaps exist, can you report results on de-duplicated splits?
- For yodaqa and wqmprop, what is the label noise rate? Did you perform any human validation or error analysis (e.g., false negatives due to lexical mismatch, entity linking errors)? How sensitive are your models to this noise?
- In the transfer study, which components are transferred (word embeddings, RNN weights, MLP scorer)? Have you tried freezing embeddings vs the encoder vs full fine-tuning? Please provide an ablation table.

Overall recommendation
The paper’s primary value is methodological and infrastructural: a unifying framework, new datasets with arguably better IR characteristics, and statistically sound reporting. However, the modeling is not novel, the “universal f2” claim is not convincingly demonstrated, and the evaluation is confounded by BM25 pruning/features and potential dataset overlap/noise. With clarifications and ablations, this could become a solid empirical resource paper. As it stands, I lean toward weak reject for a modeling-focused venue, but I encourage resubmission emphasizing the framework/datasets with stronger experimental controls and clearer comparisons.
