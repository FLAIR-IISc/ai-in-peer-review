Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes treating several NLP problems as instances of sentence pair scoring, unifying tasks such as answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, and textual entailment/STS under a common f2(s0, s1) formulation. The work evaluates a suite of standard neural architectures (AVG/DAN, CNN, bi-GRU RNN, RNN-CNN, attention) and IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) across multiple datasets. It emphasizes improved evaluation methodology via multiple runs and confidence intervals, and explores cross-task transfer learning by pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue and fine-tuning on other tasks. It also introduces new datasets intended to address shortcomings of existing answer selection benchmarks and releases an open-source framework (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS).

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength):
1) A unified experimental framework for sentence-pair tasks, with standardized adapters, baselines, multiple model families, and statistically grounded reporting (averaging across 16 runs and 95% CIs), plus open-source release to enable reproducibility.
2) New answer selection datasets (yodaqa/large2470; wqmprop) designed to address instability and small candidate pools in prior work, with IR-aware characteristics closer to realistic retrieval.
3) Cross-task transfer learning evidence showing that pretraining on a large next-utterance ranking dataset (Ubuntu) improves performance on other sentence-pair tasks with minimal architectural changes.

Strengths
- Unification and methodological rigor: The paper presents a coherent framework that runs the same set of models across diverse f2 tasks, enabling more meaningful comparisons than single-task studies. Reporting averages over 16 seeds with confidence intervals directly addresses high variance in prior evaluations (e.g., wang test MRR sd ~0.025), reducing overinterpretation of single-run improvements.
- Realistic IR baselines and integration: Strong non-neural baselines (TF-IDF/BM25) are included and sometimes competitive, reflecting real-world retrieval characteristics. The approach of combining overlap features and BM25 with neural scoring mirrors common practice and provides more realistic application-oriented performance.
- New datasets addressing known issues: The yodaqa/large2470 dataset increases the number and diversity of candidate sentences and shows substantially lower baseline scores, likely reducing metric instability and making improvements more visible. The wqmprop dataset broadens the sentence-pair formulation to property selection, enhancing coverage of f2 task variants.
- Cross-task transfer effectiveness: Pretraining an RNN on Ubuntu Dialogue and fine-tuning on other tasks yields consistent gains over task-specific training, bolstering the claim that sentence-pair models can capture transferable “text comprehension” capabilities. The authors also document practical aspects (dropout handling during retraining, optimizer choice).
- Reproducibility and extensibility: The release of dataset loaders, adapters, and Keras-based model templates should lower barriers for future research and facilitate fairer, standardized comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Limited model novelty and incomplete SOTA comparisons: The architectures are largely standard; the attention variant is adapted from prior work. The paper acknowledges lagging behind SOTA on RTE/STS and does not systematically compare against stronger, task-specific contemporaries (e.g., sequence-to-sequence attention for entailment) or report detailed numerical gaps, reducing the strength of claims about model suitability across tasks.
- Potential confounds from IR feature integration and candidate pruning: Injecting BM25 scores into the neural MLP and pruning training/evaluation to top-20 BM25 candidates blur attribution between neural modeling and IR pre-filtering. For a fair assessment of the neural components’ generality, model-only results and analyses of sensitivity to BM25 pruning are needed.
- Dataset overlap/leakage risk and labeling noise: The paper notes possible overlaps between wang and yodaqa but proceeds with both; without strict de-duplication guarantees, transfer and evaluation could be inflated. The yodaqa dataset uses distant supervision (answer string matching), which may introduce noise; annotation quality, error rates, and inter-annotator agreement (if any) are not quantified.
- Insufficient experimental detail for reproducibility and diagnosis: Key details are missing or dispersed, e.g., exact hyperparameter search protocols per task, batch sizes/sequence truncation, tokenization, training time/compute, parameter counts, and ablations (e.g., effect of overlap features, dropout rates, projection layers, and attention). Figures/tables referenced (with critical characteristics and results) are not fully available here, making it hard to verify claims.
- Transfer learning setup under-specified: While the Ubu.RNN configuration helps, the paper lacks systematic analysis of what transfers (embeddings vs. encoder vs. scorer), comparisons of freezing vs. fine-tuning, and controls (e.g., pretraining on unrelated data or multi-task training). Using only 10% of Ubuntu pairs for training further limits conclusions about scaling.

Questions to Authors
- Can you quantify and ensure no train–dev–test leakage across wang and yodaqa (and among their splits)? Please provide de-duplication statistics at the question and sentence levels.
- For the new datasets, what are detailed statistics (candidate pool sizes distributions, positives per query, length distributions), and what is the estimated labeling noise (e.g., via a sampled manual audit or IAA)?
- How much do model performances change if BM25 features are removed and if BM25 top-k pruning is varied or disabled? Please report model-only scores and sensitivity to k.
- Please provide full numeric results (means with 95% CIs) per model and task, and, where possible, comparisons to published SOTA with their variance. Did you perform statistical significance tests when CIs overlap?
- What is the exact transfer procedure (layers initialized, frozen or fine-tuned; optimizer, LR schedules; epochs; class balancing specifics)? Did you try multi-task training jointly on Ubuntu and target tasks?
- What are the parameter counts per model and max sequence lengths used? How were hyperparameters tuned per task, and was the same search budget applied across models?

Overall, this work’s primary value lies in its unified framework, datasets, and evaluation methodology rather than novel architectures. With clearer dataset hygiene, ablations disentangling IR and neural contributions, and more comprehensive, reproducible reporting, the paper would make a solid reference benchmark for sentence-pair modeling.
