Summary and claimed contributions
- A unified framing of multiple “sentence pair” tasks (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, STS) as learning f2(s0, s1), with a modular evaluation framework (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS) to run shared neural architectures across tasks, report variability, and compare to IR baselines.
- Introduction of new datasets for answer sentence selection: yodaqa/large2470 (Wikipedia passages sourced by YodaQA and auto-labeled by gold-answer string matches) and wqmprop (ranking knowledge-base property labels given a question).
- Empirical study across tasks of several standard neural models (avg/DAN, CNN, BiGRU, RNN-CNN, an attention variant), with an emphasis on reporting across 16 random seeds and 95% CIs, plus a transfer-learning result pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue and fine-tuning on other tasks.

Strengths
- Broad, task-agnostic experimental lens. Evaluating multiple standard architectures with shared preprocessing and scoring modules across f2 tasks is useful for the community. The inclusion of IR baselines (BM25/TF-IDF) is appropriate and often neglected in neural papers.
- Attention to variance and reporting. Averaging across 16 runs and providing confidence intervals is a step in the right direction; calling out instability on “wang” and dev–test mismatch is important and under-discussed.
- Transfer-learning signal. The “Ubu. RNN” pretraining yields notable gains over training from scratch on several downstream tasks, supporting the thesis that cross-task seeding helps.
- Open-source tooling (claimed). A common codebase and dataset loaders, if truly released and maintained, could lower barriers to reproducible comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Dataset quality and leakage concerns. The new yodaqa/large2470 set is auto-labeled by string matching gold answers in passages retrieved by the same YodaQA pipeline, risking retrieval bias and label noise; no human validation or IAA is reported. The authors explicitly note overlap in ancestry with “wang” (possibly across train/test), yet still evaluate across both; without strict de-duplication and leakage audit, reported generalization is questionable. The wqmprop “sentences” are KB property labels concatenated into paths, not natural language; treating this as sentence pair ranking muddies claims about “universal text comprehension.”
- Confounded evaluation by BM25 pruning and feature injection. For answer selection, candidates are pre-pruned to top-20 by BM25 and BM25 is fed as an explicit MLP input, while overlap flags are appended to token embeddings. This both (a) biases the task toward lexical overlap and (b) makes results incomparable to prior work that ranks over full candidate sets. It also undermines the claim of task-independent universal f2 models, since task-specific IR signals are hard-wired. No ablations isolate the contribution of BM25 pruning, BM25-as-feature, and overlap flags.
- Limited novelty in modeling and incomplete baselines. All models are standard variants; the attention model is a straightforward adaptation of prior work. The paper concedes lagging SOTA on RTE/STS and does not establish SOTA on answer selection. Ubuntu “baseline setting” claims are not substantiated with head-to-head, metric-aligned comparisons to Lowe/Kadlec on the same version/splits; only 10% of the training pairs are used, further complicating comparability.
- Inconsistent and under-specified training protocol. Key choices differ across experiments (e.g., dropout p=0.8 for RNNs, none for CNNs, none for RNN-CNN; Adam vs RMSprop for transfer; training on “1/4 of the dataset in each epoch”; BM25 pruning only for some tasks). Batch sizes, learning rates, gradient clipping, early stopping criteria, and parameter counts are not fully specified. The authors note heavy overfitting and early-epoch selection, but do not mitigate it systematically (e.g., stronger regularization, data augmentation, pretraining/finetuning schedules, or hyperparameter search). Using Pearson’s r directly as an optimization objective is unusual; convergence details are missing.
- Reporting, formatting, and reproducibility issues. Results are referenced via figures/tables not provided here; quantitative claims (e.g., “new baseline on Ubuntu”) cannot be verified. The code/data URL is “redacted,” so reproducibility cannot be assessed at review time. References contain pervasive errors (missing years, malformed entries, typos like “\Q2015\E,” author names inconsistent), and the text has numerous typos (“AnNLPmachine,” “succesfuly,” “Retrival,” “Reciprocial,” “denoisified,” “preferrentially,” “interpunction,” “weighed” instead of “weighted”). These issues reflect a lack of polish for a unifying benchmark paper.

Questions to authors
- Leakage control: How exactly did you remove overlap between yodaqa/large2470 and wang (across all splits)? Please quantify remaining duplicates/near-duplicates by question text and by answer content. Provide a script to reproduce the audit.
- Label quality: What proportion of yodaqa/large2470 dev/test labels were manually verified? Report an estimate of false positives/negatives from the string-matching heuristic and its impact on MAP/MRR.
- Ubuntu comparability: Which Ubuntu dataset version and exact evaluation protocol did you use? Did you match candidate pool sizes and metrics (R@1/2/5, MRR) with Lowe/Kadlec? If not, provide matched numbers or explain differences.
- BM25 effects: Provide ablations for (a) no BM25 pruning, (b) pruning but not used as a feature, (c) without token overlap feature flags. How do conclusions change?
- Transfer learning: Report the exact architecture, hidden sizes, parameter counts, dropout usage, and fine-tuning hyperparameters for “Ubu. RNN.” Compare against size-matched from-scratch baselines and against alternative pretraining (e.g., SNLI).
- Stability: You average across 16 seeds but select the best epoch per run; does this introduce dev-overfitting? Show test curves averaged over seeds with fixed epoch budgets to decouple early stopping variability.
- Reproducibility: Will you release the full code, trained checkpoints, and deterministic scripts to reproduce every table/figure, including all dataset splits and the YodaQA retrieval pipeline snapshot used to construct large2470?
- Editing: Please correct the references and numerous typos; ensure all claims are supported by visible tables/figures with numbers and confidence intervals.

Overall, while the goal of unification is valuable and the variance reporting is commendable, the paper currently suffers from dataset issues (quality and leakage), confounded evaluation procedures, incomplete baselines/ablations, and substantial presentation problems. These cumulatively undermine the central claims about universal f2 modeling.
