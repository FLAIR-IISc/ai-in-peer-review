Summary
This paper argues for treating a range of NLP problems as sentence-pair scoring (f2) tasks and aims to unify evaluation across them. It implements a common framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) and evaluates several standard neural architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, and a simple attention variant) alongside IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) on multiple datasets spanning Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, RTE (SNLI/SICK), and STS. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) and emphasize better evaluation practice by averaging across 16 runs with confidence intervals. They also report that weights pretrained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset transfer well to other f2 tasks.

Claimed contributions (as I see them; ordered by strength)
1) Unified evaluation framework and methodology: a reusable codebase integrating multiple datasets/tasks, consistent model implementations, inclusion of strong IR baselines, and reporting of variance and confidence intervals across seeds.
2) New datasets for Answer Sentence Selection and property selection (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop), designed to be larger and more realistic than widely used alternatives, with evidence that they are more challenging for models.
3) Empirical finding that cross-task transfer learning (pretraining on Ubuntu dialogues) yields sizable gains on other sentence-pair tasks.

Strengths
1) Reproducibility and statistical rigor. Averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs addresses a real problem in this literature: high variance across random initializations and unstable dev/test selection. This is a valuable methodological contribution likely to improve future comparisons.
2) Breadth and practical baselines. The paper covers several task families under the f2 umbrella and consistently includes strong IR baselines (TF-IDF/BM25). The observation that IR features remain competitive—even when combined with neural models—is informative for practitioners and helps calibrate expectations.
3) Transfer learning evidence. Pretraining on a large dialogue dataset and fine-tuning on other tasks appears to help materially, suggesting a path toward more task-agnostic sentence representations. The practical guidance (e.g., avoid dropout mismatch, optimizer choice) is useful.
4) Open-source release. Making the framework and model code available increases the impact of the work and lowers barriers to replication and extension by others.
5) Honest assessment of results. The paper does not overclaim; it acknowledges where models lag SOTA and discusses overfitting and dev/test mismatch issues, which builds trust.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty in modeling and weak evidence for “universal f2.” The architectures are standard, and the attention variant is a straightforward adaptation. The main conceptual claim—that universal, task-independent f2 models are within reach—is not convincingly demonstrated; transfer is shown from one large source (Ubuntu) to a few targets, but without thorough ablations, alternative sources, or multi-task comparisons.
2) Dataset quality and contamination concerns. The new yodaqa/large2470 is described as sharing ancestry with wang; the paper cautions against mixing them but does not quantify overlaps or guarantee clean splits. Labels via string matching in passages and property-path generation may introduce noise or leakage. Without a careful de-duplication/overlap analysis and a manual audit of labeling quality, it is hard to treat these as definitive benchmarks.
3) Methodological confounds in Answer Selection. Pruning candidates to top-20 by BM25 and feeding BM25 as a feature into the neural scorer blur lines between the neural model’s capacity and IR heuristics. This can inflate apparent gains and complicates cross-task claims of generality. Results without BM25 pruning/features, or with standardized candidate sets, are needed for fair model comparison.
4) Incomplete experimental controls for transfer. The transfer setup changes architectural/scoring choices (dot-product vs MLP), regularization (dropout mismatch), and optimizer, confounding attribution of gains to pretraining vs configuration differences. There is no comparison to other pretraining sources (e.g., SNLI) or to multi-task training, and the reported improvements lack detailed effect sizes/intervals per target task.
5) Gaps relative to strong task-specific baselines. On SNLI/SICK/STS the models trail prior work, and the paper omits several competitive architectures for those tasks (e.g., stronger attention for entailment, tree-structured models). As a result, the study sets useful baselines but stops short of advancing the state of the art or providing decisive insights into what architectural choices matter in those tasks.

Questions to Authors
- Overlap analysis: What is the measured question/sentence overlap between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across train/dev/test? Can you provide hashes or IDs to enable strict de-duplication? Did you observe any leaks within the new datasets themselves?
- Labeling quality: For yodaqa/large2470, what fraction of positives/negatives were manually verified? How often does string matching mislabel answers (e.g., aliases, multi-sentence evidence, negations)? For wqmprop, what is the accuracy of the entity linking/path generation used to produce candidates?
- BM25 confounds: Please report Answer Selection results (i) without BM25 pruning, (ii) without BM25 as a feature, and (iii) with a fixed candidate pool across systems, to isolate the neural model’s contribution. How do CIs change under these settings?
- Transfer specifics: Which layers are transferred and which are reinitialized? Did you compare transferring only embeddings vs the full encoder? Can you report per-task effect sizes and CIs for the transfer gains, and a comparison to pretraining on SNLI?
- Dev/test mismatch: You note low dev–test correlation on wang. Did you try cross-validation or bootstrapped test evaluation to stabilize selection? Can you share the correlation statistics across tasks?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Strengthen the empirical case for universality: add multi-source pretraining (e.g., Ubuntu+SNLI), multi-task training, and ablations on which components transfer. Compare to pretraining purely on language modeling or entailment.
- Tighten datasets: release de-duplication scripts, provide overlap audits, and consider a small manually verified subset to benchmark label noise.
- Expand analyses: provide results with/without IR features, candidate-pruning sensitivity, and attention heatmaps to interpret what the models learn.
- Presentation: clean up citation formatting and footnotes, and include full tables/figures with exact numbers and confidence intervals.

Overall, this is a useful engineering and evaluation paper with solid open-source contributions and good experimental hygiene, but the modeling novelty is limited and the evidence for task-agnostic “universal” sentence-pair scoring remains preliminary. Strengthening the datasets and disentangling IR heuristics from the neural components would appreciably improve the work.
