Summary and main contributions
This paper argues for a unified view of sentence-pair tasks (f2), presents an evaluation framework and empirical study across several such tasks, introduces two new datasets, and shows preliminary cross-task transfer learning.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A unified benchmarking framework (dataset-sts, KeraSTS) with task adapters and consistent evaluation practice (multi-seed runs, confidence intervals) plus inclusion of strong IR baselines. This is the core, highest-value contribution.
2) Two new datasets for answer selection and property selection (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) that aim to be larger and more realistic than Wang et al. and better reflect IR scenarios.
3) An empirical study across multiple neural architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, attention) and tasks, with evidence that pretraining on Ubuntu next-utterance ranking transfers to other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Reproducibility and evaluation rigor: The framework and open-source code (dataset loaders, Keras-based models) are valuable to the community. Reporting averages over 16 seeds and 95% CIs addresses a real problem of variance in small datasets (e.g., wang). Including strong non-neural baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) and discussing their competitive strength increases credibility.
- Dataset contribution and analysis: The new answer selection dataset (large2470) has larger, more variable candidate pools; baseline scores are lower, suggesting more headroom and difficulty than wang. The wqmprop dataset targets a related, practically relevant selection task. The paper motivates deficiencies of existing datasets and quantifies some characteristics.
- Breadth of empirical study: The paper evaluates several standard model families under a unified setup and across tasks, highlighting trends (e.g., CNNs for smaller data, RNNs for larger). The attention variant’s impact in IR-like tasks is noted.
- Transfer learning results: Pretraining on Ubuntu and fine-tuning yields consistent gains over training from scratch on multiple target tasks, with pragmatic training insights (e.g., avoid dropout mismatch during transfer). This supports the premise that sentence-pair models can learn reusable semantics.
- Practical IR-aware setup: BM25-based candidate pruning and overlap features are reasonable in real-world pipelines and show how to sensibly combine IR signals with neural scoring.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The architectures are standard or borrowed (CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, basic attention). The paper’s novelty lies in infrastructure and evaluation practice, not in new modeling ideas. For a research venue, this can be a weakness unless the datasets and results are exceptionally strong and clean.
- Dataset construction clarity and potential confounds: The large2470 labeling relies on string-matching gold answers in retrieved passages, which risks noise and false negatives; the paper does not quantify label quality. The authors acknowledge overlap ancestry with wang (even across splits), which complicates comparisons and transfer experiments. BM25 top-20 pruning is used in training and scoring; the paper claims no adverse effects but does not report recall (e.g., fraction of questions with at least one gold retained), nor a sensitivity analysis. These factors can bias evaluation and comparability.
- Empirical gaps vs. state of the art: On SNLI/SICK/STS the models lag behind SOTA; on wang, the paper states it’s “not possible to statistically determine” relation to SOTA, which weakens claims. The Ubuntu result is said to be a “new baseline,” but comparisons to the strongest contemporaneous baselines (e.g., Kadlec et al.) are not fully resolved. Hyperparameters are primarily tuned on large2470, potentially suboptimal for other tasks; overfitting and early-epoch selection further obscure the true capacity of models.
- Transfer analysis is shallow: Only one source task (Ubuntu) is explored; there’s no comparison to other pretraining regimes (e.g., SNLI, NLI+STS, generic sentence encoders such as skip-thoughts/InferSent), no ablation on which layers transfer, and no multi-task training. Gains might partly reflect architecture or optimization idiosyncrasies; stronger controls are needed.
- Presentation and completeness issues: The submission contains numerous typos/formatting glitches, some incomplete citations, figures/tables are referenced but not visible here, and URLs are redacted (hindering verification during review). Important details (e.g., exact hyperparameters per task, dev/test selection protocols for each dataset, label balancing procedures) are inconsistently specified.

Questions to authors
- BM25 pruning: What is the gold recall after pruning to top-20 in train/dev/test for large2470 and wang? How often are all positives pruned away, and how is this handled? Please report metrics without pruning and without feeding BM25 as a feature to quantify its influence.
- Dataset quality and overlap: How was labeling noise assessed in large2470 (precision/recall of string-match against human judgments)? Can you guarantee no train-test leakage via wang ancestry (or provide filtered splits)? Please release exact scripts for retrieval and labeling to ensure reproducibility.
- Transfer learning: How does Ubuntu pretraining compare to SNLI or multi-task pretraining? Do the gains hold if you control for architecture and optimization (e.g., same hidden size, same dropout schedules)? Which layers are most beneficial to transfer?
- Comparisons: Can you include stronger contemporaneous baselines for Ubuntu and SNLI (e.g., attention-based or memory-network variants), and report significance tests between top systems using your multi-seed evaluation?

Overall assessment
This is primarily an evaluation/resource paper with a useful unified framework, better reporting practices (multi-seed CIs), and two datasets designed to be more realistic for answer selection-like tasks. These are valuable contributions, and the transfer learning observation is promising. However, the modeling novelty is limited; several methodological choices (BM25 pruning, labeling by string match, dataset overlap with wang) raise concerns about the validity and portability of the reported numbers; and the empirical section does not convincingly position the results against the best contemporary baselines. Presentation issues further detract from clarity.

I lean toward rejection in its current form, but I encourage resubmission after: tightening dataset construction and documentation (with recall and noise analyses), removing or carefully controlling IR pruning/feature confounds in reported core results, adding stronger comparative baselines and transfer ablations, and improving writing and completeness. The framework and evaluation rigor have real community value once the above issues are addressed.
