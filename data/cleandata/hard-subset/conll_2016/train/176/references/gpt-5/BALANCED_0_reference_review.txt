Summary
This paper argues for treating a broad set of NLP problems as sentence-pair scoring tasks (f2), and studies whether relatively simple neural models can serve as task-independent text comprehension modules with task-specific output heads. The authors:
- unify multiple tasks (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, STS) within a single evaluation framework;
- propose two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 for answer sentence selection and wqmprop for property selection);
- benchmark several neural architectures and IR baselines with multi-run evaluation and confidence intervals; and
- present transfer learning results where a model trained on Ubuntu response selection improves performance on other tasks.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) Unified evaluation framework and methodology: An open-source toolkit that standardizes data loading, model-task adapters, includes strong IR baselines, and reports multi-run statistics with confidence intervals. This is the clearest and most valuable contribution.
2) New datasets and critique of existing ones: A larger, more challenging answer sentence selection dataset and a property-selection dataset, with an analysis of instability in widely used benchmarks.
3) Empirical study of cross-task transfer for f2 models: Evidence that pretraining on a large-scale dialogue ranking task improves performance on other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Methodological rigor and transparency: The paper highlights the high variance of neural models, reports results over 16 random seeds with 95% CIs, and cautions against overfitting to small dev sets and unstable metrics. This addresses a real pain point in the literature and improves reproducibility.
- Strong IR baselines and realistic pipelines: Including BM25, token overlap features, and candidate pruning reflects real IR/QA settings and provides a meaningful point of comparison that many neural-only studies lack.
- Broad, task-agnostic benchmarking: Running the same family of models across diverse f2 tasks surfaces useful patterns (e.g., CNNs on small data vs. RNNs on larger data; attention helping in retrieval-heavy tasks), which could guide practitioners.
- Dataset contributions with practical motivation: yodaqa/large2470 appears larger and harder than wang, likely enabling future headroom; wqmprop connects sentence scoring to knowledge-graph property selection, broadening the scope.
- Transfer learning signal: The Ubuntu-pretrained RNN consistently outperforms task-specific training in downstream tasks, supporting the premise that generic sentence-pair models can be beneficial.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; missing contemporary baselines: The architectures (avg/DAN, GRU RNNs, CNNs, RNN-CNN, simple attention) are standard. Given current field standards, the absence of pretrained transformers (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa, cross-encoders/bi-encoders), dual-encoder vs. cross-encoder comparisons, or stronger attention mechanisms limits the paper’s significance. The key empirical claims should be validated against modern baselines.
- Confounding of “universal” modeling with task-specific IR features: The reliance on BM25 pruning and explicit overlap features (and adding BM25 to the scoring MLP) blurs the line between the purported task-independent f2 model and task-engineered signals. An ablation showing model-only performance vs. IR-augmented pipelines across tasks is necessary to support claims about universal sentence comprehension.
- Dataset concerns and potential leakage: The new yodaqa/large2470 shares ancestry with wang; the paper warns against mixing, but it is not fully clear how overlap across splits was audited or eliminated. Without a documented deduplication process and released clean splits, results may be hard to trust and compare.
- Underperformance on core semantics tasks: The models lag substantially behind SOTA on RTE/STS; attention variants are limited; no error analysis is provided. This weakens the central claim of “universal” f2 modeling beyond IR-style tasks.
- Incomplete experimental detail and analysis: Several choices (e.g., training on 1/4 of data per epoch, massive dropout, choice of dot-product vs. MLP scorers per task) are not systematically ablated. The effect of candidate set size, BM25 pruning thresholds, and overlap of vocabulary/entities are not analyzed. The “new baseline on Ubuntu” claim lacks rigorous comparison to the strongest published results and settings.

Questions to Authors
- Dataset hygiene: How exactly did you identify and remove overlaps between yodaqa/large2470 and wang across train/dev/test? Can you provide statistics on duplicate/near-duplicate pairs and a script to reproduce the splits?
- Ablations: What is the performance of the neural models without BM25 pruning and without overlap/orthographic features across tasks? Conversely, what is the gain from adding each feature individually?
- Transfer learning specifics: Which layers are transferred and which are reinitialized? How sensitive are the gains to the source-task choice (Ubuntu vs. other large f2 tasks)? Did you compare RMSprop vs. Adam during finetuning beyond a single configuration?
- Metrics and stability: For Ubuntu, do you report MRR in addition to Recall@k? How do CIs look across tasks when not selecting the best epoch by dev performance (to avoid selection bias)?
- Reproducibility: Will you release the exact code (including preprocessing/tokenization, BM25 configurations, IDF computation scope), random seeds, and hyperparameters per task, as well as the datasets with licensing cleared?

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate strong, current baselines: Compare against pretrained transformer bi-encoders and cross-encoders, with and without IR retrieval, and report latency/throughput trade-offs. This is essential for CoNLL-level significance.
- Clean dataset releases and documentation: Provide deduplicated splits, overlap audits, and detailed metadata; include scripts for BM25 indexing and candidate generation. Consider creating “IR-free” subsets to isolate semantic modeling effects.
- Clarify the “universal” claim via ablations: Separate model-only vs. IR-augmented performance, quantify generalization under distribution shift, and add error analysis highlighting where pretraining helps/hurts.
- Broaden attention modeling and multi-task learning: Explore stronger attention/cross-attention architectures and true multi-task training across f2 tasks, not only sequential transfer.
- Strengthen analysis: Systematically study candidate set size effects, scorer choice (dot vs. MLP), dropout schedules, and training-on-subset choices; report calibration metrics where applicable.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The evaluation framework and statistical rigor are valuable, and the datasets could benefit the community if released cleanly. However, the modeling and empirical comparisons are not competitive with current standards, and the central universality claim is insufficiently supported without stronger baselines and clearer ablations. With the improvements above, this work could make a solid empirical/benchmarking contribution.
