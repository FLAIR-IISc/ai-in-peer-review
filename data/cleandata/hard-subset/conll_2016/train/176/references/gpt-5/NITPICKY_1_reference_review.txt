Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper argues for a unified view of sentence pair problems (f2), spanning answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment, and STS. It implements a common experimental framework (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS), evaluates several standard neural architectures with task-specific output adapters, and emphasizes better evaluation methodology (multiple runs, confidence intervals, IR baselines).
- It introduces two new datasets: yodaqa/large2470 for answer sentence selection and wqmprop for property selection, aiming to address shortcomings of wang and to provide larger, more realistic candidate sets.
- It demonstrates transfer learning by pretraining an RNN on Ubuntu Dialogue and fine-tuning on other tasks, claiming consistent gains.

Contributions as I see them (strongest first)
1) Evaluation methodology and tooling: repeated runs with confidence intervals, inclusion of strong IR baselines, and an open-source framework for cross-task experimentation.
2) New datasets for sentence pair ranking, particularly a larger, more challenging answer selection set.
3) A transfer learning result showing that pretraining on next-utterance ranking can help other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Methodological emphasis on variance and statistical reporting: Averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs directly addresses a widespread problem in f2 literature (especially on wang) where small dev/test sets and stochasticity lead to unreliable single-number comparisons. This is a concrete, valuable contribution to experimental rigor.
- Strong baselines and practical IR perspective: Including BM25/TF-IDF and discussing ensembling with neural scores reflects realistic deployment considerations. The paper also highlights that IR baselines remain competitive on large-candidate ranking tasks—an important empirical finding for the community.
- Cross-task experimental breadth: Evaluating multiple standard tasks (answer selection, Ubuntu next-utterance ranking, SNLI, SICK RTE/STS) within one pipeline provides useful comparative signal about when CNNs vs RNNs work better, and where attention helps.
- Engineering contribution and potential reusability: A unified codebase that integrates datasets, models, and adapters can lower barriers for future work, encourage consistent evaluation, and facilitate reproduction and extension.
- Transfer learning demonstration: Even a simple cross-task pretraining (Ubuntu → other tasks) yields sizable gains over a baseline RNN, reinforcing the promise of task-agnostic sentence encoders for f2 problems.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling and weak evidence for “universal” f2 models: All architectures are standard (avg/DAN/CNN/biGRU/RNN-CNN/attention per Tan et al. 2015), with no new model. The “unified f2” narrative is largely engineering and evaluation; the transfer study is single-source (Ubuntu), with minimal analysis and no comparison to stronger pretraining baselines (e.g., SNLI pretraining, skip-thoughts). Claims about task-independent comprehension are therefore under-supported.
- Questionable evaluation protocol for answer selection: The paper prunes candidates to top-20 by BM25 and feeds BM25 as a feature to the MLP scorer. This likely biases both training and evaluation in favor of models that exploit overlap and/or the retrieval heuristic. It breaks comparability with work that ranks over all candidates and obscures how much the neural model vs BM25 contributes. No ablations are reported (e.g., no-prune, no-BM25 input, no overlap flags). It’s also unclear whether pruning is applied at test time (if so, the metric reflects a reranking setup different from prior art; if not, training/test mismatch).
- Dataset quality and leakage concerns insufficiently addressed: The new yodaqa/large2470 is automatically labeled “by matching gold standard answers in the passages” with additional MTurk denoising; crucial details are missing (exact sizes, candidate distributions, annotator agreement, heuristics for matching, error analysis). The paper also notes potential overlap between wang and yodaqa (possibly across splits) yet does not rigorously ensure that cross-dataset training/testing avoids leakage. The wqmprop construction (paths in Freebase) lacks clarity on path generation, negative sampling, and label noise.
- Reproducibility gaps and hyperparameter inconsistency: Many training choices are ad hoc and under-specified (e.g., sampling only 1/4 of answer selection data per epoch; massive dropout p=0.8 for RNNs; no dropout for CNNs; tuning “primarily on yodaqa/large2470”; switching optimizers for transfer; “rarely use a model from later than a couple of epochs”). Important details are missing (negative sampling in RankNet, batch sizes, learning rates/annealing schedules per model/task, max sequence lengths, tokenization, OOV handling beyond GloVe init). The framework URL is “redacted” and critical figures/tables are referenced but not fully described, making verification difficult during review.
- Writing and formatting quality is poor: The manuscript contains numerous typos, grammatical errors, and inconsistent terminology (e.g., “AnNLPmachine,” “succesfuly,” “weighed,” “interpunction,” “denoisified”), broken references (missing years, placeholders “? \Q2015\E”), incomplete citations, and footnote artifacts. Equation and symbol formatting is inconsistent. This significantly hinders clarity, and for a unifying framework paper, precision is essential.

Questions to authors
- Clarify the BM25 pruning protocol: Is pruning to top-20 by BM25 applied during training only, or also at evaluation? If applied at test time, how do reported MAP/MRR compare to a no-prune setup? If training-only, what is the impact on test performance? Please provide ablations: no-prune; no BM25-as-feature; no overlap flags; and the combination thereof.
- Dataset details for yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop: Please provide exact statistics (number of questions, average/min/max candidate set sizes, positives per query, splits), label generation procedure, agreement/noise estimates, and evidence that no overlap exists with other datasets’ test splits. How are answer strings matched (normalization, aliases)? Any manual validation and error analysis?
- Transfer learning protocol: Why pretrain on only 10% of Ubuntu? How does performance scale with more pretraining data? How does Ubuntu pretraining compare to SNLI pretraining or other sentence encoders on the same target tasks? Please include these baselines.
- RankNet setup: How are positive/negative pairs sampled for bipartite ranking? How many negatives per positive? Any hard negative mining? What margin or loss temperature is used? What are batch sizes per model/task?
- Consistency across tasks: Why are dropout and optimizer choices so different across models/tasks (e.g., no dropout for CNNs)? Please justify with ablations. Also, why are scorer choices (dot-product vs MLP) not standardized or compared systematically?

Additional comments
- The paper would benefit from a thorough language and formatting pass, standardized references, and a clear, reproducible experimental protocol that does not conflate retrieval heuristics and reranking with model ability.
- Given the strong emphasis on methodology, the lack of ablations and the ambiguity around BM25 pruning are particularly problematic. I cannot recommend acceptance without these clarifications and cleaner, more comparable evaluations.
