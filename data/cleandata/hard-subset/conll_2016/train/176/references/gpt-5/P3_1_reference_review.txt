Summary
This paper argues for a unified view of sentence-pair problems (f2) and presents an evaluation framework, datasets, and a suite of neural baselines across multiple tasks: answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, textual entailment (SNLI, SICK), and STS. The authors (i) release an open-source framework integrating datasets and models, (ii) introduce two new datasets for answer selection and property selection, (iii) emphasize better evaluation practice (averaging over seeds, reporting confidence intervals), and (iv) demonstrate transfer learning by pretraining on Ubuntu dialogues and fine-tuning on other tasks.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Evaluation methodology and framework: A unified experimental setup across tasks with seed-averaged metrics and confidence intervals, plus open-source tooling that lowers the barrier to fair, reproducible comparisons.
2) New datasets for answer selection with larger, more realistic candidate sets (yodaqa/large2470) and property selection (wqmprop), addressing shortcomings of the widely used Wang et al. dataset.
3) Empirical evidence for cross-task transfer: Pretraining on Ubuntu Next Utterance Ranking improves performance on other f2 tasks with simple architectures.

Strengths
- Rigor and reproducibility: Reporting variance across 16 runs and 95% CIs directly addresses a pervasive issue in sentence-pair literature where single-run gains are often within noise. This is a concrete community service that will improve methodological standards.
- Practical impact via resources: The integrated, open-source framework (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS) and new datasets are valuable artifacts for the community. The larger and harder answer selection dataset is especially useful, given known brittleness and small size of wang.
- Cross-task perspective: Running consistent baselines across diverse f2 tasks yields informative patterns (e.g., CNNs fare better on small data; RNNs improve with scale; IR baselines remain strong) that are helpful for practitioners.
- Transfer learning results: The Ubuntu-pretrained RNN substantially outperforms from-scratch counterparts on several target tasks, supporting the hypothesis that generic sentence-pair competence can transfer. The paper also offers useful practical notes (e.g., turn off dropout if not used in source training).
- Sensible IR integration: Highlighting the importance of lexical overlap, capital/number features, and BM25 as both baseline and feature reflects realistic IR-NLP pipelines, and the pruning-to-top-20 setting mirrors practical candidate generation.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling novelty and missing strong baselines: Architectures are standard (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, a simple asymmetric attention akin to Tan et al.). The study omits competitive cross-sentence interaction models that dominated entailment/STS even at the time (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM-like token alignment), and the authors acknowledge excluding serial RNNs with attention. As a result, statements about universal f2 models rest on relatively weak encoders.
- Dataset construction risks and potential leakage: yodaqa/large2470 positives appear to be labeled by surface-form answer matching in retrieved sentences, which likely biases towards lexical overlap and advantages BM25 and overlap-heavy models. The paper notes ancestry overlap between wang and yodaqa but does not quantify or fully control it; cross-dataset contamination could inflate transfer or multi-dataset results. wqmprop construction (path selection, candidate generation, negatives) lacks detail to assess noise and task difficulty.
- Evaluation confounds: BM25 is used both to prune candidates and as an explicit feature to the scorer, entangling neural and IR contributions. This makes it hard to attribute gains to the neural model; results without BM25 gating/features are necessary. Hyperparameters are tuned “primarily” on large2470, risking overfitting across tasks. While seed-averaging is commendable, validation–test mismatch is noted but not resolved; choosing epochs by dev performance under known poor correlation could bias results.
- Reporting gaps: Many key claims refer to figures/tables not visible here (e.g., “establishes new baseline on Ubuntu”), and there is no direct, numerical comparison to strongest published results on SNLI/SICK or Ubuntu (e.g., Kadlec et al.). Without explicit numbers and matched setups, it is difficult to verify the empirical contributions.
- Limited analysis and ablations: There is little probing of what transfers (embeddings vs encoder vs scorer), the effect of token overlap features, or the impact of BM25-as-feature. No error analysis or qualitative inspection is provided to understand failure modes across tasks or domains.

Questions to authors
- Overlap control: What is the exact overlap between wang and yodaqa (and among their splits)? Did any experiment involve training on one and testing on overlapping questions in the other? Please provide a de-dup protocol and rerun affected results if necessary.
- Labeling quality for yodaqa/large2470: How are positives defined—exact/partial string match to gold answer(s)? What fraction of positives are not surface-form matches? Any human validation or estimated noise rates?
- IR entanglement: Please report (a) results without BM25 pruning and (b) without feeding BM25/overlap features to the MLP, so we can assess neural-only performance, alongside the IR-augmented setting.
- Ubuntu baseline comparison: Under the standard R@1/2/5 with 10 candidates, how do your best models compare numerically (with CIs) to Lowe et al. and Kadlec et al. (improved baselines)?
- Transfer ablations: In the Ubuntu→target transfer, which layers are reinitialized vs fine-tuned? What happens if you freeze embeddings, the encoder, or the scorer? Do gains persist if you match dropout regimes between source and target?

Additional comments
- Consider adding stronger non-neural LTR baselines (e.g., LambdaMART with overlap and semantic features) for answer selection.
- Explore multi-task joint training across f2 tasks with shared encoders and task-specific heads; this may outperform sequential fine-tuning.
- Incorporate token-level cross-attention/alignment to better model inter-sentence interactions, which are critical for entailment/STS and often help retrieval tasks.
- Use stratified bootstrap over questions for MAP/MRR significance, and report per-question candidate set size distributions to contextualize variance.

Overall: This paper’s primary value lies in the framework, datasets, and evaluation rigor rather than modeling novelty. With clearer reporting, leakage controls, and disentangled IR vs neural effects, it would be a solid resource paper. As is, I view it as borderline; I would lean toward acceptance if the artifacts are released and the evaluation clarifications are addressed.
