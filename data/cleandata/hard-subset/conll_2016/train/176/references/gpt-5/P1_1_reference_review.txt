Summary and main contributions
This paper argues for a unified view of “sentence pair scoring” tasks (f2), encompassing answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment, and semantic textual similarity. It presents:
- A unified evaluation framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) with consistent model adapters, IR baselines, and reporting practices (multiple seeds with confidence intervals), aimed at reducing fragmentation and improving comparability.
- New datasets for answer selection: yodaqa/large2470 (larger, more realistic candidate sets) and wqmprop (property selection), plus an empirical critique of the widely used wang dataset (small, unstable splits).
- Cross-task experimental study of simple neural architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, attention) and a demonstration of transfer learning: pretraining on the Ubuntu Dialogue next-utterance task substantially improves performance on other tasks.

In my view, the primary contributions are: (1) evaluation methodology and unified framework with statistical rigor and strong IR baselines; (2) the new answer selection datasets and analysis of instability in existing datasets; (3) empirical evidence that simple cross-task transfer can help.

Strengths
- Methodological rigor and reproducibility focus: The paper rightly highlights the instability of single-run reporting on small/uneven datasets (e.g., wang) and substantiates this with empirical variance estimates (e.g., test MRR std ~0.025). Averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs is a valuable practice for the community. Including strong non-neural baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) and using consistent metrics across tasks improves interpretability. The open-source framework increases reproducibility and reusability.
- Cross-task unification and comprehensive baseline coverage: Framing diverse tasks under a single f2 umbrella and evaluating multiple simple architectures across answer selection, next-utterance ranking, SNLI/SICK (RTE/STS) is informative. The observations (e.g., CNNs faring better on smaller datasets; RNNs benefiting from more data; attention most helpful in IR-style selection) are plausible and useful to practitioners.
- New datasets that stress realistic IR conditions: yodaqa/large2470 introduces larger, more uneven candidate sets and yields lower baseline performance, which should make progress more measurable and robust than wang. The discussion of candidate-set size effects and dev-test mismatch is practically important.
- Transfer learning result with clear impact: Pretraining a biGRU dot-product model on Ubuntu dialogue and fine-tuning on other tasks yields consistently strong gains over training from scratch, sometimes outperforming stronger in-task baselines. The paper offers practical guidance (e.g., dropout handling, optimizer choice) that others can build on.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; missing strong contemporary baselines: The architectures are standard (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, a simple attention variant), and the paper explicitly omits several stronger, task-specific contemporaries (e.g., serial attention for NLI, Tree-LSTM variants, skip-thoughts). Consequently, the models lag SOTA on SNLI/SICK/STS, and the paper cannot establish competitiveness on key benchmarks. This limits the claim toward “universal” text comprehension beyond showing transfer among relatively similar matching tasks.
- Potential confounding from IR features and pruning: For answer selection, the models take BM25 as an input feature and prune to top-20 by BM25. While realistic in a pipeline, this couples learning tightly to the retriever and obscures the pure contribution of the neural scorer. Ablations without BM25-as-feature and without BM25 pruning are needed. As presented, some of the reported improvements may be attributable to retrieval heuristics rather than the sentence encoders.
- Dataset overlap and construction concerns: The new yodaqa/large2470 is said to share ancestry (and possible overlap) with wang, with a warning against mixing training/evaluation across them. A thorough overlap analysis and leakage safeguards are essential. The wqmprop dataset’s construction (matching property labels, paths, entity linking) and its linguistic naturalness are not described in sufficient detail; error/noise characteristics, positive/negative balance, and split design are unclear. Labeling answer sentences by string-matching gold answers may induce false negatives/positives; the paper should quantify noise and denoising steps.
- Evaluation design choices and fairness: Hyperparameters and model capacities differ across tasks (e.g., smaller dimensionality for the Ubuntu-pretrained model), making some comparisons less clean. Reporting that heavy dropout is sometimes essential, sometimes removed, suggests instability without a clear tuning protocol per task. Only 10% of Ubuntu training is used; rationale and impact on transfer are not examined. For Ubuntu, the claim to establish a new baseline is not substantiated with direct apples-to-apples comparisons to prior best results with statistical testing.
- Scope of the “universal f2” claim: The positive transfer is compelling, but evidence is limited to pretraining on one large conversational corpus. There is no multi-source pretraining, no reverse-direction transfer (e.g., from SNLI to Ubuntu), and no analysis of when transfer helps or hurts (task similarity, label spaces, sequence lengths). Without such analysis, the universality claim remains tentative.

Questions to authors
- Datasets:
  - Please provide detailed statistics for yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop: number of questions/pairs, candidate set distribution, positives per query, vocabulary, average lengths, and train/dev/test splits. How were splits constructed? What measures ensure no leakage across splits and no overlap with wang? Can you quantify any overlap?
  - For yodaqa/large2470, how were labels created and denoised? What is the estimated label noise rate (precision/recall) of string-matching? Any human validation?
- Evaluation and ablations:
  - Please report ablations for answer selection without (a) BM25 as a feature into the MLP and (b) BM25 top-20 pruning, to isolate the neural model’s contribution.
  - For Ubuntu, what maximum context/response lengths and truncation strategies were used? Why train on only the first 1M pairs? Does using the full training set change the conclusions?
  - Can you add comparisons to stronger task-specific baselines on SNLI/SICK (e.g., attentive/Tree-LSTM models) to contextualize the gap?
- Transfer learning:
  - Please provide CIs for the transfer gains, and controls such as: pretraining on SNLI or SICK instead of Ubuntu; random-label or random-initialization warm-starts; and reverse-direction transfer (e.g., SNLI→Ubuntu). Do results persist when model capacity matches the from-scratch baselines exactly?
- Reproducibility:
  - Will you release exact data splits, code to recreate them, and the pretraining/fine-tuning checkpoints? Are there any licensing constraints for the proposed datasets?
