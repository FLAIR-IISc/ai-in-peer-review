Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
- A unified evaluation framework for sentence pair scoring (f2) tasks, with released code (dataset-sts/KeraSTS), standardized data loaders/adapters, common architectures, and a statistically careful protocol (averaging over 16 runs and reporting confidence intervals). This is the primary and most novel contribution: methodology, tooling, and comparability across tasks.
- New datasets and problem instantiations for answer sentence selection and related ranking: yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, with analyses of shortcomings of the widely used wang dataset. This is useful for pushing beyond overly small/unstable benchmarks, though some caveats remain.
- Empirical findings across multiple f2 tasks (Answer Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, RTE, STS) comparing several neural baselines (avg, DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, attention) and strong IR baselines (BM25/TF-IDF), plus an encouraging transfer learning result (pretrain on Ubuntu Dialogue, finetune on other tasks).

Strengths
- Methodological rigor and transparency: The paper foregrounds variance and instability in neural evaluations, averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs. This directly addresses a known problem in published f2 results (small splits, sensitivity to seeds/dropout) and improves the field’s ability to make meaningful comparisons. This practice, if adopted, would materially raise the bar for empirical reliability.
- Unification and reproducibility: A practical, open-source framework that makes it easy to run the same models across disparate f2 tasks is a real service to the community. The modularity (task adapters, scorer heads) and use of standard libraries lower the barrier for future work and reduce “dataset engineering” friction that often fragments progress.
- Strong baselines and realistic IR considerations: Including BM25/TF-IDF, adding overlap features, and pruning with BM25 reflect realistic IR pipelines. Demonstrating that IR baselines remain competitive is an important empirical reminder and helps calibrate claimed neural gains.
- Cross-task transfer finding: The “Ubu. RNN” pretraining substantially boosts performance on other tasks relative to training from scratch, supporting the paper’s core premise that shared sentence-pair competence transfers. While not the first transfer result in NLP, it is a pertinent demonstration within f2 problems and strengthens the case for task-agnostic models.
- Diagnostic cross-task insights: The observed pattern (CNNs preferable on smaller datasets; RNNs catch up/prevail with larger data; attention helps IR-style retrieval) provides useful guidance for practitioners and for designing future benchmarks.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: The architectures are standard variants (avg/DAN/CNN/biGRU, a simple RNN-CNN, an existing attention formulation). No new modeling ideas are introduced, and the strongest results are driven by engineering choices and transfer rather than architectural innovation. This is acceptable for a benchmark/framework paper, but should be clearly positioned as such.
- Dataset issues and potential leakage: The new yodaqa/large2470 shares ancestry with wang; the paper itself cautions against mixing them within one model instance. This undermines the utility of combining them in transfer/ablation unless strict de-duplication is guaranteed and documented. For wqmprop, labels appear to be heuristically derived (property path labels, string-matching-based supervision), raising concerns about noise and construct validity; details on annotation quality and inter-annotator agreement are missing.
- Fairness and confounds in evaluation: For Answer Selection, BM25 is both used to prune candidates to top-20 and fed as a feature into the scorer. This can advantage the proposed pipelines relative to prior work that reported results without such pruning or without including BM25 as a learned feature. Ablations that (a) remove BM25 from the feature set and (b) avoid BM25 pruning are needed to isolate neural contributions and to enable apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Mixed results vs. state of the art: The models lag SOTA on RTE/STS; Ubuntu results are claimed to be near/baseline-setting, but comparisons to the strongest contemporaneous Ubuntu systems are limited (and only 10% of the training pairs are used). The paper’s central claim about universal f2 competence remains suggestive rather than conclusive, given modest gains and absence of multi-task training experiments.
- Clarity/presentation issues: There are formatting/citation glitches and occasional imprecision (e.g., incomplete numerical reporting, reliance on figures not shown here; limited hyperparameter details per task; choice rationales such as massive dropout without ablation). A more systematic ablation (token overlap features, BM25 feature, projection layers, dropout schedules) would strengthen the causal story.

Questions to authors
- Data hygiene: How exactly did you de-duplicate or guard against overlap between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across train/dev/test? Can you quantify any residual leakage? Will you release scripts to reproduce the splits and overlap checks?
- wqmprop labeling: What is the supervision quality? Are labels manually verified or fully automatic? Any estimate of noise rate or a small-scale IAA study? How sensitive are results to noisy paths?
- BM25 usage: Can you provide ablations that (a) remove BM25 as a feature and (b) avoid top-20 BM25 pruning, to quantify its impact and facilitate comparison to prior publications that did not use these steps?
- Transfer protocol: Which layers are transferred vs. reinitialized? Can you share learning rates, number of finetuning steps, and whether vocabulary/embeddings are frozen or adapted? Did you try multi-task joint training, and if so, how did it compare?
- Statistical testing: Beyond CIs, did you perform paired significance tests between top models on shared runs/seeds? For Ubuntu, can you report exact metrics (R@1/2/5 and MRR) with CIs alongside prior SOTA on identical candidate set sizes?

Overall assessment relative to CoNLL criteria
- Novelty: Low in modeling; moderate in methodology and evaluation practice.
- Significance: Moderate; the framework, variance-aware evaluation, and datasets are valuable, especially if cleaned and broadly adopted.
- Technical quality: Generally solid, but some confounds (BM25 feature/pruning) and missing ablations weaken causal claims.
- Clarity: Adequate but could be improved; more rigorous dataset documentation and ablations are needed.
- Reproducibility: Strong intent (code release, seeds, CIs), but full reproducibility hinges on transparent data preprocessing, overlap handling, and ablations.

If strengthened with clearer dataset hygiene, BM25 ablations, and fuller reporting, this could be a useful community resource paper.
