Summary and claimed contributions
This paper argues for a unified view of “sentence pair scoring” tasks (f2), covering answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment, and semantic textual similarity. It presents a common evaluation framework (dataset loaders, task adapters, and Keras-based model code), introduces two new datasets for answer selection/property selection, compares several standard neural architectures against strong IR baselines across tasks, proposes better evaluation practice using multiple runs with confidence intervals, and demonstrates transfer learning across tasks (pretraining on Ubuntu dialogues improves other tasks).

Contributions (as assessed)
1) Unified benchmarking framework and methodology: an open-source pipeline (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) that runs task-independent models with task-specific adapters across diverse f2 tasks, with multi-run reporting and confidence intervals, and inclusion of strong IR baselines.
2) Dataset contributions: two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 for answer sentence selection; wqmprop for property selection) with larger, more realistic candidate pools and lower baseline scores, intended to improve robustness and headroom of evaluation.
3) Empirical findings on cross-task transfer: pretraining on Ubuntu dialogues and fine-tuning on other tasks yields consistent gains, supporting reusability of f2 models.

Strengths
- Reproducibility and evaluation rigor: The framework and code promise reuse and comparability across tasks and models. Reporting means over 16 runs with 95% confidence intervals is a significant improvement over single-run reporting common in the literature, especially given the large empirical variance on datasets like wang. This directly addresses comparability and significance concerns.
- Breadth of cross-task evaluation with strong baselines: The paper evaluates multiple families (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU RNN, RNN-CNN, attention) and anchors them with robust IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25), highlighting when simple IR remains competitive and when neural models pull ahead. The observation that CNNs fare better on smaller data while RNNs benefit from larger data is useful guidance for practitioners.
- Practical IR-oriented design: Incorporating BM25 both as a pruning mechanism and as a feature reflects realistic pipelines and delivers strong performance, especially in answer selection with large candidate sets. The token-level overlap features (n-gram matches, capitalization/number flags) are pragmatic and likely improve robustness to OOV/NEs.
- Dataset contributions with improved characteristics: The new answer selection/property selection datasets increase candidate set size and difficulty, making metrics less volatile and progress more meaningful. If released permissively with construction scripts, these could become valuable benchmarks.
- Transfer learning evidence: Pretraining on Ubuntu dialogues (Ubu.RNN) and fine-tuning improves performance across several target tasks, with concrete training observations (e.g., turning off dropout on retuning, using RMSprop) that facilitate replication and application.

Weaknesses
- Limited algorithmic novelty: The models are standard instantiations (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, attention adapted from prior work). The main novelty lies in engineering, benchmarking, and datasets. Claims toward developing “universal” f2 models remain suggestive rather than demonstrated.
- Evaluation confounds and missing ablations: Using BM25 for top-k pruning and as a feature materially changes the task and complicates fair comparison to prior neural-only results. The paper lacks ablations that isolate the contribution of (a) BM25 features, (b) BM25 pruning, and (c) token-overlap flags. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to the neural architectures versus IR cues and candidate restriction.
- Dataset quality and leakage concerns: The paper acknowledges potential overlap between wang and the new yodaqa/large2470 datasets, but does not quantify or eliminate it. The yodaqa labeling via answer string matching risks noise and bias tied to a particular retrieval system; there is no annotation agreement estimate or error analysis. These issues may affect both training efficacy and reported metrics.
- Incomplete reporting against state of the art: While the paper notes lagging performance on SNLI/SICK and sets a new Ubuntu baseline, it does not provide thorough, like-for-like comparisons to recent strong models on RTE/STS (e.g., decomposable attention/ESIM-era baselines), nor statistical tests against prior work. The decision to use only 1M (10%) of Ubuntu training data is not justified and limits conclusions about ceilings and transfer scaling.
- Clarity/presentation gaps: References and formatting are inconsistent; some methodological descriptions are informal (e.g., heavy dropout choices, early stopping criteria). Important details (tokenization, stopword list, OOV handling, hyperparameter tuning protocol across tasks) are underspecified, and URLs are redacted in the submission, hindering immediate reproducibility assessment.

Questions to authors
- Overlap/leakage: Can you quantify and remove overlaps between wang and yodaqa/large2470 (and any other pairs), and report results after de-duplication? How do you prevent leakage when pretraining and fine-tuning across datasets?
- Ablations: Please provide ablations for (i) without BM25 pruning, (ii) without BM25 as a feature, and (iii) without token-level overlap flags, across answer selection datasets. How do these affect both absolute performance and relative model rankings?
- Dataset construction: Detail the retrieval pipeline for yodaqa/large2470, the exact answer-match heuristics, estimated noise rates, and any human validation. Will you release the datasets and scripts with a permissive license?
- Ubuntu training scale: Why use only 1M pairs? Provide learning curves with more training data and analyze how pretraining scale impacts downstream transfer.
- Transfer protocol: Which layers are transferred and which reinitialized? Did you experiment with alternative sources (e.g., SNLI) and targets? Any observed negative transfer?
- Hyperparameter tuning: Given dev/test mismatch on wang, what was your tuning protocol to avoid overfitting to dev? Did you fix hyperparameters across tasks or tune per task with a held-out scheme?
- Comparators: Can you include results for more competitive entailment/STS baselines (e.g., decomposable attention/ESIM) within your framework to contextualize the gap and test your transfer approach against them?
- Reproducibility specifics: Provide preprocessing details (tokenization, casing, stopwords), exact embedding settings (fine-tuning on/off), regularization, and early stopping criteria, along with the non-redacted code/data links.
