Summary
This paper argues for a unified view of sentence-pair scoring tasks (f2), covering answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, textual entailment, and semantic textual similarity. It presents an open-source framework integrating datasets, models, and evaluation; proposes two new datasets for answer selection; compares multiple neural architectures alongside strong IR baselines; and reports cross-task transfer learning (pretraining on Ubuntu dialogue, finetuning elsewhere). The authors emphasize better evaluation practice by averaging across multiple runs and reporting confidence intervals.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Unified evaluation framework and methodology for sentence-pair tasks, including standardized loaders, models, IR baselines, and statistically sound reporting (averaging over 16 runs with 95% CIs). This is the primary contribution.
- New datasets for answer sentence selection (yodaqa/large2470) and property selection (wqmprop), with analysis indicating they are more challenging than wang and better reflect practical IR scenarios.
- Empirical study across several architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, attention) and tasks, plus a simple but effective cross-task transfer learning demonstration (Ubuntu pretraining), yielding insights (e.g., CNNs on small data; RNNs on larger).

Strengths
- Unification and evaluation rigor: The framework and the decision to average over 16 seeds and report confidence intervals directly address well-known reproducibility issues (e.g., authors note wang test MRR stdev ≈ 0.025). Including strong IR baselines and treating s0 as query provides meaningful anchors.
- Broader, cross-task perspective: Evaluating the same set of model families across heterogeneous f2 tasks is valuable for the community, highlighting when architectures fail/succeed (e.g., attention benefiting IR-style tasks; CNN vs RNN trade-offs).
- New datasets with realistic characteristics: The large candidate sets and lower baseline performance on yodaqa/large2470 increase headroom and can better expose true model differences than the small and unstable wang splits.
- Practical transfer learning result: Pretraining on Ubuntu and finetuning elsewhere consistently helps, offering a simple recipe for practitioners and supporting the claim that shared sentence-pair structure can be exploited.
- Open-source tooling: A modular codebase (dataset-sts, PySTS, KeraSTS) can reduce reimplementation effort and promote more comparable results.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The model architectures are standard (avg/DAN, CNN, GRU-RNN, RNN-CNN, borrowed attention variant), and the paper does not introduce new modeling ideas. The main novelty lies in engineering unification rather than algorithmic contribution.
- Evaluation confounds and insufficient ablations: For answer selection, the use of BM25 both as a pruning step (top-20) and as a feature in the scorer conflates semantic modeling with lexical overlap. This undermines claims about task-independent “universal” encoders. There is no systematic ablation showing the impact of BM25 features, overlap flags, or pruning on metrics or gold coverage (e.g., what fraction of gold answers are excluded by top-20 pruning?).
- Dataset construction and leakage risks: The paper acknowledges possible overlap between wang and yodaqa/large2470 yet does not provide de-duplication/overlap quantification or guarantees against cross-split leakage. The labeling by gold-answer string match suggests distant supervision with unknown noise rates; there is no human validation or error analysis. For wqmprop, construction (path sampling, negative set generation, path lengths) is underspecified.
- Incomplete comparisons and use of partial training data: On Ubuntu, only the first 1M pairs (≈10%) are used; the paper claims a “new baseline” but does not demonstrate superiority over stronger published baselines on the full dataset. For SNLI/SICK/STS, the authors note lag behind SOTA but do not contextualize with recent competitive systems or pre-trained sentence encoders. The absence of direct, up-to-date baselines weakens the empirical claims.
- Presentation and reproducibility gaps: The manuscript contains typos/formatting issues, incomplete references, and references to figures/tables we cannot inspect here. Key hyperparameters and training details are scattered (e.g., early stopping, exact learning rates, negative sampling) and the code link is “redacted,” limiting assessability of reproducibility.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Ablations and retrieval bias: What is the recall@20 of the BM25 pruning on answer selection test sets (i.e., how often is the gold candidate pruned out)? Please report results without BM25-based pruning and without BM25/overlap features, to isolate the contribution of the neural encoder.
- Dataset integrity: Can you quantify overlaps between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across train/dev/test and ensure no leakage? For yodaqa/large2470, what is the estimated label noise rate from string-matching? Any human validation or inter-annotator agreement? For wqmprop, please detail path extraction, negative sampling, path length distribution, and release canonical splits.
- Comparisons: How do your Ubuntu results compare to the strongest published baselines on the same version/splits when trained on the full dataset? For SNLI/SICK/STS, can you add comparisons to stronger baselines (including recent pre-trained encoders) to contextualize performance?
- Transfer learning specifics: Which layers are transferred? Are embeddings shared/frozen? What is the effect of freezing vs finetuning each component? Please report with/without dropout during finetuning and justify the mismatch handling (no dropout during finetuning if not in pretraining).
- Statistical reporting: Are the 16 seeds varying all stochasticity sources (init, data shuffling, dropout masks)? Please provide per-model CIs for all datasets and formal significance tests against the best baseline per task.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Decouple first-stage retrieval and reranking in answer selection. Report both retrieval (BM25/TFIDF recall) and reranking metrics to avoid conflating stages.
- Include systematic error analyses (e.g., lexical overlap dependence, handling of entities/numbers) to support claims about “universal” semantic comprehension.
- Consider multi-task training across f2 tasks to better support the universality claim, and include stronger modern baselines where appropriate.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). The unification, datasets, and evaluation rigor are valuable, but the submission lacks methodological novelty, contains confounding evaluation choices without ablations, and leaves important dataset and comparison details insufficiently addressed. With clearer datasets, stronger and fairer comparisons, and ablation-backed conclusions, this work could make a solid contribution.
