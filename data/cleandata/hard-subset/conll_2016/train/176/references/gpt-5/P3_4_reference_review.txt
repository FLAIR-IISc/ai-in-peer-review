Summary
This paper argues for a unified view of sentence pair scoring tasks (f2), spanning answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, textual entailment, and semantic textual similarity. It presents a common evaluation framework and codebase, introduces two new datasets for answer selection (yodaqa/large2470) and property selection (wqmprop), and benchmarks a suite of neural architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, attention) alongside strong IR baselines. A notable methodological focus is on variance-aware evaluation (averaging over 16 seeds, reporting CIs). The paper also explores cross-task transfer, showing that an RNN trained on Ubuntu Dialogue can be fine-tuned to other f2 tasks with consistent gains.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Evaluation framework and methodology: a unified pipeline (dataset loaders, Keras models) with variance-aware reporting (multi-seed averaging and confidence intervals), standardization of metrics across tasks, and inclusion of IR baselines.
2) New datasets and task formulations: yodaqa/large2470 for answer sentence selection and wqmprop for property selection, intended to address limitations of prior datasets and raise task difficulty.
3) Empirical study of cross-task transfer: evidence that pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue improves downstream f2 tasks under a simple transfer setting.

Strengths
- Methodological rigor in evaluation: Reporting averages over 16 runs with t-based 95% CIs directly addresses a pervasive issue in sentence pair work where single-seed results and small dev/test splits yield unreliable comparisons. The paper demonstrates concretely that variance can exceed SOTA deltas on common benchmarks.
- Comprehensive, task-agnostic framework: The unified Siamese setup with interchangeable scorers (dot vs MLP), loss functions (RankNet, cross-entropy, Pearson r), and a common set of neural baselines enables meaningful cross-task comparisons and is likely to be practically useful to the community. Inclusion of IR baselines (TF-IDF/BM25) is particularly valuable and often omitted.
- New datasets with realistic characteristics: yodaqa/large2470 appears larger and more challenging than early answer selection sets, with broader candidate pools and lower baseline performance, which should reduce metric brittleness and overfitting to small validation/test splits.
- Empirical insights on model behavior across data regimes: The documented trend—CNNs faring better on small data and RNNs on large—is consistent with recent findings and helpful for practitioners. The benefit of simple attention in IR-style answer selection is also informative.
- Initial but promising cross-task transfer: Pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue and fine-tuning yields consistent gains across disparate tasks, supporting the central thesis that f2 tasks share useful commonalities.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; missing strong contemporary architectures: The model zoo covers standard 2014–2015 baselines (CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, simple attention), but omits more competitive entailment/similarity models (e.g., decomposable attention, ESIM/bi-attention, sentence encoders like skip-thoughts or more recent universal encoders). The paper also acknowledges lagging behind SOTA on SNLI/SICK/STS; without stronger models or more extensive ablations, the empirical message is muted.
- Dataset construction and validation details are insufficient: For yodaqa/large2470, positive/negative labels are derived by string-match against gold answers in retrieved passages. This risks false negatives (answers expressed paraphrastically) and lexical overlap bias. For wqmprop, treating Freebase property labels/paths as “sentences” raises questions about linguistic naturalness and generalization. The paper does not report annotation quality, inter-annotator agreement, or manual validation of noise. Potential overlap with wang is acknowledged but not quantified; this threatens clean comparisons and transfer experiments.
- Comparability concerns due to IR feature injection and pruning: Using BM25 both as a feature to the MLP scorer and to prune candidates to top-20 changes the task difficulty and complicates comparison to prior work that scores full candidate sets or does not ensemble IR features. Ablations isolating the impact of overlap features and BM25 preselection are missing; hence it is unclear how much of the reported gains derive from the neural models versus IR heuristics.
- Scope of transfer learning claim is narrow: The transfer story relies on a single source (Ubuntu) and one architecture variant; no comparisons to multi-task training, other sources (e.g., SNLI pretraining), or different transfer directions are provided. It is unclear whether improvements stem from generic language modeling, domain overlap, or task-specific artifacts. Freezing layers did not help, but more thorough analyses (layerwise transferability, effect of dropout, dataset size) are absent.
- Clarity and completeness of reporting: Several critical tables/figures are referenced but not fully interpretable here; some implementation choices (e.g., training on 1/4 data per epoch, early heavy overfitting, dropout p=0.8, using only the first 1M Ubuntu pairs) warrant justification and ablation. The related work discussion is broad but misses some influential recent models for sentence pair tasks.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide ablations for (a) removing token-level overlap features, and (b) running without BM25 pruning and without BM25 as an input feature, across answer selection datasets? How do results and conclusions change?
- How noisy are the new datasets? For yodaqa/large2470, do you have manual validation of positive/negative labels and an estimate of false negatives due to lexical mismatch? For wqmprop, can you justify treating property paths as sentences and quantify their linguistic characteristics?
- What is the degree of overlap (questions, passages) between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across splits? Can you provide non-overlapping splits to avoid leakage for cross-dataset transfer?
- Why use only the first 1M Ubuntu training pairs? Does using the full dataset change transfer results? Did you try SNLI pretraining as an alternative source for transfer?
- How do your Ubuntu results compare numerically (with CIs) to Lowe et al. (2015) and Kadlec et al. (2015) under identical evaluation? You state a “new baseline”; is that statistically superior?

Overall assessment
This submission’s primary value lies in its evaluation framework, attention to statistical rigor, and the release of larger, more realistic datasets for answer selection. The modeling is incremental, and empirical performance lags SOTA on entailment/STS; the universality claim is only partially substantiated. With stronger ablations, clearer dataset validation, and inclusion of more competitive models, this could be a solid contribution. As is, I view it as a useful resource/methodology paper with moderate novelty and practical impact.
