Summary and main contributions
This paper argues for a unified “sentence pair scoring” (f2) perspective across tasks (answer selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, STS). It implements a common evaluation framework, compares standard neural models (avg/DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, a simple attention variant) and IR baselines, introduces two new datasets for answer selection (yodaqa/large2470) and property selection (wqmprop), and explores transfer learning by pretraining on Ubuntu next-utterance ranking.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) Evaluation framework and methodology: a unified codebase, multiple datasets under one interface, reporting over 16 seeds with confidence intervals, and public release.
2) New datasets for answer selection/property selection with larger and more realistic candidate sets than wang, plus an emphasis on strong IR baselines.
3) Empirical study across tasks and a preliminary transfer learning result from Ubuntu pretraining.

Strengths
- Reproducibility and rigor in reporting: Averaging over 16 runs with 95% CIs and explicitly showing run-to-run variance addresses a common shortcoming in sentence-pair literature (particularly on wang). The open-source release (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) is valuable for the community.
- Broader, task-agnostic empirical benchmark: Running a common set of architectures across answer selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, and STS, with consistent metrics (MAP/MRR, recall@k, Pearson r) provides a useful watermark and reveals dataset/model interactions (e.g., CNNs on small data vs RNNs on larger data).
- Strong IR baselines and realistic retrieval setting: Including TF-IDF/BM25 and acknowledging their strength in answer selection is appropriate, and candidate pruning emulates a practical IR pipeline.
- New datasets likely to be more challenging than wang: Larger candidate pools and lower baselines should reduce metric instability and make improvements more meaningful.
- Transfer learning signal: Pretraining on Ubuntu and fine-tuning improves downstream results, suggesting shared structure across f2 tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The models (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, simple attention) and scoring schemes are largely standard. The paper does not introduce new algorithms, architectures, or training techniques. Claims about “universal semantic comprehension” are therefore speculative given the incremental methodology and mixed results.
- Confounds from lexical overlap features and BM25 integration: The core neural scorer is augmented with BM25 as an input feature and overlap flags; samples are pruned to BM25 top-20. This makes it difficult to attribute gains to learned semantic representations rather than lexical matching. Ablations (no BM25 pruning; no BM25 feature; no overlap flags) are necessary to support claims of task-independent semantic modeling.
- Dataset construction and potential leakage: The new yodaqa/large2470 dataset shares ancestry with wang; the paper warns against mixing, but does not quantify de-duplication/overlap across train/dev/test or across datasets. Labeling via answer string matching likely induces lexical shortcuts and may bias models toward overlap-heavy heuristics. A thorough analysis of leakage and noise (and its impact on performance) is missing.
- Incomplete comparisons to established baselines/SOTA: On RTE/STS the models lag behind state of the art; the Ubuntu results are positioned as “new baseline” but it is unclear whether they match or exceed the best published numbers. IR comparisons stop at BM25; stronger IR variants (e.g., RM3/PRF, BM25+word expansions) or classical feature-based rerankers are absent. Without stronger baselines, it is hard to judge the practical value of the proposed setups and datasets.
- Transfer learning evidence is thin: Only one source task and one architecture are explored; controls are limited. It is unclear how much benefit comes from generic supervised pretraining vs task similarity or just tuning embeddings. Comparisons to established transfer sources (e.g., SNLI pretraining) or multitask training would make the claim more convincing. The note about not using dropout in fine-tuning if absent in pretraining suggests sensitivity to training protocol; more systematic analysis is needed.
- Evaluation choices and stability: Training on only 1/4 of data per epoch, heavy dropout (p=0.8), early overfitting, and selection of the best dev epoch (despite dev/test mismatch) introduce methodological concerns. Sensitivity analyses (varying pruning depth, proportion per epoch, dropout, early stopping criteria) are needed to argue robustness.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide ablations for answer selection showing: (a) no BM25 pruning, (b) pruning but no BM25 feature in the scorer, (c) no overlap flags? How do conclusions about “semantic” modeling and attention hold under these settings?
- What de-duplication steps were taken to eliminate overlap between yodaqa/large2470 and wang, and within splits? Please quantify any remaining overlap and its effect on results.
- For the new datasets, can you characterize label noise (due to string matching) and report performance on a manually verified subset?
- How do your Ubuntu results compare against the strongest published baselines at the time (e.g., the best numbers in Kadlec et al.)? Please include exact numbers and statistical tests.
- For transfer learning, can you compare Ubuntu pretraining to SNLI pretraining and to multitask training with shared encoders? Also report controls where only embeddings are transferred vs full encoder transfer.

Additional comments
- The paper’s emphasis on variance and reproducibility is commendable and worth preserving; tightening claims, adding the requested ablations, and strengthening baselines would significantly improve the paper.
- Clarify whether hyperparameters were fixed across tasks or tuned per task; currently they are “primarily tuned on large2470,” which may bias cross-task comparisons.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The framework and datasets are potentially useful, and the evaluation discipline is a plus, but the methodological novelty is limited, the empirical evidence for the central “unified/transferable” claim is not yet compelling, and confounds (BM25/overlap, dataset overlap/noise) undermine the conclusions. Strengthening ablations, de-duplication, and baseline comparisons would change my assessment.
