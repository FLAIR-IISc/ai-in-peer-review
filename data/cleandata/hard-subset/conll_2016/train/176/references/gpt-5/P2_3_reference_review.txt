Summary
The paper argues for framing many NLP tasks as sentence-pair scoring problems (f2), proposes a unified evaluation framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS), introduces two new datasets for answer sentence selection and property selection, and benchmarks a suite of neural and IR baselines across multiple f2 tasks (answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, STS). It emphasizes robust evaluation (multiple seeds, confidence intervals) and shows that a model pretrained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset transfers well to other f2 tasks.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Unified framework and evaluation methodology: An open-source toolkit integrating datasets, model adapters, and consistent training/evaluation with multiple runs and confidence intervals, plus strong IR baselines.
2) New datasets for more realistic answer selection settings (yodaqa/large2470) and property selection (wqmprop), intended to alleviate issues in wang and support broader f2 evaluation.
3) Empirical study across architectures and tasks, including a transfer learning result showing that pretraining on large-scale next-utterance ranking boosts performance on other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Reproducibility and methodological rigor: Reporting averages over 16 runs with confidence intervals directly addresses well-known variance issues in neural models. Highlighting instability of wang and the poor correlation between dev/test is valuable community guidance. Inclusion of strong IR baselines (BM25/TF-IDF) and token-overlap features reflects realistic deployment and prevents overclaiming neural gains.
- Breadth and unification: Implementing and comparing multiple families (avg/DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, attention) under one codebase across diverse f2 tasks is useful. This reduces the fragmentation across task-specific papers and provides a common baseline set.
- Resources: The proposed large2470 dataset appears more challenging (more candidates per question, lower baseline scores), which could better differentiate models than wang. The property selection dataset targets a task relevant to KB-QA pipelines. If released cleanly, these are meaningful resources.
- Transfer learning evidence: Pretraining on Ubuntu and finetuning yields consistent improvements, suggesting some task-agnostic sentence-pair competence. The paper also documents practical nuances (e.g., disable dropout when not used in pretraining, balance labels, use RMSprop) that will help practitioners.
- Clear practical insights: Observations that CNNs tend to do better on smaller datasets and RNNs on larger ones; attention gives the biggest gains in IR-like answer selection; BM25 pruning and overlap features matter—all are actionable takeaways.

Weaknesses
- Limited model novelty: The architectures are mostly standard (avg/DAN, CNN, GRU RNN, simple RNN-CNN, attention akin to Tan et al.). The attention variant is relatively basic and asymmetric. The core technical advances lie more in engineering and evaluation than in new modeling ideas. For a venue like CoNLL, this reduces originality.
- Confounding evaluation choices and fairness: Several ad hoc design decisions—BM25-based pruning to top-20 candidates, adding BM25 as a feature, heavy dropout for some models but not others, using different scorers (dot vs MLP) depending on task—complicate clean cross-model comparisons and the claim of a “task-independent” model. Ablations quantifying the impact of each choice are insufficiently reported.
- Dataset quality and leakage risk: The large2470 dataset is automatically labeled by matching gold answers in retrieved passages; the noise rate is not quantified, and there is no human validation beyond “denoisified by Mechanical Turkers” (details missing). The authors acknowledge possible overlaps with wang/curatedv2, including potential cross-split leakage, but do not present controls or de-duplication procedures. This undermines the reliability of some conclusions and the dataset’s utility unless addressed.
- Comparative results lag SOTA on key tasks: The models “clearly yet lag behind” state of the art on RTE and STS, and the Ubuntu claim is essentially “new baseline” rather than state of the art (with only 10% of training used). Absent stronger results or deeper analysis (e.g., why their attention underperforms stronger entailment models), the empirical significance is moderate.
- Clarity and completeness issues: Important details are missing or redacted (dataset URLs, full stats tables/figures referenced as Fig. 1–4), and references contain formatting errors. Some training choices are unclear or unusual (“train on 1/4 of the dataset in each epoch” without justification; early heavy overfitting but limited mitigation). Without all artifacts and precise dataset construction protocols, reproducibility is uncertain.

Questions to authors
- Dataset release and quality: Will you publicly release large2470 and wqmprop with licenses, exact construction scripts, retrieval settings, and train/dev/test splits? How did you ensure no leakage across splits and across related datasets (wang/curatedv2)? What is the estimated label noise rate after “denoising,” and do you have human validation statistics?
- Ablations on IR integration: What is the contribution of BM25 pruning and BM25-as-feature to final performance per task/model? Can you report results without pruning/features to make neural-only comparisons cleaner?
- Hyperparameter and scorer choices: Why use different scorers (dot vs MLP) for different tasks in a “unified” setup? Did you tune scorer type uniformly across tasks, or fix it per model? How sensitive are conclusions (e.g., CNN vs RNN preference) to these choices?
- Transfer learning controls: How does Ubuntu pretraining compare to (a) SNLI pretraining, (b) language-model or skip-thought pretraining, and (c) initializing only embeddings vs full-network transfer? Please report absolute gains and CIs per target task.
- Variance reporting: Are the 16-run averages and CIs reported for all models and datasets? How were random seeds selected? Did you use the same tokenization and preprocessing for all baselines and models?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses a real need—more unified and rigorous evaluation for sentence-pair tasks—and contributes useful resources, code, and methodological guidance (multi-seed CIs, IR baselines). However, model novelty is limited, several evaluation choices confound fair comparison, dataset quality/leakage remain unclear, and results are not competitive on key benchmarks. If the datasets and framework are fully released with clarified construction and strong ablations, this would be a valuable resource paper. As it stands, I view the submission as a solid engineering and evaluation effort with moderate significance and insufficient novelty for acceptance at CoNLL. My recommendation is weak reject, unless the author response can conclusively address dataset quality/leakage and provide cleaner, stronger empirical comparisons.
