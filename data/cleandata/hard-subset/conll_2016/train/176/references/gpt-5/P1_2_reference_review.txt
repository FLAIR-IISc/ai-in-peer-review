Summary
This paper argues for a unified view of “sentence pair scoring” tasks, where a model f2(s0, s1) assesses the semantic relation between two sentences. The authors (a) aggregate multiple datasets and tasks (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, STS) under a shared evaluation framework; (b) introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 for answer sentence selection; wqmprop for property selection); (c) benchmark a set of neural architectures and IR baselines with improved evaluation methodology (multiple runs, confidence intervals); and (d) demonstrate transfer learning by pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue and fine-tuning on other tasks. The codebase (dataset-sts, PySTS, KeraSTS) is released to facilitate reproducibility and extensibility.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Unified evaluation framework with rigorous methodology: Cross-task, cross-model benchmarking with strong IR baselines, multiple seeds, and confidence intervals; practical insights about variance, dev/test mismatch, and the relative performance of CNN/RNN families.
2) New datasets for sentence-pair ranking: yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, designed to address shortcomings of wang and to reflect more realistic retrieval settings with larger candidate sets.
3) Empirical evidence for transfer learning across f2 tasks: Pretraining on a large-scale dialogue ranking task improves performance when fine-tuning on other sentence-pair tasks.

Strengths
1) Methodological rigor and transparency: The paper explicitly addresses high variance and instability in common benchmarks (e.g., wang), averages over 16 runs, and reports 95% CIs. This is valuable and often missing in sentence-pair literature. The discussion on dev/test mismatch, random seed sensitivity, and early stopping pitfalls is insightful and actionable.
2) Strong, fair baselines and practical evaluation choices: Including TF-IDF/BM25 and ensembling overlap features is appropriate for retrieval-style tasks and underscores that IR baselines remain competitive. The candidate pruning by BM25 mirrors realistic IR pipelines and helps scale training.
3) Breadth of cross-task benchmarking: The study spans answer selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, and STS with a consistent modeling/evaluation setup. The comparative findings (CNNs favored on small data; RNNs on larger data; attention especially helpful for IR-style ranking) are useful for practitioners.
4) New datasets addressing known issues: yodaqa/large2470 aims to mitigate small candidate pools and instability of wang; wqmprop offers a semantically interesting variant (property selection). The initial statistics and baseline gaps suggest these datasets are more challenging and thus useful for future work.
5) Demonstration of transfer learning: Pretraining on Ubuntu and fine-tuning elsewhere (Ubu.RNN) yields consistent gains over task-specific training, supporting the thesis that universal sentence pair encoders are feasible.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty in modeling: The architectures (avg/DAN, biGRU, CNN, RNN-CNN, a simple attention variant) are standard; no new model is proposed. The principal contributions are dataset, framework, and evaluation practices rather than methodological advances.
2) Dataset construction and potential leakage/noise: The paper acknowledges overlap risk between yodaqa and wang (even across splits) and relies on distant supervision (answer string matching) for labeling, which can introduce false positives/negatives. There is no quantitative analysis of label noise, overlap de-duplication, or its effect on evaluation. This undermines the strength of the new datasets unless carefully addressed.
3) Insufficient ablations on critical design choices: BM25 is used both for candidate pruning and as a feature into the MLP scorer. It is unclear how much of the reported gains come from the neural model vs. the IR feature. Ablations removing BM25 as an input feature and/or changing the pruning threshold are necessary to isolate the neural contribution. Similarly, the impact of overlap token flags and feature engineering should be quantified.
4) Coverage of SOTA models and results: On RTE/STS, the models lag significantly behind state of the art; several known strong baselines (e.g., decomposable attention, NLI-focused attention architectures, skip-thought or other sentence encoders) are not included. While the paper notes this as future work, it weakens the claim of a strong, unified benchmark if the models substantially trail reasonable baselines in some tasks.
5) Experimental choices and reporting gaps: Training on only 10% of Ubuntu (1M pairs) limits the strength of transfer conclusions; no learning curve is shown to justify this choice. Details on hyperparameter tuning across tasks are sparse (primarily tuned on large2470 risks biasing results). Reporting confidence intervals is good, but paired statistical tests or per-run distributions across competing models would better support significance claims.

Questions to authors
- New datasets:
  - Can you quantify label noise in yodaqa/large2470 (precision/recall of the distant supervision signal)? Are there manual audits? How often do “answer string match” heuristics fail?
  - What concrete steps were taken to prevent train/test leakage both within each dataset and across datasets (e.g., deduplication, near-duplicate removal, shared ancestry resolution with wang)?
  - Please provide full dataset statistics (number of questions, candidates per question, positive/negative distribution) and release exact splits.
- Ablations:
  - What is the performance without using BM25 as an input feature, and how sensitive are results to BM25-based pruning (e.g., top-10 vs top-20 vs no pruning)?
  - What is the impact of overlap and capitalization/number flags? Can you report ablations to separate neural model capability from feature engineering?
- Transfer learning:
  - Why restrict Ubuntu pretraining to 1M pairs? How do transfer gains scale with more pretraining data? Which layers benefit most (any layer freezing experiments worth reporting)?
  - How are OOVs handled between source and target domains when transferring embeddings and encoders?
- Variance and selection:
  - Beyond reporting CIs, did you evaluate paired differences to establish significance between models? Can you share per-run distributions to corroborate high variance claims across all tasks?
- Comparisons:
  - Can you add a stronger attention baseline (e.g., decomposable attention, NLI-style attention) and/or a more recent sentence encoder to better anchor results on SNLI/SICK/STS?

Overall, this paper’s primary value lies in its unified evaluation framework, rigorous methodology, openly released code, and the introduction of more challenging datasets. The modeling novelty is limited, and stronger ablations and dataset quality analyses are needed. With additional clarity and experiments addressing the questions above, this work could serve as a useful benchmark and resource for sentence-pair modeling research.
