Summary
The paper argues for a unified view of a broad class of NLP problems as sentence-pair scoring f2(s0, s1) and presents an empirical framework to study task-independent models across diverse datasets. The authors implement several standard neural architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU RNN, RNN-CNN, a simple attention variant), couple them with task-specific scorers and losses, and compare them to IR baselines (TF-IDF/BM25). They introduce two new datasets for answer selection/property selection (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), provide an open-source evaluation framework (dataset-sts / KeraSTS), and advocate better evaluation practice by averaging over 16 random initializations and reporting confidence intervals. Finally, they demonstrate transfer learning: pretraining an RNN on Ubuntu Dialogue (next utterance ranking) consistently improves results on other tasks.

Claimed contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
1) A unified, reproducible evaluation framework for sentence-pair scoring with multi-task adapters, strong non-neural IR baselines, and statistically sound reporting (multi-seed averages with CIs), plus open-source code.
2) New, harder answer selection datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) with larger and more realistic candidate sets than wang, addressing instability and small test/validation issues.
3) An empirical demonstration that cross-task transfer (Ubuntu→others) yields consistent gains for simple RNN models.

Strengths
- Broad, unified benchmarking: The paper assembles a coherent cross-task evaluation spanning answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, and STS. Evaluating the same family of models and baselines under one roof is valuable for understanding model suitability vs. dataset size and task type (e.g., CNNs faring better on small data; RNNs on larger).
- Sounder evaluation methodology: Averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs addresses a well-known issue—high variance due to random seeds, especially on small/unstable datasets like wang. The authors also highlight poor dev–test correlation in wang, which is an important caution to the community.
- Inclusion of strong IR baselines and hybridization: BM25/TF-IDF are competitive in retrieval-like settings; including them (and discussing ensembling) helps anchor neural gains and avoids inflated claims.
- New datasets with clearer motivation: yodaqa/large2470 appears larger and more realistic than wang (more candidates, more challenging), which should reduce metric instability and make future improvements meaningful. wqmprop broadens the scope to property selection and can stimulate work beyond classic answer sentence selection.
- Transfer learning evidence: The Ubuntu-pretrained RNN consistently outperforms training-from-scratch baselines across tasks, which supports the central thesis that generic pairwise comprehension capabilities can transfer.

Weaknesses
- Limited model novelty: The architectures are largely re-implementations of known baselines (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, simple attention). The attention mechanism is adopted from prior work and the paper does not introduce new modeling ideas. As such, the scientific novelty hinges on the framework/datasets rather than modeling advances.
- Dataset construction and validity concerns: yodaqa/large2470 is built via automatic matching of gold answers in retrieved passages; the paper does not quantify annotation noise, false negatives/positives, or the effect of exact-string vs. semantic matches. The authors acknowledge possible overlap in ancestry with wang, raising contamination risks. More rigorous de-duplication across splits/datasets and detailed quality analyses are needed.
- Methodological confounds for “task independence”: The stated goal is task-independent models, yet the training adds task-specific signals: BM25 is injected both to prune candidates (top-20) and as a feature to the MLP scorer, and token-level overlap/capitalization/number flags are appended to embeddings. This muddies attribution of gains (neural vs. IR heuristics) and weakens the universality claim. Ablations isolating the effect of these features are missing.
- Incomplete and uneven comparisons: On RTE/STS, the authors state they lag behind SOTA but still position results as “new baselines.” Stronger, task-appropriate comparison points (e.g., entailment-specific attention models, tree-/cross-sentence attention, or more competitive STS models) are necessary to justify claims. For Ubuntu, only 10% of the training data is used; it is unclear why the full set wasn’t used if the goal is to establish robust baselines.
- Transfer learning analysis is underdeveloped: Only one source task (Ubuntu) and one architecture variant are studied. There are no ablations on what transfers (embeddings vs. encoder), no comparisons to alternative sources (e.g., SNLI) or to multi-task training, and no controls vs. simple initialization tricks (e.g., longer training, different optimizers). This limits the generality of the transfer claim.
- Reproducibility details and clarity: Important training specifics are missing or inconsistent (batch sizes, gradient clipping, epoch counts, early stopping policy, exact BM25 parameters, whether pruning is applied at test time, and per-task hyperparameters). Figures are referenced for core claims; however, without thorough tabular reporting it is hard to assess effect sizes. The references and formatting have numerous typos.

Questions to Authors
- Datasets: Can you provide quantitative noise analyses for yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop (annotation precision/recall, inter-annotator checks if any, false match rates)? How did you ensure no overlap across train/dev/test and across datasets (wang vs. yodaqa)?
- BM25 usage: Is BM25 top-20 pruning applied at both training and test time? How sensitive are results to the pruning threshold? What are the exact BM25 k1/b parameters and tokenization?
- Ablations: What is the impact of removing (a) BM25 as a feature, (b) overlap/case/number flags, and (c) the BM25 pruning? Please report to isolate neural contributions.
- Ubuntu data: Why was only 10% used? Do results and transfer gains hold when using the full training set?
- Transfer: Which layers were reinitialized vs. fine-tuned? Did you try alternative sources (e.g., SNLI) or multi-task training? How do gains compare to simply training longer or with different optimizers on the target task?
- Reporting: Please provide per-task hyperparameters (batch size, LR schedules, gradient clipping), training epochs, and early stopping criteria, and clarify whether test-set CIs are computed over independent retrainings without tuning on test.

Overall, I view this as primarily a resource/methodology paper. The unified framework, emphasis on statistical rigor, and new datasets are valuable. However, to meet a high bar, the paper should (a) strengthen dataset validation and de-duplication, (b) disentangle neural gains from IR heuristics via ablations, (c) provide stronger and fairer task-specific comparisons, and (d) deepen the transfer analysis.
