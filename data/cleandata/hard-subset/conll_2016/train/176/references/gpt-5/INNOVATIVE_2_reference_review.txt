Summary
This paper argues for a unified view of sentence-pair scoring (f2) across a range of tasks (answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, STS), releases an open-source framework to run models across datasets, introduces two new datasets aimed at more realistic IR-style evaluation, proposes a more statistically sound evaluation protocol (multi-run reporting with confidence intervals), and demonstrates cross-task transfer (training on Ubuntu dialog and fine-tuning on other tasks) as evidence toward task-independent sentence comprehension.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Unified f2 framework plus tooling: an open-source pipeline (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS) that normalizes datasets, model adapters, and evaluation for sentence-pair tasks.
2) New IR-oriented datasets: yodaqa/large2470 for answer sentence selection and wqmprop for property selection, with substantially larger and more variable candidate sets than commonly used wang, exposing stronger baselines and headroom.
3) Evaluation protocol and transfer: multi-run reporting with confidence intervals to address randomness, and evidence that models pretrained on Ubuntu next-utterance ranking transfer well to other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Clear, timely unification and resources: The paper tackles a real fragmentation problem. A common interface spanning multiple f2 tasks, with released code, is valuable for the community and will likely increase reproducibility and cross-task comparison. This is more than incremental benchmarking; it sets a direction toward task-agnostic modeling.
- Datasets that stress IR realism: The new answer selection datasets have larger, more uneven candidate sets and yield lower baseline scores, which better reflect real deployments, reduce leaderboard saturation, and create space for innovation. This is a meaningful contribution beyond yet another small clean benchmark.
- Statistically principled evaluation: Reporting averages over 16 seeds and 95% CIs is a strong methodological improvement over single-run reporting common at the time, and the paper quantifies variance that often dwarfs reported SOTA gaps on wang. This raises the bar for future work and is impactful even if the models themselves are not novel.
- Cross-task transfer signal: Pretraining on Ubuntu and fine-tuning elsewhere yields consistent gains, supporting the central hypothesis of reusable f2-style sentence understanding. While not a full universal model, this is a credible step in that direction and an under-explored angle for these tasks.
- Useful empirical insights: The observed pattern (CNNs favored on smaller data; RNNs on larger) and the importance of IR baselines/ensembling are practical takeaways that help position future research.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling novelty: The architectures are standard (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, simple attention). The main novelty lies in engineering, datasets, and evaluation. For a modeling venue, this is modest. The paper would be stronger with at least one new architectural idea targeting cross-task universality.
- Potential dataset leakage/overlap: The authors note overlap between yodaqa/large2470 and wang due to shared ancestry and caution against mixing. However, the extent of overlap is not quantified, and the risks to reported results and to future comparisons are unclear. This undermines the clean evaluation claim.
- Confounding IR features and pruning: For answer selection, BM25 is both a feature to the scorer and used to prune to top-20 candidates. This makes it hard to isolate the contribution of the neural model and may bias comparisons against methods that don’t use the same pre-retrieval step. The “universal” story weakens if performance hinges on IR features.
- Underperformance on key tasks and incomplete SOTA comparison: On RTE/STS the models lag significantly behind SOTA, and several relevant strong baselines (e.g., serial attention RNNs) are acknowledged but not included. This makes it hard to assess whether the framework would stand up in more competitive settings.
- Limited substantiation of “universal f2” claim: The transfer result is encouraging but narrow (one source task, specific configuration, sensitivity to dropout/optimizer), and no joint multi-task training is attempted. The paper stops short of stronger evidence (e.g., consistent transfer from multiple sources, ablating which layers transfer, or task-agnostic heads).

Questions to authors
- Overlap quantification: Can you quantify the degree of overlap between yodaqa/large2470 and wang across train/dev/test splits? Can you provide filtered splits that remove any such overlaps to facilitate clean cross-dataset experiments?
- IR pruning fairness: Can you report, for answer selection, results without BM25 features and without top-20 pruning to isolate model capacity? Alternatively, ensure all compared neural variants use the identical retrieval step and clarify this in the tables.
- Dataset release details: Are yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop fully released (data, scripts, retrieval pipelines) under permissive licenses? Please specify exact URLs and versioned artifacts to ensure longevity and reproducibility.
- Label noise and construction: For yodaqa, what is the estimated noise from distant supervision via answer-string matching? Any human validation? For wqmprop, how often do property paths have ambiguous semantics relative to the question text?
- Transfer protocol: Which layers are transferred and which reinitialized? Could you report a small ablation (embedding only vs encoder only vs scorer only) and whether performance correlates with source-target task similarity?

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. While modeling novelty is limited, the paper makes a substantive, community-oriented contribution by unifying f2 tasks, releasing useful datasets and tooling, and elevating evaluation standards. The transfer results, though preliminary, point toward a promising direction the field should explore. Addressing the overlap and IR-pruning concerns would further strengthen the case.
