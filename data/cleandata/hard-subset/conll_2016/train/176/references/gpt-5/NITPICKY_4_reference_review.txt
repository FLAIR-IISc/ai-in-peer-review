Summary
This paper argues for a unified “sentence pair scoring” (f2) framing across multiple NLP tasks (answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, STS). It presents a modular framework (dataset loaders + Keras-based models), introduces two new datasets for answer selection/property selection, evaluates a set of standard neural baselines and IR baselines with multi-seed runs and confidence intervals, and shows that pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue transfers to other tasks.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A unified evaluation framework with open-source code integrating multiple f2 tasks, baselines, and models, plus multi-run reporting with confidence intervals.
- New answer selection-style datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop) intended to address limitations of wang, and to stress-test models with more realistic candidate sets.
- An empirical finding that pretraining on Ubuntu next-utterance ranking can improve performance when transferring to other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Sound evaluation practice: averaging over 16 random seeds with t-based confidence intervals directly addresses the high variance problem that has plagued wang-style evaluations and makes comparisons more reliable.
- Breadth: the authors run a consistent set of models across several heterogeneous tasks (IR-flavored answer selection, dialogue response ranking, RTE/STS). This helps reveal where different architectures (CNN vs RNN vs attention) excel or fail.
- Strong non-neural baselines: reporting TF-IDF/BM25 and explicitly discussing their competitiveness is valuable and often missing in neural papers.
- Practical engineering: the framework (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS) lowers the barrier for future comparisons and reuse. Including token-level heuristic features is a pragmatic step toward better real-world performance.
- Transfer learning signal: pretraining on Ubuntu and transferring weights provides a positive, repeatable gain over from-scratch baselines across tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: the model set comprises well-known architectures (avg/DAN/CNN/biGRU/RNN-CNN, a simple attention variant). There is no new modeling contribution beyond combining standard components. The attention model is relatively weak and asymmetric; other competitive f2 architectures (e.g., cross/bi-encoders with richer attention, ESIM/InferSent-era models, serial RNN with attention) are acknowledged but not implemented.
- Confounded “universality” claim: the approach relies heavily on lexical overlap signals: BM25 pruning to top-20 candidates at train and test and injecting BM25 score directly into the neural scorer, plus token-level overlap/capitalization/number flags. Without rigorous ablations, it is impossible to disentangle gains from overlap features vs sentence encoders. This undercuts the central claim of learning task-independent semantic comprehension.
- Dataset issues and potential leakage: the authors note overlap ancestry between wang and yodaqa and caution against mixing, but do not quantify overlap or enforce deduplication across splits. The new datasets’ construction details are insufficient (candidate retrieval, labeling noise, dedup, entity leakage, final split procedure). Using BM25 to prune candidates at evaluation risks removing true positives and makes MAP/MRR incomparable to published numbers unless recall of gold answers in the top-20 is guaranteed and reported.
- Incomplete reporting and reproducibility concerns: many claims reference figures/tables not present here (e.g., Fig. 1–4), yet these underpin conclusions (e.g., CNN vs RNN regimes, baseline gaps). Key hyperparameters are under-specified or inconsistent (e.g., dropout p=0.8 “massive” on RNN input/output; no dropout elsewhere; training on 1/4 of data per epoch; optimizer switches between Adam/RMSprop without clear justification). The Ubuntu “new baseline” claim lacks explicit numbers vs SOTA. URLs are redacted; pretrained models are not provided; random seed control and exact preprocessing (tokenization, stopword list, lowercasing, OOV handling) are not fully described.
- Evaluation choices may bias results: BM25 top-20 filtering during training and testing accelerates experiments but changes the task. The assertion of “no adverse effects” is anecdotal; no recall-at-k of the candidate generator is reported, nor sensitivity to top-k. The practice also privileges IR-heavy tasks and masks weaknesses of the encoders on low-overlap pairs. Hyperparameters are “primarily tuned on yodaqa/large2470” yet reused elsewhere, which may be suboptimal or unfair.
- Writing and formatting quality: numerous typos and inconsistencies (“AnNLPmachine”, “successfuly”, “Reciprocial”, “Information Retrival”, “sentenes”, “preferrentially”, “interpunction”), inconsistent notation (N vs 2N; MLPmodule), and many malformed references (missing years/pages, TeX artifacts, duplicate author strings). Footnote-style asides interrupt flow. This reduces clarity and raises concerns about overall rigor.

Questions to Authors
- For the new datasets, what are the exact train/dev/test split procedures? Are there any shared questions or near-duplicates across splits or across wang/yodaqa? Please quantify overlap and enforce deduplication if not already done.
- When pruning to top-20 by BM25, what fraction of questions lose all gold answers? Please report recall of gold answers after pruning and re-compute MAP/MRR accordingly. Do you evaluate on the full candidate set anywhere?
- Provide ablations: (a) without BM25 as an input feature; (b) without overlap/capitalization/number token flags; (c) without BM25 pruning. How do conclusions change?
- What exact tokenization, normalization, and stopword list are used? Is case preserved? How is punctuation handled? How are numbers normalized?
- Ubuntu pretraining: what is the vocabulary size, OOV handling, and sequence truncation policy? Why only 1M training pairs? Did you explore using the full dataset?
- Transfer learning: which layers are transferred and which reinitialized? What target-task learning rates and batch sizes were used? Did you try freezing embeddings or lower layers?
- Confidence intervals: are the 16 runs identical except for random seeds? Are dev-set selections fixed across runs, or is early stopping also stochastic? Please clarify independence assumptions.
- Can you release the datasets, code, and exact preprocessing scripts (with non-redacted URLs) and pretrained checkpoints to ensure reproducibility?
- Please report explicit numbers vs published SOTA on Ubuntu, wang, SNLI, and SICK (with CIs), or temper claims accordingly.

Additional comments
- The unified framing and multi-seed evaluation are valuable, but the heavy reliance on lexical overlap and IR pruning, coupled with incomplete dataset hygiene and reporting, make it hard to draw strong scientific conclusions about “universal” sentence encoders at this stage.
- Recommendation: leaning toward rejection unless the authors can (i) provide rigorous ablations disentangling overlap features, (ii) ensure dataset cleanliness and fair, unpruned evaluations, and (iii) substantially improve reporting, references, and clarity.
