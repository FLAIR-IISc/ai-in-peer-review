Summary and claimed contributions
This paper argues for a unified perspective on sentence pair scoring tasks (semantic similarity, answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, STS), proposes a modular evaluation framework (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS), introduces two new datasets for answer sentence selection (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and empirically compares a set of neural architectures and IR baselines across tasks. It further advocates stronger evaluation methodology (multi-run reporting with confidence intervals) and explores cross-task transfer learning (pretraining on Ubuntu dialogues).

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Methodological: A unified experimental framework with multi-task adapters and statistically sound reporting (averaging across 16 runs with 95% CIs), plus solid IR baselines. This directly improves comparability and rigor for f2 tasks.
2) Data: Introduction of larger, more challenging answer sentence selection datasets (large2470, wqmprop), and a consolidated treatment of the Ubuntu next-utterance ranking task.
3) Empirical insight: Cross-task transfer from Ubuntu pretraining improves downstream performance, suggesting some task-agnostic sentence-pair competence.

Strengths
- Rigor in evaluation and reporting variance: The paper confronts a chronic issue in this area—large variance across seeds—and responds by averaging over 16 runs and reporting CIs. The point that performance deltas on wang are smaller than run-to-run variance is important and well-illustrated. This is a meaningful community contribution.
- Unified, task-agnostic setup: Framing diverse f2 tasks under a single modular pipeline (with standardized scoring heads and losses) is valuable. It lowers barriers for fair cross-task comparisons and future extensions, and the open-source release increases potential impact.
- Introduction of harder answer selection data: The new datasets appear larger, with many candidates per question and lower baseline performance, which will better expose improvements over simple retrieval baselines. The property selection formulation (wqmprop) is a nice link to KB-QA and path selection literature.
- Broad, sensible baselines: Including strong IR methods (TF-IDF/BM25) and simple neural baselines (avg/DAN) alongside RNN, CNN, RNN-CNN, and an attention variant grounds the results and prevents overclaiming. The observation that CNNs fare better on smaller data while RNNs improve with scale is consistent and useful.
- Transfer learning evidence: Pretraining on Ubuntu and fine-tuning yields consistent gains, suggesting some cross-task generality. The paper shares practical tips (e.g., dropout handling, optimizer choices) that practitioners can use.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling and incomplete coverage of competitive architectures: The architectures are largely standard (biGRU, multi-filter CNN, simple attention over post-RNN features). Notably missing are stronger pairwise interaction models prevalent around RTE/STS and QA (e.g., serial attention/match-LSTM-style encoders, decomposable attention/ESIM-type architectures, multi-perspective matching). The authors acknowledge some as future work, but this limits the strength of claims about a “universal” model.
- Confounding IR pre-filtering and feature engineering with “task-agnostic” learning: For answer selection, the pipeline prunes to BM25 top-20 and feeds BM25 and token overlap flags into the scorer. This mirrors practical systems but weakens claims about universal f2 semantics, as a substantial portion of performance may derive from IR features/preselection. Ablations removing BM25 input and pre-filtering are needed to quantify the neural model’s standalone capability.
- Dataset construction details and contamination risk: The new datasets are insufficiently documented. Labeling positives by string-matching gold answers in Wikipedia passages risks false positives/negatives (context, polysemy). The authors mention possible overlap with wang due to shared question ancestry and advise against mixing, but it remains unclear how rigorously overlaps are removed across train/dev/test and across datasets in their experiments. More transparent scripts, splits, and leakage checks are needed.
- Transfer learning evidence lacks ablations: Reported improvements from “Ubu.RNN” could stem from architectural differences (dot-product scorer, reduced dimensionality) and optimization choices rather than pretraining per se. The paper states that a target-task model with the same configuration but without pretraining underperforms, yet those numbers are not reported. Ablations that (i) pretrain only word embeddings vs full network, (ii) freeze subsets of layers, (iii) vary pretraining domains, and (iv) remove the change in scorer/dimension would strengthen the claim.
- Ubuntu training downsampled and comparability unclear: Only 10% of Ubuntu training is used “as in past papers,” yet the paper claims to establish a new baseline. Given rapid progress on Ubuntu at the time, this may understate achievable performance and complicate comparisons. A full-data setting would better support the claims.

Questions to authors
- Data: Can you provide precise dataset construction details for large2470 and wqmprop (positive/negative labeling heuristics, human verification rates, entity/linking accuracy, and overlap removal across splits and between wang/large2470)? Do you release scripts to regenerate splits and a list of overlapping questions?
- IR confounds: What is the performance of your best neural models without BM25 prefiltering and without injecting BM25/overlap features? How does performance degrade as you vary the prefilter k? This is important to isolate the model’s semantic capability.
- Transfer learning: Please report explicit ablations for: (a) same architecture without pretraining; (b) pretraining only embeddings; (c) freezing vs fine-tuning different layers; (d) using the same scorer/dimensionality as baseline RNN. Also, how sensitive are gains to domain mismatch (e.g., pretraining on SNLI instead of Ubuntu)?
- Variance and early stopping: Given weak dev/test correlation for wang, how do you select the epoch across the 16 runs? Could you provide test metrics for multiple checkpoints to show robustness of selection?
- Ubuntu: Why restrict to 10% of training data? Do conclusions or transfer gains change when using the full corpus?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include stronger pairwise interaction baselines (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM/ESIM-style) and a multi-hop memory model, to better position claims about universality.
- Provide error analyses across tasks (e.g., failure modes on long sequences, named entities, numerics) and calibration plots; this will inform where transfer helps.
- Report training budgets (batch sizes, sequence truncation, wall-clock), and per-task hyperparameters to enhance reproducibility.
- Consider evaluating without top-k pruning on at least one answer selection dataset to enable apples-to-apples comparison with approaches that do not rely on IR preselection.

Overall, this paper’s main value lies in its methodological stance (multi-run CIs, unified framework) and new datasets, plus early evidence for cross-task transfer. The modeling is incremental, and several experimental confounds should be addressed. With stronger baselines, more transparent datasets/splits, and rigorous ablations, this work could serve as a useful benchmark reference for sentence pair scoring.
