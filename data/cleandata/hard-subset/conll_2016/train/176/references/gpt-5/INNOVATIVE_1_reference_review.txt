Summary and contributions
- Unified f2 framework and tooling: The paper articulates “sentence pair scoring” as a unifying abstraction across multiple tasks (answer selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, STS), and releases an open-source framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) that standardizes datasets, adapters, models, metrics, and statistical reporting (16-run averages with CIs), including strong IR baselines. Primary contribution.
- New datasets for realistic answer selection: yodaqa/large2470 (Wikipedia answer sentence selection) and wqmprop (property selection via Freebase paths), designed to be larger, harder, and less unstable than wang. Secondary contribution.
- Cross-task transfer learning evidence: Training on Ubuntu Dialogue and transferring weights to other tasks yields strong and consistent gains, suggesting feasibility of task-independent f2 models. Tertiary contribution.

Strengths
- Clear, timely unification with practical impact: The paper takes a broad, bold view that many core NLP problems are instances of f2 and operationalizes it with a modular, open-source framework. The evaluation hygiene (16 seeds, CIs) directly addresses a reproducibility gap (notably on wang), and the inclusion of IR baselines (TF-IDF/BM25) provides much-needed anchors for neural results. This raises the bar for future work and is highly reusable.
- Valuable datasets that fix known pain points: yodaqa/large2470 tackles the instability and small-test-set issues of wang, with many more candidates per question and lower baselines that better expose model differences. wqmprop adds a distinct but compatible f2-ranking setting grounded in KB paths. These resources can reorient the community toward more realistic answer selection set-ups.
- Compelling cross-task transfer result: The “Ubu. RNN” pretraining on large-scale next-utterance ranking and subsequent finetuning consistently outperforms task-specific RNNs on diverse targets. Even if preliminary, this is strong evidence that universal f2 models are viable and that large conversational data can seed generalizable sentence encoders—an exciting direction beyond incremental architecture tweaks.
- Breadth and consistency of comparisons: The study covers CNNs, GRUs, RNN-CNN hybrids, and a simple attention variant, evaluated across multiple tasks with consistent embeddings and scoring, and reports that CNNs excel on small data while RNNs benefit from scale—useful guidance to practitioners. The work also surfaces the importance of overlap features and the outsized role of IR signals in hard answer selection.
- Community contribution and replicability: The software, dataset adapters, and standardized evaluation methodology (including the call for reporting MRR, MAP, recall@k, and CIs) are likely to be adopted, improving comparability and accelerating progress.

Weaknesses
- Confounds from IR coupling and pruning: The use of BM25 both for pruning (top-20) and as a feature in the scorer blurs the comparison among neural models and the claim of a “task-independent” f2 encoder. Without ablations per model with/without BM25 and pruning, it is hard to disentangle what the encoder learns vs. what BM25 contributes. This also biases training/evaluation toward IR-friendly overlap signals.
- Potential data leakage not fully audited: The authors note overlap between wang and yodaqa but do not present a systematic deduplication audit or demonstrate that no training-test contamination affects reported results (especially for transfer). Given the focus on unified evaluation, rigorous leakage control is essential.
- Modeling novelty is limited: Architectures are largely standard (GRU, CNN, RNN-CNN, a single asymmetric attention). Results lag SOTA on RTE/STS, and more powerful cross-encoders/attention or recent architectures are not explored. The novelty is thus primarily in framing, datasets, and evaluation, not in modeling advances.
- Incomplete experimental controls and ablations: Important choices—BM25 feature/pruning, token overlap/case/number flags, fine-tuning vs. freezing embeddings, massive dropout regimes, using only 10% of Ubuntu for pretraining, training on 1/4 data per epoch—are not ablated. The reported heavy overfitting and weak dev–test correlation further argue for stronger controls (e.g., nested CV, early stopping protocols).
- Statistical scope: While averaging over seeds with t-intervals is welcome, there is no assessment over data resamples or alternative dev splits, and hyperparameter tuning appears concentrated on yodaqa, risking overfitting methodological choices to that dataset.

Questions to authors
- Please quantify BM25’s impact: For each model/task, report performance without BM25 pruning and without BM25-as-feature (and with neither). Do the model rankings and transfer gains persist?
- How did you prevent leakage between wang and yodaqa? Did you deduplicate by exact question text, paraphrases, and candidate sentences across splits? Are any transfer results affected?
- Ubu. RNN transfer details: Which layers were initialized and which re-trained? What dimensionality and parameter count vs. baseline RNN? Exact learning-rate schedules (Adam vs. RMSprop) per stage and dropout usage?
- Why train on only 10% of Ubuntu? Does pretraining on 100% further improve transfer? Any negative transfer observed on any target task?
- Ablations on token flags and embedding fine-tuning: How much do overlap/capitalization/number features contribute? What happens with fixed GloVe vs. trainable?
- Release: Will you release the new datasets, code, and full scripts (including seeds and preprocessing) to reproduce all numbers?

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The unifying f2 framing, open-source infrastructure with stronger evaluation practice, introduction of harder datasets, and the cross-task transfer result collectively chart a promising, novel direction toward universal sentence-pair models. Despite methodological imperfections and modest SOTA standing on RTE/STS, the paper’s conceptual and infrastructural contributions are valuable and likely to catalyze future work. I encourage the authors to strengthen the experimental controls and ablations, and to provide a thorough leakage audit and BM25 ablations in the camera-ready.
