Summary and main contributions
- A cross-task “sentence pair scoring” benchmark: the authors implement a unified framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) that runs a collection of standard neural models (avg/DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, a simple attention variant) across several f2-style tasks (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, STS), with standardized evaluation and reporting of confidence intervals over multiple seeds.
- Two new datasets for answer selection/property selection: yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, intended to address limitations of wang and to reflect realistic IR scenarios.
- Empirical observations on variance and transfer: repeated runs with CIs to quantify instability on wang; a transfer-learning experiment pretraining on Ubuntu and finetuning on other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Evaluation discipline: Reporting results averaged over 16 seeds with 95% confidence intervals is a significant improvement over single-run reporting common in this literature. The observation that wang MRR variance exceeds many reported SOTA deltas is important.
- Breadth and practical focus: Running multiple model families across multiple tasks, and explicitly including strong IR baselines (TF-IDF/BM25), is useful. The note that IR features remain competitive in answer selection is realistic and actionable.
- Dataset realism: The proposed yodaqa/large2470 seems larger and harder, with many candidates per question, which can make future improvements more meaningful than on wang. The property-selection task (wqmprop) is a plausible addition.
- Open-source intent: Unifying loaders, models, and adapters in Keras is valuable for reproducibility and for lowering entry costs for future work (though see weaknesses on current reproducibility).
- Transfer-learning signal: The finding that Ubuntu-pretrained RNNs can substantially outperform from-scratch models on other f2 tasks is encouraging and aligns with current trends.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling and claims: The models are standard baselines or direct reimplementations (avg/DAN/CNN/biGRU, simple attention per Tan et al. 2015). The “unified f2 framework” is mainly a packaging and evaluation exercise; there is no new architecture or algorithmic insight. The claim that the work “demonstrates universal semantic comprehension” is not supported by the modest, often sub-SOTA results and lacks strong ablation or analysis.
- Methodological comparability issues in answer selection: Using BM25 both to prune candidates to top-20 and as an extra feature fed to the scorer conflates retrieval with re-ranking and risks inflating performance relative to prior work that ranks the full candidate set. It is unclear whether metrics reported are computed on pruned sets, which would not be directly comparable. There is no ablation showing how much of the gain comes from BM25 features or pruning. Hyperparameters are “primarily tuned on yodaqa/large2470,” introducing bias when reporting across tasks.
- Dataset quality and leakage concerns: The paper acknowledges potential overlap between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across splits but does not quantify or control for it (e.g., de-duplication, lexical overlap analysis). The yodaqa labeling procedure relies on string matching to gold answers in Wikipedia passages, which is likely noisy; no noise analysis or inter-annotator validation is provided. For wqmprop, dataset statistics, split protocol, and labeling quality are under-specified. These issues undermine the datasets’ reliability as benchmarks.
- Reporting gaps and missing details: Many conclusions depend on figures/tables (Figs. 1–4, Table 1) that are not interpretable from the text, and precise numbers are often absent. Key training details are missing or inconsistent: batch sizes, truncation lengths, tokenization, casing, stopword list for overlap features, handling of OOV, sampling strategy when using “1/4 of the dataset per epoch,” and randomization protocol. On Ubuntu, training is restricted to 10% of the data without justification or sensitivity analysis, weakening claims of establishing baselines. The transfer section lacks quantitative results and ablations (e.g., with/without dropout changes, freezing layers).
- Presentation and formatting: The manuscript contains numerous typos and stylistic/formatting issues that hinder readability and professionalism: misspellings (“AnNLP,” “Reciprocial,” “preferrentially,” “interpunction,” “weighed” vs “weighted”), punctuation problems, footnotes interleaved as inline numerals, inconsistent math notation, and a highly inconsistent bibliography (missing years/pages, stray question marks and LaTeX escape artifacts, incomplete entries). References to “URL redacted but included as supplementary material” conflict with the claim of open-source availability.

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- Answer selection protocol: Do you compute MAP/MRR over the full candidate sets or only over the BM25-pruned top-20? If the latter, can you report results without pruning and an ablation without feeding BM25 as a feature?
- Dataset overlap: What exact procedure did you use to detect and remove overlaps between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across splits? Please provide quantitative overlap stats (e.g., Jaccard at question level, near-duplicate detection).
- yodaqa labeling quality: What is the estimated noise rate of string-match labels? Any human validation or inter-annotator agreement? Are there cases with paraphrased answers that are mislabeled negative?
- Reproducibility: Please provide complete hyperparameter settings (batch sizes, sequence length caps, tokenization/casing, optimizer settings per task, negative sampling), and the randomization protocol across the 16 seeds. Will you release exact code/configs and fixed train/dev/test splits for the new datasets?
- Transfer learning: Provide the exact numbers for Ubu.RNN vs baseline RNN across all tasks, with CIs, and ablations on (i) varying the amount of Ubuntu pretraining, (ii) freezing layers, (iii) matching dropout regimes.

Overall recommendation
Reject. While the intent to unify evaluation across sentence-pair tasks and to quantify variance is commendable, the paper offers limited novelty, contains methodological choices that confound fair comparison (BM25 pruning/feature use), introduces new datasets without sufficient quality control or overlap analysis, and suffers from substantial reporting and presentation issues. With stronger rigor on experimental protocol, thorough ablations, clearer and complete reporting, and careful dataset curation, a revised version could make a useful benchmark paper. In its current form, the accumulation of methodological and editorial flaws outweighs the practical contributions.
