Summary and main contributions
- The paper argues for a unified sentence-pair (f2) framing across multiple tasks (answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE/STS) and implements a common evaluation framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS), with standardized training/evaluation pipelines and reporting of confidence intervals across multiple random seeds.
- It introduces two datasets related to answer selection: yodaqa/large2470 (Wikipedia-based) and wqmprop (property selection via Freebase paths), and reports baseline results alongside standard IR baselines (TF-IDF/BM25).
- It presents transfer learning experiments where an RNN trained on Ubuntu Dialogue is fine-tuned on other f2 tasks, showing notable gains over training from scratch.

Strengths
- Emphasis on evaluation methodology. Averaging over 16 random seeds and reporting 95% confidence intervals is a welcome departure from single-run reporting and highlights variance issues, which is critically needed in this literature. The observation that test variance can be larger than incremental “SOTA gaps” on wang is important for the community.
- Strong IR baselines and pragmatic features. Including TF-IDF/BM25 baselines, adding simple overlap and token-type flags, and highlighting the strength of overlap in answer selection tasks provide a realistic anchor that many neural papers omit.
- Cross-task breadth and reusable tooling. Unifying multiple f2 tasks in one codebase and releasing loaders/models (upon de-anonymization) is likely to be useful for practitioners, especially for running standard architectures with consistent settings.
- Transfer learning signal. The Ubuntu-pretrained RNN consistently outperforms training from scratch, suggesting that cross-task weight transfer helps, and is a reasonable baseline to carry forward in future work.
- Empirical observations about model capacity vs. data size (CNNs preferred on smaller data; RNNs on larger data) are plausible and consistent with prior anecdotal reports; quantifying this across tasks is useful.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The architectures are standard (avg/DAN/CNN/biGRU, RNN-CNN, simple attention) with minor engineering choices. The paper’s core claim is more about unification than new modeling. For CoNLL, novelty in method or insight should be stronger, or the empirical study must be unusually thorough; here it is not.
- Potential evaluation confounds in answer selection. Pruning candidates to top-20 by BM25 and feeding BM25 as a feature changes the task setup and may not be comparable to prior reports that score all provided candidates. The claim that pruning has “no adverse effects” is not rigorously supported; evaluating MAP/MRR on a pruned list can change the metric and bias comparison. An ablation with full candidate sets and without BM25 features is needed.
- Dataset quality and leakage concerns. The new yodaqa/large2470 is auto-labeled by exact/heuristic answer string matching, which risks label noise and bias toward lexical overlap; no robustness checks or human validation statistics are provided. The authors acknowledge overlap between wang and yodaqa (potentially across train/test), but do not show deduplication or leakage prevention analyses. This undermines claims of generalization and comparability.
- Weakness of baselines on key tasks and selective reporting. The paper admits lagging behind SOTA on RTE/STS and omits some results. There is no comparison against stronger sentence-pair models available at the time (e.g., sequential attention RTE models), nor against simple but strong baselines like enhanced decomposable models, ESIM-like architectures, or skip-thought/infersent-style sentence encoders. Omitting STS/paraphrase results because they underperform weakens the universality narrative.
- Inconclusive transfer learning analysis. The transfer result is only shown from Ubuntu; no comparison to pretraining on SNLI (also large and arguably closer to inference tasks), multi-task training, or weaker/stronger pretraining signals (e.g., more Ubuntu data). No ablation disentangles the benefit of large-scale pretraining vs. domain match. The heavy reliance on task-specific features (BM25/overlap flags) also detracts from the “task-independent” claim.
- Overfitting and unstable validation. The paper reports heavy overfitting and poor dev–test correlation but proceeds with standard early stopping on dev. A more robust selection protocol (e.g., repeated holdout, cross-validation, or nested selection across the 16 runs) is warranted if the goal is to set a reliable benchmark.

Questions to authors
- For answer sentence selection, do you compute MAP/MRR on the full set of candidates provided by the datasets or only on the BM25-pruned top-20? If the latter, please report results without pruning and without BM25-as-feature to ensure comparability to prior work.
- What steps were taken to remove overlap and leakage between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across train/dev/test? Can you quantify residual overlap and its impact on scores?
- For yodaqa/large2470, what is the label noise rate from automatic answer matching? Please provide a small-scale human validation and error taxonomy (lexical matches lacking semantic correctness, partial matches, multi-span answers, etc.).
- For transfer learning, why pretrain only on Ubuntu rather than comparing against SNLI or multi-task pretraining? Please include an ablation comparing: random init; Ubuntu pretrain; SNLI pretrain; multi-task (Ubuntu+SNLI).
- How sensitive are the transfer gains to the presence of BM25/overlap features and to candidate pruning? Please report transfer results in a fully neural, task-agnostic setting.
- Do you release exact scripts and seeds to reproduce the 16-run aggregates, and are the datasets released with fixed splits and deduplicated IDs?
- Given the reported overfitting, did you try stronger regularization (e.g., weight decay sweeps, variational dropout, label smoothing) or early stopping based on moving-average dev metrics across multiple runs?

Recommendation
While I appreciate the focus on evaluation rigor, IR baselines, and the intent to unify tasks, the methodological novelty is limited, the new datasets raise unresolved quality/leakage concerns, and the evaluation choices (BM25 pruning/features) confound comparability. The transfer learning story is promising but under-analyzed. I lean toward rejection in the current form; tightening the evaluation, providing ablations, and strengthening baselines would substantially improve the paper.
