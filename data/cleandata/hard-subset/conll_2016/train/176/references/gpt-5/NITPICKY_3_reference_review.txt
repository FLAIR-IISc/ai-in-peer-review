Summary and claimed contributions
- A unified evaluation framework for sentence-pair (f2) tasks, with open-source tooling (dataset-sts, PySTS, KeraSTS), standardized training/evaluation across multiple datasets, and reporting of mean over multiple seeds with confidence intervals.
- Introduction of two new datasets for answer selection/property selection (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), with an argument that commonly used datasets (e.g., wang) have limitations.
- Empirical study comparing several standard neural architectures across tasks and demonstrating cross-task transfer by pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue and finetuning on other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Methodological emphasis on variance: Reporting averages over 16 runs with 95% CIs directly addresses the well-known instability in neural models. This is valuable and should be more widely adopted.
- Unification and tooling: A single codebase that plugs diverse f2 tasks into a shared API lowers the barrier for reproducible research and fairer comparison; this is practically useful even if the models are standard.
- Dataset critique and harder benchmarks: The discussion of shortcomings of wang (small/unstable candidate sets, weak dev/test correlation) is on point; proposing larger, more IR-like sets is a step toward more realistic evaluation.
- Coverage of strong non-neural baselines: Including TF-IDF/BM25 and highlighting their competitiveness in some settings grounds the evaluation and is a useful reminder to the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: All architectures are re-implementations or minor variants of established models (avg/DAN/biGRU/CNN/RNN-CNN, simple attention after Tan et al., 2015). There is no methodological contribution in model design beyond recombination; the transfer experiment is relatively shallow and not novel.
- Questionable evaluation protocol in answer selection: Using BM25 both as an additional feature and to prune candidates to top-20 before scoring changes the task and undermines comparability with prior work, especially on datasets with large candidate pools. It is unclear whether gold answers ever fall outside the top-20 at test time, which would bias metrics. Results without BM25 features/pruning are not reported.
- Dataset construction and leakage concerns insufficiently addressed: For yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, key details are missing—exact construction pipeline, positive/negative rates, candidate generation recall, split methodology, inter-annotator agreement, noise level, and licensing. The paper itself admits overlap risks between wang and yodaqa and merely advises not to mix them, but does not demonstrate that no leakage affects reported experiments.
- Missing quantitative substance and unclear comparability: Many claims refer to figures/tables (e.g., Fig. 1–4) but the text reports few concrete numbers. Statements like “cannot statistically determine relation to state-of-art on wang” and “lag behind SOTA on RTE/STS” weaken the empirical contribution. Without side-by-side numbers, significance tests, and protocol matching prior work, it is hard to assess real progress.
- Presentation and formatting quality: The manuscript contains numerous typographical errors (“AnNLP,” “successfuly,” “Reciprocial,” “Retrival,” “interpunction”), inconsistent section numbering (e.g., “Model Performance .1”), broken or malformed references (e.g., “Agirre et al.,? \Q2015\E”), inline footnotes that disrupt flow, and missing details (years, page numbers). This level of sloppiness raises concerns about rigor.
- Experimental detail gaps: Important choices are unspecified or ad hoc—sequence truncation length, tokenization, OOV handling beyond boolean flags, negative sampling and class balancing, early stopping criteria, hyperparameter tuning per task, and rationale for extreme dropout (p=0.8) vs. “no dropout” in other settings. Transfer learning guidance (e.g., “do not apply dropout if source didn’t”) seems heuristic without ablations.

Questions to authors
- Candidate pruning: Do you prune to top-20 candidates at test time on all answer selection datasets? What fraction of examples have gold answers outside the top-20 under BM25? Please report metrics with and without pruning and with and without BM25 as a feature.
- Dataset leakage: Exactly how did you ensure no overlap between train/dev/test across wang and yodaqa/large2470 for any single model instance? Provide statistics on question overlap and the measures taken to avoid contamination.
- New datasets: Provide full dataset cards—sizes, splits, candidate set size distributions, positive rates, annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, examples, licensing terms, and evidence of denoising effectiveness.
- Quantitative results: Please include complete numerical tables (means ± 95% CI) for each model and task, and comparisons to prior state-of-the-art under matched protocols.
- Preprocessing: What are the maximum sequence lengths and truncation strategies per task? How are numbers, casing, and OOVs handled? Are embeddings updated for all tasks? Is the same tokenizer used everywhere?
- Transfer learning: Show ablations isolating the effect of Ubuntu pretraining vs. random init, with and without dropout, and alternative optimizers; provide statistical significance across tasks.

Additional comments
- Consider adding results for models without overlap flags to test the “universal” claim; the current reliance on IR features weakens the argument for task independence.
- Clarify whether you evaluated on the full Ubuntu training set; using only 10% might leave performance untapped.
- Provide training budget and time per model per task to contextualize the practicality of multi-seed evaluation.
- Clean up writing, fix references, standardize notation, and ensure figures/tables are complete and legible.
- Multi-task training is a natural extension; even a small pilot experiment could strengthen the transfer story.

Overall, while the framework-and-datasets angle is useful, the lack of modeling novelty, methodological issues around BM25-based pruning, incomplete dataset documentation, and presentation problems outweigh the strengths for a venue like CoNLL. I do not recommend acceptance in the current form.
