Summary and main contributions
This paper argues for unifying a broad class of sentence-pair problems under a single “f2” framework and investigates whether task-independent neural models can transfer across such tasks. The authors (a) curate a unified experimental framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) with modular adapters, (b) introduce two new datasets for answer sentence selection (yodaqa/large2470) and property selection (wqmprop), along with a more careful evaluation protocol (multiple seeds with confidence intervals; IR baselines; candidate pruning), and (c) demonstrate transfer learning from a model trained on Ubuntu Dialogue to other f2 tasks, suggesting the feasibility of more universal semantic models.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A unified framework and methodology for sentence pair scoring across heterogeneous tasks, including statistical reporting over multiple seeds and strong IR baselines. This is a direction-setting contribution.
2) New datasets that aim to address shortcomings of widely-used benchmarks (e.g., instability/noise in wang), designed to be larger and more realistic for IR-style answer selection.
3) Empirical demonstration that transfer from a large next-utterance ranking dataset can materially improve performance on other f2 tasks, supporting the hypothesis of reusable sentence-pair semantics.

Strengths
- Directional novelty via unification: Treating diverse tasks (answer selection, next utterance ranking, RTE/STS) under a single f2 modeling/evaluation umbrella is a valuable conceptual reframing. The framework and adapters lower barriers for future cross-task research and comparison, which the community currently lacks.
- Methodological rigor that addresses a real pain point: Reporting averages over 16 runs with confidence intervals, explicitly quantifying instability on wang, and consistently comparing to IR baselines (BM25/TF-IDF) are meaningful improvements. The candid analysis of dev/test mismatch and overfitting, and the use of BM25-based pruning, reflect a realistic IR pipeline and strengthen external validity.
- New datasets targeting realism and difficulty: The yodaqa/large2470 dataset (with many candidates per question) and wqmprop property selection set push beyond small/unstable benchmarks, where rank-based metrics are noisy. The observed lower baseline performance suggests more headroom for innovation—useful for driving the field.
- Transfer learning result that is practically useful: The Ubuntu-trained RNN transferred well and often outperformed same-architecture baselines on target tasks, even without sophisticated multi-task training. This is a strong signal that large, weakly structured datasets can seed broadly useful f2 models.
- Open-source tooling (promised): Providing dataset loaders and Keras-based model implementations, if indeed released, will boost reproducibility and adoption, amplifying the paper’s impact beyond its immediate results.

Weaknesses
- Limited model novelty and uneven SOTA: The architectures are largely standard (avg/DAN, biGRU, CNN, RNN-CNN, simple attention), and performance lags on SNLI/SICK/STS. The scientific value is more in the framework and evaluation than in modeling advances. This is acceptable for a direction-setting paper, but it weakens claims around “universal” comprehension.
- Dataset construction details and overlap risks: yodaqa/large2470 and wang share ancestry, with possible cross-split overlaps; the paper warns against mixing them but does not quantify overlap or leakage risk. Labeling based on answer string matches in passages introduces potential noise; data quality and inter-annotator validation are not reported. Without clearer diagnostics, the new datasets’ reliability is hard to judge.
- Heavy reliance on IR features/pruning with limited ablations: BM25 scores are used both for candidate pruning and as a feature to the scorer. Ablations quantifying the contribution of token overlap flags, BM25 feature injection, and pruning choices are not shown. This makes it difficult to isolate neural modeling gains versus IR heuristics.
- Transfer learning scope is narrow: Transfer is shown from a single source task (Ubuntu) with one configuration; there is no controlled analysis of what transfers (embeddings vs encoder vs scorer), whether domain mismatch matters, or comparisons to multi-task training. Claims about “universal” comprehension are suggestive but not yet strongly substantiated.
- Clarity and presentation: There are noticeable typos, broken references, and missing figure/table details in the submission. While minor, they impede understanding and replication, especially given the centrality of datasets and protocols.

Questions to authors
- Dataset quality/overlap: Can you quantify the overlap between yodaqa/large2470 and wang (at question and sentence levels) across splits? What is the estimated label noise rate for yodaqa/large2470 (e.g., via a human sanity check)? Any inter-annotator agreement or spot-check statistics?
- BM25/pruning ablation: How much of the final performance is attributable to BM25-based pruning and BM25 as a feature? Please report ablations removing (a) BM25 feature, (b) candidate pruning, and (c) token-level overlap/number/cap features.
- Transfer details: Which layers were transferred and fine-tuned? Did you try freezing embeddings or the encoder? How sensitive are transfer gains to class imbalance, optimizer choice, and dropout mismatches? Any negative transfer cases?
- Reproducibility: Will you release exact preprocessing scripts, dataset splits, and random seeds? Can you include the promised URLs and commit hashes in the camera-ready?
- Statistical reporting: For all reported numbers, can you provide the per-task standard deviation and the number of runs used to compute CIs? For tasks where CIs overlap with prior SOTA, can you statistically compare?

Overall assessment
Despite modest model novelty and some dataset concerns, this paper takes a bold, timely step toward unifying sentence-pair tasks with stronger methodology and demonstrates promising cross-task transfer. Given the potential to reorient evaluation practices and seed future multi-task/few-shot work, I view it as a valuable, direction-setting contribution, provided the authors clarify dataset quality/overlap and include key ablations.
