Summary and claimed contributions
This paper argues for a unified view of sentence-pair scoring tasks (f2), spanning answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, textual entailment, and STS. It presents a common experimental framework (dataset loaders + Keras-based modeling library), introduces two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 for answer sentence selection and wqmprop for property selection), compares a set of standard neural and IR baselines across tasks with improved evaluation methodology (multiple seeds and confidence intervals), and demonstrates transfer learning from Ubuntu Dialogue pretraining.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Evaluation framework and methodology: a unified pipeline with task adapters, strong classical IR baselines, and statistically sound reporting (multiple runs and CIs).
2) New datasets for answer selection/property selection with more challenging characteristics than the widely used wang benchmark.
3) Empirical evidence for cross-task transfer (pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue improves performance on other tasks).

Strengths
- Methodological rigor and reproducibility: The paper foregrounds an important and under-discussed issue—the high variance of neural models across seeds—and addresses it by averaging 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs. Including strong IR baselines (TF-IDF/BM25) anchors results and discourages overclaiming. The open-source framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) is a valuable contribution that can improve comparability across future work.
- Cross-task breadth: Evaluating the same families of models across four distinct f2-style tasks provides informative trends (e.g., CNNs better on small data; RNNs benefit from larger data), and highlights where IR features dominate (answer selection with many candidates).
- New datasets that surface headroom: The proposed yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop appear larger and more challenging than wang, with many candidates per query and lower baseline performance, which should magnify future improvements. The paper also reports dataset characteristics and caveats.
- Transfer learning demonstration: The Ubuntu-pretrained RNN transferring well to other tasks is a useful empirical result at a time when task-agnostic sentence encoders were still emerging, and it’s implemented with practical guidance (e.g., avoid dropout mismatch).

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling novelty and “unified” claim undercut by task-specific engineering: The architectures are standard (avg/DAN/CNN/biGRU, simple attention variant); the main novelty lies in software and datasets. The purported task-independent approach is compromised by per-task adaptations such as including BM25 as an input feature and pruning candidates to BM25 top-20 during both training and evaluation in answer selection, token-level overlap flags, different dropout policies, and scorer choices (dot vs MLP). These choices likely drive performance but reduce the generality of conclusions about “universal” f2 models.
- Dataset construction and leakage risks insufficiently quantified: yodaqa/large2470 labels are derived by string-matching gold answers in retrieved passages, which can bias toward lexical overlap and introduce noise; wqmprop uses English property labels from KB paths, which may be idiosyncratic and sparsely aligned with natural language. The paper acknowledges overlapping ancestry with wang but does not quantify overlap or enforce de-duplication across splits/datasets—this threatens fair evaluation and transfer claims.
- Evaluation gaps and ablations: The paper notes lagging SOTA on RTE/STS but does not compare against the strongest contemporary sentence-pair models for these tasks. Critical ablations are missing: impact of (i) BM25 pruning and BM25-as-feature, (ii) token-overlap flags, (iii) removing IR features in answer selection, (iv) training on full Ubuntu vs 10%, (v) using listwise vs pairwise ranking losses. Given that IR features can dominate, it’s important to isolate neural model contributions. Reporting “train on 1/4 of dataset per epoch” without analyzing implications further confounds interpretability.
- Overstated generality of transfer: The transfer result is shown for one source (Ubuntu) and one model configuration; there is no comparison to other plausible pretraining sources (e.g., SNLI) or to multi-task training. There’s little analysis of what transfers (embeddings vs encoders), sensitivity to dropout/optimizer choices, or domain mismatch effects. As such, the claim of learning task-independent “text comprehension” is suggestive but not yet substantiated.
- Clarity and completeness: Some implementation details are ad hoc (e.g., turning off dropout in fine-tuning if absent in pretraining; training 1/4 data per epoch), with limited justification. Important hyperparameters and selection procedures are described briefly; error analysis is minimal. The paper would benefit from clearer separation of shared vs task-specific components, and from a more systematic experimental design.

Questions to authors
- Data overlaps/leakage: Can you quantify the overlap between wang and yodaqa/large2470 (and across train/dev/test splits)? Did you remove duplicates or near-duplicates? How do results change if overlaps are eliminated?
- Label quality: What is the estimated noise rate for yodaqa/large2470 from answer-string matching? Was any human validation performed (sample size, agreement)? For wqmprop, how were negative candidates sampled and how often are property labels underspecified or ambiguous?
- IR feature dependence: Please provide ablations removing (a) BM25 top-20 pruning and (b) BM25 as an input feature and token-overlap flags. How much of the observed gains persist without these task-specific signals?
- Transfer details: In Ubuntu→target transfer, which layers were initialized vs fine-tuned (embeddings, encoder, scorer)? How much improvement comes from pretrained word embeddings vs RNN weights? Does using 100% of Ubuntu data further improve transfer?
- Ranking objective: Why choose bipartite RankNet over listwise losses (e.g., ListNet/ListMLE) for answer selection and next-utterance ranking? Any sensitivity analysis?
- Reproducibility and licensing: Will you release all preprocessing, splits, and scripts to recreate yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop? Are there any licensing constraints from Wikipedia/Freebase extraction or YodaQA outputs?

Overall, I view this as a useful infrastructural and empirical paper with clear methodological contributions (framework, evaluation with CIs, inclusion of IR baselines) and promising datasets, but with limited modeling novelty and several issues that weaken the central “unified, task-independent” claim. Strengthening the dataset validation, de-leakage, and ablations around IR features and transfer would substantially improve the paper.
