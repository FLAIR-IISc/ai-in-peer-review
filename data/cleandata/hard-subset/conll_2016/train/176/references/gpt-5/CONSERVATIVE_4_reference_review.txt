Summary
The paper proposes to unify a broad class of sentence-pair tasks (semantic similarity, answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE/STS) under a single “f2” framework. It implements a common evaluation pipeline (dataset-sts/KeraSTS), reports results for a set of standard neural architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, GRU-RNN, RNN-CNN, and an attention variant), and emphasizes more rigorous evaluation practices (multiple runs, CIs, IR baselines). The authors also introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 for answer sentence selection and wqmprop for property selection) and show that a model pre-trained on Ubuntu Dialogue transfers to other tasks.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A unified evaluation framework with code, integrating multiple sentence-pair datasets, common task adapters, and reporting protocols that include variance estimates and IR baselines.
- Two additional datasets intended to remedy limitations of the commonly used wang set and to broaden coverage (answer selection with larger/more challenging candidate sets; property selection).
- An empirical study showing cross-task transfer from Ubuntu dialogue ranking to other f2 tasks, with some gains over task-specific training.

Strengths
- Emphasis on evaluation rigor. Reporting performance averaged over 16 seeds with confidence intervals is a welcome step beyond the single-run reporting common in prior work, particularly on wang where variance is known to be high. The explicit discussion of validation/test mismatch and variance is valuable for the community.
- Inclusion of strong IR baselines and IR-informed practices. Using BM25/TF-IDF provides critical anchors; the analysis that IR baselines remain competitive (especially when candidate sets are large) helps temper over-claims around neural models and reflects realistic system design.
- Open-source framework. Providing dataset adapters and model code in a widely used toolkit (Keras) lowers barriers to replication and comparison; this can facilitate community benchmarks if adopted.
- Broader empirical coverage. Training and evaluating across answer selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE/STS, with consistent modeling choices, yields informative cross-task comparisons, e.g., CNNs doing better on small datasets vs. RNNs benefiting from large data.
- Initial transfer results. The observation that Ubuntu-pretrained RNNs can outperform task-specific RNNs on other tasks is interesting and aligns with the trend toward pretraining; the paper documents some practical considerations (dropout, optimizer, label balancing) for reuse.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The models are largely standard (avg/DAN, CNN, GRU, RNN-CNN, a simple attention variant), and the attention mechanism is adopted from prior work. The primary contribution is engineering unification rather than new modeling ideas. Given the strong claims about a “unified framework,” one would expect either new architectures that explicitly share knowledge across tasks or principled multi-task learning, which is absent.
- Confounding evaluation design choices. For answer selection, the pipeline both prunes candidates to the top-20 by BM25 and feeds BM25 as a feature to the MLP scorer. This can strongly shape the task distribution and risks overstating neural gains; it also makes comparisons to prior “pure” neural systems ambiguous. Ablations without pruning and without IR-feature injection are necessary to isolate the contribution of the neural encoders.
- Dataset concerns and potential leakage. The new yodaqa/large2470 shares ancestry with wang, and the paper itself warns against mixing training/evaluation across them. This overlap risk undermines the dataset’s utility for clean benchmarking unless overlaps are exhaustively identified and removed. Further, automatic labeling by answer-string matching can create false positives/negatives; the paper mentions denoising but provides limited quantitative analysis of label quality and inter-annotator reliability.
- Incomplete comparisons to state of the art. The paper notes that results “lag behind SOTA” on RTE/STS and that the Ubuntu and wang claims are statistically unclear. Stronger baselines (e.g., then-contemporary attention-based RTE models, skip-thought/sentence2vec, or multi-perspective CNNs) and more recent Ubuntu systems should be included. Without competitive baselines, the claimed “new baseline” status on Ubuntu is not persuasive.
- Transfer learning evidence is narrow. The transfer experiments rely on one source (Ubuntu), a specific RNN configuration, and do not compare against other pretraining sources or multi-task training. There is no ablation to separate the effect of source data size from domain similarity. The reported gains, while promising, do not yet establish a generally useful, task-agnostic f2 representation.

Questions to Authors
- For answer selection, please provide ablations: (a) no BM25 pruning; (b) no BM25 feature; (c) both removed. How do these affect MAP/MRR and the relative ranking of models?
- How extensive is the overlap between wang and yodaqa/large2470 at the question and sentence levels? Can you quantify the leakage risk and release de-duplicated splits?
- What is the label quality of yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop? Please report sampling-based annotation accuracy, inter-annotator agreement, and typical error modes of string-match labeling.
- On Ubuntu, which prior systems do you compare against, and are your improvements statistically significant under your 16-run protocol? Please report exact numbers and CIs versus Kadlec et al. and other contemporaneous baselines.
- Did you evaluate multi-task training across f2 tasks within your framework? If not, what impediments did you encounter, and can you share preliminary results or negative findings?
- For transfer learning, how does Ubuntu pretraining compare to pretraining on SNLI or multi-domain mixed pretraining, controlling for total token count? Can you provide learning curves showing sample efficiency gains?

Overall, the paper’s main value is infrastructural and methodological (evaluation practice, open code, IR anchoring). The modeling and empirical claims are incremental and, in places, confounded by design choices. Clearer ablations, stronger baselines, and stricter dataset hygiene would substantially strengthen the case.
