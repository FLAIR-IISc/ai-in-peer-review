Summary
This paper argues for a unified view of sentence-pair tasks (f2), covering answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, textual entailment, and semantic textual similarity. It implements a shared experimental framework with task adapters, evaluates a suite of standard neural architectures and IR baselines across multiple datasets, introduces two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and emphasizes sounder evaluation practices (averaging over 16 runs with confidence intervals). It also demonstrates transfer learning: a model pretrained on Ubuntu Dialogue improves results when fine-tuned on other tasks.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Evaluation framework and methodology: a unified pipeline (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) that normalizes data handling across f2 tasks, includes strong IR baselines, and reports robust statistics (averaging over seeds, CIs), highlighting instability issues common in prior work.
2) New datasets: yodaqa/large2470 (answer sentence selection with larger, harder candidate sets) and wqmprop (property selection as sentence ranking), positioned to alleviate limitations of the widely used wang dataset.
3) Cross-task empirical study and transfer learning evidence: showing that pretraining on a large dialogue ranking task (Ubuntu) yields consistent gains on other f2 tasks, supporting the viability of more universal sentence-pair models.

Strengths
1) Methodological rigor and reproducibility: The paper directly addresses a pervasive problem—high variance and weak comparability in sentence-pair evaluations—by averaging over 16 runs and providing 95% CIs. This is valuable and actionable for the community; the empirical demonstration that single-run results on wang are unreliable is compelling.
2) Unified framework and baselines: Consolidating datasets, task adapters, and multiple standard neural models (avg/DAN, CNN, BiGRU, RNN-CNN, an attention variant) alongside IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) will save others substantial engineering effort and enables fairer cross-task comparisons. Open-source release increases impact.
3) New datasets with better characteristics for IR-style selection: yodaqa/large2470 appears harder and more realistic (larger/uneven candidate sets), addressing known weaknesses of wang. The paper backs this with descriptive statistics and lower baseline performance, increasing headroom for future work.
4) Cross-task transfer learning: The Ubuntu-pretrained RNN consistently improves over task-specific training, providing a practical recipe (pretrain on large ranking data) and supporting the central claim that f2 models can learn transferable sentence comprehension capabilities.
5) Honest analysis of pitfalls: The paper surfaces overfitting, dev–test mismatch, and the strong role of lexical overlap, and it provides pragmatic mitigations (BM25 features, token overlap flags, candidate pruning), which are instructive for practitioners.

Weaknesses
1) Limited model novelty and incomplete coverage: The neural architectures are largely standard; more competitive or recent approaches (e.g., serial attention for RTE, stronger cross-sentence attention, pretrained sentence encoders like skip-thoughts/InferSent) are acknowledged but not included. As a result, the paper lags SOTA on RTE/STS and provides limited insight into where newer modeling would help.
2) Comparability concerns due to IR feature integration and pruning: Incorporating BM25 as an input feature and pruning to top-20 candidates changes the task distribution and may complicate comparison to prior work on answer sentence selection. Ablations that report results without these heuristics are missing, making it hard to isolate the neural models’ standalone contribution.
3) Dataset construction details and potential leakage: For yodaqa/large2470, labels are derived by matching gold answers in retrieved passages (distant supervision). The noise properties, quality control, and any human validation are not quantified. The paper notes possible train–test overlap between wang and yodaqa; more rigorous de-duplication or strict protocols to prevent leakage are needed to ensure uncontaminated evaluation.
4) Analysis depth: The transfer learning result is promising but under-analyzed: which layers/features transfer, how sensitive is it to source/target mismatch, what is the effect of dropout and optimizer choices beyond anecdotal notes, and how does it compare to simple multi-task or joint training? Similarly, an ablation of token-level overlap flags and BM25 inputs would clarify their contribution across tasks.
5) Presentation and completeness: Some references/footnotes and figures are hard to follow or incomplete; hyperparameters differ across tasks but are not fully specified per setting. Reporting for Ubuntu should include standard Recall@k alongside MRR (authors propose MRR, but comparability to prior work depends on Recall@k). Overall readability could be improved.

Questions to authors
- Dataset release and licensing: Will yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop be fully released with scripts? What is the licensing status for Wikipedia-derived passages and Freebase labels, and are there any restrictions?
- Noise and validation: How were answer-bearing sentences labeled in yodaqa/large2470 (exact/normalized string match, alias expansion)? Do you have any estimates of label noise or inter-annotator agreement on a subset?
- De-duplication and leakage: What concrete steps were taken to prevent overlap (near-duplicate questions, paraphrases) between train/dev/test splits, especially across wang and yodaqa? Can you provide hash-based or lexical overlap statistics?
- Ubuntu evaluation: Do you report Recall@1/2/5 for 10-candidate ranking to enable direct comparison to Lowe/Kadlec et al.? If not, can you include them? How large is the performance variance there across seeds?
- BM25 and overlap features: Can you report ablations without BM25 input and without token-overlap flags, and without top-20 pruning, to quantify their impact and allow fairer comparison to prior neural models?
- Transfer learning specifics: Which layers are transferred and which are reinitialized? Did you try freezing subsets or using discriminative learning rates? Any negative transfer observed for particular tasks?

Recommendation
Borderline accept (weak accept). The principal value lies in the unified, statistically sound evaluation framework, open tooling, and the introduction of more realistic datasets for answer sentence selection, plus an informative cross-task study with credible transfer learning gains. While modeling novelty is limited and some experimental choices hamper comparability, the paper addresses an important need in the community and provides artifacts that can improve research practices. Strengthening the dataset documentation, adding missing metrics/ablations, and clarifying leakage controls would further solidify the contribution.
