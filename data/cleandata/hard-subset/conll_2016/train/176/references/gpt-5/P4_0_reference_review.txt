Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a unified view of sentence-pair (f2) problems and evaluates a suite of generic neural architectures across multiple tasks and datasets. The main contributions, as I see them, are:
1) Unified framework and evaluation methodology: an open-source toolkit (dataset-sts, KeraSTS) that standardizes data loaders, task adapters, and model implementations; reporting across multiple random seeds with confidence intervals; and inclusion of strong IR baselines for anchoring results.
2) New datasets for answer sentence selection: yodaqa/large2470 with larger, more realistic candidate sets, and wqmprop (property selection) derived from WebQuestions/Freebase paths.
3) Empirical study across tasks and transfer learning: comparative evaluation of avg/DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, and attention models on answer selection, next utterance ranking (Ubuntu), SNLI/SICK RTE, and STS; and a demonstration that pretraining on Ubuntu improves performance on other tasks.

Strengths
- Methodological rigor and reproducibility: The paper tackles a real issue in f2-model evaluation—high variance across runs—by averaging over 16 seeds and reporting 95% CIs. Including BM25/TF-IDF baselines anchors the results and helps avoid misleading neural-only comparisons. The release of a unified open-source framework is practically valuable for the community and lowers barriers to comparable experiments.
- Dataset contributions that stress models more realistically: yodaqa/large2470 presents larger and more uneven candidate sets than wang, which should reduce evaluation instability and make gains more meaningful. The authors note lower baseline performance on the new data, suggesting room for future improvements. Both datasets avoid restrictive licenses.
- Breadth of empirical coverage and distilled insights: The paper compares several standard architectures under a single setup and derives consistent takeaways: CNNs perform better on small datasets; RNNs benefit from larger data; attention is especially impactful for IR-style sentence selection; IR baselines remain strong and ensembling is beneficial. These task-level patterns are useful guidance for practitioners.
- Transfer learning evidence: Pretraining an RNN on Ubuntu next-utterance ranking and fine-tuning on other tasks yields sizable gains over from-scratch training, supporting the central claim that generic sentence-pair representations are reusable. The paper provides concrete training recipes (e.g., dropout consistency, optimizer changes) that improve transfer stability.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling novelty: The architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, GRU-RNN, RNN-CNN, attention) and scoring modules are standard, and the attention variant follows prior work (Tan et al., 2015). The primary novelty lies in engineering a unified framework and new datasets, rather than proposing new modeling techniques.
- Comparability concerns due to IR features and pruning: For answer selection, using BM25 both to prune candidates to top-20 and as an input feature may shift the task away from “pure” sentence-pair modeling and complicate comparisons to prior work that did not adopt these steps. There is no ablation quantifying the specific contribution of BM25 features/pruning versus the neural components, and simple averaging ensembling is reported only briefly with mixed effects.
- Potential data leakage and split issues: The authors note that yodaqa/large2470 and wang share ancestry and may have overlapping questions, advising against mixing training and evaluation across them. It is unclear whether all reported experiments strictly enforce non-overlap and whether overlap statistics are quantified. The paper also reports small, unstable dev/test splits for wang, but does not introduce a remedy beyond multi-run averaging.
- Incomplete SOTA alignment and analysis: Results lag behind state of the art on RTE/STS tasks, and the paper omits comparisons with more advanced architectures known to perform well (e.g., serial attention for RTE, Tree-LSTM variants). Claims about “universal” f2 models are therefore somewhat overstated given mixed performance. Error analyses and per-category breakdowns (e.g., by lexical overlap, sentence length) are absent.
- Statistical reporting gaps and experimental choices: While confidence intervals across seeds are provided, pairwise significance testing is not reported, and figures/tables (e.g., Fig. 1–4) are central but details are sparse here. The Ubuntu experiments use only 10% of available training pairs without justification; the effect of pretraining data size on transfer is not explored. Ablations on critical design choices (overlap flags, dropout rates, optimizer choices) are limited.

Questions to Authors
- BM25/pruning ablations: What is the isolated effect of (a) BM25 pruning, (b) BM25 as an input feature, and (c) token-level overlap features on answer selection metrics? Please provide ablations to establish fair comparability and clarify how much the neural model contributes beyond IR heuristics.
- Data overlap controls: What concrete steps did you take to ensure no leakage between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across train/dev/test? Can you report overlap statistics and, if needed, provide cleansed splits?
- Ubuntu pretraining scale: Why use only 1M (10%) training pairs? How do transfer gains scale with more pretraining data? Please include a curve or at least a larger-data point.
- Transfer baselines: How does Ubuntu-pretrained fine-tuning compare to (i) training longer from scratch, (ii) initializing/fine-tuning only embeddings, (iii) freezing lower layers vs full fine-tuning, and (iv) multi-task joint training on source and target tasks?
- Dataset transparency: Can you release dataset creation scripts and detailed statistics (candidate set sizes distribution, positives per question, lexical overlap metrics, label noise estimates from Turker denoising) to facilitate future use?
- Pure neural evaluations: Can you report results on answer selection without BM25 features/pruning and with the evaluation protocols used by prior work on wang, to isolate neural capacity and ensure apples-to-apples comparisons?
- RTE/STS gap: Which bottlenecks prevent matching SOTA on SNLI/SICK/STS (e.g., model capacity, attention form, task-specific architectures)? Any preliminary results with serial/soft alignment attention or tree-structured models within your framework?

Overall, the paper’s unification, datasets, and evaluation methodology are valuable contributions. The empirical study is broad and the transfer-learning evidence is promising. Addressing the comparability and leakage concerns and adding key ablations would significantly strengthen the submission.
