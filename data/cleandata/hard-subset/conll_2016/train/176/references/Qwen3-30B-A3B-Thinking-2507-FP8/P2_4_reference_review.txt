

# Review for "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

## Summary
This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks (f2(s0,s1)), arguing that many NLP tasks (Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, etc.) can be treated as semantic similarity problems between two sentences. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations of existing datasets, present a unified software framework (dataset-sts), and evaluate multiple neural models across multiple tasks. They claim to demonstrate the feasibility of universal semantic comprehension models through cross-task transfer learning.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **New datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop)** - Addressing critical limitations in existing datasets (small size, uneven candidate sets, etc.)
2. **Unified evaluation framework (dataset-sts)** - Providing a modular, open-source implementation for cross-task comparison
3. **Empirical demonstration of cross-task transfer learning** - Showing modest performance gains when transferring models across tasks

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution of new datasets**: The authors correctly identify major limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets (Wang, WikiQA, InsuranceQA), particularly their small size, uneven candidate sets, and problematic splits. The new yodaqa/large2470 dataset is well-motivated and addresses these issues with a larger scale (2470 questions vs. Wang's ~600), better candidate sentence distribution, and careful data curation. This is a substantial contribution that will enable more reliable future research.

2. **Improved evaluation methodology**: The paper makes a compelling case for reporting confidence intervals due to high variability in neural model performance (empirical standard deviation of 0.025 for Wang MRR). This is an important methodological improvement over the standard practice of reporting single test runs, which the authors rightly point out leads to misleading comparisons between papers.

3. **Demonstration of transfer learning**: The authors show that training a model on one task (Ubuntu Dialogue) and transferring to other tasks yields consistent improvements over task-specific training. This is an important finding suggesting models learn more general sentence representations rather than task-specific patterns. The transfer learning results are particularly strong for the Answer Sentence Selection task, where the transferred model outperforms the baseline RNN.

4. **Practical implementation and open source**: The creation of the dataset-sts framework and KeraSTS library provides a valuable resource for the NLP community, making it easier to compare models across tasks. The open-source nature and use of popular Keras library enhance reproducibility and reusability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated claims about "universal semantic comprehension"**: The paper claims to demonstrate "universal semantic comprehension models," but the evidence is limited. The transfer learning gains are modest (typically 1-2% absolute improvement), and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that models are learning truly general representations. The paper shows models can transfer to related tasks, but this doesn't necessarily mean they've achieved universal comprehension across diverse semantic relationships.

2. **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper claims to "establish the watermark for future research" but doesn't adequately compare to the current state-of-the-art on each task. For example, on the Answer Sentence Selection task, they don't compare to the best models on Wang (which have reached 0.8+ MRR), making it unclear if their new baseline is actually competitive. The paper acknowledges their models "clearly yet lag behind state-of-art" on RTE and STS tasks without providing specific comparisons.

3. **Insufficient justification for dataset creation**: While the authors identify problems with existing datasets, they don't thoroughly demonstrate that their new datasets avoid these issues. The paper states that Wang and yodaqa share common questions, but doesn't provide evidence of how much overlap exists between train/test splits. This is particularly concerning for the claim that the new dataset avoids the data leakage problem.

4. **Limited model innovation**: The paper evaluates standard neural architectures (RNN, CNN, attention) rather than presenting novel model structures. The core contribution is the framework and datasets, not the models themselves. While this is fine, the paper overstates the novelty of the approach by suggesting they're pioneering a new direction in model architecture.

5. **Inconsistent task representation**: The paper treats Answer Sentence Selection as a ranking task but acknowledges that InsuranceQA is "effectively a classification task rather than a ranking task." However, they don't clarify how their framework handles this distinction, which could impact model performance and interpretation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the newly proposed datasets have much lower baseline performance," suggesting future improvements will be more apparent. Can you provide specific baseline numbers (e.g., MRR for BM25 on yodaqa/large2470) to demonstrate this claim?

2. The paper notes that Wang and yodaqa datasets share common questions. How did you verify that there's no significant overlap between train/test splits? What percentage of questions are shared between the two datasets?

3. On the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, the authors report that their model "consistently ranks as the best or among the best classifiers." Can you provide specific performance metrics (MRR, Recall@1, etc.) to support this claim?

4. The paper states that "the RNN configuration used for the transfer, when trained only on the target task, is not shown in the tables but has always been worse than the baseline RNN configuration." Can you provide these missing numbers to help readers understand the magnitude of the transfer learning gains?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the NLP community by introducing two well-motivated new datasets that address critical limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection resources, and by providing a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models. The improved evaluation methodology (reporting confidence intervals) is particularly commendable. However, the paper overstates its contribution regarding "universal semantic comprehension" and doesn't adequately compare to the state-of-the-art on each task. While the transfer learning results are interesting, they don't justify the stronger claims made about universal models.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better moderate the claims about "universal semantic comprehension" and to provide more specific comparisons to state-of-the-art results on each task. The paper has significant value in its datasets and framework, but the claims need to be more carefully calibrated to the evidence provided.
