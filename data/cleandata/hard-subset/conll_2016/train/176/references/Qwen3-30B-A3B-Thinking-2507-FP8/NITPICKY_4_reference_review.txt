

# Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

## Summary
This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, introducing new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) and evaluating various neural models across multiple tasks. The authors claim to demonstrate the feasibility of universal, task-independent semantic comprehension models through cross-task transfer learning.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Introduction of two new datasets for Answer Sentence Selection (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop)
2. Creation of a unified software framework (dataset-sts) for sentence pair scoring tasks
3. Demonstration of transfer learning across sentence pair scoring tasks

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in NLP: the fragmentation of research across different sentence pair tasks. The creation of the dataset-sts framework is a valuable contribution that could facilitate future research. The paper makes a reasonable effort to address evaluation methodology by reporting confidence intervals across multiple training runs, which is a significant improvement over standard practices.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Critical Data Leakage in Evaluation Methodology (Most Serious)
The paper describes using BM25 to prune candidate sentences to the top 20 before scoring with their neural model. This is a fundamental methodological flaw. As stated in Section 4.2: "we use the BM25 overlap baseline as an additional input to the MLP scoring module, and prune the scored samples to top 20 based on BM25." This creates data leakage because the model isn't actually learning to score all pairs - it's only learning to score a subset pre-selected by BM25. The claim that "this speeds up training greatly and models well a typical Information Retrieval scenario" is irrelevant to the paper's stated goal of evaluating universal semantic comprehension. This flaw invalidates the entire evaluation for Answer Sentence Selection tasks.

### 2. Inconsistent Notation and Terminology
The paper inconsistently defines the task function: "f2(s0, s1) ∈ [0, 1]" in the introduction but later uses "f2(s0, s1) ∈ R" without explanation. More importantly, the paper claims to unify "semantic similarity" tasks, but includes classification tasks (like RTE) where the output isn't a similarity score. This conceptual confusion undermines the paper's main premise.

### 3. Incomplete and Inconsistent References
The references contain multiple formatting inconsistencies: some have "?", "Q", "E" markers; some entries are incomplete (e.g., "Agirre et al\.,? \Q2015\E, Agirre et al\.,"); and the citation style is inconsistent throughout. This is unprofessional for a conference paper and indicates poor attention to detail.

### 4. Vague and Unsupported Claims
The paper makes several unsupported claims:
- "the attention mechanism also has the highest impact in this kind of Information Retrieval task" (Section 4.4) - no evidence provided
- "our models clearly yet lag behind the state-of-art" (Section 4.4) - no state-of-the-art numbers provided for comparison
- "it is not possible to statistically determine its relation to state-of-art" (Section 4.4) - no statistical tests provided

### 5. Methodological Inconsistencies
The paper claims "we have found that applying massive dropout p = 4/5 both on the input and output of the network helps to avoid overfitting" (Section 3.2). A dropout rate of 4/5 is extremely high and would likely cause severe underfitting, contradicting the claim of avoiding overfitting. The paper fails to provide evidence for this unusual hyperparameter choice.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you justify the BM25 pruning step given the paper's stated goal of evaluating universal semantic comprehension models? Is this not a data leakage issue that undermines your evaluation methodology?

2. The paper claims to unify "semantic similarity" tasks but includes classification tasks like RTE where the output isn't a similarity score. How does your framework handle this conceptual inconsistency?

3. Your Figure 1 is mentioned but not included in the paper. Could you provide the missing figure to clarify your dataset comparisons?

4. You claim "it is not possible to statistically determine its relation to state-of-art on the wang Answer Sentence Selection dataset" (Section 4.4), yet you don't provide any statistical tests to support this claim. What statistical evidence supports this assertion?

## Overall Recommendation
While the paper addresses an important problem in NLP and has some valuable contributions (particularly the dataset-sts framework), the critical data leakage issue in the evaluation methodology fundamentally undermines the paper's main claims. The inconsistent notation, unsupported claims, and formatting issues further diminish the paper's credibility. The flaws are substantial enough that I must recommend rejection, as the paper's core claims about universal semantic comprehension cannot be validated given these methodological shortcomings.

The paper's most serious flaw is the data leakage in the Answer Sentence Selection evaluation, which invalidates the results for the primary task the authors focus on. Without a valid evaluation methodology, the paper's main contribution cannot be assessed. The other issues, while less severe, collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail that would make this work difficult to build upon in the future.
