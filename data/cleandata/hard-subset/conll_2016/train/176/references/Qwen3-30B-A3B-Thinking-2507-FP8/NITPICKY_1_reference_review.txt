

# Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

## Summary
This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks (f2-type problems) including Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, and Semantic Textual Similarity. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), develop a software framework (dataset-sts), and evaluate various neural models across these tasks. They claim to demonstrate that simple neural models can learn universal semantic comprehension through cross-task transfer learning.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. Introduction of a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks
2. Creation of new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) for Answer Sentence Selection
3. Development of the dataset-sts software framework

## Strengths
1. The proposed software framework (dataset-sts) is well-designed and could benefit the community by enabling easier implementation and comparison of sentence pair scoring models.

2. The paper's claim of dataset limitations for existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets (particularly wang) is valid, and the introduction of yodaqa/large2470 addresses a genuine need for better benchmarking.

3. The demonstration of transfer learning (RNN model trained on Ubuntu Dialogue and then fine-tuned on other tasks) provides promising evidence for the feasibility of task-independent semantic comprehension models.

## Weaknesses (Major Issues)

1. **Citation and Formatting Errors**: The paper contains numerous critical citation errors that undermine its credibility. For example:
   - References contain inconsistent formatting like "Agirre et al.,? \Q2015\E" and "Baudiš and Šedivý.,? 2015"
   - "MRR" is misspelled as "Reciprocial Rank" in the text
   - Inconsistent use of "Fig." vs. "Figure" and inconsistent spacing in references

2. **Methodological Ambiguity**: The paper lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility:
   - "Massive dropout p = 4/5" is vague and non-standard terminology
   - "Standard parameters" for Adam optimization is not specified
   - Training on "1/4 of the dataset in each epoch" is unclear and likely a typo
   - The claim about "no adverse effects" of pruning to top 20 candidates based on BM25 lacks evidence

3. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: The paper makes several assertions without supporting evidence:
   - "The set of candidate sentences is often very small and quite uneven" (no quantitative evidence provided)
   - "Validation and test sets are very small" (no specific size metrics given)
   - "Minimum correlation between performance on validation and test sets" (no correlation values reported)
   - "wang test MRR has empirical standard deviation of 0.025" (no details on number of repeated runs)

4. **Reproducibility Issues**: The GitHub URL for the dataset-sts framework is redacted, making it impossible for readers to access the implementation. Additionally, the paper claims to "extend the embedding of each input token by several extra dimensions" but doesn't specify exactly how these are implemented.

5. **Logical Inconsistency**: The paper claims to unify research within a single framework but then states "Mixing training and evaluation on wang and yodaqa datasets within a single model instance is not advisable" because they share a common ancestry. This directly contradicts the unification claim.

## Minor Technical Issues
- Inconsistent use of "f2" vs. "f2-type" throughout the paper
- "Siamese architecture" is mentioned but not properly defined
- The paper claims to "use the dot-product module E0 · ET1" but then later refers to "E0 · ET1" which is a typo (should be E0 · E1^T)
- The claim about "the empirical standard deviation of 0.025" lacks context about the number of repeated runs

## Questions to Authors
1. What specific evidence supports the claim that the wang validation and test sets are "very small" and have "minimum correlation" between performance?
2. Why is the dropout rate p=4/5 described as "massive" rather than simply stating the value?
3. Can you provide the exact correlation values between validation and test set performance for the wang dataset?
4. How many repeated runs were used to calculate the empirical standard deviation of 0.025 for the wang test MRR?
5. Why was the training restricted to 1/4 of the dataset per epoch? Was this a typo for "1/4 of the training set"?

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a potentially valuable unified framework and introduces new datasets, the numerous technical, methodological, and formatting flaws significantly undermine its credibility and scientific rigor. The citation errors alone are unacceptable for a scientific publication, and the lack of specific methodological details makes the work difficult to reproduce. The paper's claims about dataset limitations and statistical significance are unsubstantiated, and the logical inconsistency regarding dataset overlap contradicts the central unification claim. These issues outweigh the paper's potential contributions, and the work would benefit from substantial revision before being suitable for publication.

The paper's flaws are not merely minor but fundamental to its scientific validity, making it difficult to assess the true significance of the proposed contributions. For a venue like CoNLL, which emphasizes rigorous methodology and clear presentation, these issues are particularly problematic.
