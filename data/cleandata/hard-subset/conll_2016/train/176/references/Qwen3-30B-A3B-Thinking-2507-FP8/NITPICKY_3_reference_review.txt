

# Review: "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

## Summary
The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks (f2-type problems), introduces new datasets for Answer Sentence Selection (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and presents a new evaluation methodology using confidence intervals. The authors claim to demonstrate transfer learning across tasks and provide a software framework (dataset-sts) to support their work.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A new evaluation methodology using confidence intervals to address neural model variability
2. A new software framework (dataset-sts) for sentence pair scoring tasks
3. New datasets for Answer Sentence Selection

## Strengths
1. The evaluation methodology with confidence intervals is a valuable contribution that addresses a genuine problem in NLP research (high variability in neural model results across runs).
2. The software framework (dataset-sts) provides a useful tool for reproducibility and future research.
3. The paper identifies meaningful limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets that warrant new benchmarking.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Formatting and Consistency Issues**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine professionalism. References use inconsistent styles (e.g., "Agirre et al\.,? \Q2015\E" vs. "Agirre et al., 2015"), typos ("successfuyly" in abstract), and inconsistent terminology ("f2-type" vs. "f2-style"). The reference list has 14 entries with "et al\.,? " formatting errors, and the paper claims "16 training runs" but doesn't specify seed selection methodology.

2. **Inadequate Dataset Justification**: The authors claim to introduce "new datasets with better characteristics" but fail to provide critical details: 
   - No clear description of dataset sizes (yodaqa/large2470's sample count is missing)
   - No evidence that their new datasets are significantly better than existing ones (e.g., "baseline performances are much lower" but no numbers provided)
   - The paper acknowledges "wang and yodaqa datasets share common ancestry" yet proposes using them together without addressing potential data leakage

3. **Methodological Weaknesses**: The paper's claims about transfer learning are undermined by:
   - Using a weak baseline (RNN) that's "not shown in the tables" but used as the comparison point
   - Not demonstrating whether transfer learning improves performance over training from scratch on each task
   - Claiming "dramatically outperforming the baseline" while providing no quantitative evidence of the magnitude of improvement

4. **Inconsistent Claims**: The paper states "the validation set performance is not a great approximator for test set performance" but then uses validation set performance for early stopping (a contradiction). The paper also claims "the total number of individual sentence pairs as well as questions is relatively small" for wang, yet later states they use "only the first 1M pairs (10%) of the training set" for SNLI, creating confusion about dataset sizes.

5. **Insufficient Evidence for Core Claims**: The paper's primary claim of "universal semantic comprehension models" is not adequately supported. The results show models perform better on some tasks than others, with no clear evidence that the models have learned a universal representation. The transfer learning results are modest, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the models have learned something generalizable beyond the specific tasks.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide the exact sizes of the new yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop datasets, and how they compare to existing datasets?
2. What specific quantitative evidence supports the claim that "baseline performances on the newly proposed datasets are much lower"?
3. How did you ensure no data leakage between the wang and yodaqa datasets when using them together in the same framework?

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a useful software framework and identifies a genuine problem with neural model variability, the numerous formatting inconsistencies, inadequate dataset justification, methodological weaknesses, and insufficient evidence for the core claims significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The paper would benefit from substantial revisions to address these issues before publication. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these minor flaws too numerous to overlook, despite the paper's potential value. The inconsistencies in presentation and methodology suggest the work may not be sufficiently mature for publication at this stage. I recommend rejection.
