

# Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

## Summary
The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, arguing for the existence of universal semantic comprehension models applicable across multiple tasks. The authors introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) for Answer Sentence Selection, evaluate multiple neural models across several tasks (Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, RTE, and STS), and demonstrate transfer learning across tasks.

## Main Contributions
1. Introduction of a unified framework (dataset-sts) for sentence pair scoring tasks
2. Creation of two new datasets addressing limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets
3. Improved evaluation methodology including confidence intervals to address random variation in neural model results

## Strengths
1. **Unified framework**: The dataset-sts software package is well-designed and would be valuable for future research. The open-source nature of the framework is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **New datasets**: The authors correctly identify limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets (small size, uneven candidate distributions) and introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) that address these issues.

3. **Evaluation methodology**: The paper's emphasis on statistical rigor (95% confidence intervals) is a major contribution. The authors correctly identify that random variation in neural models has been overlooked in previous work, with standard deviations exceeding the gap between successive papers on the same dataset.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent formatting and technical errors**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. The dataset is referred to as "yodaqa/large2470" in the text but "yodaqa/large2470" in the references (with inconsistent capitalization). The model "Ubu. RNN" uses an unclear abbreviation with a period, and the paper contains typos like "successfully applied" (should be "successfully applied"). The references are messy with inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Agirre et al.,? \Q2015\E"), which is unprofessional for a conference submission.

2. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims to "demonstrate the feasibility of pursuing universal, task-independent f2 models" but the results don't clearly support this. The models still lag behind state-of-the-art on RTE and STS tasks, and the transfer learning results (Ubu. RNN) show modest improvements over baselines. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the transfer learning benefits are due to "universal semantic comprehension" rather than task-specific characteristics.

3. **Lack of statistical rigor**: While the paper mentions confidence intervals, it fails to provide statistical significance tests for the differences between models. The paper claims to "establish the new baseline" but doesn't demonstrate that the differences are statistically meaningful. For example, the paper states "it is not possible to statistically determine its relation to state-of-art on the wang Answer Sentence Selection dataset" - this undermines the paper's main claim.

4. **Insufficient methodological details**: Critical details for reproducibility are missing. The paper states "we prune the scored samples to top 20 based on BM25" but doesn't explain how this affects evaluation. The exact hyperparameter values (beyond the vague "tuned primarily on yodaqa/large2470") are missing. The paper claims "p = 4/5" dropout "helps to avoid overfitting" but this is an extremely high dropout rate (typically 0.2-0.5) and lacks justification.

5. **Contradictory statements**: The paper states "we apply massive dropout p = 4/5" but later says "it is important not to apply dropout during retraining if it wasn't applied during the source model training" - this is contradictory since dropout was applied in the source model. This inconsistency suggests poor attention to detail.

## Questions to Authors
1. How exactly was the yodaqa/large2470 dataset created, and how does it differ from the existing wang dataset in terms of question-answer distribution and candidate sentence diversity?
2. Why was "p = 4/5" dropout used when this is significantly higher than standard practice, and what evidence supports this choice?
3. The paper claims transfer learning "dramatically outperforms the baseline RNN model" - please provide statistical significance tests to support this claim.

## Overall Recommendation
This paper has valuable elements (the unified framework, new datasets, improved evaluation methodology) but is marred by numerous technical inconsistencies, overstated claims, and insufficient methodological details. The formatting errors, contradictory statements, and lack of statistical rigor are particularly concerning for a conference like CoNLL where attention to detail is paramount. While the framework itself is promising, the paper fails to properly demonstrate its significance due to these issues. The minor flaws collectively outweigh the paper's contributions, leading to a recommendation for rejection.
