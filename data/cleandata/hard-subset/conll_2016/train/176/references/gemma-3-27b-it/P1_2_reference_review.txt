## Peer Review: Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating and developing models for sentence pair scoring (f2-type tasks) – a class of problems involving semantic similarity assessment between two sentences. The authors identify several such tasks (answer selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment), survey existing baselines (both neural and non-neural), and introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) designed to address shortcomings in existing resources. They also demonstrate the feasibility of transfer learning across these tasks, suggesting the potential for developing more general semantic comprehension models.  A key component is the release of an open-source framework (“dataset-sts”) to facilitate research in this area.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Framework & Benchmarking:** The paper provides a valuable, unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across diverse tasks. This is a significant contribution, addressing the fragmented nature of research in this area and enabling more meaningful comparisons.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop datasets addresses recognized limitations in existing benchmarks (small size, uneven distributions, noisy evaluation) and offers richer resources for future research, especially in answer selection.
3. **Open-Source Implementation:** The release of “dataset-sts” is a practical and valuable contribution, lowering the barrier to entry for researchers and promoting reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Real Problem:** The paper tackles a significant issue in NLP – the lack of standardized evaluation and the difficulty of comparing models across different semantic similarity tasks. The proposed framework directly addresses this.
2. **Thorough Baseline Comparison:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of both traditional IR baselines *and* neural network models, providing a solid foundation for assessing the performance of new approaches. The inclusion of IR baselines is particularly commendable, as they often represent strong competitors.
3. **Dataset Improvements are Significant:** The new datasets, particularly yodaqa/large2470, appear to be well-motivated and designed to overcome limitations of existing resources.  The numerical analysis in Figure 1 effectively demonstrates these shortcomings.
4. **Emphasis on Statistical Rigor:** The authors rightly highlight the instability of results due to randomness in neural networks and appropriately advocate for reporting confidence intervals. This enhances the credibility of the reported findings.
5. **Practical Software Release:** The “dataset-sts” framework is a major strength. Its open-source nature and modular design will undoubtedly benefit the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** The models themselves (RNNs, CNNs) are fairly standard. While the framework and datasets are strong contributions, the modeling component feels less innovative.  The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, but it's a notable limitation.
2. **Transfer Learning Results are Preliminary:** The transfer learning experiments, while promising, are limited in scope. The specific configuration used (dot-product scorer, smaller dimensionality) feels somewhat arbitrary and the reasoning for choosing it isn’t fully explained. More extensive experiments with different architectures and transfer strategies would strengthen this claim.
3. **Insufficient Analysis of Dataset Characteristics:** While the authors point out problems with the Wang dataset, a deeper analysis of the new datasets’ characteristics (e.g., error analysis, difficulty distribution) would be valuable. Understanding *why* the new datasets are challenging is crucial for guiding future model development.
4. **Presentation Could Be More Concise:** The paper is quite lengthy, and some sections (especially the model descriptions) could benefit from more concise writing.  This impacts readability and makes it harder to focus on the core contributions.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Transfer Learning Details:** Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the transfer learning experiments?  Specifically, did you tune hyperparameters specifically for the target tasks, or did you use the same hyperparameters as the source task?  What role did dropout play in the transfer learning setup?
2. **Dataset Error Analysis:** Have you conducted any error analysis on the yodaqa/large2470 or wqmprop datasets to identify common types of errors and potential areas for model improvement?
3. **Model Architecture Justification:** Why was the dot-product scorer with smaller dimensionality chosen for the transfer learning experiments? Were other configurations explored?
4. **IR Baseline Integration:** You mention ensembleing IR baselines with neural models. Could you elaborate on the specific methods explored and the results obtained?
5. **Framework Scalability:** How scalable is the “dataset-sts” framework to other f2-type tasks?  What modifications would be necessary to incorporate a completely new task with a novel data format?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive, which is good.
*   The inclusion of GitHub URLs for the datasets and framework is excellent.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of all datasets used in the evaluation (size, distribution, difficulty).
*   While the paper mentions a potential issue with overlap between Wang and yodaqa datasets, could this overlap be quantified?




Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in NLP. The unified framework, new datasets, and open-source implementation represent valuable contributions to the field. While the modeling component lacks significant novelty, the paper's overall impact is substantial. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and clarifying the questions raised.
