This paper presents a unified framework for sentence pair scoring (f2-type problems) across multiple NLP tasks, including answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, and textual entailment. The authors introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations in existing datasets and provide a comprehensive evaluation of various neural and non-neural baselines. A key contribution is demonstrating the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning to improve model performance.

**Contributions:**

1. **Unified Evaluation Framework:** The paper establishes a standardized framework (dataset-sts, KeraSTS) for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across diverse tasks, addressing the fragmented nature of previous research and providing a consistent basis for comparison. This framework, being open-source, is a valuable resource for the research community.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop provides more robust and challenging datasets, particularly for answer sentence selection, overcoming shortcomings of existing benchmarks like wang. The larger scale and improved characteristics of these datasets are significant.
3. **Demonstration of Cross-Task Transfer Learning:** The results show that transferring knowledge from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset to other tasks (via fine-tuning) leads to performance improvements, supporting the hypothesis that a universal semantic comprehension model is achievable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a very thorough evaluation, comparing multiple models (IR baselines, DAN, RNN, CNN, attention-based) across several tasks. The inclusion of confidence intervals enhances the reliability and interpretability of the results.
2. **Addressing Dataset Limitations:** The authors clearly identify and address the limitations of existing datasets, specifically those related to size, data quality, and evaluation metrics. The introduction of new datasets is a strong contribution to the field.
3. **Practical Framework & Open Source Release:** The creation and open-source release of the dataset-sts and KeraSTS framework directly benefits the research community by providing readily available tools for experimentation and comparison.  This facilitates reproducibility and further research.
4. **Rigorous Methodology:** The detailed description of the experimental setup, including hyperparameters, training procedures, and regularization techniques, demonstrates a rigorous scientific approach. The discussion of potential pitfalls (e.g., dataset overlap, validation set overfitting) is appreciated.
5. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the f2-type problem and its relevance to a wide range of NLP tasks, providing a compelling motivation for the research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** The models employed (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) are relatively standard in the field. While the framework and evaluation are significant, the architectural innovations are limited.  The core models themselves don't represent a major leap forward in model design.
2. **Transfer Learning Results Could Be Stronger:**  While the transfer learning results are positive, the performance gains are not always dramatic. A more in-depth analysis of the transfer learning process (e.g., examining which layers benefit most from transfer, exploring different transfer strategies) would be valuable.
3. **Overemphasis on Baseline Performance:** While anchoring against strong baselines is commendable, the paper sometimes appears overly focused on establishing a baseline rather than pushing the state-of-the-art. The results on RTE and STS are acknowledged to be lagging behind existing work.
4. **Dataset Specific Tuning:** Hyperparameters were primarily tuned on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset. While reasonable, it’s unclear how well these hyperparameters generalize across all tasks. A more systematic hyperparameter optimization strategy across all datasets would strengthen the findings.
5. **Inconsistent Reporting:** The text mentions a URL for supplementary material, but it is redacted. This hinders reproducibility and thorough evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the ablation studies performed to assess the contribution of individual components of your models (e.g., dropout, embedding dimensionality, choice of scorer)?
2.  Regarding the transfer learning experiments, what specific layers were fine-tuned during the transfer process? Did you experiment with freezing different layers or using different learning rates for different layers?
3.  You mention potential overlaps between the wang and yodaqa datasets. How did you address this potential issue during your evaluation?  Did you perform any analysis to quantify the extent of the overlap and its impact on the results?
4.  Could you provide the complete URL for the supplementary material referenced in the text?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The use of LaTeX and proper referencing is commendable.
*   The discussion of future work is relevant and provides potential directions for further research.
