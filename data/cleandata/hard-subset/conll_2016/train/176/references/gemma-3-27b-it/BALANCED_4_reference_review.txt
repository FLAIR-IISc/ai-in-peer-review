This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into sentence pair scoring (f2-type problems) as a unified framework for text comprehension. The authors identify several tasks amenable to this formulation (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, recognizing textual entailment/semantic similarity), survey relevant baselines, introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and evaluate a range of neural models within a consistent framework, including experiments with cross-task transfer learning.

The most significant contribution of this work is the creation and release of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset for answer sentence selection. The authors convincingly demonstrate shortcomings in the existing Wang dataset and provide a well-motivated and substantially larger alternative, addressing issues of small candidate set size, uneven distributions, and unreliable validation/test splits.  The rigorous evaluation methodology, including reporting confidence intervals, also constitutes a valuable contribution, particularly given the inherent variability in neural network performance. Finally, the unified framework and accompanying KeraSTS/PySTS software package enable easier reproducibility and comparison of results across different tasks, which benefits the research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset (yodaqa/large2470):** The introduction of this dataset is a substantial contribution. The authors clearly articulate the problems with existing datasets and provide a compelling justification for their new resource. The dataset's size and construction methodology appear sound.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:**  The emphasis on reporting confidence intervals alongside point estimates is commendable. This acknowledges and quantifies the inherent stochasticity of neural models, offering a more honest and informative assessment of performance. The comparison to strong, non-neural IR baselines further strengthens the evaluation.
3. **Unified Framework & Software:** Providing a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models, alongside the corresponding software libraries, facilitates reproducibility and accelerates future research. This is a practical and valuable contribution.
4. **Comprehensive Survey:** The paper provides a broad survey of relevant tasks, datasets, and models. This serves as a useful reference point for researchers entering the field.
5. **Demonstrated Transfer Learning:** The positive results from transfer learning experiments, showing that models trained on one task (Ubuntu Dialogue) can generalize to others, support the authors’ hypothesis about universal semantic comprehension models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the authors evaluate a diverse range of models, none of the proposed architectures represent a significant advancement in the state-of-the-art. The models are largely standard implementations (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) without novel architectural modifications or training techniques. The primary contribution lies in the systematic evaluation and framework, not the models themselves.
2. **Incomplete Results & Baselines:** Despite the comprehensive survey, the results presented in the paper are somewhat selective. The authors acknowledge that their models lag behind state-of-the-art performance on RTE and STS tasks without providing detailed results for those experiments. A more complete comparison, even if unfavorable, would strengthen the work.  Furthermore, while strong IR baselines *are* included, some potentially relevant neural baselines seem to be missing from the comparative analysis.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions hyperparameters were “tuned primarily on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset,” but provides limited details regarding the specific tuning procedure.  A more thorough description of the hyperparameter search space and optimization strategy would improve reproducibility.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper could benefit from ablation studies to better understand the contribution of different components (e.g., word overlap features, dropout rates) to the overall performance of the models.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details regarding the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and optimization strategy used?
2.  While you mention models lagging on RTE and STS, can you provide specific results (even preliminary) demonstrating this, and a comparison to current state-of-the-art models on those tasks?
3.  Were any attempts made to incorporate more recent neural architectures, such as Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), into the evaluation framework? If not, why?
4.  Regarding the dropout strategy, you mention applying it during training on some tasks but not others. What was the rationale behind this decision, and did you explore the impact of consistently applying dropout across all tasks?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and generally clear, although some sections are dense and could benefit from further explanation.
*   The reference list is extensive and appropriate.
*   The code availability is a major strength and should be highlighted in the final version of the paper.
*   The datasets, yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, should be made readily accessible to the research community along with detailed documentation.



Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of sentence pair scoring. While the proposed models themselves are not groundbreaking, the authors’ comprehensive evaluation, the introduction of a new and important dataset, and the development of a unified framework represent significant advancements. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
