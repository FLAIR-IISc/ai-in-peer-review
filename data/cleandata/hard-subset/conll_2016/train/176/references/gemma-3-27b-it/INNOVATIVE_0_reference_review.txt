This paper tackles the problem of sentence pair scoring, framing it as a broadly applicable `f2` function with potential for universal semantic comprehension. The core contribution is a unified framework for evaluating models across diverse tasks (answer selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment) and the introduction of new, larger datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop) intended to address shortcomings in existing benchmarks. A secondary, but promising, contribution is the demonstration of cross-task transfer learning, suggesting a path toward genuinely task-independent semantic models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold Vision & Unified Framework:** The paper isn’t incrementally improving a single task; it's proposing a fundamental shift in *how* we approach sentence pair modeling. The `f2` framing and the emphasis on universal comprehension are ambitious and, if successful, could significantly advance the field.  The framework provides a crucial structure for comparing different models fairly across tasks—something sorely lacking currently.
2. **Dataset Contribution:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 is a significant positive. Addressing the limitations of the existing Wang dataset (small size, uneven examples, noisy validation) is critical. A larger, more well-constructed dataset immediately raises the bar for future research. The wqmprop dataset also offers a novel angle.
3. **Focus on IR Baselines & Rigorous Evaluation:**  The inclusion of strong non-neural IR baselines and the emphasis on statistical significance (confidence intervals) elevate the evaluation beyond typical neural network papers.  Acknowledging and quantifying the randomness inherent in these models is commendable.
4. **Promising Transfer Learning Results:** While not conclusive, the demonstrated success of transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset is encouraging. It provides a preliminary validation of the core hypothesis – that models can learn transferable semantic representations.
5. **Open-Source Framework:** Releasing the dataset loaders and model building tools (KeraSTS, PySTS) as open source significantly increases the impact and reproducibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** The models themselves (RNN, CNN, attention) are largely standard. While they are implemented within the novel framework, there's no groundbreaking architectural innovation.  The paper leans heavily on established techniques.
2. **Incomplete Transfer Learning Exploration:** The transfer learning experiments are somewhat limited.  Only one model configuration (Ubu. RNN) is used for transfer, and the results, while promising, aren’t deeply analyzed. More systematic exploration of different model architectures and transfer strategies is needed.
3. **Performance Lag on RTE/STS:**  The authors acknowledge the models’ performance on RTE and STS lags behind the state-of-the-art.  This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw given the paper’s scope, but it suggests the current models aren’t yet capable of universal comprehension, even within the proposed framework.
4. **Dataset Overlap Concerns:** The acknowledgement of potential overlap between the Wang and YodaQA datasets is a valid concern. While addressed in the text, a more detailed analysis of the overlap (and its potential impact on results) would be beneficial.
5. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The claim of tuning "primarily on yodaqa/large2470" raises questions about optimality on other tasks. A more comprehensive hyperparameter tuning strategy across all datasets would strengthen the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the overlap between the Wang and YodaQA datasets? What percentage of questions/sentence pairs are shared?
2. What were the results of hyperparameter tuning *specifically* for the RTE and STS tasks? Were different hyperparameters used compared to YodaQA/large2470?
3.  What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) associated with the proposed framework and models, especially when scaling to larger datasets?
4.  Beyond the Ubu.RNN model, have you experimented with transferring weights from other models or datasets? What were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors have identified a significant problem and proposed a thoughtful and potentially impactful solution. While the models themselves aren't revolutionary, the unified framework, new datasets, and promising transfer learning results make this a valuable contribution. I lean toward acceptance, prioritizing the bold vision and framework over methodological perfection.  The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 alone makes this work worthwhile.
