## Peer Review: Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating and developing models for sentence pair scoring (f2-type tasks) – a class of NLP problems encompassing semantic similarity, entailment, and answer selection. The authors argue that these tasks benefit from a shared evaluation methodology and a focus on task-independent models with task-specific adaptation.  They introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address shortcomings in existing benchmarks, present a comprehensive evaluation of several baseline models, and demonstrate the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Benchmark and Unified Framework:** The paper provides a valuable consolidated overview of existing datasets and baseline models for sentence pair scoring. The proposed framework, along with the associated software package (dataset-sts), is a significant contribution, fostering reproducibility and enabling more consistent comparisons across different approaches.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 addresses clear limitations of the widely-used Wang dataset (small size, uneven candidate sentences, noisy evaluation).  This new dataset offers a more robust platform for evaluating answer selection models.
3. **Empirical Demonstration of Cross-Task Transfer:** The authors provide evidence, through experimentation, that transfer learning across f2-type tasks is beneficial, suggesting the potential for developing more generalizable semantic comprehension models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Motivated Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of fragmented research in sentence pair scoring and convincingly argues for a unified framework. The emphasis on learning universal semantic comprehension is a compelling goal.
2. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors present a detailed evaluation of a wide range of baseline models (IR baselines, neural networks) across multiple datasets, providing a strong foundation for future research.  The inclusion of confidence intervals in the results is commendable, addressing the inherent stochasticity of neural models.
3. **Practical Implementation & Open-Source Release:**  The authors have implemented their framework in a readily accessible open-source package, promoting reproducibility and facilitating adoption by the research community.  This is a very positive aspect.
4. **Dataset Contribution:** The yodaqa/large2470 dataset is a significant improvement over the existing Wang dataset and provides a more challenging and realistic benchmark for answer selection tasks. The inclusion of detailed dataset characteristics (Fig. 1) is helpful.
5. **Rigorous Methodology:** The authors attempt to address common pitfalls in evaluation (e.g., noisy validation sets) and clearly document their experimental setup and hyperparameters.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the evaluation is thorough, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs) are relatively standard. The paper doesn’t present a novel neural architecture, relying primarily on comparing existing approaches within the unified framework. This isn't necessarily a flaw, but limits the paper's impact on model development itself.
2. **Transfer Learning Results are Moderate:** The performance gains from transfer learning, while statistically significant, are not dramatic. It would be helpful to analyze *which* tasks benefit most from transfer from specific source tasks, providing insights into shared semantic features. The limited exploration of different transfer learning strategies (e.g., freezing layers) feels somewhat incomplete.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from more detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of specific components (e.g., BM25 features, different scoring modules) to the overall performance.  This would provide more concrete guidance for future model design.
4. **Discussion of STS/RTE Results Limited:**  The authors acknowledge that their models lag behind the state-of-the-art on STS and RTE tasks. A more detailed discussion of *why* this is the case, and potential areas for improvement, would be valuable.
5. **Dataset Overlap Concerns:** The authors acknowledge potential overlap between the Wang and yodaqa datasets.  While they advise against joint training/evaluation, a more thorough analysis of the extent of this overlap would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of semantic relationships that are most easily transferred between tasks? For example, does pre-training on the Next Utterance Ranking task improve performance on Answer Sentence Selection more than vice versa?
2.  Regarding the dataset overlap between Wang and yodaqa, can you quantify the amount of overlap (e.g., percentage of identical question-answer pairs)?  
3.  You mention that models often overfit during training.  Have you explored more sophisticated regularization techniques (e.g., variational dropout, early stopping criteria based on validation loss) to mitigate this issue?
4. Could you elaborate on why the Ubu.RNN model performed particularly well in the transfer learning experiments? What characteristics of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset contribute to its effectiveness as a source task?
5. What are the computational resources (e.g., GPU memory, training time) required to reproduce the results presented in this paper?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The use of figures and tables is effective in presenting the results.
*   The inclusion of a comprehensive list of references is appreciated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of sentence pair scoring. The unified framework, new datasets, and thorough evaluation provide a strong foundation for future research. While the models themselves are not particularly novel, the paper's overall contribution lies in its systematic approach and practical tools.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
