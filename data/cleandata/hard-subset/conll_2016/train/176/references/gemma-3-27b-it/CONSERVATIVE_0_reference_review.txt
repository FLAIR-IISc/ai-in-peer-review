This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating semantic similarity between sentence pairs (f2-type tasks) across multiple datasets and tasks, aiming to identify universally applicable models. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations in existing benchmarks and demonstrate transfer learning as a means to achieve this goal. 

The primary contribution is the creation and release of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset, addressing deficiencies in the widely used wang dataset for answer selection. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive benchmarking of various neural and non-neural models across a range of f2 tasks. Finally, the demonstration of beneficial cross-task transfer learning, specifically from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, is a positive, although preliminary, finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Contribution:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 is a significant positive. The authors convincingly argue for the shortcomings of the existing wang dataset and provide a larger, more realistically complex alternative. This will be valuable for the research community, providing a more robust testbed for evaluating models.
2. **Comprehensive Benchmarking:** The paper provides a broad overview of existing baselines – both traditional IR methods and neural models – across several tasks. While not exhaustive, it creates a useful starting point for comparing future work. The inclusion of statistical significance analysis (confidence intervals) is commendable and sets a higher standard for reporting results in this area.
3. **Emphasis on Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors rightly point out the issues with inconsistent reporting and lack of statistical rigor in previous work. Their effort to establish a standardized evaluation methodology is a valuable step towards more reliable research in semantic similarity.
4. **Open-Source Framework:** The release of the `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` packages promotes reproducibility and encourages further research by providing accessible tools for experimenting with different models and datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Models:** The models investigated are largely established architectures (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms). While the application to this unified framework is valuable, the paper doesn’t present any genuinely novel modeling approaches. The focus on *applying* existing models, rather than *developing* new ones, limits the overall impact. The claim of pursuing “universal semantic comprehension models” feels overstated given the reliance on existing architectures.
2. **Limited Transfer Learning Results:** The transfer learning experiment, while showing positive results, is somewhat limited in scope. The focus on a single source dataset (Ubuntu Dialogue) and a single configuration (RNN with dot-product scorer) doesn’t provide a comprehensive understanding of the conditions under which transfer learning is most effective. The reasons *why* transfer from Ubuntu Dialogue works well are not explored.
3. **Performance Gaps:** The paper acknowledges that their models lag behind state-of-the-art performance on several tasks (RTE and STS). This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but it raises questions about the effectiveness of the chosen models and evaluation framework. While the new dataset is valuable, the models themselves don’t demonstrate significant advancements.
4. **Overly Broad Scope:** The attempt to unify *all* f2-type tasks feels ambitious and potentially unfocused. The diversity of these tasks may make it difficult to identify truly universal models. A more targeted approach, focusing on a smaller set of related tasks, might have yielded more conclusive results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of why the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset proves to be a particularly effective source for transfer learning? What characteristics of this dataset might contribute to its success?
2.  You mention that the validation set performance is not always a good predictor of test set performance. What alternative strategies are you considering for model selection and hyperparameter tuning?
3.  Regarding the performance gaps on RTE and STS, what specific modifications to the models or training procedures are planned to address these shortcomings?
4.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of your software framework and potential areas for future development?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and organized. The figures and tables are clear and informative. The extensive referencing is appreciated. However, the overall impression is one of solid engineering and evaluation, rather than groundbreaking innovation. The paper provides a valuable contribution to the community by creating a new dataset and establishing a standardized evaluation framework, but the lack of novel modeling approaches limits its impact.
