## Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating models on various sentence pair scoring (f2) tasks, arguing for the pursuit of task-independent semantic comprehension models. The authors survey existing tasks and datasets, establish baselines (both neural and non-neural), introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations of existing ones, and demonstrate the benefits of transfer learning across these tasks. The work also releases an open-source framework (dataset-sts) to facilitate research in this area.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **The introduction of two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) addressing shortcomings in existing benchmarks for sentence pair scoring.** These datasets appear thoughtfully constructed and provide a more realistic and challenging evaluation environment, particularly for answer selection tasks.
2. **A comprehensive and well-structured survey of f2 tasks, datasets, and models.** This provides a valuable resource for researchers entering this field and helps to standardize evaluation practices.
3. **The release of the dataset-sts framework.** This open-source toolkit streamlines experimentation and promotes reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Evaluation Methodology:** The authors are commendably cautious about the reliability of single-run evaluations, explicitly acknowledging the variability in neural model performance and reporting confidence intervals.  This is a critical point that is often overlooked in NLP research.  The comparison with strong IR baselines (BM25) is also a strong point, ensuring neural models are truly adding value.
2. **Practical Focus and Problem Identification:**  The paper clearly articulates the challenges in the current fragmented landscape of sentence pair scoring research. The criticisms of the existing Wang dataset are well-supported and justify the introduction of the new datasets.
3. **Comprehensive Baseline Comparison:**  The authors thoughtfully explore a range of baseline models, from simple word overlap methods to more sophisticated recurrent and convolutional networks. This provides a solid foundation for evaluating the performance of future models.
4. **Demonstrated Transfer Learning Benefits:** The results showing successful transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset to other tasks support the authors’ central hypothesis of universal semantic comprehension.
5. **Open-Source Contribution:** Releasing the dataset-sts framework is a significant contribution that will accelerate research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Model Architectures:** While the framework and evaluation are strong, the neural models themselves (RNNs, CNNs) are relatively standard. The paper doesn’t present a novel architecture achieving state-of-the-art results; it primarily focuses on establishing a common ground for *comparing* models.  The attention mechanism is mentioned, but its implementation seems to draw heavily from existing work without significant innovation.
2. **Incomplete Results Presentation:** The paper states that models lag behind SOTA on RTE and STS but doesn't provide those results.  Including these (even if negative) would strengthen the paper by offering a complete view of the models' capabilities. It's difficult to assess the full impact of the framework without seeing how it performs across all considered tasks.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:**  The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but the details are somewhat sparse.  A more thorough description of the tuning process (e.g., search space, optimization strategy) would increase the reproducibility of the results.
4. **Missing Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates the *what* (transfer learning works), it lacks a deeper analysis of *why*. Error analysis, identifying common failure cases for different models and tasks, could provide valuable insights for future research.  



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide the results for the RTE and STS tasks, even if they are not state-of-the-art? This would give a more complete picture of the model’s performance.
2.  Can you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what search space was used, and what optimization strategy was employed?
3.  The paper mentions that training can be unstable. What regularization techniques (beyond L2) were explored, and did they have a significant impact on performance?
4.  Do you have any insights into *why* transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset is so effective? What characteristics of that dataset might make it particularly suitable for transfer?
5.  Regarding the overlap features, was any ablation study conducted to measure their relative contribution to the overall performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sections (e.g., the description of the models) could benefit from more detailed explanations.
*   The use of a GitHub URL redacted in the paper should be corrected in the final version.
*  Consider providing more information about the computational resources required to reproduce the experiments (e.g., GPU type, training time).




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of sentence pair scoring. While the model architectures themselves aren't groundbreaking, the comprehensive evaluation, the introduction of new datasets, and the release of the dataset-sts framework represent significant advancements. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The paper's emphasis on rigorous evaluation and reproducibility is particularly commendable and will be beneficial to the research community.
