This paper presents a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across multiple tasks – Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, and Recognizing Textual Entailment/Semantic Similarity. The authors introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations in existing benchmarks, and demonstrate the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning.

The most significant contribution is the creation and release of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset. Existing datasets in this space are demonstrably flawed, and a large, well-constructed dataset is a valuable resource for the community. The comprehensive evaluation across multiple tasks *within a single framework* is also a notable contribution, providing a much-needed common ground for comparing different approaches. While the transfer learning results aren’t groundbreaking, the demonstration that even simple models benefit from it supports the core hypothesis of universal semantic comprehension.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The yodaqa/large2470 dataset is a substantial contribution. The authors clearly articulate the shortcomings of existing benchmarks (small size, uneven distribution, unreliable validation splits) and offer a compelling solution. This directly addresses a bottleneck in the field.
2. **Unified Evaluation Framework:** The attempt to unify evaluation across disparate tasks is commendable. The framework itself (dataset-sts and KeraSTS) is a practical deliverable that will benefit other researchers.  The focus on reporting confidence intervals, rather than single point estimates, is a strength, acknowledging the inherent stochasticity of neural models.
3. **Bold Hypothesis:** The central idea – pursuing universal sentence pair scoring models – is a bold and potentially impactful direction. While not fully demonstrated, the paper provides initial evidence suggesting its feasibility. The focus on f2 functions as a general semantic understanding proxy is a compelling framing.
4. **Strong Baselines & IR Focus:**  Including strong IR baselines (BM25) and acknowledging the importance of word overlap features is a pragmatic and sensible approach.  The authors aren't solely focused on neural network complexity; they ground their evaluation in established IR techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** The neural models themselves (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) are not novel. The paper largely applies existing architectures to the unified framework. The exploration of model architectures feels somewhat cursory.
2. **Transfer Learning Results are Incremental:** While transfer learning *works*, the improvements aren’t dramatic. The claim of demonstrating "powerful" seeding feels overstated. More extensive experimentation with different transfer strategies (e.g., freezing layers, fine-tuning rates) would strengthen this aspect.
3. **Statistical Significance Concerns:** Although the authors report confidence intervals, the overall scale of experimentation and the number of models evaluated raises concerns about multiple comparison issues. A more rigorous statistical analysis (e.g., correcting for multiple hypotheses) would be beneficial.
4. **Dataset Overlap:** The acknowledgement of potential overlap between *wang* and *yodaqa* is concerning. This could inflate performance results and make comparisons less reliable. A clearer statement about the degree of overlap and its potential impact is needed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you quantify the overlap between the *wang* and *yodaqa* datasets more precisely (e.g., percentage of shared questions, sentence pairs)?
2.  What is the rationale behind choosing dot-product or MLP scorers? Have you explored other scoring mechanisms, and if so, how did they perform?
3. You mention that the STS and Paraphrasing tasks lag behind state-of-the-art. Could you briefly outline the primary challenges encountered when applying your framework to these tasks?
4.  What specific considerations were made when choosing the YodaQA system for generating candidate sentences for the *yodaqa/large2470* dataset? How does the quality of YodaQA’s retrieval influence the quality of the labeled dataset?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and well-organized. The paper benefits from a clear structure and a strong focus on reproducibility (through the release of code and datasets). The acknowledgement of limitations is appreciated.



**Overall:**

Despite some limitations in the novelty of the models, I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. The creation of a high-quality dataset and the unified evaluation framework are significant achievements. The authors have identified an important research direction and provided a solid foundation for future work. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions to address the statistical significance concerns and provide more detail on the dataset overlap. The boldness of the core hypothesis and the practical value of the delivered resources outweigh the incremental nature of the model results.
