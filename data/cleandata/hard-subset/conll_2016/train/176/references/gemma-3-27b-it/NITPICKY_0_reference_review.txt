## Review

This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating semantic sentence pair scoring models (f2-type tasks). The authors identify several existing tasks, introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and perform an extensive empirical evaluation of various neural and non-neural baselines. The core argument is that f2 tasks benefit from training models to understand semantic relationships, leveraging the second sentence as a complex label. 

The most substantial contribution is the introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset for answer sentence selection. While the Wang dataset is a common benchmark, the authors convincingly demonstrate its limitations and present a larger, more reliable alternative. The effort in creating this dataset is commendable. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive empirical evaluation, providing a standardized comparison across multiple tasks and models, alongside thorough statistical analysis (confidence intervals). The unified framework itself, implemented in the `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` packages, is a useful resource for the community, albeit reliant on Keras.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Contribution:** The yodaqa/large2470 dataset is a significant addition to the field. The identified shortcomings of the Wang dataset, coupled with the scale and generation process of the new dataset, make it a valuable resource for more robust evaluation of answer selection models.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation Methodology:** The inclusion of confidence intervals and the explicit acknowledgement of the inherent randomness in neural network performance are commendable. This demonstrates a level of scientific rigor often absent in NLP publications. The comparison against strong IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) provides a useful context for assessing neural model performance.
3. **Comprehensive Baseline Coverage:** The paper covers a wide range of baseline models, including both traditional IR methods and various neural network architectures (RNNs, CNNs, attention-based models). This provides a solid foundation for comparison and future research.
4. **Framework Availability:** The release of the `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` packages promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research by providing a standardized platform for experimentation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Models:** The neural network architectures employed (RNNs, CNNs, attention) are well-established and lack significant novelty. While a comprehensive evaluation is valuable, the paper doesn't introduce any fundamentally new model components or training techniques. The transfer learning experiments, while promising, are limited in scope and lack detailed analysis of *why* the transfer is effective.
2. **Overly Stringent Experimental Setup & Hyperparameter Tuning:** The authors state they tuned hyperparameters primarily on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset. While this is reasonable, the specific choices (e.g., using BM25 as input to the MLP scorer, pruning based on BM25 score) feel somewhat arbitrary and could be overly specific to that dataset.  The reliance on a single dataset for primary tuning raises concerns about generalization.
3. **Presentation and Clarity Issues:** The writing is occasionally dense and repetitive. The introduction is overly verbose and could be streamlined. The numerous citations within the text disrupt the flow of reading. Some figures (e.g. Fig. 1) lack sufficient detail or context to be fully understood. The footnote regarding the wang dataset’s overlap with yodaqa is important but buried.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the results are presented comprehensively, there is a noticeable lack of detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* models fail and *why* would provide valuable insights for future research. For instance, characterizing the types of questions or sentence pairs that pose the greatest challenge would be helpful.
5. **Keras Dependency:** Building the framework on top of Keras introduces a dependency that may limit its adoption by researchers who prefer other deep learning frameworks (e.g., PyTorch, TensorFlow).

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for the specific choices made during hyperparameter tuning, particularly the integration of BM25 scores?
2. What specific types of errors does the Ubu.RNN model *avoid* when transferred to other tasks, compared to training a new RNN model from scratch? Could you provide examples?
3. You mention the possibility of jointly training a model on multiple tasks. Have you explored this avenue, and if so, what were the results?
4.  The overlap between wang and yodaqa is mentioned. Could you quantify this overlap more precisely (e.g., percentage of shared questions, sentence pairs)?
5.  Regarding the choice of dot-product versus MLP scorer - could you elaborate on the experiments conducted to determine which performed better on different datasets, and provide a more systematic analysis of their strengths and weaknesses?



**Additional Comments:**

* The URL for the github repository was redacted in the paper but should be provided to reviewers.
* The use of abbreviations (e.g., RNN, CNN) without first defining them in the text is unhelpful.
* The statement “Sadly, we typically observe heavy overfitting…and rarely use a model from later than a couple of epochs” suggests a potential issue with the model architectures or training procedures.  Further investigation is warranted.



**Overall Assessment:**

While this paper presents a valuable dataset and a comprehensive evaluation framework, the lack of novelty in the models and several presentation issues prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The stringent attention to detail, while admirable, borders on excessive and overshadows the core contributions. I recommend **rejection** but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by adding more detailed error analysis and clarifying the experimental setup.
