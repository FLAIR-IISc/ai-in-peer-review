This paper presents a valuable effort to unify research on sentence pair scoring (f2 tasks) and establish a more rigorous evaluation framework. The primary contribution is the *framing* of a diverse set of NLP tasks—answer selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment—under a single, conceptually clean paradigm. This isn't a novel *method*, but a novel *perspective* that has the potential to significantly streamline future research. The introduction of the `yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop` datasets, designed to address shortcomings of existing benchmarks, is a strong supporting contribution, as is the `dataset-sts` framework for standardized evaluation. Finally, the empirical demonstration of cross-task transfer learning, even with relatively simple models, is encouraging, hinting at the feasibility of universal semantic representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold Conceptualization:** The paper’s central idea – unifying f2 tasks – is ambitious and impactful. The current fragmented landscape hinders progress; this work offers a compelling path towards consolidation. Prioritizing a unified framework over incremental improvements to individual tasks is a commendable stance.
2. **Dataset Contribution:** The new datasets, particularly `yodaqa/large2470`, are a significant asset to the community. Addressing the issues of small size and uneven data distribution in existing benchmarks is crucial for reliable model evaluation. The effort to create these datasets deserves strong recognition.
3. **Practical Framework:** The release of the `dataset-sts` framework lowers the barrier to entry for researchers working in this area. A standardized toolkit promotes reproducibility and facilitates fair comparisons.
4. **Positive Transfer Learning Results:** While not earth-shattering, the demonstration that transfer learning works even with simple models suggests a worthwhile direction for future research. It reinforces the idea that these tasks share underlying semantic structure.
5. **Rigorous Evaluation Methodology:** The emphasis on reporting confidence intervals and comparing against strong IR baselines (BM25) elevates the rigor of the evaluation. Recognizing and addressing the randomness inherent in neural network training is excellent practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Limitations:** The models themselves are largely standard. While thoroughly evaluated, they don't represent a novel architectural breakthrough. The paper focuses more on the *evaluation* of existing methods within the new framework than on developing entirely new approaches. This isn't necessarily a flaw, given the paper's stated goals, but it limits its potential impact on model development.
2. **Limited Depth of Analysis:** The analysis of transfer learning results is relatively superficial. While the authors show *that* transfer works, they don’t delve deeply into *why* certain tasks are more easily transferred from than others, or which model architectures benefit most.
3. **Dataset Overlap Concern:** The acknowledgement of potential overlap between `wang` and `yodaqa` datasets is worrying. This overlap, even if partial, could artificially inflate performance and compromise the validity of comparisons. A more thorough analysis of the degree of overlap is needed.
4. **Heavy Reliance on GloVe:** The exclusive use of GloVe embeddings might limit the ability to capture more nuanced semantic relationships. Exploring other embedding strategies (e.g., BERT, ELMo) could yield better results, though this would necessitate significant re-evaluation.
5. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a qualitative error analysis to better understand the types of errors the models are making. This would provide valuable insights for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you quantify the overlap between the `wang` and `yodaqa` datasets? Specifically, what percentage of question-answer pairs are shared between the two datasets?
2. Do you have plans to expand the `dataset-sts` framework to include other f2 tasks, such as paraphrase detection or semantic similarity scoring (outside of RTE/STS)?
3. Could you elaborate on the reasons for choosing Adam optimization and a specific learning rate annealing schedule? Was this choice data-dependent, or based on prior experience?
4. What are your thoughts on incorporating attention mechanisms into the simpler models (e.g., DAN, avg) to potentially improve performance?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The extensive references are appreciated. While the methodological aspects may not be groundbreaking, the conceptual contribution and practical tools provided are significant. Given the paper's bold approach and valuable contributions to the field, I lean towards acceptance. The weakness regarding potential dataset overlap is the most concerning, but can hopefully be addressed by the authors.
