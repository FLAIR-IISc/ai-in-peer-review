This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating semantic similarity between sentence pairs (f2-type tasks), aiming to identify models capable of “universal semantic comprehension.” The authors introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address shortcomings in existing benchmarks and demonstrate transfer learning across tasks. The primary contribution, as presented, is the framework itself and the accompanying datasets. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of cross-task transfer learning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing Dataset Limitations:** The identified problems with existing datasets like wang (small size, uneven candidate sets, noisy validation/test splits) are valid and well-articulated. Introducing yodaqa/large2470 is a significant undertaking and provides a more robust benchmark, particularly for answer sentence selection.  The acknowledgement of dataset lineage (potential overlap between wang and yodaqa) is responsible.
2. **Comprehensive Baseline Comparison:** The extensive comparison against both IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) and a range of neural network architectures (from simple averaging to attention mechanisms) is commendable.  Anchoring results against established methods provides a useful point of reference, even if the neural performance isn’t state-of-the-art *yet*.
3. **Systematic Framework:** The authors’ effort to unify evaluation under a single framework (dataset-sts, KeraSTS) is a positive step toward reproducibility and standardized comparison. Open-sourcing the framework enhances the potential impact of this work.
4. **Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** The initial results showing the benefit of transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset (Ubu. RNN) are promising, supporting the hypothesis that a more general semantic representation can be learned.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Models:** While the framework and datasets are valuable, the *models* themselves are largely based on established architectures. The authors don't present a novel neural network architecture or training methodology. Simply applying existing models within a unified framework, even with transfer learning, doesn’t constitute a significant theoretical advance. The reliance on established models reduces the overall impact.
2. **Insufficient Performance Gains:** Despite the thorough evaluation, the reported performance improvements on many tasks are modest. The authors acknowledge lagging behind state-of-the-art on RTE and STS, and even on the new yodaqa dataset, the gains over baselines aren’t dramatic enough to justify the considerable effort invested.  The paper would be stronger if it demonstrated a more substantial leap in performance on at least one task.
3. **Overemphasis on Framework, Underemphasis on Analysis:**  The paper spends a considerable amount of space describing the framework itself, but less time on in-depth analysis of *why* certain models perform better on specific tasks or the nature of the learned representations. A deeper understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of different architectures across various f2-type problems would have been more insightful.
4. **Statistical Significance Concerns:** The acknowledgement of high variance in model performance (e.g., MRR standard deviation on wang) is concerning. While reporting confidence intervals is a good practice, the large deviations suggest instability and potentially limit the reliability of the conclusions. The reliance on averages obscures the variability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study demonstrating the impact of individual components within the models (e.g., the effect of dropout, different activation functions, the MLP scorer vs. dot product)?
2.  The paper mentions potential overlap between the wang and yodaqa datasets. How were efforts made to mitigate the impact of this overlap on the transfer learning results?
3.  What are the computational costs (training time, memory usage) associated with the different models and datasets? This information is crucial for assessing the practicality of the proposed framework.
4. The paper reports statistical significance as 95% confidence intervals.  Considering the observed standard deviation, how many runs were performed to achieve this confidence level, and do you believe this number of runs is sufficient given the observed variance?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper’s organization is logical and the writing is generally clear. The referencing is extensive. However, the formatting of the citation list is slightly inconsistent.  I recommend investigating strategies for reducing the variance in model performance, perhaps through more robust regularization techniques or ensemble methods.
