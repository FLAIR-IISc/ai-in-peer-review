This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating semantic similarity between sentence pairs (f2-type tasks), arguing for the potential of task-independent models fine-tuned for specific applications. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address perceived shortcomings in existing benchmarks and perform extensive experiments with various neural network architectures.

The primary contribution is a comprehensive and well-organized evaluation framework for sentence pair scoring tasks. The introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset is a valuable addition, offering a larger and arguably more realistic testbed than the commonly used wang dataset. The thorough comparison against strong, non-neural IR baselines is commendable and highlights the continued relevance of these methods. The effort to provide a standardized platform (dataset-sts, KeraSTS) for researchers in this area is also a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Evaluation & Baseline Comparison:** The paper’s strength lies in its meticulous evaluation methodology. The insistence on comparing against strong IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) is crucial. Often, neural models are evaluated in isolation, making it difficult to assess true progress. The inclusion of confidence intervals demonstrates a responsible approach to reporting results, acknowledging inherent randomness in neural network training.
2. **Dataset Contribution:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 addresses genuine issues with existing datasets (small size, uneven distribution, unreliable validation splits) and provides a much-needed resource for the community.  The detailed explanation of the dataset creation process adds to its credibility.
3. **Comprehensive Model Suite:** The authors explore a broad range of neural architectures – RNNs, CNNs, and their combinations – providing a solid foundation for future research. This allows for a nuanced understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches across various tasks.
4. **Focus on Reproducibility:** The release of the dataset-sts and KeraSTS frameworks promotes reproducibility, a vital aspect of scientific progress.  Open-sourcing the code and datasets is highly appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the framework and datasets are valuable, the core neural network architectures investigated (RNNs, CNNs) are well-established. The paper doesn't present a fundamentally new modeling approach. The argument for "universal semantic comprehension" feels speculative and isn't convincingly demonstrated, despite the transfer learning experiments. Showing competitive *relative* improvement over strong baselines is not equivalent to demonstrating a new principle.
2. **Transfer Learning Results are Modest:** The reported gains from transfer learning, while positive, are not substantial enough to strongly support the claim of learning “universal semantic comprehension.” The transfer is performed from a specific dataset (Ubuntu Dialogue) and its applicability to *all* f2-type tasks remains questionable. The parameters chosen for transfer learning appear somewhat arbitrary and are not rigorously justified.
3. **Overstated Claims and Lack of Critical Self-Assessment:** The paper frequently uses strong language (e.g., “establishes the watermark,” “demonstrate the feasibility”) that isn't fully warranted by the experimental results. The authors should acknowledge the limitations of their findings more explicitly. For example, the performance on RTE and STS tasks is admitted to be lagging, yet it's presented almost as an aside.
4. **Dataset-Specific Tuning:**  The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters primarily on yodaqa/large2470.  It's unclear whether this tuning generalizes well to other datasets, potentially skewing the comparative results. More cross-validation and ablation studies would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study on the impact of the added features (bigram overlap, unigram overlap, capitalization, numerical flags) in the Answer Sentence Selection tasks? How much performance gain is attributable to these features specifically?
2.  The transfer learning results are presented with limited detail. What specific layers were frozen or fine-tuned during the transfer learning process? What was the learning rate schedule used?
3.  While you acknowledge the limitations of the RTE and STS results, can you provide a more in-depth analysis of why the proposed models perform poorly on these tasks? Are there specific characteristics of these datasets that pose challenges to the chosen architectures?
4.  You mention that the validation set performance is not always a reliable indicator of test set performance, especially on the wang dataset. Given this observation, why was the validation set used for hyperparameter tuning?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are extensive, which is good. However, a few entries are incomplete (e.g., "Agirre et al.,? \Q2015\E"). Please ensure all references are fully formatted. The paper is generally well-written and organized, but the language could be more cautious and less hyperbolic.
