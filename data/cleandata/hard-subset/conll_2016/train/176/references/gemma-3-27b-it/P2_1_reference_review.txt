## Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models (f2-type tasks) across a range of semantic similarity tasks including answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, and recognizing textual entailment. The authors identify the fragmented nature of research in this area, and aim to address this by providing a common evaluation platform (dataset-sts and KeraSTS), introducing new datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop), advocating for more rigorous evaluation methodology (including statistical significance testing), and demonstrating the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Creation and Release of New Datasets:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 is the strongest contribution. Addressing the limitations of the existing Wang dataset (small size, uneven candidate sentences) with a larger, potentially more representative dataset is valuable to the community.
2. **Unified Evaluation Framework:**  Developing dataset-sts and KeraSTS, while perhaps not a groundbreaking architectural contribution, provides a useful, open-source toolkit for researchers working in this area, promoting standardization and comparability of results.
3. **Emphasis on Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors rightly point out the issues with reporting single-run results in the field and advocate for reporting confidence intervals. This push for statistical rigor is a welcome addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Real Problem:** The paper correctly identifies the lack of a unified framework and standardized evaluation in sentence pair scoring research. The fragmented nature of the field hinders progress, and this work attempts to bridge that gap.
2. **Practical Contributions:** The new datasets and software tools (dataset-sts, KeraSTS) are tangible contributions that other researchers can directly benefit from.  The yodaqa/large2470 dataset, in particular, has the potential to become a standard benchmark.
3. **Sound Methodology:** The emphasis on statistical significance (confidence intervals) is crucial for reliable comparison of models and is a strong methodological point. The comparison to IR baselines is also a valuable addition, providing a solid grounding for assessing neural model performance.
4. **Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** The initial results showing the effectiveness of transfer learning (using a model trained on Ubuntu Dialogue) provide promising evidence for the feasibility of universal semantic comprehension models.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand their contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** The neural network architectures investigated (RNNs, CNNs, attention) are fairly standard and lack significant novelty.  While the focus is on the *framework* for evaluation, the models themselves don’t push the state-of-the-art in terms of architectural innovation. The transfer learning demonstration is also preliminary and doesn’t represent a significant architectural advance.
2. **Performance Lags Behind SOTA:** The paper acknowledges that performance on RTE and STS lags behind the state-of-the-art.  While the authors focus on establishing a baseline, a stronger showing on these established tasks would strengthen the overall impact.
3. **Dataset Characteristics Not Fully Explored:** While the authors motivate the new datasets well, a more in-depth analysis of their characteristics (e.g., error analysis, difficulty levels, types of semantic relationships) would be beneficial.  Specifically, a better discussion of potential biases in the new datasets would be valuable.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions that hyperparameters were primarily tuned on the YodaQA dataset.  It’s unclear how well these parameters generalize to other tasks, and a more comprehensive hyperparameter search across all tasks would be beneficial.
5. **Limited Ablation Studies:** More extensive ablation studies exploring the impact of different components (e.g., BM25 integration, dropout rates) would help to understand the effectiveness of the proposed approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more details on the creation process of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset, specifically addressing potential biases introduced by the YodaQA system used for candidate sentence retrieval?
2.  You mention the importance of statistical significance. Could you provide the raw results (all runs) for the reported results, allowing for a more thorough statistical analysis by reviewers?
3.  The transfer learning results are promising. What specific architectural choices (e.g., dimensionality, scorer type) were found to be most effective for transfer learning, and why?
4.  Were there any notable differences in the types of errors made by the models on the different datasets?  If so, could you provide examples?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The URL for the software package was redacted, which hinders reproducibility. Please provide the link in the author response.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of related work on transfer learning in NLP, particularly methods for domain adaptation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of sentence pair scoring. While the proposed models are not particularly novel, the creation of new datasets, the development of a unified evaluation framework, and the emphasis on rigorous evaluation methodology are all significant strengths. The paper is well-written and provides a clear roadmap for future research in this area.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
