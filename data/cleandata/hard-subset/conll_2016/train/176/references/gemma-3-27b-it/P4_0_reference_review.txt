This paper presents a framework for evaluating and comparing models on a variety of sentence pair scoring (f2) tasks. The authors aim to unify research in this area, improve evaluation methodologies, and demonstrate the feasibility of universal semantic comprehension models. The primary contribution lies in the creation and release of two new datasets, `yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop`, designed to address shortcomings of existing datasets like `wang`. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive evaluation of multiple baseline models (IR baselines and neural networks) across several f2 tasks, providing a standardized benchmark. A tertiary, though less compelling, contribution is the demonstration of cross-task transfer learning, showing improvements can be achieved by pre-training on one task and fine-tuning on another.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Datasets:** The introduction of `yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop` is a significant strength. The authors identify and articulate specific weaknesses in the commonly used `wang` dataset (small size, uneven candidates, noisy validation/test splits) and provide datasets that attempt to address these issues. The data generation process, leveraging the YodaQA system and Freebase, seems reasonable, and the larger scale of `yodaqa/large2470` is particularly valuable for training more robust models.
2. **Unified Evaluation Framework:** The authors’ effort to establish a unified framework, along with the `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` software packages, is commendable. This framework facilitates fair comparison of models across different tasks, a key challenge in the field. The emphasis on statistical rigor, reporting confidence intervals, addresses a significant weakness of many published NLP results.
3. **Comprehensive Baseline Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation of a wide range of baseline models, including both traditional IR methods (TF-IDF, BM25) and several neural network architectures (RNNs, CNNs). This detailed comparison establishes a strong baseline for future research and provides valuable insights into the relative strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.
4. **Focus on Statistical Significance:** The inclusion of 95% confidence intervals demonstrates a commitment to rigorous evaluation and encourages researchers to focus on meaningful performance differences. This is a departure from many publications that report only point estimates, and it adds significant credibility to the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Models:** While the evaluation is comprehensive, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) are not particularly novel. The paper doesn’t introduce any new architectural innovations, relying instead on established techniques. The claim of demonstrating “universal semantic comprehension” feels overstated given the use of standard models.
2. **Transfer Learning Results are Modest:** The reported gains from transfer learning, while positive, are not dramatic. The authors primarily show that pre-training on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset improves performance on other tasks, but the magnitude of the improvement doesn't fully justify the claim of demonstrating the "feasibility" of universal models. Further investigation into optimal transfer learning strategies and model architectures is needed.
3. **Lack of Detailed Analysis on New Datasets:** While the authors highlight the issues with existing datasets and introduce new ones, there isn’t an in-depth error analysis of the new datasets. Understanding the types of errors models make on `yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop` could provide valuable insights for future model development. Simply stating that baselines perform lower isn’t sufficient; more nuanced analysis is needed.
4. **Overly Broad Scope and Limited Depth:** The paper attempts to cover a very wide range of tasks and models, leading to a lack of depth in certain areas. For example, the discussion of different attention mechanisms is brief, and the ablation studies are limited. Focusing on a smaller subset of tasks and models could have allowed for more detailed analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the `yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop` datasets? Specifically, what types of errors are the baseline models making on these datasets?
2. The paper mentions that training runs can vary significantly. What is the variance in performance *within* a single task (e.g., across different random seeds)? Is the 95% confidence interval sufficient to account for this variance, particularly for smaller datasets?
3. What steps have been taken to ensure the quality and consistency of the labels in the `yodaqa/large2470` dataset, given its reliance on the YodaQA system for candidate sentence retrieval and Mechanical Turk for labeling?
4. Given the modest improvements observed with transfer learning, what specific hyperparameters were used during the fine-tuning process? Was any hyperparameter optimization performed on the target tasks?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more concise introduction.
* The formatting of the tables could be improved for readability.
* Providing links to the datasets and code repositories in the main text would be helpful.
