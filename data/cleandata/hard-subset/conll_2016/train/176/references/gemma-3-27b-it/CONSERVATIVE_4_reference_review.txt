This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models (f2-type tasks) across multiple datasets, introduces two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and demonstrates the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning for these models. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the creation and release of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset, which addresses some documented shortcomings of the existing Wang dataset. The unified framework and empirical exploration of transfer learning, while valuable, feel less novel given existing work in these areas.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Contribution:** The yodaqa/large2470 dataset appears to be a significant addition to the field. The authors identify legitimate issues with the commonly used Wang dataset – small size, uneven distribution, noisy validation/test splits – and provide a more robust alternative. This is a pragmatic contribution that should benefit future research.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The effort to establish a consistent evaluation methodology, including reporting confidence intervals and comparison to non-neural baselines (TF-IDF, BM25), is commendable. This addresses a critical issue in the fragmented landscape of sentence pair scoring research.  The inclusion of IR baselines provides a valuable grounding for assessing the progress of neural models.
3. **Empirical Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** While not entirely surprising, the finding that transfer learning from Ubuntu Dialogue to other tasks improves performance is a positive signal. It supports the hypothesis that a degree of universal semantic comprehension is achievable.
4. **Open-Source Framework:** The release of the `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` software packages facilitates reproducibility and encourages further research in this area.  Openly available tools are always a plus.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Modeling:** The neural network architectures explored (RNN, CNN, attention) are well-established and do not represent a significant advancement in modeling techniques. The paper doesn't present any novel architectural components or training procedures. The focus is on the *application* of existing methods within a new framework, not on *advancing* those methods.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison Depth:** While IR baselines are included, the neural baselines are somewhat superficial. The paper mentions several established models but doesn’t provide a fully competitive comparison, particularly against more recent state-of-the-art architectures like BERT or other transformer-based models.  The claim of establishing a "watermark" for future research is weakened by this omission.
3. **Overstated Claims of "Universal" Comprehension:** The assertion of pursuing “universal, task-independent f2 models” feels premature. The transfer learning results are promising but limited. The paper doesn’t adequately address the challenges of domain adaptation or the potential for negative transfer. A few positive results do not justify the strong claim of uncovering a truly universal model.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of individual components or design choices. For example, the impact of the BM25 integration or the different scoring modules (dot-product vs. MLP) is not thoroughly investigated. This makes it difficult to determine which aspects of the models are truly effective.
5. **Evaluation Metric Focus:** While MAP, MRR, and Pearson’s r are standard, a more nuanced evaluation of model performance would be beneficial. For example, analyzing error cases or looking at performance across different types of sentence pairs could provide valuable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the existing body of work on transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) for sentence similarity, why were these models not included in the evaluation?  Do you anticipate that these models would significantly outperform the reported results?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the error cases made by the models, particularly on the new yodaqa/large2470 dataset?  What types of sentence pairs are most challenging?
3.  The paper mentions the potential for overlap between the Wang and yodaqa/large2470 datasets. Have you investigated the extent of this overlap and its potential impact on the reported results?
4.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing RMSprop for transfer learning and why it outperformed Adam in your experiments?
5.  What specific aspects of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset (beyond size and distribution) do you believe contribute most to its improved performance compared to the Wang dataset?



**Additional Comments:**

* The description of the datasets is clear and well-organized.
* The code release is a significant positive aspect of this work.
* While the motivation for a unified framework is sound, the paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of this approach.
