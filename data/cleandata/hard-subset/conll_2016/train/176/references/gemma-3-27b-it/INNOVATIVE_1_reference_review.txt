This paper presents a valuable, if somewhat sprawling, attempt to unify research on sentence pair scoring (f2 tasks). The primary contribution is the framing of diverse NLP tasks – answer selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment – under a single, conceptually clean objective: learning a universal semantic comprehension model. Secondary contributions include the introduction of two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) aimed at addressing limitations of existing benchmarks and a thorough empirical evaluation across multiple tasks, including a beneficial transfer learning experiment.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold Vision & Unifying Framework:** The paper’s core idea – seeking a task-agnostic sentence representation capable of excelling across diverse semantic similarity tasks – is genuinely compelling. The f2 formulation is elegantly presented and provides a strong rationale for this approach. This framing has the potential to significantly re-orient research away from task-specific solutions towards more generalizable models.
2. **Dataset Contribution:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 is a significant contribution. The authors rightly identify flaws in the widely used wang dataset (small size, uneven splits) and proactively address them with a larger, more carefully constructed dataset. This addresses a crucial bottleneck in progress for this type of research.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper's extensive empirical evaluation is commendable. Testing models across multiple datasets and reporting confidence intervals demonstrates a commitment to rigorous methodology, even if performance isn't always state-of-the-art. The inclusion of IR baselines is particularly valuable for context.
4. **Positive Transfer Learning Results:** The demonstration that a model pre-trained on one task (Ubuntu Dialogue) can improve performance on others supports the central hypothesis of universal semantic comprehension. This is a critical first step toward validating the f2 framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Model Architectures:** While the evaluation is thorough, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs, attention) are largely standard. The paper doesn't introduce novel architectural components or training techniques. This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw given the focus on the *framework* and *evaluation*, but it limits the paper’s impact on the modeling side.
2. **Results Aren’t Consistently SOTA:** The paper acknowledges lagging behind state-of-the-art results on some tasks (RTE, STS). While not a requirement for acceptance, a stronger showing in at least one area would have bolstered the argument for the proposed framework.
3. **Overly Broad Scope and Organization:** The paper covers a lot of ground, and the organization occasionally feels disjointed. Sections detailing the datasets and models are quite lengthy, and the connection between these sections and the overarching goal could be more clearly articulated. A more focused scope might have allowed for a deeper investigation of the core ideas.
4. **Limited Analysis of Transfer Learning:** While positive transfer learning is demonstrated, the analysis is relatively shallow. Exploring *which* tasks are most beneficial for pre-training and *why* would strengthen the conclusions.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the practical challenges encountered when tuning models on the wang dataset, beyond those described in Fig. 1? Specifically, what strategies did you employ to mitigate the issues with validation/test set correlation?
2.  Given that the datasets were chosen specifically for their availability without licensing restrictions, how might the findings generalize to tasks where access to high-quality, but commercially restricted, datasets is necessary?
3.  You mention the potential for jointly training models on multiple tasks. Have you explored this avenue, even preliminarily? If so, what challenges did you encounter?

**Additional Comments:**

The GitHub repository mentioned would be extremely valuable to reviewers. A link would be appreciated. Also, the formatting of citations within the text is inconsistent (e.g., “Wang et al., 2007” vs. “(Wang et al., 2007)”).



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I recommend acceptance. The paper's bold vision, the valuable dataset contribution, and the comprehensive evaluation outweigh the limitations in modeling novelty. The paper presents a compelling argument for a unified approach to sentence pair scoring and establishes a solid foundation for future research in this area. The authors have identified a critical gap in the field and offered a promising direction for addressing it. I believe the potential impact of this work justifies overlooking minor flaws.
