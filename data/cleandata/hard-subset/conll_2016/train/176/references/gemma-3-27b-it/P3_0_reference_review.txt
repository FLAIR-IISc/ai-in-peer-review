## Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic text similarity by advocating for a unified framework (f2-type tasks) and providing a comprehensive benchmark across several datasets. While the proposed models themselves don't represent a groundbreaking advance in neural architectures, the rigorous evaluation methodology, introduction of new datasets, and emphasis on statistical significance are significant and address critical gaps in the current literature. The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making it accessible to a broad audience. However, the work would benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the proposed framework and a more nuanced exploration of the transfer learning results.

**Summary of the Paper & Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Benchmark & Statistical Rigor:** The most substantial contribution is the establishment of a standardized evaluation framework for sentence pair scoring tasks. The paper’s insistence on reporting confidence intervals and comparison with strong IR baselines addresses a major issue in the field – the lack of statistically sound comparisons.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of `yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop` datasets is a significant addition to the resources available for research. Addressing the shortcomings of the widely used `wang` dataset is a valuable service to the community.
3. **Advocacy for a Unified Framework:** The paper convincingly argues for the benefits of framing various semantic similarity tasks as f2-type problems, promoting a more holistic approach to text comprehension.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Gap in Evaluation:** The emphasis on statistical significance and comparison against strong IR baselines is crucial.  The authors rightly point out the instability of single-run evaluations and the need for confidence intervals. This significantly elevates the credibility of the results.
2. **High-Quality Dataset Creation:**  The new datasets (`yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop`) appear well-constructed and address identified limitations in existing datasets. The process of deriving these datasets from existing resources (curatedv2, YodaQA) is clearly explained.  The introduction of larger, more realistic datasets is vital for training robust models.
3. **Thorough Baseline Comparison:**  The inclusion of a broad range of baselines, from simple word overlap methods (TF-IDF, BM25) to various neural network architectures (RNN, CNN, Attention) provides a solid foundation for evaluating the performance of the proposed models.  The ablation-style analysis helps to understand the contribution of each component.
4. **Open-Source Framework:** The release of the `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` libraries is a commendable effort to facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research in the field. This fosters collaboration and accelerates progress.
5. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is logically structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the tasks, datasets, and models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the paper provides a thorough evaluation of existing models within the proposed framework, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs with attention) are not novel. The primary contribution lies in the framework and evaluation, not the architectural innovations. This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but it limits the paper’s impact in terms of algorithmic advancement.
2. **Overstated Transfer Learning Results:** The transfer learning results, while promising, are presented without sufficient nuance.  The fact that a model trained on Ubuntu Dialogue consistently outperforms the baseline on other tasks is interesting, but the paper doesn’t fully explore *why* this is the case. Is it due to the dataset size, the specific characteristics of the Ubuntu dialogue data, or the model architecture? A more detailed analysis is needed. The claim of learning “universal semantic comprehension” feels premature.
3. **Limited Discussion of Framework Limitations:** The paper champions the f2-type task formulation, but doesn’t adequately address its limitations.  Not all semantic similarity problems easily fit into this framework. The assumption of a "complex label" (the second sentence) may not hold for all tasks, and the paper could benefit from discussing scenarios where this framing is less appropriate.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on aggregate performance metrics. A more in-depth error analysis would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the models and identify areas for improvement.  What types of sentence pairs are the models consistently struggling with?
5. **Dependence on GloVe Embeddings:** The reliance on pre-trained GloVe embeddings might limit the models’ ability to capture domain-specific knowledge. Exploring the use of fine-tuned word embeddings or contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) could potentially improve performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper highlights the instability of the `wang` dataset's test set. How do the performance differences observed across runs affect the conclusions drawn from previous work that relies solely on single-run evaluations on `wang`?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* the transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset is so effective? What characteristics of that dataset contribute to its generalizability?
3.  What are the potential limitations of framing all semantic similarity tasks as f2-type problems? Can you provide examples of tasks where this formulation might be less appropriate?
4.  Have you considered using contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) in your models? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
5.  Could you provide examples of specific error cases where your models fail, and discuss the potential reasons for these failures?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of the figures (e.g., Fig. 1) could be improved for better readability.
*   Consider adding a discussion of related work on domain adaptation and transfer learning in the context of semantic similarity.
*   The supplementary material should include detailed hyperparameters used for each experiment.




In conclusion, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic text similarity. While the models themselves are not groundbreaking, the rigorous evaluation methodology, the introduction of new datasets, and the advocacy for a unified framework are significant achievements. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
