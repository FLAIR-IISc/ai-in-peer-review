This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating semantic similarity between sentence pairs (f2-type tasks), arguing for the potential of task-independent models fine-tuned for specific applications. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address perceived shortcomings in existing benchmarks and present a comprehensive evaluation of various neural network architectures alongside established IR baselines. A key claim is demonstrating the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning, suggesting a path toward universal semantic comprehension.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the creation and release of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset. The issues with the original Wang dataset (small size, uneven distribution, unreliable validation split) are convincingly presented, and a larger, more robust alternative is valuable to the community. The accompanying analysis of existing datasets is also a worthwhile contribution, highlighting their limitations. The unified evaluation framework, while conceptually sound, is largely an implementation detail enabled by the datasets and the provided software package. The transfer learning results, while positive, are not groundbreaking and are presented without sufficient statistical rigor (see weaknesses).

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Contribution:** The yodaqa/large2470 dataset addresses a clear need in the community for a more reliable and sizable benchmark for answer sentence selection. Its creation and public release are a significant contribution.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a relatively thorough comparison of a variety of models, including both neural and non-neural baselines, across multiple tasks. This provides a useful reference point for future work.
3. **Identified Dataset Issues:** The detailed critique of existing datasets (particularly wang) is well-supported by data and highlights crucial issues with commonly used benchmarks.
4. **Software Package:** The provision of an open-source framework (dataset-sts, KeraSTS) promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Models:** The models themselves (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) are well-established. The authors apply them within a unified framework, but there’s no novel architectural contribution.  The argument for "universal semantic comprehension" feels overly ambitious given the models employed.
2. **Insufficient Statistical Rigor:**  While acknowledging the randomness inherent in neural networks, the authors primarily report average performance with 95% confidence intervals *only* in the results section, but do not provide confidence intervals for *all* experiments. The claim of "not possible to statistically determine its relation to state-of-art" for Wang is weak without a proper statistical test demonstrating this. Simply observing a lack of significant difference does not validate the claim.
3. **Transfer Learning Results are Preliminary:** The transfer learning results are interesting, but limited. The improvement observed with the Ubu.RNN model is not substantial enough to convincingly demonstrate the feasibility of truly task-independent representations. The transfer is from a *very* specific domain (dialogue) to others, limiting the generality of the findings.  More extensive transfer experiments are needed.
4. **Overly Optimistic Conclusion:** The conclusion overstates the accomplishments of the work.  Demonstrating a benchmark and showing *some* transfer learning success does not equate to establishing a path toward “AI-complete question answering.” The claim of establishing a “watermark for future research” is also premature.
5. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While some tuning details are given, there isn't enough information to fully reproduce the experiments. The statement that hyperparameters were "primarily tuned on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset" is vague.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of hyperparameters, especially those tuned on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset?
2.  Regarding the transfer learning experiments, could you provide a more rigorous statistical analysis demonstrating the significance of the observed improvements? Specifically, provide p-values comparing the performance of the transferred model to a model trained from scratch on the target task.
3.  You mention that the validation set performance is not always a good indicator of test set performance. How did you address this issue during hyperparameter tuning and model selection? Did you employ techniques like cross-validation or multiple validation splits?
4.  What are the computational resources (e.g., GPUs, training time) required to replicate your experiments?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
* The references are comprehensive.
* The use of the Keras framework is a good choice for accessibility and reproducibility. However, the dependency on a specific framework could be a limitation for some researchers.
