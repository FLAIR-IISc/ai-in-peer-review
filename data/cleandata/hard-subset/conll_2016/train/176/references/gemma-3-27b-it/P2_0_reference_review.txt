## Review for "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic sentence pair scoring by advocating for a unified evaluation framework and introducing new datasets. While the models themselves aren’t groundbreaking, the emphasis on standardized evaluation, the creation of more robust datasets, and the demonstration of transfer learning potential are significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary and Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Unified Evaluation Framework:** The most significant contribution is the proposal of a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models.  The authors correctly identify the fragmented nature of current research and the difficulty in comparing results due to varying datasets and evaluation metrics. The `dataset-sts` package is a concrete realization of this framework, which will be highly valuable to the community.
2. **New Dataset Creation:** The introduction of the `yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop` datasets addresses acknowledged weaknesses in existing datasets like Wang et al. (2007).  These new datasets, specifically designed to be more challenging and realistic, provide a better testbed for evaluating models.
3. **Demonstration of Cross-Task Transfer Learning:**  The paper successfully demonstrates that transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset to other tasks can improve performance, supporting the hypothesis that task-independent semantic comprehension models are feasible.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Need:** The paper tackles a genuine bottleneck in the field - the lack of standardized evaluation. The fragmented landscape hinders progress, and the proposed framework offers a solution. The availability of the `dataset-sts` package is a strong asset.
2. **Robust Dataset Design:** The authors meticulously analyze the shortcomings of existing datasets and provide a clear rationale for the design of the new datasets, specifically addressing issues like small dataset size and uneven candidate sentence distributions. The inclusion of a larger, more challenging dataset is a key strength.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation of various baseline and neural models across multiple tasks.  The inclusion of IR baselines provides a valuable point of comparison and grounds the performance of the neural models. The reporting of confidence intervals is commendable, addressing the inherent randomness in neural network training.
4. **Practical Relevance:**  The focus on tasks with readily available datasets and practical applications (question answering, dialogue systems) enhances the paper's relevance. The discussion of applying the framework to future tasks, such as memory networks, further highlights its potential.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Model Novelty:** While the experimentation is thorough, the models themselves (RNN, CNN, attention) are not particularly novel. The paper primarily *applies* existing architectures within the proposed framework, rather than introducing significant algorithmic advancements.  This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, given the focus on evaluation and data, but it limits the paper’s impact on the modeling side.
2. **Limited Analysis of Transfer Learning:**  The transfer learning results are promising, but the analysis is somewhat limited. A more in-depth investigation of *why* transfer learning works (e.g., what features are being transferred) would strengthen the findings.  The configuration used (dot-product scorer, smaller dimensionality) feels a little arbitrary – justification for these choices would be helpful.
3. **Reporting could be more concise:** The paper is quite lengthy, and some sections (especially the detailed model descriptions) could be more concise without losing essential information. While detail is appreciated, streamlining the presentation would improve readability.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Transfer Learning Analysis:** Can you provide more insights into *what* is being transferred during the cross-task learning process? Are there specific features or representations that are particularly effective for transfer?
2. **Dataset Overlap:** You acknowledge potential overlap between the `wang` and `yodaqa` datasets.  Have you explored the impact of this overlap on your results, and how might it affect comparisons to previous work on the `wang` dataset?
3. **Framework Scalability:** How easily can the `dataset-sts` framework be extended to accommodate new datasets and tasks with different input formats or evaluation metrics? What modifications would be required?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The URLs for the GitHub repository and datasets should be checked to ensure they are active and accessible.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of each dataset (size, type of task, evaluation metric) for easy comparison.
