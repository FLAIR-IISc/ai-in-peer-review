This paper tackles the problem of sentence pair scoring, framing it as a unifying task (f2) for semantic understanding. The authors aim to move beyond task-specific models toward more generalizable approaches, supported by a unified evaluation framework and the introduction of new datasets. 

The primary contribution is the **explicit articulation and framing of a wide range of NLP tasks as instances of sentence pair scoring (f2)**. This is a surprisingly potent idea, reframing the landscape and suggesting a path towards universal semantic models. The secondary contribution lies in the **creation of two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop)** designed to address shortcomings of existing benchmarks, specifically concerning size, data quality, and evaluation stability. Finally, the authors provide a **thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, establishing a baseline for future work** and demonstrating initial success with transfer learning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and unifying vision:** The core premise of the paper – that many NLP problems can be cast as sentence pair scoring – is a genuinely novel and exciting idea. This shifts the focus from bespoke solutions to a more holistic approach to semantic understanding. The potential impact of this framing is significant, potentially accelerating progress across multiple subfields.
2. **Practical dataset contributions:** The new datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop) are a valuable addition to the community. The authors correctly identify deficiencies in existing benchmarks (small size, noisy labels) and proactively address them. The focus on freely available datasets is also commendable.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation framework:** The authors go beyond simply presenting results. They thoughtfully discuss evaluation methodologies, highlight the instability of existing metrics, and introduce confidence intervals to provide a more robust assessment of model performance.  The inclusion of IR baselines is a strong point, offering a practical comparison point.
4. **Initial evidence of transfer learning:** The preliminary results showing the benefits of transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset are encouraging, lending credence to the idea of universal semantic models. While not conclusive, it’s a promising signal.
5. **Open-source framework:** The release of `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` demonstrates a commitment to reproducibility and accessibility, which is highly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological limitations:** While the extensive evaluation is appreciated, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs) are relatively standard. The paper doesn't present a novel model architecture that substantially advances the state-of-the-art. This is acceptable given the focus on the unifying framework, but it limits the immediate impact of the work.
2. **Incomplete validation of universality:** The transfer learning results are promising but limited to a single source dataset. A more extensive investigation across a wider range of source tasks would be necessary to truly validate the claim of universal semantic comprehension.
3. **Lack of ablation studies:**  The paper would benefit from ablation studies exploring the impact of different components (e.g., BM25 integration, dropout rates) on performance. This would provide a deeper understanding of the factors driving the observed results.
4. **Limited discussion of error analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the models fail would provide valuable insights for future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with training and evaluating your models, particularly for the larger datasets?
2.  Beyond the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, what other datasets would you prioritize for further transfer learning experiments to test the generality of your approach?
3.  How sensitive are your results to the choice of word embeddings? Did you experiment with different pre-trained embeddings or fine-tune the embeddings during training?
4.  Given the importance of dataset quality, what steps did you take to ensure the reliability of the labels in the newly created datasets beyond the Mechanical Turk denoise?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The figures are helpful and informative. The reference list is extensive. While the presented models are not groundbreaking, the paper's conceptual contribution and practical dataset additions are significant.  I recommend acceptance, as the potential of this work outweighs its current limitations. The core idea is strong enough to warrant further research, and the authors have provided a valuable foundation for future investigations.
