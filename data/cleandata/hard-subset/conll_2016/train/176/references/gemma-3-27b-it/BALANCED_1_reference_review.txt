This paper presents a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across a variety of semantic similarity tasks. The authors identify a class of problems framed as f2(s0, s1) and advocate for their study as a means to achieve universal semantic comprehension. They introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address shortcomings in existing resources and provide a comprehensive evaluation of several baseline models, including both traditional IR methods and neural network architectures.

The primary contribution of this work is the curated collection of datasets and the unified evaluation framework (dataset-sts & KeraSTS). While the idea of applying a single model to multiple semantic similarity tasks isn’t novel, the practical effort in compiling diverse datasets with detailed analyses of their characteristics and shortcomings is significant. The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 is a valuable addition to the research community, providing a larger and more reliable dataset for answer sentence selection. A second contribution lies in the thorough benchmarking of various models. The comparison against strong IR baselines is crucial for establishing a realistic performance watermark. Finally, the preliminary demonstration of cross-task transfer learning provides encouraging evidence for the feasibility of universal semantic comprehension models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Contribution:** The yodaqa/large2470 dataset is a substantial contribution. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing datasets (wang) and present a compelling case for the need for a more robust resource.  The dataset creation process, leveraging Mechanical Turk for quality control, is well described.
2. **Unified Evaluation Framework:**  The development of dataset-sts and KeraSTS provides a valuable tool for researchers. The modular design facilitates easy integration of new datasets and models, promoting reproducibility and comparability of results. The emphasis on reporting confidence intervals (using Student’s t-distribution) is particularly commendable, acknowledging the inherent randomness in neural network training.
3. **Comprehensive Benchmarking:** The paper presents a thorough comparison of a wide range of models, including both traditional IR methods (TF-IDF, BM25) and diverse neural network architectures (RNNs, CNNs, attention-based models). This breadth of evaluation provides a solid baseline for future research.
4. **IR Baseline Comparison:** Explicitly comparing neural models to strong IR baselines is critical. The authors rightly point out the importance of achieving improvements over these well-established methods.
5. **Transfer Learning Results:** While preliminary, the results showing the benefits of transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset are promising and support the authors’ hypothesis regarding universal semantic comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** The models explored (RNNs, CNNs, attention) are relatively standard and lack significant architectural innovation. The paper focuses more on the evaluation framework and datasets than on proposing novel modeling techniques. The novelty lies in applying these models *across* tasks, not *in* the models themselves.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Transfer Learning:** While the transfer learning results are encouraging, the analysis is limited.  The authors state the Ubu. RNN configuration consistently performs well after transfer, but lack a detailed investigation into *why* this is the case. A more in-depth analysis of what the model learns from the Ubuntu dataset and how it generalizes to other tasks would be valuable.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning & Scalability:** The authors mention that hyperparameters are primarily tuned on yodaqa/large2470. It is unclear how well these hyperparameters generalize to other datasets.  Also, the paper does not address the computational cost of training and evaluating these models on large datasets like Ubuntu Dialogue.
4. **Clarity of Motivation:** While the authors state their goal of achieving “universal semantic comprehension,” this remains a somewhat abstract concept. A more concrete definition of what constitutes “universal comprehension” would strengthen the paper’s focus.  What specific semantic properties should a truly universal model capture?
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by the models on different tasks could provide valuable insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered during the creation of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset?  Were there any unexpected biases or difficulties in obtaining reliable labels from Mechanical Turk?
2.  The paper mentions that validation set performance is not always a reliable indicator of test set performance.  What strategies did you employ to address this issue during hyperparameter tuning and model selection?
3.  Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for each dataset?  Specifically, what search space was used for each model, and how were the best hyperparameters selected?
4.  Regarding the transfer learning experiments: What specific layers (if any) were frozen during the fine-tuning process? Did you observe any differences in performance when freezing different layers?
5.  What computational resources (e.g., GPUs, memory) were required to train and evaluate the models on the largest datasets (Ubuntu Dialogue, yodaqa/large2470)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, although some sections (e.g., the model descriptions) could be more concise.
*   The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
*   The GitHub link to dataset-sts is a valuable resource for the research community.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic similarity. While the models themselves are not particularly novel, the authors’ efforts in creating high-quality datasets and establishing a unified evaluation framework are commendable.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding a deeper analysis of transfer learning and hyperparameter generalization, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
