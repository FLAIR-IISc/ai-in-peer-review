This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating semantic similarity between sentence pairs (f2-type tasks), encompassing multiple datasets and models. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) and aim to demonstrate the feasibility of task-independent semantic comprehension models via cross-task transfer learning. 

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the creation and release of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset. While the motivation for addressing the limitations of the Wang dataset is sound, and the described data collection process is reasonable, the value truly lies in its availability to the research community.  A secondary, though less impactful, contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation of various neural network architectures across several f2-type tasks within a single, well-documented framework (dataset-sts).  The exploration of cross-task transfer learning, while preliminary, demonstrates a promising avenue for research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Contribution:** The yodaqa/large2470 dataset addresses genuine shortcomings of the existing Wang dataset, particularly regarding size, data quality, and stability of rank-based metrics. This is a valuable addition to the field, providing a more robust testbed for evaluating sentence pair scoring models.
2. **Unified Evaluation Framework:** The development of dataset-sts is commendable. A standardized framework, coupled with accessible code, facilitates reproducible research and fair comparisons between different models. The inclusion of both neural and non-neural baselines strengthens the benchmarking process.
3. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors evaluate a broad range of models – from simple averaging to attention-based RNNs – providing a thorough comparative analysis. Reporting confidence intervals, while tedious, is a critical aspect of responsible research, particularly given the inherent variability in neural network training.
4. **Focus on Practical Considerations:** The authors' attention to practical aspects, such as the impact of BM25 weighting and the use of dropout, demonstrates a commitment to building robust and effective models. The acknowledgement of the importance of large datasets for eliciting meaningful improvements is also appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Models:** The core models employed – RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms – are well-established in the NLP literature. While the authors explore various configurations, the architectural innovations are minimal.  The paper primarily *applies* existing techniques rather than *advancing* the state-of-the-art in model design.
2. **Limited Transfer Learning Results:** The transfer learning experiment, while promising, lacks depth. The results are presented without a rigorous ablation study to determine which components contribute most to the performance gains. The choice of a dot-product scorer, while noted as configuration-specific, wasn’t fully justified in the transfer learning setting.
3. **Statistical Significance Concerns:** Despite reporting confidence intervals, many reported performance differences appear marginal and lack clear statistical significance. The discussion frequently mentions “improvements,” but without rigorous statistical testing (beyond confidence intervals), it’s difficult to ascertain whether these improvements are genuine or due to random chance.
4. **Formatting and Writing Inconsistencies:** The paper suffers from numerous minor inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, varying indentation) and writing (e.g., awkward phrasing, unnecessary jargon).  For example, the frequent use of “e.g.” is often followed by a list of more than two examples, making it inappropriate. The extensive list of references also suffers from formatting issues.
5. **Insufficient Motivation for Design Choices:** Several design decisions, such as the choice of GloVe embeddings and the specific hyperparameters used, are not adequately motivated. The justification for using RMSprop for transfer learning feels arbitrary.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study of the transfer learning experiment, specifically identifying which model components contribute most to the performance gains?
2.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to determine whether the reported performance differences are statistically significant? If so, please provide the p-values.
3.  Why was the dot-product scorer specifically selected for the transfer learning experiments? Was this choice motivated by empirical results or by theoretical considerations?
4. Can you clarify the rationale for choosing 300-dimensional GloVe embeddings? Were alternative embedding methods considered, and if so, why were they rejected?

**Additional Comments:**

* A thorough proofreading pass to address the formatting and writing inconsistencies is essential.
* Consider providing a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset.
* The paper would benefit from a more concise and focused presentation of the results.



Overall, while the paper presents a valuable dataset and a useful evaluation framework, the lack of significant novelty in the models and the numerous minor flaws raise concerns. I recommend **rejection** pending substantial revisions that address the weaknesses outlined above. The dataset contribution alone isn’t sufficient to warrant acceptance given the incremental nature of the remaining work.
