This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across multiple tasks, aiming to identify models exhibiting universal semantic comprehension. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address shortcomings in existing benchmarks, and comprehensively evaluate a range of neural and non-neural baselines. A key finding is that transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset improves performance on other tasks, supporting the idea of task-independent semantic understanding.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. **Introduction of two new datasets:** The yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop datasets are a significant contribution. The authors convincingly argue that the existing wang dataset suffers from issues related to size, imbalance, and noisy evaluation splits. The new datasets appear well-motivated and designed to address these problems, providing a more robust platform for benchmarking.
2. **Unified Evaluation Framework:** The creation of a software package (dataset-sts) facilitating consistent evaluation across diverse tasks is a valuable contribution to the community. This framework, incorporating standardized datasets, loaders, and model building tools, will streamline future research in this area.
3. **Demonstration of Cross-Task Transfer Learning:** The authors demonstrate that pre-training on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset improves performance on other tasks, providing empirical support for the hypothesis of universal semantic comprehension. While not a groundbreaking result, it's a concrete step towards this goal and offers practical guidance for model development.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation of numerous models across several tasks.  The inclusion of both neural and non-neural baselines allows for a meaningful comparison and establishes a strong benchmark. The reporting of confidence intervals is commendable, addressing the inherent variability in neural network performance.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the need for a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models and the potential benefits of discovering task-independent representations. The rationale for introducing new datasets is well-explained and supported by analysis of existing resources.
3. **Practical Software Contribution:** The open-source framework (dataset-sts) lowers the barrier to entry for researchers interested in this area. The modular design and use of Keras further enhance its usability.
4. **Rigorous Methodology:** The authors demonstrate attention to methodological details, such as addressing the imbalance in datasets (using BM25 pre-scoring) and employing appropriate optimization techniques. The discussion of hyperparameter tuning and overfitting adds credibility to the results.
5. **Well-structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and easy to follow. The clear separation of tasks, models, and experimental settings facilitates comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the evaluation is comprehensive, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) are relatively standard. The paper does not introduce any novel architectural components or training techniques.  The main innovation lies in the experimental setup and evaluation, rather than the models themselves.
2. **Transfer Learning Results Lack Depth:** While the transfer learning results are promising, the analysis is somewhat superficial. The authors state that “transfer learning … dramatically outperforms the baseline RNN model”, but the degree of improvement isn't always clear across all tasks. A more detailed ablation study examining the impact of different transfer learning parameters would strengthen this claim.
3. **Baseline Performance Discrepancies:** The paper mentions that baseline performances on the new datasets are much lower, suggesting potential for future improvement. However, it doesn't adequately explain *why* this is the case. Are the new datasets inherently more difficult, or are the baselines simply not well-suited to their characteristics? Understanding this would be valuable.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on quantitative results. Including qualitative error analysis (e.g., examples of sentence pairs where the models fail) would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the current approaches and potential directions for future research.
5. **Dataset Overlap Concern:** The authors acknowledge the potential overlap between the wang and yodaqa datasets. This overlap, even if partial, could artificially inflate performance. While they advise against mixing training and evaluation data, the issue deserves further discussion, and perhaps a more rigorous analysis of the overlap itself.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the overlap between the wang and yodaqa datasets? Specifically, what percentage of question-answer pairs are shared between the two?
2.  You mention that the new datasets have lower baseline performance. Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for this, and how the characteristics of these datasets differ from those of the existing benchmarks?
3.  Can you provide a more granular breakdown of the performance improvements achieved through transfer learning? For example, how does the improvement vary across different tasks and model architectures?
4. Can you provide some example sentence pairs where the model performs poorly, along with a brief explanation of why the model might be failing in those cases?
