## Peer Review: Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating and developing models for sentence pair scoring (f2-type tasks) – problems involving determining the semantic relationship between two sentences. The authors identify several relevant tasks (answer selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment), survey existing baselines (both neural and non-neural), introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 & wqmprop), and present a comprehensive evaluation of various neural network architectures. A key focus is demonstrating the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning for building more general semantic comprehension models.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation & Unified Framework:** The paper provides a valuable and much-needed consolidated empirical evaluation of various models across multiple f2-type tasks, employing a consistent methodology. The creation of the `dataset-sts` framework and its open-source availability is a significant contribution to the community, enabling reproducible research.
2. **Introduction of New Datasets:** The introduction of the `yodaqa/large2470` dataset is a positive contribution. Addressing limitations of the existing `wang` dataset (small size, uneven distribution) with a larger, more realistic dataset is crucial for robust model evaluation.
3. **Demonstration of Transfer Learning Benefit:**  The initial results showing the benefit of transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset to other tasks provide encouraging evidence supporting the pursuit of universal semantic comprehension models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Methodology:** The authors employ a thorough evaluation methodology, including reporting confidence intervals and acknowledging the impact of randomness in neural model performance. This level of statistical rigor is commendable and crucial for meaningful comparisons.
2. **Broad Coverage of Models:** The survey and evaluation of a diverse range of models (TF-IDF, CNNs, RNNs, attention mechanisms) provide a solid baseline for future research.
3. **Practical Focus:** The paper’s focus on realistically complex datasets and the introduction of the `dataset-sts` framework demonstrate a commitment to practical application and reproducibility. The BM25 integration with neural models is also a practical and sensible improvement.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and generally clear in its presentation of tasks, models, and results. The motivations behind the research are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the evaluation is extensive, the core neural network architectures employed (CNNs, RNNs with attention) are not particularly novel. The paper primarily *applies* existing techniques to a unified framework rather than introducing significantly new model architectures.
2. **Transfer Learning Results are Preliminary:** The transfer learning results, while promising, are not deeply explored.  The authors primarily demonstrate *that* transfer learning is helpful, but not *why* or *how* to maximize its effectiveness. More detailed analysis of which layers transfer best, and the impact of different transfer learning strategies, is needed.
3. **Performance Gaps with SOTA:** The authors acknowledge lagging behind the state-of-the-art on RTE and STS tasks. While this is understandable given the focus on a broader framework, it raises questions about the competitive advantage of the proposed approach *on those specific tasks*.  More discussion of the limitations in addressing those tasks would be valuable.
4. **Dataset Reporting Could Be More Complete:** While the new datasets are a great addition, more detailed statistics about their characteristics (e.g., sentence length distributions, vocabulary size) beyond what's in Figure 1 would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Transfer Learning Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the transfer learning experiment? Specifically, what layers of the Ubuntu Dialogue model were most beneficial when transferred to other tasks? Did fine-tuning the entire model versus freezing certain layers yield different results?
2. **RTE/STS Performance:**  Why do you believe your models lag behind the state-of-the-art on the RTE and STS tasks?  Are there specific architectural choices or training procedures that could be adjusted to improve performance on these tasks without sacrificing the generality of the framework?
3. **Dataset Overlap:** You mention potential overlap between the `wang` and `yodaqa` datasets. Could you quantify the extent of this overlap? What steps were taken to mitigate any potential data contamination issues during evaluation?
4. **Computational Resources:**  What computational resources (e.g., GPUs, training time) were required to perform the experiments presented in the paper? This information would be valuable for researchers looking to reproduce your results.

**Additional Comments:**

* The URL to the GitHub repository was redacted. Please ensure this is included in the final version.
* Consider providing ablation studies to better understand the contribution of individual components within your models (e.g., the effect of dropout, different scoring modules).
* The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in the area of cross-lingual or multi-task learning for sentence pair scoring.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic text understanding. The unified framework, rigorous evaluation, and introduction of new datasets are significant accomplishments. While the models themselves are not particularly novel, the comprehensive empirical analysis and demonstration of transfer learning potential lay a strong foundation for future research.  Addressing the weaknesses identified above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
