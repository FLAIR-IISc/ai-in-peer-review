This paper presents a framework for unifying research on sentence pair scoring (f2-type tasks) and introduces two new datasets for answer sentence selection. The authors aim to establish a benchmark for evaluating universal semantic comprehension models applicable across diverse tasks.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Unified Framework and Dataset Integration:** The paper provides a valuable contribution by establishing a common framework (dataset-sts and KeraSTS) for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across multiple tasks. This facilitates more meaningful comparisons and reduces fragmentation in the research landscape.
2. **New Datasets (yodaqa/large2470 & wqmprop):** The introduction of these two new datasets, particularly yodaqa/large2470, addresses limitations of the existing Wang dataset (small size, uneven distribution) and provides a more challenging and realistic evaluation resource. The creation process, leveraging the YodaQA system, is well-explained.
3. **Empirical Analysis of Model Transfer:** Demonstrating successful transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset to other tasks provides initial evidence supporting the feasibility of developing task-independent semantic comprehension models, although the gains aren’t overwhelming.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper articulates a compelling rationale for focusing on f2-type tasks and the pursuit of universal semantic comprehension. The motivation for avoiding fragmented research and the need for better evaluation methodologies is well-justified.
2. **Comprehensive Baseline Comparisons:** The authors thoughtfully include a wide range of baselines, from traditional IR methods (TF-IDF, BM25) to various neural network architectures (DAN, RNN, CNN, attention-based models). This provides a solid foundation for assessing the performance of their approach.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation Methodology:** The emphasis on reporting confidence intervals (using Student’s t-distribution) acknowledges the inherent randomness in neural model training and provides a more reliable estimate of performance than single test runs. This is a critical strength and raises the bar for reporting results in this field.
4. **Open-Source Implementation:** The release of the dataset-sts framework and KeraSTS library promotes reproducibility and encourages further research in this area.
5. **Detailed Dataset Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough analysis of the characteristics of the datasets used, including a clear visualization (Fig. 1) highlighting the improvements offered by the new datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the framework and datasets are valuable, the neural network architectures employed (RNN, CNN, attention) are relatively standard and lack substantial novelty. The paper doesn't introduce a significantly new modeling approach, and the performance improvements observed are incremental rather than transformative.
2. **Transfer Learning Results are Modest:** The transfer learning experiments demonstrate some positive results, but the performance gains are not dramatic. The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation into strategies for more effective transfer (e.g., different fine-tuning approaches, architectural adaptations) is needed. The specific configuration (dot-product scorer and smaller dimensionality) used for transfer should be justified more thoroughly.
3. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies to dissect the contributions of different components within the models. For example, the impact of the BM25 feature integration could be analyzed more systematically.
4. **Dataset Overlap Concerns:** The authors acknowledge the potential overlap between the Wang and yodaqa datasets, which could introduce bias in the evaluation. More detailed analysis of this overlap and its impact on the results is warranted.
5. **Discussion of Limitations Could be Stronger:** While the paper touches upon limitations, a more in-depth discussion of the challenges and potential drawbacks of the proposed framework and datasets would strengthen the overall contribution.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the overlap between the Wang and yodaqa datasets? Specifically, what percentage of question-answer pairs are shared between the two datasets?
2. You mention that the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset configuration (Ubu. RNN) performs well in transfer learning. Can you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the dot-product scorer and smaller dimensionality for this configuration?  Were other configurations explored?
3.  Regarding the limited performance improvements from transfer learning, could you speculate on the reasons for this? Is it due to task dissimilarity, model capacity, or other factors?
4.  How does the performance of your framework compare to state-of-the-art models specifically tailored to each individual task, rather than those evaluated within your unified framework? This would help understand the trade-offs of using a single framework for all tasks.




**Additional Comments:**

* The citation style is inconsistent; some references are shortened (e.g., "Baudiš and Šedivý.") while others are not.
* The use of "redacted" for acknowledgements is unusual.
* Fig. 2-4 captions are a little brief - adding information about statistical significance would be helpful.
* The term 'f2-type problems' is used frequently, but a more intuitive explanation or alternative phrasing might improve readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of sentence pair scoring. The introduction of new datasets and a unified framework for evaluation are significant achievements. However, the lack of substantial novelty in the modeling approaches and the modest gains from transfer learning prevent me from recommending a strong accept. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly focusing on exploring more innovative model architectures and conducting more in-depth analysis of the transfer learning results. The authors have created a useful resource for the community, and with further development, this work has the potential to significantly advance the field.
