This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating semantic similarity models (f2-type tasks) across a variety of tasks, including answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, and recognizing textual entailment. The authors introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address shortcomings in existing benchmarks and advocate for rigorous evaluation practices, including comparison to strong IR baselines and reporting confidence intervals. A key claim is that simple neural models can learn universal semantic comprehension through cross-task transfer learning.

The primary contribution is the creation and release of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset, which appears to address genuine issues with the existing Wang dataset. The attempt to establish a standardized evaluation methodology is also valuable, particularly the emphasis on statistical significance and baseline comparison. A third, weaker contribution is the demonstration of some cross-task transfer learning success, although the scale of this success seems limited based on the presented results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Contribution:** The introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset is a significant contribution. The authors convincingly argue for the limitations of the Wang dataset (small size, uneven distribution, noisy evaluation), and the new dataset appears to offer a more robust and challenging benchmark. This alone justifies attention.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation Focus:** The authors correctly identify the problematic reporting practices common in semantic similarity research (single test runs, lack of baseline comparison). Their insistence on statistical rigor (confidence intervals) and inclusion of strong IR baselines is commendable and will force the community to adopt more reliable evaluation strategies.
3. **Framework for Standardization:** The "dataset-sts" framework, while not fully detailed in the paper, represents a step towards standardizing evaluation procedures and facilitating reproducible research. Open-sourcing this framework is a positive move.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Shallowness:** While the paper surveys various neural models, the experimental setup feels superficial. The choice of hyperparameters seems somewhat arbitrary ("primarily tuned on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset"), and the justification for specific architectural decisions (e.g., GRU vs. LSTM) is weak. A deeper investigation of model architectures and hyperparameter optimization would significantly strengthen the work.
2. **Limited Transfer Learning Results:** The transfer learning results, while positive, are not particularly compelling. The improvement achieved by pre-training on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset is not dramatic across all tasks, and the reported gains could be due to factors other than "universal semantic comprehension" (e.g., domain adaptation). More extensive transfer learning experiments, including varying pre-training datasets and architectures, are needed.
3. **Excessive Detail & Formatting Issues:** The paper suffers from an overabundance of detail regarding implementation choices (e.g., dropout rates, regularization parameters) that distract from the core contributions. The writing style is also somewhat convoluted in places, and there are several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent citation styles, broken URLs). For example, the extensive discussion of dropout parameters feels unnecessary for a conference paper. A more concise and focused presentation would improve readability.
4. **Lack of Novelty in Models:** The models themselves (RNNs, CNNs) are not novel. The paper largely applies existing architectures to the proposed framework. While this is not inherently a flaw, it limits the overall impact of the work.
5. **Inconsistent Reporting:** While advocating for statistical rigor, the paper presents results in a way that sometimes obscures the full picture. The focus on mean performance without a clear presentation of confidence intervals in all tables makes it difficult to assess the significance of the observed differences.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for the hyperparameter choices made in your experiments?
2. What is the size of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset? Please provide exact numbers for training, validation, and test sets.
3. What is the computational cost of training the models on the new datasets?
4. Beyond the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, have you explored transfer learning from other tasks? If so, what were the results?
5. Can you provide a link to the dataset-sts framework? The provided URL in the paper is redacted.



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more thorough literature review, particularly regarding recent advances in transfer learning and semantic similarity modeling.
* The discussion of the IR baselines is somewhat superficial. A more detailed comparison of the strengths and weaknesses of different IR approaches would be valuable.
* Please proofread the paper carefully to address the minor formatting inconsistencies and grammatical errors.



Despite the contributions of the new dataset and focus on rigorous evaluation, the methodological weaknesses and limited novelty of the models lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper shows promise, but requires significant revisions to address the identified shortcomings. A more focused presentation, deeper experimental analysis, and a stronger emphasis on novel contributions are necessary for acceptance.
