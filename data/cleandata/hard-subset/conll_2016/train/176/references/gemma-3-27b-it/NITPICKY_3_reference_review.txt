The paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models (f2-type tasks) across various semantic similarity tasks. The authors introduce two new datasets, yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, to address limitations in existing benchmarks, and present a comprehensive evaluation of several neural and non-neural baselines. A key claimed contribution is demonstrating the feasibility of universal, task-independent f2 models via cross-task transfer learning.

However, despite the ambition and scope, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings that, in my assessment, necessitate rejection. While the intention to unify evaluation is commendable, the execution is riddled with inconsistencies, questionable methodological choices, and a lack of rigorous analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Introduction:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 is potentially valuable. The identified shortcomings of the Wang dataset (small size, uneven candidate sentences) are legitimate, and a larger, more balanced dataset is beneficial for the research community.
2. **Comprehensive Baseline Evaluation:** The authors deserve credit for evaluating a wide range of baseline models, including both traditional IR methods and various neural network architectures. This provides a relatively thorough comparison point for future work.
3. **Framework for Unified Evaluation:** The concept of a unified framework is sound and could be a useful tool for standardizing evaluation procedures in this area. The *attempt* to provide such a framework is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Statistical Rigor & Reporting:** The paper’s claim of establishing a "watermark for future research" is undermined by insufficient statistical rigor. The acknowledgement of high variance in results (e.g., MRR on Wang dataset) *without* fully addressing it through appropriate statistical testing (beyond reporting 95% confidence intervals) is deeply problematic. The reported confidence intervals are a step in the right direction, but they don’t excuse the presentation of results with demonstrably large standard deviations as if they were definitive.
2. **Methodological Inconsistencies & Justification:** The rationale behind several methodological choices is unclear or weakly justified. For example, the decision to use only 1/4 of the training data for each epoch is unexplained. The use of BM25 as a feature in the MLP scorer, while potentially helpful, is introduced without sufficient justification for *why* this combination is expected to be effective. The choice of hyperparameters appears largely empirical with limited discussion of sensitivity analysis.
3. **Transfer Learning Results - Limited Analysis:** The transfer learning results are presented with insufficient detail. While the claim of improved performance is made, the extent of the improvement and the specific conditions under which it occurs are not thoroughly analyzed. Simply stating that the "Ubu. RNN" model outperforms the baseline is not sufficient; a more detailed comparison of the learned representations and model behavior is needed.  The lack of ablation studies to pinpoint the precise contributions of the transfer learning process is a major weakness.
4. **Dataset Quality & Evaluation Concerns:** While yodaqa/large2470 addresses issues with the Wang dataset, the evaluation reveals *lower* baseline performance on the new dataset.  This suggests the dataset may be more difficult, but the paper does not adequately explore *why* this is the case. Is the difficulty due to intrinsic characteristics of the data, or potential labeling errors?  The statement that lower performance "suggests that future model improvements will be more apparent in evaluation" is a weak justification.
5. **Formatting & Redundancy:** Numerous minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent citation styles, broken URLs) and redundancies (repetitive phrasing, restatement of obvious points) detract from the paper’s overall professionalism. These seemingly small issues accumulate and create an impression of carelessness.  The constant referencing of GitHub URLs (rather than directly citing implementations) is also poor practice.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What specific statistical tests were performed to determine the significance of the performance differences between models, beyond the calculation of confidence intervals?
2.  Please provide a detailed justification for the decision to use only 1/4 of the training data per epoch.
3.  Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the transfer learning results, including a comparison of the learned representations before and after transfer learning? Specifically, what changes are observed in the model's internal representations after adapting to a new task?
4.  What measures were taken to ensure the quality and consistency of the labels in the yodaqa/large2470 dataset? What is the estimated inter-annotator agreement?
5.  Please address the numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and redundancies throughout the paper.



**Additional Comments:**

The paper reads as a collection of experiments rather than a cohesive research contribution. While the individual components (dataset, baseline evaluation, framework) have merit, they are not integrated into a compelling narrative. The lack of critical self-assessment and the superficial analysis of the results weaken the paper's overall impact. The level of polish and attention to detail is far below the standard expected for a conference like CoNLL.
