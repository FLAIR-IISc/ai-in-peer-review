This paper presents a comprehensive framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across multiple tasks, aiming to identify models with universal semantic comprehension capabilities. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations in existing datasets, and provide a thorough evaluation of various neural and non-neural baselines. The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated, with a strong emphasis on reproducibility through the release of a software package (dataset-sts).

**Contributions:**

1. **Unified Evaluation Framework:** The primary contribution is the creation of a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across diverse tasks (Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, and Recognizing Textual Entailment). This allows for more meaningful comparisons than task-specific evaluations.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop datasets is a significant contribution. These datasets address shortcomings of existing resources (small size, uneven splits, lack of realistic complexity) and provide a more robust testbed for models.  The motivation for creating these datasets is clearly articulated and justified.
3. **Empirical Baseline and Transfer Learning Results:**  The thorough empirical evaluation of various models, including strong IR baselines, provides a valuable benchmark for future research.  The initial results demonstrating the benefit of transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset further support the hypothesis of universal semantic comprehension.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Evaluation Methodology:** The authors are meticulous in their evaluation methodology, reporting confidence intervals and acknowledging the impact of randomness in neural models. This strengthens the credibility of their results.  The comparison against strong IR baselines is particularly commendable.
2. **Practical Focus and Reproducibility:** The release of the dataset-sts software package significantly enhances the practical impact of the work, facilitating reproducibility and encouraging further research. The detailed description of experimental settings also contributes to reproducibility.
3. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of fragmented research in sentence pair scoring and provides a compelling argument for the need for a unified framework. The definition of the f2-type problem is well-explained.
4. **Comprehensive Model Coverage:** The paper evaluates a broad range of models, from simple IR baselines to more complex neural architectures, providing a comprehensive overview of the state-of-the-art.
5. **Detailed Dataset Analysis:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of existing datasets, motivating the creation of their own resources.  Figure 1 effectively illustrates the shortcomings of the wang dataset.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Model Architectures:** While the evaluation is thorough, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs) are not particularly novel. The primary novelty lies in the framework and the datasets, not in the individual model architectures. This is acceptable, but should be acknowledged.
2. **Transfer Learning Results Could Be Stronger:** Although the transfer learning results are promising, the improvements observed are not dramatic.  A more extensive exploration of different transfer learning strategies might yield more significant gains.
3. **Performance Gap on RTE/STS:** The authors acknowledge that their models lag behind the state-of-the-art on the RTE and STS tasks. While this is understandable, a more in-depth discussion of the reasons for this gap and potential avenues for improvement would be beneficial.
4. **Dataset Overlap Concern:** The authors mention a potential overlap between the wang and yodaqa datasets. While they advise against mixing training and evaluation, a more detailed analysis of the extent of this overlap would be valuable.  Quantifying the overlap would increase confidence in the results.
5. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While the authors state they primarily tuned hyperparameters on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset, a more detailed description of the tuning process (e.g., search space, optimization algorithm) would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more quantitative analysis of the overlap between the wang and yodaqa/large2470 datasets?
2. What specific challenges hindered performance on the RTE and STS tasks, and what steps could be taken to address these challenges?
3.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and optimization algorithm used?
4.  Were any attempts made to explore more sophisticated transfer learning techniques beyond simply fine-tuning the model on the target task?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The inclusion of URLs (even redacted) is helpful.
*   The thorough referencing is appreciated.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of all datasets used in the evaluation (size, type of task, etc.). This would provide a quick reference for readers.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of sentence pair scoring. The unified framework, the introduction of new datasets, and the thorough evaluation of baselines provide a solid foundation for future research. While the model architectures themselves are not groundbreaking, the comprehensive nature of the work and the emphasis on reproducibility make it a worthwhile addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
