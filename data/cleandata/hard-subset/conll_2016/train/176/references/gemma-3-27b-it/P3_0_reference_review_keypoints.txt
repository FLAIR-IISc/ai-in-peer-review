- Paper proposes a unified f2-type framework for sentence pair scoring and provides a comprehensive benchmark across multiple datasets.  
- Emphasizes statistical rigor by advocating confidence intervals and comparisons to strong IR baselines to address instability of single-run evaluations.  
- Introduces two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) addressing limitations of existing datasets like wang.  
- Offers broad baseline comparisons (TF-IDF, BM25, RNN, CNN, Attention) and ablation-style analysis to clarify component contributions.  
- Releases open-source libraries (dataset-sts and KeraSTS) to improve reproducibility.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly structured, and accessible.  
- Main novelty lies in the framework and evaluation methodology rather than in novel neural architectures.  
- Transfer learning claims (Ubuntu Dialogue pretraining) are promising but presented without sufficient analysis of why they generalize.  
- The f2-type framing is well-argued but the paper lacks discussion of scenarios where it may be inappropriate.  
- No in-depth error analysis; paper reports mostly aggregate metrics without exploring consistent failure cases.  
- Heavy reliance on pre-trained GloVe embeddings; lacks experiments with contextualized embeddings (BERT/RoBERTa) or fine-tuning.  
- Questions raised: impact of wang instability on prior single-run results; reasons Ubuntu dialogue transfers well; limits of f2 formulation; use/results of contextual embeddings; examples of model failure cases.  
- Minor presentation issues: improve figure formatting and include detailed hyperparameters in supplementary material.  
- Suggest adding related work discussion on domain adaptation and transfer learning.  
- Overall recommendation: valuable contribution deserving acceptance with minor revisions.
