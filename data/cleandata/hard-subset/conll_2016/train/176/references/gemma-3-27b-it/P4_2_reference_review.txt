This paper presents a unified framework for sentence pair scoring, aiming to establish a common ground for evaluating models across diverse semantic similarity tasks. The authors identify several tasks (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, recognizing textual entailment) and datasets, introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop), and evaluate a range of baseline and neural models within this framework. The primary contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation and comparison of models across multiple tasks, establishing a baseline for future research. A secondary contribution is the introduction of the new datasets, designed to address limitations found in existing resources. Finally, the authors demonstrate the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning, suggesting the potential for universal semantic comprehension models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The paper’s most significant strength is the effort to unify evaluation across different sentence pair scoring tasks. By adopting a consistent framework, the authors facilitate meaningful comparisons between models, which is often challenging in the fragmented landscape of NLP research. This is crucial for identifying genuinely generalizable models and advancing the field. The inclusion of IR baselines is particularly valuable, providing a strong point of reference for neural models.
2. **Introduction of New Datasets:** The authors identified limitations in existing datasets and addressed them by introducing yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop. These datasets appear thoughtfully constructed, addressing issues like small dataset size, uneven candidate distributions, and noisy validation/test splits. The yodaqa/large2470 dataset, in particular, addresses the limitations of the commonly used wang dataset, making it a valuable resource for the community.
3. **Empirical Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** The paper provides empirical evidence suggesting that transfer learning across tasks can be beneficial. This supports the hypothesis of universal semantic comprehension models, and the observed improvements when transferring from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset are encouraging. This provides a practical direction for future research.
4. **Detailed Model and Experimental Setup:** The paper offers a thorough description of the models used (including various neural network architectures) and the experimental setup (hyperparameter tuning, evaluation metrics).  The provision of a software package (dataset-sts) promoting reproducibility and further research is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the evaluation framework is strong, the models themselves are largely standard architectures (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) without significant novel contributions. The paper focuses primarily on applying and comparing existing models, rather than proposing new ones. The paper acknowledges this limitation, however, the lack of novel architecture exploration weakens the overall impact.
2. **Overemphasis on Implementation Details:** A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to describing the experimental setup and implementation details. While detail is important, it sometimes overshadows the key findings and insights. Streamlining this section could improve the paper’s clarity and focus.
3. **Statistical Significance and Confidence Intervals:** While the authors report confidence intervals, the discussion of statistical significance could be strengthened. The reported standard deviations are often relatively large, raising questions about the robustness of some of the findings. A more rigorous statistical analysis would bolster the claims made in the paper.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from more extensive ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components within the models. For example, investigating the impact of the BM25 feature or different dropout rates would provide valuable insights into the model’s behavior.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational resources required to reproduce the results presented in the paper? Specifically, how long did training take for each model on each dataset?
2.  You mention that the validation set performance is not always a good indicator of test set performance. What strategies did you employ to address this issue, and how did they impact your final model selection?
3.  Could you provide more details on the process of denoisification applied to the curatedv2 question dataset when constructing yodaqa/large2470? What specific criteria were used to identify and remove noisy samples?
4.  Given the large standard deviations reported, how confident are you in the transfer learning results? Are the observed improvements statistically significant after accounting for the variability?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation "f2" is used extensively but could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.
*   The URLs mentioned are incomplete. Please ensure the full, functional URLs are included in the final version of the paper.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of all datasets used in the study.
