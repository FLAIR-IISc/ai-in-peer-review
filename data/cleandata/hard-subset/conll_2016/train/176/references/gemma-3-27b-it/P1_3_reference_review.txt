## Peer Review: Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension

**Summary:**

This paper presents a framework for evaluating and comparing models designed to score the semantic similarity of sentence pairs (f2 tasks). The authors identify a range of such tasks (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment) and advocate for a unified evaluation methodology, including the introduction of new datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop) to address shortcomings in existing benchmarks. They evaluate a variety of neural network architectures (RNN, CNN, attention-based models) alongside strong IR baselines, and demonstrate the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The paper provides a valuable, well-structured framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across multiple datasets and tasks. This promotes comparability and facilitates progress in the field, which has previously suffered from fragmentation.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 provides a more robust and realistic benchmark for answer sentence selection, addressing issues with the existing Wang dataset (small size, uneven distribution).
3. **Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** Showing positive transfer learning results across the different f2 tasks strengthens the argument for developing universal semantic comprehension models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the need for a unified approach to evaluating sentence pair scoring models and justifies the focus on f2-type tasks.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors evaluate a diverse set of models, including strong baselines, and report confidence intervals, acknowledging the inherent variability in neural network performance. The choice of GloVe embeddings and Adam optimizer are standard and appropriate.
3. **Rigorous Dataset Analysis:** The detailed analysis of the Wang dataset’s limitations and the motivation for creating yodaqa/large2470 are compelling. The provided characteristics (Fig. 1) are informative.
4. **Open-Source Framework:** Providing the `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` frameworks as open-source resources is a significant contribution to reproducibility and future research.
5. **Good Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ arguments and understand the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the experimental setup is thorough, the models themselves are largely standard architectures. The paper doesn’t introduce novel architectural innovations, and the performance gains aren’t dramatic, relying more on the rigorous evaluation and new datasets.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** Although various models are evaluated, there’s a lack of detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of individual components within those models (e.g., the effect of dropout, different scoring functions).
3. **Transfer Learning Results Limited Scope:** The transfer learning experiment uses a specific configuration (RNN with dot-product scorer) and doesn’t explore a wider range of configurations or pre-training strategies. More detailed analysis of *why* transfer learning works in this setting is needed.
4. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper presents performance numbers but lacks a deeper analysis of *where* the models are failing. Error analysis could provide valuable insights into the limitations of current approaches and suggest directions for future research.
5. **Datasets Primarily English:** The focus on English datasets limits the generalizability of the framework and findings. Exploring multilingual datasets would enhance the value of the research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the limitations of the Wang dataset regarding validation/test set correlation. Did you explore techniques to mitigate this issue (e.g., k-fold cross-validation, data augmentation)?
2.  Could you provide more details on the Mechanical Turk labeling process for yodaqa/large2470? What quality control measures were implemented to ensure the accuracy of the labels?
3.  The transfer learning results are promising.  Could you elaborate on why you chose the specific RNN configuration for transfer and whether other configurations were attempted?
4.  Were any attempts made to analyze the types of errors made by the models on the different datasets?  A brief discussion of common failure cases would be helpful.
5.  Could you comment on the computational resources required to reproduce your experiments, particularly the training time for the larger models on the larger datasets?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper frequently refers to URLs that are redacted. Please ensure these are included in the supplementary material.
* The notation f1 and f2 is introduced without clear justification. While understandable in the context, a more explicit explanation could improve readability.
* In the conclusion, the authors suggest exploring joint training on multiple tasks. This is a promising direction, but potentially challenging due to task-specific optimization objectives.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of sentence pair scoring. The paper’s strength lies in its comprehensive evaluation framework, the introduction of a new and improved dataset, and the demonstration of the feasibility of transfer learning. While the models themselves are not particularly novel, the rigorous evaluation and practical resources provided make this a worthwhile read for researchers in this area.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
