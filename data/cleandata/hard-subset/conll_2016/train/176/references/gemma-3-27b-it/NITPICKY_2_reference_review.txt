This paper proposes a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models (f2-type tasks) across diverse tasks like answer selection, next utterance ranking, and textual entailment. The authors introduce two new datasets, yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, to address perceived shortcomings in existing datasets and advocate for more rigorous evaluation methodology, including comparison to strong IR baselines and reporting confidence intervals. A core claim is the potential for training universal semantic comprehension models through cross-task transfer learning.

The primary contribution, as I see it, lies in the curated datasets and the accompanying evaluation framework. While the concept of a unified f2 framework isn't entirely novel, the effort to create more robust datasets (yodaqa/large2470, specifically) and establish a more statistically sound evaluation pipeline is valuable. The introduction of confidence intervals is a particularly positive step, often missing in NLP literature. A secondary contribution is the baseline performance reported across several tasks, providing a useful benchmark for future research. The empirical demonstration of transfer learning, while not groundbreaking, offers preliminary support for the core hypothesis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Creation:** The yodaqa/large2470 dataset appears to be a significant addition, addressing limitations of the standard wang dataset – namely, small size, uneven sentence pairs, and noisy validation/test splits. The detailed explanation of its construction methodology is commendable.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The insistence on statistical significance (confidence intervals) and comparison with strong IR baselines represent a substantial improvement over typical NLP evaluation practices. This promotes a more objective and reliable assessment of model performance.
3. **Comprehensive Baseline:** The thorough evaluation of a wide range of neural and non-neural baselines provides a strong foundation for comparison and establishes a clear watermark for future research. The inclusion of IR baselines is particularly pertinent.
4. **Modular Framework:** The introduction of the dataset-sts framework and KeraSTS library is a practical contribution, facilitating reproducible research and enabling easier experimentation with different models and datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Models:** The neural models explored (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) are relatively standard and lack significant architectural innovation. While the framework is valuable, the models themselves don't represent a major advance. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by focusing on the *evaluation* framework rather than novel model architectures.
2. **Transfer Learning Results – Incremental:** The transfer learning results, while positive, are somewhat incremental. The improvements gained from pre-training on the Ubuntu dataset are not dramatic and don’t definitively prove the feasibility of truly “universal” semantic comprehension models. More extensive transfer learning experiments with varying degrees of task relatedness are needed.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning – Insufficient Detail:** The description of hyperparameter tuning is relatively sparse. It’s stated that parameters were primarily tuned on yodaqa/large2470, but the specific search space, optimization strategy, and rationale for parameter choices are not detailed. This lack of transparency makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the reported results.
4. **Formatting Inconsistencies & Typos:** The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, varying spacing) and contains several typographical errors. While not critical, these detract from the overall polish and credibility of the work. For example, the reference to “Sec. 6” is inconsistent; sometimes it’s “Sec. 6”, sometimes “Section 6”. Also, the URL in footnote 10 is redacted, rendering it useless.
5. **Ambiguous Task Definition:** The initial definition of f2 as simply returning a real number (R) is overly simplistic and doesn’t fully capture the nuances of tasks like entailment (which has three classes). This imprecision introduces some ambiguity into the paper's framing.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space, optimization strategy, and rationale for your parameter choices?
2. What specific ablation studies were conducted to determine the contribution of the BM25 overlap feature in the Answer Sentence Selection tasks?
3. While you mention potential overlap between the wang and yodaqa datasets, did you investigate the extent of this overlap empirically?  What percentage of questions are shared?
4. Could you elaborate on the reasons for choosing RMSprop during transfer learning, as opposed to Adam, which was used for initial training?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is substantial, but appears to be missing some necessary formatting (e.g., consistent capitalization of article titles).
* Please provide the full URL in footnote 10.
* A table summarizing the key characteristics of each dataset (size, task type, evaluation metric) would be helpful.



I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a valuable contribution in terms of dataset creation and evaluation methodology, the lack of novelty in the models, the incremental transfer learning results, and the numerous minor flaws (formatting, hyperparameter tuning details) outweigh the strengths. The paper feels more like a carefully constructed benchmark report than a significant research advance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing greater detail regarding experimental setup and model choices, could significantly improve the quality of the work, but as it stands, it doesn’t meet the standards for acceptance.
