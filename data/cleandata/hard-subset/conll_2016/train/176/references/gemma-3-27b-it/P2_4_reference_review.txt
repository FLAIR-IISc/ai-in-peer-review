## Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a framework for unifying research on sentence pair scoring (f2) tasks, arguing that these tasks are valuable for developing general semantic comprehension models. The authors survey existing tasks and datasets, provide a detailed overview of baseline models (both neural and non-neural), introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and present experimental results comparing various models across multiple tasks. They also demonstrate the benefits of transfer learning from a model trained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Survey & Unified Framework:** The paper provides a valuable overview of f2 tasks, datasets, and models, framing them within a common evaluation paradigm.  This consolidation effort is useful for the community.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 addresses shortcomings in the commonly used wang dataset, providing a larger and arguably more robust resource for answer sentence selection research.
3. **Empirical Benchmarking & Transfer Learning Demonstration:** The extensive experimentation and demonstration of successful cross-task transfer learning provide a useful baseline and encourage further exploration of universal semantic representation learning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Motivated Problem & Scope:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of f2 tasks and the potential benefits of developing broadly applicable semantic comprehension models. The motivation for seeking a unified framework is compelling.
2. **Thorough Baseline Coverage:**  The survey of existing models is impressively comprehensive, covering a wide range of neural architectures and traditional IR baselines.  The inclusion of strong IR baselines is a particularly positive aspect of the evaluation.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation Methodology:** The authors acknowledge the inherent randomness in neural models and address it by reporting confidence intervals, which is commendable and significantly strengthens the validity of the results.  The introduction of the `dataset-sts` framework facilitates reproducibility.
4. **Practical Dataset Contribution:**  The `yodaqa/large2470` dataset is a significant contribution. The authors clearly identify the limitations of existing datasets and provide a compelling argument for the need for a better resource. The description of the dataset creation process is also helpful.
5. **Demonstrated Transfer Learning:** The positive results from transfer learning, specifically from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, provide encouraging evidence for the feasibility of learning task-independent semantic representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the paper provides a thorough evaluation of existing models, it does not introduce any fundamentally new architectures. The models used are largely standard and well-established in the literature.  The contribution lies more in the *application* and *comparison* of these models than in novel model design.
2. **Performance Gap to State-of-the-Art:** The authors acknowledge that their models lag behind the state-of-the-art on several tasks (RTE, STS). While establishing a new baseline is valuable, a more substantial performance improvement would have strengthened the paper’s impact.
3. **Overly Detailed Model Descriptions:** The descriptions of the models, particularly in Section 3, are excessively detailed, bordering on implementation-level specifics. This level of detail detracts from the overall narrative and could be streamlined without losing critical information.  The focus should be on the *why* not just the *how*.
4. **Limited Analysis of Transfer Learning:** While transfer learning is demonstrated, the analysis of *why* it works is relatively limited.  Investigating which aspects of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset contribute to successful transfer would provide valuable insights.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the limited novelty in model architectures, can you elaborate on the rationale for focusing on these specific models? Were there specific computational constraints or design choices that motivated these selections?
2.  The paper mentions the importance of ensemble methods with IR baselines. Can you provide more details on the attempts made to combine these approaches and the reasons for the limited success observed?
3.  What specific characteristics of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset do you believe contribute to its effectiveness as a source domain for transfer learning?  Have you explored other potential source domains?
4.  Could you elaborate on the overlap between the wang and yodaqa datasets? While you mention avoiding training/evaluation on both simultaneously, could this overlap introduce biases into the comparison of results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized, but some sections (e.g., Section 3) could benefit from more concise phrasing.
*   The provided URL for the `dataset-sts` framework was redacted.  Please ensure this is included in the final version of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a solid contribution to the field of semantic sentence pair scoring.  It provides a valuable service by unifying research, introducing a new dataset, and establishing a rigorous evaluation framework. While the lack of novel models and the performance gap to state-of-the-art are weaknesses, the paper’s strengths outweigh these limitations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses and questions raised above.
