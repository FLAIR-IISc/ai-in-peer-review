**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, framing problems like Answer Sentence Selection (AS), Next Utterance Ranking, and Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE) under a single f2(s0, s1) formulation. The authors' main contributions are threefold: (1) they propose a unified framework with a corresponding open-source software package for benchmarking models across various tasks; (2) they introduce two new, larger datasets for the AS task and advocate for a more rigorous evaluation methodology based on multiple runs and confidence intervals; and (3) they demonstrate the feasibility of learning "universal" text comprehension models by showing that a model pre-trained on a large dialogue dataset can be successfully transferred to other tasks.

**Contributions**

1.  **Improved Evaluation Methodology:** The most significant contribution is the rigorous approach to evaluation. The authors convincingly argue against the common practice of reporting single-run results by demonstrating high variance across runs on standard datasets (e.g., `wang`). Their use of 16 runs to compute means and 95% confidence intervals is a commendable practice that significantly increases the reliability of the presented results and sets a higher standard for future work in this area.
2.  **New Datasets and Analysis:** The paper introduces two new datasets for Answer Sentence Selection (`yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop`), which appear to be larger and more challenging than existing alternatives. The analysis comparing dataset characteristics in Figure 1 is a valuable resource.
3.  **Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** The experiments in Section 5 provide strong evidence that representations learned for one sentence-pair task (Next Utterance Ranking) can be effectively transferred to others, outperforming models trained from scratch. This supports the central thesis that pursuing universal sentence comprehension models is a viable research direction.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Rigor:** The paper's strongest aspect is its emphasis on robust evaluation. The analysis of result instability and the subsequent use of confidence intervals is exemplary and directly addresses a major methodological weakness in the field.
2.  **Valuable New Resources:** The introduction and analysis of new, large-scale datasets, coupled with an open-source framework (`dataset-sts`), are significant practical contributions that will likely facilitate future research.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results for Transfer Learning:** The performance of the `Ubu. RNN` model across multiple tasks provides compelling evidence for the paper's core hypothesis. The gains from transfer learning are substantial and consistent, making a strong case for the "universal model" approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Flaws:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of typographical and grammatical errors (e.g., "AnNLPmachine", "sentenes", "world overlap", "CNNmodels"). More critically, the bibliography is in a state of disarray, with rampant formatting artifacts (e.g., `\Q2015\E`), inconsistencies, and bare URLs in footnotes. This lack of polish raises serious concerns about the overall care with which the research was conducted and documented.
2.  **Opaque and Inconsistent Methodological Details:** Several crucial details are poorly explained, inconsistent, or relegated to footnotes.
    *   The two-stage ranking process for Answer Sentence Selection, where candidates are first pruned to the top 20 by BM25, is a highly consequential design choice that is not adequately discussed or justified. It complicates the interpretation of model performance and deviates from a purely neural "comprehension" model.
    *   The claim that element-wise sum and absolute difference in the MLP scorer perform equally, with the sum being chosen for unspecified "technical reasons," is unsubstantiated.
    *   Key hyperparameters are not well motivated (e.g., why no dropout for CNNs? why the massive p=0.8 dropout for RNNs?).
    *   The experimental detail that the model trains on "1/4 of the dataset in each epoch" is ambiguous and requires clarification.
3.  **Incomplete and Potentially Misleading Reporting:** The authors admit their models "clearly yet lag behind the state-of-art on the RTE and STS tasks" and explicitly state that results for STS and Paraphrasing are omitted because they "lag behind... significantly and show little difference". For a paper aiming to establish a unified benchmark, hiding negative results is poor scientific practice. Furthermore, the claim that the model's performance on the `wang` dataset cannot be statistically distinguished from the state-of-the-art is a weak phrasing; it would be more transparent to state that the model's confidence interval includes the SOTA point estimate, which does not imply equivalence.
4.  **Weakened Core Claim:** The argument for a "universal text comprehension" model is undermined by the reliance on non-universal, engineered features. The model is augmented with boolean flags for unigram/bigram overlap, capitalization, and numbers, in addition to the heavy reliance on BM25 pre-filtering for some tasks. This suggests that the neural models alone are insufficient and that significant task-specific engineering is still required.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The statement that you "train on 1/4 of the dataset in each epoch" for answer selection tasks is unclear. Does this mean you randomly sample 25% of the data for each epoch? If so, why was this strategy chosen over training on the full dataset?
2.  The BM25 pre-filtering step seems critical to the performance on the AS tasks. How does this fit into a "unified framework"? Have you performed an ablation study to quantify the impact of this filtering, i.e., what is the performance of the neural models on the full, unfiltered candidate set?
3.  Can you provide concrete justifications for several key modeling choices? Specifically: a) the "technical reasons" for preferring element-wise sum over the more standard absolute difference in the MLP scorer; and b) the reason for not applying dropout to the CNN model, while applying a very high rate to the RNN model.
4.  For a paper presenting a comprehensive benchmark, the omission of poor results on STS and Paraphrasing is concerning. Could you include these results in the final version for completeness and transparency?
5.  There seems to be a contradiction regarding dropout in the transfer learning experiment. Section 3.2 states the RNN model uses p=4/5 dropout, but Section 5 implies the source model for transfer learning did not use dropout. Please clarify the exact configuration of the source `Ubu. RNN` model.
