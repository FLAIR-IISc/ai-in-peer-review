### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a unified framework for "sentence pair scoring" (which the authors term f2-type tasks), arguing that a single, universal text comprehension model can be adapted for various tasks like Answer Sentence Selection (ASS), Next Utterance Ranking, and Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE). The authors' main aims are to: (A) unify research with a modular framework, (B) improve evaluation methodology, and (C) demonstrate the feasibility of universal models via transfer learning. To this end, they introduce two new ASS datasets to address shortcomings in existing ones, implement a range of neural models (RNN, CNN, hybrids) within a new open-source framework, and perform a cross-task evaluation. They emphasize rigorous statistical evaluation by averaging multiple runs and reporting confidence intervals. Finally, they show that a model pretrained on a large dialogue dataset can be successfully transferred to other tasks, outperforming models trained from scratch.

### Contributions
1.  **New Datasets and Analysis:** The strongest contribution is the introduction of two new datasets for Answer Sentence Selection (`yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop`). The authors provide a compelling analysis of the statistical deficiencies of the widely used `wang` dataset (small size, noisy validation/test correlation) and create larger, more challenging benchmarks.
2.  **Emphasis on Methodological Rigor:** A significant contribution is the strong advocacy for and implementation of a more rigorous evaluation methodology. By averaging results over 16 runs and reporting 95% confidence intervals, the authors convincingly demonstrate that single-run results on noisy datasets can be misleading, a crucial point for the community.
3.  **Cross-Task Benchmarking and Transfer Learning:** The paper provides a useful benchmark of several standard neural architectures across a diverse set of sentence-pairing tasks within a single codebase. The transfer learning experiment provides compelling evidence for their central hypothesis that task-independent text comprehension models are a viable research direction.

### Strengths
1.  **Well-Motivated Datasets:** The introduction of the new ASS datasets is well-justified. The critique of the `wang` dataset is backed by clear numerical illustration (Fig. 1), and the new datasets appear to offer a more stable and challenging testbed for future research, as evidenced by the lower baseline performances.
2.  **Statistically Sound Evaluation:** The paper's insistence on proper statistical evaluation is commendable and should be a standard for the field. The example given, where the standard deviation of their model on the `wang` test set is larger than the reported gains of successive state-of-the-art papers, is a powerful argument that highlights a systemic issue in prior work.
3.  **Ambitious and Unifying Vision:** The core idea of framing multiple NLP tasks under the single umbrella of sentence pair scoring and pursuing a universal model is ambitious and valuable. The release of an open-source framework to facilitate this line of research is a welcome practical contribution. The transfer learning results, showing strong performance from a model pre-trained on the Ubuntu Dialogue Corpus, provide solid initial support for this vision.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Care in Presentation:** The paper is marred by a substantial number of formatting errors and typos that suggest a lack of careful proofreading. The very first sentence begins with "AnNLPmachine...", a glaring concatenation error. The references section is riddled with BibTeX processing errors (e.g., "Agirre et al\.,? \Q2015\E, Agirre et al\.,"), rendering it difficult to read and unprofessional. Footnote 1 contains a dead link to the ACL wiki. Such sloppiness undermines confidence in the meticulousness of the research itself.
2.  **Methodological Inconsistencies and Unexplained Design Choices:** The paper contains numerous methodological choices that are either inconsistent or poorly justified.
    *   The RNN model uses "massive dropout p = 4/5" on both input and output, whereas the CNN model has no dropout applied at all. The reason for this stark difference is not explained.
    *   Footnote 8 states that element-wise sum was used instead of the more common absolute difference in the MLP scorer for "technical reasons," which are never specified.
    *   The attention model (attn1511) has a completely different architecture (asymmetric, non-shared weights, no projection layer, different CNN parameters) from the other models, making direct comparison difficult. These deviations are noted but not justified.
3.  **Confounding Factors in Experimental Design:** The experimental setup includes details that significantly limit the generality of the conclusions.
    *   For ASS tasks, candidates are pruned to the top 20 based on BM25 before being scored by the neural model. This means the models are not solving the full task but are rather re-ranking candidates from a strong IR baseline. This is a critical detail that should be highlighted as a major caveat.
    *   Hyperparameters were "tuned primarily on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset" and seemingly applied to all other tasks. This is poor practice, as optimal hyperparameters are often task-dependent. The performance on other datasets may be artificially depressed, weakening the cross-task comparison.
4.  **Selective Reporting of Results:** The authors state in Section 6.1 that they do not report results for Semantic Textual Similarity and Paraphrasing because their models "lag behind the state-of-the-art significantly." This omission is a serious flaw. For a paper claiming to provide a "unified framework" for benchmarking, it is essential to report all results, even poor ones. Selectively presenting favorable outcomes misrepresents the true capabilities of the proposed models and framework.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The use of BM25 to pre-filter candidates in the Answer Sentence Selection tasks seems to fundamentally change the problem from sentence scoring to re-ranking. How do you believe this impacts the claim that your models are learning "universal semantic comprehension," given they only operate on a pre-filtered, high-relevance subset of the data?
2.  Could you provide justifications for the numerous inconsistent design choices across your models? For instance, why is massive dropout beneficial for RNNs but dropout is entirely absent for CNNs? Why was a different RNN configuration (dot-product scorer, smaller dimensionality) used for the transfer learning experiment in Section 5 compared to the baseline RNN in Section 3.2?
3.  Why were the results for STS and Paraphrasing omitted? Given that your paper’s goal is to establish a unified framework and watermark, doesn’t omitting poor results undermine this goal by presenting an incomplete picture of the models' performance across the f2-task landscape?
