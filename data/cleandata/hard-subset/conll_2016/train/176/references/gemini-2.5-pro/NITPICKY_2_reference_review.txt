### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes to unify a variety of sentence pair comparison tasks under a single "sentence pair scoring" (f2) framework. The authors argue for the pursuit of universal, task-independent text comprehension models that can be adapted to specific tasks. They implement a range of standard neural models within an open-source framework, introduce two new datasets for answer sentence selection, and conduct a broad empirical study.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A Methodological Argument for Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper's strongest contribution is its insistence on, and demonstration of, more robust evaluation practices. By showing the high variance of model performance across different random seeds on a standard dataset and subsequently reporting all results as averages over 16 runs with 95% confidence intervals, the authors set a high and necessary standard for future work in this area.
2.  **New Resources for the Community:** The paper introduces two new, large-scale datasets for answer sentence selection (`yodaqa/large2470`, `wqmprop`) that address documented weaknesses in existing benchmarks. Additionally, the release of their `dataset-sts` software framework is a practical contribution that could facilitate future comparative studies.
3.  **An Empirical Benchmark and Feasibility Study:** The work provides a comprehensive benchmark of several baseline and neural models across a diverse set of tasks. The transfer learning experiments provide compelling, albeit preliminary, evidence for the authors' central hypothesis that models can learn a form of universal text comprehension.

### Strengths

1.  **Statistical Rigor in Evaluation:** The paper's critique of single-run evaluations is sharp, timely, and convincingly demonstrated. The example of the `wang` dataset, where the standard deviation across runs of a single model is larger than the reported gains between successive state-of-the-art papers, is a powerful indictment of common practices. Adopting multi-run averaging with confidence intervals is a major strength.
2.  **Creation of New and Well-Motivated Datasets:** The authors clearly identify the weaknesses of the standard `wang` dataset for answer selection (small size, unstable metrics, train/dev/test mismatch). Their new datasets appear to be a direct and thoughtful attempt to remedy these issues, offering larger scale and lower baseline performance, which leaves more room for future models to show improvement.
3.  **Unified Framework and Open-Source Code:** The effort to unify disparate tasks and models into a single, modular, and open-source framework is highly commendable. This lowers the barrier to entry for other researchers and makes the presented results more transparent and reproducible.
4.  **Promising Transfer Learning Results:** The experiment in Section 5, where a model pre-trained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset is successfully adapted to other tasks, provides strong support for the paper's core thesis. The fact that this transferred model (`Ubu.RNN`) is consistently one of the best performers is a significant finding.

### Weaknesses

Despite the paper's strengths, it is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a general lack of polish that collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Errors:** The paper appears to have been hastily prepared.
    *   The reference list is improperly formatted, with BibTeX artifacts like `et al\.,? \Q2015\E,` appearing in nearly every entry. This is a significant oversight.
    *   There are typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "AnNLPmachine learning task" in the first sentence).
    *   Section numbering is incorrect ("Model Performance" is labeled `.1` and there is no Section 4.1).
    *   Several important details are relegated to footnotes, such as the potential data overlap between `wang` and `yodaqa` (footnote 3) and the justification for using `sum` instead of `absolute difference` in the MLP scorer (footnote 8), which is vaguely explained as "for technical reasons."

2.  **Lack of Methodological Justification and Clarity:** Several key design choices are either poorly explained or inconsistent.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The authors state that hyperparameters were "tuned primarily on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset." It is unclear if these same parameters were then used for all other tasks. If so, this is a flawed methodology, as optimal hyperparameters are highly task-dependent, and it could explain why the models "clearly yet lag behind the state-of-art on the RTE and STS tasks."
    *   **Inconsistent Model Architectures:** Dropout strategies vary wildly between models ("massive dropout" for RNN, no dropout for CNN, no dropout for RNN-CNN) without any justification. The lack of dropout on the CNN is particularly surprising and unconventional.
    *   **Conflation of Neural Models with Heuristics:** The models for answer selection are not purely neural; they rely on a strong BM25 baseline to pre-filter candidates to the top 20 and use the BM25 score as a direct input to the final MLP. This makes the system a re-ranker rather than a full retrieval model, a crucial distinction that is downplayed. The use of hand-crafted boolean features (capitalization, number, n-gram overlap) as input also complicates the narrative of learning "universal semantic comprehension" from raw text.

3.  **Insufficient Detail in Key Experiments:** The transfer learning experiment, a cornerstone of the paper's argument, is described with insufficient clarity.
    *   The `Ubu.RNN` model used for transfer had a "dot-product scorer and smaller dimensionality," a different configuration from the primary RNN model described in Section 3.2. This change is not justified and makes the comparison to other models less direct.
    *   The crucial control experiment mentioned in footnote 15—training this specific RNN configuration from scratch—is dismissed in a single sentence as being "worse than the baseline RNN configuration." This result is important enough to warrant a proper place and discussion in the main results table.
    *   The switch from Adam to RMSprop for fine-tuning is justified with the vague claim that Adam's schedule "might not be appropriate," which requires more substantiation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify your hyperparameter tuning strategy? Were the parameters optimized on `yodaqa/large2470` applied without change to all other datasets, including SNLI and Ubuntu Dialogue?
2.  Can you quantify the performance impact of the BM25 pre-filtering and the hand-crafted boolean features in your answer selection experiments? How do the pure neural models perform without these components?
3.  Regarding the transfer learning experiment in Section 5: Why was a different RNN architecture (smaller dimensionality, dot-product scorer) used for this experiment compared to the main RNN model? Could you please provide the full results for the control experiment (training this model from scratch) mentioned in footnote 15, so a clearer comparison can be made?
