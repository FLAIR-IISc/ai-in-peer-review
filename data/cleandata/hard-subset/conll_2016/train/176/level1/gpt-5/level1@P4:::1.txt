Summary
The paper argues for a unified view of multiple sentence-pair tasks (answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, STS, paraphrase) as instances of a single f2(s0, s1) scoring problem. It implements a shared evaluation framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) with task adapters and task-independent neural encoders (avg, DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, attention), compares them against IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25), and proposes improved reporting practices (multiple seeds, confidence intervals). It introduces two new datasets for answer sentence selection (yodaqa/large2470) and a property selection dataset (wqmprop). Finally, it explores cross-task transfer by pretraining an RNN on Ubuntu dialogue ranking and fine-tuning on other tasks.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Unified evaluation framework with standardized data loaders, model adapters, and reporting (multi-run with CIs), plus release of code. This primarily improves comparability and reproducibility across f2 tasks.
2) New datasets for answer sentence selection (yodaqa/large2470) and property selection (wqmprop), designed to be larger/more challenging and unrestricted for research use.
3) Empirical evidence that cross-task transfer (pretraining on Ubuntu dialogue) improves performance on other f2 tasks with simple architectures.

Strengths
- Unification and reproducibility: The framework spans multiple tasks with shared encoders and task-specific scoring layers. The code release and explicit reporting of 16-run averages with 95% confidence intervals address common reproducibility gaps, especially in settings (e.g., wang) with high run-to-run variance. This is methodologically solid and practically useful for future comparisons.
- Strong baselines and consistent evaluation: Inclusion of TF-IDF/BM25 and integration of overlap features recognize the competitiveness of IR-style methods in sentence selection. The paper also explains instability in small/heterogeneous splits and motivates reporting MRR/MAP with uncertainty, which improves the soundness of conclusions.
- New datasets that better stress models: The yodaqa/large2470 dataset appears to have larger candidate sets and lower baseline scores than wang, providing a more discriminative testbed. This is valuable given known issues with small, unstable candidate pools in wang.
- Breadth of model coverage with clear design trade-offs: The study compares several commonly used architectures (avg/DAN/CNN/RNN/RNN-CNN/attention) under a single setup, highlighting trends (e.g., CNNs for smaller data, RNNs for larger data; attention helps answer selection). While not novel architecturally, the cross-task comparison is informative.
- Transfer learning evidence: Pretraining on Ubuntu next-utterance ranking and fine-tuning on other tasks yields consistent gains for the transferred RNN relative to task-only training. This supports the paper’s central hypothesis that f2 encoders can learn transferable text comprehension signals.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: The architectures and attention mechanism are taken from prior work with minor adjustments. The main novelty lies in engineering the framework and datasets, not in proposing new algorithms or theoretical insights. This limits the research contribution beyond resource and methodology consolidation.
- Potential evaluation confounds in answer selection: The approach prunes candidates to the top 20 using BM25 and feeds the BM25 score into the MLP. While realistic for IR pipelines, this alters the distribution of training/evaluation examples and conflates neural modeling with a strong lexical prior. It also hinders fair comparisons to methods that do not prefilter or do not use BM25 features as inputs. Ablations on the impact of preselection and BM25-as-feature are missing.
- Dataset construction and leakage risks insufficiently quantified: The paper acknowledges shared ancestry and possible overlap between wang and yodaqa/large2470, advising not to mix training/evaluation across them. However, it does not quantify overlap or demonstrate that splits are cleanly separated across datasets and within them. Without precise leakage analysis, reported gains and cross-task transfer could be overestimated.
- Incomplete and uneven empirical evidence: Many claims (e.g., “establishes the new baseline” on Ubuntu) are not accompanied by explicit numbers or statistical comparisons to the strongest published results. For RTE/STS, the models lag behind SOTA; for some tasks (e.g., broader STS/paraphrase), results are omitted entirely. The transfer-learning section uses a different RNN configuration (“Ubu. RNN”) than the baseline RNN; it is unclear if improvements stem from pretraining or hyperparameter changes.
- Clarity and presentation issues: Numerous typographical errors, incomplete references, and missing/uncited figures/tables weaken clarity. Important implementation details (e.g., tokenization, max lengths, optimizer schedules per task, learning rate, exact hyperparameter grids, label balancing procedure, and sample sizes for reported CIs) are under-specified. The rationale for extreme dropout (p=0.8) and its effect on convergence is not systematically analyzed.

Questions to Authors
- Dataset overlaps: What is the exact overlap (questions/sentences) between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across train/dev/test splits? Can you provide a leakage analysis and, if needed, revised splits guaranteeing no cross-dataset contamination?
- Answer selection protocol: How sensitive are results to BM25 prefiltering (e.g., no prefilter, top-50)? What is the incremental effect of including BM25 as an MLP input vs not? Please provide ablations to quantify these effects.
- Transfer learning fairness: For Ubu. RNN, please report the same architecture trained from scratch on each target task and compare to the pretrained variant with identical hyperparameters. Which layers are transferred, and what are the exact parameter counts? Do you observe similar gains when transferring from other large f2 datasets?
- Reporting detail: Please provide the full numeric results (means and 95% CIs) for all tasks, including Ubuntu recall@k/MRR and comparisons to the strongest published baselines, as well as the number of runs used per estimate.
- Data release: Will yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop be released with fixed splits, candidate lists, and evaluation scripts to ensure comparability? Are there any licensing or preprocessing constraints?

Overall, the paper’s core value is in standardizing evaluation across sentence-pair tasks, releasing code and datasets, and highlighting the benefits of robust reporting and transfer learning. The methodological contributions are solid, but the modeling novelty is limited, and some evaluation choices and dataset issues require clarification and ablation to strengthen the conclusions.
