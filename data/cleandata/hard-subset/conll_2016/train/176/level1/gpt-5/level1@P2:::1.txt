Summary and contributions
This paper studies “Sentence Pair Scoring” as a unifying view over multiple pairwise sentence tasks (answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, STS, paraphrase). It contributes primarily:
1) A unified experimental framework and methodology for f2-type tasks: shared model interfaces, adapters for multiple datasets, open-source code, and improved evaluation practices (multiple random restarts, confidence intervals, strong IR baselines).
2) New datasets for answer sentence selection (yodaqa/large2470) and property selection (wqmprop) intended to address shortcomings of wang (small size, unstable metrics).
3) A broad, controlled empirical study of several neural architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, simple attention) across tasks, including initial cross-task transfer learning (pretrain on Ubuntu Next Utterance Ranking, fine-tune on others), suggesting some task-agnostic benefits.

These are primarily empirical/resource/methodological contributions rather than novel models.

Strengths
- Methodological rigor and reproducibility emphasis: The paper highlights and quantifies seed variance (e.g., wang test MRR SD ≈ 0.025) and responds by averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs. This is a valuable corrective given the small gaps among prior reported SOTA, and it directly improves the credibility and comparability of results. Including strong IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) and ensembling/pruning reflects realistic IR-centric setups.
- Unified, multi-task perspective with open-source tooling: Providing dataset loaders, task adapters, and a Keras-based library lowers barriers to cross-task comparisons and replication, which the field lacked at the time due to fragmented pipelines. This has clear broader impact even if models themselves are standard.
- New, more challenging datasets for answer sentence selection: The large2470 dataset addresses concrete weaknesses of wang (few candidates, small dev/test, unstable rank metrics). Baselines are shown to be lower, making headroom for future improvements. This is significant for a widely used task where existing benchmarks had limitations.
- Cross-task empirical insights and transfer learning signal: The observation that CNNs fare better on small datasets while RNNs improve with scale is useful guidance. Attention mechanisms help most on IR-like answer selection. Transfer from Ubuntu-trained RNNs consistently outperforms task-only training on several targets, supporting the “universal f2” hypothesis in a preliminary but promising way.

Weaknesses
- Limited technical novelty in models: The architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, simple attention) are replications/variants of known approaches. The main novelty lies in unification, datasets, and evaluation practice. This is acceptable for a resource/methodology paper but constrains scientific originality claims.
- Dataset construction and overlap concerns insufficiently quantified: The large2470 dataset is auto-labeled by matching gold answers in passages; this risks false negatives/positives and bias toward lexical match. The authors note possible overlap/ancestry with wang and caution against mixing, but do not rigorously quantify overlap or leakage nor detail de-duplication across train/dev/test. Without these details, the reliability and independence of evaluations are uncertain.
- Coupling to IR features and pruning complicates attribution: Using BM25 both as a feature to the MLP scorer and as a top-20 pruning mechanism can dominate performance and obscure the incremental contribution of neural models. There is no ablation quantifying how much each component (BM25 as input, pruning, token overlap flags) contributes to gains, nor analysis of how often pruning drops true positives.
- Evaluation coverage versus SOTA: The paper “establishes a baseline” on Ubuntu and reports lagging behind SOTA on RTE/STS, but does not compare against the strongest contemporary models per task (e.g., more competitive attention/ESIM-style models for SNLI, stronger answer selection CNN/LSTM hybrids). While CIs are commendable, the lack of head-to-head, per-task tuned comparisons limits conclusions about the approach’s competitiveness.
- Clarity and completeness: The writing contains formatting errors and incomplete references; several implementation and training details (exact hyperparameters per task, early stopping criteria given weak dev-test correlation, preprocessing specifics) are scattered. Transfer learning is shown only from one source (Ubuntu) and one configuration, with minimal ablation (e.g., freezing layers, impact of dropout mismatch is noted but not systematically probed).

Questions to authors
- Datasets: Please provide precise statistics for yodaqa/large2470 (number of questions, candidate set distribution per question, positives per query, dev/test sizes), annotation procedure quality checks, and an estimate of false negative rate from answer-string matching. Quantify overlap with wang (at question and sentence levels) and confirm no cross-split leakage.
- BM25 pruning and features: What fraction of gold positives is pruned by the top-20 BM25 heuristic on train/dev/test? How sensitive are results to the cutoff (e.g., 20 vs 50)? Provide ablations: (a) without BM25 as a feature; (b) without pruning; (c) without token overlap flags.
- Transfer learning: Report more details on the Ubuntu-pretrained configuration (hidden sizes, training steps/epochs, optimizer parameters), and add ablations (freezing lower layers vs full fine-tuning, effect of source-task dropout schedule). Did you try other sources (e.g., SNLI) or multi-task joint training?
- Variance and selection: Given weak dev-test correlation on answer selection, how are epochs selected? Could you report fixed-epoch results to complement best-dev selection and avoid implicit dev overfitting?
- Comparisons: Can you include stronger per-task baselines (e.g., ESIM-style RTE/STS models available at submission time) and replicate their variance-aware reporting for at least one benchmark to contextualize performance?

Overall assessment
This paper meets the reviewing criteria well on significance (practical impact of unified framework, datasets, and evaluation methodology) and soundness (variance-aware evaluation, strong baselines), but is weaker on novelty (models) and on clarity/completeness of dataset creation and experimental ablations. If the datasets and code are indeed released with sufficient documentation and the overlap/pruning issues are clarified, I lean toward weak accept as a valuable resource and empirical study. Without those assurances and ablations, it would be borderline.
