Summary and contributions
This paper proposes to unify a broad family of sentence pair problems under a single “Sentence Pair Scoring” framework f2(s0,s1) and to study task-independent neural models with task-specific adapters. The authors:
- Implement and release an open-source framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) to load multiple datasets, run a common set of models, and report statistically robust results (averages over 16 runs with 95% CIs), alongside strong IR baselines.
- Introduce new datasets for answer sentence selection: yodaqa/large2470 (candidate passages from Wikipedia) and wqmprop (Freebase property selection), designed to address shortcomings of existing resources.
- Provide cross-task benchmarking of standard neural architectures (avg/DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, a simple attention model) and demonstrate transfer learning, notably that an RNN trained on Ubuntu Dialogue transfers well to other tasks.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A unified evaluation framework with stronger methodology, including IR baselines and statistical reporting across runs.
2) New, larger and arguably more realistic answer selection datasets.
3) Empirical evidence that cross-task transfer (Ubuntu→others) improves performance.

Strengths
- Methodological rigor and reproducibility: The paper tackles a real problem in our community—noisy single-run reporting on small datasets—by averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs. The authors also include strong non-neural IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) and explicitly discuss the instability of common benchmarks (e.g., MRR variance on wang exceeding typical SOTA gaps). This is valuable for the field and directly addresses evaluation pitfalls.
- Open-source framework and task unification: Providing dataset loaders, model building blocks, and a common interface in Keras lowers the barrier to entry and encourages comparable, multi-task experimentation. This can have outsized community impact relative to incremental model innovation.
- New datasets designed to expose headroom: The yodaqa/large2470 set has more candidates per question and lower baseline performance, making gains more detectable than on wang. The wqmprop resource connects QA with knowledge-base relation selection. These fill gaps in the public, redistributable space, given licensing issues of WikiQA/InsuranceQA.
- Cross-task transfer results: Training on Ubuntu Dialogue and fine-tuning yields consistent gains over task-specific training for multiple tasks, suggesting the viability of more universal sentence-pair models and seeding future multi-task work.
- Breadth of empirical study: The paper benchmarks several common architectures under a single setup and surfaces trends (e.g., CNNs better on small datasets, RNNs on larger ones; attention helpful in IR-like settings), which are practically useful insights.

Weaknesses
- Limited model novelty: The architectures are standard (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, a simple one-sided attention). The paper contributes little in algorithmic advances. Given CoNLL’s emphasis on learning, the novelty lies mostly in resources and evaluation practice.
- “Task-independent” claim is undermined by task-specific features and pruning: For answer selection, the models (a) prune candidates to top-20 by BM25 and (b) feed BM25 as an input feature; they also augment tokens with overlap/capitalization/number flags. While pragmatic, this deviates from a pure f2 formulation and conflates learning with an IR pre-filter. The paper does not quantify the impact of these choices (e.g., recall of the top-20 filter) nor provide ablations showing what the neural model learns without these aids.
- Dataset quality and leakage concerns insufficiently addressed: The authors acknowledge possible overlaps between yodaqa and wang (even across train/test), but do not quantify overlap or enforce leak-free splits. The automatic labeling of yodaqa via answer-string matching in retrieved passages can introduce noise and lexical bias; the paper lacks an analysis of label noise, inter-annotator agreement, or error typology. For wqmprop, it is unclear how natural the language of property labels is and how balanced/ambiguous the candidates are.
- Underdeveloped baselines on RTE/STS and missing stronger models: The models “clearly lag behind state-of-the-art” on SNLI/SICK and STS, but the paper stops short of including established stronger entailment architectures (e.g., serial attention/conditional encoding; Tree-LSTM; more modern attention mechanisms) that were contemporaneous. This limits the conclusions about the generality of the proposed framework on inference-oriented tasks.
- Training and reporting choices raise questions: Heavy dropout (p=0.8) and early overfitting with “rarely use a model from later than a couple of epochs” suggest suboptimal training; hyperparameter search is described as primarily guided by large2470, but it is unclear how much tuning transfers vs. per-task adjustments. The use of only 1M training pairs on Ubuntu may underutilize that dataset. Important ablations (e.g., token flags, BM25 feature, projection vs. dot-product, with/without dropout) are not reported, making it hard to isolate what drives gains.
- Presentation clarity: The abstract is truncated, some references are malformed, and footnotes bleed into the main text. Dataset statistics and model hyperparameters are scattered; clearer tables with core settings and consistent metrics (including the proposed MRR on Ubuntu) would help.

Questions to authors
- Datasets: Will yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop be fully released with clear licenses and fixed train/dev/test splits? Please quantify any overlap with wang (question and sentence level) and, if present, provide leak-free alternative splits.
- BM25 pruning and features: What is the recall@20 of the BM25 pre-filter on your new datasets? How do results change without pruning and without BM25 as a feature? Please provide ablations for token-level overlap/capitalization/number flags.
- Transfer learning: Which layers are transferred and which are reinitialized? Could you report exact numbers (mean±CI) for the Ubu.RNN vs. the best non-transferred counterpart on each target task? Did you try transfer from SNLI to other tasks as a contrast to Ubuntu?
- Training details: What is the hyperparameter search protocol across tasks (ranges, budgets, selection criteria)? How sensitive are results to dropout rates and optimizer choice? Why train on only 1/4 of data per epoch for answer selection, and how does that affect convergence?
- Dataset quality: For yodaqa, what is the estimated label noise introduced by string-matching gold answers? Any manual validation or IAA? For wqmprop, how many properties per question on average, and how often are multiple properties acceptable (ambiguity)?

Overall, this is a useful resource and methodology paper with solid empirical breadth and promising transfer results, but its claims of task independence are diluted by IR-specific engineering, and important dataset and ablation details are missing. Strengthening the evaluation (ablations, leakage quantification, recall of pre-filter) and clarifying dataset quality would significantly improve the paper.
