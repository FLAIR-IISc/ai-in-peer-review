Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes “Sentence Pair Scoring” as a unified framing for multiple semantic similarity tasks (answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, STS, paraphrase), and evaluates a family of standard neural architectures and IR baselines across datasets within a single open-source framework. It also introduces two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop) and argues for rigorous evaluation with confidence intervals and multiple random seeds, and demonstrates cross-task transfer from Ubuntu Dialogue to other tasks.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A unified, open-source evaluation framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) with improved methodology (multiple seeds, CIs, strong IR baselines), plus two new datasets that expose harder retrieval conditions.
2) An empirical cross-task benchmark showing that simple transfer learning (pretraining on Ubuntu Dialogue) consistently improves performance across tasks.
3) Practical insights on model scaling and task fit (e.g., CNNs outperform on small data, RNNs on large; attention helps IR-style tasks), establishing competitive, transparent baselines.

Strengths
- Methodological rigor and reproducibility: Reporting results over 16 seeds with 95% CIs addresses pervasive variance issues and sets a higher bar for evaluation; inclusion of strong IR baselines prevents illusory gains.
- Useful infrastructure and resources: The framework and new datasets will likely lower barriers for fair comparisons, and the larger, noisier retrieval settings are closer to practical QA scenarios.
- Transfer learning evidence: Clear, consistent gains from Ubuntu-pretrained RNNs substantiate the feasibility of task-agnostic sentence-pair representations.
- Honest, cross-task analysis: The paper surfaces when simple IR is strong and when attention matters, offering actionable guidance for future model selection.

Weaknesses
- Limited model novelty: The architectures are standard (GRU/CNN/RNN-CNN/attention) and the attention variant is adapted from prior work; the technical novelty lies mostly in evaluation and framing.
- Gap to state of the art on RTE/STS and some tasks, weakening claims of universal modeling.
- Potential data leakage: Overlap between wang and yodaqa may contaminate comparisons; precautions are discussed but not quantified.
- Dependence on BM25 pruning and overlap features undermines the “task-independent” claim and complicates fairness when comparing to IR baselines.
- Missing ablations: No systematic analysis of token-overlap features, BM25 pruning thresholds, or dropout/regularization choices driving overfitting.

Questions to Authors
- Can you quantify train/test overlap between wang and yodaqa and redo critical comparisons excluding overlaps?
- Will you release the datasets and exact scripts used to compute CIs and multiple-seed runs?
- What ablations isolate the impact of BM25 pruning and token overlap features on each task?
- For transfer, which layers were reused, and are the improvements statistically significant vs. strong single-task baselines across seeds?
