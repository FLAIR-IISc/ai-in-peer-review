## Review for Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension

This paper presents a comprehensive review of the sentence pair scoring task, framing it as a unifying problem across diverse NLP areas like question answering, textual entailment, and dialogue systems. The authors identify key challenges in current research – fragmentation, inconsistent evaluation, and lack of task-independent models – and propose a unified framework, new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and a thorough evaluation of existing and baseline models. 

The primary contribution of this work is the creation and release of the *yodaqa/large2470* dataset.  Existing answer selection datasets suffer from issues of size and unevenness, and this new dataset appears to address those concerns, as evidenced by the lower baseline performance (which indicates a harder problem). The paper also provides a valuable, systematic evaluation of various neural network architectures (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) across multiple datasets using a standardized framework. Finally, the paper’s emphasis on rigorous evaluation, including reporting confidence intervals and comparisons to strong IR baselines, is commendable and elevates the methodological standard for this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The introduction of *yodaqa/large2470* is a significant contribution. The documented shortcomings of existing datasets and the detailed description of the data creation process lend credibility to its value. The lower baseline performance suggests a more challenging and representative dataset, which is crucial for driving progress.
2. **Unified Framework and Evaluation:**  The paper's attempt to unify diverse tasks under the f2(s0, s1) formulation is conceptually valuable. The standardized evaluation setup, comparing neural models against IR baselines and reporting confidence intervals, provides a robust basis for comparison and future research. This level of methodological rigor is often lacking in the field.
3. **Comprehensive Model Evaluation:**  The authors explore a wide range of neural network architectures, offering a valuable comparative analysis of their performance across different tasks. This provides useful insights into the strengths and weaknesses of various approaches. 
4. **Open-Source Framework:** The release of the “dataset-sts” and “KeraSTS” libraries facilitates reproducibility and encourages further research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the evaluation is thorough, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs, attention) are not novel. The paper primarily *applies* existing architectures to a unified framework rather than proposing new ones. This reduces the paper's impact on the modeling side of the field.
2. **Transfer Learning Results are Limited:** The initial transfer learning experiment, while promising, lacks depth.  A more extensive exploration of different transfer learning strategies and hyperparameters is needed to substantiate the claim that truly "universal" models are feasible.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn’t include a detailed ablation study for each model to understand which components contribute the most to performance. This information would be helpful for guiding future model development.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the data curation process for *yodaqa/large2470*, particularly the criteria used by the Mechanical Turk workers for labeling?
2. The paper mentions that training can overfit quickly. What specific regularization techniques (beyond L2) were explored, and why were they not adopted?
3.  Can you provide more details about the choice of hyperparameters for each model on the *yodaqa/large2470* dataset? Was a formal hyperparameter optimization procedure used?
