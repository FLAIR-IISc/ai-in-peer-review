This paper presents a survey and unification framework for sentence pair scoring (f2) tasks, encompassing Answer Sentence Selection, Semantic Textual Similarity, Next Utterance Ranking, and Recognizing Textual Entailment. The authors identify a fragmentation in research across these tasks and aim to establish a common ground for evaluation and model development. They introduce new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations in existing ones, and evaluate a range of neural and non-neural baselines within their unified framework, demonstrating some success with transfer learning.

**Contributions:**

1. **Unified Framework & Benchmarking:** The primary contribution is the proposal of a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across diverse tasks. This is valuable as it addresses the issue of inconsistent evaluation methodologies and facilitates more meaningful comparisons between models. The comprehensive evaluation of several baselines within this framework provides a strong starting point for future research.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop datasets is a significant contribution. The authors clearly articulate the shortcomings of existing datasets (e.g., small size, uneven distribution, noisy labels in wang) and address them with these new resources. The larger scale and better characteristics of yodaqa/large2470 are particularly promising.
3. **Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** The authors demonstrate the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning, showing that a model trained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset can improve performance on other tasks. This supports the hypothesis of underlying universal semantic comprehension models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Real Problem:** The paper tackles a genuine issue in the NLP community – the lack of a standardized evaluation framework for sentence pair scoring tasks. The current fragmented landscape hinders progress and makes it difficult to assess the true capabilities of different models.
2. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of a diverse set of models, ranging from simple IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) to more complex neural architectures (RNNs, CNNs, Attention-based models).  The inclusion of confidence intervals in the results (due to inherent randomness in neural models) is commendable and strengthens the validity of the findings.
3. **High-Quality Datasets:** The newly introduced datasets, especially yodaqa/large2470, are well-motivated and appear to be valuable resources for the community. The authors provide a clear rationale for their creation and highlight the advantages over existing datasets.
4. **Open-Source Framework:** The release of the `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` frameworks as open-source software greatly enhances the reproducibility of the results and facilitates further research by other researchers.
5. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, methodology, and results. The organization is logical, and the figures and tables are informative.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the framework and datasets are significant contributions, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) are not particularly novel. The authors primarily focus on applying existing architectures to the unified framework, rather than proposing entirely new model designs.
2. **Transfer Learning Results Could be Stronger:** The transfer learning results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing. The performance gains are not substantial across all tasks, and further investigation is needed to understand the conditions under which transfer learning is most effective. The authors also mention needing to avoid dropout during re-training, which hints at fragility in the transfer process.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors acknowledge that their models lag behind the state-of-the-art on some tasks (RTE, STS). While establishing a baseline is valuable, a more direct comparison to the most recent and advanced models would have strengthened the paper’s impact. This is especially true for tasks with existing strong performing models.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While mentioning tuning primarily on yodaqa/large2470, more details on the specific hyperparameters used for each model and task would have been beneficial for reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the reasons why the Ubu. RNN model performs particularly well as a source model for transfer learning? What specific characteristics of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset contribute to this effect?
2. You mention the importance of avoiding dropout during transfer learning. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case, and whether there are any techniques to mitigate this issue?
3.  Given the limitations of the models compared to the state-of-the-art on some tasks, what are the next steps you envision for improving model performance within your unified framework? Are there specific architectural modifications or training strategies that you plan to explore?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The use of a consistent framework and the introduction of new datasets are substantial contributions to the field.
* The paper's focus on reproducibility through open-source tools is highly commendable.
* The authors have done a good job of identifying and addressing a critical issue in the NLP research landscape.
