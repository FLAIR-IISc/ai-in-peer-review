This paper presents a comprehensive review of the sentence pair scoring task, framing it as a unifying task (f2) for various NLP problems. The authors aim to provide a unified framework for research, improve evaluation methodology, and demonstrate the feasibility of task-independent semantic comprehension models. The main contributions, as I see them, are: 1) A well-defined and motivated framing of a broad range of NLP tasks as sentence pair scoring, enabling comparative analysis; 2) The introduction of two new datasets – yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop – designed to address shortcomings of existing datasets like wang; and 3) A thorough empirical evaluation of various neural network architectures and baselines across multiple tasks, including a demonstration of cross-task transfer learning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Unified Framework & Task Definition:** The central idea of reframing diverse tasks (answer selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, STS) as sentence pair scoring (f2) is insightful and valuable. This allows for a more direct comparison of models and encourages the development of general-purpose semantic understanding systems, rather than task-specific solutions. The authors effectively argue for the benefits of this perspective.
2. **Novel Datasets:** The introduction of `yodaqa/large2470` is a significant contribution. The authors clearly identify limitations in the widely used `wang` dataset (small size, uneven data, noisy validation splits) and attempt to address them with a larger, more realistically complex dataset. The creation process, leveraging the YodaQA system, is well-explained.  `wqmprop` also contributes to broader coverage of f2 tasks.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors perform an extensive evaluation of numerous baselines (TF-IDF, BM25, avg embedding, DAN, RNN, CNN, attention-based models) across a diverse set of datasets. This provides a strong empirical foundation for future research. The inclusion of 95% confidence intervals is commendable, acknowledging the inherent randomness in neural network training and providing a more robust assessment of model performance.
4. **Demonstrated Transfer Learning:** The positive results from transferring a model trained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset to other tasks provide promising evidence for the feasibility of learning task-independent semantic representations. This supports the core hypothesis of the paper.
5. **Open-Source Framework:**  The authors’ development and release of `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` facilitates reproducibility and further research in this area. This commitment to open science is a significant strength.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the evaluation is thorough, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs, attention) are relatively standard.  The paper doesn’t introduce fundamentally new architectural innovations. The focus is primarily on *applying* existing models to a unified framework and evaluating their performance, rather than proposing groundbreaking new architectures. This is not necessarily a flaw, but it limits the paper's contribution to the modeling space.
2. **Dataset Detail and Analysis:** While the authors describe the creation of `yodaqa/large2470`, more details regarding the filtering criteria applied during the retrieval from Wikipedia and the quality control process for labeling would be beneficial. A more in-depth analysis of the dataset's characteristics beyond the summary statistics in Fig. 1 is needed. What kinds of errors are most common in this dataset?
3. **Transfer Learning Depth:** The transfer learning experiment is encouraging, but somewhat limited in scope.  Only one model configuration (RNN with dot-product scorer) was used for transfer. Exploring different architectures and transfer strategies (e.g., freezing different layers) could provide more insight into the effectiveness of transfer learning. A more detailed ablation study to understand *why* transfer learning works would be valuable.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  The authors state that hyperparameters were “primarily tuned on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset.” While understandable, it's unclear how well these hyperparameters generalize to the other tasks. A more systematic hyperparameter tuning procedure across all datasets might improve overall performance and comparability.
5. **Results Presentation:** The tables with results are dense and difficult to quickly parse.  Highlighting the best-performing models for each task and including statistical significance tests (beyond confidence intervals) would improve clarity.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail regarding the quality control process used during the creation of the `yodaqa/large2470` dataset? What measures were taken to ensure label accuracy?
2.  Why was only the RNN with dot-product scorer used for the transfer learning experiment? Were other configurations explored, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Were any systematic methods used to ensure the hyperparameters tuned on `yodaqa/large2470` were reasonably effective on the other tasks, or were they used as-is?
4.  You mention the potential for jointly training models on multiple tasks. Did you explore any preliminary experiments in this direction? If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
* The references are comprehensive.
* The URL for the GitHub repository should be included in the final version of the paper (currently redacted).
* It would be interesting to see a discussion of the computational cost associated with the different models and datasets.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes valuable contributions to the field of semantic text understanding. While the models themselves are not novel, the unified framework, the new datasets, and the comprehensive evaluation provide a significant step forward in our understanding of sentence pair scoring and its potential for building general-purpose NLP systems. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
