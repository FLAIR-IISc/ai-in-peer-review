This paper presents a comprehensive review of the "Sentence Pair Scoring" task, framing it as a unified f2-type problem applicable across diverse NLP tasks like answer selection, textual entailment, and next utterance ranking. The authors identify key challenges in the field – fragmented research, inconsistent evaluation, and a lack of universally applicable models – and aim to address them by providing a unified framework (dataset-sts), strong baselines, new datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop), and demonstrating the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Unified Framework and Dataset Collection (dataset-sts):** The creation and release of the `dataset-sts` package, integrating multiple datasets and providing tools for model building and evaluation, is the most significant contribution. This directly addresses the problem of fragmented research and inconsistent evaluation by providing a common platform.
2. **New Datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop):** Introducing `yodaqa/large2470` tackles the limitations of the existing Wang dataset (small size, uneven distribution) and offers a more robust evaluation benchmark for answer sentence selection. `wqmprop` provides a unique property selection task, expanding the range of f2-type tasks studied.
3. **Empirical Demonstration of Cross-Task Transfer Learning:** The authors successfully demonstrate that models pre-trained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset can be effectively transferred to other tasks, supporting the hypothesis of universal semantic comprehension models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Survey & Problem Definition:** The paper provides a thorough overview of the f2-type tasks and clearly articulates the need for a unified approach. This is valuable for researchers entering the field.
2. **Strong Baseline Implementation & Evaluation:**  The inclusion of strong IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) alongside neural models is crucial for establishing a meaningful comparison and demonstrates the performance gap. The thorough evaluation methodology, including reporting confidence intervals, significantly strengthens the results.
3. **Practical Tool Release:**  The release of `dataset-sts` is a highly valuable contribution to the community, enabling reproducibility and further research. It is well documented and provides a readily usable resource.
4. **Addressing Dataset Issues:** The critical analysis of the Wang dataset's shortcomings and the subsequent development of `yodaqa/large2470` are impactful improvements. A larger, better-balanced dataset is essential for reliable model evaluation.
5. **Demonstrated Transfer Learning Success:** The positive results from cross-task transfer learning provide evidence supporting the core hypothesis of the paper – that learning a universal semantic representation is feasible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Model Architectures:** The models used (RNN, CNN, attention) are relatively standard and lack significant architectural innovations. While thoroughly evaluated, the primary contribution isn’t in novel model design, but in the framework and datasets.
2. **Performance Relative to State-of-the-Art:** The paper acknowledges lagging behind state-of-the-art on RTE and STS, and while comparisons are made, a deeper analysis of *why* the proposed models underperform would be beneficial. Is it dataset-specific, architectural limitations, or hyperparameter tuning?
3. **Focus on Ranking Tasks:** While the paper aims for a unified framework, the evaluation heavily emphasizes ranking-based metrics (MAP, MRR).  Exploring other evaluation metrics more suitable for different tasks (e.g., Pearson's r for STS) could broaden the applicability of the framework.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn’t include detailed ablation studies to analyze the contribution of individual components (e.g., BM25 integration, dropout) to the overall performance. Understanding the impact of these choices would be valuable.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks a detailed error analysis to understand the types of errors the models make and guide future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational resources required to reproduce the experiments, particularly concerning training time and memory requirements for the larger datasets?
2.  You mention the limitations of the existing models on RTE and STS. Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of the specific challenges encountered in these tasks and potential directions for improvement within your framework?
3. What are the long-term maintenance plans for the `dataset-sts` package, specifically regarding dataset updates and the addition of new datasets?
4. In the introduction, you state an argument for preferring f2 to f1. Could you provide more concrete examples illustrating scenarios where an f2 formulation offers a significant advantage over an f1 approach in terms of learning semantic relationships?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The inclusion of a table summarizing the key characteristics of the datasets is helpful.
* The reference list is comprehensive.
* It would be beneficial to include a link to the `dataset-sts` package in the abstract.
