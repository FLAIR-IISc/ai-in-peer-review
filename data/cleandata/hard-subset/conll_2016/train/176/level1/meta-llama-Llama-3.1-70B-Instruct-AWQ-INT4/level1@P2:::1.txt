**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring, which encompasses various natural language processing tasks such as answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, recognizing textual entailment, and semantic textual similarity. The authors argue that these tasks can be viewed as a single problem of scoring the semantic similarity between two sentences. They introduce a new dataset, yodaqa/large2470, and evaluate several neural network models, including recurrent neural networks (RNNs), convolutional neural networks (CNNs), and attention-based models, on multiple tasks. The results show that the models can be effectively transferred across tasks, and the authors demonstrate the feasibility of pursuing universal, task-independent models.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Unified framework for sentence pair scoring**: The authors provide a comprehensive framework that unifies various NLP tasks under a single problem of scoring semantic similarity between sentence pairs.
2. **New dataset and evaluation methodology**: The authors introduce a new dataset, yodaqa/large2470, and propose a new evaluation methodology that reports model performance averaged across multiple training runs with different seeds.
3. **Transfer learning across tasks**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of transfer learning across tasks, showing that models trained on one task can be fine-tuned and achieve competitive performance on other tasks.

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive framework**: The authors provide a thorough and well-structured framework that unifies various NLP tasks, making it easier to compare and evaluate different models.
2. **New dataset and evaluation methodology**: The introduction of a new dataset and evaluation methodology provides a more comprehensive understanding of model performance and generalizability.
3. **Transfer learning results**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of transfer learning across tasks, which has significant implications for the development of more generalizable NLP models.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited model comparisons**: While the authors evaluate several neural network models, they do not provide a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art models, such as transformer-based models.
2. **Lack of detailed analysis**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the results, including error analysis and ablation studies, which would help to better understand the strengths and weaknesses of the models.
3. **Overemphasis on transfer learning**: While transfer learning is an important aspect of the paper, the authors may have overemphasized its importance, potentially overlooking other critical factors that contribute to model performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art models, including transformer-based models?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting, which is a common problem in neural network models?
3. Can you provide more insight into the transfer learning process, including the specific layers and weights that are transferred across tasks?

**Additional Comments**

The paper provides a comprehensive framework for sentence pair scoring and demonstrates the effectiveness of transfer learning across tasks. However, the authors could have provided more detailed analysis and comparisons with other state-of-the-art models. Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of NLP.
