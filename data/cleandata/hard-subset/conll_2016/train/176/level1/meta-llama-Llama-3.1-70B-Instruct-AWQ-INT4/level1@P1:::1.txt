**Summary**

This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring, which encompasses various natural language processing tasks such as answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, recognizing textual entailment, and semantic textual similarity. The authors argue that existing research is fragmented, and there is a need for a task-independent model that can be adapted to different tasks. They introduce a new dataset, yodaqa/large2470, and evaluate several neural network models, including recurrent neural networks, convolutional neural networks, and attention-based models, on various tasks. The results show that the models can be successfully transferred across tasks, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments.

**Contributions**

1. **Unified framework for sentence pair scoring**: The authors propose a unified framework that can be applied to various tasks, providing a common platform for model evaluation and comparison.
2. **New dataset and evaluation methodology**: The authors introduce a new dataset, yodaqa/large2470, and propose a new evaluation methodology that takes into account the randomness of neural models and provides a more robust estimate of model performance.
3. **Transfer learning across tasks**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of transfer learning across tasks, showing that models trained on one task can be successfully applied to other tasks.

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of various models on multiple tasks, providing a thorough understanding of their strengths and weaknesses.
2. **New dataset and evaluation methodology**: The introduction of a new dataset and evaluation methodology provides a fresh perspective on the task of sentence pair scoring and allows for more robust model evaluation.
3. **Transfer learning results**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of transfer learning across tasks, which has significant implications for the development of more generalizable models.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty in models**: While the authors evaluate various models, they do not propose any new models or architectures that significantly advance the state-of-the-art.
2. **Limited analysis of results**: While the authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, they do not provide a detailed analysis of the results, which would help to better understand the strengths and weaknesses of each model.
3. **Overemphasis on Ubuntu Dialogue dataset**: The authors place a significant emphasis on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, which may not be representative of all tasks and domains.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the results, including a breakdown of the performance of each model on different tasks and datasets?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting, which is a common problem in neural network models?
3. Can you provide more information on the yodaqa/large2470 dataset, including its size, composition, and any preprocessing steps that were applied?

**Additional Comments**

* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and concise language.
* The authors provide a thorough review of the literature, which helps to situate their work in the context of existing research.
* The use of a unified framework and evaluation methodology is a significant contribution to the field, as it allows for more robust model evaluation and comparison.
