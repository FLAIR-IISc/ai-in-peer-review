

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary of Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, which encompass various NLP problems including Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Semantic Textual Similarity. The authors introduce a new software framework (dataset-sts) that enables consistent implementation and evaluation of models across these tasks. They also propose two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations in existing datasets for Answer Sentence Selection, particularly regarding dataset size and candidate set balance. The authors demonstrate transfer learning across tasks, showing that a model trained on one task (Ubuntu Dialogue) can achieve significantly better performance on other tasks through weight transfer.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1. The introduction of new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) that address well-documented limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets, providing more robust evaluation benchmarks.
2. The development of a unified software framework (dataset-sts) that enables consistent evaluation across multiple sentence pair scoring tasks, improving methodological rigor in the field.
3. The demonstration of transfer learning across tasks as a promising approach for developing more general semantic comprehension models, with the Ubu.RNN model significantly outperforming baseline approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Methodological Contribution**: The paper addresses a critical fragmentation in NLP research by providing a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models. The dataset-sts framework is well-designed, open-source, and modular, making it easy for other researchers to implement and compare models across tasks. This is a substantial contribution to the field's methodology, as it enables more meaningful comparisons between models that have previously been evaluated in isolation.

2. **Careful Evaluation Methodology**: The authors demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of evaluation challenges in neural NLP. They properly address the impact of randomness in neural models by reporting confidence intervals (95% CI) rather than single-point estimates, which is a major improvement over current practice. The paper correctly identifies that standard practices (like selecting the best validation run) can lead to overfitting, and their proposed methodology provides a more reliable basis for comparing models across different datasets.

3. **Practical Dataset Improvements**: The authors correctly identify limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets (small size, uneven candidate sets, etc.) and address them with new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) that have more realistic characteristics. The paper provides compelling evidence that these new datasets offer more challenging benchmarks, with baseline performance significantly lower than on existing datasets, which will make future model improvements more apparent.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Demonstration of "Universality"**: The paper's central claim is that the framework demonstrates the feasibility of "universal semantic comprehension models," but the evidence provided is limited. The transfer learning results are promising but only cover a narrow range of tasks (Ubuntu Dialogue to Answer Sentence Selection, RTE, etc.). The paper doesn't provide evidence that the models can handle more complex semantic relationships or generalize to a wider variety of tasks. The authors claim "even simple neural models learn universal semantic comprehension," but the results don't sufficiently support this strong claim.

2. **Lack of State-of-the-Art Comparison**: The paper mentions that their models "clearly yet lag behind the state-of-art on the RTE and STS tasks" but doesn't provide detailed comparisons with current state-of-the-art approaches. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess how much their framework advances the field, or whether their methodology would actually help close the performance gap. For a paper claiming to establish a "watermark for future research," this is a significant omission.

3. **Limited Scope of Transfer Learning Demonstration**: The transfer learning results are presented as evidence for universal comprehension, but the paper doesn't explore the full potential of this approach. The authors only transfer from Ubuntu Dialogue to other tasks, but don't investigate whether transfer learning from multiple source tasks would yield even better results. They also don't explore how the framework would handle tasks with very different semantic requirements (e.g., paraphrasing vs. entailment).

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the yodaqa/large2470 and wang datasets share a common ancestry. How did you ensure there was no data leakage between these datasets during your experiments, particularly in the transfer learning experiments?

2. The paper claims the RNN-CNN model is recommended as a primary model for new f2-type tasks, but the results show that the transfer learning approach (Ubu.RNN) outperforms it. Can you clarify why the RNN-CNN is recommended over the transfer learning approach, given that transfer learning appears to yield better performance?

3. The paper mentions that "the models lag behind the state-of-art significantly" on STS and paraphrasing tasks but doesn't provide specific comparison metrics. Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art models for these tasks, to better contextualize their framework's potential?

## Additional Comments

The paper's focus on methodological rigor and practical evaluation is commendable, and the new datasets will likely be valuable for future research. The software framework (dataset-sts) is a particularly strong contribution that should be widely adopted by the community. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by more thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art models and a more nuanced discussion of the "universality" claim, rather than treating it as a demonstrated fact. The authors should also consider expanding their transfer learning experiments to include more diverse source tasks to better demonstrate the framework's potential.
