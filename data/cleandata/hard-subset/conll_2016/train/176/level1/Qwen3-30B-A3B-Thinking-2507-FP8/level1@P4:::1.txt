

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, which encompasses Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Semantic Textual Similarity. The authors introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations in existing datasets, propose a modular software framework (dataset-sts), and evaluate various neural models across these tasks. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:
1. A unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks that allows cross-task comparison and evaluation
2. New datasets that address limitations of existing datasets (size, quality, licensing)
3. A novel evaluation methodology emphasizing confidence intervals to address the high variability in neural network results

## Strengths
1. The paper successfully unifies disparate NLP tasks under a single framework, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors clearly articulate the fragmentation problem in current research and demonstrate how their framework enables meaningful cross-task comparisons. This is particularly valuable as it provides a common benchmarking platform that could accelerate future research in this area.

2. The new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) are well-motivated and address specific limitations of existing datasets. The authors provide detailed characterizations showing how their datasets solve problems with candidate set size and imbalance (Fig. 1) and demonstrate that the new datasets lead to more challenging evaluation scenarios with lower baseline performance, which will better facilitate future model improvements.

3. The proposed evaluation methodology is a strong contribution that addresses a critical issue in the field: the high variability in neural network results due to randomness in training. The authors provide empirical evidence (Fig. 2) showing that standard reporting practices (single-point estimates) can be misleading, as the standard deviation of results on the wang dataset is larger than the performance gap between successive state-of-the-art papers. Their approach of reporting confidence intervals is methodologically sound and should become a standard practice.

4. The transfer learning experiments provide meaningful evidence supporting the authors' hypothesis that models can learn universal semantic comprehension. The RNN model trained on Ubuntu Dialogue (Next Utterance Ranking) outperforms the baseline when transferred to other tasks, demonstrating the feasibility of cross-task transfer learning. This is a significant step toward the goal of developing task-independent semantic comprehension models.

5. The open-source software framework (dataset-sts) is a practical contribution that will facilitate future research by providing a modular implementation for comparing different models across different tasks. The framework's integration with Keras makes it accessible to the broader research community.

## Weaknesses
1. The novelty of the technical approach is limited. While the unification of tasks is valuable, the paper does not present a fundamentally new model architecture or learning approach. Most of the models evaluated (RNN, CNN, attention-based) have been used in similar contexts before, and the main innovation appears to be the unification and evaluation across tasks rather than a new technical contribution.

2. The evidence for "universal semantic comprehension" is insufficient. The paper claims to demonstrate that models learn universal semantic comprehension through transfer learning, but the results only show modest improvements when transferring from Ubuntu Dialogue to other tasks. The authors don't provide evidence that the models have truly learned a general semantic representation rather than just benefiting from the size of the Ubuntu dataset or specific architectural choices.

3. The paper does not sufficiently compare with the most recent state-of-the-art models for each task. The authors acknowledge that their models "clearly yet lag behind the state-of-art on the RTE and STS tasks" but don't provide detailed comparisons with current SOTA approaches. This weakens their claim that they've established a meaningful new baseline for future research.

4. The evaluation methodology, while well-motivated, lacks sufficient detail on implementation. The paper reports confidence intervals but doesn't provide enough information on how these were calculated or how they would change with different numbers of training runs. The authors also don't address how the confidence intervals would affect the interpretation of results when comparing models.

5. The paper's claim of "universal" models is undermined by the results showing different model architectures perform better on different tasks (CNN for smaller datasets, RNN for larger datasets). This suggests that the "universal" model might not be so universal, and the authors don't adequately address this contradiction in their claims.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you explain the performance differences between models across tasks (e.g., CNNs performing better on smaller datasets while RNNs perform better on larger ones)? Does this contradict your claim of "universal" semantic comprehension?

2. The transfer learning results show improvement when transferring from Ubuntu Dialogue to other tasks, but the authors note that the Ubuntu dataset is larger than most others. How do you account for the possibility that the performance gain is due to the larger size of the Ubuntu dataset rather than true transfer of semantic comprehension?

3. The paper claims to establish a new baseline for future research, but acknowledges that current models lag behind state-of-the-art on several tasks. What specific improvements do you recommend to reach parity with current SOTA approaches?
