

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks across multiple NLP applications (Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, etc.), arguing that these tasks can be treated as variations of a single underlying problem. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a unified framework for sentence pair scoring, (2) new datasets with improved characteristics (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and (3) improved evaluation methodology with statistical rigor. The most significant contribution is the unified framework and the new datasets, which address fragmentation in the field and provide better evaluation resources. The improved evaluation methodology is also substantial, though not as novel as the framework and datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Unified Framework**: The paper successfully demonstrates that diverse sentence pair scoring tasks can be treated within a single framework, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors effectively show how different tasks (Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, etc.) share the same fundamental problem structure, enabling model transfer across tasks. This addresses a real fragmentation problem in NLP research where different tasks have been treated in isolation with different datasets and metrics.

2. **New Datasets with Improved Characteristics**: The authors identify critical flaws in existing datasets (small size, uneven candidate sets, noisy evaluation metrics) and propose two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) that address these issues. The yodaqa/large2470 dataset, in particular, is well-motivated and addresses the limitations of the widely used wang dataset. The paper's analysis of dataset characteristics (Fig. 1) is thorough and demonstrates why these new datasets are superior for meaningful model evaluation.

3. **Statistically Rigorous Evaluation Methodology**: The paper makes a strong case for improved evaluation methodology by demonstrating that neural model performance varies significantly across random seeds (with standard deviation of 0.025 on the wang dataset, larger than the gap between successive papers). The authors report results with 95% confidence intervals and multiple training runs, which is a significant methodological improvement over typical NLP paper practices.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novel Model Architecture**: The paper uses standard neural network architectures (RNN, CNN, attention models) without introducing any new model components. The main contribution is the framework and datasets, not the models themselves. This is a significant weakness because the field is moving toward more innovative architectures, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why this framework is necessary if the models themselves aren't novel. The authors could have made a stronger case by introducing a new model specifically designed for the unified framework.

2. **Limited and Questionable Transfer Learning Results**: While the authors claim that transfer learning demonstrates the feasibility of "universal semantic comprehension models," their results are limited to one model (RNN) transferred from Ubuntu Dialogue to other tasks. The improvement over the baseline RNN model is marginal (e.g., "Ubu. RNN consistently ranks as the best or among the best classifiers"), and the paper doesn't explore why transfer learning works better for some models than others. The claim that "a quest for generic neural model capable of task-independent text comprehension is becoming a meaningful pursuit" is significantly overstated based on the limited evidence presented.

3. **Insufficient Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper states that "our models clearly yet lag behind the state-of-art on the RTE and STS tasks" but doesn't provide sufficient comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art models. The authors acknowledge that "it is not possible to statistically determine its relation to state-of-art on the wang Answer Sentence Selection dataset," which suggests they haven't properly compared with the current best models. This weakens their argument for the significance of their framework.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper makes a strong claim about the existence of "universal f2 type measures" but provides little theoretical justification beyond empirical evidence from limited experiments. The argument that "the other sentence in the pair is essentially a very complex label when training the sequence model" is not sufficiently supported, and the paper would benefit from more theoretical grounding for this claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "a quest for generic neural model capable of task-independent text comprehension is becoming a meaningful pursuit" based on limited transfer learning results. What theoretical or empirical evidence supports the assumption that such universal models exist, beyond the limited experiments presented?

2. The authors note that "the RNN configuration used for the transfer, when trained only on the target task, is not shown in the tables but has always been worse than the baseline RNN configuration." Why does transfer learning improve performance, and is this due to the larger training data from the source task, or is there something special about the transfer learning process?

3. The paper mentions that "we have tried freezing the weights of some layers, but this never yielded a significant improvement." Why didn't freezing layers help, and what does this imply about the nature of the transfer learning process for these tasks?

4. The authors state that "the validation set performance is not a great approximator for test set performance" and that "randomness plays a large role in neural models." Given these issues, why do they still use the validation set for early stopping rather than using a separate development set for hyperparameter tuning?
