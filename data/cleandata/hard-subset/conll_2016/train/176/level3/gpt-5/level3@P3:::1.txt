Summary
This paper proposes a unified view of sentence-pair scoring as a single semantic-relation problem f2(s0, s1), spanning tasks such as answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, and STS. The authors implement a modular evaluation framework (dataset-sts / KeraSTS), benchmark multiple neural architectures (avg, DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, attention), and emphasize robust evaluation with multiple random seeds and confidence intervals. They also report transfer learning results, showing that an RNN pretrained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset (Ubu.RNN) substantially improves performance when fine-tuned on other tasks. They introduce two new datasets for answer sentence selection/property selection and argue for stronger IR baselines and principled ensembling.

Recommendation
Accept for presentation only with major revisions. The paper is novel and promising, but feels unfinished: key tables are incomplete, a dataset promised in the abstract is not actually presented, and the analysis is too brief relative to the breadth of results.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A unified empirical framework and methodology for multi-task sentence-pair evaluation, with robust reporting (multi-run averages and CIs) and strong IR baselines.
2) Cross-task transfer learning evidence (Ubu.RNN) suggesting that some sentence-pair tasks can share a common representation.
3) New datasets and adapters intended to stress-answer sentence selection and property selection (though current presentation is incomplete; see weaknesses).

Strengths
- Clear and timely unification: Framing diverse sentence-pair tasks under a single f2 paradigm and testing shared, task-agnostic models is novel and practically valuable for the community.
- Evaluation rigor: Averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% CIs directly addresses instability in small test sets (e.g., wang). This sets a higher bar for reproducibility and comparability than many prior papers.
- Breadth of benchmarking: A wide sweep across answer selection (wang and new datasets), next-utterance ranking (Ubuntu), RTE (SNLI, SICK), and STS highlights where models generalize and where they fail.
- Strong baselines and realistic setups: Inclusion of TF-IDF/BM25 and discussion of ensembling and candidate pruning reflects realistic IR conditions and anchors neural results.
- Transfer learning result: The Ubu.RNN initialization consistently improves downstream tasks, indicating potential for a general sentence-pair encoder and motivating future multi-task learning.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete results and missing content: The first results table is incomplete. Given the paper’s goal (a watermark for future research), every model–task combination promised in the text should be reported with primary metrics and 95% CIs. Without a complete table, the main claim (cross-task comparability) is weakened.
- Dataset claim mismatch: The abstract promises a “new, more challenging dataset,” but the paper does not fully present it (statistics, splits, licensing, access link, qualitative examples) and the URL is redacted. This must be resolved with a complete dataset section (for both yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop): detailed statistics versus wang, candidate set sizes, overlap with prior datasets, licensing, and download instructions.
- Insufficient analysis of heterogeneous results: Performance varies widely across tasks and models (e.g., CNN vs RNN vs RNN-CNN, attention). The discussion is too short. The paper should analyze why models succeed/fail per task (e.g., reliance on lexical overlap, OOV/entity density, length, domain mismatch) and whether these tasks truly form a unified group or cluster into subfamilies with different inductive biases.
- Ambiguity in Ubu.RNN transfer gains: It is unclear whether gains stem from the nature of Ubuntu dialogs (generality) or simply from dataset size. Without controls (pretrain-size ablations, alternative large pretraining corpora like SNLI, matched-size subsamples), the conclusion that Ubuntu pretraining is generally preferable is premature.
- Lack of qualitative examples: There are no example instances to ground the quantitative claims. Prototypical and failure-case pairs from each dataset would help interpret results, diagnose error modes, and justify design choices (e.g., overlap features and candidate pruning).
- Paper polish and completion: Numerous typos, inconsistent references, and unclear terms suggest the paper is not camera-ready. The presentation should be finalized and tightened (e.g., consistent notation, complete figures/tables).

Questions to Authors
1) Results completeness: When will you provide the complete first results table? Please include, for each dataset: metric definitions, validation/test scores with 95% CIs across 16 runs, and the exact hyperparameters used per task.
2) Dataset presentation: For yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, please include a full dataset card: sizes, splits, candidate set statistics, average lengths, overlap rates with wang/WebQuestions, licensing, access link, and any known data leakage issues. Are there overlaps across train/test when combined with wang, and how were they prevented in all experiments?
3) Ubu.RNN transfer: Are the observed gains due to corpus size or domain characteristics? Please run:
   - Pretrain on SNLI (and on size-matched SNLI subsets) with the same architecture, then fine-tune.
   - Pretrain on Ubuntu subsets of varying sizes to derive scaling curves.
   - Control for architectural/training differences (e.g., dot-product vs MLP scorer, dropout on/off) to isolate the effect of pretraining.
4) Overlap and BM25 ablations: How much do BM25 inputs and candidate pruning drive gains? Please provide ablations removing BM25 features and pruning, and a lexical-overlap ablation (remove overlap features) to quantify model reliance on surface forms.
5) Task unification: Do you observe task clusters? Please provide a correlation matrix of model rankings across tasks and a brief taxonomy (e.g., IR-like ranking vs inference-like reasoning) with failure analyses per cluster.

Additional Comments
- Examples requested: Please add a small table of prototypical and challenging sentence pairs for each dataset, with gold labels and brief commentary.
- Polishing: Fix “weigh/weighed” to “weight/weighted,” correct multiple misspellings of “sentence,” replace “interpunction” with “punctuation,” and “world overlap” with “word overlap.” Ensure consistent citation formatting and capitalization (e.g., SemEval).
- Finalization: Standardize notation (f1/f2), ensure all figures are legible and referenced, and consolidate dataset statistics into a clear table.

Overall, this is a novel and exciting direction with strong potential impact. With complete results, fuller dataset presentation, and deeper analysis, it would make a valuable CoNLL contribution.
