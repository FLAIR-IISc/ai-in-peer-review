Summary
This paper proposes to unify a broad family of sentence-pair tasks (f2) under a single framework for text comprehension, including Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Semantic Textual Similarity. The authors implement task adapters and a suite of neural models (AVG/DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, attention), emphasize stronger evaluation methodology (multiple seeds with confidence intervals, IR baselines), and explore transfer learning by pretraining on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset and fine-tuning to other tasks. They also discuss introducing new datasets for answer selection (yodaqa/large2470) and property selection (wqmprop), and release open-source tooling (dataset-sts, KeraSTS).

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) A unified, task-agnostic evaluation framework with strong baselines and statistical reporting across diverse f2 tasks, enabling more reliable comparisons than much prior single-task reporting.
2) An empirical cross-task study with multiple neural architectures and a transfer-learning result (Ubu.RNN) showing that pretraining on a large dialog corpus improves several other f2 tasks.
3) Potential new datasets for answer and property selection, intended to address limitations of prior benchmarks (though currently under-documented; see weaknesses).

Strengths
- Unifying perspective and tooling: Framing multiple NLP tasks as instances of sentence-pair scoring with shared architectures and task-specific output layers is a valuable organizing idea. The open-source adapters and code lower barriers for reproducibility and cross-task benchmarking.
- Sounder evaluation practices: Reporting results averaged over multiple random seeds with 95% CIs is a meaningful step forward for this literature, where single-run deltas are often within noise. Inclusion of strong IR baselines (TF-IDF/BM25) and overlap features is appropriate and clarifies where neural models help beyond lexical matching.
- Broad empirical coverage: The paper evaluates several model families across diverse tasks and datasets, providing a useful “first look” at which architectures tend to work where (e.g., CNNs on smaller data, RNNs on larger).
- Transfer learning signal: The Ubuntu-pretrained RNN often outperforms training from scratch on target tasks, supporting the premise that a general sentence-pair encoder can be seeded from large-scale supervision.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete reporting: The first results table is incomplete, preventing a full assessment. All reported experiments should be complete, with consistent metrics, CIs, and comparable settings.
- Dataset claim mismatch and under-specification: The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset, but the paper does not fully present it. For yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, the construction process, splits, filtering/labeling, statistics (size, positives/negatives per query, length distributions), licensing, and release details are insufficient. Potential overlap with wang is acknowledged but not resolved; this must be addressed to avoid leakage.
- Insufficient analysis/interpretation: The discussion is too short relative to the breadth of results. The paper should investigate why models succeed or fail per task (e.g., role of named entities/nums vs embeddings, attention utility, IR baselines), quantify effects of BM25 pruning and overlap features, and provide per-task error typologies and ablations.
- Variability questions and “unified” claim: Performance varies widely across tasks. The paper does not convincingly argue that these tasks form a unified group for which a single encoder suffices. Negative transfer, stability across seeds, and sensitivity to hyperparameters/task adapters should be analyzed.
- Ambiguity of Ubu.RNN gains: It is unclear whether transfer gains stem primarily from dataset size or domain/task characteristics. Without controls for pretraining size/domain and layer-wise transfer ablations, the recommendation of Ubu.RNN remains under-justified.
- Presentation/polish: The paper feels unfinished, with typos, unclear terminology, and missing details. This undermines clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Results completeness: When will the missing entries in the first results table be provided? Please include CIs and number of seeds for all metrics.
- Datasets: Provide full documentation for yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop (construction, labeling, splits, leakage prevention, statistics, licenses, and download URLs). Report IR baseline scores and inter-annotator agreement if applicable.
- Overlap/leakage: How do you ensure no train–test leakage within and across datasets (especially wang vs yodaqa)? Did you deduplicate questions/candidates globally?
- Transfer controls: Is Ubu.RNN’s benefit due to size or domain? Please run: (a) pretrain on SNLI with matched sample count; (b) sub-sample Ubuntu to match SNLI size; (c) layer-freezing and layer-wise reinitialization ablations; (d) multi-task joint training vs sequential fine-tuning; (e) ablate dropout consistency between pretrain and finetune.
- IR integration and pruning: What is the impact of BM25 top-20 pruning on recall and training bias? Provide comparisons with and without pruning and with/without overlap features.
- Error analysis: Include representative examples from each dataset (typical and failure cases), and where applicable, attention visualizations to substantiate the “why” behind performance differences.

Additional comments
- Language/typos: Please correct “weigh/weighed” to “weight/weighted,” “sentenes” to “sentences,” “Reciprocial” to “Reciprocal,” “interpunction” to “punctuation,” and “world overlap” to “word overlap.”
- Clarify training details: Justify very high dropout (p=0.8), training on 1/4 of data per epoch, and early stopping when dev–test correlation is low. Report tokenization, OOV handling, max sequence lengths, and any truncation policy.
- Metrics: For Ubuntu, also report MRR as proposed. For STS, include both Pearson’s r and Spearman’s ρ. Where claiming “new baselines,” compare against the strongest published numbers with significance tests.
- Figures/tables: Ensure all are complete, legible, and referenced in text with consistent numbering.

Recommendation
Borderline accept, conditional on major revisions. The unifying framework, statistical rigor, and transfer results are promising and of interest to the community, but the paper must be completed (full results), datasets must be properly presented/released, and substantially deeper analysis is needed to support the central claims.
