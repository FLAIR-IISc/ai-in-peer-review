Recommendation: Accept for presentation only with major revisions.

Summary and contributions: The paper argues that a wide family of sentence-pair tasks can be framed as a unified semantic-relation problem, proposes task-agnostic neural architectures with light task adapters, and evaluates them across multiple datasets with an open-source framework. In my view, the main contributions are:
1) A unifying formulation and reusable framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) enabling cross-task benchmarking with consistent methodology.
2) A statistically sound evaluation protocol (multi-run reporting with confidence intervals) that challenges single-run “SOTA-chasing.”
3) Initial evidence for cross-task transfer (Ubu.RNN) suggesting task-independent sentence comprehension is feasible.

Strengths:
- Conceptual unification: Treating diverse sentence-pair problems under one f2 framework is novel and timely, with clear benefits for comparability and transfer.
- Open-source infrastructure: The released tools lower barriers to replication and extension; modular adapters make it easy to test new models and tasks.
- Evaluation rigor: Averaging over 16 seeds with CIs is a welcome corrective to noisy, single-run reporting common in this area; discussion of wang-set instability is valuable.
- Practical baselines and features: Strong IR baselines (BM25/TF-IDF) and overlap features are thoughtfully integrated, reflecting real-world retrieval setups.

Weaknesses:
- Incomplete/unfinished presentation: The first results table is incomplete; several sections (e.g., “Model Performance .1”) read as partial drafts. The paper needs polishing before publication.
- Dataset claim mismatch: The abstract promises a “new, more challenging dataset,” but this is not clearly presented or fully documented; resolve the inconsistency and provide full statistics, splits, and licensing/availability.
- Insufficient analysis: The narrative discussion of results is far too short. Performance varies widely across tasks; the paper should analyze error types, task characteristics, and why some models succeed/fail, testing the “unified” assumption.
- Transfer claim ambiguity: Clarify whether Ubu.RNN’s transfer gains derive from dataset size or task generality; provide ablations controlling for pretraining size and domain, and report negative-transfer cases.
- Missing qualitative evidence: Include representative examples per dataset (prototypical and failure pairs) to ground the claims and illuminate model behavior.
- Language issues: Numerous typos/misuses reduce clarity and professionalism.

Questions to authors:
- Precisely define and release the “new, more challenging” datasets (e.g., yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop): statistics, splits, overlap with wang, licensing, and URLs.
- Provide ablations isolating pretraining size vs. domain for Ubu.RNN; what happens with smaller Ubuntu subsets or alternative large corpora?
- Does joint multi-task training help relative to sequential transfer?
- What error categories dominate per task, and do they align with the proposed unified f2 view?

Minor language fixes: replace “weigh/weighed” with “weight/weighted,” fix “sentences” misspellings, use “punctuation” instead of “interpunction,” and “word overlap” (not “world overlap”).
