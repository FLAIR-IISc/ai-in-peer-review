Summary
This paper proposes a unified view of sentence pair scoring problems (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, and STS) and evaluates a set of task-agnostic neural architectures within a single framework. It emphasizes methodological rigor (averaging over multiple random seeds, reporting confidence intervals), compares against strong IR baselines, and explores cross-task transfer learning, notably transferring a model trained on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset to other tasks. The authors also claim to introduce new answer selection datasets intended to alleviate limitations of wang.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Unified evaluation framework and methodology: An open-source toolkit (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) integrating multiple f2-tasks with standardized adapters, repeated runs, and confidence intervals; and systematic comparisons to IR baselines. This is the primary contribution and valuable to the community.
2) Cross-task evaluation and transfer learning evidence: Systematic, side-by-side evaluation of CNN/RNN/RNN-CNN/attention variants across multiple datasets, with evidence that weights learned on Ubuntu (Ubu.RNN) transfer well to other tasks.
3) New datasets for answer sentence selection: yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, claimed to be more challenging and larger than wang, with discussion of characteristics and motivation.

Strengths
- Clear, general problem formulation and unification: Framing diverse sentence-pair tasks as instances of a single f2(s0, s1) scoring problem is compelling. The paper operationalizes this via a shared set of models and adapters, enabling meaningful cross-task comparisons that are often missing in the literature.
- Methodological rigor on variance and reporting: Averaging over 16 runs and reporting 95% confidence intervals directly tackles the high variance seen in small, noisy datasets (e.g., wang). The demonstrated std dev of 0.025 MRR on wang underscores the need for this practice and strengthens the credibility of results.
- Strong baselines and realistic IR considerations: Including TF-IDF/BM25, overlap features, and candidate pruning reflects real-world pipelines, and the finding that IR baselines remain competitive in answer selection is important. The ensemble-style inclusion of BM25 scores into the neural scorer is practical and informative.
- Breadth of models and cross-task findings: The comparative results (CNN vs RNN vs RNN-CNN vs attention) across tasks of varying size and difficulty offer actionable insights (e.g., CNNs on smaller datasets, RNNs on larger ones; attention particularly helpful for IR-style ranking).
- Transfer learning signal: The Ubu.RNN initialization consistently improves performance on several target tasks, indicating that large-scale next-utterance training confers useful sentence-level semantics transferable to other f2 tasks.

Weaknesses
- Paper feels unfinished and incomplete: At least one results table is incomplete; several sections promise content that is not fully presented. The paper requires polishing before publication.
- Dataset claim mismatch and insufficient documentation: The abstract promises “new datasets with better characteristics,” but the datasets are not fully presented, analyzed, or released in a way that allows replication. Clear dataset statistics, splits, examples, and comparison to wang are needed, along with an explicit discussion of potential overlaps and leakage noted by the authors.
- Insufficient analysis and interpretation: The written discussion is too terse relative to the breadth of experiments. The paper should explain why models succeed on some tasks and fail on others, provide ablations (e.g., with/without BM25 features and pruning), and offer qualitative error analysis. Without this, the “unified” claim remains under-justified.
- Possible confounds in evaluation: BM25-based pruning to top-20 candidates and injecting BM25 as a feature may dominate gains in answer selection; the extent to which neural models add value is unclear without careful ablation. Similarly, the attention formulation is asymmetric and not extensively probed.
- Limited competitiveness on RTE/STS: Models “clearly lag behind the state of the art” on SNLI/SICK/STS, but reasons are not explored (architecture, training regime, objective mismatch, or reliance on hand-crafted overlap features). This weakens the claim of a general, task-agnostic f2 model.

Questions to authors
- Dataset release and analysis: Will you release yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop with clear train/dev/test splits, deduplication procedures, overlap audits versus wang, and detailed statistics? Please include representative examples (typical and failure cases) for each dataset.
- BM25 integration: How much of the answer selection performance derives from (a) BM25-based candidate pruning, and (b) BM25 as an input feature? Please provide ablations for both and discuss the trade-off between realism and potential bias.
- Variance and selection strategy: You average over 16 seeds; how are early stopping and model selection handled to avoid validation overfitting on noisy splits (especially wang)? Do the CIs change materially if you select the best seed vs. average all seeds?
- Transfer learning (Ubu.RNN): Is the benefit primarily due to dataset size or to the nature of next-utterance supervision? Could you contrast with pretraining on a similarly sized but non-dialog corpus, or with multi-task training, to disentangle size vs. generality?
- Attention mechanisms: Did you try symmetric or more recent attention variants (e.g., token-to-token cross-attention) beyond attn1511? If so, how do they fare across tasks with long contexts (Ubuntu) vs. short (SNLI/SICK)?

Additional comments
- Please complete all missing tables/figures and expand the results discussion with targeted ablations, qualitative examples, and error analysis. A simple per-task “what works and why” section would greatly strengthen the paper’s unification claim.
- Clarify training details per task (token limits, padding/truncation, optimizer schedules for transfer, dropout consistency) to ensure reproducibility.
- Minor language/typo fixes: replace “weigh/weighed” with “weight/weighted”; fix multiple misspellings of “sentence”; replace “interpunction” with “punctuation”; correct “world overlap” to “word overlap.”

Overall, this is a timely and valuable direction with solid infrastructure and methodology. I recommend acceptance only with major revisions that complete the results, release and document the datasets, and substantially expand the analysis.
