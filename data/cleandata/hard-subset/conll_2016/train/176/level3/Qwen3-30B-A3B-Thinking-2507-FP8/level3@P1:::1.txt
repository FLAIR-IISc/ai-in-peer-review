

This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, treating various NLP problems (Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, etc.) as a single semantic relation scoring problem. The main contributions are: (1) a unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across multiple tasks; (2) new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) addressing limitations of existing datasets; and (3) demonstration of transfer learning across tasks suggesting potential for universal semantic comprehension models.

Strengths: The unified framework is a significant contribution that addresses a fragmented research landscape. The paper successfully identifies commonalities across different tasks and demonstrates how a single model architecture can be adapted to multiple tasks with task-specific output layers. The new datasets address critical limitations of existing benchmarks (small size, uneven candidate distributions, etc.), providing more robust evaluation. The transfer learning results (Ubu.RNN) are particularly promising, showing that pre-training on one task and fine-tuning on another significantly improves performance, suggesting a path toward general-purpose semantic comprehension models.

Weaknesses: The paper's abstract claims a "new, more challenging dataset" that is not sufficiently presented or justified. The yodaqa/large2470 dataset is introduced as a solution to limitations of existing datasets, but the paper fails to clearly demonstrate how it is significantly more challenging than alternatives like Wang or WikiQA. The results section is incomplete (first results table is missing) and the discussion of results is far too brief. The paper claims to unify tasks but provides insufficient analysis of why models succeed on some tasks and fail on others, or whether the tasks truly form a coherent group. The Ubu.RNN transfer learning claim needs clarification: is the success due to dataset size, dataset characteristics, or model generalizability? The paper also fails to address why models lag behind state-of-the-art on RTE and STS tasks despite the unified framework.

The paper requires major revisions before acceptance. The most critical issue is the inconsistency between the abstract's promise of a new, more challenging dataset and the actual presentation of the datasets. The authors must provide a clear justification for why yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop represent significant improvements over existing benchmarks, including specific metrics demonstrating increased challenge. The results section must be completed with the missing table and expanded with detailed analysis of model performance across tasks, addressing why certain models work better on specific tasks. The transfer learning claim needs stronger justification through ablation studies or comparative experiments to determine whether the success is due to dataset size, task characteristics, or model generalizability.

Additional questions for authors: 
1. How specifically does yodaqa/large2470 address the limitations of Wang dataset (e.g., size, candidate distribution) in a way that makes it significantly more challenging for models?
2. Can you provide more detailed analysis of why models perform differently across tasks (e.g., why RNNs work better on Ubuntu Dialogue but CNNs on smaller datasets)?
3. What evidence can you provide to distinguish between the success of Ubu.RNN being due to dataset size versus dataset characteristics or model generalizability?

The paper has promising ideas but currently lacks the completeness and depth required for publication. The framework is novel and potentially valuable for the field, but the current version feels unfinished. The authors should address the missing results table, expand the discussion with thorough analysis of the results, and provide clear justification for the new datasets and transfer learning claims. The paper also contains several minor language issues (e.g., "weigh" vs. "weight," "interpunction," "world overlap" instead of "word overlap") that should be corrected.

While the unified framework concept is compelling and the new datasets address legitimate limitations in the field, the current paper does not sufficiently demonstrate the proposed unification or the claimed improvements. The transfer learning results are promising but require stronger evidence to support the claim of universal semantic comprehension. With major revisions to complete the results, expand the analysis, and clarify the dataset claims, this work could make a valuable contribution to the field. As currently written, it is not ready for publication.
