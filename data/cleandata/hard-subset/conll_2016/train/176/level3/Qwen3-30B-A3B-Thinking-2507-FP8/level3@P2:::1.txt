

# Review for "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, arguing that various semantic similarity problems (Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, etc.) can be treated as a single problem with task-independent neural models. The authors introduce a new software framework (dataset-sts) to facilitate cross-task evaluation, propose two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) for Answer Sentence Selection, and evaluate several neural models across multiple tasks with transfer learning.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. A unified framework for evaluating sentence pair scoring models across multiple tasks, with a new software package that enables systematic cross-task comparison and model benchmarking.

2. New datasets that address limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets, providing more realistic and challenging evaluation settings with better characteristics than previous datasets.

3. Demonstration of transfer learning across tasks, showing that models trained on one task (Ubuntu Dialogue) can be effectively fine-tuned for other tasks, providing evidence for the feasibility of universal semantic comprehension models.

## Strengths

1. The unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks is a significant contribution that addresses a major fragmentation in the literature. By creating a common evaluation platform for multiple tasks, the authors enable meaningful comparisons that were previously difficult due to inconsistent evaluation methodologies and dataset characteristics.

2. The careful statistical analysis of model performance (with 95% confidence intervals) represents a substantial methodological improvement over typical reporting practices in this area. The authors correctly identify that randomness plays a large role in neural model performance, and their approach to reporting results with confidence intervals allows for more reliable comparisons between models.

3. The new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) address important limitations in existing Answer Sentence Selection datasets, particularly the small size and poor characteristics of the Wang dataset. The authors provide a clear rationale for why these new datasets are more suitable for benchmarking, and their preliminary results suggest they will provide more meaningful evaluation for future model improvements.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper claims in the abstract to introduce a new, more challenging dataset, but the presentation of the datasets is inconsistent with this claim. The paper does not adequately present the new datasets or demonstrate their improved characteristics as promised, creating a significant discrepancy between the abstract and the main content.

2. The discussion of results is insufficiently detailed and lacks meaningful interpretation. The authors present multiple results tables but provide minimal analysis of why models perform well on some tasks but not others, or whether the tasks truly form a unified group as claimed. The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of the factors affecting model performance across tasks.

3. The first results table is incomplete, making it impossible to properly evaluate the models' performance. The authors must complete this table to allow for proper assessment of their claims.

4. The claim about the effectiveness of the Ubu.RNN transfer learning approach is not sufficiently justified. The authors need to clarify whether the success is due to the generalizability of the model or the size of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, and provide a method to distinguish between these factors. Without this clarification, the transfer learning claim lacks sufficient support.

5. The paper is incomplete and needs significant polishing and completion before publication. Many aspects, including the discussion of results and the presentation of datasets, are underdeveloped and require substantial revision.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify the discrepancy between the abstract's promise of a new, more challenging dataset and the actual presentation of datasets in the paper? Please provide a clear description of the new datasets and demonstrate their improved characteristics.

2. Could you provide example instances from each dataset, showing prototypical pairs and problematic pairs, to better illustrate the characteristics of your datasets?

3. How can we determine whether the success of Ubu.RNN is due to the generalizability of the model or the size of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset? What experiments could be conducted to distinguish between these factors?

The paper presents an exciting and novel idea that has the potential to significantly impact the field of sentence pair scoring. However, it currently requires major revisions to address the issues identified above before it can be considered for publication. The framework is promising, but the execution is incomplete and the claims need stronger support through more thorough analysis and presentation.
