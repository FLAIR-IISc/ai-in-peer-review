

**Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"**

This paper proposes a novel unified framework for sentence-pair scoring tasks, treating them as a single semantic-relation problem with generally-applicable neural models. The main contributions are: (1) a unified framework that enables cross-task transfer learning, (2) an open-source implementation (dataset-sts) for easy model and dataset integration, and (3) evidence that simple neural models can learn universal semantic comprehension through cross-task adaptation.

**Strengths:** The unified framework approach is highly valuable for addressing fragmentation in the sentence-pair scoring literature. The proposed dataset-sts framework is well-designed and could significantly benefit the community. The transfer learning results (Ubu.RNN) are compelling and suggest a promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:** The paper suffers from critical completeness issues. The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset (yodaqa/large2470), but the dataset description is incomplete and not properly presented in the paper. The first results table is missing crucial data and must be completed. The discussion of results is superficial, failing to address why performance varies widely across tasks or whether these tasks truly form a unified group. The paper also contains multiple language errors (e.g., "interpunction" instead of "punctuation," "world overlap" instead of "word overlap," incorrect "weigh"/"weighted" usage) and missing examples from each dataset showing both typical and problematic sentence pairs.

**Questions to Authors:** 
1. Please clarify the relationship between Ubu.RNN's success and dataset characteristics versus size, and propose an experiment to distinguish these factors.
2. Provide the complete results table and include examples from each dataset showing both successful and failed cases.

The paper presents an exciting research direction but currently lacks the completeness and depth required for publication. The framework has significant potential, but the current version feels unfinished and requires major revisions to address the missing dataset details, incomplete results, insufficient analysis, and language issues before it can be accepted.
