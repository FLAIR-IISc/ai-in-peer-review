**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, treating them as a single semantic-relation problem with generally applicable neural models. The authors aim to unify research in this area by introducing a new software package, dataset-sts, which integrates various datasets, a Python dataset adapter, and a Python library for constructing deep neural NLP models. They evaluate their models on several tasks, including Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Semantic Textual Similarity. The results show that their models can learn a generic task akin to some form of text comprehension, and they propose transfer learning as a way to improve performance on new tasks.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Unified Framework**: The authors propose a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, which can be applied to various tasks with minimal modifications. This framework has the potential to simplify research in this area and enable more efficient model development.
2. **Dataset-sts Software Package**: The authors introduce a new software package, dataset-sts, which integrates various datasets, a Python dataset adapter, and a Python library for constructing deep neural NLP models. This package can facilitate research in sentence pair scoring tasks and make it easier to compare models across different tasks.
3. **Transfer Learning**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of transfer learning in improving performance on new tasks. They show that a model trained on one task can be fine-tuned on another task with significant performance gains.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Proposal**: The authors propose a novel and exciting approach to treating many sentence-pair scoring tasks as a unified semantic-relation problem with generally applicable neural models.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors evaluate their models on several tasks, providing a comprehensive understanding of their strengths and weaknesses.
3. **Open-Source Software Package**: The authors make their software package, dataset-sts, available as open-source, which can facilitate research in sentence pair scoring tasks and make it easier to compare models across different tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Incomplete Results**: The first results table is incomplete, and the authors should provide complete results to facilitate a thorough understanding of their models' performance.
2. **Insufficient Analysis**: The written discussion of the results is far too short and needs more interpretation and discussion to provide a deeper understanding of the models' strengths and weaknesses.
3. **Dataset Claim Mismatch**: The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset, but this dataset is not actually presented in the paper. The authors should resolve this inconsistency and provide a clear explanation of their dataset.
4. **Result Variability**: The performance of the models varies widely across tasks, and the authors should investigate why models fail on some tasks and succeed on others. They should also discuss whether tasks truly form a unified group.
5. **Lack of Examples**: The authors should include example instances from each dataset, showing prototypical pairs and problematic pairs, to provide a better understanding of the tasks and models.
6. **Ubu.RNN Transfer Claim**: The authors should clarify whether Ubu.RNN's recommendation is due to dataset nature (generality) or dataset size and propose how to determine which.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide complete results for the first results table to facilitate a thorough understanding of your models' performance?
2. How do you plan to address the dataset claim mismatch in the abstract and provide a clear explanation of your dataset?
3. Can you provide example instances from each dataset, showing prototypical pairs and problematic pairs, to provide a better understanding of the tasks and models?
4. How do you plan to investigate why models fail on some tasks and succeed on others, and whether tasks truly form a unified group?
5. Can you clarify whether Ubu.RNN's recommendation is due to dataset nature (generality) or dataset size and propose how to determine which?

**Additional Comments**

The paper needs polishing and completion before publication. The authors should fix incorrect uses of "weigh"/"weighed" vs. "weight"/"weighted", multiple misspellings of "sentence", unclear term "interpunction", and "world overlap" → "word overlap".
