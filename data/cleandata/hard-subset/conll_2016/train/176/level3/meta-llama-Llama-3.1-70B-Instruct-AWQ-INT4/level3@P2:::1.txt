**Paper Review: Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension**

**Summary**

This paper proposes a unified framework for various sentence-pair scoring tasks, treating them as a single semantic-relation problem that can be solved using generally-applicable neural models. The authors aim to unify research within a single framework, improve model evaluation methodology, and demonstrate the feasibility of pursuing universal, task-independent models. They introduce new datasets and evaluate several neural network models, including recurrent neural networks (RNNs), convolutional neural networks (CNNs), and attention-based models.

**Contributions**

1. **Unified framework for sentence-pair scoring tasks**: The authors propose a novel approach to treat various sentence-pair scoring tasks as a single semantic-relation problem, which can be solved using generally-applicable neural models.
2. **New datasets**: The authors introduce new datasets, including yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, which can be used to evaluate models on sentence-pair scoring tasks.
3. **Evaluation of neural network models**: The authors evaluate several neural network models, including RNNs, CNNs, and attention-based models, on various sentence-pair scoring tasks.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel proposal**: The paper proposes a novel and exciting approach to treat various sentence-pair scoring tasks as a single semantic-relation problem.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate several neural network models on various sentence-pair scoring tasks, providing a comprehensive analysis of their performance.
3. **New datasets**: The introduction of new datasets provides a valuable resource for the research community.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Incomplete results**: The first results table is incomplete and must be completed to provide a thorough analysis of the models' performance.
2. **Insufficient analysis**: The written discussion of the many results is far too short and needs much more interpretation and discussion.
3. **Dataset claim mismatch**: The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset that is not actually presented, which must be resolved.
4. **Result variability**: Performance varies widely across tasks, and the authors should investigate why models fail on some tasks and succeed on others and whether tasks truly form a unified group.
5. **Lack of examples**: The paper would benefit from including example instances from each dataset, showing prototypical pairs and problematic pairs.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a complete results table to facilitate a thorough analysis of the models' performance?
2. How do you plan to address the result variability across tasks, and what insights can be gained from this analysis?
3. Can you provide example instances from each dataset to illustrate prototypical pairs and problematic pairs?
4. Can you clarify whether Ubu.RNN's recommendation is due to dataset nature (generality) or dataset size, and propose how to determine which?
5. How do you plan to finalize the paper, including polishing and completing it before publication?

**Additional Comments**

The paper needs minor language and typo fixes, including incorrect uses of "weigh"/"weighed" vs. "weight"/"weighted", multiple misspellings of "sentence", unclear term "interpunction", and "world overlap" → "word overlap".
