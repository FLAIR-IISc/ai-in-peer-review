This paper proposes a novel and exciting approach to treat various sentence-pair scoring tasks as a unified semantic-relation problem using generally-applicable neural models. The authors introduce a framework that employs task-independent models and task-specific adaptation modules, aiming to unify research within a single framework. They also improve the methodology of model evaluation by comparing with strong non-neural IR baselines and introducing new datasets with better characteristics.

The paper's main contributions are:

1. A unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, allowing for the comparison of different models across various tasks.
2. The introduction of new datasets, such as yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, which have better characteristics than existing datasets.
3. The demonstration of the feasibility of pursuing universal, task-independent models using cross-task transfer learning.

The strengths of the paper lie in its novelty and potential impact on the field. The authors' approach to unifying various sentence-pair scoring tasks under a single framework is innovative and has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in natural language processing. The paper's weaknesses, however, are significant and must be addressed before publication.

The primary weakness of the paper is its lack of completeness. The first results table is incomplete, and the written discussion of the results is far too short and lacks interpretation and analysis. The authors must provide a more comprehensive discussion of their results, including an analysis of why their models fail on some tasks and succeed on others. Additionally, the authors claim to introduce a new, more challenging dataset, but this dataset is not presented in the paper. This inconsistency must be resolved.

Furthermore, the authors should investigate the variability in performance across tasks and whether the tasks truly form a unified group. They should also include example instances from each dataset, showing prototypical pairs and problematic pairs. The paper would also benefit from a clarification of the Ubu.RNN transfer claim, including whether the recommendation is due to the dataset's nature (generality) or dataset size, and how to determine which.

Finally, the paper requires polishing and completion before publication. The authors should address minor language and typo issues, such as incorrect uses of "weigh"/"weighed" vs. "weight"/"weighted" and multiple misspellings of "sentence".

In conclusion, while the paper has potential, it requires significant revisions before it can be considered for publication. The authors must address the weaknesses mentioned above and provide a more comprehensive and polished paper.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide a more comprehensive discussion of your results, including an analysis of why your models fail on some tasks and succeed on others?
2. Can you clarify the inconsistency regarding the introduction of a new, more challenging dataset?
3. How do you plan to investigate the variability in performance across tasks and whether the tasks truly form a unified group?
4. Can you include example instances from each dataset, showing prototypical pairs and problematic pairs?
5. Can you clarify the Ubu.RNN transfer claim, including whether the recommendation is due to the dataset's nature (generality) or dataset size, and how to determine which?

Additional Comments:

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed description of the datasets used, including their characteristics and limitations.
* The authors should consider including a more comprehensive review of related work, including a discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of existing approaches.
* The paper's conclusion could be strengthened by providing a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results and the potential applications of the proposed framework.
