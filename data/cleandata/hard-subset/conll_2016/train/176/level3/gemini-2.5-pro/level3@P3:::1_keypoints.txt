- Proposes unifying diverse sentence-pair tasks as a single semantic function f(s0, s1) learned by general neural models.  
- Implements and benchmarks several neural architectures (averaging, CNN, RNN, hybrids) across multiple tasks within an open-source framework.  
- Introduces two new answer-selection datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) and releases code.  
- Shows that pre-training on the large Ubuntu Dialogue Corpus (Ubu.RNN) and fine-tuning yields strong transfer performance.  
- Main contribution: conceptual unification of many f2-type tasks with large-scale empirical comparison.  
- Main contribution: demonstration of transfer learning from out-of-domain dialogue data improving smaller tasks.  
- Main contribution: provision of an open-source framework and new datasets as community resources.  
- Strength: novel and important high-level framing encouraging general, reusable semantic models.  
- Strength: broad, useful empirical study establishing cross-task baselines.  
- Strength: compelling transfer-learning results exemplified by Ubu.RNN.  
- Weakness: paper feels unfinished and lacks polish (missing Table 1; new datasets not well analyzed or highlighted).  
- Weakness: analysis of results is superficial—no deep investigation into why architectures perform differently across datasets.  
- Weakness: entirely quantitative with no qualitative examples illustrating model successes and failures.  
- Question: is Ubu.RNN’s transfer success due to dataset size or conversational/nature characteristics; suggest experiment with Ubuntu subsets.  
- Question: request elaboration on core semantic challenges common across tasks versus distinguishing properties that affect model performance.  
- Question: provide the missing Table 1 and better justify why the new datasets are more challenging, with illustrative examples.  
- Additional comments: paper needs proofreading—typos and word-choice errors (e.g., "sentenes"→"sentences", "weighed"→"weighted", "world overlap"→"word overlap", "interpunction"→"punctuation").
