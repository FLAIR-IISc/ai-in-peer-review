**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a unified framework for treating various sentence-pair scoring tasks—such as Answer Sentence Selection, Textual Entailment, and Next Utterance Ranking—as a single semantic-relation problem. The authors evaluate several standard neural architectures within this framework and explore cross-task transfer learning. The primary contribution is the conceptual proposal of this unified framework, which is a novel and valuable perspective for a fragmented research area. A secondary contribution is the benchmarking of these general models across a diverse set of tasks, providing a useful point of comparison for future work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Unifying Proposal:** The core idea of framing disparate sentence-pair tasks within a single, cohesive framework is exciting and timely. If successful, this approach could consolidate research efforts and encourage the development of more general text comprehension models.
2.  **Promising Transfer Learning Results:** The demonstration that a model (Ubu.RNN) trained on a large dialogue dataset can be successfully transferred to other tasks is a strong piece of evidence supporting the paper's central hypothesis. This suggests that genuine, task-independent semantic understanding is being learned.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Paper Feels Unfinished:** The submission, in its current state, reads like a draft. There are major omissions, such as an incomplete primary results table, alongside numerous minor language errors ("weigh" vs. "weight", misspellings, "world overlap"). This lack of polish significantly detracts from the work and it is not ready for publication as-is.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Interpretation:** The performance of the proposed models varies widely across the different tasks, yet the paper offers very little discussion or analysis of why this is the case. The authors should investigate what properties of these tasks cause certain models to excel or fail, which is critical for validating the "unified group" claim. The paper would also benefit from qualitative analysis, including examples of prototypical and problematic pairs from the datasets.
3.  **Inconsistent Claims:** The abstract explicitly promises the introduction of a new, more challenging dataset, which is a key motivation for the work. However, this dataset is not presented or described in the paper. This is a major inconsistency that must be resolved.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your transfer learning results with Ubu.RNN are impressive. Do you attribute its success to the general nature of the dialogue task, or primarily to the large size of the dataset? Could you propose an experiment to distinguish between these two factors?
2.  Given the significant performance variance, can you provide a more detailed analysis of the relationship between task characteristics and model performance? What makes some tasks harder for your models than others?
