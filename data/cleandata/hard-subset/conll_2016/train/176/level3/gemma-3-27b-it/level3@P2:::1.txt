## Review for CONLL 2016: Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension

This paper proposes a unifying framework for a variety of sentence-pair scoring tasks, framing them all as instances of a general semantic relation problem solvable with task-independent neural models adapted through task-specific output layers. This is a novel and potentially exciting direction, aiming to address fragmentation in the field and facilitate cross-task learning. However, the paper currently feels somewhat unfinished and requires substantial revision to fully realize its potential.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work lies in the **proposal of a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks**. While the idea of viewing these tasks through a common lens isn’t entirely new, the authors systematically present a compelling argument for the benefits of this approach, emphasizing the potential for cross-task transfer learning and standardized evaluation. A secondary contribution is the **introduction of two new datasets, yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop**, designed to address limitations in existing benchmarks, particularly concerning size and data quality. Finally, the paper offers a **broad empirical evaluation** across multiple tasks and models, establishing a baseline for future research within this unified framework, even if the results require deeper analysis (discussed below).

**Strengths:**

1. **Compelling Motivation and Framing:** The authors clearly articulate the problem of fragmentation in sentence-pair scoring research and present a convincing rationale for a unified approach. The argument that the second sentence in a pair serves as a rich, complex label during training is insightful and forms a strong foundation for the proposed framework.
2. **Practical Implementation and Open-Source Release:**  The development of the `dataset-sts` and `KeraSTS` libraries and their public release demonstrate a commitment to reproducibility and facilitate further research in this area. Providing tools for easy implementation and evaluation is a significant strength.
3. **Extensive Experimental Setup:** The authors evaluate a wide range of models – including IR baselines, simple neural networks, and more complex RNN and CNN architectures – across several tasks. This breadth of experimentation provides a valuable resource for the community.  The acknowledgment and reporting of confidence intervals, acknowledging the inherent randomness of neural models, is particularly commendable and statistically rigorous.
4. **Dataset Creation Addresses Real Issues:** The identified limitations of existing datasets (e.g., small size, uneven distribution in Wang et al., 2007) are well-documented, and the introduction of yodaqa/large2470 represents a concrete attempt to address these deficiencies.  The motivation for wqmprop also adds value.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incomplete Results and Missing Table:** A key issue is the incomplete first results table.  This significantly hinders the ability to assess the performance of the models and the validity of the comparisons. Completion of this table is essential.
2. **Insufficient Analysis & Discussion:** The paper presents a large number of results, but the discussion is surprisingly superficial. The authors should provide a more in-depth analysis of *why* certain models perform well on some tasks but not others. What specific characteristics of each task contribute to these differences? A deeper investigation into the model failures would be incredibly valuable.
3. **Dataset Claim Mismatch & Limited Evaluation of New Datasets:** The abstract promises a new, more challenging dataset, but the paper does not fully deliver on this promise. The performance on the newly introduced datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop) is significantly lower than on existing benchmarks. This needs to be thoroughly discussed: are these datasets genuinely more challenging, or are they simply poorly suited to the evaluated models? Is the lower baseline performance a positive sign indicating the need for more sophisticated methods?
4. **Transfer Learning Claim Requires Further Justification:**  The claim that transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset (Ubu.RNN) consistently improves performance is interesting but requires more careful investigation. Is this improvement due to the generalizability of the learned representations, or simply to the large size of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset? Further experiments are needed to disentangle these factors.
5. **Language and Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor language and typographical errors (e.g., incorrect use of “weigh/weighed” vs. “weight/weighted”, multiple misspellings of “sentence”, “interpunction”, “world overlap”). These detract from the overall polish of the manuscript.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop:** Can you provide example instances from each dataset, illustrating both prototypical and problematic sentence pairs? This will help to understand the characteristics of these datasets and the challenges they pose.
2. **Regarding Ubu.RNN transfer learning:** What experiments have you conducted to determine whether the improved performance is primarily due to the dataset size of Ubuntu Dialogue versus the learned representations being more general? Would performance be comparable if the model was pre-trained on a similarly large dataset from a different task?
3. **Regarding Task Unification:** Are there tasks you considered including in your framework but ultimately excluded? If so, why? What are the limitations of this unified framework, and what types of sentence-pair scoring problems might *not* be well-suited to this approach?
4. **Regarding the incomplete results table:** When will the table be completed?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please clarify the rationale for choosing N=300 for the embedding dimensionality.
*   A brief discussion of computational resources used for the experiments would be helpful.



Overall, this paper presents a promising research direction. However, significant revisions are needed to address the identified weaknesses and fully develop the potential of the proposed framework. I recommend acceptance contingent upon substantial revisions addressing the issues outlined above.
