## Review for "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

This paper proposes a unifying framework for various sentence-pair scoring tasks, viewing them as instances of a general semantic relation problem solvable with adaptable neural models. The authors aim to establish a standardized evaluation methodology, introduce new datasets, and demonstrate the feasibility of task-independent models via cross-task transfer learning. The core idea of framing diverse tasks under a single f2 function is a potentially valuable contribution, offering a more holistic perspective on semantic understanding. The accompanying software package, dataset-sts, also represents a significant contribution by providing a modular platform for research in this area. 

The strongest argument for acceptance lies in the novelty of the proposed framework and the potential for streamlining research in sentence-pair scoring. The attempt to unify seemingly disparate tasks is ambitious and could foster progress by enabling more meaningful model comparisons. Furthermore, the introduction of the yodaqa/large2470 dataset, though needing verification (see below), addresses identified shortcomings in existing datasets. The effort to create a well-documented, open-source toolkit (dataset-sts) is commendable and will likely be beneficial to the broader research community.

However, the paper feels significantly unfinished and requires substantial revision before publication. The initial results table (Fig. 2-4) is incomplete, lacking performance figures for several models on multiple datasets, hindering a proper assessment. More importantly, the discussion of results is extremely brief and superficial, failing to provide meaningful interpretation of observed performance variations across tasks. The authors claim the introduction of a new dataset (yodaqa/large2470), but this claim is undermined by the incomplete results presented for it. The substantial performance variability across tasks raises questions about the validity of the “unified” framework. Why do models perform well on some tasks but poorly on others? Are these tasks truly comparable, or are there inherent differences impacting model performance? The claim regarding Ubu.RNN's strong transfer performance requires clarification. Is this due to the generalizability of the model or simply the large size of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset? Proposing methods to disentangle these factors is crucial. 

Finally, the paper contains numerous minor errors: inconsistent use of “weigh/weighted” vs. “weight/weighted”, misspellings of "sentence”, an unclear term ("interpunction"), and "world overlap" should be "word overlap."  

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Please complete the results tables and provide a more detailed discussion of the results, including an analysis of the observed performance variations across tasks.
2.  Please provide example instances from each dataset, including both prototypical pairs and those where the models struggle.
3.  How can you determine whether Ubu.RNN’s performance is due to dataset size or the model’s inherent generalizability? Propose specific experiments to address this.
4. Is the yodaqa/large2470 dataset publicly available? If not, when will it be?
