This paper proposes a unified framework for understanding various sentence-pair scoring tasks – Answer Sentence Selection, Semantic Textual Similarity, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Paraphrasing – by framing them as instances of a more general semantic relation classification problem (f2). The core idea is to leverage task-independent neural models adapted via task-specific output layers, aiming for universal semantic comprehension. The authors contribute a comprehensive survey of existing approaches, introduce two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations in existing benchmarks, and demonstrate initial evidence supporting cross-task transfer learning.

The most significant contribution of this work lies in the attempt to consolidate a fragmented research landscape. By explicitly framing these diverse tasks under a common umbrella, the authors provide a valuable service to the community, enabling more direct comparison of models and fostering the development of generalizable semantic understanding systems. The introduction of new datasets, particularly yodaqa/large2470, is also a key strength, offering a potentially more robust and realistic evaluation platform than existing benchmarks, and addressing acknowledged deficiencies in datasets like wang. Finally, the preliminary results showing the benefit of transfer learning across tasks, while not conclusive, suggest a promising direction for future research.

Several strengths support this submission. First, the paper provides a thorough and well-structured survey of the existing literature on sentence pair scoring, effectively contextualizing the proposed framework. The inclusion of both neural and non-neural baselines ensures a comprehensive evaluation, and the focus on statistical significance is commendable, highlighted by the reporting of confidence intervals. Second, the authors’ proactive identification of shortcomings in existing datasets and their subsequent creation of yodaqa/large2470 represent a valuable contribution to the field. The datasets' improved characteristics—larger size and reduced bias—offer a more challenging and reliable testing ground for future models. Third, the exploration of cross-task transfer learning provides initial empirical support for the central hypothesis of universal semantic comprehension. The observation that models trained on one task (Ubuntu Dialogue) can improve performance on others is encouraging and merits further investigation. 

However, several weaknesses require attention before acceptance. A primary concern is the incomplete presentation of results. The first results table referenced in the text is missing, hindering a proper assessment of the model performance. Furthermore, the discussion of the results is insufficient. Given the extensive experimental setup and the numerous models evaluated across multiple datasets, the authors need to provide a much more in-depth analysis of the findings. A detailed interpretation of why certain models excel on some tasks but struggle on others is crucial. The claim made in the abstract about introducing a new, more challenging dataset is also misleading as it isn’t fully realized in the final version. This inconsistency needs to be resolved. The wide variation in performance across tasks raises questions about the validity of the “unified” framework. The authors should investigate whether these tasks genuinely represent a single underlying semantic relation or whether the observed discrepancies indicate fundamental differences that require task-specific modeling approaches.

Specifically, the paper would benefit from including example instances from each dataset, illustrating both prototypical pairs and challenging cases that expose model limitations. Clarification is also needed regarding the claim that the Ubu.RNN model is the preferred recommendation. Is this solely due to the dataset's characteristics (generality or size), or are there other underlying factors? Proposing methods to disentangle these influences would strengthen the analysis. Finally, the paper requires polish and completion; the authors acknowledge it is not fully finished. There are also minor issues – inconsistencies in terminology ("weigh"/"weighed" vs. "weight"/"weighted"), misspellings ("sentence"), unclear terms ("interpunction"), and typos ("world overlap").

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide the missing results table referenced in section 4.4.
2.  Can you elaborate on the specific challenges presented by the yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop datasets? Provide example pairs that are difficult for current models and explain why.
3.  What experiments have been conducted to investigate the reasons for the performance variations across different tasks?
4.  What is the rationale behind choosing the dot-product scorer for the Ubu.RNN model during transfer learning, and how does this choice affect the transferability of knowledge?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the ablation studies performed to determine the impact of individual components (e.g., BM25, dropout) on performance?
