## Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, aiming to treat diverse problems like Answer Sentence Selection, Semantic Textual Similarity, and Next Utterance Ranking as instantiations of a common semantic relation problem solvable with generally applicable neural models. The core idea – leveraging the second sentence as a “complex label” – is intriguing and potentially valuable for advancing research in this area.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Framing Diverse Tasks as Sentence Pair Scoring:** The paper’s most significant contribution is its explicit articulation of the broad range of NLP tasks that can be effectively formulated as sentence pair scoring problems. This provides a valuable conceptual unification.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors present a broad experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and model architectures, offering a valuable resource for future research. The creation of the `dataset-sts` framework and associated libraries (KeraSTS, PySTS) is a positive step toward reproducibility and standardization.
3. **Introduction of New Datasets:** The introduction of `yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop`, despite needing further validation (discussed below), represent an attempt to address the limitations of existing datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The proposed unification is a potentially significant step towards generalizable semantic understanding models. If successful, a single, adaptable model capable of performing well across diverse sentence pair scoring tasks would represent a substantial advance.
2. **Extensive Evaluation:** The paper undertakes a comprehensive empirical evaluation, testing a variety of models on multiple datasets. The inclusion of IR baselines provides important context for the neural model performance.
3. **Open-Source Framework:** The release of the `dataset-sts` framework demonstrates a commitment to reproducibility and enables other researchers to build upon this work. This is a significant asset.
4. **Transfer Learning Results:** The reported success of transfer learning from the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset (`Ubu.RNN`) suggests the possibility of leveraging large, readily available datasets to improve performance on other tasks, which is a promising direction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incomplete Results & Missing Data:** Table 1 is incomplete, significantly hindering evaluation of the dataset characteristics. Crucially, the results for several models and datasets are missing, making it impossible to draw definitive conclusions about their relative performance. This is a major issue.
2. **Insufficient Analysis and Discussion:** The discussion of the results is remarkably brief, given the scope of the experiments. The authors claim certain trends (e.g., CNNs performing better on smaller datasets), but these claims lack sufficient justification and deeper analysis. Why do models succeed on some tasks and fail on others? What are the limitations of the different architectures? The paper needs a much more thorough examination of the experimental findings.
3. **Dataset Claim Discrepancy:** The abstract promises a “new, more challenging dataset,” but the presented datasets, while valuable additions, don’t demonstrably exhibit significantly increased difficulty compared to existing options. The claim about improved characteristics needs to be substantiated with a more rigorous comparison.  
4. **Result Variability & Task Coherence:** The performance variability across tasks is considerable. The authors should investigate *why* models perform so differently on different datasets. Is there evidence that these tasks truly form a unified group, or are they fundamentally distinct problems that require specialized approaches? The analysis of task-specific challenges is missing.
5. **Lack of Illustrative Examples:** The paper would benefit immensely from including example instances from each dataset, showcasing prototypical pairs and, more importantly, *problematic* pairs where the models fail. This would provide valuable insights into the challenges of each task and help diagnose model shortcomings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Completion:** Please complete Table 1 with all relevant data. Also, please provide the missing results for models and datasets that are currently absent from the tables.
2. **Dataset Challenge:** Please provide a more detailed justification for the claim that `yodaqa/large2470` and `wqmprop` are “more challenging” than existing datasets. What specific characteristics contribute to this increased difficulty?
3. **Ubu.RNN Analysis:** Is the success of `Ubu.RNN` transfer primarily due to the size of the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset, its specific characteristics (e.g., informal language, IRC context), or a combination of factors? What experiments were conducted to disentangle these influences? How would you propose to determine which factor is dominant?
4. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more in-depth error analysis, focusing on specific failure cases for each task? Including example instances of incorrect predictions would be very helpful.
5. **Task Coherence:** What evidence supports the claim that these diverse sentence pair scoring tasks form a unified group suitable for a single, general-purpose model?

**Additional Comments:**

* Please address the minor language issues: correct "weigh"/"weighed" vs. "weight"/"weighted," fix misspellings of "sentence," clarify the term "interpunction" (likely intended as “punctuation”), and replace "world overlap" with “word overlap.”
* The paper feels unfinished and requires substantial polishing before publication. A more detailed discussion of the results, completion of the tables, and addressing the points raised above are crucial.
