Summary and claimed contributions
This submission proposes “sentence pair scoring” as a unifying framework for a range of f2-type problems (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, textual entailment, semantic textual similarity, paraphrasing). Within this framing, the work:
1) Introduces an open-source evaluation and modeling framework (dataset-sts / KeraSTS) to run task-independent neural models with task-specific adapters, with standardized reporting (multiple seeds, confidence intervals) and strong IR baselines.
2) Contributes two datasets oriented to answer selection and property selection (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop), with claims of better size/complexity characteristics and lower baseline scores than wang.
3) Presents extensive cross-task experiments with simple neural architectures (avg, DAN, CNN, RNN, RNN-CNN, attention), emphasizes sound evaluation methodology (16 seeds, 95% CIs), and demonstrates transfer learning from Next Utterance Ranking (Ubuntu) to other tasks.

Strengths
- Unified perspective with a practical, open-source framework: The paper integrates many f2 tasks under one experimental scaffold, with modular dataset loaders and models, and makes code available. This directly addresses fragmentation in the literature and lowers barriers to fair comparisons and reproducibility.
- Rigorous evaluation methodology: Reporting means across 16 random seeds and 95% confidence intervals addresses a real problem in f2 tasks where seed variance is large (e.g., reported wang test MRR std ≈ 0.025, exceeding typical SOTA claim margins). Including strong IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) and discussing their ensembling reflects good scientific practice.
- New datasets with more challenging characteristics: yodaqa/large2470 appears to have larger, more uneven candidate sets and lower baselines than wang, which should stretch models and reduce leaderboard noise. The additional property selection dataset (wqmprop) extends the f2 scenario to KB path labels, broadening applicability.
- Cross-task transfer learning: Pretraining on a large-scale Next Utterance Ranking task and fine-tuning elsewhere yields consistent gains (Ubu.RNN outperforming non-transferred counterparts), supporting the central hypothesis that generic sentence-pair comprehension can transfer across related tasks.
- Useful empirical insights: The observation that CNNs fare better on small datasets while RNNs benefit from scale, and that attention helps IR-style answer selection with many candidates, provides actionable guidance for practitioners. Practical training notes (dropout magnitude, label balancing during transfer, optimizer choices) are valuable.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling novelty and incomplete coverage of competitive architectures: Most models are standard baselines (avg/DAN/CNN/GRU, a simple attention variant). The study excludes stronger serial/interactive attention models and more recent inferential encoders for RTE/STS (e.g., Rocktäschel-style entailment attention, more expressive cross-sentence interaction). Consequently, results lag behind SOTA on RTE/STS and the claim of advancing a universal f2 model is not yet substantiated on these tasks.
- Potential evaluation confound from BM25 pruning and features: For answer selection, pruning to top-20 by BM25 and feeding BM25 scores into the MLP introduces dependence on a particular IR pre-filter. This may limit generality and comparability, and risks overstating neural gains in settings where retrieval is weaker or unavailable. Sensitivity to the pruning cutoff and to the inclusion of BM25 as a feature is not analyzed.
- Dataset curation and overlap concerns insufficiently quantified: yodaqa/large2470 is derived from YodaQA retrieval with labels via answer-string matching; noise characteristics and annotation quality (e.g., manual audit, false-negative/false-positive rates) are not reported. The paper acknowledges possible overlap with wang and WebQuestions ancestry but does not quantify or control it; this threatens cross-dataset comparisons and transfer experiments unless deduplication is enforced.
- Inconclusive evidence for “universal” modeling: While transfer from Ubuntu helps, the best-performing configurations differ across tasks (e.g., scorer choice, dimensionality, dropout), and the same model does not reach competitive performance on SNLI/SICK. Without stronger baselines and wider task coverage, the central universality claim remains preliminary.
- Clarity and completeness: Several figures/tables are referenced but not available in the text provided (e.g., Figs. 1–4). Some presentation issues (typos, references formatting, redacted URLs) hinder assessment and reproducibility. Hyperparameter details, parameter counts, and training budgets per task are only partially specified.

Questions to clarify during rebuttal
- Overlap analysis: What is the measured overlap (questions, passages) between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across splits? Were deduplication and leakage checks performed before any cross-task pretraining/fine-tuning?
- Dataset quality: For yodaqa/large2470, is there a manual validation of labeling quality from string matching? Provide an estimate of label noise and an inter-annotator or auditing sample.
- BM25 dependence: How sensitive are answer selection results to the BM25 pruning cutoff (e.g., top-10/20/50/∞)? What is performance without BM25 as an input feature? Please include an ablation.
- Transfer protocol details: For Ubu.RNN, specify parameter counts, target-task learning rates/schedules, and whether any layers were frozen in the reported best runs. Did full Ubuntu training (beyond 10%) change transfer gains?
- Token-level feature impact: What is the isolated benefit of the added boolean token features (overlap flags, capitalization/number) across tasks?

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Quantify and mitigate dataset overlap; release scripts for deduplication. Provide dataset statistics, label quality assessment, and licensing details for new datasets.
- Add ablations for BM25 pruning and feature injection; report sensitivity to k and to removal of BM25 from the scorer.
- Include at least one stronger cross-sentence attention/interactions baseline (e.g., a bidirectional attention or ESIM-style model) to better situate the framework against contemporary methods, especially for RTE/STS.

Optional but beneficial:
- Provide parameter counts, training time, and memory footprint per model/task to aid practical adoption.
- Explore multi-task training within the framework to complement the transfer results.
- Improve presentation (proofreading, complete figures/tables, finalized code/data URLs).

Overall assessment
The paper’s core value lies in unification, resources, and methodology rather than algorithmic novelty. The open-source framework, careful evaluation with confidence intervals, challenging datasets, and transfer-learning evidence meaningfully advance experimental rigor in sentence-pair modeling. Addressing the evaluation and dataset concerns and adding a stronger attention baseline would significantly strengthen the case for acceptance.
