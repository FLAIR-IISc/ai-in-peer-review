Summary
The paper argues for a unified view of sentence-pair scoring (f2) across tasks such as answer sentence selection, next-utterance ranking, RTE, and STS. It presents a common evaluation framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS), introduces two new datasets for answer selection, benchmarks several simple neural architectures alongside strong IR baselines across tasks, and studies cross-task transfer (pretraining on Ubuntu dialog).

Contributions (as I see them)
- A rigorous, cross-task evaluation framework with standardized training/evaluation, 16-run reporting with confidence intervals, and competitive IR baselines.
- Two new answer selection datasets (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop) designed to address instability and small-split issues of existing corpora, with public tooling.
- An empirical study showing practical benefits of transfer learning from a large next-utterance ranking corpus to other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Methodology: Reporting over 16 seeds with 95% CIs is a welcome step beyond single-run reporting, and the paper convincingly shows why variance matters (e.g., wang instability).
- Breadth and comparability: A consistent setup across diverse f2 tasks, with clear metrics (MAP/MRR, Recall@k, Pearson r), enables meaningful model comparisons and future extensions.
- Baselines and IR integration: Strong IR baselines and pragmatic ensembling (BM25 features, token overlap flags) set a realistic bar for applications and reveal where neural models add value.
- Resources: Open-source framework and new datasets are likely to be useful to the community and lower friction for reproducible cross-task studies.
- Transfer insights: Pretraining on Ubuntu substantially improves downstream results, supporting the paper’s claim that general f2 representations are feasible.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling novelty: The architectures are largely standard (avg/DAN/CNN/RNN/RNN-CNN/attention), and state-of-the-art is not reached on RTE/STS, weakening the “universal comprehension” claim.
- Confounds in “task-agnostic” claim: Reliance on BM25 pruning and overlap features blurs whether gains come from universal representations vs. lexical heuristics; ablations are insufficient.
- Dataset hygiene: Potential overlap between wang and yodaqa is acknowledged but not quantified; this threatens fair comparisons and cross-dataset transfer claims.
- Incomplete analyses: Little error analysis, efficiency reporting, or ablation on dropout choices, candidate pruning, and feature flags; negative results (overfitting) are noted but not deeply investigated.

Questions to Authors
- Can you quantify wang–yodaqa overlap and ensure no train–test leakage in any reported setting?
- What is the performance without BM25 pruning and without overlap features across tasks?
- Will you release scripts for dataset construction and fixed evaluation splits/checksums?
- What are training/inference costs per model and task, and how do they trade off with accuracy?
