Summary and contributions
This paper advocates a unified view of sentence pair scoring tasks (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, STS, paraphrase) and reports cross-task experiments with a suite of standard neural architectures and strong IR baselines. The key contributions as I see them are:
1) A unified, open-source framework (dataset-sts/KeraSTS) that normalizes datasets, adapters, and models to enable consistent evaluation across f2 tasks, together with an evaluation protocol that explicitly addresses variance by averaging over 16 random seeds and reporting confidence intervals.
2) Two new datasets for answer selection/property selection (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) designed to mitigate issues in existing resources (small candidate sets, noisy/unstable validation/test splits), plus a critical analysis of the instability of the popular wang dataset.
3) Empirical findings across tasks, including strong IR baselines and preliminary transfer learning showing that RNNs trained on Ubuntu Dialogue transfer well to other f2 tasks.

Strengths
- Unification and tooling: Providing a modular, task-agnostic framework with shared model backbones and scorer modules is valuable for the community. By lowering friction to run consistent experiments across tasks, this work can improve comparability and reuse.
- Evaluation rigor: The paper makes a compelling case that single-run reporting on small/unstable datasets is unreliable; averaging across 16 seeds and reporting 95% CIs is a concrete and useful contribution. This is especially important for wang, where reported gains in prior work are within run-to-run variability.
- Strong baselines and fair perspective: Including TF-IDF/BM25 baselines, overlap features, and ensembling acknowledges the competitiveness of IR signals in answer selection, and the results appropriately reflect where neural models do and do not outperform.
- New datasets emphasizing realistic candidate sets: The focus on larger candidate pools and noisier retrieval-like settings addresses a common weakness of current answer selection benchmarks and should better highlight meaningful model improvements.
- Transfer learning signal: Pretraining an RNN on Ubuntu and fine-tuning on other tasks consistently improves performance, suggesting the feasibility of task-agnostic sentence-pair encoders. The practical guidance (e.g., avoid dropout mismatch, balance labels, use RMSprop) is helpful.

Weaknesses
- Limited algorithmic novelty: The model zoo (avg/DAN/CNN/biGRU/RNN-CNN/attention) is composed of known architectures and a reimplementation of a prior attention mechanism. The main novelty lies in the framework/datasets/analysis rather than new modeling ideas. This may limit the paper’s technical impact for a venue like CoNLL.
- Incomplete experimental transparency and ablations: The use of BM25 to prefilter to top-20 candidates and as an auxiliary feature is a strong design choice that can dominate performance in answer selection. There is no thorough ablation quantifying its effect (with/without prefilter; with/without BM25 input; effect of token-overlap flags). This makes it hard to assess the neural models’ standalone contribution and to compare fairly to prior work that may not use such prefiltering.
- Dataset overlap and reproducibility concerns: The paper notes shared ancestry and potential overlap between wang and yodaqa/large2470, cautioning against mixing them, but does not quantify overlap or enforce deduplication across splits. The release details are “redacted” in the submission, and dataset statistics for the new resources (size, positive/negative balance, candidate distributions, annotation quality) are incomplete. Mechanical Turk denoising is mentioned but not documented (guidelines, inter-annotator agreement, QA). These are central to the claimed dataset contribution.
- Comparisons to contemporaneous baselines are thin on some tasks: On Ubuntu, only simple neural baselines are cited; the paper uses only 10% of the released training pairs (1M), which undermines the claim of a new baseline on this large-scale task unless justified and compared to full-data results. On RTE/STS, the paper acknowledges lagging SOTA but provides little analysis as to why or how the proposed framework helps close the gap.
- Presentation quality: There are numerous typos, truncated sentences, dangling figure/table references, inline footnotes, and incomplete citations. These issues hinder readability and make it difficult to verify claims (e.g., figures “Fig. 1–4” and tables not clearly visible here; several references have placeholders).

Questions to authors
- Dataset overlap: What is the exact overlap (pair-level and question-level) between wang and yodaqa/large2470 across train/dev/test? Did you perform deduplication across splits and across datasets? Please quantify leakage risk and, if present, re-run affected experiments.
- BM25 prefiltering: How often does the top-20 BM25 prefilter remove all positives for a question? Please report MAP/MRR with and without prefiltering, and with/without BM25 as a feature. Also report results without token-overlap flags to isolate their effect.
- Transfer learning: What is the absolute and relative gain from Ubuntu pretraining across tasks with confidence intervals, and how does this compare to simple multi-task training on mixed task batches? Did you try freezing lower layers vs. full fine-tuning systematically?
- Ubuntu usage: Why is only 10% of Ubuntu training data used? How do results scale with more data? How do your best models compare to Kadlec et al. (2015) under identical candidate settings?
- New datasets: Please provide detailed statistics (instances, candidate distributions, label balance), annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, and licensing details. Are the candidate retrieval pipelines (e.g., YodaQA) fixed and released for reproducibility?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide per-task ablations and error analyses to understand where neural models help beyond IR, especially on entity-heavy and compositional phenomena.
- For statistical reporting, consider paired randomization or bootstrap tests over queries for MAP/MRR in addition to CIs across runs.
- Clarify hyperparameters per task (dropout, hidden sizes, scorer choice), early stopping selection given validation instability, and training compute/time.
- Consider subword/character embeddings to mitigate OOV and domain-specific jargon (notably Ubuntu).
- Clean up writing and references; ensure all resources (code, data, preprocessing) are accessible upon publication.

Overall, this work’s primary value is infrastructural and methodological: a unified framework, new datasets with more realistic retrieval characteristics, and a careful evaluation protocol addressing variance. With stronger ablations, clearer dataset documentation, and improved presentation, it could make a solid contribution. The modeling component is incremental.
