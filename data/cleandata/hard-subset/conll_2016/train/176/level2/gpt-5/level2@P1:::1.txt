Summary and main contributions
This paper argues for a unified view of sentence pair scoring (f2) tasks and presents an empirical framework to study them across diverse datasets (answer sentence selection, next utterance ranking, RTE, and STS). The authors evaluate several neural architectures within a common pipeline, propose improved evaluation methodology with variance-aware reporting, introduce two new datasets for answer selection and property selection, and demonstrate cross-task transfer learning.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A unified, open-source benchmarking framework (dataset-sts/PySTS/KeraSTS) with task adapters, standardized training/evaluation, strong IR baselines, and variance-aware reporting (16-run averages with 95% CIs).
2) New datasets for answer selection and property selection (yodaqa/large2470, wqmprop), designed to address deficiencies of widely used wang-style data and to provide more realistic, harder evaluation settings.
3) Empirical findings across tasks, including that simple, task-agnostic neural models benefit from transfer learning (Ubuntu Dialog pretraining) and that attention and IR ensembling matter most on large candidate-set IR-style tasks.

Strengths
- Unification and open-source impact: The paper fills a real need by consolidating heterogeneous f2 tasks into a single, reusable framework. The availability of loaders, adapters, and model implementations should materially lower the barrier for future work and enable more reliable comparisons across tasks.

- Methodological rigor on variance and baselines: Reporting performance averaged across 16 seeds with confidence intervals is a major step forward compared to single-run reporting, especially on datasets like wang where run-to-run variance is high. Inclusion of strong IR baselines (TF-IDF/BM25), and explicit ensembling with neural outputs, grounds the comparisons in realistic IR settings.

- New, harder datasets with practical characteristics: The yodaqa/large2470 dataset addresses well-known issues of wang (small/uneven candidate sets, tiny dev/test splits, unstable metrics) and appears more challenging, making progress more measurable. The property-selection dataset (wqmprop) adds a distinct, practically motivated f2 task and broadens coverage.

- Cross-task transfer learning result: The finding that an RNN pretrained on Ubuntu Dialog yields strong gains when transferred and fine-tuned on other f2 tasks is compelling and directly supports the paper’s central thesis about universal f2 modeling.

- Breadth of models under a common setup: The study compares several families (avg/DAN, CNN, biGRU, RNN-CNN, attention) with consistent hyperparameterization and training protocols, providing a useful “watermark” for future improvements.

Weaknesses
- Limited model novelty and partial coverage of SOTA: The architectures are mostly standard baselines, and the paper does not include more recent/stronger sentence encoders (e.g., sequential attention for RTE, tree-structured models, or strong sentence embedding approaches). On RTE/STS the results lag behind SOTA, and the paper does not close the loop with a deeper discussion of why and how to get closer.

- Potential data leakage and dataset hygiene: The authors note shared ancestry between wang and yodaqa/large2470 and caution against mixing them within a single model instance. However, the paper does not quantify overlap, provide a de-duplication script, or report any controlled experiments to estimate the effect. Without clear hygiene guarantees, results across these datasets may be confounded.

- Evaluation coupling to BM25 pruning: For answer selection, training/evaluation prunes candidate sets to top-20 by BM25 and feeds BM25 as an explicit feature. While this mirrors IR pipelines, it also entangles neural model evaluation with the BM25 stage and may bias performance (e.g., hiding failures on truly hard negatives outside top-20). The paper would be stronger with ablations: without pruning; without BM25 features; and with alternative first-stage retrieval.

- Dataset construction noise and documentation: The new answer selection data is labeled via matching gold answers in retrieved passages, which risks label noise (partial matches, paraphrases, entity normalization). There is no quantification of noise rates, inter-annotator agreement for the denoising step, or guidance on license/distribution terms beyond “open source.” Clear documentation and quality analysis would increase trust and adoption.

- Limited qualitative/error analysis: The paper emphasizes aggregate metrics and variance, but provides little insight into where models succeed/fail (e.g., length effects, entity-heavy queries, lexical overlap dependence, attention visualizations). Similarly, the effect of token-level features (overlap, capitalization, numeric flags) is not ablated.

Questions to authors
- Overlap analysis: Can you quantify and publish the overlap between wang and yodaqa/large2470 (train/dev/test) at the question and sentence levels? Will you release scripts to enforce non-overlap for future work?

- BM25 reliance: What is the performance of the neural models without BM25 input features and without top-20 pruning? How sensitive are conclusions to using a different first-stage retriever or a larger top-k?

- Dataset quality: How were positive/negative labels validated in yodaqa/large2470? Can you report an estimate of labeling noise (e.g., sample audit), and any inter-annotator agreement for the Mechanical Turk denoising?

- Transfer protocol: For the Ubuntu-pretrained RNN, could you provide more specifics (embedding freezing or not, exact learning rates, batch sizes, training time)? Did you try mixed-task multi-task training, or pretraining on SNLI for RTE/STS as a comparison?

- Feature ablations: What is the incremental contribution of the added token features (overlap flags, capitalization, numeric) across tasks? Similarly, can you show an ablation of dropout settings and L2 strength, given the reported overfitting?

Additional comments
- The call for variance-aware reporting is important; consider proposing a concrete, minimal protocol (e.g., 5 seeds with CIs, fixed evaluation code) and providing a public leaderboard to encourage adoption.
- Qualitative case studies (e.g., attention heatmaps, success/failure examples before/after transfer) would make the transfer learning story more convincing and actionable.
