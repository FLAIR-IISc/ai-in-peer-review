This paper presents a comprehensive review of sentence pair scoring, framing it as a unified task (f2) applicable to diverse NLP problems like answer selection, entailment, and next utterance ranking. The central claim is that a universal semantic comprehension model can be achieved through task-independent models adapted via task-specific output layers and transfer learning. A significant contribution is the introduction of two new datasets, yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, designed to address shortcomings in existing resources. Finally, the work provides a valuable empirical evaluation of various neural network architectures within this unified framework, including a demonstration of the benefits of transfer learning.

Strengths:

1. **Unified Framework:** The paper’s articulation of sentence pair scoring as a fundamental task (f2) is a strong point. This framing offers a compelling perspective for consolidating disparate research areas and promotes comparability. The emphasis on task-independent models with adaptable output layers represents a pragmatic approach to achieving generalizable semantic understanding.
2. **Dataset Contribution:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 is a substantial contribution. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of the existing Wang dataset and convincingly demonstrates the need for a larger, more reliable resource. The description of the dataset creation process, including the use of Mechanical Turk for denoising, lends credibility to its quality.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The systematic evaluation of various neural models (RNNs, CNNs, attention-based networks) provides a valuable benchmark for future research.  The inclusion of both neural and non-neural baselines, along with thorough statistical analysis including confidence intervals, strengthens the validity of the results. The explicit comparison to IR baselines is particularly insightful.
4. **Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** The successful transfer learning results using the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset offer empirical support for the central hypothesis regarding universal semantic comprehension. This provides a strong indicator that models can learn generalizable features applicable across different tasks.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Novelty in Model Architectures:** While the evaluation is thorough, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs) are not novel. The paper relies on established architectures, and the primary contribution lies in the evaluation framework and datasets, not the models *per se*.  This is not necessarily a fatal flaw, but the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the architectural choices and their limitations.
2. **Overemphasis on Implementation Details:** The paper includes a considerable amount of detail regarding the software framework (dataset-sts and KeraSTS) and specific hyperparameters. While reproducibility is important, this level of detail detracts from the core contributions of the work. It would be more impactful to focus on the insights derived from the experiments rather than the implementation specifics.
3. **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper acknowledges that the models lag behind the state-of-the-art on tasks like RTE and STS. However, the discussion of this gap feels somewhat cursory. A more detailed analysis of the reasons for this gap and potential avenues for improvement would be valuable.  Specifically, elaborating on the limitations of the chosen architectures for these tasks would strengthen the work.
4. **Ambiguous Reporting of Results:** The reporting of results is occasionally confusing, particularly concerning the baseline performances and the impact of the BM25 overlap feature. Clarifying how these features interact with different models and datasets would improve the clarity of the findings.

Questions to Authors:

1.  The paper introduces yodaqa/large2470 as addressing limitations in the Wang dataset. Could you provide a more quantitative comparison of the statistical properties of the two datasets beyond what is presented in Figure 1?  Specifically, what is the correlation between validation and test set performance on Wang, and how does this compare to yodaqa/large2470?
2.  The paper mentions applying L2 regularization.  Was the regularization strength tuned for each task and model, or was a single value used across all experiments? If tuned, what method was used for tuning?
3.  The paper briefly mentions dropout during transfer learning. Could you elaborate on why applying dropout during transfer learning if it wasn't applied during the original training is important? What is the underlying rationale?
