This paper presents a comprehensive review of the sentence pair scoring task, framing it as a unified problem (f2) applicable to diverse NLP tasks like answer selection, entailment, and next utterance ranking. The authors aim to (A) unify research with task-independent models and task-specific adaptation, (B) improve evaluation methodology, and (C) demonstrate the feasibility of universal semantic comprehension models via cross-task transfer learning.  The primary contribution lies in the systematic survey and benchmarking of various models (IR baselines, DNNs) across multiple datasets, accompanied by the introduction of two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) designed to address limitations in existing resources. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the release of the dataset-sts framework, facilitating research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Survey & Unified Framework:** The paper provides a much-needed consolidation of a fragmented research area.  By framing diverse tasks under the common f2 formulation, the authors offer a valuable perspective and lay the groundwork for more generalizable models. This is a significant contribution towards establishing a clearer research direction.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 is a strong positive.  The authors clearly articulate the shortcomings of existing datasets (e.g., wang) and provide a substantial, well-motivated alternative. The process of generating this dataset—leveraging a QA system for candidate sentence retrieval and Mechanical Turk for labeling—is well-described. wqmprop, while smaller in scope, also addresses a specific need in property selection.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation & Baselines:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting results; it includes detailed discussions of evaluation methodology, acknowledging the inherent randomness in neural models and providing confidence intervals. The inclusion of strong IR baselines (TF-IDF, BM25) provides a crucial point of comparison, demonstrating the current state of neural models relative to more traditional approaches.
4. **Open-Source Framework:** The release of dataset-sts is commendable. Providing a readily usable toolkit (with PySTS and KeraSTS) will significantly lower the barrier to entry for researchers interested in exploring this area.  This promotes reproducibility and further development.
5. **Demonstration of Transfer Learning:** The evidence presented for the effectiveness of transfer learning—specifically, pre-training on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset—is encouraging and supports the authors’ hypothesis regarding universal semantic comprehension.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the survey of models is thorough, the models themselves (RNNs, CNNs, attention mechanisms) are relatively standard and lack substantial novelty.  The paper primarily *evaluates* existing architectures rather than proposing new ones.  This is not necessarily a fatal flaw, given the focus on unification and benchmarking, but it does limit the paper’s impact in terms of algorithmic advancement.
2. **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper mentions lagging behind state-of-the-art on RTE and STS tasks but doesn’t provide detailed comparisons or analysis. It would be more informative to present specific results against existing SOTA models, even if they are not competitive, to give a clearer picture of the current landscape.  Simply stating they “lag behind” is insufficient.
3. **Dataset-Specific Tuning:** The discussion of hyperparameters suggests significant tuning, primarily on yodaqa/large2470. While this is understandable, a more detailed description of the tuning process and sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to different hyperparameter settings.
4. **Ambiguity in "Universal" Comprehension:** The concept of “universal semantic comprehension” is somewhat vaguely defined. The paper doesn’t clearly articulate what constitutes universal comprehension and how it’s being measured beyond cross-task transfer. A more rigorous definition or operationalization of this concept would strengthen the claims.
5. **Potential Bias in Dataset Creation:** While the yodaqa/large2470 dataset addresses some issues with wang, the reliance on the YodaQA system for candidate sentence retrieval introduces a potential bias. If YodaQA has inherent biases in its retrieval process, these could be reflected in the dataset.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detailed results comparing your best-performing models to current state-of-the-art models on the RTE and STS tasks? Even if the gap is substantial, a direct comparison would be valuable.
2.  Can you elaborate on the dataset creation process for yodaqa/large2470, specifically addressing potential biases introduced by the YodaQA system used for candidate sentence retrieval?  Were any steps taken to mitigate such biases?
3.  You mention that tuning was primarily done on yodaqa/large2470. How sensitive are your results to different hyperparameter settings across the various datasets? Did you observe significant variations in optimal hyperparameters?
4. Could you provide a more concrete definition of what you mean by "universal semantic comprehension" and how it is being measured in your experiments?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The URL for the dataset-sts framework was redacted, but I assume it’s readily available on GitHub as mentioned in the text.
* The use of confidence intervals in the results is excellent and a significant improvement over reporting single point estimates.
