## Review of "Sentence Pair Scoring: Towards Unified Framework for Text Comprehension"

This paper presents a comprehensive review of the sentence pair scoring task, framing it as a unifying problem across diverse NLP areas like question answering, textual entailment, and dialogue ranking. The authors identify a need for standardized evaluation and propose a framework for cross-task transfer learning using neural network models. A key contribution is the introduction of two new datasets, yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop, designed to address shortcomings in existing benchmarks like the wang dataset (small size, uneven distribution). The paper also provides a thorough survey of baseline methods, both neural and non-neural, and establishes a new baseline performance on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Framework:** The paper successfully positions sentence pair scoring as a fundamental task, offering a valuable unifying perspective. This framing is beneficial for researchers working across different subfields of NLP.
2. **New Datasets:** The introduction of yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop is a significant contribution. Addressing the limitations of existing datasets (size, quality) is crucial for reliable model evaluation and progress. The detailed description of dataset creation is helpful.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The extensive evaluation across multiple datasets and models, including detailed comparisons with strong IR baselines, provides a solid foundation for future research. The inclusion of confidence intervals is commendable, acknowledging the inherent randomness in neural model performance.
4. **Practical Implementation:** The release of the dataset-sts framework and KeraSTS library promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Models:** While the paper provides a strong empirical analysis, the neural models themselves (RNNs, CNNs) are relatively standard. The exploration of more advanced architectures (e.g., transformers, attention mechanisms beyond attn1511) is limited.
2. **Transfer Learning Results:** The transfer learning results, while promising, are not deeply investigated. The authors mention the importance of not applying dropout during retraining, but a more detailed analysis of the impact of different transfer learning strategies would be valuable.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to understand the contribution of specific components (e.g., BM25 integration, word overlap features) to the overall performance. 
4. **Verbose and Repetitive:** The paper is quite lengthy and contains some repetitive phrasing. Condensing the content would improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered when tuning models on the wang dataset, beyond the issues of small size and uneven distribution?
2.  What were the primary motivations for choosing the specific architecture (dot-product scorer) for the transfer learning experiments?
3.  Do you have any insights into why the performance on the new datasets is lower than on established benchmarks like SNLI or SICK? Is this due to inherent difficulty, or potentially issues with the dataset creation process?
