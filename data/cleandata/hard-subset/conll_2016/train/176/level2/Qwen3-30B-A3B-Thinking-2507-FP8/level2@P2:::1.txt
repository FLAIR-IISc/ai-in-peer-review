

**Summary and Contributions**
The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, arguing that a single model architecture could serve multiple semantic similarity tasks (Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, RTE, STS) with task-specific adaptation. The main contributions are: (1) a unified software framework (dataset-sts) for implementing and evaluating sentence pair models across tasks; (2) new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) addressing limitations of existing datasets; and (3) improved evaluation methodology with confidence intervals and transfer learning experiments demonstrating model reusability.

**Strengths**
1. The evaluation methodology significantly advances the field by recognizing that neural model performance is highly sensitive to random initialization and dropout, leading to substantial variance in results. Reporting 95% confidence intervals (rather than single runs) is a major improvement that enables more reliable comparisons across models and tasks.

2. The new yodaqa/large2470 dataset is well-motivated and addresses critical limitations of the existing wang dataset (small candidate sets, uneven distributions, noisy validation/test splits). The authors provide strong evidence that their new dataset will yield more meaningful model improvements.

3. The transfer learning results are compelling: training on Ubuntu Dialogue and transferring to other tasks consistently outperforms task-specific training. This provides the strongest evidence yet that models can learn general semantic representations, supporting the paper's central thesis.

4. The paper's software framework (dataset-sts) is well-designed, modular, and open source, significantly lowering the barrier for future research in this area. This is a valuable contribution that will likely be adopted by the community.

**Weaknesses**
1. The claim of "universal semantic comprehension models" is overstated. While transfer learning shows promise, the results don't demonstrate true universality—models still underperform on certain tasks (e.g., STS), and the paper doesn't show models transfering effectively in multiple directions or from multiple source tasks. The framework is task-specific despite the claim of universality.

2. The yodaqa/large2470 dataset appears to have significant overlap with the existing wang dataset (as the authors themselves acknowledge), undermining its validity as a separate benchmark. The authors note "there may be some overlaps, even across train and test splits," which could lead to inflated performance on the new dataset.

3. The paper fails to properly compare with recent state-of-the-art models on the tasks evaluated. For instance, on the STS task, the authors claim "our models clearly yet lag behind the state-of-art" but provide no detailed comparison to recent models (e.g., those using attention or more sophisticated architectures), making it impossible to assess the significance of their results.

4. The transfer learning experiments are limited to a single source task (Ubuntu Dialogue). The paper doesn't explore whether transfer learning would work equally well from other tasks or whether multi-task learning could further improve performance, which would strengthen the argument for universal models.

**Questions to Authors**
1. Given the overlap between wang and yodaqa datasets, how did you verify that the test sets are truly disjoint? Could you provide statistics on the overlap between train/test splits?
2. The paper claims to demonstrate "universal semantic comprehension," but the results show significant task-specific performance variations. What evidence do you have that the models are learning truly universal representations rather than task-specific adaptations?
3. How do your results compare to recent state-of-the-art models for Answer Sentence Selection (e.g., those using more sophisticated attention mechanisms)? Could the performance gap be due to architectural limitations rather than lack of universality?
