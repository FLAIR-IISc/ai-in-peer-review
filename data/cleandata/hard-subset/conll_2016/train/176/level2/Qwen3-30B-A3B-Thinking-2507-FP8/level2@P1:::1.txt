

Summary: This paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, including Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Semantic Textual Similarity. The authors introduce a new software framework (dataset-sts), two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop), and conduct a comprehensive evaluation of various neural models across multiple tasks with proper statistical analysis.

Main contributions:
1. A unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks that allows for consistent evaluation across different semantic similarity problems
2. Introduction of new datasets addressing limitations of existing benchmarks (small size, uneven candidate sets, etc.)
3. Comprehensive evaluation methodology with proper statistical analysis (95% confidence intervals) to address randomness in neural models

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a much-needed unification of fragmented research on sentence pair scoring tasks. By framing these diverse problems under a single framework, the authors enable meaningful cross-task comparisons and model evaluations that have been lacking in the literature. This organizational contribution is significant for the field.

2. The authors' careful evaluation methodology, including reporting 95% confidence intervals across multiple training runs, addresses a critical issue in neural NLP research where results are often reported based on a single random seed. This is a substantial methodological improvement over standard practice, as demonstrated by their analysis showing that standard deviations on the wang dataset are larger than the gaps between successive state-of-the-art papers.

3. The new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) are well-motivated and address specific limitations of existing benchmarks. The authors clearly explain why previous datasets (like wang) have problematic characteristics and how their new datasets overcome these issues. The yodaqa/large2470 dataset, in particular, offers a more realistic and challenging benchmark for Answer Sentence Selection.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's claim of demonstrating "universal semantic comprehension models" is overstated. While the authors show modest improvements from transfer learning (e.g., training on Ubuntu Dialogue and transferring to other tasks), the results show that models still perform significantly worse than task-specific state-of-the-art on several benchmarks. The evidence for "universal" models is limited, and the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of what "universal" means in this context.

2. The paper lacks sufficient comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art models for each specific task. For example, the authors state their models "lag behind the state-of-art on the RTE and STS tasks" without providing specific comparisons with the most recent SOTA models. Including such comparisons would strengthen the paper's claims about the baseline performance.

3. The software framework (dataset-sts) is presented as a contribution, but the paper doesn't sufficiently explain how it improves upon existing NLP frameworks or libraries. A brief comparison with popular alternatives (like AllenNLP or spaCy) would help readers understand the framework's unique value.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper reports modest improvements from transfer learning (e.g., Ubu.RNN model). Could you elaborate on why transfer learning from Ubuntu Dialogue (Next Utterance Ranking) doesn't generalize better to other tasks? Is there a specific characteristic of the Ubuntu Dialogue task that makes it particularly useful for transfer?

2. The paper mentions that the new yodaqa/large2470 dataset has "much lower baseline performances" than existing datasets. Could you provide specific numbers showing how the baseline performance on this new dataset compares to the baseline performance on the wang dataset, and how this difference affects the interpretability of the results?

3. The authors state that "it is not possible to statistically determine its relation to state-of-art on the wang Answer Sentence Selection dataset" (referring to their best model). Could you clarify what statistical tests were performed and why they were inconclusive?
