

Summary: The paper proposes a unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks, including Answer Sentence Selection, Next Utterance Ranking, Recognizing Textual Entailment, and Semantic Textual Similarity. It introduces two new datasets (yodaqa/large2470 and wqmprop) to address limitations in existing resources, establishes a comprehensive evaluation methodology with proper statistical analysis, and demonstrates transfer learning across tasks to support the hypothesis of universal semantic comprehension.

Primary contributions: (1) A unified framework for sentence pair scoring tasks with modular implementation; (2) New datasets addressing critical limitations in existing resources; (3) Improved evaluation methodology with statistical confidence intervals.

Strengths: The paper identifies a genuine fragmentation issue in the NLP field where similar tasks are treated separately, which is a significant pain point for researchers. The new datasets effectively address well-documented limitations of existing resources (small size, uneven splits, etc.), particularly for Answer Sentence Selection. The evaluation methodology with 95% confidence intervals represents a substantial methodological improvement over typical reporting practices in the field. The transfer learning results provide compelling evidence for the paper's core hypothesis, showing consistent performance gains when transferring models across tasks. The open-source framework (dataset-sts, KeraSTS) is a valuable community resource that will facilitate future research.

Weaknesses: The paper's claim of "universal semantic comprehension" is overstated given the limited evidence provided. The transfer learning results are only demonstrated for one specific model (RNN) and one source task (Ubuntu Dialogue), with no comprehensive evaluation across multiple source tasks or model architectures. The paper fails to provide a thorough comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art models for the specific tasks, particularly for tasks like Recognizing Textual Entailment where the models lag behind the state-of-the-art. The paper does not sufficiently explain why certain models (e.g., CNNs vs. RNNs) perform better on specific tasks beyond noting dataset size effects, which limits the theoretical contribution.

Questions to Authors: How does the proposed framework compare to other unified approaches for sentence pair tasks that have been proposed in the literature? What specific evidence supports the claim of "universal semantic comprehension" beyond transfer learning results from a single source task? The paper mentions that transfer learning from Ubuntu Dialogue significantly outperforms task-specific training, but does not provide quantitative comparison with the best results reported for the target tasks in the literature. How does the framework's performance compare to the current state-of-the-art on the Ubuntu Dialogue dataset?

Additional Comments: The paper's claim that "even simple neural models learn universal semantic comprehension" requires stronger evidence. The transfer learning results are promising but insufficient to support such a broad claim without demonstrating consistent transfer across multiple model architectures and source tasks. The paper's evaluation methodology is strong, but the statistical analysis could be further improved by including effect sizes alongside confidence intervals to better quantify the practical significance of performance differences. The paper's assertion that the new datasets will make "future model improvements more apparent" is reasonable, but the paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of why the new datasets present more challenging evaluation scenarios. The framework's modularity is a strength, but the paper would be more persuasive if it demonstrated how easily the framework could be extended to include additional tasks or model architectures.
