## Review of “Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec”

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that combines the strengths of word embeddings (word2vec) and topic models (LDA) by jointly learning dense word vectors with Dirichlet-distributed latent document mixtures of topic vectors. The core idea is to incorporate document-level context into the skip-gram model, while maintaining interpretable topic representations through a non-negative simplex constraint.

The most significant contribution of this work is the practical demonstration of seamlessly integrating differentiable topic modeling into a word embedding framework. While hybrid approaches have been explored, the ease with which lda2vec leverages automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer) is a key strength, enabling rapid prototyping and efficient training.  A secondary contribution is the empirical validation on both the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News corpus, showcasing the model's ability to learn coherent topics and capture semantic relationships specific to these domains. Finally, the inclusion of a detailed ablation study (Figures 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6) provides valuable insight into model performance and the interplay between different components.

Strengths:

1. **Practicality & Implementation:** The use of automatic differentiation is a strong advantage, removing a major barrier to entry for researchers exploring hybrid topic modeling approaches. The open-source implementation further enhances reproducibility and accessibility.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that lda2vec can learn meaningful topic representations and word embeddings. The use of topic coherence measures (Twenty Newsgroups) and qualitative analysis (Hacker News) supports the claim of interpretability. 
3. **Novel Combination:** The authors successfully integrate the strengths of word embeddings and topic modeling.  The sparse document mixtures, enforced by the Dirichlet prior, are particularly valuable for interpretability—a common drawback of dense document embeddings.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The inclusion of comprehensive experiments and visualizations, showcasing topic coherence, word similarities, and learned analogies, strengthens the paper's claims.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** The fundamental idea of combining word embeddings and topic models isn’t entirely new. The paper doesn't deeply contrast itself with prior hybrid approaches, and the specific implementation, while practical, lacks substantial algorithmic innovation.
2. **Dependency on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The Twenty Newsgroups experiment relies on pre-trained word embeddings. While stated as a design choice, it obscures the model’s ability to *learn* embeddings from scratch and potentially introduces bias.
3. **Hacker News Data Specifics:** The specialized tokenization used for the Hacker News corpus (noun phrases) might limit the generalizability of the results.  A more standard tokenization scheme would offer a stronger comparative baseline.



Questions to Authors:

1.  Can you provide a more detailed comparison to other hybrid topic modeling approaches (e.g., those leveraging neural topic models) highlighting the key differences and advantages of lda2vec?
2.  How sensitive is the model performance to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the Dirichlet concentration parameter (alpha) and the regularization strength (lambda)?
3.  What are the computational costs associated with training lda2vec compared to standard word2vec or LDA, especially for large-scale datasets?
