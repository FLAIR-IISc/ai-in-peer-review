

# Peer Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that integrates Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings to create interpretable document representations. The primary contributions are: (1) a unified framework that jointly learns dense word vectors and sparse document-topic mixtures, (2) a simple implementation approach using automatic differentiation that enables efficient training, and (3) empirical validation showing the model can learn coherent topics and meaningful semantic relationships. The authors position this as a bridge between the interpretability of topic models and the semantic richness of word embeddings.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Integration of Interpretability and Semantic Richness**: The core contribution is well-motivated and effectively executed. By embedding document representations as sparse mixtures over topic vectors (rather than dense vectors), the model achieves both interpretability (through the Dirichlet constraint) and semantic richness (through word embeddings). The experimental validation in Figure 3 demonstrates that topics are coherent and align with human interpretations (e.g., "Space" topic with astronomical terms), which is a significant advancement over purely dense representations that lack this interpretability.

2. **Practical Implementation and Efficiency**: The paper demonstrates how modern automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer) simplify the implementation of this hybrid model. The authors correctly note that traditional topic models require deriving new approximations, which is time-consuming, while their approach leverages existing frameworks. This practical aspect lowers the barrier to adoption for researchers interested in interpretable representations, making it a valuable contribution to the NLP community.

3. **Demonstration of Semantic Regularities**: The Hacker News experiments (Figures 4-6) convincingly show that the model learns not just topics but also meaningful semantic relationships (e.g., "Silicon Valley - California + technology" and "Bitcoin - digital currency"). This goes beyond typical topic models by demonstrating that the model captures relationships between tokens that are meaningful to the domain, which is a significant advance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparative Analysis**: The paper fails to compare lda2vec against state-of-the-art topic models (e.g., LDA, CTM, or recent neural topic models) on standard evaluation metrics. While the authors use topic coherence (Palmetto) as a measure, they don't show how their approach compares to existing methods on this metric. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the model offers meaningful improvements over the current state of the art.

2. **Omission of Key Related Work**: The paper doesn't cite the most relevant prior work, particularly the LDA2VEC model by Tang et al. (2016), which was presented at the same conference (ACL 2016) and shares a similar goal. This omission is significant as it undermines the novelty claim. The paper also doesn't discuss how lda2vec differs from or improves upon LDA2VEC, which would be necessary to establish the contribution's significance.

3. **Inadequate Justification of Key Parameters**: The authors set α = n⁻¹ (where n is the number of topics) for the Dirichlet concentration parameter to encourage sparsity, but provide no ablation study or theoretical justification for this specific choice. Without exploring how different α values affect model performance, it's unclear whether this is a necessary design choice or merely a heuristic.

4. **Limited Scalability Analysis**: While the Hacker News corpus is large (75 million tokens), the paper doesn't discuss how the model scales with corpus size or topic count. The training time and memory requirements aren't reported, which is critical for a model intended for practical use on large datasets. This omission limits the assessment of the model's real-world applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does lda2vec compare to LDA2VEC (Tang et al., 2016) on standard topic modeling metrics? The authors cite the paper but don't discuss how their approach differs or improves upon it.

2. The paper states that "topic vectors are shared and are a common component to all documents." How does this sharing mechanism differ from standard topic models, and what theoretical advantage does it provide over independent topic vectors?

3. The model uses a fixed window size of five tokens for context. How sensitive is the model to this parameter, and would a dynamic window size improve performance?

4. The authors claim the model "learns linear relationships between them" (word vectors), but don't provide a quantitative analysis of how these relationships compare to those learned by pure word embedding methods (e.g., Word2Vec). How does the semantic quality of the word vectors change when trained jointly with topic models versus standalone?

The paper presents an interesting hybrid model with clear practical advantages, but the lack of comparative analysis and omission of key related work significantly undermine its contribution. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the submission. The empirical results are promising, but without proper comparison to existing methods, the paper's significance remains unclear.
