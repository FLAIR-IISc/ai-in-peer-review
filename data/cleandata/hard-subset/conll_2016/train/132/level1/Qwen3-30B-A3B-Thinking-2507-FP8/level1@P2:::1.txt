

# Review for "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes lda2vec, a model that integrates Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings to create interpretable document representations. It extends the Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) objective by adding a Dirichlet likelihood term to induce sparsity in document-topic mixtures. The main contributions are:
1. A unified framework for learning interpretable document representations that combine topic modeling with word embeddings
2. A sparsity-inducing Dirichlet likelihood term that produces interpretable document-topic mixtures
3. Demonstration of semantic relationships preserved in the learned representations

## Strengths

1. **Novel integration of topic modeling and word embeddings**: The paper successfully bridges two complementary approaches (topic modeling for interpretability and word embeddings for semantic richness) in a unified framework. The model's simplicity of implementation within automatic differentiation frameworks is a significant practical contribution. Unlike previous work that treats these as separate problems, lda2vec learns both word vectors and topic mixtures simultaneously, preserving semantic relationships while producing interpretable document representations.

2. **Effective sparsity-inducing mechanism**: The Dirichlet likelihood term with α < 1 effectively produces sparse, interpretable document-topic mixtures. The paper demonstrates this through topic coherence scores (0.712 for "Space" topic in Twenty Newsgroups) that correlate with human evaluations (Röder et al., 2015). The authors provide clear evidence that without this sparsity-inducing term, topics become incoherent, making their approach theoretically sound and empirically validated.

3. **Preservation of semantic relationships**: The paper compellingly demonstrates that lda2vec preserves the semantic richness of word embeddings through its ability to solve word analogies specific to the corpus (e.g., "Silicon Valley" - "California" + "technology"). Figure 6 shows the model successfully captures relationships important to the Hacker News community, indicating the representations have semantic meaning beyond just topic discovery.

4. **Practical implementation and accessibility**: The model is straightforward to implement using existing automatic differentiation frameworks like Chainer. The authors provide open-source code, documentation, and unit tests, significantly lowering the barrier to adoption. This practical aspect is particularly valuable for researchers and practitioners interested in interpretable unsupervised representations.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparative evaluation with relevant baselines**: The paper compares with LDA but fails to compare with more recent topic modeling approaches that also integrate word embeddings, such as LDA2Vec (which the authors cite but don't properly contrast with). The paper should demonstrate how lda2vec improves upon or differs from existing approaches, particularly in terms of topic coherence, computational efficiency, and interpretability.

2. **Limited evaluation beyond topic coherence**: While topic coherence is a useful metric, the paper lacks evaluation on downstream tasks where document representations would be used (e.g., text classification, clustering). Without demonstrating utility on practical applications, it's difficult to assess the true value of the representations beyond their interpretability.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (e.g., the effect of the Dirichlet likelihood term alone, the effect of shared word vectors for pivot/target). This makes it challenging to determine which aspects of the model are most critical to its performance.

4. **Unclear novelty statement**: The paper doesn't clearly articulate how it differs from existing work. The reference to LDA2Vec in the introduction suggests this work builds on existing research, but the paper fails to properly position itself within the literature or clarify the specific innovations that distinguish it from prior approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does lda2vec specifically differ from and improve upon LDA2Vec (which is mentioned in the references but not properly compared)? What novel contributions does this model offer over existing work in the space?

2. Have you evaluated the document representations learned by lda2vec on downstream tasks (e.g., text classification, clustering)? If so, how do they compare to representations from LDA and other topic modeling approaches?

3. Could you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of the Dirichlet likelihood term to the model's performance, and the effect of using the same word vectors for pivot and target representations? This would help clarify which components are most important.

4. The paper uses topic coherence as the primary evaluation metric, but coherence scores are known to be sensitive to vocabulary size and corpus characteristics. How do you address potential biases in this metric for your specific evaluation scenarios?
