

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that combines Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings to create document representations that are both interpretable (like traditional topic models) and semantically rich (like word embeddings). The model extends Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) to include document-topic mixtures with a Dirichlet prior to enforce sparsity in document-topic memberships.

Main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):

1. A practical implementation that successfully bridges the gap between interpretable topic models and semantic word embeddings, creating document representations that are both human-interpretable and semantically meaningful.

2. The sparsity-inducing Dirichlet prior with low concentration parameter (α = n⁻¹) that effectively enforces interpretable document-topic mixtures, addressing a key challenge in topic modeling where dense representations hinder interpretation.

3. The simple implementation within automatic differentiation frameworks, making the model accessible to practitioners without requiring extensive expertise in probabilistic modeling.

## Strengths

1. The model successfully addresses a significant gap in the literature by combining the interpretability of topic models with the semantic richness of word embeddings. The empirical results on both standard (Twenty Newsgroups) and specialized (Hacker News) corpora demonstrate that lda2vec can learn meaningful topics and semantic relationships. For example, the model identifies "Space" and "Encryption" topics in Twenty Newsgroups that align with actual newsgroup labels, and discovers domain-specific themes in Hacker News like "Housing Issues" and "Bitcoin."

2. The Dirichlet prior with α = n⁻¹ is a well-motivated and effective solution to the problem of document-topic sparsity. The authors convincingly demonstrate that without this sparsity-inducing term, document weights become too homogeneous, making interpretation difficult. This is a critical contribution to the field, as interpretability is one of the key advantages of topic models over dense vector representations.

3. The implementation is genuinely simple to incorporate into existing automatic differentiation frameworks, as claimed. The model can be trained using standard frameworks like Chainer (which the authors use) and the open-source code availability significantly lowers the barrier to entry for practitioners. This practical contribution is particularly valuable given the complexity of deriving new approximations for probabilistic topic models.

4. The model's ability to learn domain-specific semantic relationships within specialized corpora is compelling. The word analogy results in Figure 6 show that lda2vec captures meaningful relationships specific to the Hacker News community (e.g., "Silicon Valley" ≈ "California" + "technology," "Bitcoin" ≈ "digital currency"), demonstrating that the model learns semantically meaningful representations rather than just superficial associations.

## Weaknesses

1. The novelty of the approach is limited. The paper builds on existing work (LDA, word2vec) without sufficiently differentiating itself from prior work in neural topic models. For instance, Miao et al. (2016) and Zhang et al. (2016) have already explored similar hybrid approaches, and the paper doesn't provide a clear comparative analysis to establish how lda2vec advances beyond these existing methods.

2. The evaluation lacks comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art topic modeling approaches. The authors compare only to traditional LDA in terms of topic coherence (which they claim correlates with human evaluations), but don't compare to neural topic models or other hybrid approaches. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether lda2vec offers meaningful improvements over existing methods.

3. The evaluation metrics are insufficiently comprehensive. Topic coherence is used as the primary evaluation metric, but the paper doesn't report perplexity (a standard metric for topic models) or compare document representations to other methods (e.g., TF-IDF, LDA, or neural document embeddings) on downstream tasks. The word analogy evaluation is interesting but doesn't demonstrate how these representations would perform on actual NLP tasks.

4. The computational complexity and scalability of the model are not addressed. Given the size of the Hacker News corpus (75 million tokens), it's important to know how the model scales with corpus size compared to traditional topic models or neural approaches. The paper mentions using GPUs for speed, but doesn't provide concrete runtime or memory usage comparisons.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does lda2vec compare to recent neural topic models (e.g., Miao et al. 2016, Zhang et al. 2016) in terms of topic coherence, interpretability, and computational efficiency? Specifically, how does it perform compared to these models on standard benchmarks?

2. The paper uses α = n⁻¹ for the Dirichlet prior to induce sparsity. How sensitive is the model to this choice of α, and what happens if α is set to 1 (which would not induce sparsity)? Would the model still perform well without the sparsity constraint?

3. The paper claims that the model is "simple to incorporate into existing automatic differentiation frameworks." Could you elaborate on what specific changes would be needed to adapt this model to popular frameworks like PyTorch or TensorFlow, and how much additional code would be required compared to a standard word2vec implementation?

4. The authors don't report any comparison to traditional LDA in terms of perplexity or other standard topic modeling metrics. How does lda2vec compare to LDA on these metrics, and why might the authors have chosen to focus only on topic coherence?
