Summary and contributions
The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that trains word embeddings via a Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) objective while simultaneously learning document representations as simplex-constrained mixtures over a set of learned topic vectors. The context vector used to predict targets is the sum of a pivot word vector and the document vector; document vectors are themselves convex combinations of topic vectors with a Dirichlet-inspired sparsity regularizer on the mixture weights. The model is implemented in an autodiff framework and evaluated qualitatively on Twenty Newsgroups (20NG) and a large Hacker News corpus, with topic coherence (Cv) reported for 20NG.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple architectural coupling of SGNS with sparse, interpretable document-topic mixtures: documents are mixtures over topic vectors that live in the same space as word embeddings, enabling interpretability and vector arithmetic across words and topics.
2) An empirical exploration showing reasonable topic coherence on 20NG and qualitative analyses (nearest neighbors, analogies) on Hacker News, with open-source code for reproducibility.
3) A practical recipe to induce sparsity in document-topic proportions using a Dirichlet-log likelihood regularizer on softmax-normalized mixture weights.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: The model cleanly bridges two paradigms—SGNS-style word embedding training and LDA-like interpretable document mixtures—without variational inference. The “sum of word and document vector” context is intuitive, and the document-as-mixture over topic vectors constrains document representations to be interpretable.
- Shared embedding space enabling diagnostics: Topics, documents, and words inhabit the same vector space. This makes it easy to inspect topics by nearest words, explore topic–word linearities, and perform analogies that mix words and topics—useful for practitioners who desire interpretability and exploration tools.
- Qualitative results are convincing: On both 20NG and Hacker News the presented topics look coherent and domain-relevant. The reported Cv on 20NG (~0.56 on average, with best topics >0.6–0.7) is in a reasonable regime for that corpus and preprocessing, and the analogy examples show meaningful corpus-specific semantics.
- Reproducibility and practicality: The implementation in an autodiff framework (with GPU support), along with released code and preprocessing scripts, lowers the barrier to adoption. The approach can plausibly be extended or integrated with other neural components.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: The core SGNS context defined as word + document vector closely resembles PV-DM (Paragraph Vector—Distributed Memory; Le & Mikolov, 2014), which also sums doc and word vectors to predict tokens. The new element here is constraining the document vector to be a convex mixture over learned topic vectors with a sparsity prior. Related lines—Topical Word Embeddings (e.g., Liu et al., 2015), DocNADE (Larochelle & Lauly, 2012), Gaussian LDA (Das et al., 2015), and neural variational document/topic models (Miao et al., 2016)—also connect embeddings and topics. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning and empirical comparison to these.
- Evaluation is inadequate and lacks baselines: There is no quantitative comparison against standard LDA, CTM, DocNADE, PV-DM/doc2vec, TWE, or NVDM on 20NG. Reported coherence values are not contextualized with LDA baselines under identical preprocessing. No document-level downstream task (e.g., 20NG classification from learned proportions/vectors) is included. The analogy results are purely anecdotal; no quantitative analogy or word similarity benchmarks are reported relative to SGNS trained standalone on the same data. Without these, it is difficult to assess trade-offs vs. simpler baselines.
- Methodological ambiguities and clarity issues: The paper states that the softmax maps “latent vectors initialized in R300 onto the simplex,” but the simplex dimensionality should be the number of topics, not the embedding dimension; this needs correction/clarification. It is also unclear why input and output embeddings are tied; SGNS typically uses separate matrices and tying can harm performance. Other standard SGNS choices (subsampling frequent words, dynamic context windows) are not discussed. Dropout on embeddings is mentioned but not specified (rates, where applied).
- Lack of ablations and sensitivity analyses: The central claims hinge on the Dirichlet regularizer producing sparse, interpretable mixtures. There is no quantitative sparsity metric (e.g., entropy of document topic distributions) nor ablations varying α and λ to show coherence/sparsity trade-offs. Similarly, the contribution of the document vector (word+doc vs. word-only context), topic count, negative sampling exponent, and tied vs. untied embeddings are not analyzed.
- Missing probabilistic grounding and likelihood evaluation: While the Dirichlet term is used as a regularizer, the model is not a coherent generative topic model; perplexity or held-out likelihood are not defined or reported. This obscures how lda2vec compares on modeling the data distribution versus established topic models.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the dimensionality of the variables in Section 2.2.1: the softmax should output a K-dimensional simplex (K = number of topics), but the text suggests R300. Is this a typo or is there a different construction?
- How does lda2vec compare quantitatively to: (a) LDA on 20NG (Cv and classification accuracy using topic proportions), (b) PV-DM/doc2vec (classification accuracy and interpretability), and (c) DocNADE/NVDM (topic coherence and classification)? Please include at least LDA and PV-DM baselines with identical preprocessing.
- What is the quantitative sparsity of document-topic mixtures (e.g., average entropy, proportion of mass in top-3 topics) and how does it vary with α and λ? Can you show a coherence–sparsity trade-off curve?
- Why tie the input and output embeddings in SGNS? Did you try untied embeddings and, if so, how did it affect coherence and analogy performance?
- Did you apply subsampling of frequent tokens (as in word2vec) and dynamic window sizes? If not, what was the effect when you tried them?
- On 20NG, what is the Cv for a standard LDA baseline (same tokenization and vocabulary)? Are your best coherences statistically significantly better/worse?
- Do topics collapse onto frequent words without additional regularization? Have you tried decorrelation or orthogonality penalties between topic vectors to reduce redundancy?

Overall, the idea is appealing and practically useful, but the current manuscript lacks rigorous empirical comparisons and methodological clarity needed for a strong claim at CoNLL. Strengthening baselines, ablations, and clarity would substantially improve the paper.
