### Review for "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces lda2vec, a novel model that jointly learns dense vector representations for words and topics, alongside sparse, interpretable document-level topic mixtures. The model elegantly extends the word2vec Skip-Gram Negative-Sampling (SGNS) architecture by redefining the context vector. Instead of representing only the pivot word, the context is modeled as the sum of the pivot word's vector and a document-specific vector. This document vector is, in turn, constructed as a linear combination of a set of global topic vectors, weighted by document-specific proportions. To achieve the sparse, interpretable mixtures characteristic of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), the model incorporates a Dirichlet-likelihood term into the overall loss function, which is then optimized end-to-end using an automatic differentiation framework.

In my view, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Conceptually Elegant Hybrid Model:** The core contribution is the formulation that unifies the predictive power of dense word embeddings with the interpretability of probabilistic topic models. The architecture, which embeds words, documents, and topics into a shared vector space and combines local (word) and global (document) context via a simple vector addition, is both novel and intuitive.
2.  **A Practical Framework for Hybrid Model Development:** By demonstrating that a probabilistic constraint like a Dirichlet prior can be successfully translated into a regularization term within a standard neural network loss function, the authors provide a powerful and accessible template for future research. This approach sidesteps the need for deriving complex, model-specific inference algorithms (like Variational Inference or Gibbs Sampling), significantly lowering the barrier to entry for developing and testing new hybrid models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Model Motivation and Design:** The synthesis of SGNS and LDA is extremely well-motivated. The goal of combining the strengths of both approaches—semantic regularities from word embeddings and interpretable document summaries from topic models—is highly desirable. The mechanism for achieving this, `~cj = ~wj + ~dj`, is a clean and simple way to inject global document context into the local word prediction task. The embedding of topics as vectors in the same space as words is a powerful feature, allowing for direct interpretation of topics by finding their nearest word neighbors, which the authors demonstrate effectively.
2.  **Methodological Significance:** The paper's successful implementation within an automatic differentiation framework is a key strength. At a time when topic modeling research was often siloed due to its reliance on specialized probabilistic methods, this work shows a clear path for integrating its core ideas into the rapidly advancing deep learning ecosystem. This not only makes the proposed model more accessible but also more extensible. One could imagine easily adding other components or loss terms to this framework.
3.  **Convincing Qualitative and Quantitative Results:** The evaluation provides strong evidence that the model works as intended. On the 20 Newsgroups dataset, the model achieves high topic coherence scores, as measured by the Cv metric, which is a strong, contemporary choice for automated topic quality evaluation. The qualitative examples of topics from both this dataset and the larger, more challenging Hacker News corpus are compelling and clearly interpretable. Furthermore, the demonstration of learned, domain-specific word analogies on the Hacker News data confirms that the word embedding component of the model remains powerful and is not compromised by the addition of the topic structure.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Direct Comparison to Baselines:** While the results presented for lda2vec are promising, the paper critically lacks quantitative comparisons to established baseline models. To properly assess the model's performance, it is essential to see how it stacks up against its constituent parts. For topic quality, how do the reported topic coherence scores on 20 Newsgroups compare to a well-tuned LDA implementation (e.g., from Mallet or Gensim)? For word embedding quality, how do the vectors perform on standard intrinsic tasks (e.g., word similarity or the analogy task) compared to standard SGNS trained on the exact same preprocessed corpus? Without this context, it is difficult to judge whether lda2vec represents a superior trade-off or simply a different one.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Hyperparameters and Ablation:** The model's behavior is heavily dependent on the balance between the SGNS loss and the Dirichlet regularization term, controlled by the hyperparameter λ. The paper sets λ=200 with little justification beyond stating it "works well." A sensitivity analysis showing how topic coherence and/or training stability varies with λ would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and reproducibility. The central claim that topics become "incoherent" without the Dirichlet term (i.e., when λ=0) is a crucial piece of motivation, but it is not backed by quantitative evidence in an ablation study. Presenting the coherence scores for λ=0 would make this argument much more forceful.
3.  **Potential Scalability Concerns and Unexplored Modeling Choices:** The Dirichlet loss term is defined over the entire corpus. The paper's solution is a minibatch-based approximation, scaling the loss by the ratio of minibatch size to corpus size. This is a pragmatic choice, but its theoretical justification and impact on model convergence are not discussed. A brief analysis of the model's computational complexity and memory footprint, particularly as the number of documents grows, would be beneficial. Additionally, the choice of vector addition to combine word and document vectors is justified by analogy, but it is one of many possible composition functions (e.g., concatenation, element-wise product). A brief discussion of why addition is preferred or an exploration of alternatives would have been valuable.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide comparative results against standard baselines? Specifically, could you report the topic coherence (Cv) for a standard LDA model and word embedding quality scores (e.g., on an analogy task) for a standard SGNS model, trained on your preprocessed 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets?
2.  The additive composition `~cj = ~wj + ~dj` is a core design choice. Did you investigate whether the document vector `~dj` might dominate the pivot word vector `~wj` in documents with very strong topic signals, thereby reducing the model's ability to capture fine-grained local context? Does the use of dropout fully mitigate this?
3.  Could you elaborate on the selection of λ=200? Was this value found via a grid search, and if so, could you provide a plot or table showing how topic coherence is affected by different values of λ? This would provide valuable guidance for practitioners wishing to apply your model.
