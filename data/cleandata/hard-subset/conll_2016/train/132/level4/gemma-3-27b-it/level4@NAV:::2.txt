This paper introduces a neural topic model, cleverly extending the word2vec objective to simultaneously learn document embeddings and, through sparsification, approximate topic model behavior. The core idea is compelling and the illustrative examples provided are promising. However, the paper is significantly weakened by the complete lack of empirical evaluation. The reliance solely on example tables offers no insight into the representativeness of these examples, nor any comparative performance against established topic models – both traditional and neural. Without quantitative evaluation, assessing the true value of the proposed model is impossible, and this severely hinders acceptance. Potential avenues for evaluation include: (1) utilizing the learned document representations in a supervised document categorization task, benchmarked against a topic model with comparable topic cardinality; or (2) directly evaluating document similarity based on pairwise comparisons of the derived topic vectors using a dataset with similarity annotations. 

While the authors’ commitment to code release is commendable, the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted manuscript compromises anonymity, which, technically, warrants rejection (though the final decision rests with the PCs). 

Further concerns include:

*   The selection criteria for examples presented in Figures 3-6 is unclear, raising concerns about potential cherry-picking.
*   Section 2.2.1 proposes calculating word representations for topics via pairwise comparison with vocabulary words. The computational feasibility of this approach, given realistic vocabulary sizes and topic numbers, is questionable.
*   Section 3.1 mentions token identification using SpaCy, but the specific method (e.g., noun chunk extraction, as in Section 3.2, or other) remains undefined. This is unclear given the subsequent use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which contain limited multiword terms.
*   The paper doesn’t address how the model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. While training on the entire document collection mitigates this in the presented experiments, applying the trained model to novel documents will introduce discrepancies between updated and pre-trained embeddings.
*   The observation that 20 topics performed optimally on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising given the dataset’s structure. An information-theoretic comparison to true document labels could provide a simple evaluation metric and allow for direct comparison with LDA.
*   The authors should consider related work, specifically: Cao et al. (AAAI 2015), Nguyen et al. (TACL 2015), and Shamanta et al. (MLKD 2015).
