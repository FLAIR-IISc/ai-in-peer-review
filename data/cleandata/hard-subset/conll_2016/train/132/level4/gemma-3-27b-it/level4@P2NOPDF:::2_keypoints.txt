- Core idea (extending word2vec to jointly learn document embeddings and sparsify them into topics) is promising and illustrative examples are compelling.
- Primary weakness: complete lack of empirical evaluation—only qualitative examples without justification of representativeness or baseline comparisons.
- Suggested evaluation strategies: (1) use learned document reps in supervised classification vs. topic models with equal topic count; (2) evaluate on datasets with document similarity annotations via pairwise topic-vector comparisons.
- Inclusion of a GitHub link breaches anonymity; this could warrant rejection and requires Program Chairs’ attention.
- Example selection for Figures 3–6 is unclear, raising concerns of cherry-picking a subset of learned topics.
- Computational feasibility concern: calculating word representations for topics via pairwise comparisons with the vocabulary may be prohibitively expensive at large scale.
- Tokenization is underspecified—unclear whether SpaCy noun chunks or another tokenization method (and how multi-word terms from pre-trained word2vec are handled) are used.
- Out-of-vocabulary handling is not addressed; applying the model to novel documents may be problematic if embeddings for new terms differ from training.
- Topic cardinality claim (20 topics best on 20 Newsgroups) is unsurprising; recommend information-theoretic comparison to document labels and to LDA-style baselines.
- Missing comparisons to recent neural topic models (e.g., Cao et al. 2015, Nguyen et al. 2015, Shamanta et al. 2015).
- Minor editorial issues: typo "it's similarity" → "its similarity"; awkward phrasing about the “topic basis” being affected; consider “terms” vs “words” in Figure 5 caption.
- Reference list formatting is inconsistent; ensure uniform citation style and include conference names for proceedings papers.
