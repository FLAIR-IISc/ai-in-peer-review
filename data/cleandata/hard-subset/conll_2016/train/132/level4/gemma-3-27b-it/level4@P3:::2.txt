This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, lda2vec, extending the objective of word2vec to simultaneously learn document embeddings and constrain them through sparsification, mimicking the output of a topic model. The core idea of jointly learning word and document representations with a Dirichlet prior to encourage interpretability is promising.

The primary contribution of this work is the lda2vec model itself – a novel combination of word embeddings and topic modeling within a differentiable framework. This allows for end-to-end training and leverages the strengths of both approaches: the semantic understanding of word vectors and the interpretability of topic models. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration of the feasibility of this approach using the Chainer framework and the resulting ability to rapidly prototype and evaluate model variations. Finally, the release of the code is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Model Formulation:** The lda2vec model is a compelling approach to bridging the gap between dense, distributed representations and sparse, interpretable topic models. The incorporation of a Dirichlet prior directly addresses the need for interpretable document representations, a known limitation of purely neural approaches. The integration of word2vec’s skipgram architecture with Dirichlet-distributed topic mixtures is a creative and potentially powerful combination.
2. **Practical Implementation & Framework Choice:** Utilizing the Chainer framework for rapid prototyping and GPU acceleration demonstrates a practical consideration for real-world application and scalability. This is a significant advantage, as developing and iterating on probabilistic topic models can be computationally expensive. The availability of the code further strengthens this point.
3. **Demonstrated Semantic Learning:** The results on the Hacker News dataset, particularly Figure 5, show that the model successfully learns meaningful relationships between tokens specific to that domain. This indicates that the learned word vectors capture nuances that might be missed by general-purpose embeddings. Figure 6 further supports this claim by demonstrating the ability to perform analogical reasoning within the learned vector space.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The most significant weakness of this submission is the complete absence of quantitative empirical evaluation. The reliance solely on illustrative examples makes it impossible to assess the true performance of lda2vec compared to existing topic modeling techniques (e.g., LDA, NMF) or other neural topic models. Without comparative results, the novelty and effectiveness of the model remain unsubstantiated. Addressing this requires a rigorous evaluation using standard metrics and datasets.
2. **Potential for Cherry-Picking:** The presentation of topics in Figures 3 and 4 relies on manual selection, raising concerns about potential cherry-picking. The authors should provide a more comprehensive and systematic evaluation of the generated topics, perhaps by reporting average coherence scores or performing a qualitative assessment on a larger sample.
3. **Scalability Concerns & Computational Cost:** While the model is conceptually appealing, the computational cost of calculating word similarities with all vocabulary terms for topic interpretation (as discussed in Section 2.2.1) could be prohibitive for large vocabularies. The paper needs to address this scalability concern, perhaps by proposing approximation techniques or demonstrating that the calculation is feasible within a reasonable timeframe.
4. **Handling of Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Terms:** The paper does not explicitly address how the model handles OOV terms when applied to new documents. This is a crucial consideration for real-world deployment and needs to be discussed, along with potential solutions (e.g., using subword embeddings or character-level models).

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Evaluation Plan:** What quantitative metrics and datasets did you consider for evaluating the performance of lda2vec? What were the reasons for not including a quantitative evaluation in the submission? Please outline a specific plan for evaluating your model against established baselines (LDA, NMF, Cao et al. 2015, Nguyen et al. 2015, Shamanta et al. 2015) if accepted.
2. **Topic Selection Criteria:** What criteria were used to select the topics presented in Figures 3 and 4? Can you provide quantitative measures (e.g., coherence scores) to support the quality of these topics?
3. **Scalability Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with calculating word similarities for topic interpretation? How does this cost scale with vocabulary size and the number of topics? Are there any optimization strategies you considered to mitigate this cost?
4. **OOV Handling:** How does the model handle OOV terms when applied to new documents? What strategies could be employed to address this issue?
