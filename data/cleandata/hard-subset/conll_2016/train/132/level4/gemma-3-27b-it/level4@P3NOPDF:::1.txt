## Review for CONLL 2016

This paper explores a combination of word2vec and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), a potentially interesting approach. However, the primary weakness of the submission lies in the lack of clarity in its technical presentation. Section 2 requires a significant rewrite to allow readers familiar with word2vec and LDA to understand the proposed model and how these techniques are integrated. The current description fails to provide even a high-level, intuitive understanding of the methodology.

Specifically, several points within the introduction and technical sections are unclear. The third paragraph of the introduction is particularly problematic. The claim that the work “requires deriving a new approximation” lacks context; the approximation *of what* is being referenced? The justification for the time required to develop prototypes is also absent. Furthermore, the assertion that feature evaluation is easier needs explanation. 

Several design choices within the model are not adequately motivated. Why are the same word vectors used for both pivot and target words, differing from the standard word2vec approach? What is the reasoning behind this decision? The phrase “separate words from a marginal distribution” requires clarification – what distribution is being referenced, and what does separation entail? The concept of “co-adaptation” is introduced without sufficient definition. Finally, the statement “If we only included structure up to this point” lacks necessary context; what specific “structure” is being discussed? A minor grammatical error exists: “it’s similarity” should be “its similarity.”

A significant concern is the violation of anonymity in Footnote 1. This must be removed to maintain the integrity of the double-blind review process.

Crucially, the paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation. Presenting only example clusters, as done in this submission, is insufficient by current standards in NLP research. Figure 2 *appears* to present quantitative results, but these are relegated to the lengthy caption and are not discussed within the main text. A proper evaluation with established metrics and a clearly defined experimental setup is essential. 

The conclusion’s claim that the model “solves word analogies” is an overstatement, given that the paper only provides a handful of selectively chosen examples (e.g., “king + queen”). Such limited evidence does not support a broad claim of solving a complex NLP task.

Finally, the citation for Chang et al. is incomplete. The conference/journal name is listed as “Advances in…”, leaving the reviewer to guess the correct venue. Accurate and complete bibliographic information is expected.



In summary, while the core idea has potential, the current presentation is too unclear and the evaluation is insufficient to support the claims made. A thorough revision addressing these points – particularly a clearer explanation of the technical details and a robust evaluation – is necessary for this paper to be considered for acceptance.
