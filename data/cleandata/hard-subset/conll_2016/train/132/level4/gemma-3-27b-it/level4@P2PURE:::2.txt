This paper presents a neural topic model, extending the word2vec objective to concurrently learn document embeddings and subsequently imposing sparsification to emulate topic model outputs. The proposed model is promising, and the illustrative examples are compelling. However, a significant weakness is the complete absence of empirical evaluation. The paper relies solely on example tables, lacking any assessment of their representativeness or comparison to established topic models – both traditional and neural. This omission prevents a robust evaluation of the model’s true value and substantially hinders acceptance. Potential avenues for evaluation include: (1) leveraging the learned document representations in a supervised document categorization task, comparing performance against a topic model with equivalent topic cardinality as an indirect measure of representation quality; or (2) directly evaluating performance on a dataset with document similarity annotations, using pairwise comparison of topic vectors. While the authors’ commitment to releasing code is commendable, the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted paper compromises anonymity (a breach potentially warranting rejection, at the discretion of the PCs).

Further concerns include:

- The selection criteria for the examples in Figures 3-6 are unclear, raising the possibility of cherry-picking.
- Section 2.2.1 discusses calculating word representations for topics via pairwise comparison with vocabulary words, a computationally expensive process for realistic vocabulary sizes and topic counts. The feasibility of this approach requires justification.
- Section 3.1 states the use of SpaCy to identify “tokens,” but lacks specifics. Are noun chunks exclusively extracted, as in Section 3.2, or is another method employed? This ambiguity is exacerbated by the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which contain limited multiword terms.
- The paper does not address handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. While the current experimental setup trains the model on the entire document collection, this may not always be the case. Applying the trained model to novel documents will necessitate updating word2vec embeddings, potentially rendering pre-trained, non-updated embeddings incomparable.
- The finding of optimal performance with 20 topics on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising given the corpus’s structure. An information-theoretic comparison to true document labels could provide a simple, direct evaluation, enabling comparison with LDA.
- The following neural topic models should be considered for comparison:
Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).
