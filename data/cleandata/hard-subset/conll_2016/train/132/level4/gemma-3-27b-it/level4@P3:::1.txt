This paper proposes lda2vec, a model that attempts to combine the strengths of distributed word representations (like word2vec) with the interpretability of topic models (like LDA). The core idea is to jointly learn word vectors and document representations, where document representations are formed as sparse mixtures of topic vectors, leveraging automatic differentiation for efficient training. The authors claim to offer a model that facilitates interpretable document representations alongside the benefits of dense word embeddings, potentially easing analysis and insight generation. While the core idea is interesting, the presentation and evaluation fall significantly short of expectations for a CONLL publication.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Integration of Topic Modeling and Word Embeddings:** The primary, and most significant, contribution is the attempt to directly integrate a probabilistic topic modeling approach (specifically, a Dirichlet-distributed mixture model) within a word embedding learning framework (word2vec). While conceptually straightforward, this combination has not been widely explored, and the potential for jointly optimizing these representations is a valuable direction.
2.  **Leveraging Automatic Differentiation:** The authors rightly point out the benefits of using automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer in this case) for rapid prototyping and experimentation with topic models. Highlighting this practical advantage is useful, particularly for researchers less familiar with these tools.
3.  **Sparse Document Representations:** The imposed Dirichlet prior, aiming for sparse document-topic distributions, is a reasonable approach to retain interpretability, a key benefit traditionally associated with topic models but often lost in dense vector representations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Potential for Interpretability:** The model's design, explicitly incorporating sparse document-topic proportions, offers a path towards more interpretable document representations compared to purely dense vector approaches. This is a crucial advantage in many applications where understanding *why* a model makes a certain prediction is important.
2.  **Sound Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind combining topic models and word embeddings – to leverage the strengths of both paradigms. The discussion of the limitations of traditional topic models and dense vector representations is well-framed.
3.  **Relevance to the Community:** The problem addressed is relevant to the CONLL community, which often deals with tasks requiring both semantic understanding and interpretability of text data.
4. **Open-Source Implementation:** The authors provide an open-source implementation, which facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Clarity & Technical Inaccessibility:** The most significant weakness is the poor clarity of the technical presentation, especially in Section 2. The explanation of how the models are combined is convoluted and difficult to follow, even for readers familiar with both word2vec and LDA. Key equations are presented without sufficient context or explanation of their components. For example, the derivation of the loss function (Equation 1) needs substantial clarification.
2.  **Insufficient Evaluation:** The evaluation is extremely weak. The paper lacks rigorous quantitative evaluation on standard datasets or tasks. Presenting only a few cherry-picked examples of topic coherence (Figure 2) and word analogies (Figure 5) is insufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the model. The lack of comparison to baselines, both standard topic models and dense vector approaches, hinders assessment of the contribution.
3.  **Unmotivated Design Choices:** Several design choices lack clear justification. For example, the decision to use the same word vectors for pivot and target words (unlike the standard word2vec approach) is not adequately explained. The impact of this choice on performance and interpretability needs to be discussed.
4.  **Anonymity Violation:** Footnote 1 breaks the double-blind review process and should be removed.
5.  **Vague Language and Informal Writing:** The paper contains instances of imprecise language and informal writing style (e.g., “makes no sense to me,” “it’s similarity”) that detract from its overall quality.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Clarify the Model Architecture:** Provide a more detailed and accessible explanation of the lda2vec model, perhaps with a step-by-step walkthrough of the inference process. A diagram illustrating the flow of information through the network would be extremely helpful.
2.  **Justify Key Design Choices:** Explain the rationale behind key design decisions, such as using the same word vectors for pivot and target words, and the choice of parameters (e.g., α, λ, β).
3.  **Present a Rigorous Evaluation:** Conduct a thorough evaluation of lda2vec on standard datasets and tasks, comparing its performance to relevant baselines (e.g., LDA, word2vec, doc2vec). Include quantitative metrics and statistical significance tests. What specific downstream tasks demonstrate the benefit of the jointly learned representations?
4.  **Explain the Coherence Metric:** Expand on the explanation of the topic coherence metric (Cv) and why it is a suitable measure for evaluating the quality of the learned topics.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list contains some formatting inconsistencies.
*   The paper would benefit from a more concise and focused presentation. Remove unnecessary details and focus on the core contributions and their evaluation.
*   The authors should carefully proofread the manuscript to eliminate grammatical errors and improve clarity.
