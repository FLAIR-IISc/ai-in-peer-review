This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, extending the objective of word2vec to also learn document embeddings, which it then constrains through sparsification, hence mimicking the output of a topic model. I found the model proposed by the authors to be promising, and the illustrative examples presented are highly encouraging. However, a significant shortcoming of the paper is the complete lack of empirical evaluation. The assessment relies entirely on example tables, without any discussion of their representativeness or comparison to established, or other neural, topic models. Without empirical evaluation, it is impossible to gauge the true merit of the model, making it difficult to justify acceptance. Potential avenues for evaluation include: (1) employing the topic representation of each document in a supervised document categorization task, comparing performance against a topic model with identical topic cardinality (providing an indirect evaluation of representation quality); or (2) performing direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations, based on pairwise comparison of topic vectors. While code release is commendable, the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted paper compromises anonymity (which, strictly speaking, is grounds for rejection, though the decision rests with the PCs).

Other concerns:

- The selection criteria for the examples in Figures 3-6 are unclear. Presenting a subset of the learned topics potentially introduces cherry-picking bias.
- In Section 2.2.1, the discussion of calculating word representations for topics via pairwise comparison with each vocabulary word raises feasibility concerns given the computational cost for reasonable vocabulary sizes and topic counts. Is this approach truly practical?
- Section 3.1 mentions identifying “tokens” using SpaCy, but lacks detail on *how*. Is it limited to noun chunks, as in Section 3.2, or is another approach used? This is unclear, especially given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which contain limited multi-word terms.
- The paper does not address handling Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) terms. While training on the entire document collection mitigates this issue in the presented experiments, the model may be applied to novel documents. Updating word2vec embeddings will create a discrepancy between tuned and untuned (OOV) embeddings, affecting comparability.
- The finding that 20 topics performed optimally on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising given the dataset's inherent structure. A simple evaluation method could involve an information-theoretic comparison to the true document labels, allowing direct comparison with LDA and other topic models.
- The following neural topic models should be considered for comparison:
  Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension." In AAAI, pp. 2210-2216. 2015.
  Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics 3 (2015): 299-313.
  Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, pp. 441-457. Springer International Publishing, 2015.
