This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, extending the objective of word2vec to also learn document embeddings, which it then constrains through sparsification, hence mimicking the output of a topic model.

I find the model proposed by the authors to be well-motivated and the examples presented highly promising. However, a significant limitation is the lack of empirical evaluation. The assessment relies entirely on illustrative examples without quantifying their representativeness or comparing the model’s performance against standard or neural topic models. This absence makes it difficult to assess the model's true value and hinders acceptance. Potential evaluation strategies include: (1) utilizing the topic representation of each document in a supervised document categorization task, comparing against a traditional topic model with the same topic cardinality to indirectly assess representation quality; or (2) employing direct evaluation using a dataset with document similarity annotations, comparing topic vectors directly. 

The authors’ decision to release code is commendable, but the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted manuscript compromises anonymity and, strictly speaking, could be grounds for rejection. 

Further concerns include:
- The selection criteria for examples presented in Figures 3-6 are unclear. Presenting a subset of topics could introduce cherry-picking bias.
- Section 2.2.1 discusses calculating word representations for topics via pairwise comparison with all vocabulary words, a computationally expensive operation for large vocabularies and numerous topics; its feasibility should be addressed.
- The use of SpaCy for tokenization in Section 3.1 is not fully explained. Is it limited to noun chunks, as implied by the description in Section 3.2? Clarification is needed, especially given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings which typically contain limited multi-word terms.
- The model’s handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms is not discussed. While the current experiments train on the entire corpus, applying the trained model to novel documents raises concerns about the comparability of updated and non-updated (OOV) word2vec embeddings.
- The finding that 20 topics performed best on the Twenty Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising given the dataset’s structure. An information-theoretic comparison with the true document labels could provide a simple, objective evaluation and facilitate comparison with LDA.
- The authors should consider and compare their work against related neural topic models, such as those by Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).

Minor points:
- line 315: “it’s similarity” should be “its similarity”
- line 361: the phrase "topic basis are affected" is awkward and requires clarification.
- In the caption of Figure 5, “words” should perhaps be “terms.”
- The reference formatting is inconsistent (e.g., “Advances in…” vs. “Advances in Neural…”), and some entries, such as Röder et al., are missing conference details.
