This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, extending the word2vec objective to also learn document embeddings, which are then constrained through sparsification, mimicking the output of a topic model.

I found the model proposed by the authors to be compelling and the presented examples highly promising. However, the paper lacks empirical evaluation. The assessment relies entirely on illustrative examples, without any indication of their representativeness or a direct comparison to standard or neural topic models. Without such evaluation, it is difficult to gauge the true value of the model and thus accept the paper. Potential evaluation strategies include: (1) using the topic representation of each document in a supervised document categorization task, comparing performance against a topic model with the same topic cardinality (serving as an indirect evaluation of representation quality); or (2) evaluating directly on a dataset with document similarity annotations, using pairwise comparison of topic vectors. The release of code is commendable, but the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted paper compromises anonymity (which, strictly speaking, is grounds for rejection, though the decision rests with the PCs).

Other issues:

- How were the examples in Figures 3-6 selected? Presenting a subset of the actual topics potentially introduces cherry-picking bias.
- In Section 2.2.1, you discuss calculating word representations for topics via pairwise comparison with each word in the vocabulary. However, this process would be computationally expensive for a reasonable vocabulary size and number of topics. Is this approach truly feasible?
- In Section 3.1, you state that you identify “tokens” using SpaCy – how? Do you extract noun chunks (and only noun chunks), as in Section 3.2, or another type of chunk? Given that you subsequently use pre-trained word2vec embeddings (which include a limited number of multi-word terms), the tokenization process remained unclear.
- How does your model handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms? While training on the entire document collection in your experiments mitigates this issue, applying the trained model to novel documents will present a challenge. Updating the word2vec token embeddings means that any non-updated (OOV) embeddings will not be directly comparable to the tuned embeddings.
- The finding that 20 topics performed best on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising, given its composition. A simple evaluation approach could involve information-theoretic comparison with the true document labels, allowing for direct comparison with LDA and other methods.
- Several other relevant neural topic models should be considered for comparison:
Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension." In AAAI, pp. 2210-2216. 2015.
Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics 3 (2015): 299-313.
Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, pp. 441-457. Springer International Publishing, 2015.
