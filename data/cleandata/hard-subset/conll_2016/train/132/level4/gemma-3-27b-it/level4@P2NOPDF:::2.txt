This paper introduces a neural topic model that extends the word2vec objective to simultaneously learn document embeddings, subsequently sparsifying them to emulate topic model outputs. The core idea is promising and the illustrative examples provided are compelling. However, the paper’s primary weakness is the complete lack of empirical evaluation. The assessment relies solely on qualitative examples, without any justification for their representativeness or comparison to established topic models—both traditional and neural. This absence hinders a proper evaluation of the model's true value and significantly impacts its acceptation. Potential evaluation strategies include: (1) employing the learned document representations in a supervised document categorization task, comparing performance against a topic model with equivalent topic cardinality, or (2) evaluating directly on a dataset with document similarity annotations via pairwise comparison of topic vectors. 

While the authors’ commitment to releasing code is commendable, the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted manuscript compromises anonymity – a violation of the review process that, strictly speaking, could warrant rejection. The Program Chairs will need to consider this.

Several other points require clarification:

*   **Example Selection:** The criteria for selecting examples in Figures 3-6 are unclear. Presenting a subset of learned topics raises concerns about potential cherry-picking.
*   **Computational Feasibility:** Section 2.2.1 proposes calculating word representations for topics through pairwise comparison with vocabulary words. This approach’s feasibility is questionable given the computational cost for large vocabularies and topic numbers.
*   **Tokenization:** Section 3.1 mentions using SpaCy to identify “tokens”, but lacks detail. Are noun chunks extracted exclusively, as in Section 3.2, or is another method employed? This is unclear, particularly given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which inherently contain limited multi-word terms.
*   **Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Terms:** The paper doesn't address handling OOV terms. While training on the entire document collection mitigates this issue in the presented experiments, applying the model to novel documents will require consideration of how updated word2vec embeddings compare to those unseen during training.
*   **Topic Cardinality & Evaluation:** The finding that 20 topics performed optimally on the 20 Newsgroups dataset is unsurprising given the corpus’s structure. A simple, information-theoretic comparison to document labels could provide an additional, direct evaluation against LDA and similar models.
*   **Related Work:** The paper would benefit from comparison to recent neural topic models, including: Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).

Minor issues:

*   Line 315: Change "it's similarity" to "its similarity".
*   Line 361: Clarify what it means for the “topic basis” to be affected; the phrasing “are” is awkward.
*   Figure 5 caption: Consider using “terms” instead of “words”.
*   Reference Formatting: The reference list exhibits inconsistencies in formatting. Ensure uniform style (e.g., "Advances in…" vs. "Advances in Neural…", include conference names for all proceedings papers like Roder et al.).
