This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, extending the objective of word2vec to also learn document embeddings, which it then constrains through sparsification, thus mimicking the output of a topic model.

I found the model proposed by the authors to be promising, and the examples presented are encouraging. However, a significant weakness is the lack of empirical evaluation. The assessment currently relies entirely on example tables, without any indication of their representativeness or comparison to standard or neural topic models. Without empirical evaluation, it’s difficult to assess the model’s true value and justify its acceptance. Potential avenues for evaluation include: (1) using the topic representation of each document in a supervised document categorization task, comparing it to a traditional topic model with the same topic cardinality (an indirect assessment of representation quality); or (2) direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations, using pairwise comparison of topic vectors.

The authors’ open-source code release is commendable, but the inclusion of the GitHub link in the submitted paper compromises anonymity (a potential basis for rejection, subject to the PCs’ discretion).

Other concerns:

- How were the examples in Figures 3-6 selected? Presenting a subset of topics raises concerns about cherry-picking.
- In Section 2.2.1, the calculation of word representations for topics via pairwise comparison with all vocabulary words appears computationally expensive for reasonable vocabularies and topic numbers. Is this feasible in practice?
- Section 3.1 mentions using SpaCy to identify "tokens"—how? Is it primarily noun chunk extraction, as implied by Section 3.2? Given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings (with limited multiword terms), the tokenization process requires clarification.
- How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms? While the current experiments train over the entire corpus, applying the trained model to novel documents introduces challenges with OOV words not having updated embeddings.
- The finding that 20 topics performed best on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising given its structure. A simple evaluation using information-theoretic measures comparing the results to true document labels, and to LDA, could strengthen the analysis.
- The following neural topic models should be considered for comparison:
Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).

Minor issues:

- Line 315: "it's similarity" should be "its similarity."
- Line 361: Clarify how the "topic basis" is affected; the phrasing "and the are" is awkward.
- In the caption of Figure 5, “terms” might be more accurate than “words.”
- The reference formatting is inconsistent (e.g., “Advances in…”, “Advances in Neural…”, missing conference names).
