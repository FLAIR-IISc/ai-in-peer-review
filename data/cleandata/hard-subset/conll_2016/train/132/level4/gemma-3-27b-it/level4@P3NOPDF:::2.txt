This paper proposes a neural topic model, framing topic modeling as a constrained learning problem where the objective extends word2vec to simultaneously learn document embeddings. These embeddings are then sparsified to mimic topic distributions. The core idea is promising, and the illustrative examples provided are compelling. However, the paper’s primary weakness is the complete lack of empirical evaluation. The assessment relies entirely on qualitative examples, and there is no discussion of their representativeness or comparison to established topic modeling approaches – both traditional and neural. Without quantitative evaluation, it is difficult to assess the true value of the proposed model and support its claims. 

Several avenues for evaluation could have strengthened the paper. First, the document representations could be used as features in a supervised document categorization task, allowing comparison to a standard topic model with the same number of topics. This would provide an indirect measure of representation quality. Second, a direct evaluation could be performed using a dataset with document similarity annotations, evaluating the correlation between the topic vectors and human judgments of similarity.

The authors’ commitment to releasing code is commendable, but the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted paper compromises anonymity – a potentially fatal flaw, though the decision to accept or reject on this basis rests with the Program Chairs.

Several other points require attention:

* **Example Selection:** The examples presented in Figures 3-6 are not clearly justified. The selection process must be transparent to avoid the appearance of cherry-picking.
* **Computational Feasibility:** Section 2.2.1 proposes calculating word representations for topics based on pairwise comparison with every word in the vocabulary. This approach is likely computationally prohibitive for realistic vocabulary sizes and numbers of topics. The authors should address its feasibility.
* **Tokenization Clarification:** Section 3.1 states that “tokens” are identified using SpaCy, but lacks detail regarding *how*. Is it solely noun chunk extraction (similar to Section 3.2), or is another approach employed? Given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings (which contain a limited number of multi-word terms), the tokenization process requires clarification.
* **Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Terms:** The paper does not address how the model handles OOV terms. While training on the entire document collection may mitigate this issue in the presented experiments, the model may be applied to novel documents in other contexts. In such cases, the difference between updated and fixed (OOV) word2vec embeddings could be problematic.
* **Newsgroups Result:** The finding that 20 topics performed well on the 20 Newsgroups dataset is unsurprising given the data’s inherent structure. An information-theoretic comparison to true document labels – and to LDA – would provide a more insightful evaluation.
* **Related Work:** The paper should acknowledge and compare itself to recent neural topic models, such as those presented in:
    * Cao et al. (2015). "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension." AAAI.
    * Nguyen et al. (2015). "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics.
    * Shamanta et al. (2015). "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases.

Finally, several minor issues should be addressed:

* line 315: Replace "it's similarity" with "its similarity."
* line 361: Clarify what it means for the "topic basis" to be affected; the phrasing is awkward.
* Figure 5 caption: Consider using "terms" instead of "words."
* Reference formatting: The reference formatting is inconsistent (e.g., “Advances in…”, “Advances in Neural…”). Ensure all references include complete information, such as conference names (e.g., for Roder et al.).
