This paper explores a hybrid approach combining the strengths of Dirichlet Topic Models (LDA) and word embeddings (word2vec) into a model called lda2vec. The central claim is that jointly learning dense word vectors with sparse, interpretable document mixtures, facilitated by modern automatic differentiation frameworks, provides a valuable approach to unsupervised document representation. The primary contribution, as I see it, lies in the seamless integration of these two traditionally distinct approaches, allowing for the benefits of both—semantic representation from word embeddings and interpretability from topic modeling—within a single, differentiable framework. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of this integration on two datasets, the Twenty Newsgroups collection and a Hacker News comments corpus, illustrating the model's ability to learn both topic structures and word analogies. The reported topic coherence scores and qualitative examples suggest promising, though not definitive, results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Integration of Established Techniques:** The core idea of combining word2vec’s distributed representations with LDA’s interpretable topic mixtures is compelling. This represents a potentially valuable direction for research, bridging the gap between dense and sparse document representations. The implementation within an automatic differentiation framework is also a practical advantage, enabling flexible model development and experimentation.
2. **Demonstration on Multiple Datasets:** Applying lda2vec to both the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and the Hacker News corpus demonstrates the model's adaptability to different data characteristics and scales. The Hacker News experiment, in particular, showcases the model's ability to learn domain-specific representations and relationships.
3. **Qualitative Results & Initial Evaluation:** The qualitative examples of learned topics and word analogies offer initial evidence of the model’s effectiveness. While not a comprehensive evaluation, these examples provide insights into the model's behavior and potential applications. The inclusion of topic coherence scores, though needing further explanation, indicates an attempt to quantify the quality of the learned topics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Technical Presentation:** The most significant weakness is the lack of clarity in the model description. Section 2, detailing the lda2vec architecture, is difficult to follow for readers familiar with both word2vec and LDA. Key motivations for design choices are not sufficiently explained, and the mathematical notation is not always clearly connected to the overall model concept. A substantial rewrite is needed to improve accessibility.
2. **Insufficient Quantitative Evaluation:** The paper lacks a rigorous quantitative evaluation. Simply presenting example topic clusters is insufficient in the current NLP landscape. A more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to state-of-the-art topic modeling techniques and/or downstream task performance, is essential to establish the model's value. The discussion of Figure 2 is relegated to the caption and requires further elaboration within the main text.
3. **Unsubstantiated Claims & Overstatement of Results:** The conclusion overstates the model's capabilities, specifically regarding its ability to "solve word analogies." The presented examples are cherry-picked and do not constitute a systematic evaluation. Several statements within the introduction and elsewhere lack sufficient justification or supporting evidence. For instance, the claim that deriving approximations for new probabilistic topic models is universally time-consuming requires more nuance.
4. **Anonymity Violation:** Footnote 1 breaks the double-blind reviewing convention by revealing a link to the authors' code repository.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the motivations behind using the same word vectors for both pivot and target words, compared to the standard word2vec approach?
2. The paper mentions the difficulty of deriving approximations for new topic models. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges in this context and how lda2vec circumvents these challenges?
3. What specific metrics were used to evaluate the quality of the learned word analogies beyond the provided examples? Would you be able to present a more systematic evaluation of this capability?
4. Could you provide a more detailed description of the experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning and the computational resources used for training?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "it's similarity" should be corrected to "its similarity."
*   Please provide the full venue name for the Chang et al. reference.
*   Consider clarifying the meaning of "separating words from a marginal distribution."
*   Expand on the concept of "co-adaptation" and its relevance to the model's design.
*   Clarify what “structure” refers to in the phrase "If we only included structure up to this point."
