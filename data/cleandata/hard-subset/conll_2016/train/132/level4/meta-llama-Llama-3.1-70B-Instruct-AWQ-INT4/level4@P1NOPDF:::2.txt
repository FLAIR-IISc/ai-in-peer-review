This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model that extends the objective of word2vec to learn document embeddings, which are then constrained through sparsification, mimicking the output of a traditional topic model.

I found the proposed model to be highly promising, and the examples presented by the authors were compelling. However, a major limitation of the paper is the lack of empirical evaluation of the model. The evaluation relies entirely on tables of examples, without any indication of how representative these examples are or any attempt to directly compare the model with standard or neural topic models. Without empirical evaluation, it is impossible to assess the true worth of the model, making it challenging to accept the paper.

To address this limitation, the authors could have employed several evaluation strategies. For instance, they could have used the topic representation of each document in a supervised document categorization setup to compare against a topic model with the same topic cardinality, indirectly evaluating the quality of the representation. Alternatively, they could have performed direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations, based on pairwise comparisons over topic vectors.

It is commendable that the authors are releasing the code, but the GitHub link in the submitted version of the paper compromises anonymity. Strictly speaking, this is sufficient grounds for rejection, but I leave this decision to the program committee.

Several other issues need to be addressed:

* The selection process for the examples in Figures 3-6 is unclear. Presenting a subset of the actual topics may be perceived as cherry-picking.
* In Section 2.2.1, the authors discuss the possibility of calculating word representations for topics based on pairwise comparison with each word in the vocabulary. However, this process would be extremely expensive for a reasonable vocabulary size and number of topics. It is unclear whether this approach is feasible.
* The authors mention using SpaCy to identify "tokens" in Section 3.1, but the specifics of this process are unclear. Do they extract noun chunks, similar to Section 3.2, or employ a different method? Given that they use word2vec pre-trained embeddings, which include only a small number of multiword terms, it is unclear what they are doing in this section.
* The authors do not explain how their model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. While this may not be an immediate problem in the reported experiments, which train the model over the entire document collection, it will be a concern when applying the trained model to novel documents. In such cases, the updating of word2vec token embeddings will make non-updated (OOV) word2vec embeddings incomparable to the tuned embeddings.
* The finding that 20 topics worked best on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is not surprising, given its composition. A simple form of evaluation could have been based on an information-theoretic comparison relative to the true document labels, allowing for a direct comparison with LDA.
* The authors should compare their model with other neural topic models, including:
    + Cao et al. (2015). A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension. AAAI.
    + Nguyen et al. (2015). Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations. Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics.
    + Shamanta et al. (2015). Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations. Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases.

Minor issues:

* Line 315: "it's similarity" should be "its similarity".
* Line 361: The phrase "the topic basis is affected" is unclear, and the word "are" is awkwardly placed.
* In the caption of Figure 5, "words" could be replaced with "terms" for clarity.
* The reference formatting is inconsistent, with errors such as "Advances in ..." and missing conference names (e.g., Roder et al.).
