This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, extending the objective of word2vec to learn document embeddings, which are then constrained through sparsification, mimicking the output of a topic model. The authors' model is innovative, and the provided examples are highly promising. However, the paper lacks empirical evaluation of the model, relying solely on tables of examples without discussing their representativeness or comparing them to standard or neural topic models. This omission makes it challenging to assess the model's true worth, rendering it difficult to accept the paper.

To address this limitation, the authors could have employed a supervised document categorization setup, utilizing the topic representation of each document to compare against a topic model with the same topic cardinality. Alternatively, they could have conducted a direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations, based on pairwise comparison over topic vectors.

While the authors' decision to release code is commendable, the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted version compromises anonymity, which is a concern that should be addressed.

Several other issues need attention:

1. The selection process for examples in Figures 3-6 is unclear. Presenting a subset of actual topics may raise concerns about cherry-picking.
2. The calculation of word representations for topics based on pairwise comparison with each word in the vocabulary, discussed in Section 2.2.1, appears computationally expensive for reasonable vocabulary sizes and topic numbers. The feasibility of this approach should be clarified.
3. The use of SpaCy for token identification in Section 3.1 is not clearly explained. The authors mention extracting noun chunks, but it is unclear if other chunk types are considered or if a different approach is employed. This is particularly confusing given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which include only a limited number of multiword terms.
4. The model's handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms is not addressed. Although the reported experiments train the model on the entire document collection, there will be contexts where the trained model is applied to novel documents. In such cases, the updating of word2vec token embeddings may render non-updated (OOV) word2vec embeddings incomparable to the tuned embeddings.
5. The finding that 20 topics worked best on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising, given its composition. A simple evaluation based on information-theoretic comparison relative to the true document labels could have been conducted, allowing for a direct comparison with LDA.
6. The authors should compare their model with other neural topic models, including:
   - Cao et al. (2015): "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension."
   - Nguyen et al. (2015): "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations."
   - Shamanta et al. (2015): "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations."

Low-level issues:

- Line 315: "it's similarity" should be corrected to "its similarity."
- Line 361: The phrase "the topic basis is affected" is unclear, and the word "are" is awkwardly placed.
- In the caption of Figure 5, "terms" would be a more suitable choice than "words."
- The reference formatting is inconsistent, with errors such as "Advances in ..." and missing conference names (e.g., Roder et al.).
