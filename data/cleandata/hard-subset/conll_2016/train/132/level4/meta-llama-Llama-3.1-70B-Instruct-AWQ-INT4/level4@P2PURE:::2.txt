This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, extending the objective of word2vec to learn document embeddings, which are then constrained through sparsification, mimicking the output of a traditional topic model. The authors' proposed model is intriguing, and the presented examples are highly promising. However, a significant omission in the paper is the lack of empirical evaluation of the model. The evaluation relies solely on tables of examples, without any indication of their representativeness or a direct comparison with standard or neural topic models. This absence of empirical evaluation makes it challenging to assess the true worth of the model, rendering it difficult to accept the paper.

To address this limitation, the authors could have employed the topic representation of each document in a supervised document categorization setup to compare with a topic model having the same topic cardinality, thus indirectly evaluating the quality of the representation. Alternatively, they could have conducted a direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations based on pairwise comparisons over topic vectors.

While the decision to release the code is commendable, the authors have compromised anonymity by publishing the GitHub link in the submitted version of the paper, which is a serious issue that could potentially lead to rejection.

Other concerns with the paper include:

- The selection process for the examples in Figures 3-6 is unclear, raising concerns about cherry-picking.
- In Section 2.2.1, the authors discuss calculating word representations for topics based on pairwise comparison with each word in the vocabulary, but this process appears to be computationally expensive for reasonable vocabulary sizes and topic numbers.
- The paper mentions using SpaCy to identify "tokens" in Section 3.1, but the specifics of this process are unclear, particularly given the use of word2vec pre-trained embeddings.
- The model's handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms is not addressed, which could lead to issues when applying the trained model to novel documents.
- The finding that 20 topics worked best on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is not surprising, given its composition. A simple evaluation based on information-theoretic comparison relative to the true document labels could have provided a direct comparison with LDA.
- The authors should also consider comparing their model with other neural topic models, such as those proposed by Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).

Minor issues include:

- Line 315: "it's similarity" should be "its similarity."
- Line 361: The phrase "topic basis" is unclear, and the sentence structure is awkward.
- The caption of Figure 5 should refer to "terms" instead of "words."
- The reference formatting is inconsistent, with errors in formatting and missing information.
