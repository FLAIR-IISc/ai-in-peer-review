This paper presents a neural-styled topic model that expands on the word2vec objective to learn document embeddings, which are then constrained through sparsification, effectively mimicking the output of a traditional topic model.

I found the proposed model to be highly promising, with the provided examples showcasing its potential. However, a significant limitation of the paper is the lack of empirical evaluation of the model. The evaluation relies solely on example tables without any indication of their representativeness or direct comparison with standard or neural topic models. This omission makes it challenging to assess the model's true value, rendering it difficult to accept the paper.

To address this, the authors could have employed a supervised document categorization setup to compare the topic representation of each document against a topic model with the same topic cardinality, indirectly evaluating the quality of the representation. Alternatively, they could have conducted a direct evaluation using a dataset with document similarity annotations based on pairwise comparison over topic vectors.

Although releasing the code is appreciated, the authors compromised anonymity by including the GitHub link in the submitted paper version. This issue should be addressed to maintain the review process's integrity.

Several other concerns need to be addressed:

- The selection process for the examples in Figures 3-6 is unclear, potentially leading to cherry-picking.
- The feasibility of calculating word representations for topics based on pairwise comparison with each word in the vocabulary, as discussed in Section 2.2.1, is questionable due to the high computational cost.
- The method used to identify "tokens" with SpaCy in Section 3.1 is unclear, particularly in relation to the extraction of noun chunks and the use of word2vec pre-trained embeddings.
- The model's handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms is not explicitly stated, which may become a problem when applying the trained model to novel documents.
- The finding that 20 topics worked best for the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising, given its composition. A simple evaluation based on information-theoretic comparison relative to the true document labels could have provided a more comprehensive assessment.
- The authors should compare their model with other neural topic models, including those presented in Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).

Additionally, several minor issues need to be addressed:

- Line 315: "it's similarity" should be corrected to "its similarity."
- Line 361: The phrase "topic basis" is unclear, and the word "are" is awkwardly placed.
- In the caption of Figure 5, "terms" would be a more suitable term than "words."
- The reference formatting is inconsistent and needs to be standardized.
