This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, extending the objective of word2vec to also learn document embeddings, which it then constrains through sparsification, hence mimicking the output of a topic model. I found the proposed model to be intriguing, and the examples presented by the authors to be highly promising.

However, the paper lacks a thorough empirical evaluation of the model, relying solely on tables of examples without any indication of their representativeness or direct comparison with standard or neural topic models. This omission makes it challenging to assess the true worth of the model, rendering it difficult to accept the paper in its current form. To address this limitation, the authors could consider evaluating the topic representation of each document in a supervised document categorization setup, comparing it to a topic model with the same topic cardinality as an indirect measure of representation quality. Alternatively, they could conduct a direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations based on pairwise comparisons over topic vectors.

It is commendable that the authors are releasing the code, but the inclusion of the GitHub link in the submitted version of the paper compromises anonymity, which is a serious concern.

Several other issues need to be addressed:

* The selection process for the examples in Figures 3-6 is unclear, raising concerns about potential cherry-picking.
* In Section 2.2.1, the authors discuss calculating word representations for topics based on pairwise comparison with each word in the vocabulary. However, this approach seems computationally expensive for reasonable vocabulary sizes and numbers of topics, and its feasibility is questionable.
* The authors mention using SpaCy to identify "tokens" in Section 3.1, but the specific method used is unclear. It is also unclear how this process relates to the extraction of noun chunks in Section 3.2, especially given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which only include a limited number of multi-word terms.
* The paper does not address how the model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. Although this may not be an immediate concern in the reported experiments, it will be a problem when applying the trained model to novel documents, as the updating of word2vec token embeddings will make non-updated (OOV) word2vec embeddings incomparable to the tuned embeddings.
* The finding that 20 topics worked best on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is not surprising, given its composition. A simple evaluation based on information-theoretic comparison relative to the true document labels could have provided a direct comparison with LDA and other models.
* The authors should also compare their model with other neural topic models, such as those proposed by Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).

Additionally, there are several low-level issues that need attention:

* Line 315: "it's similarity" should be corrected to "its similarity."
* Line 361: The phrase "the topic basis is affected" is unclear, and the word "are" is awkwardly used.
* In the caption of Figure 5, "words" should be replaced with "terms" for accuracy.
* The reference formatting is inconsistent, with errors such as missing conference names (e.g., Roder et al.) and varying formats for the same publication (e.g., "Advances in ..." and "Advances in Neural ...").
