This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model that extends the objective of word2vec to learn document embeddings, which are then constrained through sparsification, mimicking the output of a topic model. The authors' proposed model is intriguing, and the examples presented are highly promising. However, the paper lacks empirical evaluation of the model, relying solely on tables of examples without any indication of their representativeness or direct comparison with standard or neural topic models.

The absence of empirical evaluation makes it challenging to assess the true worth of the model, rendering it difficult to accept the paper. To address this, the authors could have employed the topic representation of each document in a supervised document categorization setup to compare against a topic model with the same topic cardinality, thereby indirectly evaluating the quality of the representation. Alternatively, they could have conducted a direct evaluation over a dataset with document similarity annotations based on pairwise comparison over topic vectors.

Although the authors are releasing code, they have compromised anonymity by publishing the GitHub link in the submitted version of the paper. This issue should be addressed to maintain the integrity of the review process.

Several other concerns need to be addressed:

1. The selection process for the examples in Figures 3-6 is unclear. Presenting a subset of the actual topics may give the impression of cherry-picking, and it is essential to provide a clear explanation for the chosen examples.
2. In Section 2.2.1, the authors discuss the possibility of calculating word representations for topics based on pairwise comparison with each word in the vocabulary. However, this process would be extremely expensive for a reasonable vocabulary size and number of topics, making it unclear whether this approach is feasible.
3. The authors mention using SpaCy to identify "tokens" in Section 3.1, but the specifics of this process are unclear. It is essential to provide more details on how they extract noun chunks and whether they use any other chunk types.
4. The model's handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms is not explicitly addressed. Although training the model over the entire document collection may not pose an immediate problem, applying the trained model to novel documents could lead to issues with updating word2vec token embeddings, making OOV word embeddings not directly comparable to the tuned embeddings.
5. The finding that 20 topics worked best over the 20 Newsgroups corpus is not surprising, given its composition. A simple form of evaluation could have been based on an information-theoretic comparison relative to the true document labels, allowing for a direct comparison with LDA.
6. The authors should compare their model with other neural topic models, including Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).

Minor issues include:

* Line 315: "it's similarity" should be corrected to "its similarity."
* Line 361: The phrase "the topic basis is affected" is unclear, and the word "are" is awkwardly used.
* In the caption of Figure 5, "examples" should be replaced with "terms" for clarity.
* The reference formatting is inconsistent and should be standardized.

Addressing these concerns and providing a more comprehensive evaluation of the model would significantly strengthen the paper and its contributions to the field.
