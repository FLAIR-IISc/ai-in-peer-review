This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, extending the objective of word2vec to also learn document embeddings, which it then constrains through sparsification, hence mimicking the output of a topic model.

**Summary of the Paper**

The authors propose a model, lda2vec, that combines the strengths of word embeddings and topic models. The model learns dense word vectors jointly with Dirichlet-distributed latent document-level mixtures of topic vectors. The document representations are constrained to be sparse and interpretable, similar to traditional topic models. The model is trained using a combination of the Skipgram Negative Sampling loss and a Dirichlet likelihood term. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on two datasets, Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News comments, and show that it can discover coherent topics and learn linear relationships between words.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. The authors propose a novel neural-styled topic model that combines the strengths of word embeddings and topic models.
2. The model learns dense word vectors jointly with Dirichlet-distributed latent document-level mixtures of topic vectors, allowing for sparse and interpretable document representations.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on two datasets, Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News comments, and show that it can discover coherent topics and learn linear relationships between words.

**Strengths of the Submission**

1. The authors propose a novel and interesting model that combines the strengths of word embeddings and topic models.
2. The model is simple to implement and can be trained using automatic differentiation frameworks.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model on two datasets, including qualitative and quantitative results.
4. The authors release their code, making it easy for others to reproduce and build upon their work.

**Weaknesses of the Submission**

1. The authors do not provide a thorough empirical evaluation of their model, relying instead on tables of examples and qualitative results.
2. The authors do not compare their model to other neural topic models, making it difficult to assess its performance relative to other state-of-the-art models.
3. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how they selected the examples presented in the paper, which may be cherry-picked.
4. The authors do not discuss how their model deals with out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, which may be a problem in certain contexts.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How did you select the examples presented in the paper, and are they representative of the typical output of the model?
2. Can you provide a more thorough empirical evaluation of your model, including comparisons to other neural topic models and quantitative results?
3. How does your model deal with OOV terms, and are there any plans to address this issue in future work?
4. Can you provide more details on the implementation of your model, including the specific hyperparameters used and the computational resources required for training?
