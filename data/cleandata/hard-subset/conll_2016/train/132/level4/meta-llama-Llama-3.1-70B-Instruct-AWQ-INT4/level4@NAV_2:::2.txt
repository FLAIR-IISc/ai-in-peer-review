This paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, extending the objective of word2vec to also learn document embeddings, which it then constrains through sparsification, hence mimicking the output of a topic model.

The authors present a model that combines the strengths of both word embeddings and topic models, allowing for interpretable document representations while preserving semantic regularities between word vectors. The model is well-motivated, and the examples presented are promising.

However, the paper lacks empirical evaluation of the model. The authors rely heavily on tables of examples without providing any indication of how representative these examples are or comparing their model to standard or neural topic models. To strengthen the paper, the authors could consider evaluating their model using a supervised document categorization setup or direct evaluation over a dataset with document similarity annotations.

The authors have compromised anonymity by publishing the GitHub link in the submitted version of the paper. While releasing code is commendable, it is essential to maintain anonymity during the review process.

Other issues with the paper include:

- The selection process for the examples in Figures 3-6 is unclear, potentially leading to cherry-picking.
- The feasibility of calculating word representations for topics based on pairwise comparison with each word in the vocabulary is questionable, especially for reasonable vocabulary sizes and numbers of topics.
- The method used to identify "tokens" using SpaCy is unclear, particularly in Section 3.1.
- The paper does not address how the model deals with out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, which could be a problem when applying the trained model to novel documents.
- The finding that 20 topics worked best over the 20 Newsgroups corpus is not surprising, and a more comprehensive evaluation could have been performed using information-theoretic comparisons relative to the true document labels.
- The paper should compare itself with other neural topic models, such as those proposed by Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).
- There are several low-level issues, including grammatical errors, inconsistent reference formatting, and unclear captions.

Summary of the paper:
The paper proposes a neural-styled topic model that combines word embeddings and topic models. The model is well-motivated, but the paper lacks empirical evaluation and has several other issues that need to be addressed.

Main contributions of the paper:
1. The authors propose a neural-styled topic model that extends the objective of word2vec to also learn document embeddings.
2. The model constrains document embeddings through sparsification, mimicking the output of a topic model.
3. The authors provide examples of topics discovered by their model in the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and the Hacker News comments corpus.

Strengths of the paper:
1. The model combines the strengths of both word embeddings and topic models.
2. The authors provide promising examples of topics discovered by their model.
3. The paper is well-motivated, and the model has the potential to be useful in various applications.

Weaknesses of the paper:
1. The paper lacks empirical evaluation of the model.
2. The authors rely heavily on tables of examples without providing any indication of how representative these examples are.
3. The paper does not address how the model deals with out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms.
4. The authors have compromised anonymity by publishing the GitHub link in the submitted version of the paper.

Questions to authors:
1. How did you select the examples in Figures 3-6?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms in your model?
3. Can you provide more comprehensive evaluation of your model using a supervised document categorization setup or direct evaluation over a dataset with document similarity annotations?
4. How do you plan to compare your model with other neural topic models in the literature?
