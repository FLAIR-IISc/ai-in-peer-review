This paper explores combining distributional word embeddings (word2vec) with topic models (LDA), an idea that is timely and potentially impactful. Unfortunately, the current presentation is very difficult to follow, and the lack of a clear technical description and rigorous evaluation prevents a fair assessment of the contribution. In particular, Section 2 needs a complete rewrite to convey a coherent high-level picture and precise details of how the models are integrated.

Strengths
- Interesting and potentially valuable direction: enriching local context-based embeddings with global topic structure.
- If executed and evaluated rigorously, the approach could bridge two influential paradigms in NLP.

Major weaknesses
- Clarity: Section 2 is incomprehensible as written. The model, objective, and inference/optimization procedure are not clearly specified.
- Missing evaluation: There is no substantive quantitative evaluation. Example clusters and a few analogy cases are insufficient by current standards.
- Overclaiming: The conclusion asserts the model “solves” analogies, which is not supported by the limited qualitative evidence.
- Anonymity and referencing issues: A footnote appears to reveal author identity, and a cited venue is incomplete.

Requests for clarification and revision
1) High-level model description
- Provide a concise overview before diving into math: what are the variables (documents, topics, word embeddings, topic embeddings, context windows), and how do they interact?
- Clearly state whether the model is generative, purely discriminative, or a hybrid (e.g., LDA likelihood plus a word2vec-style context objective).
- Include a schematic figure and/or pseudocode for the training loop.

2) Objective and inference/optimization
- Write the full objective with all terms and weighting coefficients. If you “derive a new approximation,” specify: an approximation to what—partition function, marginal likelihood, variational bound, or negative sampling surrogate? Explain why it is needed here.
- Specify the inference/optimization method: variational inference, EM, stochastic variational inference, or simply SGD on a surrogate loss? If negative sampling or noise-contrastive estimation is used, define the noise distribution and justify it.
- Clarify what you mean by “separate words from a marginal distribution.” If this refers to drawing negatives from a smoothed unigram (e.g., p(w)^3/4), state it explicitly and cite the appropriate source.
- Define “co-adaptation.” If you mean features co-adapting during training (as in the dropout literature), say so and provide a citation; otherwise define the term in this context.

3) Modeling choices
- Explain the decision to use the same embedding for pivot and target words. In word2vec, input and output embeddings are distinct and later combined or averaged. What is your rationale, and what empirical effect does this have? Include an ablation.
- “If we only included structure up to this point” — specify which structure (document–topic proportions, topic–word distributions, local context windows?) and what additional structure you then introduce.

4) Evaluation
- Provide quantitative results with clear baselines:
  - Intrinsic: word similarity (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999), analogy accuracy (Mikolov et al., 2013), topic coherence (UMass, NPMI).
  - Extrinsic (optional but valuable): document classification or clustering using learned representations.
- Baselines: standard skip-gram/CBOW, GloVe, LDA, and at least one topical embedding baseline (e.g., prior “topical word embedding” approaches). Include ablations that remove the topic component or the context component, and the shared-vs-separate embeddings choice.
- Experimental details: datasets, preprocessing, hyperparameters, optimization settings, number of topics, context window size, negative samples, and training time. Report variance over multiple runs.
- Integrate the content of Figure 2 into the main text. If it is intended as a quantitative evaluation, present metrics, axes, and a clear description in the body, not only in a long caption.

5) Writing and structure
- Rewrite Section 2 with:
  - 2.1 Intuition and overview
  - 2.2 Formal definition (variables, distributions, and embeddings)
  - 2.3 Objective function and approximations
  - 2.4 Optimization/inference algorithm
  - 2.5 Relationship to word2vec and LDA (recovering each as a special case, if applicable)
- Define all notation on first use and keep it consistent.
- Reduce overly long figure captions; move substantive explanations into the text.

Specific questions and issues from the current draft
- Introduction, paragraph 3: “requires deriving a new approximation” — of what? Why is it time-consuming to develop prototypes, and why is feature evaluation easier? Provide justification or references.
- Motivation for shared pivot/target embeddings vs. word2vec’s separate input/output embeddings?
- “Separate words from a marginal distribution” — define precisely.
- “Co-adaptation” — define and cite.
- “If we only included structure up to this point” — which structure?
- Conclusion: Do not claim to “solve” analogies. The few hand-picked examples (e.g., king/queen) are not evidence of general performance.
- Figure 2: If it presents results, describe them in the main text with proper labels, metrics, and comparisons.
- Footnote 1 appears to break anonymity; remove or revise for double-blind review.
- Typo: “it’s similarity” → “its similarity.”
- References: The Chang citation lists only “Advances in ….” Provide the full venue (e.g., Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, year, volume) and standard bibliographic details for all references.

Recommendation
At present, I cannot recommend acceptance. The idea is promising, but the paper needs a substantial rewrite for clarity and a thorough, quantitative evaluation with appropriate baselines and ablations. If the authors can (i) provide a clear, rigorous description of the model and training procedure; (ii) justify key design choices; and (iii) deliver strong comparative results on standard benchmarks, the work could become a solid contribution.
