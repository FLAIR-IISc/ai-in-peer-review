Summary
This paper proposes a neural-style topic model that extends the word2vec objective to learn document embeddings, and then applies sparsification to these embeddings to mimic the output of a topic model.

Strengths
- The proposed model is interesting and intuitively appealing.
- The examples provided are promising and suggest the method could yield meaningful topics.

Major concerns
- The paper lacks empirical evaluation. The evaluation relies solely on example tables, with no indication of how representative these examples are and no direct comparison against standard or neural topic models. Without quantitative evaluation, it is impossible to assess the true merit of the approach, which makes the paper difficult to accept.

Suggestions for evaluation
- Use the learned topic representations in a supervised document categorization task, comparing against a topic model with the same number of topics (as an indirect evaluation of representation quality).
- Perform a direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations, using pairwise comparisons over topic vectors.

Anonymity
- While it is commendable that you plan to release code, including a GitHub link in the submission compromises anonymity. Strictly speaking, this alone could justify rejection; I defer to the program chairs on this point.

Other issues
- How were the examples in Figures 3–6 selected? Presenting only a subset of topics risks cherry-picking; please clarify the selection criteria and consider providing aggregate statistics.
- Section 2.2.1 suggests computing word representations for topics via pairwise comparison with each word in the vocabulary. For realistic vocabulary sizes and topic counts, this appears extremely expensive. Is this computationally feasible, and if so, how is it implemented efficiently?
- Section 3.1 states that you identify “tokens” using SpaCy. How exactly? Do you extract noun chunks (as in Section 3.2) or something else? Given that you later use pre-trained word2vec embeddings (which include relatively few multiword terms), the tokenization/granularity choice is unclear and should be reconciled with the embeddings.
- How does the model handle OOV terms? In your experiments you train on the entire document collection, which may mask the issue. In deployment on novel documents, however, updating the word2vec embeddings during training means that non-updated OOV embeddings will not be directly comparable to the tuned in-vocabulary embeddings. Please discuss how you address this.
- The finding that 20 topics work best on 20 Newsgroups is unsurprising given the dataset’s composition. A simple additional evaluation would be an information-theoretic comparison relative to the true document labels (e.g., mutual information), which would also enable direct comparison with LDA and related baselines.
- You should compare against the following neural topic models:
  - Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. “A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension.” AAAI, 2015.
  - Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. “Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations.” TACL 3 (2015): 299–313.
  - Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. “Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations.” In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, 2015.

Low-level comments
- Line 315: “it’s similarity” → “its similarity”
- Line 361: Please clarify what it means for the “topic basis” to be affected (the use of “are” here is also awkward).
- Figure 5 caption: The examples are better described as “terms” rather than “words.”
- References: The formatting is inconsistent (e.g., “Advances in …” vs. “Advances in Neural …”), Roder et al. is missing the conference name, etc. Please standardize the references.

Overall assessment
I appreciate the novelty and promise of the proposed approach, but the absence of quantitative evaluation and comparisons to established baselines prevents a conclusive assessment. Addressing the evaluation gaps and clarifying the methodological details above would significantly strengthen the paper.
