This paper proposes a neural-style topic model that extends the word2vec objective to learn document embeddings, then enforces sparsity to mimic topic-model outputs. I like the core idea and find the illustrative examples promising. However, the paper lacks any substantive empirical evaluation. The evidence consists solely of example tables, with no indication of how representative they are and no direct comparisons against standard or neural topic models. Without empirical validation, it is impossible to assess the model’s true value, which makes the paper difficult to accept.

At minimum, the authors should include one or both of the following:
- Indirect evaluation: use the learned topic representation for each document in a supervised document classification task, and compare against a topic model with the same number of topics.
- Direct evaluation: assess document similarity using a dataset with human similarity annotations, via pairwise comparison of topic vectors.

While it is excellent that you plan to release code, the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted version compromises anonymity. Strictly speaking, this alone can warrant rejection; I defer to the PCs on this point.

Other issues:
- Selection of examples (Figures 3–6): How were these chosen? Presenting only a subset of topics/outputs risks cherry-picking. Please clarify the selection protocol or provide comprehensive results.
- Section 2.2.1 feasibility: You propose computing word representations for topics via pairwise comparison with every word in the vocabulary. For realistic vocabulary sizes and topic counts, this appears prohibitively costly. Please quantify the complexity and discuss whether it is tractable in practice (or describe approximations).
- Tokenization with SpaCy (Section 3.1): How exactly are “tokens” defined? Are you extracting noun chunks only (as in Section 3.2), or other chunk types, or plain tokens? Given that you rely on pre-trained word2vec embeddings—which include relatively few multiword terms—the tokenization/granularity mismatch is unclear. Please specify the pipeline and justify compatibility with the embeddings.
- OOV handling: How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary terms at inference time? In the reported experiments you train on the full collection, but in realistic scenarios you will apply the trained model to novel documents. If you fine-tune word2vec embeddings during training, untouched OOV embeddings will not be directly comparable to tuned ones. Please describe your OOV strategy and its impact on comparability.
- Number of topics on 20 Newsgroups: The finding that 20 topics perform best is unsurprising given the dataset’s structure. A simple, informative evaluation would be to compare the learned topic distributions to the true labels using an information-theoretic measure, and to benchmark directly against LDA and related methods under the same topic cardinality.
- Related neural topic models for comparison:
  - Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. “A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension.” AAAI, 2015.
  - Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. “Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations.” TACL 3 (2015): 299–313.
  - Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. “Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations.” In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, pp. 441–457. Springer, 2015.

Low-level comments:
- Line 315: “it’s similarity” → “its similarity.”
- Line 361: Please clarify what it means for the “topic basis” to be affected; the construction with “are” is also awkward.
- Figure 5 caption: The examples should likely be described as “terms” rather than “words.”
- References: Formatting is inconsistent (e.g., capitalization of “Advances in …” vs. “Advances in Neural …”), and some entries are incomplete (e.g., Röder et al. missing the conference name). Please standardize and complete all references.

In summary, the modeling idea is appealing and the qualitative results are encouraging, but the absence of quantitative evaluation and comparative baselines prevents a fair assessment. Addressing the evaluation gaps (classification or similarity benchmarks, efficiency analysis, OOV strategy), clarifying data processing and example selection, and adding comparisons to neural and standard topic models would substantially strengthen the paper.
