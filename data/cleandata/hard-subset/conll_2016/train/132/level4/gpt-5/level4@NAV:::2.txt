This paper introduces a neural-style topic model that extends the word2vec objective to jointly learn document embeddings, then enforces sparsity on those embeddings to emulate the outputs of a topic model.

I found the proposed model compelling, and the examples provided are very promising. The major shortcoming, however, is the absence of empirical evaluation—assessment relies entirely on tables of examples, with no indication of how representative they are and no direct comparisons against standard or neural topic models. Without quantitative evaluation, it is impossible to judge the model’s true merit, which makes acceptance difficult. Possible ways to address this include: (1) using each document’s topic representation in a supervised document classification task to compare with a topic model of the same number of topics (as an indirect measure of representation quality); or (2) conducting a direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations (via pairwise comparisons over topic vectors).
It’s excellent that you plan to release code, but including a GitHub link in the submission breaks anonymity (strictly speaking, this alone could justify rejection, though I defer to the PCs).

Other issues:
- How were the examples in Figures 3–6 selected? Presenting only a subset of topics, etc., risks the perception of cherry-picking.
- In Section 2.2.1 you discuss computing word representations for topics via pairwise comparisons with every word in the vocabulary, which will be extremely costly for realistic vocabulary sizes and topic counts; is this actually feasible?
- You state in Section 3.1 that you identify “tokens” using SpaCy—how exactly? Do you extract noun chunks (but not other chunk types), as in Section 3.2, or something else? Since you use pre-trained word2vec embeddings (which contain relatively few multiword terms), this step is unclear.
- How does your model handle OOV terms? While in the reported experiments you train on the entire collection (so this may not surface immediately), in scenarios where the model is applied to new documents, updating the word2vec token embeddings means any non-updated (OOV, relative to training) embeddings won’t be directly comparable to the tuned ones.
- The finding that 20 topics worked best on 20 Newsgroups is unsurprising given the dataset. A simple additional evaluation would be an information-theoretic comparison against the true labels, which would also allow a direct comparison with LDA, etc.
- Additional neural topic models you should compare against include:
Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. “A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension.” In AAAI, pp. 2210–2216. 2015.
Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. “Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations.” Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics 3 (2015): 299–313.
Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. “Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations.” In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, pp. 441–457. Springer International Publishing, 2015.
