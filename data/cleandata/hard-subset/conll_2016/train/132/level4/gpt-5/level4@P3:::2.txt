Summary
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that combines the skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS) objective with a Dirichlet prior over document–topic mixtures. Words, topics, and documents are embedded in a shared space. Each document vector is constrained to be a convex combination of topic vectors (simplex constraint), encouraging sparsity and interpretability via a Dirichlet likelihood. The model is implemented in an automatic differentiation framework (Chainer) and demonstrated on Twenty Newsgroups (with topic coherence scores) and a large Hacker News comments corpus (with qualitative topics and word analogies).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical neural topic model that integrates SGNS with sparse, interpretable document–topic mixtures by constraining document vectors to be convex combinations of topic vectors.
2) An implementation in an autodiff framework enabling joint training of word, topic, and document vectors, with qualitative evidence of coherent topics and meaningful analogies.
3) Open-source code release facilitating reproducibility (though the submission currently breaches anonymity).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and interpretability: The model elegantly ties SGNS to topic modeling by summing document and word vectors and constraining documents to lie on a topic simplex. The non-negativity and simplex constraints promote readable document mixtures, addressing a common critique of dense document embeddings.
- Practicality and extensibility: The objective is easy to implement in modern autodiff frameworks and scales with standard SGNS tooling (negative sampling, dropout, Adam). This makes the approach attractive for practitioners who want interpretable document representations without bespoke variational inference.
- Qualitative results are compelling: On Twenty Newsgroups, topics align with expected categories; on Hacker News, topics capture salient community themes and the learned embeddings exhibit domain-specific linear regularities (e.g., analogy-style relations).
- Clear exposition of the model: The loss, architecture, and the role of the Dirichlet sparsity term are described clearly, with intuitive motivation for summing document and word vectors to capture long- and short-range context.
- Use of topic coherence: Reporting Cv provides a principled, human-correlated metric for topic quality on 20NG (even though the evaluation is limited; see weaknesses).

Weaknesses
- Insufficient empirical evaluation and lack of baselines: Beyond a small table of mean topic coherence on 20NG, there are no quantitative comparisons to standard LDA or neural topic models. There is no downstream evaluation (e.g., document classification on 20NG), no document similarity/retrieval experiments, no per-topic coherence distributions, and no topic diversity metrics. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess the model’s value beyond qualitative examples.
- Potential cherry-picking: Figures 3–6 show hand-picked topics and analogies with no methodology for selection or coverage. This undermines the evidence. Reporting all topics with coherence statistics (mean/variance), topic diversity, and nearest neighbors for all topics would mitigate this.
- Ambiguity in preprocessing and tokenization: Section 3.1 states tokens are identified with SpaCy, but it is unclear whether multiword expressions were used (as in 3.2) or only lemmas. This matters because pretrained word2vec contains few multiword entries and affects comparability, vocabulary size, and coherence computation. The mismatch between pretrained embeddings and tokenization is not addressed.
- Generalization and OOV handling: The paper does not explain how to handle novel documents and OOV terms at inference time. If word embeddings are fine-tuned during training, how are unseen tokens or frozen/pretrained vectors aligned for test-time encoding of new documents?
- Missing comparisons to related neural topic models: There is no empirical or conceptual comparison to prior neural or embedding-enhanced topic models (e.g., Cao et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2015; Shamanta et al., 2015), leaving the novelty and advantages unclear.
- Anonymity breach: The submission includes a public GitHub URL, which compromises double-blind review and may warrant rejection on policy grounds.
- Minor clarity and feasibility concerns: 
  • Computational feasibility of computing topic–word similarities at scale (O(KV) per pass) is not discussed. 
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity (α, λ, number of topics, dropout) is not explored; the choice of α = 1/n and λ = 200 is asserted without ablation.
  • Reporting only average Cv on 20NG (with unsurprising best performance at 20 topics) is weak; other simple evaluations leveraging the gold labels are natural baselines.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Quantitative evaluation: Can you report (i) document classification accuracy on 20NG using your document-topic proportions vs. LDA and a neural baseline with matched topic/latent dimensions; (ii) document retrieval/similarity using labeled pairs or category labels; (iii) per-topic coherence distributions and topic diversity; (iv) ablations on λ, α, K, and dropout?
- Baseline comparisons: How does lda2vec compare to LDA, GloVe+LDA, and neural topic models such as Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015) on coherence and classification?
- Tokenization details: Precisely how were tokens formed in 3.1 (lemmas only vs. noun chunks/entities)? How did you reconcile pretrained word2vec (few MWEs) with SpaCy-derived tokens? Did you normalize multiword tokens before querying Palmetto?
- OOV and inference: How do you encode new documents at test time? Do you fix word embeddings and optimize only document-topic proportions? How are OOV words handled (subword models, hashing, or exclusion)?
- Efficiency: For topic interpretability, do you precompute topic–word similarities via matrix multiplication (T×W) or approximate with ANN? What are the practical costs at your reported V and K?

Minor comments

- Figure 5 caption: “words” → “terms”
- References: Please standardize formatting (complete venue names, consistent style; e.g., EMNLP, NIPS/NeurIPS; fill in missing venue for Röder et al., 2015; avoid placeholders like “Advances in …” and inconsistent author-year markers).

Overall assessment
The model is appealing and practically motivated, and the qualitative results are promising. However, the lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation and absence of baseline comparisons significantly limit the paper’s evidential strength. With solid experiments (classification/retrieval, coherence/diversity distributions, ablations) and proper anonymization, this work could make a useful contribution as a simple, interpretable neural topic model. As it stands, I cannot recommend acceptance.
