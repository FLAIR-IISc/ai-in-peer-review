Summary and contributions
This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that augments the word2vec/SGNS objective to learn document embeddings jointly with word embeddings, while constraining document representations to be sparse mixtures over topic vectors via a Dirichlet prior on simplex-constrained proportions. The core idea is to sum a document vector with a pivot word vector to form the context, thereby tying document-level and lexical signals in a shared embedding space.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple neural formulation that produces interpretable, sparse document-topic mixtures while learning word and topic vectors jointly, without bespoke inference.
2) A practical implementation within an automatic differentiation framework, with qualitative demonstrations on Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News.
3) Release of code to facilitate reuse.

Strengths
- Conceptual elegance and practicality: Combining SGNS with Dirichlet-sparse topic mixtures is a neat, implementable idea that promises interpretable document representations while retaining the useful linearities of word embeddings. The design (summing document and pivot word vectors) is intuitive and easy to integrate into existing pipelines.
- Interpretability by design: Enforcing non-negativity and a simplex constraint on document-topic proportions, and using a sparsity-inducing Dirichlet, pushes the model toward LDA-like interpretability—often a missing piece in neural document models.
- Qualitative results are compelling: The listed topics (e.g., Space, Encryption, Bitcoin) and word analogies on the Hacker News corpus look promising and suggest that the model captures salient themes and corpus-specific semantics.
- Open-source release: Providing code lowers the barrier to adoption and reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Lack of empirical evaluation and baselines: The paper does not provide quantitative evaluations or direct comparisons to standard topic models (e.g., LDA) or neural topic models. Evaluation relies on a few tables of examples, with no evidence of how representative these are. Without empirical comparisons (e.g., topic coherence vs. LDA, downstream classification, or document similarity tasks), it is impossible to assess the model’s effectiveness.
- Breach of double-blind review: The paper includes a GitHub link that compromises anonymity. This alone can be grounds for rejection under standard reviewing policies.
- Risk of cherry-picking: Figures 3–6 present selected topics and examples, but there is no explanation of how these were chosen or whether they are representative of the overall model output.
- Methodological clarity gaps: Tokenization in Section 3.1 is underspecified. You state that SpaCy is used to identify “tokens,” but it is unclear whether you extract only noun chunks (as in Section 3.2) or use standard tokenization. This is especially important given the use of pre-trained word2vec vectors (which contain few multiword terms). The paper also does not address how the model handles OOV terms when applying a trained model to new documents, given that word embeddings are updated during training; non-updated embeddings may not be comparable to tuned ones.
- Computational feasibility of topic-word similarity: Section 2.2.1 suggests identifying the top words for each topic via pairwise similarity across the full vocabulary. For realistic vocabularies and topic counts, this can be expensive unless you exploit approximate nearest neighbor search or other accelerations. The paper does not discuss feasibility or runtime.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide quantitative comparisons against LDA and neural topic models (e.g., classification using document-topic proportions, document similarity correlation with human annotations, and topic coherence against strong baselines)? At a minimum, please compare against LDA with the same number of topics on Twenty Newsgroups.
- How were the topics and examples in Figures 3–6 selected? Please report systematic metrics (e.g., average coherence) and show full topic lists or randomized samples to mitigate cherry-picking concerns.
- What is the exact tokenization procedure in Section 3.1? Are noun chunks used, or standard tokenization with lemmatization only? How do you reconcile this with pre-trained word2vec vectors that largely lack multiword entries?
- How do you handle OOV words at inference time on new documents? If embeddings are updated during training, how do you ensure comparability for unseen tokens or frozen embeddings?
- What is the computational complexity for deriving top words per topic? Do you use approximate nearest neighbor search or other optimizations?
- Given that the dataset has 20 newsgroups, have you evaluated information-theoretic measures or supervised classification accuracy across different topic counts to show that n=20 is not simply matching the label cardinality?

Additional comments
- Anonymity: Please remove or anonymize the GitHub link for the submission version.
- Related work to compare with or discuss:

  • Nguyen et al., “Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations,” TACL 2015.
  • Shamanta et al., “Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations,” ECML PKDD 2015.
- Minor issues:
  • Line 315: “it’s similarity” → “its similarity.”
  • Line 361: “the topic basis are strongly affected” is unclear and ungrammatical; please clarify “topic basis” and fix agreement.
  • Figure 5 caption: “words” should be “terms.”
  • References: formatting is inconsistent (e.g., venue names abbreviated differently, missing conference names such as for Röder et al.). Please standardize all entries.

Overall, the model is appealing and the qualitative results are interesting, but without quantitative evaluation and comparisons it is difficult to judge the contribution. Addressing the evaluation gaps and methodological clarifications, and fixing the anonymity issue, would substantially strengthen the submission.
