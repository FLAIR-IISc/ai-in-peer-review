Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid of SGNS word embeddings and LDA-style document-topic mixtures. The context vector is the sum of a pivot word vector and a document vector; the document vector is a simplex-constrained mixture of shared topic vectors. A Dirichlet term encourages sparse document-topic proportions. Training is done with negative sampling and automatic differentiation.
- As I see it, the main claimed contributions are: (1) an architecture that jointly embeds words, documents, and topics in a shared space, using additive composition to condition word prediction on document context; (2) a sparsity-inducing Dirichlet prior over document-topic weights to improve interpretability; (3) an implementation that is straightforward in autodiff frameworks.
- The idea is potentially useful, but the paper’s technical exposition is too unclear to assess novelty and soundness, and the evaluation is inadequate.

Strengths
- Conceptual appeal: Combining interpretable, sparse document-topic mixtures with dense word vectors in a shared space is an interesting direction. The additive context formulation could bias local predictions by global document themes.
- Interpretability affordance: Because topics live in the same space as words, nearest-neighbor words for a topic vector can be inspected directly. This is a sensible design for topic interpretability.
- Qualitative plausibility: On 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News, several example topics look coherent and domain-appropriate, suggesting the model learns meaningful structures.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and technical rigor (primary): Section 2 needs a complete rewrite. A reader familiar with word2vec and LDA should be able to understand the model without guessing. Key terms and steps are underspecified or ambiguous. Examples:
  - “requires deriving a new approximation”: approximation to what (e.g., posterior, evidence)? Why does this make prototypes time-consuming, and how does your approach avoid it?
  - Why tie the input (pivot) and output (target) word embeddings, unlike standard word2vec? What benefit or prior work motivates this?
  - “separate words from the marginal distribution”: what exactly is being separated in the negative sampling objective?
  - “co-adaptation”: define the phenomenon and explain why dropout on dj and wj mitigates it here.
  - “If we only included structure up to this point”: specify which parts of the model you mean.
  - Precisely define all variables and dimensions at first use; give the full training objective with weighting and batching details; specify normalization/constraints for topic and document vectors; clarify the role and tuning of α and λ.
- Evaluation (primary): The paper lacks rigorous quantitative evaluation and baselines.
  - Topic coherence: You report Cv for different n and β, but provide no comparison to LDA or other neural/topic baselines on the same data and vocabulary. Without baselines, it is hard to judge whether lda2vec improves coherence.
  - Downstream utility: No document classification, retrieval, or clustering tasks are reported to demonstrate the usefulness of the document or topic vectors.
  - Ablations: No ablation of the Dirichlet sparsity term, tied embeddings, or the additive context is shown to isolate what matters.
  - Word analogies: The conclusion claims the model “solves word analogies,” but only a few cherry-picked examples are shown; no standard analogy benchmark or coverage statistics are provided.
- Reproducibility and anonymity: Footnote 1 reveals a code repository, which breaks anonymity. Training details (e.g., batching of Ld, initialization, preprocessing choices) are scattered and incomplete for reproduction without the code.
- Presentation issues: Some results are only explained in long figure captions rather than in the main text. Several references are incomplete or malformed (e.g., “Advances in …” for Chang et al., venue missing).

Questions to authors
- Model specification:
  - Provide a clear derivation of the full objective L = Σ Lneg + λΣ(α−1) log pjk, including how Ld is scaled in minibatches and how pjk are parameterized and constrained. Are topics or document vectors normalized?
  - Why tie input and output embeddings? Did you compare tied vs untied?
  - What exactly is meant by “separating observed words from the marginal distribution” in the context of negative sampling?
  - Define “co-adaptation” and justify the specific dropout scheme. What dropout rates were used and how sensitive are results?
  - When you say “structure up to this point,” which components are included?
- Hyperparameters and training:
  - How were α and λ chosen? Provide sensitivity plots for topic coherence and sparsity as a function of α and λ.
  - What is the effect of removing the Dirichlet term (α=1) quantitatively (e.g., coherence, sparsity metrics)?
  - Did you constrain topic vectors’ norms or apply any regularization?
- Evaluation:
  - Please compare topic coherence to an LDA baseline trained on the same preprocessed vocabulary and to neural variants if possible. Report per-topic Cv distributions, not only means.
  - Provide a downstream task (e.g., 20NG document classification) using document/topic features vs baselines (LDA, doc2vec).
  - For analogies, report standard analogy accuracy or at least systematic coverage (number attempted, number correct) and compare to SGNS trained on the same corpus.
- Anonymity: Will you remove code links and any other identifying information for the camera-ready process?

Additional comments
- Typos and style:
  - “it’s similarity” → “its similarity”.
  - Avoid vague phrasing such as “structure up to this point.”
  - Ensure all symbols are defined on first use and maintain consistent notation.
- Figures and captions:
  - Move essential methodological descriptions from captions into the main text; keep captions concise.
- References:
  - Fix incomplete or placeholder venues (e.g., Chang et al. 2009).
  - Verify all citations and formats.
- Overall recommendation will hinge on a substantial rewrite of Section 2 for clarity, addition of baselines and ablations, and toned-down claims about analogies. If these issues are addressed, the idea could be a valuable contribution.
