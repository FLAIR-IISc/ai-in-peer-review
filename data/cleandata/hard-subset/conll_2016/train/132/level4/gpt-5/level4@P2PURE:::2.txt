This paper introduces a neural-style topic model that extends the word2vec objective to jointly learn document embeddings, and then enforces sparsity to yield topic-like outputs. I found the proposed model compelling, and the illustrative examples promising. However, the paper lacks any empirical evaluation: the assessment relies entirely on example tables, with no evidence of representativeness and no direct comparisons to standard or neural topic models. Without quantitative evaluation, it is impossible to gauge the model’s true value, which makes the paper difficult to accept. Two straightforward evaluation options would be:
1) Use the document-level topic representations in a supervised document classification task, comparing against a topic model with the same number of topics (as an indirect evaluation of representation quality).
2) Conduct a direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations, using pairwise comparisons over the topic vectors.

I appreciate the intent to release code, but including a GitHub link in the submission compromises anonymity (strictly speaking, grounds for immediate rejection; I defer to the PCs).

Other issues:
- How were the examples in Figures 3–6 selected? Showing only a subset of topics risks cherry-picking.
- In Section 2.2.1 you suggest computing word representations for topics via pairwise comparisons with every word in the vocabulary. For realistic vocabulary sizes and topic counts, this seems prohibitively expensive—how is this computationally feasible?
- In Section 3.1 you state that SpaCy is used to identify “tokens.” How exactly? Are you extracting noun chunks (and not other chunk types), as in Section 3.2, or doing something else? Given that you then use pre-trained word2vec embeddings (which include relatively few multiword expressions), the tokenization/chunking choices are unclear.
- How does the model handle OOV terms? While you appear to train on the full document collection in the reported experiments (so OOV may not arise there), in deployment you would want to apply the trained model to new documents. If you update word2vec embeddings during training, any untouched (OOV relative to training) pre-trained embeddings will not be directly comparable to the tuned ones—how is this addressed?
- The result that 20 topics performed best on 20 Newsgroups is unsurprising given the dataset’s structure. A very simple additional evaluation could compare information-theoretic measures against the true labels, enabling direct comparison with LDA and related baselines.
- You should compare against several relevant neural topic models, including:
  Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. “A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension.” AAAI, 2015.
  Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. “Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations.” TACL 3 (2015): 299–313.
  Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. “Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations.” In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, pp. 441–457. Springer, 2015.
