- Proposes a neural-style topic model extending the word2vec objective to learn document embeddings, followed by sparsification to mimic topic outputs.
- Strength: The model is interesting, intuitive, and example outputs look promising.
- Major concern: No quantitative evaluation or comparisons to standard/neural topic models; reliance on illustrative tables prevents assessing merit.
- Suggestion: Evaluate learned topics via supervised document classification against topic-model baselines with the same number of topics.
- Suggestion: Directly evaluate on datasets with document similarity annotations using pairwise comparisons over topic vectors.
- Anonymity issue: Including a GitHub link in the submission compromises double-blind anonymity and could justify rejection.
- Issue: Unclear how Figures 3–6 topics were selected; risk of cherry-picking—provide selection criteria and aggregate statistics.
- Scalability concern: Computing topic–word representations via pairwise comparisons with the full vocabulary appears computationally expensive—clarify feasibility and implementation.
- Tokenization ambiguity: Clarify how SpaCy is used to identify “tokens” (noun chunks or other) and reconcile with pretrained word2vec that lacks many multiword terms.
- OOV handling: Unclear how out-of-vocabulary terms are handled at deployment given embeddings are updated during training—discuss applicability to novel documents.
- Evaluation critique: Reporting that 20 topics works best on 20 Newsgroups is unsurprising; recommend information-theoretic comparisons (e.g., mutual information) against true labels and LDA.
- Baseline recommendation: Compare against existing neural topic models (Cao et al. 2015; Nguyen et al. 2015; Shamanta et al. 2015).
- Low-level edits: Fix typos/grammar (e.g., “it’s” → “its”), clarify awkward phrasing about “topic basis,” use “terms” not “words” in Figure 5 caption, and standardize reference formatting.
- Overall: Novel and promising approach but lack of quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons prevents conclusive assessment; addressing evaluation and methodological clarifications would strengthen the paper.
