The idea of combining word2vec-style embeddings with LDA-style topic modeling is potentially interesting and relevant. However, the current manuscript is very hard to follow at the technical level. Section 2, in particular, needs a substantial rewrite so that a reader familiar with word2vec and LDA can quickly grasp the high-level model, the training objective, and how the two components interact. As written, the presentation does not provide a coherent picture of the model.

Major concerns:
- Clarity and completeness of the method. Please provide a clear, self-contained description of the model:
  - What is the probabilistic story or objective function? If it is a hybrid, spell out the joint objective (or the generative process), the role of topics, and the role of embeddings.
  - How do you parameterize the model? Do you learn one embedding per word or separate embeddings for different roles (e.g., pivot vs. target) as in skip-gram/CBOW? If you deviate from word2vec by using the same vector for both roles, motivate this choice and discuss its implications.
  - What is “separating words from a marginal distribution”? Define the distribution, how it is estimated, and why this separation helps.
  - What is “co-adaptation” in your context? Provide a precise definition and explain how your method mitigates it.
  - When you say “If we only included structure up to this point,” specify exactly what structure you refer to (context windows? topic assignments? document-level priors?).
  - If an approximation is required, specify what is being approximated and why. For example, are you approximating an intractable posterior, partition function, or expected log-likelihood? Describe the approximation (e.g., variational, negative sampling, hierarchical softmax) and its consequences.
- Evaluation. The paper appears to lack a rigorous empirical evaluation. Example clusters are insufficient in 2026 NLP standards (and even by 2016 standards). Please include:
  - Clear experimental setup: datasets, preprocessing, hyperparameters, training details, and compute budget.
  - Baselines: vanilla word2vec (SGNS/CBOW), LDA, and relevant joint models (e.g., topical embeddings, lda2vec-style methods) if applicable.
  - Intrinsic metrics: word similarity/relatedness benchmarks, word analogy using standard datasets (e.g., Google, BATS). If you claim the model “solves” analogies, report quantitative results rather than a few cherry-picked examples.
  - Extrinsic tasks: demonstrate whether the learned representations improve downstream performance (e.g., document classification, topic coherence, or retrieval).
  - Ablations: effect of sharing vs. separating embeddings; effect of topic components; effect of any regularizers.
  - Statistical significance or confidence intervals where appropriate.
- Presentation issues:
  - The third paragraph of the introduction is unclear. “Requires deriving a new approximation”: approximation to what quantity, and why? Why is prototype development time-consuming, yet feature evaluation easier? Please explicate the argument and evidence.
  - Figure 2 appears to be a quantitative result but is only explained in a long caption. Move the key description to the main text, define the metric and axes, and interpret the findings.
  - Footnote 1 violates anonymity and should be removed or deferred until the camera-ready version, per the conference policy.
  - Typo: “it’s similarity” should be “its similarity.”
  - Reference formatting: the Chang citation lists “Advances in …” without the venue. Please provide complete and correct bibliographic details.

Actionable suggestions for revision:
- Rewrite Section 2 around a clear structure: problem setup; notation; model definition (with either the generative process or explicit objective); training/inference method; computational complexity; relation to word2vec and LDA; design choices (e.g., shared vs. separate embeddings) with motivation.
- Provide an intuitive high-level overview before diving into equations, and include a schematic diagram that shows how topics and embeddings interact.
- Define all nonstandard terms (“separate words from a marginal distribution,” “co-adaptation,” “structure”) with formal definitions and, where possible, small illustrative examples.
- Include a thorough empirical section with strong baselines and both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations. Ensure that analogy claims are supported by standard benchmarks rather than isolated examples.
- Ensure anonymity, fix typos, and standardize references.

Overall assessment: The research direction is promising, but the current submission falls short on clarity and empirical validation. A substantial rewrite focused on precise modeling, transparent training details, and rigorous evaluation is needed before the contribution can be properly assessed.
