Summary
The paper proposes a neural topic modeling approach that extends the word2vec objective to learn document embeddings and then applies sparsification to approximate topic distributions. The idea is intuitive and the qualitative examples are promising.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing modeling idea that bridges distributed representations with sparse topic-like structure.
- Well-written exposition of the core method, with illustrative examples.
- Code release is appreciated and supports reproducibility (though see anonymity concern below).

Major concerns
- Lack of quantitative evaluation: The paper relies almost entirely on qualitative tables of examples. Without empirical comparisons to standard topic models (e.g., LDA) and recent neural topic models, it is impossible to assess the effectiveness, robustness, or generalizability of the approach. This is the primary reason I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
- Anonymity violation: Including a live GitHub link in the submission compromises the double-blind review process. Please remove or anonymize all such links for compliance with conference policy.

Suggestions for empirical evaluation
At minimum, please include standard, quantitative evaluations that allow comparison with strong baselines.
- Supervised document classification or clustering: Use the learned sparse document/topic representations as features for document categorization (e.g., on 20 Newsgroups), and compare against LDA and existing neural topic models at matched topic cardinalities. Report performance with multiple random seeds.
- Document similarity/retrieval: Evaluate the learned representations against datasets with document similarity or relatedness annotations. Alternatively, evaluate pairwise similarity over the topic vectors for retrieval, reporting metrics such as MAP or AUC.
- Topic coherence: Report topic coherence scores (e.g., NPMI or UCI) to assess the interpretability of induced topics and compare with LDA and neural baselines.
- Sensitivity to topic count: Systematically vary the number of topics and analyze stability and performance to show that the model is not tuned to a single plausible K (e.g., 20 on 20 Newsgroups).
- Ablations: Compare with and without sparsification; assess the impact of fine-tuning word embeddings versus keeping them fixed; and explore dimensionality effects.

Comparative baselines
Please include direct comparisons with both classical and neural topic models. In particular, compare against:
- LDA and related classical models at matched K.
- Neural topic models such as:
  - Cao et al., “A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension” (AAAI 2015).
  - Nguyen et al., “Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations” (TACL 2015).
  - Shamanta et al., “Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations” (ECML/PKDD 2015).
These comparisons are essential to position your contribution.

Methodological clarifications and feasibility
- Selection of qualitative examples (Figures 3–6): How were the shown topics and terms selected? Please describe the selection criteria and provide either (i) a random sample of topics, or (ii) a comprehensive appendix with all topics, to avoid concerns about cherry-picking.
- Computing topic “word representations”: Section 2.2.1 suggests pairwise comparisons between each topic and every word in the vocabulary. For realistic vocabularies and topic counts, this is likely expensive. Please discuss computational complexity, runtime, and whether you use approximations (e.g., top-k pruning, ANN search, or negative sampling).
- Tokenization and multiword units: Section 3.1 states that tokens are identified using SpaCy. Are you using token-level units, noun chunks only, or other phrase chunks? How do these choices interact with pre-trained word2vec embeddings that include few multiword terms? Please clarify preprocessing, vocabulary construction, and how multiword expressions are handled.
- OOV and deployment to unseen documents: If word embeddings are fine-tuned during training, how do you handle OOV words at test time so that their embeddings remain comparable to tuned vectors? Please discuss strategies (e.g., freezing embeddings, subword composition, retrofitting, or UNK handling) and how document encodings generalize.
- On the choice of 20 topics for 20 Newsgroups: This alignment is unsurprising given the dataset structure. Beyond varying K, consider reporting information-theoretic alignment with ground-truth labels (e.g., mutual information or purity) and comparing directly to LDA and neural baselines under the same protocol.

Overall assessment
The modeling idea is interesting and the qualitative results are encouraging. However, the absence of quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons makes it impossible to judge the true merit of the approach. The anonymity issue also needs to be addressed. With thorough empirical validation and policy-compliant anonymization, this work could become a strong contribution.

Minor comments

- Line 361: “the topic basis are affected” is ungrammatical and unclear; please define “topic basis” precisely and fix agreement (e.g., “the topic basis is affected by …”).
- Figure 5 caption: consider “terms” rather than “words” if multiword units are included.
- References: Ensure consistent formatting (capitalization of titles, venue names). Some entries are incomplete (e.g., Röder et al. missing the conference name); please standardize all citations.
