- Proposes a neural-style topic model extending word2vec to learn document embeddings with enforced sparsity; core idea and qualitative examples are promising.
- Major flaw: no substantive empirical evaluation—only example tables with unknown representativeness and no comparisons to standard or neural topic models.
- Recommendation: add indirect evaluation (use topic representations in supervised document classification vs a topic model baseline).
- Recommendation: alternatively or additionally add direct evaluation (document similarity on datasets with human annotations via pairwise topic-vector comparisons).
- Anonymity breach: including a GitHub link in the submission compromises anonymity and could warrant rejection.
- Example selection unclear: clarify how Figures 3–6 were chosen or provide comprehensive results to avoid cherry-picking.
- Complexity concern (Section 2.2.1): computing topic word representations via pairwise comparisons with every vocabulary word seems computationally prohibitive—quantify complexity and discuss tractability/approximations.
- Tokenization ambiguity (Section 3.1): specify what “tokens” are (noun chunks only, other chunks, or plain tokens) and justify compatibility with pretrained word2vec embeddings that lack many multiword terms.
- OOV handling: explain strategy for out-of-vocabulary terms at inference and how OOVs affect comparability if embeddings are fine-tuned during training.
- 20 Newsgroups experiment: the 20-topic result is unsurprising; compare learned topic distributions to true labels via information-theoretic measures and benchmark against LDA and related methods with the same topic count.
- Related work: should compare to existing neural topic models (Cao et al. 2015; Nguyen et al. 2015; Shamanta et al. 2015).
- Low-level edits: fix “it’s similarity” → “its similarity,” clarify “topic basis” wording, use “terms” instead of “words” in Figure 5, and standardize/complete reference formatting (e.g., Röder et al.).
- Summary: modeling idea is appealing and qualitative results encouraging, but lack of quantitative evaluation, baselines, efficiency analysis, OOV strategy, and data-processing clarity prevents fair assessment—addressing these would substantially strengthen the paper.
