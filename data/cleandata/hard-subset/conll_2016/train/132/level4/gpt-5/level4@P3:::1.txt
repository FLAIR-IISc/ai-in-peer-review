Summary
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that combines Skip-gram with Negative Sampling (SGNS) word embeddings with Dirichlet-distributed, sparse document–topic mixtures. The context vector used to predict target words is the sum of a word vector and a document vector; document vectors are constrained to be convex combinations of topic vectors via a softmax over topic weights, with a Dirichlet prior encouraging sparsity. The authors implement the model in an automatic differentiation framework and present qualitative topic examples on Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News, along with topic coherence scores (Cv) on Twenty Newsgroups.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple formulation that joins SGNS embeddings with sparse, interpretable document–topic mixtures by summing word and document vectors and constraining the latter via a Dirichlet prior on simplex-normalized topic proportions.
2) An implementation that fits naturally in autodiff frameworks and learns word, topic, and document vectors jointly.
3) Empirical demonstrations showing reasonable topic coherence on Twenty Newsgroups and qualitative topic and analogy examples on Hacker News. (However, the evaluation lacks baselines and rigor.)

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and potential practical utility: The model offers a straightforward way to inject LDA-like interpretability (sparse topic mixtures) into embedding-based models. The context-as-sum formulation is intuitive and consistent with compositional properties observed in word embeddings. This is appealing for practitioners who need both semantics and interpretability.
- Unifying representation space: By placing word, topic, and document vectors in the same space, the model enables direct inspection of topic–word similarities and interpretable document memberships. The examples in Figures 3–5 show that topic vectors retrieve semantically coherent word sets (e.g., Space, Encryption) and that word similarities reflect corpus-specific semantics.
- Use of topic coherence (Cv): Reporting Cv on Twenty Newsgroups is a step toward quantitative evaluation and uses a metric known to correlate with human judgments. The choice of β = 0.75 aligns with best practices in negative sampling.
- Implementation details and training setup: The paper specifies window size, negative sampling rate, and some regularization (dropout). The optimization objective is explicit, and the model is compatible with GPU-accelerated autodiff, which lowers the barrier to adoption.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and exposition (major): Section 2 is difficult to follow for readers familiar with SGNS and LDA but not the proposed hybrid. Key terms and claims are imprecise or undefined. Examples:
  - “requires deriving a new approximation” (approximation to what? variational/posterior inference?) and the associated claims about prototyping speed need concrete context.
  - The motivation for tying input and output embeddings (“use the same word vectors for pivot and target”) is not explained; how does this affect training stability or performance relative to SGNS with separate matrices?
  - “separate[d] from the marginal distribution” is unclear; please state precisely what distributional separation the loss induces (e.g., maximizing the log-odds of observed pairs relative to negatives drawn from u^β).
  - “co-adaptation” is mentioned without explanation; justify the use of dropout on both document and word vectors and its expected effect.
  - “If we only included structure up to this point” – specify exactly which components are meant (SGNS with summed word/document vectors but without the Dirichlet/topic mixture constraint?).
  - Notational and grammatical issues (“it’s similarity” → “its similarity”) and overloaded symbols (e.g., pjk both pre- and post-softmax) impede readability.
  The paper would benefit from a clean, self-contained presentation of the computational graph: define all variables, show the full objective with hyperparameters, detail the simplex constraint, and explain gradient flow through the softmax and Dirichlet term.
- Evaluation is insufficient (major): There are no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., LDA topic coherence on the same data, doc2vec, topical word embedding methods) nor to closely related hybrids. There are no downstream or intrinsic document-level tasks (classification, retrieval, clustering) to demonstrate the utility of the learned representations. For analogies, only a handful of cherry-picked examples are shown without standard benchmarks or coverage metrics. Figure 2 presents a parameter sweep but no baselines or statistical analysis; it is hard to interpret the absolute Cv values.
- Positioning and related work (major): The paper does not adequately engage with prior work that combines topics and embeddings or learns interpretable document vectors. Without comparisons or a clear positioning, the degree of novelty is unclear.
- Anonymity and reproducibility: Footnote 1 and linked repositories/datasets reveal author identities and violate anonymity. Moreover, although some hyperparameters are given (e.g., α, λ = 200), there is no ablation showing the effect of the Dirichlet strength α, λ, or dropout, and some training details (learning rates, batching strategy for Ld, early stopping) are underspecified.
- Presentation quality: Several references are incomplete or malformed (e.g., “Advances in …” without venue; unusual author lists for scikit-learn). Figure captions carry key methodological content that should be in the main text. Figure 1 is hard to parse and should be redrawn with a clear legend.

Questions to Authors
- Please provide a clear rationale for tying input and output embeddings. Did you compare to the standard SGNS setup with separate matrices? What impact does tying have on topic coherence and word similarity?
- How exactly is the Dirichlet prior optimized through the softmaxed topic proportions? Any issues with vanishing gradients when proportions become sparse? Why α = 1/n and λ = 200—how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Can you report Cv for LDA (with matched preprocessing and number of topics) on Twenty Newsgroups, and provide a downstream document task comparing lda2vec to LDA and doc2vec?
- On Hacker News, can you provide quantitative evaluations (e.g., topic coherence computed on a compatible vocabulary, or an extrinsic task) rather than only qualitative topics and analogies?
- Please clarify the statement about “separating observed words from the marginal distribution” and the role of β. What negative sampling distribution did you ultimately use per experiment?

Additional comments
- Please remove identifying links for the anonymous submission; code/data can be provided via an anonymous repository or released post-review.
- Fix grammar and typos (e.g., “its similarity”), standardize notation, and correct references (complete venues, years, and author lists).
- Consider moving critical methodological details out of figure captions into the main text, and provide a concise algorithmic summary.

Overall assessment
The idea is promising and potentially useful, but in its current form the paper lacks clarity and rigorous evaluation. I encourage a substantial revision focusing on a clear, self-contained description of the model, comparisons to strong baselines, and at least one quantitative document-level downstream evaluation.
