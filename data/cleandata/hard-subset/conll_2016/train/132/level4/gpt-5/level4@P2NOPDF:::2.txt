This paper introduces a neural topic-like model that extends the word2vec objective to also learn document embeddings, regularized with sparsity to approximate topic mixtures. The core idea is elegant, and the qualitative examples are promising.

The central weakness is the absence of empirical evaluation. The paper relies almost entirely on illustrative tables, with no indication of how representative the examples are and no quantitative comparisons to standard topic models (e.g., LDA) or neural variants. Without such evaluation, it is impossible to gauge the model’s effectiveness, which makes acceptance difficult.

Concrete evaluation paths you could pursue include:
- Use the inferred topic representation of each document as features in a supervised document classification task, and compare against a topic model with the same number of topics (as an indirect test of representation quality).
- Perform direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations by comparing pairwise similarities over the learned topic vectors.

Anonymity: Including a GitHub link in the submission compromises double-blind review. This alone can be grounds for rejection; I defer to the program chairs but strongly recommend removing any identifying links in the anonymized version.

Other issues and requests for clarification:
- Selection of examples (Figures 3–6): Please detail how these were chosen and whether they reflect all topics or a subset. Without a clear selection protocol, concerns about cherry-picking arise.
- Feasibility of topic-to-word scoring: In Section 2.2.1, you mention computing topic “word representations” via pairwise comparisons with every vocabulary item. For realistic vocabularies and topic counts, this appears computationally expensive. How is this made tractable (e.g., via pruning, ANN search, batching, or caching)?
- Tokenization with spaCy (Section 3.1): What exactly counts as a “token”? Are you extracting only noun chunks (as in Section 3.2) or using standard tokenization or other chunk types? Given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings (which include relatively few multiword terms), the tokenization/granularity needs to be spelled out.
- OOV handling: How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary terms at inference time on new documents? If you fine-tune word embeddings during training on the corpus, how do you ensure comparability between tuned embeddings and any untouched OOV embeddings when applying the model out of domain?
- Number of topics on 20NG: That 20 topics worked best on 20 Newsgroups is unsurprising given the dataset’s structure. A simple additional check would be an information-theoretic comparison against the gold labels (e.g., mutual information or NMI) with a direct LDA baseline under matched topic cardinality.
- Related neural topic models to compare against include:
  - Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. “A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension.” AAAI, 2015.
  - Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. “Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations.” TACL 3 (2015): 299–313.
  - Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. “Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations.” In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, 2015.
