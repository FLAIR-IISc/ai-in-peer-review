This paper introduces a neural variant of a topic model by extending the word2vec objective to jointly learn document embeddings, which are then sparsified to emulate topic-model-style outputs. I found the proposed model appealing, and the qualitative examples are encouraging. However, the paper lacks any substantive empirical evaluation—the assessment relies entirely on illustrative examples, with no indication of how representative they are, nor any direct comparison against standard or neural topic models. Without quantitative evaluation, it is impossible to assess the true merit of the approach, which makes acceptance difficult. Potential evaluation routes include: (1) using the inferred document-topic representations as features in a supervised document classification task, and comparing against a topic model with the same number of topics (i.e., an indirect probe of representation quality); or (2) direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations (e.g., pairwise similarity computed from the topic mixtures). It is great that you plan to release code, but including the GitHub link in the submission compromises anonymity (strictly speaking, grounds for desk rejection; I leave this to the PCs).

Other issues:
- How were the examples in Figures 3–6 selected? Showing only a subset of topics, tokens, or analogies raises concerns about cherry-picking.
- In Section 2.2.1 you suggest computing topic “word representations” by comparing each topic vector to every word vector in the vocabulary. For realistic vocabulary sizes and topic counts, this nearest-neighbor search can be quite expensive at scale; please discuss feasibility and any approximations.
- In Section 3.1 you state that you identify “tokens” using SpaCy. How exactly? Are you extracting noun chunks (as in Section 3.2) or using a different strategy? Given that you also use pretrained word2vec embeddings (which include relatively few multiword expressions), the tokenization–embedding alignment is unclear.
- How does the model handle OOV terms at inference time? While you train on the full corpus here, practical use will involve applying the model to new documents. Since you fine-tune token embeddings during training, embeddings for unseen tokens (or unadjusted pretrained vectors) may not be directly comparable to the tuned embedding space.
- The finding that 20 topics perform best on the 20 Newsgroups dataset is not surprising given its composition. A simple information-theoretic evaluation relative to the true labels (e.g., mutual information or clustering metrics) would provide additional validation and enable a direct comparison with LDA and related baselines.
- Related neural topic models you should compare against include:

  Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics 3 (2015): 299–313.
  Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, pp. 441–457. Springer, 2015.
