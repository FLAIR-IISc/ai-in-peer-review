A combination of word2vec and LDA is potentially interesting. However, the paper’s technical presentation is currently too opaque. Section 2, in particular, needs a complete rewrite so that a reader already familiar with word2vec and LDA can easily grasp a high-level picture of how the models are combined. As written, the paper does not provide this overview, and the details are very hard to follow.

More detailed comments:
- The third paragraph of the introduction is unclear. It claims the approach “requires deriving a new approximation,” but an approximation to what (objective, inference, or likelihood)? It also asserts that developing prototypes is time consuming—why?—and that evaluating features is easier—why? Please justify these claims.
- Why do you use the same word vectors for pivot and target words, unlike the standard word2vec setup that has distinct input and output embeddings? What motivates this decision, and what are its implications?
- What does it mean to “separate words from a marginal distribution”? Please define this precisely and explain how it is used in the model or training procedure.
- What is meant by “co-adaptation” in this context?
- “If we only included structure up to this point” — what structure, exactly, has been included so far?
- Typo: “it’s similarity” should be “its similarity.”
- Footnote 1 breaks anonymity.
- There appears to be no substantive evaluation. The era when a few example clusters sufficed is long past in NLP. Figure 2 looks like it might be a quantitative evaluation, but it is only described in an overly long caption. Please move a clear description and analysis into the main text and provide systematic, quantitative results.
- The conclusion overstates the findings by claiming the model solves word analogies. The paper presents only a handful of cherry-picked examples (e.g., king/queen-style pairs), which do not support a claim of solving analogies.
- The Chang reference lists the venue only as “Advances in …”. Please provide the complete and correct conference or journal name.

Overall, the idea may be promising, but the paper requires substantial rewriting for clarity and a proper, quantitative evaluation before it is ready for publication.
