This paper proposes a neural-style topic model that extends the purpose of word2vec by learning document embedding, which is limited by specifications, and thus mimics the output of a topic model.

I really liked the authors' proposed model, and the examples they presented seemed very promising. However, what was truly missing from the paper was any empirical evaluation of the model – the evaluation relied on a complete table of examples, without any indication of how representative the examples were, or any attempt to directly compare it with standard or neural topic models. Without any empirical evaluation, it is impossible to get an idea of the actual value of the model, which makes it very difficult to accept the paper. Here are some ideas on how the authors could have achieved this:

(1) Use the topic representation of each document in a supervised document classification setup and compare it with a topic model with the same topic cardinality (i.e., as an indirect evaluation of the quality of the representation); or (2) directly evaluate the document similarity through a dataset with annotations ( pairwise comparisons based on topic vectors).

I am glad you are publishing the code, but you have concealed your identity by publishing the GitHub link in the submitted version of the paper (strictly speaking, this is enough to reject the paper directly, but I leave it to your discretion).

Other problems:

- How did you select the examples in Figure 3-6? Presenting a subset of actual topics probably sounds like cherry-picking.
- In section 2.2.1, you discussed the possibility of counting word representations for topics by comparing them with each word in the vocabulary, but this is going to be an extremely expensive process for a reasonable vocabulary size and number of topics; is this really possible?
- You say that you identify "tokens" using space in section 3.1 – how? Do you extract noun chunks (but not any other chunk types), similarly in section 3.2, or something else? Since you are going to say that you use word2vec pre-trained embeddings (which only contain a small number of multi-word terms), it is not clear what you are doing here.
- How does your model deal with OOV terms? Yes, in the tests mentioned in the paper, you train the model on the entire document collection, so this is probably not an immediate problem, but there will be some contexts where you want to apply the trained model to new documents, in which case updating the word2vec token embeddings means that any non-updated (with respect to OOV, the training collection) word2vec embeddings will not be directly comparable to the tuned embeddings.
- The finding that 20 topics worked best in the 20-newgroups corpus was not surprising given its structure. Perhaps another (very simple) form of evaluation could have been some metadata comparison that could be done with respect to the actual document labels, where you could again directly compare with LDA, etc.
- A few more neural topic models that you need to compare with your own:
