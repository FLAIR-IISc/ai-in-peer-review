This paper presents a neural topic model that extends the word2vec objective to learn document embeddings. These embeddings are then constrained via sparsification to mimic the sparse document-topic distributions of a conventional topic model.

The proposed model is conceptually appealing, and the qualitative examples provided in the paper are compelling. However, the paper's most significant shortcoming is the complete lack of quantitative empirical evaluation. The assessment of the model's performance relies exclusively on illustrative tables of topics and terms, with no information on how representative these examples are. Furthermore, there is no attempt to compare the model's output directly with that of standard or other neural topic models. Without a rigorous empirical evaluation, it is difficult to assess the true contribution of the model, making it challenging to recommend for acceptance. The authors could have strengthened the paper significantly by including such an evaluation. For example:

1.  An indirect, task-based evaluation could have been performed by using the learned document-topic representations as features in a supervised document classification task. Performance could then be compared against representations generated by a baseline topic model (e.g., LDA) with the same number of topics.
2.  A direct evaluation could have been conducted on a dataset with ground-truth document similarity annotations, using pairwise comparisons of the model's generated topic vectors to measure performance.

While the intention to release the source code is commendable, the inclusion of a public GitHub link in the submitted manuscript is a violation of the double-blind review policy. This is a serious issue that could be grounds for rejection, though the final decision rests with the program chairs.

Other Issues:

*   The methodology for selecting the examples presented in Figures 3-6 is not described. Without a clear selection process, presenting a subset of the learned topics raises concerns about potential cherry-picking.
*   In Section 2.2.1, the paper discusses calculating topic representations through pairwise comparison with every word in the vocabulary. This process seems computationally infeasible for reasonably sized vocabularies and topic numbers. The authors should comment on the practical scalability of this approach.
*   The description of tokenization in Section 3.1 is ambiguous. The paper mentions identifying "tokens" with SpaCy but does not clarify the exact procedure—for instance, whether it involves extracting only noun chunks (as in Section 3.2) or other token types. This lack of clarity is compounded by the subsequent use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which have limited coverage of multi-word expressions.
*   The paper does not address how the model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. While the reported experiments train on the entire document collection, a trained model must be applicable to new documents. In such a scenario, the fine-tuned embeddings from the training set would not be directly comparable to the original, non-updated embeddings of OOV words encountered in new text.
*   The finding that 20 topics performed best on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising. A simple yet more informative evaluation here could have involved an information-theoretic comparison between the model's document clusters and the ground-truth labels, which would also have enabled a direct quantitative comparison with models like LDA.
*   The discussion of related work should be expanded to include a comparison with several other relevant neural topic models, such as:
    *   Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension." In AAAI, pp. 2210-2216. 2015.
    *   Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics 3 (2015): 299-313.
    *   Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, pp. 441-457. Springer International Publishing, 2015.

Minor Points:

*   Line 315: "it's similarity" should be "its similarity".
*   Line 361: The phrase "the topic basis are affected" is grammatically awkward and its meaning is unclear. Please rephrase for clarity.
*   Figure 5 Caption: Given the potential for multi-word expressions, "terms" would be a more accurate descriptor than "words".
*   The reference formatting is inconsistent. For example, conference/journal titles are abbreviated differently ("Advances in ..." vs. "Advances in Neural ..."), and some citations are incomplete (e.g., Roder et al. is missing the conference name).
