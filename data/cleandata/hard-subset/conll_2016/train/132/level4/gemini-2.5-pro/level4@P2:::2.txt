### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that jointly learns dense word embeddings alongside interpretable, sparse document-level topic representations. The model extends the skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS) objective from word2vec. It achieves this by defining the context vector as a sum of the pivot word's vector and a document-specific vector. This document vector is, in turn, represented as a weighted mixture of a set of shared, latent topic vectors. To encourage interpretability and mimic the output of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), a Dirichlet-based likelihood term is added to the loss function, promoting sparsity in the document-topic distributions.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Hybrid Model Architecture:** The primary contribution is the elegant formulation that unifies dense, distributed word representations (like word2vec) and sparse, mixed-membership topic models (like LDA) within a single, end-to-end trainable neural framework. The approach of embedding words, documents, and topics into the same vector space is conceptually appealing and allows for direct comparison between these different semantic units.

2.  **Demonstration of a Practical Implementation:** The work successfully demonstrates that such a hybrid model can be effectively implemented and optimized using modern automatic differentiation frameworks. This approach simplifies the development process compared to deriving custom inference procedures, which is often required for traditional probabilistic topic models, thus facilitating more rapid prototyping and extension.

### Strengths

1.  **Model Novelty and Coherence:** The core idea of integrating a document vector into the word2vec context and then decomposing that document vector into a sparse mixture of topics is both novel and well-motivated. It provides a principled way to combine the local, token-level semantics captured by word embeddings with the global, document-level themes captured by topic models. The joint training objective allows information to flow between the word and document representations, potentially enriching both.

2.  **Qualitatively Compelling and Interpretable Results:** The paper presents strong qualitative evidence that the model works as intended. The topics discovered in both the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets (Figures 3 and 4) are highly coherent and intuitively align with the expected themes of those corpora. Furthermore, the model successfully retains the ability to capture the fine-grained semantic regularities of word embeddings, as demonstrated by the word similarity and analogy tasks (Figures 5 and 6). These examples convincingly showcase the model's potential.

3.  **Potential for Broader Impact and Extensibility:** By leveraging a standard automatic differentiation library, the authors have created a model that is accessible and extensible. This framework makes it easier for other researchers to reproduce the results, build upon the model, and explore variations (e.g., incorporating other metadata, using different network components) without needing specialized expertise in variational inference or Gibbs sampling. The public release of the code further strengthens this point.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Lack of Quantitative Evaluation and Comparison:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the complete absence of a quantitative, comparative evaluation against established baselines. The assessment of the model relies entirely on presenting selected qualitative examples. While these examples are promising, they provide no objective measure of the model's performance and are susceptible to cherry-picking. To substantiate its contributions, the paper must compare lda2vec against standard models like LDA, Paragraph Vectors (doc2vec), and other neural topic models on well-defined tasks. Such an evaluation could include:
    *   **Downstream Task Performance:** Using the learned document representations as features for a document classification or clustering task and comparing the accuracy/purity against baselines.
    *   **Topic Quality Metrics:** While the paper reports topic coherence (Cv), it does not compare these scores to those achieved by a standard LDA model on the same preprocessed data. This comparison is essential to claim that the model produces "coherent topics."
    *   **Document Similarity:** Evaluating the learned document vectors on a benchmark dataset with human-annotated document similarity ratings.
    Without this empirical grounding, the true value and effectiveness of the proposed model are difficult to assess.

2.  **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work:** The paper fails to position itself with respect to several highly relevant works on neural topic models that also combine topic modeling with word embeddings. Several models with similar goals were published around the same time (e.g., Cao et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2015). A proper discussion of these alternative approaches is needed to clarify the specific novelty and advantages of the lda2vec formulation.

3.  **Anonymity Violation:** The submission violates the double-blind review policy by including a direct, non-anonymized link to a public GitHub repository in the footnote on page 4. This link clearly identifies the author and is a serious breach of conference policy.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The evaluation rests heavily on the examples presented in Figures 3-6. How were these specific topics, words, and analogies selected for inclusion in the paper? Can you provide evidence that these examples are representative of the model's overall output quality and not the result of cherry-picking?

2.  Could you clarify the preprocessing details for the Twenty Newsgroups experiment? Section 3.1 states you identify "tokens" using SpaCy and then initialize with pre-trained word2vec embeddings. How were multi-word expressions or named entities handled in this pipeline, especially given that standard word2vec embeddings are primarily for unigrams?

3.  How is the model intended to handle unseen documents or out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words at inference time? Since the word embeddings are fine-tuned during training, simply using a pre-trained vector for an OOV word would place it in a different semantic space than the model's tuned embeddings. What is the proposed strategy for generating a document representation in this scenario?
