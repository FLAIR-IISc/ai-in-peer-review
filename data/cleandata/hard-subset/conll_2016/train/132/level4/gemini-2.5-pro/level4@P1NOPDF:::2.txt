This paper proposes a neural topic model that extends the word2vec objective to simultaneously learn document embeddings. These embeddings are then sparsified to mimic the sparse topic distributions produced by traditional topic models.

The proposed model is elegant and the qualitative examples presented are promising. However, the paper's primary weakness is its complete lack of empirical evaluation. The evaluation relies entirely on a few selected examples, with no quantitative analysis to demonstrate their representativeness or to facilitate a direct comparison with standard or other neural topic models. This omission makes it impossible to assess the model's effectiveness and is a significant barrier to acceptance.

To address this, the authors could have performed several types of evaluation, for instance:
1.  An extrinsic evaluation, such as using the learned document representations as features in a supervised document classification task. The performance could be compared against baseline topic models (e.g., LDA) with the same number of topics.
2.  An intrinsic evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations, comparing the similarity of the model's document vectors (e.g., using cosine similarity) against human judgments.

While the authors' commitment to releasing their code is commendable, the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted manuscript is a serious breach of the anonymity policy. While I leave the final decision to the Program Chairs, this is often grounds for rejection.

Other Issues:
*   The paper does not specify how the examples in Figures 3-6 were selected. Presenting only a subset of topics without a clear selection methodology raises concerns about potential cherry-picking.
*   In Section 2.2.1, the authors suggest calculating topic-word representations via pairwise comparison with every word in the vocabulary. This approach seems computationally prohibitive for any reasonably sized vocabulary and number of topics. The authors should comment on its feasibility or clarify if a more efficient method was used.
*   Section 3.1 states that SpaCy is used to identify "tokens," but the process is unclear. Is it based on noun chunking (as in Section 3.2) or another method? This is particularly confusing given the subsequent use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which typically contain few multi-word expressions. The tokenization strategy needs to be clarified.
*   The paper does not address how the model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. While this may not be an issue when training and evaluating on a closed corpus, it is a critical consideration for applying the model to new documents. Since the model fine-tunes the initial word2vec embeddings, the resulting embeddings for vocabulary words would no longer be directly comparable to the original, pre-trained embeddings for OOV words. The authors should discuss a strategy for this scenario.
*   The finding that 20 topics works best for the 20 Newsgroups dataset is unsurprising given the corpus's structure. The authors missed an opportunity for a simple quantitative evaluation here. For instance, they could have used an information-theoretic measure (e.g., Normalized Mutual Information) to compare the topic clusters against the ground-truth document labels, which would have enabled a direct comparison with LDA and other models.
*   The related work section could be strengthened by discussing and comparing the proposed model to other recent neural topic models, such as:
    Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension." In AAAI, pp. 2210-2216. 2015.
    Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics 3 (2015): 299-313.
    Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, pp. 441-457. Springer International Publishing, 2015.

Minor Issues:
*   Line 315: "it's similarity" should be "its similarity".
*   Line 361: The phrase "topic basis are affected" is unclear. Please clarify what "topic basis" refers to and rephrase for clarity.
*   Caption for Figure 5: Given that some examples are multi-word expressions, "terms" would be a more accurate descriptor than "words".
*   The reference formatting is inconsistent. For example, some entries use abbreviated titles ("Advances in..."), and the Roder et al. reference is missing the conference name. The bibliography should be carefully checked for consistency and completeness.
