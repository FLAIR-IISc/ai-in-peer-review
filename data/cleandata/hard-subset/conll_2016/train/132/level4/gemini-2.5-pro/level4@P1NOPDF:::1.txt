The proposed combination of word2vec and LDA is a potentially interesting research direction. However, the paper's central weakness is its profoundly unclear technical description, which makes the core contribution nearly impossible to understand. Section 2, in particular, requires a complete rewrite. As it stands, even a reader familiar with both word2vec and LDA would struggle to form a high-level picture of how the models are being combined.

More detailed comments on the lack of clarity and other issues are listed below:

**Clarity of Technical Description and Motivation**

*   The third paragraph of the introduction is difficult to follow. For instance, the paper states that prior work "requires deriving a new approximation," but it is unclear what is being approximated. The text also fails to explain why developing prototypes is "time consuming" or why evaluating features is "easier."

*   The paper omits the motivation for key modeling decisions. For example, unlike the original word2vec model, the proposed approach uses the same vectors for pivot and target words. The paper provides no justification for this significant departure from the established method, leaving the reader to guess the rationale.

*   Several key technical terms and phrases are used without clear definition. For example:
    *   What does it mean to "separate words from a marginal distribution"?
    *   What is the precise definition of "co-adaptation" in this context?
    *   The phrase "If we only included structure up to this point" is ambiguous, as the nature of this "structure" is never specified.

**Evaluation**

*   The paper is critically lacking in quantitative evaluation. In the modern NLP landscape, presenting a few example clusters as the sole evidence of a model's performance is no longer sufficient.

*   While Figure 2 appears to present some form of quantitative results, it is described only in a lengthy caption and is not mentioned, let alone analyzed, in the main body of the paper. This is not an acceptable way to present experimental results.

*   The conclusion's claim that the model "solves word analogies" is a significant overstatement. This claim is supported only by a few cherry-picked examples (of the "king - man + woman = queen" variety), which is insufficient evidence to demonstrate a general capability for solving analogy tasks.

**Minor Issues**

*   **Anonymity:** Footnote 1 appears to break the anonymity of the review process.
*   **Typos:** The paper contains grammatical errors, such as using "it's similarity" instead of "its similarity."
*   **Citations:** The reference for Chang et al. is incomplete, listing the conference/journal name only as "Advances in...". Please provide the full citation.
