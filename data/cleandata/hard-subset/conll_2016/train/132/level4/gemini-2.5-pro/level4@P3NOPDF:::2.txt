This paper introduces a neural topic model that extends the word2vec objective. The proposed method learns both word and document embeddings simultaneously. The document embeddings are then constrained through a sparsification process, designed to produce outputs analogous to traditional topic models.

The proposed model is conceptually interesting and elegant, and the qualitative examples presented in the paper are highly promising. However, the paper's primary and most significant weakness is the complete absence of quantitative empirical evaluation. The evaluation relies entirely on showcasing selected topics and terms, which, while illustrative, provides no objective measure of the model's performance or any basis for comparison with existing work. Without a systematic evaluation, it is impossible to assess the model's effectiveness relative to the state of the art, making it very difficult to recommend for acceptance in its current form.

The authors could have strengthened the paper considerably by including standard evaluation methods. For instance:
1.  **Extrinsic Evaluation:** The learned document-topic representations could be used as features in a supervised downstream task, such as document classification. Performance on this task (e.g., accuracy or F1 score) could be directly compared against representations generated by standard models like LDA or other neural topic models with the same number of topics.
2.  **Intrinsic Evaluation:** The model could be evaluated on a dataset with document similarity annotations. The quality of the learned representations could be measured by comparing the cosine similarity of the model's document vectors with the ground-truth similarity scores.

Additionally, a critical procedural issue is the violation of the double-blind review policy. The inclusion of a public GitHub link in the submission directly compromises the authors' anonymity. While the final decision rests with the Program Chairs, this is typically sufficient grounds for rejection.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for Clarification:**

*   **Selection of Examples:** The selection process for the examples in Figures 3-6 is not described. To avoid the appearance of cherry-picking, the authors should clarify how these specific topics and terms were chosen and whether they are representative of the model's overall output.

*   **Computational Feasibility:** In Section 2.2.1, the paper discusses calculating topic representations via pairwise comparison with every word in the vocabulary. For a large vocabulary and a moderate number of topics, this computation seems prohibitively expensive. Could the authors comment on the feasibility and computational cost of this approach in practice?

*   **Preprocessing Clarity:** The description of the text preprocessing in Section 3.1 is unclear. The paper states that "tokens" are identified using SpaCy, but later mentions extracting only noun chunks. It is also unclear how this tokenization strategy aligns with the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which typically operate on single words and a limited set of multi-word expressions. Please clarify the exact tokenization and feature extraction pipeline.

*   **Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Terms:** The paper does not address how the model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms when applied to new, unseen documents. Since the model fine-tunes word embeddings during training, the learned embeddings for vocabulary words will no longer be directly comparable to the original, pre-trained embeddings for OOV words. This is a crucial practical consideration that should be discussed.

*   **20 Newsgroups Evaluation:** The finding that 20 topics works best for the 20 Newsgroups dataset is expected given its structure. A more insightful evaluation on this dataset could have involved an information-theoretic measure (e.g., Normalized Mutual Information) comparing the discovered topic clusters against the ground-truth document labels. This would also have enabled a direct quantitative comparison with baselines like LDA.

*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper would benefit from discussing and comparing its approach to other relevant neural topic models. The current literature review is missing several key works in this area, including:
    *   Cao, Ziqiang, et al. "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension." AAAI, 2015.
    *   Nguyen, Dat Quoc, et al. "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." TACL, 2015.
    *   Shamanta, Debakar, et al. "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." ECML PKDD, 2015.
