This paper proposes a neural topic model that extends the word2vec objective to jointly learn word and document embeddings. The document embeddings are then constrained through sparsification to mimic the output of a traditional topic model.

The proposed model is conceptually interesting, and the qualitative examples presented are promising. However, a significant weakness of this paper is the complete absence of empirical evaluation. The assessment of the model's performance relies entirely on tables of examples, with no indication of how representative these examples are, nor any attempt at a direct comparison with standard or other neural topic models. Without quantitative evaluation, it is impossible to gauge the model's effectiveness, making the paper difficult to accept in its current form. The authors could have strengthened the paper considerably by including such an evaluation. For instance:

1.  **Indirect Evaluation:** Use the learned topic representation for each document as features in a supervised document classification task. The performance could then be compared against a baseline topic model (e.g., LDA) with the same number of topics.
2.  **Direct Evaluation:** Evaluate the quality of the document representations on a dataset with document similarity annotations, using pairwise comparisons of the topic vectors.

A serious procedural issue is that the authors have compromised the double-blind review process by including a link to a public GitHub repository in the submitted manuscript. While the decision rests with the program chairs, this is typically sufficient grounds for rejection.

**Specific Issues:**

*   **Example Selection:** The method for selecting the examples in Figures 3-6 is not described. Presenting a subset of topics without a clear selection protocol raises concerns about potential cherry-picking.
*   **Scalability of Topic Representations:** In Section 2.2.1, the paper discusses calculating word representations for topics based on pairwise comparison with every word in the vocabulary. The feasibility of this approach is questionable, as it would be computationally prohibitive for any reasonably sized vocabulary and number of topics.
*   **Tokenization Clarity:** Section 3.1 states that "tokens" are identified using SpaCy. The description is ambiguous. Is the process limited to extracting noun chunks, as in Section 3.2, or does it involve another method? This lack of clarity is compounded by the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which typically include very few multi-word expressions.
*   **Handling of OOV Terms:** The paper does not address how the model would handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms when applied to new, unseen documents. Since the word embeddings are fine-tuned during training on a specific collection, the resulting vectors are not directly comparable to the original, pre-trained embeddings of OOV words that have not been updated.
*   **Evaluation on 20 Newsgroups:** The finding that 20 topics works best for the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising given the dataset's structure. This observation presented an opportunity for a simple quantitative evaluation. The authors could have performed an information-theoretic comparison (e.g., using Normalized Mutual Information) between the topic assignments and the ground-truth document labels, allowing for a direct comparison with models like LDA.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to compare its approach with several relevant neural topic models, including:
    *   Cao et al., "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension." (AAAI 2015)
    *   Nguyen et al., "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." (TACL 2015)
    *   Shamanta et al., "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." (ECML PKDD 2015)

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 315: "it's similarity" should be "its similarity".
*   Line 361: The phrase "the topic basis are affected" is awkward. Please clarify what is meant by the "topic basis" and rephrase for clarity.
*   Figure 5 Caption: The examples listed are multi-word expressions; "terms" would be a more accurate descriptor than "words".
*   Reference Formatting: The formatting of the bibliography is inconsistent and contains errors (e.g., inconsistent journal/conference name capitalization, missing conference name for Roder et al.).
