This paper proposes a model that combines word2vec and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to generate topic-aware word embeddings. While the core idea of integrating distributional semantics with topic modeling is promising, the paper is hindered by severe clarity issues in its technical presentation and a lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation. These issues make it impossible to properly assess the model's contributions in its current form.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Lack of Technical Clarity:** The primary weakness of this paper is the incomprehensibility of the model's description. Section 2, which should serve to explain the model's technical details, requires a complete rewrite. A reader with a solid background in both word2vec and LDA should be able to grasp the high-level intuition and key mechanics of how the models are combined, but the current presentation fails to achieve this. The authors must clearly define their terms, motivate their architectural choices, and provide a step-by-step derivation that is legible to the target audience.

2.  **Absence of Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper lacks any standard quantitative evaluation. The results are limited to qualitative examples of word clusters and a few cherry-picked word analogies. In the modern NLP landscape, this is insufficient. To demonstrate the value of the proposed model, it must be benchmarked against established baselines on well-defined intrinsic tasks (e.g., word similarity datasets like WordSim-353, analogy tasks like the Google analogy dataset) and/or extrinsic downstream tasks (e.g., text classification, document clustering).

3.  **Unexplained Results and Overstated Claims:** Figure 2 appears to present some form of quantitative analysis, but it is never discussed or explained in the main body of the paper. Relying solely on an overly long figure caption to describe what might be the paper's only quantitative result is not acceptable. Furthermore, the claim in the conclusion that the model "solves word analogies" is a significant overstatement. Showing a few classic, successful examples does not constitute a systematic evaluation or a solution to the general problem.

**Questions and Points for Clarification**

*   **Introduction:** The third paragraph of the introduction is confusing. Could the authors please clarify what is being approximated when they mention "deriving a new approximation"? Additionally, the motivations behind the statements that developing prototypes is "time-consuming" and that it is "easier to evaluate features" are not clear in this context.

*   **Model Design:** The paper deviates from the standard word2vec formulation by using the same word vectors for both pivot and target words. What is the technical motivation for this design choice?

*   **Terminology:** Several key terms are used without adequate explanation. Could the authors please provide clear definitions for the following:
    *   What is meant by "separating words from a marginal distribution"?
    *   What is "co-adaptation" in the context of this model?
    *   When the text states, "If we only included structure up to this point...," what specific "structure" is being referred to?

**Minor Issues**

*   **Anonymity:** Footnote 1 appears to violate the double-blind review process by revealing potentially identifying information. This should be removed.

*   **Typos:** There is a grammatical error in the phrase "it's similarity," which should be "its similarity."

*   **Citations:** The reference for Chang et al. is incomplete. The venue is listed as "Advances in ...", which is ambiguous. Please provide the full conference or journal name.
