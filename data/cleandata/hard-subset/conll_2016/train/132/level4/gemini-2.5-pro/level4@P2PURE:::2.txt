This paper proposes a neural topic model that extends the word2vec objective to jointly learn document embeddings. These embeddings are then constrained via sparsification to produce topic-like representations.

The proposed model is conceptually interesting, and the qualitative examples presented are promising. However, the paper's primary weakness is a complete lack of empirical evaluation. The assessment of the model relies entirely on qualitative examples, with no quantitative evidence of their representativeness or a direct comparison against standard or other neural topic models. In the absence of such an evaluation, it is difficult to assess the model's effectiveness, which presents a significant barrier to acceptance.

A quantitative evaluation could have been performed in several ways. For instance, the learned document representations could be used as features in a supervised document classification task and compared against baselines like LDA with the same number of topics. Alternatively, the quality of the document embeddings could be evaluated directly on a document similarity task using a dataset with human annotations.

While the intention to release code is commendable, the inclusion of a link to a public GitHub repository in the submitted manuscript compromises the anonymous review process. This is a serious policy violation that could be grounds for rejection, though the final decision rests with the Program Chairs.

Other issues:
- The paper should clarify the selection process for the example topics shown in Figures 3-6. Without this information, the presented results raise concerns about potential cherry-picking.
- Section 2.2.1 describes computing topic representations via pairwise comparison with every word in the vocabulary. The feasibility of this approach is questionable, as it appears computationally prohibitive for standard vocabulary and topic sizes. The authors should comment on the scalability of this method.
- Section 3.1 states that "tokens" are identified using SpaCy, but the process is unclear. It is not specified whether this is limited to noun chunks (as in Section 3.2) or involves another method. This ambiguity is compounded by the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which typically contain few multi-word expressions, making the preprocessing pipeline difficult to understand.
- The paper does not address how the model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms when applied to new documents. Since the model fine-tunes word embeddings during training, the resulting vector space may not be comparable with the original pre-trained embeddings for OOV words. The authors should discuss a strategy for inferring topics for documents containing unseen words.
- The finding that 20 topics is optimal for the 20 Newsgroups dataset is unsurprising. This setup could have been leveraged for a simple quantitative evaluation. For example, an information-theoretic measure comparing the inferred topic distributions to the ground-truth document labels would have enabled a direct comparison with models like LDA.
- The related work section should be expanded to include and discuss other relevant neural topic models, such as:
Cao, Ziqiang, Sujian Li, Yang Liu, Wenjie Li, and Heng Ji. "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension." In AAAI, pp. 2210-2216. 2015.
Nguyen, Dat Quoc, Richard Billingsley, Lan Du, and Mark Johnson. "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics 3 (2015): 299-313.
Shamanta, Debakar, Sheikh Motahar Naim, Parang Saraf, Naren Ramakrishnan, and M. Shahriar Hossain. "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." In Machine Learning and Knowledge Discovery in Databases, pp. 441-457. Springer International Publishing, 2015.
