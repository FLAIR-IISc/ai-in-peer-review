This paper proposes a model that combines word2vec-style distributional embeddings with Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to create topic-aware word representations. The core idea of integrating local context-based embeddings with global document-level topic models is promising and potentially interesting. Such a model could capture nuances of word meaning that are missed by either approach in isolation.

However, the paper in its current form suffers from two major problems: a lack of technical clarity and an almost complete absence of empirical evaluation.

The primary weakness is that the technical description is exceptionally difficult to comprehend. Section 2, which contains the core contribution, needs a complete rewrite. For a paper combining two well-known models, a reader familiar with both word2vec and LDA should be able to develop a high-level understanding of the proposed integration with relative ease. The current presentation fails to achieve this. The exposition is opaque, leaving the reader with many unanswered questions about the model's architecture and the motivations behind key design choices.

A second critical flaw is the lack of quantitative evaluation. The standards for empirical validation in NLP are well-established, and presenting a few qualitative examples of word clusters is no longer sufficient. The authors should evaluate their model on standard intrinsic tasks (e.g., word similarity, word analogy benchmarks) and/or extrinsic downstream tasks (e.g., text classification, sentiment analysis) to demonstrate its effectiveness. This would allow for a direct comparison against baseline models like standard word2vec and LDA-based features, substantiating the benefits of the proposed combination. While Figure 2 appears to show a quantitative result, it is not described in the main body of the paper and is instead relegated to an overly long caption, making it difficult to interpret or assess its significance.

More detailed comments and questions for the authors:

*   The third paragraph of the introduction is confusing. It states the model "requires deriving a new approximation," but it is unclear what is being approximated. The text also claims it is "time-consuming to develop prototypes" and "easier to evaluate features," but the reasoning behind these assertions is not provided.
*   What is the motivation for using the same word vectors for both pivot and target words? This is a departure from the standard word2vec formulation, and the rationale for this design choice should be explained.
*   The paper uses phrases like "separate words from a marginal distribution" and "co-adaptation" without clear definitions. These concepts need to be formally defined or explained in the context of the model.
*   In the sentence, "If we only included structure up to this point," what specific "structure" is being referred to? This needs clarification.
*   The claim in the conclusion that the model "solves word analogies" is a significant overstatement. The evidence provided consists of a few cherry-picked examples, which is not a substitute for a systematic evaluation on a standard analogy dataset.
*   Footnote 1 appears to compromise the anonymity of the review process and should be removed or rephrased.
*   There is a minor grammatical error: "it's similarity" should be "its similarity."
*   The reference for Chang et al. is incomplete, with the venue listed only as "Advances in ...". Please provide the full and correct citation.

In summary, while the underlying idea has merit, the paper is not ready for publication due to the incomprehensible technical description and the absence of a proper evaluation. A major revision is required to address these fundamental issues.
