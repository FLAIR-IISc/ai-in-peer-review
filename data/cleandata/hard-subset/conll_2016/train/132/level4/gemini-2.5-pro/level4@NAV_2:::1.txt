### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that jointly learns dense word vectors and Dirichlet-distributed latent topic mixtures for documents. The proposed method integrates the skip-gram negative sampling objective from word2vec with a document representation constructed as a sparse combination of topic vectors. This allows word, document, and topic vectors to be embedded in the same continuous vector space. The goal is to combine the semantic richness of word embeddings with the interpretability of traditional topic models.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel model architecture that unifies dense word embeddings and sparse topic modeling within a single, jointly trained framework. The approach of modifying the word2vec context vector to include a document-specific topic mixture is an interesting and promising concept.
2.  The demonstration that such a hybrid model can be implemented within modern automatic differentiation frameworks, which simplifies the development process compared to deriving custom inference algorithms required for many probabilistic models.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of the Hybrid Approach:** The core idea of integrating word embeddings and topic models into a single representation space is compelling. A successful model of this type could provide significant advantages, offering both the powerful semantic regularities captured by dense word vectors and the sparse, interpretable document-level representations characteristic of topic models. This has the potential to be a valuable contribution to unsupervised text representation.
2.  **Qualitative Results:** The qualitative examples presented from the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News experiments are promising. The topics discovered appear coherent and sensible (Figures 3 & 4), and the model learns plausible word similarities and analogies specific to the training corpus (Figures 5 & 6). This suggests the model is capable of capturing meaningful structure in text data.
3.  **Practical Implementation:** The paper highlights the practicality of implementing complex probabilistic models using automatic differentiation tools. By avoiding the need to derive bespoke inference procedures (e.g., variational inference or Gibbs sampling), this approach lowers the barrier to entry for developing and experimenting with novel hybrid models, which is a valuable practical contribution for the research community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Technical Clarity:** The description of the model in Section 2 is very difficult to comprehend, even for a reader with a strong background in both word2vec and LDA. The paper lacks clear motivation for key design choices and fails to define critical terms. For example, the rationale for using the same vector for both pivot and target words (a departure from the original word2vec) is not provided. Terms like "co-adaptation" are used without explanation, and phrases such as "separating words from the marginal distribution" are vague. The model description requires a substantial revision to make the work understandable and reproducible.
2.  **Insufficient Evaluation and Overstated Claims:** The experimental evaluation is inadequate for a modern NLP paper. The majority of the results are qualitative and consist of cherry-picked examples of topics and word analogies. While illustrative, this is not a substitute for rigorous, quantitative comparison against established baselines (e.g., standard LDA for topic quality, or other document embedding methods). The only quantitative metric provided (topic coherence in Figure 2) is presented without context or comparison and is only explained in a caption, not analyzed in the main text. Consequently, the claim in the conclusion that the model "solves word analogies" is a significant overstatement based on the limited evidence provided.
3.  **Breach of Anonymity:** Footnote 1 contains a link to a public GitHub repository, which clearly identifies the author(s) and violates the conference's double-blind review policy. This is a major procedural issue that undermines the integrity of the review process.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the motivation behind using a single embedding matrix for both pivot and target words, unlike the standard word2vec implementation? What are the theoretical or empirical benefits of this choice in your model?
2.  The introduction (third paragraph) argues that your approach avoids the need for "deriving a new approximation." Could you elaborate on what specific approximation methods (e.g., variational inference, collapsed Gibbs sampling) in traditional topic modeling you are referring to and how your optimization scheme based on automatic differentiation relates to or improves upon them?
3.  How do the topic coherence scores reported in Figure 2 compare to those achieved by a standard LDA implementation on the same preprocessed Twenty Newsgroups dataset? Without a baseline, it is difficult to assess the quality of the topics produced by lda2vec.
