### Summary

This paper introduces a neural topic model that extends the word2vec objective to jointly learn embeddings for both words and documents. The model then enforces sparsity on the document embeddings to produce representations that resemble the output of traditional topic models. The core idea is interesting, and the qualitative examples provided are promising.

However, the paper's primary weakness, and a critical one, is the complete absence of empirical evaluation. The assessment of the model relies exclusively on a few selected examples, which provides no measure of how representative these results are or how the model performs in a quantitative sense. Without a systematic evaluation and comparison against established baselines, it is impossible to gauge the model's contributions and effectiveness. This makes the paper difficult to accept in its current form.

### Major Weaknesses: Lack of Empirical Evaluation

The main shortcoming of this work is the lack of any quantitative or comparative evaluation. To properly situate the model's performance within the field, the authors should have compared it against standard topic models (e.g., LDA) and other neural topic models. Here are two potential evaluation strategies that could have been employed:

1.  **Extrinsic Evaluation:** The quality of the learned document representations (topic distributions) could be assessed via a downstream task. For instance, the document vectors could be used as features in a supervised document classification setting. The performance on this task would serve as an indirect measure of the representation quality, allowing for a direct comparison with other models that produce document-level representations with the same dimensionality (i.e., number of topics).

2.  **Intrinsic Evaluation:** The model could be evaluated directly on a dataset with document similarity annotations. By computing the pairwise similarity between the model's topic vectors for different documents, the results could be compared against the ground-truth similarity judgments.

### Procedural Issue: Anonymity Violation

A significant procedural issue is the violation of the double-blind review process. The inclusion of a GitHub link in the submission compromises the authors' anonymity. While releasing code is commendable, this must be done in a way that preserves anonymity (e.g., via an anonymized link or by promising to release code upon acceptance). This issue alone is often sufficient grounds for rejection, a decision I will leave to the discretion of the Program Chairs.

### Additional Questions and Points for Clarification

*   **Example Selection:** The method for selecting the examples in Figures 3-6 is not described. Without a clear selection protocol (e.g., random sampling of topics, or showing the best and worst performing topics), this raises concerns about potential cherry-picking.

*   **Computational Feasibility:** In Section 2.2.1, the paper discusses calculating word representations for topics via pairwise comparison with every word in the vocabulary. The computational cost of this step appears prohibitive for large vocabularies and a significant number of topics. The authors should comment on the scalability and practical feasibility of this approach.

*   **Tokenization Process:** The description of tokenization in Section 3.1 lacks clarity. The paper states that "tokens" are identified using SpaCy and then mentions extracting only noun chunks. It is unclear how this process relates to the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings, which primarily operate on single words. Further details are needed to understand the exact preprocessing pipeline.

*   **Handling of OOV Terms:** The paper does not address how the model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms when applied to new, unseen documents. Since the model fine-tunes word embeddings during training, the pre-trained embeddings for OOV words in new documents would not be directly comparable to the updated embeddings of known vocabulary words. This is a crucial aspect for the model's practical application and requires discussion.

*   **Evaluation on 20 Newsgroups:** The finding that 20 topics perform well on the 20 Newsgroups dataset is expected given the dataset's structure. A more insightful evaluation could have involved an information-theoretic comparison (e.g., using Normalized Mutual Information) between the discovered topic clusters and the ground-truth document labels. This would have enabled a direct, quantitative comparison with baseline models.

*   **Missing Comparisons to Related Work:** The related work section should be strengthened by discussing and comparing the proposed model to other recent neural topic models that also integrate distributed word representations. Relevant works include:
    *   Cao et al. "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension." (AAAI, 2015).
    *   Nguyen et al. "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations." (TACL, 2015).
    *   Shamanta et al. "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations." (ECML PKDD, 2015).
