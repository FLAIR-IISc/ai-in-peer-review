**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces lda2vec, a novel hybrid model that extends the word2vec skip-gram architecture to jointly learn word embeddings, topic vectors, and document-level topic distributions. The model constructs a context vector for the skip-gram objective by summing a pivot word's vector with a document vector. This document vector is, in turn, a weighted combination of topic vectors. By adding a Dirichlet-based likelihood term to the loss function, the model encourages the document-level topic weights to be sparse, producing interpretable document representations akin to those from traditional Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA).

The primary contribution of this work is the model itself: an elegant and unified framework that embeds words, documents, and topics into a shared vector space. This approach combines the semantic regularities captured by word embeddings with the interpretability of sparse topic models. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of the model on both a standard benchmark (20 Newsgroups) and a large, specialized corpus (Hacker News), showing promising qualitative results.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Intuitive Model:** The proposed lda2vec model is an intuitive and well-motivated synthesis of two highly successful paradigms in NLP: dense word embeddings (word2vec) and latent topic modeling (LDA). By defining the skip-gram context as a sum of word and document vectors, and representing the document vector as a mixture of topics, the model creates a direct and powerful link between these two representation types. The formulation is clean and allows for end-to-end training within modern automatic differentiation frameworks.

2.  **Promising Qualitative Results:** The qualitative examples provided in the paper are compelling and demonstrate the model's potential. The topics discovered in both the 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets appear coherent and interpretable (Figures 3 and 4). Furthermore, the model successfully learns domain-specific word similarities and linear analogies on the Hacker News corpus (Figures 5 and 6), suggesting that the word embedding component remains effective within this joint framework.

3.  **Open Source Implementation:** The authors' decision to release their code is commendable and a significant asset to the research community. An open-source implementation facilitates reproducibility and encourages further exploration and extension of the proposed model. (Note: Please see the point regarding anonymity in the Weaknesses section).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Quantitative Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's most significant weakness is the complete lack of quantitative empirical evaluation. The assessment of the model's performance relies exclusively on curated tables of topics and word analogies. While these examples are illustrative, they provide no objective measure of the model's quality, nor do they allow for a direct comparison with standard baselines. Without empirical validation, it is impossible to gauge the model's true effectiveness relative to existing methods. This is a critical omission. A proper evaluation could have been conducted in several ways, for instance:
    *   **Extrinsic Evaluation:** Using the learned document-topic representations as features in a downstream supervised task, such as document classification. The model's performance could then be directly compared against features generated by standard LDA and other neural topic models with the same number of topics.
    *   **Intrinsic Evaluation:** Evaluating the quality of the document representations directly on a dataset with document similarity annotations, for example by comparing the cosine similarity of the model's document vectors against human judgments.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper fails to position its contribution within the growing body of literature on neural topic models that also seek to combine the strengths of topic models and distributed representations. The discussion of related work is limited, and the experiments do not include any comparison to these highly relevant models. The authors should compare and contrast their approach with recent works such as:
    *   Cao et al. (AAAI 2015), "A Novel Neural Topic Model and Its Supervised Extension."
    *   Nguyen et al. (TACL 2015), "Improving Topic Models with Latent Feature Word Representations."
    *   Shamanta et al. (ECML PKDD 2015), "Concurrent Inference of Topic Models and Distributed Vector Representations."

3.  **Potential for "Cherry-Picking" in Evaluation:** Related to the first weakness, the exclusive reliance on a small set of hand-picked examples raises concerns about cherry-picking. The authors provide no information on how the examples in Figures 3-6 were selected, nor any indication of how representative they are of the model's overall output. A more systematic presentation, or at least an acknowledgment of the selection process, is needed to build confidence in the results.

4.  **Major Procedural Issue: Anonymity Breach:** The submission is not anonymous. The inclusion of a public GitHub link in the footnote on page 4, which identifies the author, is a clear violation of the double-blind reviewing policy. This is a serious procedural flaw and is typically grounds for rejection. I leave the final decision to the Area Chairs, but this must be addressed.

**Questions for Authors**

1.  **Handling of Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Terms:** How does the proposed model handle OOV terms when applied to new, unseen documents? Since the model fine-tunes the word embeddings during training, the learned vector space will have shifted. This means that pre-trained embeddings for OOV words would not be directly comparable or usable within the trained model. Does this limit the model's application to transductive settings only?

2.  **Clarity on Preprocessing:** Could you clarify the tokenization process used for the 20 Newsgroups dataset in Section 3.1? The paper states that tokens were identified using SpaCy but also that pre-trained word2vec embeddings were used. Given that these pre-trained embeddings contain few multi-word expressions, it is unclear what "tokens" (e.g., single words, noun chunks) were used and how they were mapped to the embeddings.

3.  **Computational Feasibility:** In Section 2.2.1, you describe identifying the top words for a topic by computing its similarity to every word in the vocabulary. For a large vocabulary (e.g., 110k in the Hacker News experiment), this seems computationally expensive, especially if performed frequently. Is this calculation feasible in practice, or is there a more efficient approximation used?

4.  **Evaluation of 20 Newsgroups:** The finding that a model with 20 topics performs best on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is not surprising. Could this observation be leveraged for a simple quantitative evaluation? For example, by measuring the correspondence between the discovered topics and the ground-truth newsgroup labels (e.g., using purity or normalized mutual information) and comparing this against LDA.
