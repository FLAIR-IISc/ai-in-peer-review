

This paper proposes a neural-style topic model that extends the word2vec objective to learn document embeddings, which are then constrained via sparsification to mimic traditional topic model outputs. While the model design is conceptually appealing and the presented examples demonstrate promising qualitative results, the paper suffers from a critical deficiency: it lacks any empirical evaluation. The evaluation relies solely on illustrative examples without demonstrating their representativeness or providing direct comparisons against standard topic models (e.g., LDA) or neural topic models. Without such evaluation, the model’s true efficacy cannot be assessed, rendering the claims unsubstantiated and the paper difficult to accept.  

To address this gap, the authors could have:  
(1) Evaluated the topic representations in a supervised document categorization task using the same topic cardinality as a baseline model, or  
(2) Performed direct evaluation on a dataset with ground-truth document similarity annotations (e.g., via pairwise topic vector comparisons).  

The authors’ decision to release code is commendable, but the inclusion of a GitHub link in the anonymized submission violates conference policy. This constitutes a procedural violation that may necessitate rejection, though the final decision rests with the PCs.  

Additional concerns:  
- **Example selection (Figs. 3–6)**: The rationale for selecting specific examples (e.g., subsets of topics) is unclear and risks cherry-picking.  
- **Topic word representations (Sec. 2.2.1)**: The proposed method for computing topic word representations via pairwise vocabulary comparisons is computationally infeasible for large vocabularies and topic sets (e.g., O(|V|×|K|) operations).  
- **Tokenization (Sec. 3.1)**: The use of SpaCy to "identify tokens" is ambiguous. The paper states noun chunks are extracted (similar to Sec. 3.2), but given reliance on pre-trained word2vec embeddings (which lack multiword terms), it is unclear how tokenization aligns with the model’s requirements.  
- **OOV handling**: The model’s approach to out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms is unaddressed. While the training corpus covers all words in the experiments, the model’s applicability to novel documents would require updating word2vec embeddings, rendering OOV terms’ embeddings incomparable to tuned representations.  
- **Topic cardinality (20 Newsgroups)**: The finding that 20 topics optimizes performance is unsurprising given the dataset’s structure. A simple information-theoretic comparison against true labels (e.g., mutual information with document categories) could have provided a more rigorous validation against LDA.  
- **Comparative baseline omission**: The paper fails to compare against key neural topic models, including:  
  Cao et al. (AAAI 2015), Nguyen et al. (TACL 2015), and Shamanta et al. (ECML/PKDD 2015).  

Low-level issues:  
- Line 315: "it’s similarity" → "its similarity"  
- Line 361: Awkward phrasing ("topic basis are affected")  
- Fig. 5 caption: "words" → "terms" (to align with tokenization discussion)  
- Reference formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Advances in..." vs. "Advances in Neural..."; missing conference names for Roder et al.)
