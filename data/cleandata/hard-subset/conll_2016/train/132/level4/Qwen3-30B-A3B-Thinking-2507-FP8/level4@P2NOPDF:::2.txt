

This paper introduces a neural topic model that extends word2vec's objective to learn document embeddings, constrained via sparsification to mimic traditional topic model outputs. The proposed approach is conceptually interesting, and the illustrative examples demonstrate promising potential. However, the absence of rigorous empirical evaluation significantly undermines the paper's contribution, making it difficult to assess the model's true effectiveness.

The core weakness lies in the lack of quantitative evaluation. The paper relies solely on qualitative examples (Figures 3-6) without providing: (1) statistical evidence of representativeness (e.g., how many examples were selected, whether they reflect typical topics), or (2) direct comparisons against standard (e.g., LDA) or neural topic models (e.g., Cao et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2015; Shamanta et al., 2015). Without such evaluation, the claimed advantages remain unsubstantiated. To address this, the authors could have: (a) evaluated the document representations in a supervised categorization task (e.g., using the same topic cardinality as LDA), or (b) leveraged datasets with explicit document similarity annotations to compare topic vector distances directly against baselines. Even a simple information-theoretic comparison against true labels (e.g., mutual information with 20 Newsgroups categories) would have strengthened the claims.

Additional methodological concerns require clarification:  
- **Example selection (Figures 3-6)**: The criteria for selecting example topics/words are unspecified, raising concerns about potential cherry-picking.  
- **Topic word representation (Section 2.2.1)**: The proposal to compute topic embeddings via pairwise vocabulary comparisons is computationally prohibitive for standard vocabularies (e.g., 100k+ words × 100 topics). The feasibility of this approach needs justification.  
- **Spacy tokenization (Section 3.1)**: The paper states "tokens" are identified using SpaCy but does not clarify the method (e.g., noun chunks only, as in Section 3.2?). Given the reliance on pre-trained word2vec embeddings (which cover few multiword terms), the tokenization process requires explicit description.  
- **OOV handling**: The model’s treatment of out-of-vocabulary terms is unclear. While experiments use full-document training, real-world application to novel documents would require updating word embeddings. Non-updated OOV terms would then lack comparability with tuned embeddings, a critical gap for deployment.  

The paper also omits key comparisons. The authors should benchmark against the neural topic models listed above (Cao et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2015; Shamanta et al., 2015), particularly given the 20 Newsgroups result (20 topics optimal) being unsurprising due to the corpus’s label structure.  

**Anonymity violation**: The GitHub link in the submitted manuscript compromises double-blind review. While this is a formal grounds for rejection, I defer to the PCs on this matter.  

**Minor issues**:  
- Line 315: "it's similarity" → "its similarity".  
- Line 361: Awkward phrasing ("the topic basis... are affected").  
- Figure 5 caption: "words" should be "terms" (to align with multiword expressions).  
- References: Inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Advances in..." vs. "Advances in Neural..."; missing conference names like Roder et al.).  

In summary, the model’s conceptual novelty is appealing, but the lack of empirical validation prevents meaningful assessment. Addressing the evaluation gap through supervised categorization, direct similarity comparisons, or information-theoretic metrics would substantially strengthen the work. The methodological ambiguities (tokenization, OOV, computational feasibility) also require resolution. With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution to neural topic modeling. As submitted, however, the absence of rigorous evaluation limits its impact.
