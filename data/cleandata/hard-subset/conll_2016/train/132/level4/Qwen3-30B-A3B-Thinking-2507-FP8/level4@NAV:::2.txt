

This paper introduces a neural-styled topic model that extends word2vec's objective to learn document embeddings, subsequently constraining these via sparsification to emulate topic model outputs. I found the proposed model conceptually compelling and the illustrative examples highly promising. However, the paper lacks essential empirical evaluation; the presented results rely solely on example tables without demonstrating their representativeness or direct comparison against standard or neural topic models. Without such evaluation, the model's true efficacy remains unassessed, rendering acceptance difficult. To address this, the authors could: (1) employ the document topic representations in a supervised categorization task (comparing against topic models of identical cardinality as an indirect quality measure); or (2) conduct direct evaluation using datasets with annotated document similarity (e.g., via pairwise topic vector comparisons).  

While commendable for releasing code, the inclusion of the GitHub link in the anonymized submission compromises anonymity (a potential grounds for immediate rejection, though this decision rests with the PCs). Additional concerns include:  
- Ambiguity in selecting examples for Figures 3–6 (risk of cherry-picking subsets of topics).  
- Feasibility of calculating topic word representations via pairwise vocabulary comparisons (computationally prohibitive for standard vocabularies and topic counts, as noted in Section 2.2.1).  
- Unclear methodology for "token" identification in Section 3.1 (SpaCy noun chunks only? How does this align with word2vec’s limited multiword coverage?).  
- Inadequate handling of OOV terms during inference on novel documents (tuned word2vec embeddings for training documents create comparability issues for unseen terms).  
- The observation that 20 topics optimized 20 Newsgroups results (given its structure) warrants a simple information-theoretic comparison against true labels for direct LDA benchmarking.  
- Omission of key neural topic model comparisons: Cao et al. (AAAI 2015), Nguyen et al. (TACL 2015), and Shamanta et al. (ECML/PKDD 2015).
