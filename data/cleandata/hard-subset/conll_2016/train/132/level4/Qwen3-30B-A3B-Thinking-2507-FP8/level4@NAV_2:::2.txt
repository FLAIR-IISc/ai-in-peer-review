

Summary: This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that extends word2vec's skip-gram architecture to jointly learn document embeddings and topic vectors through a sparsification constraint. The primary contributions are: (1) a framework for jointly learning word vectors and document-level topic mixtures within automatic differentiation frameworks, (2) a method for generating interpretable document representations through sparsification of topic mixtures, and (3) demonstration of meaningful topic representations and semantic relationships in specialized corpora.

Strengths: 
1. The model effectively bridges neural word embeddings with interpretable topic representations, offering a promising middle ground between dense neural representations and sparse topic models while maintaining simplicity of implementation.
2. The visual examples in Figures 3-6 compellingly demonstrate the model's ability to learn meaningful topics (e.g., "Space," "Encryption," "Housing Issues") and semantic relationships (e.g., "Silicon Valley" ≈ "California" + "technology") in specialized corpora.
3. The implementation is straightforward to incorporate into existing automatic differentiation frameworks, as evidenced by the open-source code release, which significantly lowers the barrier to adoption.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks rigorous empirical evaluation, with no quantitative comparison to standard topic models (e.g., LDA) or other neural topic models on standard metrics, relying solely on illustrative examples without indicating their representativeness.
2. The submission violates anonymity guidelines by including a GitHub link to the code repository, which would require rejection under standard conference policies.
3. The methodology for token extraction using SpaCy is unclear, particularly regarding how noun phrases are handled and how this affects the vocabulary compared to standard word embeddings, which impacts reproducibility.
4. The paper does not address how the model handles out-of-vocabulary terms when applied to novel documents, a critical limitation for real-world applications.
5. The paper omits comparison with recent neural topic models (Cao et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2015; Shamanta et al., 2015), making it difficult to assess the model's relative novelty and performance.

Questions to Authors:
1. How were the example topics in Figures 3-6 selected? Please clarify if these represent a random sample or were cherry-picked to highlight the best results.
2. How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary terms when processing documents not seen during training?
3. Could you provide quantitative results comparing lda2vec with LDA on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset using standard topic coherence metrics and document classification performance?
