

This paper proposes a neural topic model that extends word2vec's objective to learn document embeddings, constrained via sparsification to mimic traditional topic model outputs. While the model architecture is well-motivated and the example outputs are promising, the absence of rigorous empirical evaluation represents the most significant flaw. The paper relies solely on illustrative examples (Figures 3–6) without demonstrating how representative these are or providing quantitative comparisons against established topic models (e.g., LDA) or contemporary neural topic models. Without such evaluation, the claimed advantages remain unverified, making it impossible to assess the model’s true efficacy or novelty.  

To address this, the authors should:  
(1) Evaluate the topic representations in a supervised document categorization task (e.g., using 20 Newsgroups), comparing against topic models with identical topic cardinality to measure representation quality indirectly;  
(2) Conduct direct evaluation on datasets with annotated document similarity (e.g., via pairwise topic vector comparisons), enabling a clear metric for model performance.  
The current lack of such analysis renders the core contribution unsubstantiated.  

**Critical Anonymity Violation**: The submission includes a GitHub link in the main text, violating CONLL’s double-blind review policy. This alone warrants rejection, though the decision rests with the Program Chairs.  

**Key Technical Concerns**:  
- **Example Selection (Figures 3–6)**: The criteria for selecting example topics are unspecified. Presenting only "ideal" cases risks cherry-picking, undermining the model’s perceived reliability. The authors must clarify selection methodology or include diverse examples.  
- **Topic Word Representation (Section 2.2.1)**: The proposal to compute topic word vectors via pairwise vocabulary comparisons is computationally prohibitive for large vocabularies (e.g., 100k+ words) and topics (e.g., 20–100). The feasibility of this approach requires justification or a practical alternative.  
- **Tokenization (Section 3.1)**: The use of SpaCy to "identify tokens" is ambiguous. The paper states noun chunks are extracted (Section 3.2), yet word2vec embeddings typically cover single words, not multiword terms. Clarify the tokenization pipeline and its alignment with pre-trained embeddings.  
- **OOV Handling**: The model trains on the full corpus, avoiding OOV issues in experiments, but this limits real-world applicability. For novel documents, OOV terms (with no pre-trained embeddings) cannot be compared to tuned embeddings. The authors must address this gap, e.g., via embedding aggregation or dynamic OOV handling.  
- **20 Newsgroups Baseline (Section 4.1)**: The finding that 20 topics maximizes performance is unsurprising given the dataset’s 20-class structure. A more meaningful evaluation would compare topic coherence or label-based information gain against LDA.  

**Missing Comparisons**: The paper should benchmark against recent neural topic models, including:  
- Cao et al. (AAAI 2015)  
- Nguyen et al. (TACL 2015)  
- Shamanta et al. (ECML-PKDD 2015)  

**Minor Issues**:  
- Line 315: "it's similarity" → "its similarity"  
- Line 361: "the topic basis are affected" → "the topic basis is affected"  
- Figure 5 caption: "words" → "terms" (to align with multiword tokenization)  
- References: Inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Advances in Neural..." vs. "Advances in..."; missing conference names for Roder et al.)  

**Conclusion**: The model’s conceptual innovation is promising, but the paper’s failure to provide empirical validation—coupled with the anonymity breach—prevents meaningful assessment. Addressing the evaluation gaps (via supervised categorization, similarity metrics, or label-based analysis) and clarifying technical ambiguities (tokenization, OOV, example selection) are essential. Without these revisions, the contribution remains speculative. I recommend rejection pending substantial revisions to include rigorous evaluation against relevant baselines.
