

The paper proposes a novel combination of word2vec and LDA, which holds potential interest for the NLP community. However, the current manuscript fails to communicate its technical contributions clearly, rendering the core innovation difficult to assess. The primary issue is a lack of sufficient detail and logical flow in Section 2, which must be substantially rewritten. A reader familiar with both word2vec and LDA should be able to grasp the high-level model architecture and integration mechanism with minimal effort; the current presentation does not achieve this. Below are specific concerns requiring resolution:

**Technical Clarity & Motivation:**  
The introduction (particularly the third paragraph) contains critical ambiguities. The phrase "requires deriving a new approximation" lacks context—approximation of what, and why is this process time-consuming? The claim that "it is easier to evaluate features" is unsubstantiated and unclear. The decision to use identical word vectors for pivot and target terms (contrary to standard word2vec practice) lacks justification; the motivation for this design choice must be explicitly stated and defended. Similarly, the phrase "separate words from a marginal distribution" is undefined and confusing. The term "co-adaptation" is used without explanation, leaving readers unable to assess its role. The ambiguous reference to "structure up to this point" in Section 2 further obscures the methodology. These omissions prevent a proper evaluation of the approach’s novelty and feasibility.

**Evaluation Deficiencies:**  
The paper provides no rigorous empirical evaluation, which is unacceptable by current NLP standards. Merely presenting example clusters (as in Figure 2) is insufficient; the field now demands quantitative metrics on established benchmarks. Figure 2’s caption describes a quantitative comparison, but the actual results, metrics, and statistical significance are buried in the caption rather than presented in the main text. This violates the principle of transparent reporting. The conclusion’s claim that "the model solves word analogies" is severely overstated. The paper only demonstrates a few cherry-picked examples (e.g., "king + queen"), which does not constitute evidence of generalizable performance. Such claims require systematic evaluation on standard datasets like Google Analogies or SemEval.

**Minor but Critical Issues:**  
- "it’s similarity" should be corrected to "its similarity."  
- Footnote 1 violates anonymity by referencing an author’s personal work.  
- The Chang reference omits the venue name ("Advances in..." is incomplete; the venue must be specified as *Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems* or similar).  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper’s core idea is potentially valuable, but the current submission is fundamentally flawed due to inadequate technical exposition and the absence of proper evaluation. The authors must:  
1. Rewrite Section 2 to clarify the model architecture, design choices (e.g., identical pivot/target vectors), and the role of "co-adaptation" and marginal distributions.  
2. Provide a complete empirical evaluation on standard NLP tasks (e.g., word similarity, analogy solving), including metrics, baselines, and statistical significance.  
3. Remove all anonymous-breaking content and correct all grammatical and reference errors.  

Without these revisions, the paper cannot be accepted. The current version fails to demonstrate either the technical soundness of the proposed method or its empirical advantages over existing approaches. The field has moved beyond qualitative examples and unsubstantiated claims; this work must meet contemporary evaluation standards to contribute meaningfully. I recommend rejection in its current form.
