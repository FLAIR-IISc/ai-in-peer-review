

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that combines Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings to produce sparse, interpretable document representations. The authors claim two primary contributions: (1) a novel architecture that jointly learns word vectors, topic vectors, and document-topic mixtures within a unified framework, and (2) a method that produces human-interpretable document representations through sparse topic mixtures constrained by a Dirichlet likelihood.

Strengths:
1. The model's conceptual integration of word embeddings with topic modeling is elegant and well-motivated. The authors successfully demonstrate how the additive property of word vectors can be leveraged to construct meaningful document representations that incorporate both local word relationships and global document themes. This approach bridges a significant gap between dense neural representations and sparse topic models.

2. The implementation is straightforward to integrate into modern automatic differentiation frameworks, which significantly lowers the barrier to experimentation with hybrid models. The authors' open-source release and clear documentation are commendable and will facilitate reproducibility and further research.

3. The qualitative results presented in Figures 3-6 are compelling, showing that the model can discover meaningful topics and semantic relationships specific to both general and domain-specific corpora. The ability to learn linear relationships between tokens (Figure 6) demonstrates that the model preserves semantic regularities from word embeddings while adding interpretability.

Weaknesses:
1. The most significant deficiency is the complete absence of empirical evaluation. The paper relies entirely on qualitative examples without any statistical comparison to standard topic models or other neural topic models. Without quantitative metrics (e.g., topic coherence scores, classification accuracy when using the topic representations), it's impossible to assess the model's actual performance or superiority over existing approaches. The authors should compare their topic coherence scores with those from LDA and other neural topic models using standard evaluation metrics.

2. The paper compromises author anonymity by including a GitHub link in the submission, which violates conference guidelines. While the authors have released code, this should be removed from the submission version to maintain anonymity during review.

3. The selection criteria for the example topics in Figures 3-6 are not clearly explained, raising concerns about potential cherry-picking. The authors should specify how these particular topics were chosen and whether they represent typical or exceptional cases.

4. The paper does not address how the model handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, which is critical for real-world applications. When applying the trained model to novel documents containing words not seen during training, the word embeddings would need to be updated, but OOV terms would lack comparable embeddings, potentially undermining the model's utility.

5. The authors fail to compare their approach with several relevant neural topic models that have appeared in the literature since 2015, including those by Cao et al., Nguyen et al., and Shamanta et al. A direct comparison with these models would strengthen the paper's contribution claim.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide quantitative topic coherence scores for lda2vec compared to LDA and other neural topic models on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset? How do these scores correlate with human evaluations?

2. Please clarify your preprocessing methodology for the Hacker News corpus. Specifically, how did you use SpaCy to extract tokens, and why did you choose this particular tokenization approach?

3. How do you plan to handle out-of-vocabulary terms when applying the trained model to new documents? What would be the impact on the quality of the document representations?

4. The paper claims that 20 topics worked best for Twenty Newsgroups, which matches the number of newsgroups. Could you provide an information-theoretic comparison (e.g., mutual information with true labels) to demonstrate that this isn't simply an artifact of the dataset's structure?

5. In Section 2.2.1, you mention calculating topic word representations through pairwise comparison with all vocabulary words. Given the computational complexity (O(V×K) for vocabulary size V and topics K), how feasible is this approach for large-scale applications?
