

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes lda2vec, a model that integrates Dirichlet-distributed topic mixtures with word embeddings to produce sparse, interpretable document representations. The primary contribution is the novel combination of Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) with LDA-style topic modeling, enabling joint learning of word vectors, topic vectors, and document-topic proportions. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of how this approach yields meaningful topic coherence and semantic relationships in specialized corpora. The third contribution is the practical implementation within automatic differentiation frameworks, simplifying model development.

**Strengths**  
The paper's core idea—merging word embeddings with interpretable topic models—is conceptually appealing and addresses a genuine gap between dense neural representations and human-readable topic structures. The model's ability to produce coherent topics in the Twenty Newsgroups dataset (with topic coherence scores of 0.567 at 20 topics) aligns with established metrics that correlate with human evaluations. The implementation's simplicity within automatic differentiation frameworks is a practical strength, as it lowers the barrier for adopting hybrid approaches.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Technical incomprehensibility**: Section 2 fails to provide a clear, high-level explanation of how the model combines word2vec and LDA. A reader familiar with both frameworks cannot easily grasp the architecture or the rationale behind key design choices (e.g., using identical word vectors for pivot and target tokens). The description lacks sufficient mathematical clarity and intuitive scaffolding.

2. **Insufficient evaluation**: The paper relies on qualitative examples (Figures 3-5) rather than rigorous quantitative validation. Figure 2's topic coherence scores are buried in a caption without contextual analysis of statistical significance, baselines, or comparison to state-of-the-art topic models. In contemporary NLP, such minimal evaluation is inadequate for claiming meaningful contributions.

3. **Misleading claims**: The conclusion overstates results by claiming the model "solves word analogies" (Figure 6), when the examples are cherry-picked and lack systematic evaluation. The paper demonstrates isolated analogies (e.g., "king - man + woman = queen"), but fails to show the model's general ability to handle diverse analogical relationships.

4. **Unclear technical justifications**: Key decisions lack motivation. For instance, the rationale for using the same word vectors for pivot and target tokens (unlike standard word2vec) is unexplained. Similarly, the phrase "separate observed words from the marginal distribution" (Section 2.1) is undefined, and the explanation of "co-adaptation" (Section 2.2) is absent.

5. **Critical omissions**: The paper incorrectly references Chang et al. (2009) as "Advances in..." without specifying the venue (it's *Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems*). Footnote 1 violates anonymity by disclosing the code repository URL. The claim that "document proportions are sparse" (Section 2.2.2) is contradicted by the Dirichlet likelihood setup (α = n⁻¹), which produces sparse *only* with specific hyperparameters, but the paper fails to clarify this dependency.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Why are identical word vectors used for both pivot and target tokens, unlike standard word2vec? How does this choice impact the model's learning dynamics?  
2. In Section 2.1, the phrase "completely separated from the marginal distribution" is unclear. Please clarify the statistical interpretation.  
3. How does the model's performance vary when α = 1 (uniform topic proportions) versus α = n⁻¹ (sparse proportions)? Provide quantitative evidence for the claimed interpretability benefits.  
4. The claim that lda2vec "solves word analogies" (Section 3.3) is overstated. Please provide a systematic evaluation of analogy accuracy across a standard benchmark (e.g., Google Analogies), not just illustrative examples.  
5. The reference for Chang et al. (2009) is incomplete. Please provide the full venue name.  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper's title suggests a "mixing" of Dirichlet topic models and word embeddings, but the technical execution fails to clarify this integration. The phrase "it's similarity" (Section 2.2.1) should read "its similarity." The Hacker News corpus analysis lacks topic coherence scores due to vocabulary mismatch with Palmetto, but this limitation should be explicitly acknowledged rather than treated as an oversight. The absence of baselines against established topic models (e.g., LDA, LDA2Vec) further undermines the evaluation.
