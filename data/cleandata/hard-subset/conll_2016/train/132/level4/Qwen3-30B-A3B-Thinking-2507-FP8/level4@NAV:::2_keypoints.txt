- Proposes a neural-styled topic model that learns document embeddings via a word2vec-like objective and sparsifies them to produce topic-like outputs.  
- Conceptually compelling and illustrative examples are promising, but the paper lacks essential empirical evaluation and representative quantitative results.  
- No direct comparisons to standard or neural topic models, so model efficacy is unassessed and acceptance is difficult without such evaluation.  
- Authors should evaluate document topic representations in supervised classification (compare against topic models with same topic counts) or use datasets with annotated document similarity for direct evaluation.  
- Including the GitHub link in the anonymized submission breaks anonymity and could justify desk rejection.  
- Example selection for Figures 3–6 is ambiguous and risks cherry-picking; representativeness must be demonstrated.  
- Computing topic word representations via pairwise vocabulary comparisons appears computationally prohibitive for realistic vocabularies and topic counts (Section 2.2.1 concern).  
- Method for identifying “tokens” in Section 3.1 is unclear (e.g., SpaCy noun chunks?), and its interaction with word2vec’s limited multiword coverage is unspecified.  
- Inference on novel documents lacks proper OOV handling—tuned word2vec embeddings on training data create comparability issues for unseen terms.  
- The 20-topic result on 20 Newsgroups may reflect dataset structure; authors should include an information-theoretic comparison to true labels for direct LDA benchmarking.  
- Paper omits comparisons to key neural topic models (Cao et al., AAAI 2015; Nguyen et al., TACL 2015; Shamanta et al., ECML/PKDD 2015).  
- Low-level corrections: Line 315 "it's similarity" → "its similarity"; Line 361 wording on "the topic basis are affected" needs rephrasing; Figure 5 caption "words" → "terms"; fix reference formatting and missing conference names.
