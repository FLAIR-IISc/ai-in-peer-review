

This paper introduces a neural topic model that extends the word2vec objective to learn document embeddings, which are then constrained via sparsification to mimic traditional topic model outputs. The proposed model is compelling, and the illustrative examples demonstrate significant promise. However, the paper lacks empirical evaluation beyond presenting examples; it provides no assessment of example representativeness or direct comparisons with standard or neural topic models. Without such evaluation, the model's true efficacy cannot be established, rendering the paper difficult to accept. To address this, the authors could have: (1) employed the document topic representations in a supervised categorization task to compare against a topic model with identical topic cardinality (as an indirect quality assessment), or (2) performed direct evaluation using a dataset with document similarity annotations (e.g., via pairwise topic vector comparisons).  

While the code release is commendable, the inclusion of the GitHub link in the submission violates anonymity (a sufficient grounds for rejection per conference policy; this decision rests with the PCs).  

Additional concerns:  
- The selection criteria for examples in Figures 3–6 are unclear. Presenting a subset of topics risks cherry-picking.  
- Section 2.2.1 proposes calculating topic word representations via pairwise vocabulary comparisons, but this approach is computationally infeasible for standard vocabularies and topic counts.  
- Section 3.1 states tokens are identified using SpaCy, but the method is unspecified (e.g., noun chunks only? Other chunk types?). Given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings (which omit multiword terms), the tokenization approach requires clarification.  
- The model's handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms is unaddressed. While experiments use full-corpus training (avoiding OOV issues), real-world deployment on novel documents would require updating word2vec embeddings, making OOV embeddings incomparable to tuned ones.  
- The finding that 20 topics optimizes performance on 20 Newsgroups is unsurprising given the corpus structure. A simple information-theoretic comparison against true document labels (enabling direct LDA comparisons) would have strengthened evaluation.  
- The authors should compare against additional neural topic models:  
  Cao et al. (AAAI 2015), Nguyen et al. (TACL 2015), and Shamanta et al. (ECML/PKDD 2015).
