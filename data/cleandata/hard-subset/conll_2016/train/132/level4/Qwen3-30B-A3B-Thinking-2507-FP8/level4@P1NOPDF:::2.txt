

This paper proposes a neural topic model that extends word2vec's objective to learn document embeddings, which it then constrains via sparsification to mimic traditional topic model outputs. The model itself is compelling, and the presented examples are promising. However, the paper lacks empirical evaluation beyond illustrative examples, with no indication of example representativeness or direct comparison to standard or neural topic models. Without such evaluation, the model's true value remains unclear, making it difficult to assess the contribution. To strengthen this, the authors could: (1) use document topic representations in a supervised categorization task (comparing against a topic model with identical topic cardinality) as an indirect quality measure; or (2) perform direct evaluation on datasets with annotated document similarity (e.g., via pairwise topic vector comparisons).

While the code release is commendable, the GitHub link in the anonymized submission compromises anonymity (a grounds for rejection, though this decision rests with the PCs).

Additional concerns:  
- Figures 3–6 present selected examples; the selection method is unspecified, raising concerns about selective presentation.  
- Section 2.2.1 proposes calculating topic word representations via pairwise vocabulary comparisons, but this approach is computationally infeasible for standard vocabularies and topic counts.  
- Section 3.1 states "tokens" are identified using SpaCy, yet the method is unclear: are noun chunks extracted (as in Section 3.2), or other chunk types? Given reliance on pre-trained word2vec (which covers few multiword terms), the tokenization approach needs clarification.  
- The model’s handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms is unaddressed. While training on the full corpus avoids immediate issues, applying the model to novel documents would require updating word2vec embeddings, rendering OOV terms' embeddings incomparable to tuned ones.  
- The observation that 20 topics performed best on 20 Newsgroups is unsurprising given its label structure. A simple information-theoretic comparison against true labels (enabling direct LDA comparison) would have strengthened evaluation.  
- The authors should compare against key neural topic models: Cao et al. (AAAI 2015), Nguyen et al. (TACL 2015), and Shamanta et al. (ECML/PKDD 2015).  

Low-level issues:  
- Line 315: "it's similarity" → "its similarity."  
- Line 361: "the topic basis are affected" → "the topic basis is affected" (subject-verb agreement).  
- Figure 5 caption: "words" → "terms" (aligns with context).  
- References: Inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Advances in..." vs. "Advances in Neural..."), missing conference names (e.g., Roder et al.), and other discrepancies.
