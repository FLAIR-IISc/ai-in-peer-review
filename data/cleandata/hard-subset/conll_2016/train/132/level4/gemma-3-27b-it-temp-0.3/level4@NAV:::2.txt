This paper introduces a neural topic model, effectively extending the word2vec objective to simultaneously learn document embeddings and then applying sparsification to align the output with topic model characteristics. The proposed model is conceptually appealing, and the illustrative examples provided are promising. However, a significant weakness is the complete lack of empirical evaluation. The paper relies solely on example tables, offering no assessment of their representativeness or comparison to established topic models – both standard and neural. Without such evaluation, it’s difficult to gauge the model’s true value and substantially hinders acceptance. Potential evaluation avenues include: (1) utilizing the document topic representations in a supervised document categorization task, benchmarked against a topic model with equivalent topic cardinality, or (2) direct evaluation using a dataset with document similarity annotations, assessed via pairwise comparison of topic vectors. While the authors’ commitment to releasing code is commendable, the inclusion of a GitHub link in the submitted manuscript compromises anonymity (potentially warranting rejection, at the discretion of the PCs).

Further concerns include:

- The selection criteria for examples in Figures 3-6 are unclear, raising concerns about potential cherry-picking.
- Section 2.2.1 discusses calculating word representations for topics through pairwise comparison with vocabulary words, a computationally expensive process for realistic vocabulary sizes and topic numbers – its feasibility is questionable.
- Section 3.1 mentions using SpaCy to identify “tokens,” but the specific method (e.g., noun chunk extraction, as in Section 3.2, or another approach) is not detailed. This is unclear given the use of pre-trained word2vec embeddings containing limited multi-word terms.
- The handling of Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) terms is not addressed. While training on the entire document collection mitigates this in the presented experiments, applying the model to novel documents will necessitate updating word embeddings, potentially creating inconsistencies with pre-trained, non-updated embeddings.
- The finding of optimal performance with 20 topics on the 20 Newsgroups corpus is unsurprising given the dataset’s structure. An information-theoretic comparison to document labels could provide a simple, direct evaluation benchmark against LDA and similar models.
- The paper would benefit from comparison to existing neural topic models, such as:
  Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).

Minor issues:

- Line 315: “it’s similarity” should be “its similarity.”
- Line 361: The phrase “topic basis” being “affected” is unclear and awkwardly phrased.
- Figure 5 caption: “words” should likely be “terms.”
- Reference formatting is inconsistent (e.g., “Advances in…”, “Advances in Neural…”, missing conference details for Roder et al.).
