This paper proposes a neural-style topic model that extends the purpose of Word2Vec to document embedding learning, which it then limits through sparsity, thus mimicking the output of a topic model.

I really liked the model proposed by the authors, and found the examples presented by the authors very promising. However, what was actually missing in the paper was an empirical evaluation of the model – the evaluation relies entirely on tables of examples, without any indication of how representative the examples are, or any attempt to directly compare with standard or neural topic models. Without an empirical evaluation, it is impossible to get an idea of the actual value of the model, which makes the paper very difficult to accept. Here are some ideas on how the authors might have achieved this:

(1) Use the topic representation of each document to compare with a topic model of the same topic cardinality in a supervised document classification setup (i.e., as an indirect evaluation of the quality of representation); or (2) through direct evaluation on a dataset with document similarity annotations (based on pairwise comparisons on topic vectors).

It's great that you are releasing the code, but you have compromised anonymity in publishing the github link in the presented version of the paper (strictly speaking, this is enough to completely reject the paper, but I leave it on PC).

Other issues:
- How did you select the examples in Figure 3-6? Presenting a subset of actual topics etc. potentially seems like cherry-picking.
- In Section 2.2.1, you discuss the possibility of computing word representations for topics based on pairwise comparisons with each word in the vocabulary, but this is going to be an extremely expensive process for a reasonable vocabulary size and number of topics; is this really possible?
- You say that you identify "tokens" using spacey in Section 3.1 – how? You extract noun fragments (but no other fragment type), similarly in Section 3.2, or something else? Given that you further state that you use Word2Vec pre-trained embeddings (which only include a small number of multi-word words), it was not clear what you were doing here.
- How does your model deal with OOV words? Yes, in the experiments you report in the paper, you seem to be training the model on the entire document collection, so this is probably not an immediate problem, but there will be contexts where you want to apply the trained model to novel documents, in which case updating the Word2Vec token embeddings would mean that no non-updated (relative to the training collection OOV) Word2Vec embeddings would be directly comparable to the tuned embeddings.
- The finding that 20 topics worked best on a 20 newsgroups corpus was not surprising, given its structure. Perhaps another (much simpler) form of evaluation could have been based on some information-theoretical comparison relative to actual document labels, where you would then be able to directly compare with LDA etc.
