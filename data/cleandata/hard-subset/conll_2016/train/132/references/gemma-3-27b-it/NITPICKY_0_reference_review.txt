The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid topic model combining Dirichlet allocations with word embeddings via a novel network architecture leveraging automatic differentiation. The authors claim to offer a method that benefits from both the interpretability of topic models and the representational power of distributed word vectors. The primary contribution, as presented, is the seamless integration of these approaches within a differentiable framework, facilitating faster prototyping and training. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of this method on the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Idea:** The fundamental concept of combining topic models and word embeddings is not novel. Previous work (e.g., incorporating word embeddings as features into topic models) exists. The paper doesn't sufficiently highlight *how* lda2vec significantly departs from existing approaches beyond the differentiable framework aspect. The claim of a unique contribution feels overstated.

2. **Methodological Imprecision and Justification:** Several methodological choices lack sufficient justification. The choice of β = 0.75 for negative sampling is presented as optimal based on the Twenty Newsgroups results, but the rationale behind this specific value is unclear. Why does emphasizing infrequent words improve performance here? Similarly, the Dirichlet parameter α being set to n-1, and λ=200 feels arbitrary; a more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of these parameters is needed. The rationale for dropout is simply stated as "to prevent co-adaptation" without further elaboration or empirical validation of its necessity.

3. **Insufficient Baselines & Comparative Analysis:** The paper lacks a robust comparative analysis against strong baselines. While the Twenty Newsgroups results are presented, there’s no comparison against state-of-the-art topic models (e.g., NMF, LDA with more sophisticated inference techniques) or recent embedding-based approaches. This makes it difficult to assess the true value added by lda2vec. The Hacker News experiment, while demonstrating qualitative success, is even weaker in this regard as no quantitative metrics are reported.

4. **Presentation and Figures:** Figure 1 is overly cluttered and difficult to interpret. The notation and annotations are small, and the flow of information is not clear. Additionally, several of the figures have numerical values that aren't explained or referenced in the surrounding text (e.g., the numbers in the first figure are unlabelled, making them useless). The formatting is inconsistent and unprofessional. For example, citation styles differ throughout the paper. Some citations also have missing information (e.g., Blei et al.,? 2003).

5. **Overstated Claims & Limited Evaluation:** The conclusion claims "semantic regularities are also learned," and demonstrates this with a limited analogy example. This feels like a stretch given the scope of the evaluation. The model appears to be capable of reproducing some relationships, but this isn’t necessarily a groundbreaking achievement. The authors also claim to have demonstrated that lda2vec "discovers the salient topics", yet the assessment of "salience" is entirely subjective and lacks a formalized evaluation.



**Strengths:**

1. **Differentiable Framework:** The utilization of automatic differentiation for topic model training *is* a valuable aspect. This offers potential benefits in terms of development speed and the ability to incorporate more complex model architectures.

2. **Joint Embedding Space:** The idea of embedding words, topics, and documents into a common space is conceptually sound and potentially useful for downstream tasks.

3. **Clear Presentation of Model Architecture:** Despite the issues with Figure 1, the core idea of the lda2vec architecture is relatively easy to grasp from the text. The mathematical formulation of the loss function is also clearly presented.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does lda2vec compare to existing approaches for combining topic models and word embeddings in terms of performance and computational cost? Please provide quantitative comparisons.

2. What is the rationale behind the specific values chosen for the hyperparameters β, α, and λ? Have you conducted an ablation study to evaluate the sensitivity of the model to these parameters?

3.  Can you elaborate on the specific advantages of using Chainer and GPUs for this particular model? What performance gains were observed compared to other frameworks or hardware configurations?

4.  Please provide a more detailed explanation of the “semantic regularities” learned by the model, along with a more rigorous evaluation of these regularities.

5. Figure 1 requires significant improvement. Please revise it to enhance clarity and readability.



**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is incomplete and contains inconsistencies in formatting. Please ensure that all references are complete and adhere to a consistent style. The paper would benefit from a more critical and self-aware discussion of its limitations. Despite the claims of novelty, the contribution feels incremental, and the presentation requires significant improvement. I recommend rejection in its current form.
