## Review of lda2vec: Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings

This paper proposes lda2vec, a model that combines Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) with Dirichlet-optimized sparse topic mixtures. The core idea is to embed both words and document vectors into a shared space, leveraging the strengths of both distributional semantics (word embeddings) and topic modeling (interpretability). The authors claim to offer a method for jointly learning representations that are both semantically rich and interpretable, expedited by modern automatic differentiation frameworks.

I see the primary contribution as an *implementation* and demonstration of a relatively straightforward combination of established techniques – word embeddings and topic models – within a modern deep learning framework. While the idea isn’t groundbreaking, the authors successfully integrate these components and show promising results on two datasets. A secondary contribution is the validation that topic coherence metrics (specifically Cv) still hold relevance when applied to models built with modern techniques.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the lda2vec model. The architectural diagram (Figure 1) is helpful in understanding the flow of information. The mathematical formulation of the loss function is concise and accurate.
2. **Sound Methodology (within established paradigms):** The authors build upon well-established methods (SGNS and LDA) which lends credibility to their approach. They acknowledge and correctly implement standard techniques like negative sampling and the Dirichlet prior. The use of automatic differentiation allows for efficient training.
3. **Demonstrated Functionality:** The experimental results on the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets show that the model learns meaningful word and document representations. The reported topic coherence scores on Twenty Newsgroups are respectable. The qualitative examples from both datasets illustrate that the learned topics align reasonably well with human intuition.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors explicitly state that their code is open-source, documented, and unit tested, which is critical for fostering scientific progress. This is a significant positive aspect, encouraging other researchers to build upon their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of combining word embeddings and topic models is not entirely new. Previous work has explored similar avenues, and this paper doesn't present a fundamentally different theoretical approach. The contribution lies more in the specific implementation and integration than a conceptual breakthrough.
2. **Limited Baselines & Comparative Evaluation:** The paper lacks a thorough comparative evaluation against strong baselines. While the authors mention existing topic models and distributed representations in the introduction, they don’t present quantitative comparisons to these models on the same datasets using the same evaluation metrics. Simply reporting coherence scores isn't sufficient; a direct comparison to standard LDA implementations, or more recent embedding methods (e.g., BERT-based topic modeling) is necessary. This makes it difficult to assess the true advantage of lda2vec.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Ablation Studies:** The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameter settings. The choice of α (for the Dirichlet prior) and λ (Dirichlet likelihood strength) appears somewhat arbitrary. Furthermore, the authors would benefit from presenting ablation studies to demonstrate the impact of different components of the model (e.g., the Dirichlet regularization term) on performance.
4. **Hacker News Dataset Specifics:** The choice to use noun phrases as tokens in the Hacker News dataset is interesting, but it introduces complexities. The comparison of learned embeddings and analogies becomes less straightforward when the tokens are not single words. This needs further justification and careful consideration of how it affects the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does lda2vec compare quantitatively to standard LDA implementations (e.g., using Gensim) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, using the same evaluation metrics (topic coherence, perplexity)?
2. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study showing the impact of the Dirichlet regularization term (λ) on topic coherence and interpretability?
3. What is the computational cost of training lda2vec compared to training standard LDA or SGNS, particularly on larger datasets?
4. Regarding the Hacker News dataset, how were the noun phrase tokens mapped back to individual words for calculating word similarities and analogies? Does the use of noun phrases affect the comparability of the results to standard word embedding techniques?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is incomplete and formatted inconsistently.
*   The phrase “automatic differentiation frameworks expedite the process” feels somewhat rhetorical. A more concrete discussion of the benefits of these frameworks in this specific context would be helpful.



Overall, this paper presents a reasonable implementation and demonstration of a well-motivated idea. However, the lack of strong comparative evaluation and the limited novelty weaken its impact. Acceptance hinges on the authors addressing the weaknesses raised above, particularly providing a more thorough comparison to existing methods.
