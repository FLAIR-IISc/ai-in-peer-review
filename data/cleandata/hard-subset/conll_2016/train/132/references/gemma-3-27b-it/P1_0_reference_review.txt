## Peer Review for "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

**Summary:**

This paper presents lda2vec, a model that combines the strengths of traditional Dirichlet topic models (like LDA) with the distributed representations learned by word embedding techniques (like word2vec). The core idea is to integrate document-wide feature vectors learned alongside word embeddings within a Skipgram Negative Sampling framework, leveraging automatic differentiation for efficient training. The model aims to produce interpretable document representations while benefiting from the semantic richness of word embeddings. The authors demonstrate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News comments corpus, showcasing its ability to learn coherent topics and capture semantic relationships.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical and streamlined approach to hybrid topic modeling:** The paper offers a relatively straightforward way to combine topic models with word embeddings, crucially utilizing modern automatic differentiation frameworks. This lowers the barrier to entry for exploring such hybrid models compared to deriving new probabilistic approximations.
2. **Demonstration of joint learning of interpretable and distributed representations:**  The authors successfully demonstrate the simultaneous learning of both interpretable topic mixtures (via Dirichlet priors) and dense word/document embeddings, within a single model. 
3. **Empirical validation on two datasets:** The experiments on both Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News, including qualitative analysis of learned topics and semantic relationships, provide evidence of the model's effectiveness and versatility.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a relevant problem:** The combination of interpretability from topic models and the power of distributed representations is a valuable research direction. Many applications benefit from having both.
2. **Practicality and Accessibility:**  The use of Chainer (and the emphasis on automatic differentiation) is a significant strength. It makes the proposed model more accessible to researchers and practitioners who may not have expertise in complex probabilistic inference.  The authors' open-source release is also commendable.
3. **Clear Model Description:** The paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the lda2vec model, supported by a helpful diagram (Figure 1) illustrating the architecture.  The mathematical notation is generally well-presented and easy to follow.
4. **Compelling Empirical Results:**  The results on Twenty Newsgroups, particularly the reported topic coherence scores, suggest the model performs competitively. The analysis of learned word similarities on the Hacker News corpus is insightful and demonstrates the model's ability to capture domain-specific semantic relationships.
5. **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a good overview of relevant research areas, including topic modeling, word embeddings, and related approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of combining topic models and word embeddings isn’t entirely new. Several previous works have explored similar concepts. The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from existing work beyond the implementation details and the use of automatic differentiation.  A more thorough comparison to closely related approaches (e.g., those that directly embed topic proportions into word embedding spaces) is needed.
2. **Evaluation Could Be More Rigorous:** While the qualitative analysis is helpful, the evaluation is somewhat limited.  A more comprehensive quantitative evaluation, including comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., standard LDA, doc2vec) on topic coherence *and* downstream tasks (e.g., document classification, information retrieval) would significantly strengthen the paper. 
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning parameters (like λ and β) but doesn’t provide enough detail on the search process.  The sensitivity of the model to these hyperparameters should be investigated more thoroughly, and ablation studies could help identify the contributions of different components (e.g., the Dirichlet prior).
4. **Limited Discussion of Scalability:** The Hacker News dataset is larger, but the paper doesn’t discuss the computational challenges of scaling lda2vec to even larger corpora.  A discussion of scalability and potential optimizations would be valuable.
5. **Presentation of Results:** While Figure 3 and 4 are useful, they don’t convey statistical significance. Reporting coherence scores with standard deviations or confidence intervals would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Related Work:**  How does lda2vec specifically differ from existing approaches that combine topic models and word embeddings, such as those that directly embed topic proportions into word embedding spaces?  Can you provide a more detailed comparative analysis?
2. **Baseline Comparisons:**  Why were standard LDA and doc2vec not used as baselines in the Twenty Newsgroups experiments?  How would you expect lda2vec to perform relative to these baselines on both topic coherence and downstream tasks?
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Could you elaborate on the process used to select the optimal values for λ and β?  Were any ablation studies performed to assess the impact of these hyperparameters?
4. **Scalability:** What are the major computational bottlenecks in lda2vec, and how do they scale with corpus size? Are there any planned optimizations to improve scalability for very large datasets?
5. **Dirichlet Prior:** Was the choice of α = n-1 driven by any theoretical considerations, or was it determined empirically? Could you comment on the potential impact of different values of α on the learned topic mixtures?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The code availability is excellent and facilitates reproducibility.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise. However, some sentences could be streamlined for better readability.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key parameters used in each experiment.
