## Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

This paper presents lda2vec, a model that combines Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) with Dirichlet-optimized sparse topic mixtures. The authors aim to leverage the strengths of both distributed word representations and interpretable topic models. The primary claimed contribution is a novel model architecture that facilitates joint training of word and document vectors, leading to more interpretable document representations. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of this approach on both the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets.

**Contributions as I see them:** The core contribution lies in the *integration* of word embeddings and topic modeling within a single, differentiable framework. While the individual components (word2vec, LDA-style topic mixtures) are well-established, the seamless combination and simultaneous training represent a potentially useful advance. The experimental validation on two distinct datasets, particularly the Hacker News corpus with its specialized vocabulary, is a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Integration:** The combination of SGNS and Dirichlet-optimized topic mixtures is a conceptually sound idea. The authors correctly identify the complementary strengths of both approaches – distributional semantics from word embeddings and interpretability from topic models. This integration attempts to bridge a gap in existing literature.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the model can learn meaningful topic representations on both datasets. The qualitative analysis of the discovered topics in the Twenty Newsgroups dataset (Figure 3) is convincing, and the topic coherence scores provide quantitative support. The Hacker News results showcase the model’s ability to handle a more specialized vocabulary and learn relevant relationships.
3. **Implementation and Availability:** The authors' emphasis on using automatic differentiation and providing open-source code is commendable. This increases the reproducibility of their results and facilitates further research in this area.
4. **Clear Presentation of the Model:** The paper clearly explains the model architecture and mathematical formulation. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful in visualizing the different components and their interactions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Quantitative Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the qualitative results are promising, the paper lacks a rigorous quantitative comparison to strong baseline models. Specifically, comparing lda2vec’s performance on document classification or retrieval tasks against standard topic modeling approaches (e.g., LDA, NMF) and dense vector representations (e.g., doc2vec) would significantly strengthen the paper. Simply stating that the model *yields* coherent topics isn’t sufficient; it needs to *outperform* existing methods.
2. **Insufficient Justification for Hyperparameter Choices:** The paper provides limited justification for the chosen hyperparameters, such as the Dirichlet concentration parameter (α = n-1) and the scaling factor (λ = 200). The rationale behind these settings is not clearly explained, and a sensitivity analysis to assess their impact on performance is missing. This raises concerns about the robustness of the results.
3. **Superficial Analysis of Linear Regularities:** The demonstration of linear regularities in Figure 5, while interesting, feels superficial. The chosen examples are somewhat cherry-picked and don’t provide a comprehensive assessment of the model’s ability to capture meaningful semantic relationships. A more systematic evaluation using established word analogy datasets would be more convincing.
4. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors (e.g., inconsistent citation styles, broken references). While individually minor, these errors detract from the overall professionalism of the manuscript. For example, multiple references are missing complete information ("Blei et al.,? 2003"). The visual formatting of Figure 1 is also quite poor with overlapping text.
5. **Redundant phrasing and verbose writing:** The introduction is overly verbose and contains repetitive phrasing. Conciseness would improve the clarity and impact of the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How do the performance of lda2vec on document classification/retrieval tasks compare to standard topic modeling approaches (LDA, NMF) and dense vector representations (doc2vec)? Please provide quantitative results.
2.  What is the rationale behind choosing α = n-1 and λ = 200? Have you performed a sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of these hyperparameters on model performance?
3.  Can you provide a more systematic evaluation of the linear regularities learned by lda2vec, perhaps using established word analogy datasets?
4.  Please address the numerous formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors throughout the manuscript.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of the term "topics" is somewhat ambiguous, as it refers to both the latent topic vectors and the interpretable themes discovered in the data. Clarifying this distinction would improve clarity.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of lda2vec and its scalability to larger datasets.
*   The reference list is incomplete and requires careful review.



**Overall Assessment:**

While lda2vec presents a promising approach to combining topic models and word embeddings, the paper suffers from several weaknesses, primarily a lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation, insufficient justification for hyperparameter choices, and numerous minor flaws in formatting and presentation. These issues, while not fatal, significantly detract from the overall impact of the work. **I recommend rejection** pending substantial revisions that address the aforementioned concerns. The core idea is interesting, but the paper needs to be significantly strengthened to justify publication at a competitive venue like CoNLL.
