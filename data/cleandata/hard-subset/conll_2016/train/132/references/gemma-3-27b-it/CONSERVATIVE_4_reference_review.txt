This paper proposes lda2vec, a model combining Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) word embeddings with a Dirichlet-optimized topic mixture. The core idea is to sum word and document vectors to form a context vector for SGNS training, effectively embedding both into a shared space while simultaneously learning interpretable document-topic proportions. The authors demonstrate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is an exploration of combining established techniques – word embeddings and topic modeling – within a unified, differentiable framework. While the *idea* of combining these isn’t novel, the authors successfully implement it and demonstrate reasonable results. A secondary contribution is the application and evaluation of the model on two distinct datasets, providing some evidence of generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Methodology:** The foundation of the model—SGNS—is a well-established and proven technique for learning word embeddings. Building upon this foundation adds a degree of credibility. The use of automatic differentiation frameworks is also a positive, enabling efficient development and experimentation.
2. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the model architecture and loss function clearly. The diagrams, particularly Figure 1, are helpful in understanding the flow of information.
3. **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide results on two datasets, including topic coherence scores (on Twenty Newsgroups) and qualitative examples of learned topics. This is a necessary step, even if the results aren’t groundbreaking.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors state their code is open-source, documented and unit-tested, which is commendable and facilitates verification of results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Strong Baseline Comparison:** The most significant weakness is the limited comparison to strong, established baselines. While the authors mention LDA, they don’t provide a direct comparison of topic coherence or qualitative interpretability against a well-tuned LDA implementation. Showing that lda2vec *outperforms* established methods is crucial, and its absence raises concerns about the practical benefit of this approach.  The reliance on topic coherence alone is insufficient; a comparison of downstream task performance (e.g., document classification) would be much more convincing.
2. **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea – combining word embeddings and topic models – has been explored in previous work. The authors need to more clearly articulate what distinguishes their approach from existing methods and why their specific combination offers advantages.  The described method feels more like an engineering effort to integrate existing techniques than a fundamentally novel contribution.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Justification:** The paper mentions tuning parameters like λ and α, but the justification for the chosen values is weak. A more thorough sensitivity analysis and justification are needed. Why were these specific values chosen, and how do they affect the results?
4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The authors should discuss the limitations of their approach more explicitly. For example, the computational cost of jointly training embeddings and topic models might be higher than traditional methods. The reliance on pre-trained word embeddings (in one experiment) introduces a potential bias.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a direct comparison to a well-tuned LDA model (e.g., using a standard library like Gensim) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, including topic coherence scores *and* a qualitative comparison of the learned topics?
2. What is the computational overhead of lda2vec compared to training SGNS and LDA separately?  Provide some runtime statistics for both datasets.
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (λ and α)? Please provide a sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of varying these parameters on topic coherence and/or downstream performance.
4. The Hacker News dataset utilizes noun phrase tokenization. How does this impact the interpretability and comparability of the topics learned compared to standard word-based topic models?



**Additional Comments:**

The claim about learning linear regularities (Figure 6) feels somewhat contrived. While word analogies can be interesting, their relevance to the core contribution of the paper (combining embeddings and topic models) is not immediately clear. A more rigorous evaluation of these analogies (e.g., against a standard word analogy benchmark) would be beneficial.
