## Peer Review for "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

**Summary:**

This paper presents *lda2vec*, a model that combines Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) word embeddings with Dirichlet-optimized sparse topic mixtures. The core idea is to add document vectors, derived from a topic model, to word vectors when predicting context, effectively grounding the distributed word representations in a more interpretable topic space. The authors demonstrate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News corpus, showcasing topic coherence and the ability to learn semantic relationships within the combined space.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Practical Hybrid Approach:** The paper offers a relatively straightforward and implementable method for integrating the strengths of distributed word representations and topic models. This is valuable because bridging the gap between these traditionally separate approaches is a significant challenge.
2. **Demonstration of Joint Learning:** The authors successfully demonstrate that jointly training word and document embeddings with the Dirichlet constraint leads to coherent topics and retains semantic relationships within the word embeddings. The empirical results provide evidence that this hybrid approach is feasible and effective.
3. **Application to a Large, Specialized Corpus:** Applying the model to the Hacker News dataset and showcasing its ability to capture domain-specific language and relationships (e.g., identifying relationships between tech companies and individuals) demonstrates the versatility of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the benefits of combining topic models (for interpretability) with word embeddings (for semantic representation).  The motivation for addressing the limitations of both approaches individually is well-established.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:** The integration of SGNS with the Dirichlet prior is conceptually sound and well-explained. The use of automatic differentiation frameworks is a practical consideration, making the model more accessible for experimentation.
3. **Empirical Validation on Multiple Datasets:**  The evaluation on both a standard benchmark (Twenty Newsgroups) and a more complex, real-world dataset (Hacker News) lends credibility to the findings. The inclusion of topic coherence metrics and example topic analysis provides qualitative insights.
4. **Open-Source Implementation:** Making the code openly available fosters reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work.
5. **Demonstrated Semantic Relationships:** The analysis in Figure 5 and the accompanying text convincingly demonstrate that the model learns meaningful semantic relationships in the Hacker News corpus, going beyond simply identifying topics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the combination is interesting, the individual components (SGNS, Dirichlet allocation) are well-established. The novelty lies primarily in the *integration* rather than entirely new algorithmic innovations. The paper doesn’t thoroughly address how this approach differs from, or improves upon, existing methods for incorporating prior knowledge into word embeddings.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to other methods for combining topic models and word embeddings.  For instance, it would be beneficial to compare against approaches that directly incorporate topic proportions as features into the word embedding training process, or methods that use word embeddings to initialize topic models.  A more thorough comparative analysis is needed to demonstrate the clear advantages of *lda2vec*.
3. **Limited Analysis of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper reports experimentation with a limited range of hyperparameters (number of topics, beta). A more detailed sensitivity analysis, exploring the impact of other parameters (e.g., dropout rate, learning rate, Dirichlet concentration parameter) on model performance would strengthen the results.
4. **Topic Coherence Metric Limitation:**  The authors acknowledge the reliance on the Palmetto coherence metric, but don’t fully address potential limitations or biases inherent in this automated metric.  Human evaluation of topic interpretability, even on a small sample, would add significant value.
5. **Explanation of Negative Sampling Parameter Choice:** While the authors mention setting β = 0.75 for the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, the rationale behind this choice isn’t deeply explored. A more detailed justification would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Comparison to Related Work:** Can you provide a more detailed comparison to existing methods that combine topic models and word embeddings, specifically outlining the advantages of *lda2vec* over these approaches?
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  Could you elaborate on the process used to select the hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, dropout rate, Dirichlet concentration parameter) and provide a more thorough sensitivity analysis of their impact on model performance?
3. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of training *lda2vec* compared to training SGNS alone, and how does this scale with the size of the dataset and the number of topics?
4. **Interpretability Evaluation:** Would you consider including a small-scale human evaluation of topic interpretability to complement the automated coherence metrics?
5. **Dirichlet Parameter Choice:**  Can you provide a more detailed explanation for choosing α = n-1 for the Dirichlet parameter, and how this impacts the sparsity of document proportions?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are generally clear and helpful.
*   The writing is generally well-organized and clear, though at times a bit dense.
*   The references are comprehensive.



Overall, this paper presents a promising approach for combining the strengths of topic models and word embeddings. While the novelty of the individual components is limited, the integration is well-executed, and the empirical results are encouraging. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the lack of strong baseline comparisons and more thorough hyperparameter analysis, would significantly strengthen the submission.
