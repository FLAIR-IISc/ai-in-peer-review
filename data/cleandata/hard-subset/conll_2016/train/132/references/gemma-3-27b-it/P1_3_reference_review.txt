## Peer Review: Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec

**Summary:**

This paper presents `lda2vec`, a novel model that combines the strengths of Dirichlet topic models (like LDA) and word embeddings (like word2vec). The core idea is to integrate document-wide feature vectors into the Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) objective, while simultaneously learning continuous document weights that load onto topic vectors. This results in a model that learns both dense document representations *and* interpretable topic mixtures. The authors demonstrate the model’s effectiveness on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a large Hacker News comments corpus, showing both topic coherence and the ability to capture semantic relationships between words.

**Main Contributions (as perceived by this reviewer):**

1. **A Principled Hybrid Approach:** The paper provides a well-motivated and clearly articulated method for integrating topic modeling with distributed word representations.  Rather than simply using word embeddings *for* topic modeling, `lda2vec` builds a unified model where both are learned jointly, leading to potentially synergistic benefits.
2. **Demonstration of Scalability & Interpretability:**  The application to the Hacker News corpus, particularly the exploration of learned word relationships and the ability to identify relevant themes within a large, noisy dataset, demonstrates that the approach can scale and retain interpretability.
3. **Practical Implementation & Open Source Release:** Providing a working implementation (and a focus on using automatic differentiation frameworks) lowers the barrier to entry for researchers interested in exploring similar hybrid models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper effectively highlights the complementary strengths and weaknesses of topic models and word embeddings, establishing a clear rationale for their combination.  The desire to retain interpretability alongside the power of distributed representations is well-articulated.
2. **Solid Technical Foundation:** The model itself appears well-designed and logically sound.  The use of the SGNS objective with the addition of a Dirichlet prior on document weights is a sensible approach, and the connection to both LDA and word2vec is clearly explained.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments on two very different datasets – a standard benchmark (Twenty Newsgroups) and a large-scale real-world corpus (Hacker News) – provide strong evidence for the model’s versatility. The inclusion of both quantitative (topic coherence) and qualitative (topic examination, word analogies) evaluations is appreciated.
4. **Implementation Details & Reproducibility:** The authors explicitly state the use of Chainer and provide links to their open-source code and datasets, which is a crucial aspect for ensuring reproducibility and further research.  Mentioning specific parameter settings (e.g., negative sampling parameters) also strengthens the reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* is interesting, the individual components (word2vec, LDA-style topic modeling) are well-established. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this work differs significantly from existing attempts to integrate these approaches or to achieve similar goals (e.g. embedding topic models).
2. **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:**  The experiments largely present the performance of `lda2vec` in isolation.  A more compelling evaluation would involve a direct comparison to other state-of-the-art topic modeling techniques (e.g., NMF, BERTopic) and potentially models that also attempt to combine dense and sparse representations. Specifically, comparing against a word2vec model trained on the same data without the topic modeling component would be insightful.
3. **Coherence Metric Limitation:** While the authors acknowledge the limitations of topic coherence metrics, relying primarily on Cv might be insufficient. The chosen metric has known biases and doesn't necessarily capture all aspects of topic quality.  Exploring other evaluation metrics, such as human evaluations, would strengthen the claims about topic interpretability.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention tuning parameters like lambda and alpha. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the methods used for parameter selection would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Related Work:** Could you elaborate on how `lda2vec` differs from existing approaches that combine word embeddings and topic models? Are there specific limitations of previous work that `lda2vec` addresses?
2. **Baseline Comparison:**  Could you provide a comparison against a standard word2vec model (trained on the same data and with the same hyperparameters, excluding the topic modeling components) to demonstrate the benefit of integrating the topic modeling aspect?
3.  **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  What strategies were employed to select the values for λ and α? Was a grid search performed?  How sensitive are the results to changes in these parameters?
4. **Computational Cost:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational resources required to train `lda2vec` compared to standard word2vec or LDA, particularly on the larger Hacker News dataset?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 is helpful but a bit crowded. A simplified diagram focusing on the key interactions between the different components might be clearer.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations and future research directions, such as exploring different architectures for combining word and document vectors or investigating the application of `lda2vec` to other domains.




Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a well-motivated and implemented model. While not groundbreaking in its individual components, the integration of word embeddings and topic modeling is a valuable contribution, and the authors provide compelling evidence for its effectiveness. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
