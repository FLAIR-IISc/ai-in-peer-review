## Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

This paper proposes lda2vec, a model combining Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) with Dirichlet-optimized sparse topic mixtures. The authors aim to leverage the strengths of both distributed word representations and interpretable topic models, creating a hybrid approach for document representation. The primary claimed contribution is a novel architecture enabling simultaneous learning of word and document embeddings while retaining topic interpretability via a Dirichlet prior. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of this approach on the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets.

**Contributions as I see them:**

1.  **Integration of established methods:** The primary contribution lies in the somewhat straightforward, though non-trivial, integration of SGNS and a Dirichlet-based topic model. While not a fundamentally new *method*, it presents a practical way to combine two well-established techniques.
2.  **Empirical evaluation on standard datasets:** The application of the proposed model to the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, a standard benchmark for topic modeling, provides a baseline for comparison, although the comparison is limited (see weaknesses). The inclusion of the Hacker News dataset, while less standard, expands the scope of evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the rationale for combining topic models and word embeddings, highlighting the benefits of interpretability and semantic representation. The discussion of existing approaches is also reasonable.
2.  **Sound methodology:** The model itself appears logically sound, building upon established techniques. The use of automatic differentiation and the Chainer framework is a sensible choice for rapid prototyping and experimentation.
3.  **Reproducibility:** The authors explicitly state that their code is open-source, documented and unit tested, which is commendable and crucial for scientific rigor.
4.  **Demonstrated capability:** The Hacker News experiments demonstrate that the model *can* learn meaningful representations tailored to a specialized corpus and recover linear relationships between tokens – a key strength of word embeddings.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of combining topic models and word embeddings isn’t entirely new. Several previous works have explored similar hybrid approaches. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from this existing body of work and doesn't fully explore related work in its introduction.
2.  **Insufficient Baselines:** The evaluation on Twenty Newsgroups lacks a thorough comparison against strong baseline topic models (e.g., standard LDA, NMF). Simply reporting topic coherence isn’t sufficient. Performance on standard topic modeling evaluation metrics (e.g., perplexity, UMass) is missing, hindering assessment of the model’s true advantage. The reliance on qualitative analysis of topics is subjective and doesn't offer strong evidence of improvement.
3.  **Limited Evaluation Depth:** While the Hacker News experiment is interesting, the absence of quantitative evaluation metrics (like coherence) and the reliance on illustrative examples make it difficult to assess the effectiveness of the model on this larger dataset. The choice of noun phrase tokenization, while potentially beneficial, isn’t thoroughly justified and introduces a dependency on a specific preprocessing pipeline.
4.  **Dirichlet Prior Justification:** The rationale for setting α = n-1 in the Dirichlet prior is somewhat weak. While the authors state it encourages sparsity, a more thorough exploration of different α values and their impact on topic coherence and interpretability is needed.
5.  **Overstated Claims:** The claim of "semantic regularities" being "preserved" is too strong. The demonstration in Figure 5 is interesting, but doesn’t guarantee that the learned relationships are consistently superior to those learned by standard word embedding techniques.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does lda2vec compare to standard LDA, NMF, or other state-of-the-art topic modeling techniques on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, using established evaluation metrics such as perplexity and UMass? Please provide a quantitative comparison.
2.  Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of α = n-1 in the Dirichlet prior? Have you experimented with other values and analyzed their impact on topic quality?
3.  What specific advantages does lda2vec offer over simply training word embeddings (e.g., word2vec) and then using these embeddings to construct document representations?
4.  What is the computational cost of lda2vec compared to standard topic modeling and word embedding techniques, particularly in terms of training time and memory usage?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The figure captions are somewhat verbose and could be more concise.
*   The reference list should be carefully checked for completeness and consistency. Some entries are incomplete (e.g., Blei et al.,? 2003).



Overall, the paper presents a reasonable, though not groundbreaking, approach to combining topic models and word embeddings. While the methodology is sound and the code is publicly available, the lack of rigorous evaluation and comparison to established baselines significantly weakens the claims of improvement. Acceptance is contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing a more thorough evaluation against standard benchmarks and justifying the design choices more convincingly.
