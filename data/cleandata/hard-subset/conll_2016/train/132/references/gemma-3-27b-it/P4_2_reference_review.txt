This paper introduces lda2vec, a hybrid topic modeling approach combining Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings, leveraging automatic differentiation for faster prototyping. The authors claim to offer a method that provides both the interpretability of topic models and the representational power of word embeddings, enabling efficient model development and the ability to incorporate advances in distributed word representations. The primary contribution is the lda2vec model itself, which integrates Skipgram Negative Sampling with Dirichlet-optimized sparse topic mixtures, and the demonstration of its effectiveness on both the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets. A secondary contribution is the application of automatic differentiation to topic modeling, facilitating rapid experimentation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Combination of Approaches:** The integration of word embeddings (specifically, Skipgram) with a traditional topic model (LDA-inspired through the Dirichlet prior) is a reasonable and potentially valuable approach. This hybridization aims to benefit from the strengths of both paradigms – the semantic richness of distributed representations and the interpretability of topic models. This blending is not trivial and represents a genuine attempt to move beyond simply using one or the other.
2. **Practical Implementation & Automatic Differentiation:** Utilizing a framework like Chainer (and emphasizing automatic differentiation) is a significant practical advantage. The authors correctly point out the difficulty of deriving approximations for new probabilistic topic models, and the use of automatic differentiation alleviates this burden, enabling faster prototyping and experimentation.
3. **Empirical Evaluation on Multiple Datasets:** The evaluation on two distinct datasets – Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News – is a positive aspect. The choice of datasets allows for some assessment of the model's generalizability, though the preprocessing steps differ significantly between the two. The reporting of topic coherence scores for Twenty Newsgroups provides a quantitative measure of topic quality. The qualitative analysis on Hacker News, showcasing learned word relationships, is also helpful.
4. **Clear Model Description and Diagram:** The paper includes a clear description of the model, accompanied by a helpful diagram (Figure 1) that visualizes the architecture and information flow within lda2vec. This aids in understanding the interplay between the Skipgram objective and the Dirichlet prior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination is interesting, neither the Skipgram model nor the Dirichlet prior are novel contributions in themselves. The core innovation lies in *how* they are combined, but the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing approaches that might, for example, use pre-trained word embeddings as features in a traditional LDA model, or directly apply topic models to word embedding spaces.
2. **Superficial Comparison to Existing Work:** The related work section mentions several relevant papers, but the discussion of how lda2vec differs from and improves upon these approaches is somewhat superficial. For example, the authors briefly acknowledge existing methods for integrating word embeddings and topic models, but do not offer a thorough comparison in terms of performance, interpretability, or computational efficiency.
3. **Preprocessing Discrepancies and their Impact:** The significant difference in preprocessing between the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets (especially the use of noun phrase tokenization for Hacker News) makes direct comparison difficult and raises questions about the robustness of the model to preprocessing choices. The impact of this choice isn’t adequately discussed.
4. **Limited Analysis of Sparsity:** While the authors emphasize the importance of the Dirichlet prior for inducing sparsity in document weights, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the degree of sparsity achieved in practice and its effect on model performance and interpretability.  A visualization of document weight distributions would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of lda2vec with existing methods for combining word embeddings and topic models? Specifically, how does lda2vec’s performance compare to methods that use pre-trained word embeddings as features in a traditional LDA model, or methods that directly apply topic models to word embedding spaces?
2.  The preprocessing steps differ significantly between the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets. How sensitive is lda2vec to these preprocessing choices, and what steps were taken to ensure a fair comparison between the two datasets?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the sparsity of document weights achieved in practice? What is the typical degree of sparsity, and how does it vary with the number of topics and the value of the concentration parameter α? A visualization of document weight distributions would be helpful.
4.  Given the reliance on the Skipgram model, does lda2vec inherit its known biases related to word frequency and data imbalances? If so, what steps have been taken to mitigate these biases?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of lda2vec compared to traditional topic modeling approaches.
* The phrase "tuning parameter λ" appears without earlier definition.
* A more rigorous quantitative evaluation, perhaps with comparisons to established baselines, would strengthen the claims of the paper.
