## Peer Review: Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec

**Summary:**

This paper presents `lda2vec`, a novel model that combines the strengths of Dirichlet topic models (like LDA) and word embeddings (like word2vec). The core idea is to integrate document-wide feature vectors, learned through a Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) objective, with continuous document weights loaded onto topic vectors. The model aims to produce both interpretable topic representations and dense document embeddings, leveraging the benefits of both probabilistic and neural network approaches to topic modeling.  The authors demonstrate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News comments corpus, showcasing its ability to learn coherent topics and capture semantic relationships between words.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical and implementable hybrid approach:** The paper successfully demonstrates a relatively straightforward way to combine the strengths of topic modeling and word embeddings within a single, differentiable framework. This allows for joint learning and utilizes the computational advantages of modern deep learning libraries.
2. **Demonstration of interpretable document representations:** By incorporating a Dirichlet prior on document topic distributions, the model provides a pathway to create document representations that are more readily interpretable than purely dense embeddings.  This is a key differentiator from many existing approaches.
3. **Empirical validation on diverse datasets:** Applying `lda2vec` to both a standard benchmark (Twenty Newsgroups) and a more complex, real-world dataset (Hacker News) provides initial evidence of its generalizability and utility.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Integration:** The combination of SGNS with Dirichlet-optimized topic mixtures is a novel approach. While both components are well-established, their integration within a single, trainable model is a valuable contribution, bridging the gap between traditional topic modeling and modern neural network methods.
2. **Interpretability:**  The core strength of the paper is the emphasis on interpretability. The Dirichlet prior encourages sparse topic mixtures, making it possible to understand the thematic composition of documents and the semantic meaning of the learned topics. The examples provided in Figures 3 and 4 support this claim.
3. **Practicality and Implementation:** The authors leverage Chainer and GPUs, making the model computationally feasible and demonstrating a practical implementation path.  The code availability is a strong positive point, allowing for reproducibility and further research.
4. **Demonstrated Semantic Regularities:**  The results in Figure 5 and the accompanying text demonstrate that the model can learn meaningful semantic relationships between words, even within the specific vocabulary of the Hacker News corpus. This highlights the power of the integrated approach. 
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the model architecture, loss function, and experimental setup. The diagrams (particularly Figure 1) are helpful for understanding the model’s internal workings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing topic modeling techniques (e.g., LDA, NMF) and document embedding methods (e.g., Doc2Vec). While topic coherence is reported for Twenty Newsgroups, a more extensive evaluation against baselines is needed to demonstrate the clear advantage of `lda2vec`.  Simply stating the advantages doesn't suffice.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention tuning parameters like λ and α, but the sensitivity of the model to these parameters isn’t thoroughly investigated.  A more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different hyperparameter settings would strengthen the results.
3. **Scalability Concerns:** While the use of GPUs is mentioned, the computational cost of training on larger datasets isn’t explicitly addressed.  The Hacker News dataset is significantly larger than Twenty Newsgroups, but a more detailed discussion of scalability limitations and potential optimizations would be valuable.
4. **Topic Coherence Metric:** The reliance on a single coherence metric (Cv) is limiting.  Different coherence measures capture different aspects of topic quality, and a more comprehensive evaluation using multiple metrics would provide a more robust assessment.
5. **Superficial Novelty in the Hacker News experiment:** While the Hacker News experiment is interesting, the observation that the model learns to represent themes relevant to the Hacker News community is somewhat expected. A deeper analysis of *why* `lda2vec` performs well in this context (compared to other methods) would be more insightful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Baseline Comparison:** Could you provide a more detailed comparison of `lda2vec` to standard LDA and Doc2Vec models on both datasets, including quantitative metrics (e.g., perplexity, topic coherence, classification accuracy) and qualitative examples?
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** What was the methodology used to select the optimal values for λ and α?  Could you present a sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of these parameters on model performance?
3. **Scalability Analysis:** What is the approximate training time for `lda2vec` on the Hacker News dataset?  Are there any bottlenecks or areas where the model’s scalability could be improved?
4. **Coherence Metric Rationale:** Why was the Cv coherence metric chosen over other commonly used metrics (e.g., UMass, NPMI)?  Do you anticipate that using different metrics would lead to different conclusions about the quality of the learned topics?
5. **Negative Sampling Distribution:**  The paper mentions using u<sup>3/4</sup> for negative sampling. What was the rationale behind choosing this specific exponent compared to u<sup>0</sup> or u<sup>1</sup>, beyond the stated preference for emphasizing infrequent words? Did you experiment with other exponents, and if so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The references section is missing complete information (e.g., year of publication for many entries).
*  The statement “Our software is open source” should include a link to the repository. (It's present in the text, but should also be in the references/acknowledgements).
