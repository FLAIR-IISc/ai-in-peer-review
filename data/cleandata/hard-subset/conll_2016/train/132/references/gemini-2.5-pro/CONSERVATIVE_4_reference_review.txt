### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces lda2vec, a novel model that aims to jointly learn word embeddings, document representations, and topic vectors within a single framework. The core idea is to extend the skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS) objective from word2vec. In this modified objective, the context vector used for prediction is a sum of the pivot word's vector and a document vector. This document vector is itself constructed as a weighted average of a set of shared topic vectors. To achieve the sparse document-topic distributions characteristic of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), a regularization term inspired by the Dirichlet distribution's log-likelihood is added to the overall loss function.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  A novel neural architecture that combines the mechanisms of SGNS and LDA to produce word, document, and topic representations in a shared vector space.
2.  A demonstration, through qualitative analysis on two corpora (Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News), that the model can produce interpretable topics and learn meaningful semantic regularities between word vectors.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty:** The paper's primary strength is its attempt to unify two highly influential but distinct modeling paradigms: distributional word embeddings and probabilistic topic models. The goal of embedding words, topics, and documents into a single, coherent semantic space where their relationships can be directly inspected (e.g., via cosine similarity) is an elegant and worthwhile objective.

2.  **Qualitative Results:** The qualitative evaluations on both datasets are compelling. The topics extracted from the Twenty Newsgroups corpus (Fig. 3) clearly correspond to the underlying newsgroup categories, and the topics from the Hacker News corpus (Fig. 4) appear coherent and capture salient themes of that community. The word analogy tasks (Fig. 6) further demonstrate that the word embedding component of the model successfully captures semantic relationships, similar to the original word2vec model.

3.  **Potential for Interpretability:** By design, the model aims to combine the predictive power of dense embeddings with the interpretability of sparse topic models. The use of a Dirichlet-like regularizer to enforce sparsity on document-topic weights is a pragmatic approach to ensure the resulting document vectors are not just opaque dense vectors, but are decomposable into human-interpretable themes.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** This is the most significant weakness of the submission. The paper proposes a new method for topic modeling and word representation, yet it provides no quantitative comparison to standard, well-established methods.
    *   **Topic Modeling:** The topic coherence scores for Twenty Newsgroups are presented in isolation. A rigorous evaluation would require comparing these scores against a standard LDA implementation (e.g., MALLET, Gensim) trained on the exact same preprocessed data. Without this baseline, the reported `Cv` score of 0.567 is meaningless; it is impossible to know if this is a strong, average, or poor result for this dataset. The paper also fails to compare against any other neural topic models.
    *   **Word Embeddings:** The quality of the learned word vectors is only assessed qualitatively through cherry-picked examples. The standard protocol for evaluating word embeddings involves reporting performance on a suite of intrinsic tasks (e.g., WordSim-353 for similarity, the Google Analogy dataset for analogies) and comparing these results against SGNS or GloVe baselines trained on the same corpus. This evaluation is entirely missing. It is unclear if the hybrid objective helps, hurts, or has no effect on the quality of the resulting word vectors compared to simpler, established methods.

2.  **Methodological Justification:** The formulation of the model seems more motivated by intuition and engineering convenience than by a principled probabilistic framework.
    *   The context vector is defined as a simple sum of the word and document vectors (`~cj = ~wj + ~dj`). This is a strong assumption that is justified only by an analogy to vector addition in word2vec. It is not clear why this simple linear combination is the optimal way to integrate document-wide and local context.
    *   The "Dirichlet-likelihood term" (`Ld`) is not a true likelihood, as the model does not specify a generative process. It is a regularization term that encourages the softmax outputs (`pjk`) to be sparse. While this is a valid technique, framing it as a "likelihood" is misleading and obscures the fact that the model departs from the probabilistic foundations of LDA.

3.  **Insufficient Ablation and Parameter Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough analysis of its own components and hyperparameters. The strength of the Dirichlet regularizer (λ=200) is presented as a fixed value that "works well," with no sensitivity analysis. This parameter critically balances the word embedding objective against the topic sparsity objective. A proper evaluation would demonstrate how varying λ impacts both topic coherence and word vector quality, to understand the trade-offs inherent in the model. Similarly, the effect of the dropout on the final performance is not quantified.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide topic coherence scores for a standard, widely-used LDA implementation (e.g., Gensim's wrapper for MALLET) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, using the identical preprocessing and vocabulary, to properly contextualize your model's performance?

2.  Can you provide quantitative results on standard word embedding evaluation benchmarks (e.g., word similarity and word analogy tasks) for both lda2vec and a standard SGNS baseline trained on the same corpora? This would be essential to validate the claim that the model learns high-quality word representations.

3.  The term "Dirichlet likelihood" seems imprecise given that the model is not generative and the term functions as a regularizer on the loss function. Would you consider re-phrasing this to be a "Dirichlet-based sparsity regularizer" to more accurately reflect its role in the model?

4.  Can you provide more justification for the additive composition of the document and word vectors? Have you explored alternative, potentially more complex, composition functions to combine the global document context with the local word context?
