### Review

**Summary of the paper**
This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that jointly learns word embeddings, topic vectors, and document topic proportions within a single framework. The model elegantly extends the word2vec skip-gram architecture by defining the context vector as a sum of the pivot word vector and a document-specific vector. This document vector is, in turn, constrained to be a sparse mixture of a set of shared topic vectors. Sparsity is encouraged by incorporating a Dirichlet-likelihood term into the overall loss function, which is optimized end-to-end alongside the standard negative sampling loss. This approach embeds words, documents, and topics into the same continuous vector space. The authors demonstrate the model's ability to produce both coherent, interpretable topics and high-quality, semantically meaningful word embeddings on the Twenty Newsgroups and a large Hacker News corpus.

**Main Contributions**
1.  A novel and intuitive model that unifies latent topic modeling and distributed word representations. The core mechanism—representing the skip-gram context as a sum of a word vector and a document's topic mixture vector—is an elegant way to inject document-level semantics into word-level prediction, allowing for the joint learning of both representations.
2.  A practical demonstration of integrating probabilistic modeling components (like a Dirichlet prior) as a loss term within a modern automatic differentiation framework. This approach sidesteps the need for complex, model-specific inference derivations, making hybrid model development more accessible to the wider research community.
3.  Strong qualitative and quantitative results on both a standard benchmark and a large, specialized corpus. The experiments effectively show that the model produces coherent topics (as measured by topic coherence) and learns high-quality, domain-specific word embeddings that capture semantic analogies, validating the joint learning approach.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper's primary strength is its clever synthesis of two major paradigms in NLP: topic modeling and word embeddings. The central idea of defining the context vector as `c_j = w_j + d_j` where `d_j` is a topic mixture is simple, intuitive, and powerful. It provides a principled way to combine local (word co-occurrence) and global (document-level theme) context. This is a significant conceptual contribution that effectively bridges two previously distinct lines of research.
2.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The authors evaluate their model on two different corpora, demonstrating its versatility. On the 20 Newsgroups dataset, they provide quantitative results using a standard topic coherence metric (Cv), showing the model generates high-quality topics, and the qualitative examples are convincing. The experiments on the much larger and more specialized Hacker News corpus are particularly compelling. They showcase the model's scalability and its ability to learn nuanced, domain-specific topics (e.g., "Bitcoin", "Compensation") and word vector relationships that go beyond generic embeddings (e.g., "Jeff Bezos - Amazon + Facebook = Mark Zuckerberg"). This demonstrates clear practical utility.
3.  **Methodological Value and Accessibility:** The paper's approach of framing the Dirichlet likelihood as a regularizer in a neural network's loss function is a valuable methodological contribution. It showcases how classical probabilistic models can be seamlessly integrated into modern deep learning frameworks. By leveraging automatic differentiation, the authors avoid the bespoke and often difficult derivations required for traditional topic model inference, which is a significant practical advantage. The authors are to be commended for making their code open-source, which enhances the reproducibility and potential impact of their work.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Baselines:** The paper presents its results largely in isolation, making it difficult to gauge their significance. For the Twenty Newsgroups experiment, topic coherence scores are reported, but they are not compared against standard, well-established baselines like LDA (e.g., from Gensim or Mallet) or other contemporary neural topic models. Without such comparisons, it is unclear if lda2vec offers a substantial improvement in topic quality. Similarly, while the word analogies are compelling, the quality of the learned embeddings is not quantitatively evaluated against a standard SGNS model trained on the same corpus using established word similarity or analogy tasks.
2.  **Lack of Ablation Studies and Hyperparameter Analysis:** The model combines several key components, notably the additive context vector and the Dirichlet sparsity-inducing loss term (`L_d`). The paper lacks an ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component. For instance, how do topic coherence and word vector quality change if the Dirichlet term is removed (i.e., `λ=0`) or if α is set to 1? This would demonstrate the necessity of the sparsity constraint. Furthermore, the paper provides little justification for the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., `λ = 200`) or analysis of the model's sensitivity to them, which is crucial for understanding the model's robustness and for practitioners seeking to apply it.
3.  **Ambiguity in Training at Scale:** The description of the training procedure for the Dirichlet term (`L_d`) is unclear. The paper states that the loss is adjusted to be "proportional to the minibatch size divided by the size of the total corpus." However, `L_d` is defined as a sum over all documents. This implies that the document proportion vectors for all documents must be held in memory and updated, which could present a significant scalability bottleneck for very large corpora. It is not clear how the parameters for documents *not* included in a given minibatch are handled. A more precise description of the minibatch update rule for the document-specific parameters is needed to fully assess the model's computational efficiency and scalability claims.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide comparative results for topic coherence against a well-tuned standard LDA implementation on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset? Similarly, for the Hacker News corpus, could you provide quantitative results on a standard word analogy or similarity task comparing lda2vec's embeddings with those from a baseline word2vec (SGNS) model trained on the same data?
2.  Could you please clarify the exact minibatch training procedure for the document proportion parameters? Specifically, how are the proportion vectors for documents outside the current minibatch handled during a gradient update step, and what is the precise formulation of the minibatch-adjusted Dirichlet loss?
3.  The context vector is formed by simple addition: `c_j = w_j + d_j`. What was the motivation for this specific composition function over others, such as concatenation followed by a linear transformation? Have you experimented with alternative compositions?
