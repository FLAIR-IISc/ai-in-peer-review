### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces lda2vec, a novel model that aims to combine the strengths of probabilistic topic models (like Latent Dirichlet Allocation) and dense word embeddings (like word2vec). The core idea is to modify the skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS) objective by defining the context vector as a sum of a pivot word vector and a document vector. This document vector is, in turn, constructed as a sparse, weighted mixture of a set of shared topic vectors. The sparsity is encouraged by adding a Dirichlet-likelihood term to the overall loss function. The entire model is trained jointly within an automatic differentiation framework.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A hybrid model architecture:** The primary contribution is the specific formulation of the lda2vec model, which jointly learns word, document, and topic representations in a single, shared vector space. The additive composition of word and document vectors to form the context for the SGNS objective is the central novel mechanism.
2.  **A practical demonstration of implementation:** The paper successfully demonstrates that such a hybrid model, which blends a probabilistic prior (Dirichlet) with a neural network loss, can be effectively implemented and optimized using modern automatic differentiation libraries.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Elegance and Interpretability:** The model presents an intuitive and elegant approach to unifying two major paradigms in text representation. By embedding words, documents, and topics into the same semantic space, the model allows for direct comparison and interpretation (e.g., finding words most similar to a topic vector). The goal of retaining LDA's interpretable, sparse document-topic mixtures while leveraging the rich semantic representations of word embeddings is a well-motivated and valuable research direction.
2.  **Plausible Qualitative Results:** The qualitative results presented for both the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets are compelling. The discovered topics (Figures 3 and 4) appear coherent and align with expected themes in the respective corpora. Similarly, the learned word analogies (Figure 6) demonstrate that the word embedding component of the model successfully captures semantic regularities, similar to standard SGNS.
3.  **Soundness of the Core Idea:** The motivation for combining document-level and word-level context is sound. The intuition that a word's context is informed by both its immediate neighbors (the pivot word) and the broader theme of the document is a reasonable assumption. The formulation `~cj = ~wj + ~dj` is a simple and direct way to encode this intuition.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Complete Lack of Comparative Baselines:** This is the most significant weakness of the paper. A new model is proposed, but its performance is never compared against established methods. The evaluation of topic quality relies on reporting the topic coherence score (Cv) for lda2vec in isolation. Without comparing this score to that of a standard LDA implementation (e.g., from Gensim or Mallet) trained on the same preprocessed data, the reported value of 0.567 is uninterpretable. Is this score high, low, or average for this dataset? We have no way of knowing. Similarly, the quality of the word embeddings is only demonstrated through cherry-picked analogies. There is no quantitative evaluation on standard word similarity or analogy benchmarks against a baseline SGNS model trained on the same corpus. This omission makes it impossible to assess whether the proposed hybridization improves upon, degrades, or simply matches the performance of its constituent parts.
2.  **Insufficient Quantitative Evaluation:** The evaluation is overwhelmingly qualitative. Beyond the single, non-contextualized coherence score, the paper relies on showing lists of topic words and word analogies. While these are useful for illustration, they are not a substitute for rigorous quantitative assessment. The quality of the learned document representations (`~dj`) is not evaluated at all on any downstream task, such as document classification or retrieval. Such extrinsic evaluation would provide a much stronger and more objective measure of the model's utility than the current analysis.
3.  **Lack of Justification for Key Hyperparameters:** The model introduces a crucial hyperparameter `λ`, which balances the SGNS loss and the Dirichlet-likelihood term. This is set to `λ = 200` with the remark that it "works well." This is insufficient for a rigorous scientific paper. There is no ablation study or sensitivity analysis to show how this parameter affects the model's performance. How was this value chosen? How robust is the model to changes in `λ`? Without this analysis, the model appears brittle and its configuration arbitrary.
4.  **Limited Technical Depth in Model Formulation:** The justification for the additive combination of word and document vectors (`~wj + ~dj`) is based on an analogy to word vector addition (e.g., `Germany + airline`). While intuitive, this connection is not formally established. Furthermore, the adaptation of the global Dirichlet likelihood term for minibatch training is mentioned only briefly ("we adjust the loss of the term to be proportional to the minibatch size divided by the size of the total corpus") without sufficient detail to assess the soundness of this approximation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide topic coherence scores (using the exact same preprocessing and evaluation pipeline) for a standard, well-regarded implementation of LDA on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset? This is essential for contextualizing your model's performance.
2.  Similarly, could you provide results for your learned word embeddings on a standard word analogy task (e.g., the Google analogy dataset, or a subset relevant to your corpora) and compare them against a standard word2vec (SGNS) model trained on the same data? This would validate the claim that semantic regularities are preserved or enhanced.
3.  Can you provide a sensitivity analysis for the `λ` hyperparameter? How does topic coherence and/or downstream task performance vary as `λ` is changed? What is the justification for selecting the value 200?
4.  Have you considered evaluating the learned document vectors (`~dj`) on a downstream task like document classification? This would provide an extrinsic evaluation of the quality of the document representations, which is a key output of your model.
