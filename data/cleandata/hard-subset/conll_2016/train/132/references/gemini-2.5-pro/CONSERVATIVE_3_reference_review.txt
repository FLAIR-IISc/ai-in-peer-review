### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that aims to combine the strengths of probabilistic topic models (like Latent Dirichlet Allocation, LDA) and distributed word representations (like word2vec). The core idea is to modify the skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS) objective by defining the context vector as an additive combination of a pivot word vector and a document vector. This document vector is, in turn, constructed as a mixture of topic vectors, with the mixture weights regularized by a Dirichlet-like term to encourage sparsity.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  A novel objective function that jointly trains word embeddings, document representations, and topic vectors within a single, unified framework. This allows all three components to reside in the same vector space, enabling direct comparison (e.g., finding words most similar to a topic).
2.  The demonstration of a hybrid model architecture that produces both dense, semantically-rich word embeddings and sparse, interpretable document-topic proportions, attempting to bridge the gap between two major paradigms in text representation.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Elegance:** The proposal to unify word embeddings and topic modeling in a single end-to-end trainable model is conceptually appealing. The architecture, where a document vector is a weighted sum of topic vectors and is then added to a word vector to predict context, is an intuitive and elegant way to merge document-level themes with local word-level semantics.
2.  **Interpretability of Components:** A key strength is that the model's components are designed for interpretability. The topic vectors can be directly interpreted by finding their nearest neighboring word vectors, and the document representations are sparse mixtures, similar to standard LDA. The qualitative results presented in Figures 3 and 4 suggest the model is successful at learning coherent and meaningful topics.
3.  **Practical Implementation:** The paper highlights the advantage of using modern automatic differentiation frameworks to build and train such a hybrid model. This simplifies a process that would otherwise require bespoke derivations for variational inference, making this type of modeling more accessible to the broader research community.

### Weaknesses

My primary concerns with this submission relate to its empirical evaluation, which lacks the rigor necessary to validate the proposed method against established techniques.

1.  **Absence of Baseline Comparisons:** This is the most significant weakness. The paper presents a new method for topic modeling and document representation but fails to compare it against any established baselines. For topic quality, a comparison against a standard implementation of LDA (e.g., from Mallet or Gensim) on the 20 Newsgroups dataset is essential. The reported topic coherence scores are presented in a vacuum; without a baseline, it is impossible to know if a score of 0.567 is good, average, or poor for this dataset and preprocessing scheme. Similarly, for document representations, there is no comparison to methods like Paragraph Vectors (doc2vec), which also learn dense document vectors. The claims of the model's effectiveness are therefore unsubstantiated.
2.  **Limited and Primarily Qualitative Evaluation:** The evaluation relies heavily on cherry-picked qualitative examples of topics and word analogies. While these are useful for illustrating the model's capabilities, they are not a substitute for rigorous quantitative analysis. The only quantitative metric provided is topic coherence, and only on one of the two datasets. The utility of the learned document vectors (`~dj`) is never quantitatively measured. A common evaluation for document representations is performance on a downstream task like document classification or information retrieval. Without this, the practical value of the lda2vec document vectors remains unproven.
3.  **Insufficient Justification for Model Design Choices and Hyperparameters:** Several key design choices appear ad-hoc and are not justified empirically. For instance, the context vector is formed by adding the word and document vectors (`~wj + ~dj`). Why is simple addition the optimal composition function? Alternatives like concatenation followed by a linear transformation are not explored or compared. Furthermore, the strength of the Dirichlet regularizer is set to `λ = 200` with little justification. The sensitivity of the model to this crucial hyperparameter is not analyzed, making it difficult to assess the model's robustness and the generalizability of the reported results. An ablation study demonstrating the impact of each model component (especially the Dirichlet term) on performance would have significantly strengthened the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide topic coherence scores for a standard, well-tuned LDA implementation on the 20 Newsgroups dataset, using the exact same preprocessing pipeline? This would provide a crucial baseline for interpreting your model's results.
2.  The paper argues for the creation of meaningful document vectors (`~dj`). Have you evaluated these vectors on any downstream supervised task (e.g., document classification on 20 Newsgroups) and compared their performance against established baselines like doc2vec (PV-DM/DBOW) or even a simple TF-IDF representation?
3.  The additive composition for the context vector (`~cj = ~wj + ~dj`) is motivated by analogy. Did you experiment with other composition functions (e.g., concatenation)? If so, how did they perform in comparison? If not, could you provide a stronger justification for this specific choice?
4.  How was the value for the Dirichlet term's strength (`λ = 200`) selected? Could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing how topic coherence and sparsity vary with different values of `λ`?
