### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces lda2vec, a hybrid model that jointly learns word, document, and topic representations in a single, unified vector space. The core idea is to augment the Skip-gram Negative-Sampling (SGNS) objective from word2vec. In lda2vec, the context vector used for prediction is not just the pivot word vector but a sum of the pivot word vector and a document vector. This document vector is, in turn, modeled as a sparse mixture of latent topic vectors. Sparsity in the document-topic proportions is encouraged by adding a Dirichlet-likelihood term to the overall loss function. The authors leverage an automatic differentiation framework to implement and train this composite model.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel architecture that elegantly integrates a document-level topic model structure directly into the word-level SGNS training objective. This creates a shared embedding space where words, documents, and topics are directly comparable.
2.  A practical demonstration of how complex hybrid models, combining elements of neural networks and probabilistic topic models, can be implemented and explored without requiring bespoke inference algorithm derivations, by instead relying on automatic differentiation frameworks.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Simplicity:** The central mechanism of the model—defining the context vector as `~cj = ~wj + ~dj`—is an intuitive and elegant way to inject global, document-level information into the local, window-based prediction task of SGNS. This additive composition is well-motivated by prior work on the compositional properties of word vectors and provides a clean method for training all components jointly.
2.  **Unified Representation Space:** A significant advantage of the proposed model is the embedding of words, topics, and documents into the same high-dimensional space. This allows for powerful and interpretable analyses, such as finding the words most semantically similar to a learned topic vector, or comparing documents and topics directly. The qualitative examples in Figures 3, 4, and 5 effectively showcase this benefit.
3.  **Demonstration of Modern Tooling:** The paper serves as a compelling case study for the utility of modern deep learning frameworks like Chainer. By abstracting away the complexities of gradient calculation, such tools enable researchers to rapidly prototype and test novel hybrid architectures that would have previously required substantial effort in deriving custom inference procedures.

### Weaknesses

My recommendation is primarily driven by a significant number of methodological and presentational flaws, which collectively undermine the paper's contribution.

1.  **Critically Insufficient Quantitative Evaluation:** The experiments lack the rigor expected for a top-tier conference. The primary quantitative result, topic coherence on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset (Figure 2), is presented in a vacuum. Without comparison to any baseline models (e.g., standard LDA, other neural topic models), the reported scores are uninterpretable. It is impossible to know if a coherence of 0.567 is state-of-the-art or worse than a vanilla LDA implementation. The evaluation on the Hacker News corpus is entirely qualitative, with the authors excusing the lack of quantitative metrics due to a custom vocabulary. This is not a valid reason; intrinsic coherence could be calculated on the training corpus, or a baseline could have been trained on the same data for a fair comparison.
2.  **Unjustified Methodological Choices:** Several key modeling and training decisions are stated without adequate justification or analysis.
    *   The model uses the same vector representation for a word when it is a pivot versus a target, a deviation from the original SGNS. The implications of this choice are not discussed.
    *   The Dirichlet concentration parameter α is set to `n⁻¹` (where n is the number of topics). This is a non-standard and seemingly arbitrary choice. Why is α dependent on the number of topics? How does this compare to a small, fixed α?
    *   The Dirichlet loss weight λ is set to 200 based on the claim that it "works well." This value is critical to balancing the two loss terms, and its selection requires a sensitivity analysis or a more principled justification.
    *   The paper mentions dropout but dismisses its impact without presenting supporting data.
3.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Errors:** The manuscript is rife with formatting errors that betray a lack of care in its preparation.
    *   The "Conclusion" is incorrectly numbered as Section 3.3, appearing as a subsection of the "Experiments" section.
    *   Figures 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 are all clearly tabular data, yet they are formatted and captioned as "Figures," a fundamental violation of academic formatting standards.
    *   Figure 1 is cluttered, and its caption is minimal, failing to adequately explain the model components as a standalone figure should.
    *   The bibliography is in a state of extreme disarray. It contains parsing errors (`Blei et al\.,?`), inconsistent formatting, missing venue information (`Advances in . . . .`), and bizarre artifacts (`\Q2013\E`). The citation for Scikit-learn in the text (Pedregosa et al., 2012) does not match the broken entry in the bibliography (Brucher et al.,? 2012). Such carelessness in the bibliography, a cornerstone of academic scholarship, is deeply concerning.

### Questions to Authors

1.  To properly assess the performance of lda2vec, could you please provide topic coherence scores for at least one widely-used baseline model (e.g., Gensim's implementation of LDA) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, using the exact same preprocessing pipeline you employed?
2.  Could you provide a justification for setting the Dirichlet parameter `α = n⁻¹`? Have you experimented with a small, fixed `α` (e.g., 0.1), which is more common in the literature for encouraging sparsity, and how do the results compare?
3.  The model generates document-topic proportions `pjk` via a softmax transform on a latent vector and then applies a Dirichlet-based loss `Ld` to these proportions. From a probabilistic modeling perspective, this is a slightly unusual construction. Could you clarify the relationship between the latent vector being optimized and the application of a loss derived from a distribution (Dirichlet) that is typically used as a prior on parameters living on the simplex?
