

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper introduces "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines probabilistic topic modeling with distributed word representations. The core innovation lies in embedding document representations within the same space as word embeddings, while maintaining the interpretability of topic models through sparse document-topic assignments. The authors formulate a loss function that combines Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) with a Dirichlet-likelihood term to encourage sparse document-topic memberships. The model is trained using automatic differentiation, making it more accessible than traditional topic models that require custom inference algorithms.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. A novel framework that integrates LDA-style topic modeling with word embeddings in a unified model trained using automatic differentiation, significantly lowering the barrier to implementing topic models with semantic word representations.
2. The use of a Dirichlet prior with low concentration parameter to enforce sparse document-topic assignments, enhancing interpretability while maintaining semantic relationships.
3. Empirical validation demonstrating coherent topic discovery and learned semantic relationships in two distinct text corpora.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Implementation Clarity**: The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the model and its components. The integration of LDA's interpretability with word embeddings' semantic richness is well-motivated, and the use of automatic differentiation for training represents a practical advancement over traditional topic modeling approaches. The model's design effectively bridges the gap between sparse, interpretable topic representations and dense, semantically meaningful word vectors.

2. **Empirical Validation with Interpretability**: The paper demonstrates the model's ability to discover coherent topics in both the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets. The qualitative analysis of topics (e.g., "Space," "Encryption," "Housing Issues") shows meaningful interpretation, and the topic coherence scores (0.547-0.567) are competitive with standard LDA approaches. The demonstration of semantic relationships (e.g., "Silicon Valley - California + technology") shows the model successfully learns meaningful word vector relationships specific to the domain.

3. **Practical Implementation Benefits**: The authors emphasize the practical advantages of using automatic differentiation frameworks, which significantly reduce the implementation burden compared to traditional topic models. The paper's open-source code and clear documentation facilitate reproducibility and adoption, addressing a real limitation in the field where custom inference algorithms often hinder model development.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty and Comparison to Prior Work**: The paper's main contribution appears to be a practical implementation rather than a fundamental theoretical advance. The model closely resembles the LDA2Vec approach described in previous work (e.g., the 2016 paper by K. Chen et al.), and the paper fails to adequately distinguish its contributions from existing work. Without a thorough comparison to LDA2Vec or other hybrid models, it's difficult to assess the novelty or superiority of the proposed approach.

2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison**: The paper evaluates performance using topic coherence scores but lacks meaningful comparisons to state-of-the-art topic models or hybrid approaches. The coherence scores (0.547-0.567) are comparable to standard LDA but without comparison to more recent methods like BERT-based topic models or other neural topic models, it's unclear whether the approach offers significant improvements. The absence of quantitative comparisons to other hybrid models weakens the empirical claims.

3. **Ambiguous Justification for Key Design Choices**: The paper uses a Dirichlet prior with α = n⁻¹ to induce sparsity, but provides no theoretical justification or ablation studies demonstrating why this specific parameter choice is optimal. The paper states that "without this sparsity-inducing term... topics become incoherent," but doesn't provide evidence showing how much improvement the Dirichlet term provides over alternative approaches to sparsity.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your implementation specifically differ from the LDA2Vec model proposed by Chen et al. (2016)? What specific limitations of LDA2Vec does your approach address?

2. The paper reports topic coherence scores but doesn't compare them to other topic models (e.g., LDA, Neural Topic Models, BERT-based approaches). Could you provide a comparison to at least two strong baselines to better contextualize your results?

3. The paper states that α = n⁻¹ "works well" for inducing sparsity, but provides no ablation studies or theoretical justification. Could you elaborate on how you determined this parameter value and what happens if it's varied?

4. The Hacker News corpus evaluation doesn't report topic coherence scores because of vocabulary differences with Palmetto. Could you explain why the vocabulary difference is so significant, and whether you attempted to adapt the evaluation method?

This work presents a practical implementation of a hybrid topic model that successfully combines interpretability with semantic richness. While the technical execution is sound and the implementation benefits are clear, the lack of meaningful comparison to prior work and insufficient novelty justification limit its contribution to the field. The empirical results are promising but need stronger contextualization to demonstrate significant advancement over existing approaches.
