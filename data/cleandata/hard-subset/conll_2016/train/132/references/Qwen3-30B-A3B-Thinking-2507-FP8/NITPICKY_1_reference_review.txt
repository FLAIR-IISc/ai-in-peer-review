

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper proposes "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines word embeddings (specifically Skipgram Negative Sampling) with Dirichlet-optimized topic mixtures to create interpretable document representations. The authors claim their model preserves the semantic regularities of word embeddings while yielding sparse, interpretable document-topic proportions similar to LDA. The paper presents experiments on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News comments corpus, reporting topic coherence scores and demonstrating word analogies.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A model that combines Skipgram Negative Sampling with Dirichlet-regularized topic mixtures
2. Demonstration of topic coherence on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset
3. Evidence that semantic relationships can be learned in a specialized corpus

## Strengths
1. The model design is conceptually sound, with a clear connection between document vectors and topic mixtures that preserves the additive property of word vectors. The intuition that document vectors can be constructed by summing topic vectors is well-motivated.

2. The paper demonstrates a practical implementation of a hybrid model using automatic differentiation frameworks, which is valuable for reproducibility and accessibility. The open-source code availability is a positive contribution.

3. The topic coherence results on Twenty Newsgroups are reasonable (0.547-0.567), which aligns with typical LDA performance on this dataset. The qualitative topic analysis provides meaningful insights into the discovered topics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical formatting errors in the paper**: The manuscript contains multiple formatting errors that would break the provided URLs (e.g., "https:// github.com/cemoody/lda2vec" with an unnecessary space). These are basic presentation errors that undermine the paper's credibility and suggest poor proofreading. This is a significant issue for a conference paper.

2. **Inconsistent notation and terminology**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout (e.g., "pij" in text vs. "pjk" in equations). The term "document weights" is used interchangeably with "document proportions," which are distinct concepts in topic modeling. The inconsistent use of "lda2vec" vs. "lda2vec" further adds to the confusion.

3. **Lack of proper comparison to related work**: The paper fails to acknowledge or compare with Wang et al.'s LDA2Vec (2015), which is nearly identical to the proposed model. The authors claim their work "extends distributed word representations," but this is essentially a reimplementation of a previously proposed method without proper attribution or differentiation.

4. **Methodological inconsistencies**: The paper claims to mix Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings, but the Dirichlet component is only used as a regularization term (Ld), not as part of the generative model. This is misleading because traditional Dirichlet topic models use a Dirichlet prior as part of the generative process. The paper doesn't clarify this fundamental difference.

5. **Incomplete experimental evaluation**: The Hacker News corpus results lack topic coherence scores because the tokenization procedure differs from Palmetto's requirements. This is a major limitation that the authors don't acknowledge. Additionally, the paper doesn't provide any ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the model (e.g., the Dirichlet regularization term).

## Questions to Authors

1. How does lda2vec differ from Wang et al.'s LDA2Vec (2015), which is nearly identical in approach? Why wasn't this work cited and compared?

2. The paper uses "pij" in the text but "pjk" in equations. Which notation is correct, and why is there inconsistency?

3. The paper claims to use "Dirichlet-optimized sparse topic mixtures," but the Dirichlet component is only used as a regularization term. How does this differ from simply adding a sparsity penalty to a standard LDA2Vec model?

4. Why were the topic coherence scores for the Hacker News corpus not reported, given that the authors emphasize topic coherence as their primary evaluation metric?

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a model that appears to function as claimed, the numerous minor flaws—particularly the formatting errors that would break the provided URLs, the inconsistent notation, the lack of proper comparison to related work, and the methodological misrepresentation—collectively undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too numerous and significant to overlook, even though the model itself is conceptually sound.

The paper fails to acknowledge prior work that is nearly identical to the proposed method, contains multiple formatting and notational errors, and presents incomplete experimental results. These issues, while individually minor, accumulate to a paper that does not meet the standards expected for a CoNLL submission.

**Recommendation: Reject**
