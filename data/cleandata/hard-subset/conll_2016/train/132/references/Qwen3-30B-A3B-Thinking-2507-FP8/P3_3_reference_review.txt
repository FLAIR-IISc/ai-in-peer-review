

# Peer Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces lda2vec, a hybrid model that combines the interpretability of traditional LDA topic models with the semantic richness of word embeddings. The main contribution is a simple yet effective approach to learn document representations as sparse mixtures of topic vectors while simultaneously capturing semantic relationships between words through a modified Skipgram architecture. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on two text corpora (Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News comments) with qualitative topic analysis and semantic regularity demonstrations.

## Strengths

1. **Elegant Integration of Two Paradigms:** The paper successfully bridges the gap between traditional topic models and neural word embeddings. The key insight that document vectors can be represented as mixtures of topic vectors (with sparsity enforced via Dirichlet prior) while maintaining a common embedding space for words, topics, and documents is particularly compelling. This allows the model to preserve both interpretability (through sparse topic mixtures) and semantic richness (through learned word embeddings).

2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The evaluation strategy is well-justified, using topic coherence as the primary metric (validated by Röder et al., 2015 to correlate with human evaluations). The qualitative analysis of topics in both datasets (Figures 3-6) provides strong evidence of the model's effectiveness. The Hacker News results particularly demonstrate the model's ability to capture domain-specific semantic relationships, with word analogies (Figure 6) showing meaningful relationships like "Silicon Valley" = "California" + "technology."

3. **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide a clear, concise implementation in Chainer that leverages automatic differentiation and GPU acceleration. The simplicity of the approach (building upon existing Skipgram architecture) makes it accessible for practitioners while still yielding meaningful results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparative Baselines:** The paper fails to compare against state-of-the-art neural topic models (e.g., LDA-VAE, Neural Topic Models, or Deep Autoregressive Networks). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether lda2vec offers significant improvements over existing approaches. For example, the authors should compare against models that explicitly combine topic modeling with word embeddings, such as the work by Wang et al. (2018) or the recent Neural Topic Model (NTM) variants.

2. **Insufficient Methodological Detail on Key Components:** The Dirichlet term (Equation 5) is presented as a simple addition to the loss, but the paper lacks crucial implementation details. How exactly is Ld incorporated into the optimization process? The paper states "the Dirichlet likelihood term Ld is typically computed over all documents," but it's unclear how this is scaled for minibatch training. Furthermore, the justification for setting α = n-1 (where n is the number of topics) is not theoretically sound—this appears to be a heuristic choice without proper justification.

3. **Theoretical Underpinnings Lacking:** While the paper demonstrates empirical success, it lacks theoretical justification for why the proposed approach works well. The assumption that document vectors can be meaningfully represented as sums of topic vectors (with the same dimensionality as word vectors) is not supported by evidence or theory. The connection between the Dirichlet prior and the sparsity of document-topic weights is also not rigorously established, making it difficult to understand why this approach outperforms alternatives.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does lda2vec compare to recent neural topic models (e.g., LDA-VAE, NTM) in terms of topic coherence, computational efficiency, and interpretability? Please provide quantitative comparisons.

2. Could you clarify how the Dirichlet loss term (Equation 5) is incorporated into the overall loss function during minibatch training? Specifically, how is the term scaled to maintain consistent optimization across different batch sizes?

3. The paper states that document vectors are "summed together to form a context vector" (Equation 3), but it's unclear how this affects the optimization process. What is the computational complexity of this approach compared to standard Skipgram?

## Overall Assessment

lda2vec presents an interesting and practical approach to combining the strengths of topic modeling and word embeddings. The model's simplicity and the quality of the qualitative results (particularly in the Hacker News corpus) are commendable. However, the lack of comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods and insufficient theoretical justification for key design choices significantly weaken the paper's contribution. While the empirical results are promising, the paper would benefit from a more rigorous evaluation against existing approaches and a clearer explanation of the model's design rationale.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that hybrid approaches can yield interpretable document representations with semantic richness, but it would be stronger with more comprehensive comparisons and theoretical grounding. The work is promising but needs more substantial evidence to establish its novelty and superiority over existing methods.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with significant revisions required to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the lack of comparative baselines and insufficient methodological detail).
