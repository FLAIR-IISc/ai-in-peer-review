

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper introduces "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic models with word embedding techniques to create interpretable document representations that also preserve semantic relationships between words. The core idea is to extend the skip-gram architecture from word2vec by incorporating document-level information through a mixture of topic vectors, with sparsity enforced via a Dirichlet likelihood term.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. A novel hybrid model that successfully integrates LDA's interpretability with word2vec's semantic richness through a unified vector space.
2. A method for enforcing sparse document-topic mixtures using a Dirichlet likelihood term with a low concentration parameter.
3. Demonstration of the model's ability to learn both interpretable topics and meaningful semantic relationships in specialized corpora.

## Strengths

1. **Successful integration of complementary strengths**: The paper effectively bridges two different paradigms (topic modeling and word embeddings) to create a model that offers both interpretability (through sparse topic mixtures) and semantic richness (through word vector relationships). The authors demonstrate that their model can produce coherent topics while still capturing meaningful semantic relationships, which is a significant achievement. The use of a Dirichlet likelihood to enforce sparsity (rather than just using softmax) is particularly well-motivated and addresses a key limitation of previous approaches.

2. **Strong empirical validation across multiple domains**: The paper evaluates the model on both standard (Twenty Newsgroups) and domain-specific (Hacker News comments) corpora, showing the model's versatility. The topic coherence scores (Figure 2) are comparable to or better than standard LDA, and the qualitative analysis in Figures 3-6 demonstrates the model's ability to discover meaningful topics and semantic relationships. The Hacker News corpus results are especially impressive as they show the model can learn domain-specific relationships (e.g., "Silicon Valley" being similar to "California" and "technology").

3. **Clear demonstration of semantic regularities**: Figure 6 provides compelling evidence that the model learns meaningful word analogies specific to the domain (e.g., "Bitcoin" being a digital currency, "Node.js" enabling server-side JavaScript), which is a strong validation of the semantic richness preserved in the model. This goes beyond typical topic modeling and demonstrates the value of the hybrid approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper fails to compare with recent neural topic models that also combine topic modeling with word embeddings, such as the original LDA2Vec (which appears to be the same model), Neural Topic Models (NTM), or other recent approaches. The paper claims "this work takes advantage of the Chainer framework" but doesn't demonstrate how this specific implementation offers advantages over existing implementations. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the model offers significant improvements over existing approaches.

2. **Questionable novelty**: The core idea of combining LDA with word embeddings by summing document and word vectors (Equation 3) has been explored in previous work, notably in the original LDA2Vec paper (which predates this submission). The paper doesn't acknowledge this prior work or explain how this implementation differs from previous approaches. The authors claim "this work takes advantage of the Chainer framework" but this is a technical implementation detail rather than a substantive contribution.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper lacks ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the model. For example, it doesn't show how the model performs without the Dirichlet sparsity term, or without the document-topic mixture. Without these studies, it's difficult to assess which components are truly essential for the model's performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper appears to be describing the same model as the original LDA2Vec paper (which was presented at the 2016 Workshop on Representation Learning at ICML). Could you clarify how this work differs from the original LDA2Vec paper and what specific contributions this paper makes that weren't present in the prior work?

2. The topic coherence scores reported (0.55-0.71) are similar to those reported in standard LDA implementations. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with traditional LDA (with the same number of topics) to demonstrate that your model actually improves upon standard topic modeling?

3. The paper mentions that "the topic basis are also strongly affected" when sparsity is not enforced, but doesn't provide any quantitative evidence of this. Could you provide specific metrics showing how the coherence or interpretability of topics changes when the Dirichlet term is removed?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper presents a well-motivated hybrid model that successfully combines topic modeling with word embeddings, and the empirical results are generally convincing. However, the lack of meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art models and the questionable novelty (due to the close resemblance to prior work) significantly weaken the contribution. The paper would be much stronger with a proper comparison to recent neural topic models and a clearer explanation of how this work advances beyond existing approaches.

The authors should address the questions raised above, particularly regarding the novelty of the approach compared to prior work and the specific advantages over existing models. Without these clarifications, it's difficult to assess the paper's contribution to the field.
