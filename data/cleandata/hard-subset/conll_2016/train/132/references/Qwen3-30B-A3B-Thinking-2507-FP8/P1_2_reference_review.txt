

# Peer Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines aspects of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic models with Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) word embeddings. The key innovation is a model that jointly learns word vectors, topic vectors, and document-topic proportions in a shared embedding space, while preserving the interpretability of LDA topic models.

The main contributions of this work (as I see them, not as the authors present them) are:

1. A practical implementation of a hybrid model that successfully combines the interpretability of topic models with the semantic richness of word embeddings, using automatic differentiation frameworks to simplify development.
2. Empirical validation showing that the model can discover coherent topics in standard datasets while simultaneously learning meaningful semantic relationships between words.
3. Demonstration that the model can be effectively applied to specialized corpora (Hacker News comments) to capture domain-specific topics and semantic relationships.

## Strengths

1. **Practical implementation leveraging modern frameworks**: The paper makes a strong case for using automatic differentiation frameworks to simplify the development of probabilistic models. By using Chainer (and implicitly modern frameworks like PyTorch/TensorFlow), the authors demonstrate that model prototyping can be significantly accelerated without requiring complex derivations of inference procedures. This is a significant practical contribution that lowers the barrier to entry for researchers wanting to experiment with hybrid models. The open-source implementation further enhances the practical value of the work.

2. **Effective integration of LDA and word embeddings**: The paper provides a clear and well-motivated integration of LDA's interpretability with word embeddings' semantic richness. The key insight that document vectors can be represented as mixtures of topic vectors while maintaining the additive properties of word vectors (Figure 1) is compelling. The empirical results (Figures 3-6) convincingly demonstrate that the model can learn both interpretable topics and meaningful semantic relationships, which is a significant achievement in the field of topic modeling.

3. **Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks**: The model achieves high topic coherence scores (0.567 for 20 topics, β=0.75) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, which correlates with human evaluations of topic quality (Röder et al., 2015). The qualitative analysis of discovered topics (Figure 3) shows that the model can recover meaningful topics that align with the known newsgroup categories, demonstrating that the model produces interpretable results.

4. **Demonstration of domain-specific semantic learning**: The Hacker News corpus experiments (Figures 4-6) provide compelling evidence that the model can capture domain-specific semantic relationships and topics. The ability to learn word analogies specific to the Hacker News community (Figure 6) shows that the model is not just learning general semantic relationships but can adapt to specialized vocabularies, which is a significant strength for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art models**: The paper fails to provide a thorough comparison with recent topic models that also incorporate word embeddings or semantic information. For example, the paper does not compare with Neural Topic Models (NTM), Deep Embedded Topic Models (DET), or other hybrid approaches. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether lda2vec offers significant advantages over existing approaches. The paper's claim that their model "extends distributed word representations by including interpretable document representations" would be much stronger with a comparison to similar approaches.

2. **Limited novelty of the core model architecture**: While the integration of LDA and word embeddings is interesting, the core model architecture bears significant resemblance to existing approaches like Doc2Vec. The document vector as a sum of word vectors and topic vectors is not a new concept. The Dirichlet constraint on topic assignments is also standard in topic modeling. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing work, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the contribution.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper does not provide a thorough ablation study to understand the contribution of each component of the model. For example, the authors don't examine how the model performs without the Dirichlet likelihood term (Ld), or how the document-vector summing contributes to the model's performance. Without these studies, it's hard to determine which aspects of the model are essential for its success.

4. **Limited evaluation beyond topic coherence**: While the paper uses topic coherence (Cv) as an evaluation metric, this is a limited measure of topic model quality. The paper doesn't provide downstream task performance (e.g., document classification accuracy) or human evaluation of topic interpretability, which would provide stronger evidence of the model's practical utility.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't address potential limitations of the approach, such as how the model handles short documents, out-of-vocabulary words, or extremely large datasets. It also doesn't discuss computational requirements or training time compared to other topic models, which is important for practical adoption.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more comprehensive comparison with recent topic models that incorporate semantic information (e.g., Neural Topic Models, Deep Embedded Topic Models, etc.)? How does lda2vec compare in terms of topic coherence, computational efficiency, and interpretability?

2. The paper states that "the model can learn word analogies specific to the Hacker News corpus" (Figure 6). How do these analogies compare to those learned by standard word2vec models trained on the same corpus? What specific aspects of the corpus make these analogies meaningful?

3. The Dirichlet likelihood term (Ld) is crucial for achieving sparse topic assignments. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how this term affects the learning process and what happens when it's removed or modified? An ablation study would be valuable to understand this component's importance.

4. How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary words during inference, and how does this compare to other topic models? What is the computational cost of training and inference compared to traditional LDA or other topic models?

5. The paper mentions using "pretrained values found in Mikolov et al. (2013)" for word vectors on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, but initializes randomly on Hacker News. Why was this difference made, and how did it affect the model's performance?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a well-motivated and well-executed hybrid model that successfully combines the interpretability of LDA with the semantic richness of word embeddings. The empirical results are promising, and the implementation is practical and well-documented. However, the paper's lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art models and insufficient differentiation from existing work (particularly Doc2Vec) limits the perceived novelty of the contribution. The absence of ablation studies and limited evaluation metrics also weaken the paper's claims.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses identified in this review, particularly by providing more comprehensive comparisons with existing models and conducting ablation studies to better understand the model's components. The core idea is valuable, but the paper needs to more clearly establish its novelty and advantages over existing approaches.
