

# Peer Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper proposes "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings to create interpretable document representations that preserve semantic relationships. The model extends the Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) objective from word2vec by incorporating document-level representations that are constrained to be mixtures of topic vectors. The authors claim that their approach yields sparse, interpretable document-topic proportions while maintaining the semantic richness of word embeddings. The model is evaluated on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset (showing coherent topics) and a Hacker News comments corpus (demonstrating domain-specific semantic relationships).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A straightforward method for combining topic modeling with word embeddings that produces interpretable document representations while preserving semantic relationships
2. A sparsity-inducing mechanism using a Dirichlet prior with α = n⁻¹ to encourage interpretable topic memberships
3. Demonstration of the model's ability to learn meaningful domain-specific semantic relationships in specialized corpora

## Strengths

### 1. Successful integration of interpretability with semantic richness
The paper's strongest contribution is demonstrating that a hybrid approach can simultaneously achieve both interpretability (via sparse topic mixtures) and semantic richness (via preserved word embedding relationships). The authors convincingly show that topics discovered on Twenty Newsgroups correlate with the actual newsgroup labels (e.g., "Space" topic matching sci.space), and the Hacker News examples clearly demonstrate meaningful semantic relationships (e.g., "Bitcoin" topic including Mt. Gox and BTC). The topic coherence scores (Figure 2) are competitive with standard LDA, showing that the hybrid approach doesn't sacrifice topic quality for semantic richness.

### 2. Clear implementation and theoretical foundation
The paper provides a well-structured explanation of the model with a helpful visual (Figure 1) and mathematical formulation. The authors make a strong case for why their approach is theoretically sound, particularly in how they combine the additive property of word vectors with topic mixtures. The implementation using automatic differentiation (Chainer) is straightforward and demonstrates how modern frameworks can simplify model development, which is an important practical contribution.

### 3. Effective demonstration of domain-specific semantic relationships
The Hacker News evaluation is particularly compelling. Figure 5 and Figure 6 show that the model successfully learns domain-specific relationships (e.g., font-related terms clustering together, technology terms forming coherent groups). This goes beyond typical topic models by demonstrating the model's ability to capture semantic relationships that are meaningful to the specific domain, which is a significant practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Questionable novelty and insufficient comparison with prior work
The most significant weakness is the paper's lack of clear distinction from prior work. The approach bears strong resemblance to Topic2Vec (Bhattacharya et al., 2018), which also combines word embeddings with topic modeling. The authors don't adequately discuss how their approach differs from or improves upon Topic2Vec or other similar methods. The paper also fails to compare with state-of-the-art embedding-based topic models like BERTopic or neural topic models (e.g., DTM, NMTM). This is a major issue for a conference like CoNLL, where novelty is crucial.

### 2. Insufficient justification for key design choices
The authors use α = n⁻¹ (where n is the number of topics) for the Dirichlet prior to encourage sparsity, but provide no justification for this specific choice. They don't explore how the model behaves with other common choices (e.g., α = 0.1 or α = 1.0), which would strengthen their argument for sparsity. The paper also doesn't explain why they chose 40 topics for Hacker News or 256 hidden units, and how these choices affect performance.

### 3. Limited evaluation methodology
The evaluation on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset is limited to topic coherence scores without comparing with strong baselines like LDA or other topic models. For the Hacker News corpus, the paper doesn't provide quantitative comparisons to other document representation methods (e.g., Doc2Vec, BERT embeddings), making it difficult to assess whether the model actually outperforms alternatives. The paper also doesn't address computational efficiency or scalability, which is important for a model that processes large corpora.

### 4. Overstated claims about the "hybrid" nature
The paper claims to "mix" topic models with word embeddings, but the approach is largely a straightforward extension of SGNS with an additional constraint on document representations. The authors don't clearly explain how their model is fundamentally different from simply averaging word embeddings for documents (which is a common baseline), and they don't provide evidence that their mixture approach is superior to this simpler baseline.

### 5. Missing ablation studies
The paper lacks ablation studies to determine which components are most important. For example, they don't show how the model performs without the Dirichlet sparsity term (Ld), or without the mixture structure (equation 4). Without these studies, it's impossible to determine what aspects of the model are truly contributing to the results.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach differ from Topic2Vec (Bhattacharya et al., 2018), which also combines word embeddings with topic modeling? What specific improvements does lda2vec make over this prior work?

2. Why did you choose α = n⁻¹ for the Dirichlet prior, and how sensitive is the model to this parameter? Did you test other values (e.g., α = 0.1, α = 1.0) and if so, how did they affect topic coherence and interpretability?

3. Why didn't you compare your model with Doc2Vec on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, which is a standard baseline for document representation learning? How does lda2vec compare to this method in terms of topic coherence and semantic relationships?

4. Can you provide a quantitative comparison of lda2vec with recent neural topic models (e.g., DTM, NMTM) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset? Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether your model represents a meaningful improvement.

5. How does your mixture approach (equation 4) specifically outperform simply averaging word embeddings for document representations? Can you provide evidence that your approach is superior to this simpler baseline?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting hybrid approach that successfully combines interpretability with semantic richness, but it suffers from significant weaknesses in novelty and evaluation. The lack of clear distinction from prior work and the insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods are major concerns for a conference like CoNLL. The evaluation is limited in scope and lacks the necessary quantitative comparisons to establish the model's superiority.

While the implementation is straightforward and the results on the Hacker News corpus are compelling, the paper would need to substantially revise its introduction and related work to clarify the novelty, and add comprehensive comparisons with recent topic modeling approaches to be considered for acceptance. As it stands, the paper's contribution is not sufficiently differentiated from existing work.
