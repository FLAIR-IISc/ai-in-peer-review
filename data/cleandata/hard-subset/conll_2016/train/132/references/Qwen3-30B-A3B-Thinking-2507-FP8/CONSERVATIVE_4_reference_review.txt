

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper introduces lda2vec, a hybrid model that combines traditional Dirichlet-based topic models (LDA) with distributed word representations. The authors claim to create a model that preserves the interpretability of LDA while incorporating the semantic richness of word embeddings. The model extends Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) by incorporating document vectors and topic mixtures, with a Dirichlet prior to encourage sparse topic assignments. The paper evaluates the model on Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News comment datasets, reporting topic coherence scores and demonstrating word analogies.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A straightforward integration of LDA topic representations with word embedding techniques
2. An implementation of this hybrid approach using automatic differentiation frameworks
3. Demonstrations of the model's ability to learn coherent topics and semantic relationships

## Strengths
1. **Well-organized implementation**: The paper provides clear descriptions of the model architecture and implementation details, with code available online. The model is well-structured and the description of the components (word vectors, document vectors, topic vectors) is clear.

2. **Demonstrated coherence on standard dataset**: The model achieves reasonable topic coherence scores (0.55-0.71) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, which is comparable to results reported in the literature for traditional LDA. The topics discovered (e.g., "Space," "Encryption") are semantically coherent and match the expected newsgroup categories.

3. **Validation of semantic relationships**: The paper shows that the model can learn meaningful semantic relationships within the Hacker News corpus (e.g., "Silicon Valley" ≈ "California" + "technology"), demonstrating that the embedding space preserves semantic regularities.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of meaningful novelty**: This work does not present a fundamentally new approach but rather combines existing techniques (LDA + word embeddings) in a straightforward manner. The paper fails to properly distinguish itself from prior work such as Neural Topic Models (Miao et al., 2016) or Deep Embedding for Topic Modeling (Wang et al., 2017), which already combine neural networks with topic modeling. The claim that "crafting a new probabilistic topic model requires deriving a new approximation" is misleading, as this paper's approach is a straightforward extension of existing methods rather than a novel model.

2. **Insufficient baseline comparison**: The paper does not compare to strong neural topic models or even to traditional LDA with the same preprocessing pipeline. The reported topic coherence scores (0.55-0.71) are within the range expected for LDA on these datasets (typically 0.5-0.7), but without proper baselines, it's impossible to determine if this approach offers any meaningful improvement over existing methods.

3. **Methodological concerns with the Dirichlet prior**: The paper uses α = n-1 (where n is the number of topics) without justification, which is problematic. The Dirichlet prior with α < 1 encourages sparsity, but the authors don't explain why α = n-1 specifically works better than other standard values (e.g., α = 0.1 or 1.0). The paper lacks ablation studies to show how different values of α affect performance.

4. **Overstated claims about interpretability**: The paper claims that lda2vec "yields sparse and interpretable document-to-topic proportions in the style of LDA" but provides no evidence that these representations are actually more interpretable than LDA's. The paper compares topic coherence scores (which measure semantic coherence) rather than interpretability, which is a different metric.

5. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to two datasets with only coherence metrics. There's no analysis of computational efficiency, scalability, or performance on downstream tasks (e.g., document classification), which would better demonstrate the utility of the approach.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does lda2vec compare to recent neural topic models (e.g., NTM, DTM) on standard topic modeling benchmarks?
2. Why was α = n-1 chosen for the Dirichlet prior? What evidence supports this specific value over other standard choices?
3. What specific advantages does lda2vec offer over traditional LDA with the same preprocessing pipeline?

## Additional Comments
The paper's title suggests a "new" approach, but the method is essentially a straightforward combination of existing techniques rather than a novel contribution. The authors fail to adequately justify why this particular integration is valuable, particularly when there are already multiple neural topic models that have been rigorously evaluated. The paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of how this approach differs from prior work and why it represents an advancement over existing methods.

The claim that "crafting a new probabilistic topic model requires deriving a new approximation" is misleading. While it's true that developing new topic models can be challenging, the paper's approach is not a new probabilistic model but rather a hybrid of existing components. The paper would be stronger if it had demonstrated a clear advantage of this hybrid approach over either pure LDA or pure neural topic models.

Given the high standards of the conference, I recommend rejection. The paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous evaluation to justify publication. The approach is incremental rather than innovative, and the evaluation does not provide convincing evidence of significant improvement over established methods.
