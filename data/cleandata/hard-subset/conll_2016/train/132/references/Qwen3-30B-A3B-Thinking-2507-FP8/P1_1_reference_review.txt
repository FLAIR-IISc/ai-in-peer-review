

# Peer Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper introduces "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines aspects of Dirichlet topic models (like LDA) with word embeddings (like word2vec). The core idea is to extend the skip-gram architecture of word2vec by incorporating document-level topic representations. Specifically, the model uses a context vector formed by summing a pivot word vector and a document vector (which itself is a mixture of topic vectors), while adding a Dirichlet likelihood term to encourage sparse document-topic memberships. The authors evaluate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News comments corpus, demonstrating that it produces interpretable topics and captures meaningful semantic relationships.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel integration of LDA's document-topic sparsity with word embedding techniques, producing interpretable document representations that maintain semantic relationships.
2. A practical implementation approach that leverages automatic differentiation frameworks, making the model accessible to practitioners without requiring complex custom approximations.
3. Empirical demonstration that the model learns meaningful topics and semantic relationships in both general and domain-specific corpora.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Integration of Two Paradigms**: The paper successfully bridges the gap between probabilistic topic modeling (with its interpretability advantages) and distributed word representations (with their semantic richness). The architectural design of adding document vectors to context vectors is well-motivated and clearly explained, with Figure 1 providing an excellent visual summary. This is a significant contribution as it addresses the fundamental tension between interpretability and semantic richness in document representation.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The model achieves competitive topic coherence scores on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset (0.567 with 20 topics, β=0.75), which the authors correctly note correlates with human evaluations. The qualitative analysis of topics (Figure 3) provides strong evidence that the model discovers meaningful themes that align with the known newsgroup categories. The Hacker News evaluation (Figures 4-6) further demonstrates the model's ability to learn domain-specific topics and semantic relationships, showing it can solve word analogies relevant to the domain (e.g., "Silicon Valley" - "California" + "technology").

3. **Practical Implementation Approach**: The paper effectively demonstrates that the model can be implemented using standard automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer), which significantly lowers the barrier to adoption. This is a major practical contribution, as traditional topic models require complex approximations that are difficult to implement, while neural topic models often require substantial custom code. The authors' emphasis on the implementation simplicity ("the practitioner can focus development time on the model design rather than the model approximations") is well-supported by their results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Topic Models**: The paper fails to compare with recent neural topic models (e.g., Neural Topic Models, Dynamic Topic Models, or other hybrid approaches) that have been shown to achieve strong performance on topic coherence metrics. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed model offers meaningful improvements over existing methods. For example, the paper doesn't compare with the Neural Topic Model (NTM) by Miao et al. (2016) or the Dynamic Topic Model (DTM) by Blei and Lafferty (2006), which are standard baselines in topic modeling research.

2. **Limited Analysis of Model Behavior and Sensitivity**: The paper provides minimal analysis of how the model behaves under different parameter settings. While they report topic coherence for different numbers of topics and β values, they don't provide:
   - A thorough analysis of how the Dirichlet concentration parameter α affects performance
   - Sensitivity analysis of the model to the number of hidden units or dropout rates
   - A comparison of training time and scalability with LDA or other topic models
   This lack of analysis makes it difficult to understand the model's limitations and optimal usage.

3. **Reproducibility Concerns**: While the authors mention that "the code for lda2vec is available online," they don't provide a specific URL for the implementation. The reference to "https://github.com/cemoody/lda2vec" appears to be incomplete (the actual URL would need to be provided). Without a clear code repository link and detailed hyperparameter specifications (e.g., exact learning rates, batch sizes, number of epochs), it would be challenging for others to reproduce the results, which is a critical issue in computational research.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "lda2vec is simple to implement in automatic differentiation frameworks" but doesn't provide a comparison of implementation complexity with other topic models (e.g., LDA, NTM). Could you provide a rough estimate of the lines of code required to implement lda2vec versus a standard LDA or Neural Topic Model implementation?

2. The paper doesn't compare with recent neural topic models (e.g., NTM, DTM) that have been shown to achieve high topic coherence scores. Could you add a comparison with these state-of-the-art methods in your revision?

3. In Figure 2, the topic coherence scores for β = 0.75 are slightly higher than for β = 1.0, but the paper doesn't provide a detailed explanation for why this is the case. Could you elaborate on the relationship between the negative sampling exponent and topic coherence?

4. The Dirichlet concentration parameter α is set to n-1 (where n is the number of topics), but the paper doesn't explain why this specific choice is optimal. Could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing how topic coherence varies with different α values?

5. The paper mentions that the Hacker News corpus is "nearly fifty times the size of the Twenty Newsgroups corpus," but doesn't discuss how the model scales with corpus size or whether the model would require additional computational resources for larger corpora. Could you address this in your revision?

## Additional Comments
The paper's writing is generally clear, and the figures (particularly Figures 1, 3, 4, and 5) are well-designed to illustrate the model's behavior. The references to related work are appropriate, though the comparison with other hybrid approaches could be strengthened. The authors should ensure that the code repository URL is complete and accessible in the final version.
