

# Peer Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper introduces lda2vec, a hybrid model that combines traditional Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings. The core idea is to represent documents as sparse mixtures of topic vectors while simultaneously learning word embeddings, using a Skipgram Negative Sampling objective. The model uses a context vector formed by summing document and word vectors, with document vectors constrained to be mixtures of topic vectors using a Dirichlet prior to enforce sparsity. The authors evaluate the model on Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets, reporting topic coherence and demonstrating semantic relationships within the learned vector space.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A practical implementation demonstrating how to combine topic models with word embeddings using modern automatic differentiation frameworks
2. A demonstration that sparse, interpretable topic representations can be learned within a unified embedding space
3. An empirical validation showing the model can learn meaningful semantic relationships within specialized corpora

## Strengths

1. **Practical implementation using automatic differentiation frameworks**: The paper correctly identifies that modern automatic differentiation frameworks simplify the development of probabilistic models. The implementation using Chainer (with GPU support) is well-documented and accessible, lowering the barrier for researchers to experiment with hybrid models. This is particularly valuable given that traditional topic model development requires deriving new approximations.

2. **Competitive topic coherence results**: On the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, lda2vec achieves topic coherence scores (0.567 with 20 topics) that are comparable to state-of-the-art topic models. The qualitative analysis (Figure 3) shows that the model successfully identifies meaningful topics corresponding to the original newsgroup categories.

3. **Demonstration of semantic relationships**: The model successfully learns meaningful word analogies (Figure 6) within the Hacker News corpus, showing that the combined model preserves semantic regularities. This demonstrates that the embedding space is not only useful for topic modeling but also for capturing linguistic relationships.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty and insufficient comparison to prior work**: The paper fails to clearly differentiate itself from existing hybrid models. The core idea of combining topic models with word embeddings is not novel—prior work such as Topic2Vec (Singh et al., 2019) and Doc2Vec (Le & Mikolov, 2014) already explore similar concepts. The paper doesn't adequately discuss how lda2vec differs from these approaches or why it's superior. Without proper comparison to state-of-the-art hybrid topic models, the claimed contribution is significantly overstated.

2. **Inadequate evaluation and ablation studies**: The evaluation is limited to two datasets with minimal quantitative comparison to other methods. The Hacker News corpus evaluation lacks topic coherence measures (despite using Palmetto in the Twenty Newsgroups evaluation), making it impossible to objectively assess model performance. Additionally, there are no ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component (e.g., the Dirichlet prior, the document-topic mixture architecture).

3. **Insufficient technical details**: The paper lacks critical implementation details necessary for reproducibility. For example, it doesn't specify how the topic vectors are initialized, what the exact dimensionality of the topic vectors is, or how the hyperparameters (α = n⁻¹, λ = 200) were selected. The claim about computational speed improvements is unsupported by any quantitative evidence.

4. **Overstated claims about interpretability**: While the paper claims to produce "interpretable representations," the interpretability is largely dependent on the quality of the topic coherence metric. The paper doesn't address potential pitfalls of topic coherence metrics or whether the topics truly match human interpretations beyond the Twenty Newsgroups case. The claim that "the existence of so many non-zero weights makes interpreting the document vectors difficult" is not substantiated with empirical evidence comparing to other models.

5. **Limited theoretical justification**: The paper doesn't provide a compelling theoretical argument for why the specific architecture (document vector = word vector + topic mixture) should outperform simpler alternatives. The Dirichlet prior for sparsity is a standard technique in topic modeling, not a novel contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does lda2vec fundamentally differ from prior work like Topic2Vec (Singh et al., 2019) or Doc2Vec (Le & Mikolov, 2014)? What specific technical innovations does this paper introduce?

2. Why were topic coherence scores not reported for the Hacker News corpus, despite using Palmetto for the Twenty Newsgroups dataset? Could you provide quantitative evaluation for this larger corpus?

3. The paper claims that "this work takes advantage of the Chainer framework to quickly develop models" but doesn't provide any evidence of computational speed improvements. Could you provide timing comparisons with traditional LDA implementations?

4. What ablation studies were conducted to determine the contribution of each component (e.g., the Dirichlet prior, the document-topic mixture architecture)? How would the model perform without these components?

5. The paper states "the best topic coherences were achieved with n = 20 topics and with negative sampling power β = 0.75" but doesn't explain why these specific values are optimal. Could you provide more insight into this hyperparameter selection?

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a practical implementation of a hybrid topic model that combines the interpretability of traditional topic models with the semantic richness of word embeddings. The implementation is well-executed and the results on standard datasets are promising. However, the paper fails to establish sufficient novelty compared to prior work and lacks comprehensive evaluation and technical details. The contributions are largely incremental rather than transformative, and the claims about novelty and advantages over existing methods are overstated.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art hybrid topic models, comprehensive ablation studies, and more detailed implementation information. Without these, the paper's contribution is limited to providing another implementation of a well-established modeling approach rather than advancing the state of the art.
