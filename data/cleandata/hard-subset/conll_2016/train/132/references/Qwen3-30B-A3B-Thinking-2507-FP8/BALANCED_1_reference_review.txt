

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper introduces "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines elements of traditional Dirichlet topic models (like LDA) with neural word embedding approaches (like word2vec). The key innovation is a simple modification to the skip-gram negative sampling objective that incorporates document-level representations as mixtures of topic vectors. The authors claim their model produces both interpretable topic representations (like LDA) and dense word vectors (like word2vec) in a shared space.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**
1. A simple yet effective integration of Dirichlet topic models with word embedding techniques that produces interpretable topic representations while maintaining semantic relationships between words.
2. A novel implementation of document-topic mixture proportions that enforce sparsity through a Dirichlet likelihood term, making the topic representations more interpretable.
3. Demonstration of the model's effectiveness on both general and specialized corpora, showing meaningful topic discovery and semantic relationships.

## Strengths

1. **Elegant Integration of Two Paradigms**: The core idea of summing document and word vectors to form a context vector is simple, well-motivated, and effectively bridges the gap between traditional topic models and neural embeddings. The paper clearly explains how this approach preserves both short-term word relationships and long-term document themes. The implementation is straightforward to implement using automatic differentiation frameworks, which lowers the barrier for adoption.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The model achieves competitive topic coherence scores (0.55-0.71) on both the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and the Hacker News corpus. The qualitative topic examples (Figures 3-5) are convincing, showing that the model can discover meaningful topics (e.g., "Space," "Encryption," "Housing Issues") that align with domain knowledge. The demonstration of semantic regularities (Figure 6) shows the model successfully captures relationships specific to the Hacker News community.

3. **Practical Implementation and Reproducibility**: The authors provide a well-documented open-source implementation, which is a significant strength for the NLP community. The paper includes sufficient technical details for replication, including preprocessing steps, hyperparameters, and evaluation metrics. The model's simplicity compared to other neural topic models is highlighted as a practical advantage.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Novelty and Comparison**: The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing neural topic models. The authors mention the neural topic model (NTM) by Miao et al. (2015) but don't clearly explain how lda2vec differs or improves upon it. Without a direct comparison to state-of-the-art neural topic models (e.g., NTM, VATEX), it's difficult to assess the contribution's significance. The claim that "crafting a new probabilistic topic model requires deriving a new approximation" mischaracterizes the field, as automatic differentiation has been used in topic modeling for several years.

2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate which components of the model are essential. For example, how does the model perform without the Dirichlet sparsity term (Ld)? What is the impact of varying α values? Without these studies, it's unclear whether the Dirichlet term is necessary or whether the model would perform similarly with other sparsity mechanisms.

3. **Limited Evaluation of Model Capabilities**: The paper focuses primarily on topic coherence and qualitative topic examples but doesn't address key metrics important for topic models, such as topic diversity, topic stability across training runs, or performance on downstream tasks. The lack of comparison with other topic models on these metrics makes it difficult to assess the model's true value.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does lda2vec specifically differ from the neural topic model (NTM) by Miao et al. (2015) and other recent neural topic models? What are the key advantages of your approach over these existing methods in terms of interpretability, performance, and computational efficiency?

2. The paper states that "the model is simple to implement in automatic differentiation frameworks." Could you provide more specific details about how much easier it is to implement compared to other topic models that use automatic differentiation? What specific implementation challenges does your approach avoid?

3. The paper reports topic coherence scores but doesn't compare them to other topic models (neural or traditional). How do these coherence scores compare to those of LDA, NTM, and other recent topic models on the same datasets? Are there specific scenarios where lda2vec outperforms these models?

## Additional Comments

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how lda2vec relates to prior work. The authors should clearly articulate the novelty of their approach in the introduction and literature review. Additionally, the paper would be strengthened by including comparisons to other neural topic models and providing ablation studies to validate the necessity of key components.

The model's potential limitations regarding out-of-vocabulary words and handling new documents (which are common challenges in topic modeling) are not addressed. The paper would benefit from discussing these aspects.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and practical approach to combining topic modeling with word embeddings, but it would need to better establish its novelty and provide more comprehensive evaluation to be accepted at a top-tier NLP conference.
