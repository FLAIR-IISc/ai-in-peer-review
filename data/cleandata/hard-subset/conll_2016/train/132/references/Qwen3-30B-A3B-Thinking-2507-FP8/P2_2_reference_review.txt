

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper introduces "lda2vec," a model that combines Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings in a unified framework using automatic differentiation. The key innovation is modifying the Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) objective to incorporate document-level topic information, where the context vector is the sum of a document vector and a word vector. The document vector is constrained to be a mixture of topic vectors with sparse weights enforced via a Dirichlet prior. The authors evaluate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and Hacker News Comments corpus, demonstrating coherent topics and preserved semantic relationships.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel integration of Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings in a unified framework that leverages automatic differentiation for efficient implementation.
2. A method to enforce sparse document-topic proportions through a Dirichlet prior, making topic representations interpretable while preserving semantic relationships.
3. Empirical validation that the model can learn both coherent topics and meaningful word relationships in two different corpora.

## Strengths

### 1. Novel Integration of Two Paradigms
The paper successfully bridges probabilistic topic modeling and distributed word representations in a principled way. The core innovation of using document vectors as mixtures of topic vectors while preserving the additive properties of word embeddings is well-motivated and clearly explained. The model architecture (Figure 1) effectively demonstrates how the components interact, and the paper correctly identifies the key insight that "what words ~wi are most similar to any given topic vector ~tk can be directly calculated."

### 2. Strong Empirical Results with Qualitative Validation
The paper demonstrates good topic coherence (0.567) on Twenty Newsgroups, which correlates with human evaluations as shown in Röder et al. (2015). The qualitative analysis of topics (Figure 3) is convincing, with topics aligning well with the actual newsgroup categories (e.g., "Space" related to sci.space, "Encryption" related to sci.crypt). The Hacker News evaluation (Figures 4-6) further demonstrates the model's ability to capture domain-specific topics and semantic relationships, showing that the model learns meaningful word analogies ("Silicon Valley" - "California" + "technology", "Bitcoin" - "digital currency").

### 3. Simple and Effective Implementation
The paper provides a clear explanation of the model and its implementation using automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer). The approach is straightforward to implement and demonstrates how modern frameworks can simplify topic model development by handling the approximations. The authors correctly note that "crafting a new probabilistic topic model requires deriving a new approximation, a procedure which takes substantial expertise," and show how automatic differentiation mitigates this problem.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Lack of Comparison with Existing Work
The paper fails to compare with other hybrid topic models that have been proposed, particularly the LDA2VEC model from the authors' own previous work (presented at ICML 2016). The authors cite "LDA2VEC: Deep Topic Models" in the references but don't cite it in the paper itself. This is a significant omission that undermines the novelty claim. The paper also doesn't compare with other recent topic models such as Neural Topic Models or Deep Semantic Topic Models.

### 2. Limited Evaluation Metrics and Baselines
The paper relies solely on topic coherence as the evaluation metric, which is good but insufficient. There's no comparison with standard LDA or other topic models in terms of topic coherence, topic diversity, or interpretability. The paper claims to "demonstrate that model inference can be performed and extended within the framework of automatic differentiation," but doesn't provide any evidence of computational efficiency gains compared to traditional topic models.

### 3. Lack of Ablation Studies
The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show the impact of key components. For example, it doesn't demonstrate how the model performs without the Dirichlet regularization (Ld), with different values of α, or with different numbers of topics. Without this, it's difficult to determine which aspects of the model are truly contributing to the performance.

### 4. Reproducibility Issues
While the authors mention "our software is open source, available online, documented and unit tested," they don't provide a specific URL in the paper. The GitHub repository is mentioned in a footnote but not in the main text, making it difficult for readers to locate. The paper also lacks details on hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, etc.) that would be necessary for reproduction.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper appears to be describing the LDA2VEC model that was presented at ICML 2016 by the same authors. Why is this paper being submitted as a new contribution without citing the earlier work? How does this work differ from the earlier LDA2VEC model?

2. The paper claims "the document weights pij are sparsified by optimizing the document weights with respect to a Dirichlet likelihood with a low concentration parameter α" but doesn't compare with other sparsity-inducing techniques (e.g., L1 regularization). What evidence do the authors have that this specific approach is superior to other methods?

3. The paper states that "the topic coherence has been demonstrated to correlate with human evaluations of topic models (Röder et al., 2015)" but doesn't provide any human evaluation results. Why wasn't human evaluation conducted to complement the coherence metric?

4. The paper mentions "the document and word vectors are summed together to form a context vector" but doesn't provide any analysis of the relative contributions of document and word vectors to the final representations. How much of the model's performance comes from the document vector versus the word vector?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated and clearly explained model that successfully combines topic modeling with word embeddings. The empirical results are strong and the qualitative analysis is convincing. However, the lack of comparison with existing work, particularly the authors' own prior LDA2VEC work, significantly undermines the novelty claim. The limited evaluation metrics and lack of ablation studies also weaken the contribution. 

The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing with existing hybrid topic models, providing ablation studies, and addressing the reproducibility issues. Without these, the contribution appears incremental rather than innovative.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject**

The paper has merit but the weaknesses, particularly the lack of comparison with existing work and the failure to acknowledge prior related work, are too significant to justify acceptance. The authors should address these issues before the paper could be considered for acceptance.
