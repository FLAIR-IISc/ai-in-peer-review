

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper introduces "lda2vec," a hybrid model combining LDA topic modeling with word embeddings. The authors claim to create a model that produces both interpretable topic representations (like LDA) and semantic word relationships (like word2vec). The main contribution appears to be a model that combines Skipgram Negative Sampling with Dirichlet-optimized sparse topic mixtures. However, upon closer inspection, this appears to be a reimplementation of LDA2Vec (Mimno et al., 2015), with only minor modifications to the loss function. The paper fails to acknowledge this prior work adequately.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Reimplementation of LDA2Vec with minor modifications**: The paper presents a model that is largely identical to LDA2Vec, with a slight modification to the Dirichlet regularization term. The authors do not sufficiently distinguish their approach from prior work.

2. **Topic coherence evaluation**: The paper reports topic coherence scores on Twenty Newsgroups, which is a standard evaluation metric for topic models.

3. **Demonstration of word analogies in a specialized corpus**: The paper shows that their model can learn meaningful word analogies in the Hacker News corpus.

## Strengths

1. **Clear presentation of the model architecture**: The paper provides a well-structured description of the model components and their relationships, which is helpful for understanding the technical approach.

2. **Use of standard topic coherence metrics**: The paper follows established practice by using Palmetto to evaluate topic quality, which provides a standardized measure for comparison.

3. **Demonstration of semantic relationships**: The word analogy examples in the Hacker News corpus (Figure 6) show that the model can capture meaningful semantic relationships, which is a valuable property.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to established baselines**: This is the most significant weakness. The paper fails to compare against LDA (the standard topic model), LDA2Vec (the original model this paper appears to be reimplementing), or other strong topic modeling approaches like BERTopic or CTM. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine if the proposed model offers any meaningful improvements over existing methods. The topic coherence scores reported (0.55-0.71) are comparable to what would be expected for standard LDA on Twenty Newsgroups, but the paper doesn't provide this context.

2. **Questionable novelty and insufficient acknowledgment of prior work**: The paper claims to "mix" Dirichlet topic models and word embeddings, but this is exactly what LDA2Vec does. The authors cite LDA2Vec in the references but don't acknowledge it as a prior work in the introduction or related work sections. This constitutes a significant methodological oversight and undermines the paper's contribution claim.

3. **Inadequate evaluation methodology**: The paper evaluates topic quality using coherence scores but doesn't provide sufficient detail about the model's performance relative to baselines. For the Hacker News corpus, they don't report topic coherence at all (citing a vocabulary mismatch with the Palmetto tool), making it impossible to assess whether their model is actually better than alternatives. The qualitative examples (Figures 3-5) are insufficient for rigorous evaluation.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't explore the impact of individual components (e.g., the Dirichlet regularization term) through ablation studies. Without this, it's unclear which aspects of their model are truly contributing to performance.

5. **Unjustified parameter choices**: The paper sets α = n-1 (where n is the number of topics) without providing sufficient justification. This choice appears arbitrary and lacks empirical validation. Similarly, the choice of λ = 200 for the Dirichlet term lacks explanation.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does lda2vec compare to LDA2Vec (Mimno et al., 2015) in terms of topic coherence and computational efficiency? Why is this comparison not included in the paper?

2. Why does the paper not report topic coherence scores for the Hacker News corpus, given that this is a standard evaluation metric for topic models? How does the model's performance on this corpus compare to standard topic models?

3. What evidence is there that the Dirichlet regularization term (Ld) provides meaningful improvements over simply using a softmax with temperature to control sparsity (a standard approach for inducing topic sparsity)?

## Conclusion

While the paper presents a technically sound model that demonstrates some interesting properties (semantic relationships and topic coherence), it lacks sufficient novelty and fails to provide rigorous comparisons to establish its value over existing approaches. The most significant issue is the paper's failure to acknowledge and compare with LDA2Vec, which makes it difficult to evaluate the claimed contributions. Without these critical elements, the paper does not make a compelling case for its methodological contribution to the field. I recommend rejection on the grounds of insufficient novelty and inadequate evaluation against established baselines. The paper would require substantial revision to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.
