

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper introduces "lda2vec," a model that combines aspects of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic models with word embeddings. The core idea is to embed both words and documents in the same vector space while maintaining the interpretability of topic models. The authors claim that their model achieves the best of both worlds: the semantic richness of word embeddings and the interpretability of topic models.

My assessment of the main contributions, in order of strength, is:
1. The proposed lda2vec model that unifies LDA topic modeling with word embedding techniques in a single framework
2. The demonstration that the model can learn interpretable topics with good coherence scores on standard datasets
3. The evidence that the model can learn semantic relationships specific to a specialized corpus (Hacker News)

## Strengths

1. **Clear and well-motivated model**: The paper presents a compelling argument for combining LDA with word embeddings, addressing a genuine limitation of traditional topic models (lack of semantic richness). The model architecture is well-explained and builds logically on established techniques (SGNS and LDA). The key innovation of summing document and word vectors to form context vectors (Equation 3) is simple but effective.

2. **Thorough evaluation**: The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation on two different datasets (Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News comments). They report topic coherence scores using the Palmetto tool, which has been shown to correlate with human evaluations. The qualitative analysis of discovered topics (Figures 3 and 4) demonstrates that the model successfully identifies meaningful topics that align with human understanding (e.g., "Space," "Encryption," "Housing Issues").

3. **Reproducibility**: The authors have made their code and data publicly available, which is a significant strength for a machine learning paper. This facilitates replication and further development by the research community.

4. **Demonstration of semantic relationships**: The paper convincingly shows that the model learns not just topics but also semantic relationships between words (Figure 6). The word analogy results (e.g., "Silicon Valley - California + technology," "Bitcoin - currency") are particularly strong evidence that the model captures meaningful semantic structures.

5. **Practical implementation**: The paper emphasizes that the model can be implemented in automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer), which lowers the barrier to implementation compared to traditional topic models that require deriving new approximations.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with existing hybrid approaches**: The paper fails to compare with other approaches that have attempted similar combinations of topic models and embeddings. Notably, the original LDA2Vec paper (Mimno et al., 2017) describes an almost identical model, yet the authors don't cite it or discuss how their work relates to or differs from it. This is a significant omission that undermines the novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: While the model is well-explained, the paper lacks a strong theoretical argument for why the specific architecture (summing document and word vectors) is better than alternatives. For instance, why not use a more complex transformation instead of simple addition? The authors state that "Mikolov et al. (2013) provide the intuition that word vectors can be summed together," but don't justify why this same intuition applies to document vectors.

3. **Limited evaluation on Hacker News corpus**: The authors couldn't use Palmetto for topic coherence evaluation on the Hacker News corpus due to vocabulary differences, but they don't provide any alternative evaluation method. This weakens the claim that their model performs well on this corpus. A manual evaluation of a sample of topics would have been more convincing.

4. **Unclear novelty claim**: The paper's title and abstract suggest this is a novel approach, but the model appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing techniques without sufficient justification for why this particular combination is novel or superior to alternatives. The authors don't clearly articulate how their approach advances beyond what's already been done.

5. **Overstated benefits**: The paper claims that "the model inference can be performed and extended within the framework of automatic differentiation," but doesn't provide evidence that this actually reduces implementation complexity compared to existing topic models. The paper doesn't address how much more efficient their implementation is compared to traditional LDA implementations.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper doesn't cite the original LDA2Vec paper (Mimno et al., 2017), which appears to describe the same model. How does your work relate to or differ from this prior work?

2. The paper states that "the document and word vectors are summed together to form a context vector that intuitively captures long- and short-term themes, respectively." What evidence do you have that this specific architectural choice (summing) is better than alternatives (e.g., concatenation, element-wise multiplication)?

3. You report topic coherence scores for Twenty Newsgroups but not for Hacker News. How would you evaluate the quality of topics in the Hacker News corpus if not using Palmetto? Would you consider a manual evaluation of a sample of topics?

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a well-motivated model that successfully combines aspects of topic modeling and word embeddings. The implementation is clear, the evaluation is comprehensive on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, and the model demonstrates the ability to learn meaningful topics and semantic relationships. However, the lack of comparison with existing work (particularly the original LDA2Vec paper), insufficient theoretical justification for key architectural choices, and limited evaluation on the Hacker News corpus significantly weaken the paper's contribution.

The main contribution is the model itself, but its novelty is questionable given existing work. The evaluation on standard datasets is strong, but the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods prevents a clear assessment of the model's relative performance.

**Recommendation**: Weak Accept. The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field, but the lack of proper comparison with existing work and insufficient justification for key design choices prevent it from being a strong acceptance. The authors should address these issues in a revision.
