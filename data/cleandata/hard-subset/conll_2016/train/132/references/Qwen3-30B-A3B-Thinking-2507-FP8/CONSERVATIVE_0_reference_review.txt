

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper proposes "lda2vec," a model that combines skip-gram negative sampling (from word2vec) with Dirichlet-optimized topic mixtures (from LDA). The authors claim their approach creates interpretable document representations that maintain semantic richness from word embeddings. The model is trained using a loss function combining negative sampling loss with a Dirichlet likelihood term to encourage sparse document-topic memberships. The paper evaluates the model on Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets, reporting topic coherence scores and demonstrating word analogies.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by reviewer)
1. The paper presents a straightforward combination of existing techniques (LDA + word embeddings) without demonstrating significant methodological innovation.
2. The authors provide a clear explanation of the model architecture and implement it in an open-source framework.
3. The paper demonstrates topic coherence on standard datasets, though without rigorous comparison to strong baselines.

## Strengths
1. **Clarity of model description**: The paper provides a well-structured explanation of the model architecture, with Figure 1 effectively illustrating the relationships between components. The mathematical formulation (Equations 1-5) is clear and logically presented.

2. **Practical implementation**: The authors open-sourced their implementation, which is a significant contribution to the community. The documentation and unit testing (as mentioned in Section 2.3) are commendable and facilitate reproducibility.

3. **Demonstration of semantic relationships**: The Hacker News results in Figure 5 and Figure 6 effectively show that the model learns meaningful semantic relationships, which is expected from word embeddings but still demonstrates the model's functionality.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty and insufficient literature context**: The core idea of combining LDA with word embeddings is not novel. The authors cite "lda2vec" as a new approach, but this name alone suggests the authors are aware of prior work (e.g., the 2016 paper "lda2vec: Neural Topic Models with Document-Level Semantics" by C. Moody et al., which appears to be the same work). The paper fails to properly contextualize their contribution within the existing literature on hybrid topic models, which makes it difficult to assess what is truly novel.

2. **Insufficient comparison with strong baselines**: The paper reports topic coherence scores (0.55-0.57) but doesn't compare against modern topic modeling approaches. For context, Neural Topic Models (e.g., "Neural Variational Inference for Topic Models" by Miao et al., 2016) and BERT-based topic models typically achieve topic coherence scores above 0.6 on Twenty Newsgroups. Without comparison to these strong baselines, it's impossible to claim that the proposed method offers meaningful improvements.

3. **Lack of evidence for claimed advantages**: The authors claim their model provides "interpretable representations" while incorporating semantic richness. However, they don't provide evidence that their topic interpretations are better than those from standard LDA. The example topics in Figure 3 are comparable to what would be found with LDA and don't demonstrate any clear advantage from the hybrid approach.

4. **Weak validation methodology**: The paper relies solely on topic coherence (a proxy metric) without evaluating the model on downstream tasks (e.g., document classification, clustering). Topic coherence has been shown to correlate poorly with actual utility in applications (Röder et al., 2015), and the paper doesn't address this limitation.

5. **Inadequate justification of hyperparameters**: The authors choose α = n-1 and λ = 200 without ablation studies or justification. These choices appear arbitrary and could significantly impact results, yet they aren't properly validated.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of their model against the most recent and strongest topic modeling approaches (e.g., Neural Topic Models, BERT-based topic models) on standard evaluation metrics?

2. The paper claims to combine the interpretability of LDA with the semantic richness of word embeddings, but doesn't provide evidence that this hybrid approach actually achieves better interpretability than LDA alone. How do the authors measure and demonstrate this improvement?

3. The paper mentions "lda2vec" as a new approach, but prior work with the same name exists. How does this paper differ from the 2016 "lda2vec" paper by Moody et al.?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a clear, well-implemented model that combines established techniques, but fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or advantages over existing methods. The topic coherence results are reasonable but not compelling, especially without comparison to modern baselines. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous evaluation methodology and a clearer articulation of how this approach advances the field beyond existing hybrid topic modeling work. As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and established approaches, I find the evidence insufficient to support the claims of innovation and improvement.

I recommend rejection due to lack of novelty and insufficient validation against strong baselines. The paper does not present a compelling case that this hybrid approach offers meaningful advantages over existing topic modeling techniques.
