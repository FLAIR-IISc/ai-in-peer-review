

# Peer Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper proposes "lda2vec," a hybrid model that integrates the interpretability of LDA topic models with the semantic richness of word embeddings. The authors aim to create a model that maintains sparse, human-interpretable document-topic proportions while incorporating distributed word representations. The model extends the Skipgram Negative Sampling objective by adding document vectors that are mixed with word vectors and incorporating a Dirichlet likelihood term to encourage sparse topic memberships. The authors evaluate their model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News comments corpus, reporting topic coherence scores and demonstrating semantic relationships in the learned space.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Integration of LDA-style sparsity with word embedding semantics**: The paper successfully combines two distinct approaches to text representation (probabilistic topic models and distributed word representations) into a single framework that can be trained end-to-end using automatic differentiation.

2. **Demonstration of meaningful topic interpretation**: The model produces coherent topics on standard datasets that correspond to human-understandable themes, with topic coherence scores competitive with existing methods.

3. **Preservation of semantic relationships**: The paper demonstrates that the model preserves word-level semantic relationships (including analogies) within the learned vector space, extending the capabilities of word2vec to document-level representations.

## Strengths

1. **Thoughtful integration of two complementary paradigms**: The paper effectively bridges the gap between traditional topic modeling (with its interpretability advantages) and neural language models (with their semantic richness). The authors clearly articulate the motivation for this integration, noting that "probabilistic topic models tend to form documents as a sparse mixed-membership of topics while neural network models tend to model documents as dense vectors." This is a valuable contribution to the field, as it addresses a genuine limitation in both approaches.

2. **Strong empirical validation of interpretability**: The topic examples in Figures 3 and 4 clearly demonstrate that lda2vec produces meaningful topics that align with human understanding. For instance, the "Space" topic contains tokens like "astronomical," "Astronomy," and "telescope," while the Hacker News "Bitcoin" topic contains "btc," "Mt. Gox," and "Bitcoin." This is a significant strength, as interpretability is a key advantage of topic models over purely neural approaches.

3. **Effective demonstration of semantic regularities**: Figure 6 provides compelling evidence that the model preserves semantic relationships between words, with word analogies like "Silicon Valley" ≈ "California" + "technology" and "Jeff Bezos" - "Mark Zuckerberg" ≈ "Amazon" - "Facebook." This shows that the model doesn't just learn topics, but also meaningful semantic relationships, which is a strong point for the model's utility.

4. **Clear presentation of model architecture**: The paper's Figure 1 provides an excellent visual summary of the model's components and relationships. The explanation of how document vectors are mixed with word vectors to form context vectors is particularly well articulated.

5. **Practical implementation and availability**: The authors have made their code publicly available, which significantly increases the paper's utility to the research community. The model is straightforward to implement using modern automatic differentiation frameworks, as the authors note: "This work takes advantage of the Chainer (Tokui et al., 2015) framework to quickly develop models while also enabling us to utilize GPUs to dramatically improve computational speed."

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art topic models**: This is the most significant weakness. The paper does not compare with recent neural topic models like BERTopic, Neural Topic Models (NTMs), or other hybrid approaches. For instance, it doesn't compare with the work by Wang et al. (2019) on neural topic models with contextualized word embeddings, or with the more recent work by Liu et al. (2022) on topic modeling with transformer-based architectures. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether lda2vec offers meaningful improvements over existing methods.

2. **Insufficient distinction from prior work**: The paper claims to be a "hybrid approach," but fails to clearly differentiate itself from the earlier "lda2vec" work by K. M. et al. (2015), which is cited but not properly distinguished from this work. The authors should clarify what makes their approach novel compared to previous work with the same name.

3. **Limited evaluation of computational efficiency**: The paper doesn't provide any discussion of computational complexity or training time compared to traditional LDA or other topic models. Given that it's using deep learning techniques, it's likely significantly more computationally expensive than traditional LDA, which would be a major drawback for large-scale applications. The paper mentions using GPUs to "dramatically improve computational speed," but doesn't provide any actual speed comparisons.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component of the model. For example, it doesn't show what happens when the Dirichlet likelihood term is removed (which is crucial for sparsity) or when document vectors aren't mixed with word vectors. This makes it difficult to understand which parts of the model are essential for its performance.

5. **Overreliance on topic coherence as the sole evaluation metric**: While topic coherence is a standard metric, the paper doesn't address its limitations. As noted by Röder et al. (2015), topic coherence scores can sometimes correlate poorly with human evaluations of topic quality. The paper should have included qualitative analysis of the topics or evaluation on downstream tasks to complement the coherence scores.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does lda2vec compare to recent neural topic models like BERTopic or Neural Topic Models (NTMs) in terms of topic coherence, computational efficiency, and interpretability? What specific advantages does lda2vec offer over these approaches?

2. The paper cites "lda2vec" by K. M. et al. (2015), but doesn't clearly distinguish this work from the authors' own approach. Can the authors clarify the key differences between their model and the earlier "lda2vec" work?

3. The paper uses a fixed number of topics (n=20 for Twenty Newsgroups, n=40 for Hacker News). How does the model's performance vary with different numbers of topics, and what criteria do the authors use to select the optimal number of topics?

4. The paper claims the model is "simple to implement in automatic differentiation frameworks," but doesn't provide any detailed comparison of implementation complexity with traditional LDA or other topic models. Can the authors provide more specific details about the implementation advantages?

5. The paper doesn't discuss how the model handles out-of-vocabulary words. Given that it's based on word embeddings, how does it handle words that weren't seen during training? How does this compare to traditional LDA's handling of OOV words?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a valid contribution to the field by successfully integrating two complementary approaches to text representation, and the empirical results are promising. However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, insufficient distinction from prior work, and limited evaluation of computational efficiency represent significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before the paper can be considered a strong contribution to the field.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding comparisons with recent neural topic models, providing a clearer distinction from prior work, and including ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each model component. With these additions, the paper would make a more compelling case for its novelty and utility.
