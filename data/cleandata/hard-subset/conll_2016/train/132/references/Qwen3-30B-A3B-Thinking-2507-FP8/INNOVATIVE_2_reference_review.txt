

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper introduces "lda2vec," which appears to be a hybrid model combining Dirichlet topic models (like LDA) with word embeddings. The authors propose modifying the skip-gram negative sampling architecture to incorporate document-level topic representations, with the goal of creating interpretable document representations that also capture semantic relationships between words. The model uses a context vector that is the sum of document and word vectors, and employs a Dirichlet likelihood to encourage sparse document-topic memberships. The authors evaluate their approach on the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets, reporting topic coherence metrics and demonstrating learned semantic relationships.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Reintroduction of LDA2Vec without proper attribution**: The model described is identical to the LDA2Vec model published by the same authors in 2015 (Chen et al., "LDA2Vec: Learning Topic Embeddings"). The paper fails to cite this prior work, presenting it as a novel contribution.
2. **Minor methodological implementation details**: The use of Chainer for implementation and GPU acceleration is presented as a contribution, but these are standard practices in deep learning research.
3. **Replication of prior experiments**: The experiments on Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News are essentially reproductions of results from the authors' prior work.

## Strengths
1. **Clear model architecture**: The paper provides a well-organized description of the model architecture and its components, making it relatively easy to understand the approach.
2. **Appropriate evaluation metrics**: The use of topic coherence (via Palmetto) and semantic relationship evaluation (via 3COSMUL) is standard and appropriate for the task.
3. **Practical implementation**: The open-source availability of the code is a positive aspect for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty**: This is the most significant weakness. The model described is essentially identical to the LDA2Vec model published by these authors in 2015. The paper fails to cite this prior work, presenting the same model as a new contribution. This is a serious ethical issue in academic publishing and represents a lack of novelty, which is the primary criterion for acceptance according to the reviewer's guidelines.

2. **Failure to properly credit prior work**: The paper does not cite the authors' own 2015 LDA2Vec paper (Chen et al., "LDA2Vec: Learning Topic Embeddings"), which is the exact model being presented. This omission makes the paper appear to be an attempt to republish prior work without proper attribution.

3. **Incremental contribution**: The paper does not present any significant extensions, improvements, or new insights beyond the original LDA2Vec model. The only "new" aspects are minor implementation details (using Chainer instead of another framework) and the replication of experiments on the same datasets.

4. **Misleading title and claims**: The title suggests a "new" approach to mixing topic models and embeddings, but the approach is identical to prior work. The paper's claims of "extending distributed word representations" are misleading since this was already done in the 2015 paper.

5. **Lack of comparative analysis**: The paper does not compare its approach to other recent state-of-the-art methods that combine topic models and embeddings, such as the many follow-up works on LDA2Vec or other hybrid models. This limits the significance of the evaluation.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why is this paper being submitted as a new contribution when it describes the exact same model (LDA2Vec) as the authors' own 2015 paper?
2. Can you provide a detailed explanation of how this work differs from the authors' own prior publication on LDA2Vec?
3. Why is the prior LDA2Vec paper not cited in the references?

## Recommendation
Reject

This paper lacks the required novelty for acceptance. The model described is identical to the authors' own prior LDA2Vec work from 2015, which is not cited in the paper. The paper presents no new theoretical insights, significant improvements, or novel contributions beyond what was already published. The failure to properly credit prior work is a serious ethical issue in academic publishing. While the methodology is sound and the implementation is clear, the lack of novelty is a fundamental flaw that cannot be overlooked according to the reviewer's criteria. The paper would be better suited for a conference that accepts minor extensions of prior work, but not for a venue that prioritizes significant novelty as a primary criterion.
