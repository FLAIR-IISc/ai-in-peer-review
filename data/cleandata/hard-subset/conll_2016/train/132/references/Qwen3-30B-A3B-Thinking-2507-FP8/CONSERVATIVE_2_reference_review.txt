

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper proposes "lda2vec," a hybrid model that attempts to combine Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) with word embeddings by modifying the Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) objective. The authors claim their model produces interpretable topic representations while preserving semantic relationships captured by word embeddings. They evaluate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News comments corpus, reporting topic coherence scores and demonstrating word analogies.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A straightforward implementation of a hybrid topic model that combines LDA with word embeddings
2. Some topic coherence results on two datasets with limited baseline comparison
3. Code availability for the model

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a clear description of the model architecture, with Figure 1 effectively illustrating the relationships between components.
2. The authors make their code and preprocessed datasets publicly available, which is commendable for reproducibility.
3. The topic coherence scores on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset are reasonable (0.55-0.71), suggesting the model produces interpretable topics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty and insufficient literature context**: The core idea of combining topic models with word embeddings is not new. The paper fails to properly contextualize its contribution within the existing literature. For instance, Tang et al. (2015) already proposed "Topic2Vec," which is conceptually very similar, and more recent work like BERT-based topic models (e.g., Zhang et al., 2020) has advanced the field significantly. The paper's claim that "crafting a new probabilistic topic model requires deriving a new approximation" ignores the fact that many hybrid approaches have already been developed using modern automatic differentiation frameworks. Without a clear distinction from existing work, the contribution appears incremental at best.

2. **Insufficient experimental validation**: The paper lacks critical baseline comparisons. There is no comparison to standard LDA, or to other hybrid approaches like Topic2Vec. The topic coherence scores reported (0.55-0.71) are not contextualized against established baselines. For example, what is the topic coherence of LDA on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset? Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine if the proposed model actually improves upon existing methods. The paper claims "high" coherence, but without baseline comparisons, this is unsubstantiated.

3. **No ablation studies to validate model components**: The paper makes several claims about the necessity of specific components (e.g., the Dirichlet prior, the document-topic mixture), but provides no ablation studies to validate these claims. For instance, the paper states that "without any sparsity inducing terms the existence of so many non-zero weights makes interpreting the document vectors difficult," but fails to provide evidence that this is actually the case in their experiments. A proper ablation study would show the performance degradation when removing key components.

4. **Superficial evaluation of semantic relationships**: While the paper claims to "learn linear relationships between words that allow it to solve word analogies," the examples provided (e.g., "Silicon Valley is similar to both California and technology") are trivial and do not demonstrate meaningful semantic relationships. More importantly, the paper provides no comparison to word2vec or other embedding methods on standard word analogy tasks (like Google's Word Analogy Test), making it impossible to determine if the semantic relationships learned are actually better than those from established embedding methods.

5. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims that "lda2vec" "yields coherent topics" and "preserves semantic regularities between the learned word vectors," but these claims are not substantiated with proper comparisons. The paper also states that "this work demonstrates a simple model," but the implementation details suggest it's not particularly simpler than existing approaches. The paper's claims about the model's advantages are not supported by rigorous evidence.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide a detailed comparison of lda2vec's topic coherence scores with standard LDA and other hybrid models (e.g., Topic2Vec) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset? How does lda2vec compare to state-of-the-art topic modeling approaches like BERT-based methods?
2. What specific evidence do you have that the Dirichlet prior (with α = n-1) is necessary for achieving good performance? Have you conducted ablation studies to verify this?
3. Why did you choose to not report topic coherence scores for the Hacker News corpus (stating "this tokenization procedure generates a vocabulary substantially different from the one available in the Palmetto topic coherence tool")? How does this limitation affect your claims about the model's ability to produce coherent topics in specialized domains?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a modest extension of existing topic modeling approaches but fails to provide sufficient evidence that it offers meaningful improvements over established methods. The lack of proper baselines, ablation studies, and contextualization within the existing literature significantly weakens the contribution. While the code availability is commendable, the paper does not meet the high standards required for publication at CoNLL. I recommend rejection.

The authors would need to substantially strengthen their claims by providing comprehensive comparisons to relevant baselines, conducting ablation studies to validate their model components, and properly contextualizing their work within the existing literature before it could be considered for publication.
