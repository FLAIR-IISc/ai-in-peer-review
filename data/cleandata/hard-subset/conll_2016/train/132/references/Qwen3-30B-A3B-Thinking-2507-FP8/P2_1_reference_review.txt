

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings. The authors aim to bridge the gap between the interpretability of traditional topic models (like LDA) and the semantic richness of word embeddings. The key contributions, as I see them, are:

1. **A simple yet effective framework for embedding documents, topics, and words in a common vector space** that preserves semantic relationships while maintaining the sparsity and interpretability of topic models. This is achieved by representing document vectors as mixtures of topic vectors with Dirichlet regularization.

2. **Demonstration that semantic relationships between words can be preserved within a topic modeling framework**, as evidenced by the model's ability to solve word analogies in specialized corpora (e.g., "Silicon Valley - California + technology" = "technology").

3. **A practical implementation approach** using modern automatic differentiation frameworks that simplifies development compared to traditional topic models requiring custom inference algorithms.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation for the hybrid approach**: The paper clearly articulates the trade-off between interpretability (topic models) and semantic richness (word embeddings), which is a well-founded problem in NLP. The authors effectively demonstrate why this hybrid approach is needed, citing limitations of both traditional topic models (lack of semantic information) and neural network approaches (lack of interpretability). This is a solid foundation for the work.

2. **Competitive empirical results with strong qualitative analysis**: The topic coherence scores on Twenty Newsgroups (0.55-0.57) are competitive with other topic models. More importantly, the paper provides excellent qualitative examples of discovered topics (Figure 3) that clearly demonstrate the model's ability to capture meaningful themes. The Hacker News corpus results (Figure 4-6) further strengthen the argument by showing the model can learn specialized semantic relationships in a domain-specific context, which is impressive.

3. **Demonstration of semantic relationships within topic modeling**: The paper's most compelling contribution is showing that the model preserves semantic regularities (Figure 6). This goes beyond typical topic models, which typically don't capture such relationships. The examples like "Bitcoin - digital + currency" and "Node.js - server + client" demonstrate the model successfully integrates word embedding capabilities with topic modeling, which is a significant achievement.

4. **Practical implementation approach**: The authors emphasize how the model can be implemented using automatic differentiation frameworks like Chainer, which is a major practical advantage. This lowers the barrier to adoption compared to traditional topic models that require developing custom inference algorithms. The open-source code availability further enhances the practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison with related work**: The paper does not compare with existing hybrid models that share similar goals. For example, there is no comparison with the LDA2VEC model (which appears to be the same name but a different approach) or other recent neural topic models like Neural Topic Models (NTM) or Deep Embedded Topic Models (DET). Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess the novelty and relative performance of the proposed approach. The paper only compares with LDA, which is not a fair comparison since LDA doesn't incorporate word embeddings.

2. **Insufficient ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide an ablation study to determine which components are most important for the model's performance. For instance, how much does the Dirichlet regularization contribute versus the document-topic mixture? How does the model perform without the document vector component? Without this analysis, it's unclear which aspects of the model are truly novel or necessary for the observed improvements.

3. **Limited analysis of key parameter settings**: The paper sets α = n-1 for the Dirichlet prior to induce sparsity, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how different α values affect performance. A systematic exploration of α would strengthen the paper by showing how sensitive the model is to this key parameter and how to choose it appropriately.

4. **Confusing model naming**: The paper's title and model name "lda2vec" is problematic because there is already a well-known model called "LDA2Vec" (with a capital V) that uses a different approach. This naming conflict will cause confusion in the literature and may lead to misattribution of the work.

5. **No discussion of computational efficiency**: The paper mentions using GPUs for speed but doesn't provide any analysis of computational complexity or training time compared to other models. For a model that claims to be easily implementable with automatic differentiation, it would be valuable to know how it scales with corpus size and model complexity.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper's title and model name "lda2vec" conflict with the existing "LDA2Vec" model (with capital V). Can the authors clarify the relationship between their work and the existing LDA2Vec model? Did they build upon it, or is this a different approach?

2. The paper claims "the code for lda2vec is available online," but the GitHub link provided (https://github.com/cemoody/lda2vec) appears to be for a different implementation than the one described in the paper. Can the authors clarify if this is the correct codebase and provide any additional documentation?

3. The paper doesn't discuss how the model handles out-of-vocabulary words. How would the model perform on documents containing words not seen during training, and how does this compare to other topic models?

4. The paper mentions using "the pretrained values found in Mikolov et al. (2013)" for word vectors in the Twenty Newsgroups experiments but initializes randomly in the Hacker News corpus. Can the authors provide justification for this difference in initialization strategies?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a straightforward and practical approach to combining topic modeling with word embeddings, with strong empirical results that demonstrate the model's ability to produce interpretable topics while preserving semantic relationships. The key strength is the demonstration that semantic relationships can be maintained within a topic modeling framework.

However, the lack of meaningful comparisons with existing hybrid models and the absence of an ablation study are significant weaknesses that prevent a clear assessment of the model's novelty and contribution. The confusing naming convention is also problematic for the field.

Given these weaknesses, I would recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper has valuable insights and strong empirical results, but the lack of proper comparison with related work and insufficient analysis of the model's components prevents it from being a major contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions addressing the weaknesses, this could be a strong paper.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Adding a comparison with existing hybrid topic models (LDA2Vec, NTM, DET)
2. Including an ablation study to determine the contribution of each model component
3. Clarifying the naming conflict with the existing LDA2Vec model
4. Providing a more thorough analysis of the Dirichlet parameter α

These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and clarity.
