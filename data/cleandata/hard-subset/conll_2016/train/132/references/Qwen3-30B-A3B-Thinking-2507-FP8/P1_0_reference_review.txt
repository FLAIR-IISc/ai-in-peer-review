

# Peer Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary

This paper introduces "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines aspects of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic models with word embeddings (specifically, the Skipgram Negative Sampling approach from word2vec). The model aims to create document representations that are both interpretable (like LDA) and semantically rich (like word embeddings). The authors formulate the model as an extension of Skipgram Negative Sampling that incorporates document-level topic mixtures constrained by a Dirichlet prior to encourage sparse document-topic memberships. The model is trained using automatic differentiation, making it computationally efficient. The authors evaluate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset (reporting topic coherence scores) and the Hacker News comments corpus (showing topic discovery and semantic relationships).

## Main Contributions

1. A novel approach to combine the interpretability of LDA topic models with the semantic richness of word embeddings through a unified model architecture.
2. A practical implementation of this hybrid model that can be efficiently trained using automatic differentiation frameworks.
3. Empirical validation demonstrating that the model can discover coherent topics while preserving semantic relationships between words in specialized domains.

## Strengths

1. **Strong conceptual integration of two complementary paradigms**: The paper successfully bridges the gap between topic modeling and word embeddings, addressing a meaningful gap in the literature. The authors clearly articulate why this integration is valuable: "Probabilistic topic models tend to form documents as a sparse mixed-membership of topics while neural network models tend to model documents as dense vectors. By virtue of both their sparsity and low-dimensionality, representations from the former are simpler to inspect and more immediately yield high level intuitions about the underlying system." The model achieves both interpretability (via sparse document-topic proportions) and semantic richness (via preserved word relationships), which is a significant conceptual contribution.

2. **Clear, well-structured model description**: The paper provides an excellent explanation of the model architecture, including the mathematical formulation and visual representation (Figure 1). The authors clearly explain the role of each component (word vectors, document vectors, topic vectors) and how they interact. The description of how document vectors are constructed as mixtures of topic vectors (Equation 4) and the use of Dirichlet prior for sparsity (Equation 5) are particularly well-articulated.

3. **Thorough empirical evaluation**: The authors evaluate their model on two distinct datasets with different characteristics (Twenty Newsgroups, a standard text classification dataset, and Hacker News, a specialized large-scale corpus). The topic coherence scores reported (0.567 for 20 topics with β=0.75 on Twenty Newsgroups) are competitive with other topic models, and the qualitative analysis of topics (Figures 3-6) provides convincing evidence that the model discovers meaningful themes. The demonstration of semantic relationships (Figure 6) is particularly strong evidence that the model preserves word vector semantics.

4. **Practical implementation and reproducibility**: The authors provide open-source code, documentation, and unit tests. The paper demonstrates that the model can be implemented using modern automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer), which makes it accessible to practitioners without requiring specialized knowledge of model approximations. This practical contribution is significant for the community.

5. **Effective use of existing evaluation metrics**: The authors appropriately use topic coherence (Cv) as evaluated by the Palmetto tool, which has been shown to correlate with human evaluations of topic models (Röder et al., 2015). This provides a strong basis for evaluating the interpretability of their topics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty compared to prior work**: The paper cites LDA2Vec as prior work but does not sufficiently distinguish their approach from this prior work. The core model appears to be a direct extension of LDA2Vec with the addition of a Dirichlet prior for sparsity. The authors claim "this work takes advantage of the Chainer framework to quickly develop models," but this is not a substantive contribution to the model itself. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with LDA2Vec and other neural topic models to clarify what is truly novel in this work.

2. **Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art topic models**: The evaluation is limited to comparing with traditional LDA (implied but not explicitly shown) and the paper doesn't compare against other neural topic models or recent advances in topic modeling. For instance, the paper could have compared against Neural Topic Models (NTMs), which also combine neural networks with topic modeling, or other recent approaches like Deep Contextualized Topic Models. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed model offers advantages over existing approaches.

3. **Limited analysis of computational efficiency**: While the paper mentions using GPUs and automatic differentiation frameworks to improve computational speed, it provides no actual data on training time, memory usage, or scalability compared to other topic models. For a model that claims to be "dramatically improve computational speed," the lack of empirical evidence on computational efficiency is a significant weakness.

4. **Overstated claims about semantic preservation**: The paper claims the model "preserves semantic regularities between tokens" (as evidenced by the word analogy examples in Figure 6), but these examples are limited to the Hacker News corpus and don't demonstrate that the model outperforms standard word2vec on standard semantic tasks. The paper would be stronger with a comparison of the model's word vector quality against standard word2vec or GloVe on standard semantic tasks (e.g., word analogy benchmarks).

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper mentions trying "variations on dropout ratios" but doesn't report any results on how this affects performance. Similarly, the choice of α = n-1 for the Dirichlet concentration parameter is made without empirical justification. An ablation study showing how different values of α affect topic coherence would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper cites LDA2Vec as prior work but doesn't clearly explain how lda2vec differs from LDA2Vec in terms of model architecture or training procedure. Could you provide a detailed comparison between your model and LDA2Vec, specifically highlighting the novel aspects of your approach?

2. The paper claims to "preserve semantic regularities between tokens" but doesn't provide a quantitative comparison of the quality of word vectors learned by lda2vec against standard word embedding models (like word2vec or GloVe) on standard semantic tasks (e.g., word analogy benchmarks). Could you provide such a comparison?

3. The paper mentions using "the Adam optimizer" but doesn't report training times or memory requirements. Could you provide a table comparing training time and memory usage of lda2vec against traditional LDA and other neural topic models on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset?

4. The paper sets α = n-1 for the Dirichlet concentration parameter but doesn't justify this choice with empirical evidence. Could you provide an ablation study showing how different values of α affect topic coherence and interpretability?

5. The paper uses topic coherence (Cv) as the primary evaluation metric but doesn't include any human evaluation of the topics. Given that topic coherence is only a proxy for interpretability, could you provide some qualitative examples of how humans evaluate the topics discovered by lda2vec compared to other models?

## Additional Comments

The paper has a strong conceptual foundation and demonstrates a useful integration of two important NLP paradigms. However, the lack of clear novelty compared to prior work and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art models are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. The authors should strengthen their claims by providing a more thorough comparison with existing neural topic models and by providing empirical evidence for their computational efficiency claims. The paper would also benefit from more detailed ablation studies to justify key hyperparameter choices.

Overall, the work is interesting and potentially useful, but the claims of novelty and contribution need to be better supported with comparative experiments and a clearer distinction from prior work.
