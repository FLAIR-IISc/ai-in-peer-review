

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper proposes "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings to create interpretable document representations. The model extends the skip-gram architecture with negative sampling by incorporating document-level representations that are constrained to be sparse mixtures of topic vectors (similar to LDA). The authors claim their approach enables both semantic relationships between words and interpretable document-topic proportions.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel framework for combining LDA with word embeddings that enables interpretable document representations
2. A method for enforcing sparsity in document-topic proportions using a Dirichlet likelihood term
3. Demonstrations of the model's effectiveness on standard text corpora

## Strengths
1. The core idea of combining LDA with word embeddings is interesting and potentially valuable for creating more interpretable document representations.
2. The paper demonstrates reasonable topic coherence scores on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, with values (0.55-0.56) that are competitive with standard topic models.
3. The implementation is designed to be simple to deploy in automatic differentiation frameworks, which is a practical advantage.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical formatting and typographical errors**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. Figure 1 contains strange characters like " .34 -0.1 0.17" that appear to be artifacts. The mathematical expressions are poorly formatted (e.g., "L = Ld + ΣijLnegij (1) Lnegij = log σ(~cj · ~wi) + Σ n l=0 log σ(−~cj · ~wl)" has inconsistent spacing and missing spaces). References contain errors like "Blei et al\.,? 2003" instead of "Blei et al., 2003." These are not minor issues but serious problems that suggest poor attention to detail.

2. **Inconsistent notation and terminology**: The paper uses "n" to represent both the number of topics and the number of negative samples (e.g., "the number of negative samples is fixed to n = 15" in Section 2.1, while in Section 2.2.1, "n = 20 topics"). This is confusing and unprofessional. The paper also inconsistently capitalizes "lda2vec" (sometimes as "lda2vec," sometimes as "lda2vec").

3. **Lack of meaningful comparison to baselines**: The paper claims "best topic coherences were achieved with n = 20 topics and with negative sampling power β = 0.75" but fails to compare to standard LDA or other neural topic models. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed model actually improves upon existing approaches. The paper only reports coherence scores without context.

4. **Inconsistent evaluation methodology**: The paper states that for the Hacker News corpus, "topic coherences are not reported because the vocabulary is substantially different from the Palmetto tool." However, the paper does not explain how they evaluated topic quality for this corpus. They also report topic coherence scores for Twenty Newsgroups that are unusually high (0.712 for "Space" topic), which is suspiciously high for topic coherence measures.

5. **Incomplete technical explanation**: The paper claims "the document and word vectors are summed together to form a context vector" but doesn't explain why this sum is preferable to other approaches (e.g., concatenation or learned linear combination). The Dirichlet likelihood term is introduced without clear explanation of how it differs from standard LDA optimization.

## Questions to Authors

1. The topic coherence score for "X Windows" (0.472) is significantly lower than other topics (0.615-0.712). Is this due to poor topic quality, or is there a methodological issue with how this topic was evaluated?

2. The paper claims to use "whole noun phrases and entities at once" for the Hacker News corpus, but the topic tokens listed (e.g., "more housing," "basic income") appear to be single words, not phrases. Can you clarify this discrepancy?

3. The paper states "the document vectors ~dj, word vectors ~wi and topic vectors ~tk all have dimensionality 300" but then claims "the topic vectors ~tk are shared and are a common component to all documents." If topic vectors are shared across documents, how do they differ between documents? The document weights pjk modulate the topic vectors, but it's unclear how the topic vectors themselves change.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting idea, the numerous formatting errors, inconsistent notation, lack of meaningful comparison to baselines, and incomplete technical explanations significantly undermine the paper's credibility and contribution. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot overlook these fundamental issues that prevent a proper assessment of the model's novelty and effectiveness. The paper fails to meet the basic standards of presentation expected in a top conference like CoNLL. The authors should address all these issues before resubmission.
