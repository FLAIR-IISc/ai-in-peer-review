

# Peer Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that combines aspects of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling with word embedding techniques (specifically Skipgram Negative Sampling). The authors aim to bridge the gap between interpretability (offered by topic models) and semantic richness (offered by neural word embeddings). The model embeds words, topics, and documents in the same vector space, where document vectors are represented as sparse mixtures of topic vectors. The key innovation is the use of a document vector (summed with a word vector) as the context vector in the skip-gram framework, while enforcing sparsity in document-topic memberships via a Dirichlet prior.

The authors evaluate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset (using topic coherence as the primary metric) and the Hacker News comments corpus (demonstrating topic discovery and semantic relationships). They claim that their model produces both interpretable topic representations and meaningful word embeddings that capture semantic regularities.

## Main Contributions

1. **Hybrid Model Architecture**: The paper's primary contribution is the novel architectural design that integrates LDA's sparse topic mixtures with the skip-gram framework. The key innovation is using the sum of document and word vectors as context, which effectively combines local word relationships with global document-level semantics.

2. **Interpretable Document Representations**: The model produces document representations that maintain the interpretability of LDA (through sparse topic mixtures) while also enabling semantic similarity calculations (through the dense vector space).

3. **Demonstration of Semantic Regularities**: The paper convincingly shows that the model learns meaningful semantic relationships within a specialized domain (Hacker News), including word analogies that reflect domain-specific knowledge.

## Strengths

1. **Clear and Compelling Motivation**: The paper effectively articulates the limitations of both traditional topic models (lack of semantic information) and neural approaches (lack of interpretability), making a strong case for the need for a hybrid approach. The comparison between LDA's sparse, interpretable representations and neural models' dense, non-interpretable representations is particularly well-explained.

2. **Strong Evaluation on Standard Dataset**: The evaluation on Twenty Newsgroups is thorough and uses topic coherence (a well-established metric that correlates with human evaluations), with clear topic examples that are interpretable and match the underlying newsgroup categories (Figure 3). The results demonstrate that the model outperforms standard LDA in terms of topic coherence (Figure 2).

3. **Demonstration of Semantic Regularities**: The Hacker News corpus results (Figures 4-6) are compelling evidence of the model's capabilities. The ability to learn domain-specific semantic relationships (e.g., "Silicon Valley" = "California" + "technology," "Bitcoin" = "digital currency") shows that the model captures meaningful relationships beyond simple topic discovery.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a clear, open-source implementation with documentation and unit tests, making the model accessible to the community. The use of automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer) simplifies implementation and enables efficient training on GPUs.

5. **Intuitive Architecture**: The model's architecture is simple yet effective, with the document vector being the sum of the word vector and document vector. This design leverages the additive property of word vectors (e.g., "Germany + airline" = "Lufthansa") in a natural way that's easy to understand.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Hybrid Models**: The paper fails to compare with more recent hybrid topic models that also integrate word embeddings, such as Neural Topic Models (NTM), Deep Embedded Topic Model (DET), or LDA2VEC (which appears to be the same as the authors' previous work). This is a significant omission, as it weakens the claim of novelty. For instance, LDA2VEC (by the same authors) is mentioned in the references but not properly compared with in the evaluation.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification**: The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for the specific architectural choices. Why should document and word vectors be summed rather than multiplied or combined in another way? The intuition provided ("meaningful sums of word vectors") is somewhat vague and would benefit from a more rigorous justification or ablation studies.

3. **Missing Ablation Studies**: The authors don't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component. For example:
   - How much does the Dirichlet prior contribute to the quality of topics?
   - What happens if you remove the document vector and just use the word vector?
   - How does the model perform without the sparse mixture constraint (i.e., with α=1)?

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the model. For instance:
   - How does the model scale to extremely large corpora (beyond the 75 million tokens in Hacker News)?
   - How does it handle polysemy (words with multiple meanings)?
   - What is the computational complexity compared to standard LDA or word2vec?
   - How does it handle out-of-vocabulary words?

5. **Inadequate Discussion of Hyperparameters**: The paper mentions some hyperparameters (e.g., α = n-1, λ = 200) but doesn't discuss their sensitivity or provide evidence for why these specific values were chosen. A more thorough hyperparameter analysis would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to "make LDA and word embeddings work together," but it's unclear how this differs from the LDA2VEC model proposed by the same authors in previous work (e.g., the 2015 paper by the first author). Could you clarify the relationship between this work and your earlier LDA2VEC work?

2. The model seems to require training both word vectors and topic vectors simultaneously. How does this compare to methods that pre-train word embeddings (e.g., GloVe or word2vec) and then use them as features for topic modeling? Would the model benefit from initializing word vectors with pre-trained embeddings?

3. The paper uses topic coherence as the primary evaluation metric, which is a relatively weak measure. Could you provide additional evaluations, such as downstream task performance (e.g., document classification or clustering), to demonstrate the practical utility of the learned representations?

4. The paper mentions that document vectors are initialized to be homogeneous but become sparse over time. How does this initialization affect convergence speed and final performance? What happens if you initialize document vectors to be sparse from the beginning?

5. The Hacker News corpus has 75 million tokens, which is much larger than Twenty Newsgroups. How long did training take for the Hacker News corpus, and what hardware was used? How does this compare to training time for standard LDA or word2vec on the same dataset?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a simple yet effective hybrid model that successfully combines the interpretability of topic models with the semantic richness of word embeddings. The model architecture is intuitive, the evaluation is thorough for the standard dataset, and the demonstration of semantic relationships in the Hacker News corpus is compelling. However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art hybrid models and insufficient ablation studies significantly weaken the paper's contribution claim. With additional comparisons and a more thorough analysis of the model's components, this work could make a significant contribution to the field.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the condition that the authors address the weaknesses identified above, particularly the comparison with more recent hybrid topic models and the addition of ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component. The paper has strong potential but requires additional evidence to fully support its claims of novelty and effectiveness.
