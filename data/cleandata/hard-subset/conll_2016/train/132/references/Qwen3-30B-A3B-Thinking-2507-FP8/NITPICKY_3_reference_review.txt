

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper introduces "lda2vec," a hybrid model that combines Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) with Dirichlet-optimized sparse topic mixtures. The authors claim to create a model that produces both interpretable topic representations (like traditional LDA) and dense word embeddings (like SGNS), all trained jointly within an automatic differentiation framework.

The main contributions I identify (in order of importance):
1. A novel framework that integrates Dirichlet-optimized sparse topic mixtures with word embedding techniques, creating a model that produces both interpretable topic representations and meaningful word embeddings
2. An implementation of this model within an automatic differentiation framework (Chainer) that enables efficient training and GPU utilization
3. Demonstrations of the model's effectiveness on two text corpora (Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News comments) showing coherent topic discovery and semantic relationships

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Integration**: The paper successfully bridges two important paradigms in NLP - traditional topic modeling and distributed word representations. The core idea of using document vectors as a mixture of topic vectors (equation 4) is conceptually sound and well-explained.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a clear implementation in Chainer with open source code, which is valuable for reproducibility and community adoption. The use of automatic differentiation frameworks to avoid deriving new approximations for each model is a practical contribution.

3. **Evaluation Approach**: The use of Palmetto for topic coherence evaluation (Röder et al., 2015) is appropriate for the task, and the demonstration of semantic relationships (Figure 6) provides compelling evidence of the model's ability to learn meaningful vector representations.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Formatting and Notational Errors**: The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. Equation (5) has a space between α and 1 ("α− 1" instead of "α-1"), and Figure 1 contains repeated placeholder text ("sally said the fox jumped over the") that appears to be a copy-paste error. The reference section contains multiple instances of "Blei et al.,? 2003" with question marks, which is incorrect academic formatting. These errors suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

2. **Inconsistent Notation and Terminology**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. The negative sampling exponent is denoted as "β" in equation (2) but later as "beta" in the text. The number of topics is denoted as "n" in equation (4), but the paper also uses "n" for the number of negative samples. The model is called "lda2vec" in the title but "LDA2vec" in the body. Such inconsistencies make the paper difficult to follow.

3. **Lack of Baseline Comparisons**: The paper claims good topic coherence scores (e.g., 0.567 in Figure 2), but fails to compare these results to established topic models like LDA or other hybrid approaches. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether the improvements are significant or merely a result of the specific dataset.

4. **Unjustified Hyperparameter Choices**: The paper sets α = n-1 (where n is the number of topics) without justification. The choice of λ = 200 for the Dirichlet likelihood term is also arbitrary. The paper claims these choices "work well" but provides no empirical evidence or ablation studies to support them.

5. **Dataset Size Discrepancies**: The paper states the Twenty Newsgroups corpus has "11,313 documents" but the standard Twenty Newsgroups dataset has 11,314 documents. Similarly, the Hacker News corpus is described as containing "66 thousand documents" but the standard preprocessing for this dataset typically results in a different number. These small but significant errors suggest the authors may not have carefully verified their experimental setup.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "the best topic coherences were achieved with n = 20 topics and with negative sampling power β = 0.75" (Figure 2), yet Figure 3 reports topic coherences of 0.712, 0.675, etc., which are substantially higher than the 0.567 reported in Figure 2. Can you clarify this discrepancy?

2. The paper states "we set α = n-1 where n is the number of topics" but provides no justification for this choice. Why is α = n-1 better than other values? What ablation studies support this choice?

3. The paper claims to have achieved "high mean topic coherences" but doesn't compare these results to standard topic models. How do your results compare to LDA, LDA2Vec, or other state-of-the-art topic models on the same datasets?

## Final Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the core idea of combining topic modeling with word embeddings is interesting, the paper contains numerous formatting, notational, and methodological inconsistencies that significantly undermine its credibility. The lack of baseline comparisons makes it impossible to assess the true contribution of the proposed model. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance when the paper contains such fundamental errors that affect its readability and scientific validity.

The paper has potential, but it requires substantial revision to address these issues before it can be considered for publication. The authors should carefully proofread the manuscript, ensure consistent notation throughout, provide baseline comparisons, and justify their hyperparameter choices with empirical evidence.
