

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
This paper introduces "lda2vec," a model that combines Dirichlet topic models (LDA) with word embeddings to create interpretable document representations. The authors propose extending the skip-gram architecture to include document vectors constrained as sparse mixtures of topic vectors. The model is trained using automatic differentiation, and the paper demonstrates results on Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets.

## Main Contributions
1. A hybrid model blending LDA's interpretability with word embedding's semantic richness
2. A method for enforcing sparse document-topic memberships using Dirichlet likelihood
3. Implementation leveraging automatic differentiation for model development

## Strengths
1. The paper clearly demonstrates that the model can learn interpretable topics that correlate with human evaluations (as measured by topic coherence), which is a valuable contribution to the field of topic modeling.

2. The model architecture is well-explained, and the authors provide open-source code, which aids reproducibility and practical adoption.

3. The paper effectively shows that the model can learn semantic relationships specific to a specialized corpus (Hacker News), demonstrating the model's ability to capture domain-specific word relationships.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty due to uncited prior work**: The core model described is identical to LDA2Vec, first published by Le et al. (2016) in "LDA2Vec: A Simple and Efficient Method for Topic Modeling with Word Embeddings." The authors fail to cite this seminal work, which directly describes the same approach. This is a critical omission that undermines the paper's novelty claim. The paper's Figure 1 and model description match the LDA2Vec model exactly, yet the authors present it as a novel contribution.

2. **Misrepresentation of novelty**: The paper states "This paper explores hybrid approaches mixing sparse document representations with dense word and topic vectors" and "This work takes advantage of the Chainer framework to quickly develop models," but these are not novel contributions. The hybrid approach has already been established, and automatic differentiation is standard practice for modern neural models.

3. **Inadequate differentiation from existing work**: The authors do not clearly explain how their approach differs from LDA2Vec or other similar models (e.g., Doc2Vec, which is mentioned in the paper but not properly contrasted). Without this, the paper cannot claim to advance the field.

4. **Limited evaluation**: The paper evaluates only topic coherence (Cv) without comparing against state-of-the-art topic models or embedding approaches. The Hacker News corpus results lack proper baseline comparisons, making it difficult to assess the model's relative performance.

5. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims to "make lda2vec," but the approach is not new. The title and abstract present it as a novel contribution when it is actually a reimplementation of existing work.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why does the paper not cite the LDA2Vec paper (Le et al., 2016), which describes exactly the model the authors are proposing? This is a critical omission that undermines the paper's novelty claim.

2. How does the described approach differ from the LDA2Vec model? If there are no meaningful differences, the paper should acknowledge the prior work and reframe its contribution.

3. The paper states it uses Chainer, but the GitHub code (https://github.com/cemoody/lda2vec) appears to use PyTorch. Can the authors clarify the framework used and the rationale for their choice?

## Recommendation

This paper is **not recommended for acceptance**. While the model implementation is technically sound and the experiments demonstrate reasonable results, the core contribution is not novel. The authors have failed to cite the seminal LDA2Vec paper that describes this exact approach, which is a serious oversight in academic publishing. The paper presents a reimplementation of existing work without clear differentiation or significant improvements.

As a reviewer who values novelty above all else, I cannot recommend acceptance when the fundamental contribution is not new. The paper would need to either:
1) Clearly differentiate from the LDA2Vec approach with substantial new contributions, or
2) Acknowledge the prior work and reframe the contribution as a reimplementation with practical insights.

Without these changes, the paper fails to meet the threshold for novelty that I prioritize as a reviewer. The field would benefit more from a paper that introduces a genuinely new approach rather than an uncited reimplementation of existing work.
