Summary
This paper proposes lda2vec, a simple neural objective that augments Skip-gram with Negative Sampling (SGNS) by adding a document vector to the pivot word vector and constraining the document vector to be a convex mixture of learnable topic vectors. A Dirichlet-inspired penalty with low concentration encourages sparse document–topic proportions, aiming to recover interpretable topics while jointly learning word, topic, and document embeddings in a shared space. The model is trained with automatic differentiation and evaluated by topic coherence on 20 Newsgroups and qualitatively on a large Hacker News comments corpus (topics, nearest neighbors, and word analogies).

Contributions (as I see them)
- A compact, differentiable objective that couples SGNS with sparse document–topic mixtures, yielding interpretable topic proportions while preserving word embedding semantics in a shared embedding space. The design choice of using a convex combination of topic vectors for the document vector is elegant and directly connects topics to words via cosine similarity.
- A practical, open-source implementation demonstrating scalability to a tens-of-millions-token corpus, with qualitative evidence (topics and analogies) that the shared space captures corpus-specific semantics.
- Empirical observation that a Dirichlet-style sparsity term (with α<normally below 1 and a tuning λ) materially improves topic coherence and interpretability in practice.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and clarity of the modeling idea: By defining the context vector as pivot+document and forcing the document vector to lie on the simplex over topic vectors, the method neatly bridges interpretable topic mixtures with predictive word embeddings. This is a clean, easy-to-implement alternative to full probabilistic models or variational neural topic models.
- Interpretability-by-design: The convex mixture and shared space allow direct inspection of topics via nearest words, and also allow measuring topic–word similarity without an extra mapping. This is useful for practitioners who need both interpretability and semantic structure.
- Practicality and reproducibility: The paper provides an open-source implementation, clear optimization choices (SGNS with negative sampling, Adam, batch scaling of the Dirichlet term), and concrete hyperparameters (e.g., λ=200, α=1/n), making the method readily testable.
- Scalability: Results on a 75M-token, 110k-vocabulary corpus illustrate that the approach can handle large datasets typical of modern NLP, leveraging standard GPU autodiff stacks.
- Empirical qualitative evidence: The examples on 20NG and Hacker News show coherent topics and sensible nearest-neighbor/analogy structure, suggesting the intended behavior emerges in practice.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and lack of baselines: The paper reports mean Cv coherence for lda2vec on 20NG but does not compare against standard LDA, doc2vec+LDA pipelines, GloVe/SGNS baselines with post-hoc topic modeling, or neural topic models (e.g., NVDM/ProdLDA). Without comparative results, it is hard to assess the improvement in coherence or interpretability the method delivers.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analysis: The central claim relies on the sparsity-inducing Dirichlet term and the mixture constraint. There is no systematic ablation (e.g., remove Dirichlet penalty, replace simplex constraint with unconstrained document vectors, separate input/output embeddings) to demonstrate which components drive coherence and interpretability. Hyperparameter sensitivity for λ, α, and number of topics is not explored beyond a narrow sweep.
- Weak probabilistic grounding: The approach borrows the Dirichlet log-likelihood as a penalty but is not a coherent generative model; it mixes a discriminative SGNS objective with a prior-like term without likelihood consistency. This is acceptable as an engineering method, but the paper at times positions itself relative to topic models without discussing the implications (e.g., no perplexity, no posterior inference, unclear calibration of mixture proportions). A clearer discussion of the trade-offs would strengthen the work.
- Evaluation breadth: On the Hacker News corpus, coherence is omitted due to tokenization mismatch; however, this could be resolved by computing coherence on an in-domain reference or an alternative metric. No extrinsic evaluation (e.g., document classification, retrieval) is provided to show utility of document/topic vectors versus baselines.
- Presentation and related work: There are formatting issues (figures/text overlaps), some notation inconsistencies (indices i/j/k), and missing or thin coverage of related works that integrate embeddings with topics (e.g., topical word embeddings, Gaussian LDA, neural variational topic models). The lack of discussion of doc2vec as a baseline is notable given the close methodological neighborhood.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide comparative Cv coherence (with confidence intervals) on 20NG against LDA (with typical settings), doc2vec+LDA pipelines, and a neural topic model baseline (e.g., NVDM/ProdLDA), using the same preprocessing and number of topics?
- Ablation: What happens to coherence and document sparsity if (a) you remove the Dirichlet penalty, (b) you drop the simplex constraint and learn free document vectors, and (c) you separate input/output embeddings as in SGNS? How sensitive are results to λ and α?
- Can you report extrinsic results (e.g., 20NG classification using document vectors; retrieval or clustering) to quantify the usefulness of learned document/topic embeddings?
- What are the computational costs (time, memory) relative to training SGNS plus a separate LDA, and how does the choice of negative sampling exponent and number of negatives affect training stability/performance?
- Why tie input and output word embeddings? Did you observe benefits versus the standard SGNS practice of separate matrices?

Overall assessment
This submission presents a neat, practical idea with clear interpretability benefits and accessible implementation. However, the lack of rigorous comparative evaluation and ablations limits its scientific contribution. I view it as promising but currently below the bar for acceptance; a stronger empirical section with baselines and analysis could change this to a positive recommendation.
