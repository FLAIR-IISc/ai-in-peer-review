Summary
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that augments the SGNS (word2vec) objective with document representations constrained to be sparse mixtures over learned topic vectors. The context vector used to predict a target word is the sum of the pivot word embedding and a document vector; the document vector itself is a convex combination of shared topic embeddings, with simplex constraints and a Dirichlet-inspired penalty to induce sparsity. Word, topic, and document embeddings live in a shared space, enabling both interpretable topics (via nearest words to topic vectors) and standard embedding properties (similarities and analogies). The model is implemented in an autodiff framework and evaluated on 20 Newsgroups (with topic coherence) and a large Hacker News comments corpus (with qualitative topics and analogies).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple and novel integration of SGNS with LDA-like sparse document-topic mixtures: document vectors are mixtures of topic vectors, and these topic vectors share the embedding space with words and are trained jointly via negative sampling.
2) A Dirichlet-inspired regularization on document-topic proportions (α < 1) to promote interpretability through sparsity, yielding more coherent topics while preserving semantic properties of embeddings.
3) A practical, end-to-end autodiff implementation that jointly learns words, topics, and document mixtures, demonstrating scalability to a large real-world corpus and qualitative gains (topic coherence, meaningful analogies).

Strengths
- Conceptually bold and elegant hybrid: The key idea—tying a doc2vec-like context composition (doc + word) to a sparse topic mixture living in the same space as the word embeddings—is both simple and original. It unifies interpretability (topic mixtures) with the strong distributional properties of SGNS without complicated inference.
- Interpretability without sacrificing embedding semantics: Topics are actual vectors in the word space, so their nearest neighbors are easily interpretable; at the same time, the model preserves familiar linear regularities and analogy behavior. This duality is compelling and useful.
- Sparse mixtures via Dirichlet penalty: The use of a Dirichlet-inspired objective to steer document proportions toward sparsity is effective and conceptually clean. The paper shows that without it, topics degrade in coherence—consistent with the intuition that sparsity aids interpretability.
- Practicality and accessibility: The formulation is straightforward to implement in modern autodiff frameworks (the Chainer implementation helps reproducibility). This lowers barriers to experimenting with topic-embedding hybrids, which is valuable for the community.
- Qualitative evidence on a large, specialized corpus: The Hacker News experiments convincingly show domain-tailored semantics (phrases, specialized analogies) and sensible topics, illustrating that the approach scales and adapts to nonstandard vocabularies.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and lack of baselines: On 20 Newsgroups, only Cv coherence is reported, without direct comparisons to LDA, doc2vec, TWE/TopicVec, GloVe+LDA, or neural variational topic models. No extrinsic tasks (e.g., doc classification, retrieval) are provided. The Hacker News analysis is purely qualitative. This makes it hard to assess improvements beyond anecdote.
- Missing or incomplete ablations: The paper asserts the importance of the Dirichlet penalty (and α < 1), but does not provide systematic ablations (e.g., α=1, varying λ) with coherence/sparsity metrics. Similarly, the additive context (doc + word) vs. word-only, or vs. a dense doc vector (doc2vec) is not rigorously compared.
- Related work coverage: Prior topical embedding approaches (e.g., Topical Word Embeddings, TopicVec, Gaussian LDA variants) and doc2vec connections are not discussed in depth. Positioning relative to these lines is necessary to sharpen the novelty claim and clarify differences (e.g., mixture constraint vs. unconstrained doc vectors).
- Ambiguities/clarity in formulation: The description of mapping to the simplex via softmax mentions “latent vectors initialized in R300,” but the document-topic logits should be of size n topics, not word-embedding dimensionality; this is confusing. More details on dropout rates, sharing of input/output embeddings, and training schedule would improve clarity.
- Evaluation metrics/scope: Coherence numbers are presented without contextual baselines on the same setup (e.g., LDA Cv on 20NG). No measures of topic sparsity (e.g., entropy of p_j) or document-level interpretability (e.g., topic concentration) are reported. Runtime/efficiency vs. LDA or doc2vec is not discussed.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide direct quantitative baselines on 20 Newsgroups (e.g., Cv for standard LDA, doc2vec + k-means topics, TWE/TopicVec if feasible)? Even one or two would greatly strengthen the case.
- How sensitive are results to α and λ? Please report coherence and sparsity (e.g., average entropy or Gini of p_j) across a small grid to substantiate the role of the Dirichlet penalty.
- What is the precise dimensionality of the document-topic logits before softmax? The text suggests R300, but p_jk should be over n topics. Please clarify the parameterization.
- How does lda2vec compare to a doc2vec DM-style model where the document vector is unconstrained (dense) but still added to the pivot word? Does the mixture constraint measurably improve coherence/interpretability at similar performance on analogy/similarity?
- Do you share input and output embeddings (pivot/target) or use two matrices? Sharing can affect SGNS performance; please justify the choice and, if possible, report the impact.
- Can you report sparsity statistics (e.g., proportion of mass in top-3 topics per doc) and topic diversity metrics? This would quantify interpretability claims.
- What is the training time and memory footprint compared to standard LDA or doc2vec on your datasets? Any optimization tips (e.g., negative sampling distribution, dropout rates)?

Overall, I find the core idea novel and promising: it is a clean bridge between interpretable topic mixtures and powerful word embeddings. Despite the evaluation gaps and some missing related work, the conceptual contribution is strong and could open a useful direction. Strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying the formulation would make this a compelling submission.
