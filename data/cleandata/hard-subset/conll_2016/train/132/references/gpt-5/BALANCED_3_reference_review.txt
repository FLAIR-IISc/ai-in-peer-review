Summary
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid between SGNS word2vec and topic models. The model learns word, topic, and document vectors in a shared embedding space. Each document vector is a convex combination of K topic vectors (via a softmax over topic weights), and the context vector used in SGNS is the sum of the pivot word vector and the document vector. A Dirichlet-inspired regularizer on the document’s topic weights encourages sparsity for interpretability. The authors implement the model in an autodiff framework and report topic coherence on 20 Newsgroups and qualitative topics and word analogies on a large Hacker News dataset.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple neural objective that combines SGNS with a sparse mixture-of-topics document representation, enabling joint training of words, topics, and documents in a common space and allowing topic interpretability via nearest neighbors.
2) An implementation detail showing the context vector as an additive composition of a pivot word and a document vector, which allows global (document) and local (word) signals to interact during training.
3) An open-source implementation with demonstrations on two corpora, including a large-scale, domain-specific dataset.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: The model is easy to implement on top of SGNS, trains with standard negative sampling, and integrates smoothly with autodiff/GPUs. The additive context (word + doc) is a clever architectural choice that encourages both local and global signals to shape the target prediction.
- Interpretability via sparse topic mixtures: Enforcing simplex-constrained topic weights with a sparsity-inducing regularizer yields interpretable document-topic proportions without sacrificing the ability to do nearest-neighbor queries in the shared embedding space. The examples on both datasets are coherent and intuitive.
- Joint space for words/topics/docs: Because topics and words live in the same space, the method supports flexible exploratory analyses (topic labels via nearest words, topic similarity, analogy-style queries). The qualitative examples for Hacker News demonstrate corpus-specific semantic structure.
- Open-source and scalable: The code availability and application to a 75M-token corpus are strong positives for reproducibility and applicability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The paper positions itself mainly against classical LDA and SGNS, but related neural/topic-embedding hybrids and document-embedding methods exist (e.g., Paragraph Vector/Doc2Vec; topical word embeddings; Gaussian LDA and other models that tie topics to embeddings). The specific combination of SGNS with a sparse simplex document mixture is interesting, but the conceptual novelty is modest without a stronger theoretical or empirical case.
- Evaluation is largely qualitative and lacks strong baselines: There is no direct comparison to LDA (e.g., coherence, topic diversity), doc2vec/document embedding baselines (e.g., downstream classification/retrieval), or related neural topic models. Even on 20 Newsgroups, coherence is reported for different K and β but not against standard baselines under the same preprocessing. The Hacker News analyses are anecdotal; no quantitative topic quality or downstream metrics are provided.
- The “Dirichlet likelihood” is effectively a regularizer with unclear modeling justification: The paper optimizes Σ(α−1) log pjk on softmax outputs. This is not a principled likelihood of observed data; it is a prior-style penalty applied to deterministic weights. Without a clear probabilistic model or an ELBO, it is hard to interpret the learned topics as a generative topic model. Moreover, the statement “set α = n−1” appears inconsistent with the earlier claim that α < 1 encourages sparsity; likely a typo for α = 1/n. This undermines clarity.
- Clarity and technical precision issues: There is a dimensionality confusion where the softmax is said to map latent vectors “in R300” to topic weights; the logits should be R^K, not R^300. Important training details (dropout rates, batch size, learning rate schedule, λ selection and scaling in minibatches) are missing. Some references are incomplete/mismatched (e.g., Scikit-learn citation). Figure captions and equations would benefit from tighter exposition (e.g., shared input/output embeddings vs standard SGNS; motivation for sharing).
- Missing ablations and robustness checks: No ablation shows the effect of the document vector addition, the sparsity regularizer (α and λ), pretraining vs random initialization, embedding tying, or negative sampling exponent β beyond a small coherence comparison. Stability across seeds and topic coherence distributions are also not reported.

Questions to Authors
- Dirichlet prior: Is α truly set to n−1 or 1/n? Please clarify and provide a sensitivity analysis over α and λ (including sparsity metrics over document-topic weights).
- Topic-weight parameterization: What is the dimensionality of the pre-softmax logits for pjk? Should be K; the text suggests R300. Please clarify.
- Embedding matrices: Do you tie input and output embeddings for SGNS? If so, why, and how does it affect performance relative to standard two-matrix SGNS?
- Training details: Please report batch size, dropout rates for word/doc vectors, learning rate schedule, early stopping, and the exact scaling of Ld in minibatches.
- Baselines and metrics: Can you include comparisons to LDA (same preprocessing) on Cv (and additional measures such as NPMI/Tc-W2V), and to doc2vec/paragraph vectors or a neural topic model on downstream tasks (e.g., 20NG classification from document vectors, document retrieval, clustering)?
- Ablations: What happens if (a) the document vector is omitted, (b) pjk are unconstrained, (c) the Dirichlet penalty is removed, or (d) topic vectors are not shared across documents? How do these variants affect coherence and sparsity?
- Robustness: How stable are topics across random seeds and different K? Please report variance of coherence and sparsity.

Overall, the idea is neat and practically useful, but the paper would benefit from stronger positioning vis-à-vis prior neural topic/embedding work, clearer modeling and training details, and a much more rigorous experimental evaluation with standard baselines, ablations, and downstream tasks.
