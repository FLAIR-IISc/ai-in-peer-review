Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes lda2vec, a simple neural architecture that augments SGNS/word2vec with document vectors constrained to be mixtures over shared topic vectors. The context vector is the sum of a pivot word vector and a document vector; document-topic proportions are encouraged to be sparse via a Dirichlet-style regularizer. Word, topic, and document vectors live in a common space, enabling interpretability via nearest neighbors to topic vectors.
- Empirical evidence is provided on 20 Newsgroups (quantitative topic coherence, qualitative topics) and a large Hacker News comment corpus (qualitative topics, nearest neighbor examples, analogies). Code is publicly released.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1) A conceptually simple joint embedding framework that ties interpretable sparse document-topic mixtures to SGNS-style training, yielding a shared space for words/topics/documents.
2) Practical demonstration that such a model is easy to implement with autodiff and scales to a large corpus, with open-source code.
3) Qualitative evidence that topics are coherent and embeddings capture domain-specific linear regularities.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and interpretability: The design choices (sum of pivot and document vectors, document vectors as mixtures over shared topic vectors) directly support interpretability. Because topics are vectors in the same space as words, inspecting top nearest neighbors to each topic is straightforward. This is a practical advantage over many neural topic models whose topics are not directly embedded alongside words.
- Simplicity and scalability: The approach reuses SGNS with negative sampling and adds a lightweight regularization on document-topic proportions. This makes the method easy to implement in common autodiff frameworks, avoids bespoke variational inference, and should scale to large datasets (as evidenced by the Hacker News experiment).
- Qualitative evidence is compelling: The 20NG topic examples align well with known newsgroups; the Hacker News topics and word analogies show that the model captures salient community-specific semantics. Such qualitative demonstrations are important for interpretability-focused work.
- Public code and preprocessing: Availability of a codebase and preprocessing scripts is a practical contribution that can facilitate adoption and replication.

Weaknesses
- Limited and unconvincing quantitative evaluation and lack of baselines: On 20NG, only topic coherence is reported for different numbers of topics and negative sampling exponents. There is no comparison to standard LDA (e.g., with the same preprocessing and number of topics), NMF, HDP, or alternative neural document/ topic models; nor to doc2vec for document representations. Without such baselines, the reported coherence (~0.55–0.57) is hard to interpret, and claims of “high mean topic coherences” are not well supported. For the Hacker News corpus, coherence is omitted; alternative within-corpus coherence (e.g., NPMI computed on the same corpus) should be feasible.
- No extrinsic task evaluation: There is no downstream evaluation (e.g., document classification using learned topic proportions, retrieval, clustering) to demonstrate utility beyond qualitative inspection. Such tasks are standard in CoNLL/ML topic modeling work and would substantiate the method’s benefits.
- Methodological clarity and rigor: The “Dirichlet likelihood” applied directly to softmaxed proportions is used as a regularizer with fixed α and λ, but the approach is not framed as a coherent probabilistic model, and there is no analysis of sensitivity to α, λ. The text suggests mapping “latent vectors initialized in R300 onto the simplex” for pjk, which appears inconsistent (the softmax logits should be in R^n_topics, not R^300). These ambiguities reduce clarity and reproducibility.
- Missing ablations: The contribution of each component is not disentangled. Specifically, it is unclear how much benefit comes from (a) adding a document vector to the pivot, (b) constraining document vectors to topic mixtures, and (c) the Dirichlet sparsity term. The authors note that removing sparsity yields incoherent topics, but no numbers are provided. Moreover, the 20NG experiment initializes word vectors with pretrained embeddings, conflating potential gains from initialization with gains from the proposed architecture.
- Presentation and polish: There are several typographical and formatting issues (broken figure elements, stray numbers, inconsistent references), and training details are incomplete (e.g., dropout rates, batch sizes, learning rate schedules). This detracts from clarity and makes it harder to fully assess and reproduce results.

Questions to authors
- Please provide quantitative baselines on 20NG: LDA (with same preprocessing and number of topics), NMF, and doc2vec (for document representations). Report comparable topic coherence and at least one extrinsic task (e.g., document classification using learned topic proportions).
- Clarify the parameterization of pjk: Are the softmax logits per document in R^n_topics? The text mentions R300, which seems at odds with the number of topics. Please correct and detail the exact parameter shapes.
- Sensitivity and sparsity: How do coherence and interpretability vary with α and λ? Report entropy or the average number of topics per document above a threshold (e.g., >5%) to quantify sparsity.
- Ablations: Report results for variants: (i) without adding the document vector to the pivot, (ii) with unconstrained document vectors (no topic mixture), (iii) with and without the Dirichlet regularizer, and (iv) without pretrained embeddings on 20NG.
- Hacker News coherence: Can you compute NPMI-based coherence using counts from the same corpus to enable quantitative comparison, even if Palmetto’s external corpus is mismatched?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a neat, practical idea that marries SGNS with interpretable document-topic mixtures in a shared embedding space, with compelling qualitative demonstrations and released code. However, it falls short of CoNLL standards due to the lack of rigorous quantitative comparisons and extrinsic evaluations, limited ablations, and clarity issues. Strengthening empirical validation with strong baselines and clearer methodological exposition would substantially improve the paper’s merit.
