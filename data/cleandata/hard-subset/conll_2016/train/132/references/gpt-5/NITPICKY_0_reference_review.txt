Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid objective that augments SGNS with document vectors formed as a convex combination of learnable topic vectors, with softmax-normalized document-topic proportions encouraged to be sparse via a Dirichlet-style regularizer. The context vector for predicting targets is the sum of the pivot word vector and the document vector, tying word, topic, and document representations in a shared space.
- Empirical demonstrations are provided on 20 Newsgroups (reporting topic coherence Cv) and on a large Hacker News comments corpus (qualitative topics and analogy-style examples). An implementation in Chainer is released.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple additive architecture merging SGNS with a Dirichlet-regularized topic mixture for interpretable document representations in the same space as word embeddings.
2) Qualitative and limited quantitative evidence that the learned topics are coherent and that word-level linearities are preserved.
3) An open-source, GPU-ready implementation illustrating rapid prototyping via autodiff.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and engineering appeal: The model is easy to implement with autodiff, requires minimal architectural changes to SGNS, and yields interpretable proportions via a softmax. The sum c = w + d is intuitive and practically convenient.
- Joint space for words, topics, and documents: Embedding topics in the same space enables direct topic-word similarity queries and preserves standard embedding operations (e.g., analogies), illustrated on a large, domain-specific corpus (HN).
- Qualitative results are sensible: On 20NG, topics align with known categories (e.g., sci.space, sci.crypt), and Cv numbers are reported. On HN, topics reflect salient community concerns; analogy-style examples look reasonable within-domain.
- Open-source release and use of GPUs: Availability of code and an emphasis on modular development lower the barrier to adoption and reproduction.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior art is underdeveloped: Closely related lines (e.g., topical/mixture-based embeddings and neural topic models) are not compared experimentally and are scarcely discussed. Examples include Topical Word Embeddings (TWE; Liu et al., 2015), TopicVec/Topical Embeddings (Ding et al., 2016), neural variational topic models (e.g., NVDM; Miao et al., 2016), LFTM, and Gaussian LDA variants. The paper largely compares to no baselines beyond SGNS intuitions, weakening the claim of contribution.
- Evaluation is too limited and lacks rigorous baselines: For 20NG, Cv is reported without comparisons to standard LDA, CTM, TWE, NVDM, or ETM-like models; no classifier-based downstream evaluation (e.g., doc classification/retrieval) is reported; no perplexity/likelihood proxy is given. For HN, coherence is omitted (despite possible adaptation using internal co-occurrence statistics), and analogy evaluations are purely anecdotal. No ablations quantify the role of the Dirichlet term, document addition, dropout, or tying input/output embeddings.
- Methodological ambiguities and ad hoc choices: 
  - The “Dirichlet likelihood” term is used as a regularizer with scale λ, but the objective is not a coherent probabilistic model (SGNS is not a likelihood). The minibatch rescaling of Ld is described informally; the effective weight on the prior is unclear. 
  - α is stated as “α = n−1”; it likely means α = n^(-1) (i.e., 1/n), but the notation is ambiguous; this is important for sparsity (α<1). No sensitivity study for α or λ is provided; potential gradient instability from (α−1)log p near zero is not discussed.
  - The topics are not normalized; interpreting dot-product similarity to tokens depends on norms. No norm constraints or temperature scaling are described.
  - Tying of input/output embeddings in SGNS deviates from standard practice; no justification or comparison is given.
- Missing quantitative evidence for sparsity and interpretability: The paper claims sparsity in p_jk and more coherent topics with the prior, but provides no histograms, sparsity metrics (e.g., average entropy/#nonzeros per doc), or topic coherence ablations with α=1 vs α<1.
- Presentation and formatting issues that impede clarity and professionalism:
  - Figures include raw numeric artifacts (e.g., “.34 -0.1 0.17 …”) and broken labels; Figure 1 is hard to parse.
  - Typos and inconsistent terminology: “F lugdienst”, “curency”, “v.s.”, “tokens similar to X demonstrate,” etc.
  - Indexing and notation are inconsistent: i/j reused for word/document indices; Eq. 4 mixes k and n with ellipses; “hidden units” vs embedding dimension mismatch (256 vs 300).
  - References are severely malformed/misattributed (e.g., scikit-learn authors, Mikolov year markup “\Q2013\E”, many “?, ?” placeholders). This is below conference standards.

Questions to authors
- Clarify α: Is it α = n^(-1) or α = n−1? Please report sensitivity to α and λ, and provide training stability observations (e.g., gradients of log p near 0).
- How exactly is Ld scaled in minibatches? Provide the precise stochastic objective used and its unbiasedness (or rationale if biased).
- Are input and output embeddings tied? If yes, why, and what is the effect relative to standard SGNS with separate matrices?
- Are topic and word vectors normalized when computing similarities? If not, why is dot product preferred over cosine, and how do norms affect interpretability?
- Please provide quantitative comparisons: 
  - Topic coherence vs LDA (same preprocessing), TWE/TopicVec, and a neural topic baseline on 20NG.
  - Document classification/retrieval using p_jk vs LDA vs doc2vec.
  - Ablations: remove Dirichlet term; remove document addition; vary dropout; tie vs untie embeddings.
- For the HN corpus, why not compute Cv using internal co-occurrence statistics (e.g., NPMI on the same corpus) or UMass coherence?
- Report sparsity metrics of p_jk (e.g., average entropy, share of mass in top-k topics per doc) to support the sparsity claim.
- Provide training time, hardware, and convergence criteria. Which code commit reproduces the reported numbers?

Overall, while the idea is appealing and the qualitative results are promising, the paper falls short on novelty positioning, rigorous evaluation with baselines, methodological clarity, and presentation quality.
