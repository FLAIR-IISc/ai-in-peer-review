Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes lda2vec, a simple hybrid of Skip-gram with Negative Sampling (SGNS) and LDA-style document-topic mixtures. The context vector used to predict target words is the sum of a pivot word vector and a document vector. Each document vector is, in turn, a convex combination of learnable topic vectors, with simplex weights regularized by a Dirichlet-inspired term to induce sparsity. Word, topic, and document vectors are trained jointly with automatic differentiation. The method is evaluated on 20 Newsgroups (topic coherence) and qualitatively on a large Hacker News corpus (topics and analogies).

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
1) A conceptually simple, end-to-end trainable framework that augments SGNS with interpretable document-topic mixtures by tying document vectors to a sparse softmax mixture of learnable topic vectors and using c = w + d as the prediction context.
2) Qualitative and limited quantitative evidence that the model yields coherent topics (Cv ≈ 0.56 on 20NG) while preserving embedding linearities for corpus-specific analogies; practical open-source implementation with GPU/AD support.

Strengths
- Simple and elegant integration of interpretability into SGNS: The model preserves the strengths of SGNS (efficient training, semantic regularities) while adding LDA-like document-topic mixtures in a very lightweight way (a softmax over topics plus a sparsity-inducing log prior). The c = w + d design nicely separates long-term (document) and short-term (local) signals.
- Interpretable shared space for words, topics, and documents: Topic vectors live in the same space as words, allowing direct nearest-neighbor inspection of topics via top words. This is practically useful and improves the transparency of embedding-based document models (vs. Doc2Vec).
- Qualitative topic quality is convincing: On 20NG, example topics align well with known newsgroups (space, encryption, X Windows, Middle East) and show good per-topic words; reported average Cv ≈ 0.56 with best ≈0.57 is in a plausible range for 20NG, suggesting reasonable coherence.
- Scalability and practicality: The approach trains with negative sampling and AD, scales to a 75M-token Hacker News corpus, and learns corpus-specific semantics and analogies. Code is open-sourced, which aids reproducibility and adoption.
- Clear, intuitive motivation and architecture: The additive composition and sparsity rationale are easy to grasp; the Dirichlet-style regularizer encourages interpretable mixtures without complex inference machinery.

Weaknesses
- Missing quantitative baselines and ablations: There is no side-by-side comparison to strong baselines (e.g., LDA topic coherence, Doc2Vec, NVDM/NVDM-like, Topical Word Embeddings, Gaussian LDA), nor to standard SGNS with post-hoc topics. Claims about coherence and interpretability would be much stronger with direct Cv comparisons to LDA on 20NG, and analogy/similarity benchmarks against SGNS/Doc2Vec. Ablations (no Dirichlet term; c = d only; c = w only; untied input/output embeddings; different λ, α) are not reported.
- Evaluation on Hacker News is entirely qualitative: Topic coherence is omitted due to tokenization differences. Alternatives exist (compute NPMI on the same corpus or adapt evaluation) and would give much-needed quantitative support. Similarly, analogy tests are shown as cherry-picked examples rather than accuracy on a benchmark (even a domain-specific one).
- Limited downstream validation of document vectors: No tasks assess whether the document representations are useful (e.g., document classification on 20NG, retrieval, clustering). Such tasks are standard to justify document modeling contributions and interpretability claims.
- Ambiguity in probabilistic framing and training details: The “Dirichlet likelihood” is used purely as a regularizer atop softmax proportions; the model is not a proper probabilistic topic model and lacks likelihood-based evaluation (perplexity) or variational inference—this should be clearly positioned as a neural objective with a Dirichlet-style penalty. Mini-batch scaling of the regularizer is mentioned but not precisely specified; stability and sensitivity to λ and α are not studied.
- Methodological choices under-justified: The model ties input and output word embeddings (unlike standard SGNS), which can affect performance; no empirical justification is provided. The number of negative samples, dropout on both w and d, and negative sampling exponent are explored minimally. Topic redundancy/overlap is not addressed (e.g., diversity regularization).
- Presentation and related work: Some figures and references are messy or incomplete. Related work on integrating topics and embeddings is under-cited or not contrasted (e.g., Topical Word Embeddings/Labeled variants, Gaussian LDA/embedding-informed topics, neural topic models such as NVDM). Stronger positioning would clarify novelty.

Questions to authors
- Can you report direct Cv comparisons to a tuned LDA baseline on 20NG (same preprocessing, #topics), and show ablations (no Dirichlet, no word addition, varied λ/α) to quantify the contribution of each component?
- How do document vectors perform on 20NG document classification (e.g., linear SVM on frozen doc vectors)? How does this compare to Doc2Vec and LDA?
- Why tie input and output embeddings? Have you tried the standard SGNS setup (separate matrices), and how does it affect results?
- For the Hacker News corpus, can you compute coherence using an internal co-occurrence-based metric (e.g., NPMI on the same corpus) and provide analogy accuracy on a curated domain analogy set?
- How are new/unseen documents handled at test time? Is there an efficient inference procedure for p_jk with fixed topics, and how stable are the inferred proportions?
- Sensitivity to number of topics: How do coherence and sparsity vary with n, and how do you recommend selecting n? Any diagnostics for topic redundancy?

Additional comments
- Reporting training time/memory vs LDA/Doc2Vec would help establish practicality.
- A discussion of topic diversity (e.g., regularizers) and failure cases (topic collapse, diffuse mixtures) would be valuable.
- Improving references and figure readability will help clarity.

Overall, lda2vec is an appealing and practical idea with promising qualitative results, but it needs stronger quantitative comparisons and ablations to substantiate its contributions.
