Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that augments the SGNS (word2vec) objective with document vectors constrained to be mixtures over a small set of topic vectors. Document-topic proportions are constrained to the simplex via softmax and encouraged to be sparse via a Dirichlet log-prior term.
- The model jointly learns word, topic, and document vectors in a shared space; the context vector is the sum of the pivot word vector and the document vector, aiming to retain SGNS semantic regularities while yielding interpretable, sparse topic mixtures.
- Empirical demonstrations on 20 Newsgroups (with Cv topic coherence) and a large Hacker News corpus (qualitative topics and analogy-style examples); code is released.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) An incremental modeling design that constrains a doc2vec-like document vector to be a convex mixture of learned topic vectors and regularizes proportions with a Dirichlet prior to encourage sparsity/interpretability.
2) A qualitative/partial quantitative demonstration that the approach can produce reasonable topics and preserve word-level semantic regularities, implemented in an autodiff framework with open-source code.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive construction: The idea of setting the context vector to word + document, and constraining the document vector to be a mixture of topic vectors on the simplex, is simple and easy to implement. The sparsity-inducing prior addresses a known weakness of dense document vectors (interpretability).
- Shared embedding space: Embedding topics, documents, and words in the same space facilitates direct inspection of topics (nearest words) and is operationally convenient for qualitative interpretation and exploratory analysis.
- Qualitative evidence of sensible topics and analogies: On both 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News, the example topics and nearest-neighbor/analogy lists look reasonable and on-theme, suggesting the method can recover salient structure without supervision.
- Practicality and availability: Use of automatic differentiation and a public implementation lowers the barrier for replication and extensions, which is valuable for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited and unconvincing evaluation against baselines: There is no direct, controlled comparison against established baselines such as LDA (collapsed Gibbs or variational) on the same preprocessed 20NG data for the same number of topics, nor against strong neural baselines (e.g., paragraph vectors/doc2vec variants) for document representations. Reporting absolute Cv numbers for lda2vec only, without LDA/NMF/CTM baselines, makes it difficult to assess improvements or trade-offs. For the embedding quality, curated analogy examples are shown, but no standard analogy or similarity benchmarks, nor comparisons to a plain SGNS run on the same corpus.
- Methodological ambiguity and lack of ablations: The Dirichlet “likelihood” term is effectively a prior/regularizer; its treatment under minibatching is only heuristically described. The sensitivity to α and λ is not studied; the paper fixes λ=200 and says α=n−1 (likely intended as 1/n, but the text is ambiguous and contradictory with the sparsity goal). There is no quantitative ablation demonstrating the incremental value of (a) document=topic mixture, (b) the Dirichlet term, (c) sharing input/output embeddings, (d) dropout on vectors. Claims such as “topics become incoherent without sparsity” are not supported with metrics.
- Novelty is modest relative to prior art: The construction is very close in spirit to PV-DM/doc2vec (document vector added/summed to word(s) to predict targets), with the main difference being a simplex-constrained mixture of learned topic vectors and a sparsity prior. Related lines such as topical word embeddings and neural document models (e.g., LDA combined with embeddings or neural variational document models) are not compared, and relevant prior work is insufficiently positioned. Without strong empirical evidence, the contribution reads as an incremental regularization of a known architecture.
- Fairness and experimental rigor: In 20NG, word vectors are initialized with pre-trained embeddings, which may advantage coherence; LDA baselines typically don’t benefit from external embeddings. On Hacker News, initialization is random, but only qualitative results are reported. There are no downstream tasks (e.g., document classification or retrieval) to test whether the learned document/topic representations are competitive. No reporting of topic diversity/uniqueness, stability across runs, or held-out likelihood/perplexity.
- Clarity and presentation issues: There are several typos/formatting artifacts (figures with garbled text), inconsistent notation (e.g., subscript indices), and incomplete/malformed references. Calling Ld a “likelihood” is misleading in this non-probabilistic objective. These issues detract from readability and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Please provide direct comparisons to strong baselines on 20NG under identical preprocessing: (a) LDA (e.g., Gibbs sampling) with Cv and other coherence metrics, (b) doc2vec (PV-DBOW/PV-DM) and SGNS for word-level quality and any downstream document task. How does lda2vec perform relative to these?
- Clarify the Dirichlet term: Is α set to 1/n (to encourage sparsity), or n−1 as written (which would be >1 for n>2)? How sensitive are results to α and λ? Please include ablations removing the Dirichlet term and/or the simplex constraint, reporting coherence and sparsity of pjk.
- Training details: What are the dropout rates on word/document vectors? Did you try using distinct input/output embeddings (as in SGNS), and how did that affect results? How were negative samples drawn per update? Any early stopping or validation criteria?
- Fairness of initialization: Pre-trained embeddings on 20NG may confound coherence comparisons. Can you report results with random initialization and LDA baselines on the same data?
- Quantitative embedding evaluation: On Hacker News (or 20NG), can you report standard word similarity/analogy benchmarks and compare to a plain SGNS baseline trained on the same corpus?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting topic diversity/uniqueness and stability across seeds, and a simple downstream classification benchmark using document mixtures.
- Please fix the references and figure artifacts, and be precise about probabilistic vs regularized objectives.

Overall, the idea is intuitive and potentially useful for practitioners seeking interpretability, but the current evidence is largely qualitative and lacks rigorous comparisons to established baselines. I would need stronger empirical validation and clearer ablations to support acceptance.
