Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid between word2vec’s Skip-gram with Negative Sampling (SGNS) and topic models. It learns:
- Word embeddings;
- Topic vectors that live in the same space as words;
- Document representations as sparse mixtures over topics, enforced by a Dirichlet-style regularizer on softmax-normalized topic proportions.
The context vector is the sum of a document vector (itself a mixture over topics) and a pivot word vector, and the model is trained end-to-end with SGNS plus a sparsity-inducing term. Experiments report topic coherence (Cv) on 20 Newsgroups and qualitative analyses on a large Hacker News (HN) comments corpus. Code is released.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A simple joint objective tying word, topic, and document vectors into a shared embedding space while encouraging sparse document-topic mixtures via a Dirichlet-inspired regularizer. This yields interpretable topics whose vectors can be queried like word embeddings.
2) Empirical demonstration that the model can produce coherent topics and preserves linear regularities useful for analogies, including on a large, specialized corpus (HN) with phrase tokenization.
3) An open-source implementation using an autodiff framework, making this class of hybrid models accessible.

Strengths
1) Conceptual simplicity and practicality. The objective is easy to implement in modern autodiff frameworks and combines two popular paradigms (embeddings and topic mixtures). The additive context (doc + word) is intuitive and leverages known compositionality of embeddings.
2) Interpretability in a shared space. Having topic vectors co-located with word vectors is useful: users can directly inspect topic–word similarities and perform vector arithmetic with topics and words. This is an appealing interface for practitioners seeking both interpretability and embedding semantics.
3) Sparsity for document mixtures. The Dirichlet-style regularizer on softmax proportions is a straightforward, effective way to encourage sparse, interpretable doc-topic memberships; the paper shows qualitatively that this matters.
4) Reproducibility and engineering value. Code, preprocessing details, and some hyperparameters are provided; the work is likely to be adopted and extended by practitioners. The HN corpus results showcase that the approach scales to sizable data and can learn domain-specific relations.
5) Qualitative analyses are compelling. The listed topics and analogy examples on HN plausibly reflect community themes and learned regularities, illustrating the method’s intended benefits.

Weaknesses
1) Lack of comparative baselines. The paper does not compare against standard topic modeling baselines (e.g., LDA, CTM) or hybrid/related methods (e.g., TWE/Topical word embeddings, Gaussian LDA, neural variational topic models, doc2vec/PV-DM). On 20NG, only absolute Cv is reported; no head-to-head topic coherence, perplexity, or downstream performance comparisons are shown. This makes it hard to assess whether lda2vec is competitive or superior.
2) Limited evaluation breadth and rigor. Beyond Cv on one dataset and qualitative examples on HN, there is no:
- Downstream evaluation (e.g., doc classification with doc-topic proportions, retrieval);
- Quantitative analogy evaluation (accuracy on standard benchmarks);
- Human evaluation of interpretability;
- Ablations on key design choices (e.g., impact of the Dirichlet term, document mixture vs. dense doc vector, using separate vs. shared input/output embeddings).
3) Confounding initialization and fairness. On 20NG, word vectors are initialized with pre-trained embeddings, while on HN they are random. If improved coherence partly stems from external pretraining, fairness vs. LDA or other baselines (which typically don’t benefit from external embeddings) is unclear. An ablation showing performance with and without pretraining, and comparative baselines with the same resources, is needed.
4) Modeling clarity and justification gaps. The “Dirichlet likelihood” is applied as a regularizer over softmax outputs rather than as part of a coherent probabilistic model; this is fine as a regularizer but should be positioned as such. Hyperparameters (e.g., α = 1/n, λ = 200) appear ad hoc; sensitivity analyses are missing. Design choices (e.g., shared pivot/target embeddings) are nonstandard and not justified empirically. Details about negative sampling distributions and their impact are only lightly explored.
5) Presentation and related work coverage. The draft has formatting issues (figures/refs), making parts hard to follow. The related work section omits several closely related prior efforts integrating topics and embeddings (e.g., TWE and variants) and neural topic models; explicit comparisons and positioning would help establish novelty more convincingly.

Questions to authors
1) Can you provide quantitative comparisons of topic coherence (Cv, NPMI) against LDA and at least one hybrid baseline (e.g., TWE) on 20NG, with and without pre-trained embeddings, and matched preprocessing?
2) How sensitive are results to α and λ? Please provide a sensitivity analysis and an ablation removing Ld (or setting α = 1) to quantify sparsity and coherence changes.
3) What is the impact of using a dense document vector (no topic mixture) vs. the proposed mixture? Similarly, what is the effect of summing doc and word vectors vs. using the doc vector alone for prediction?
4) Can you report a downstream task (e.g., 20NG document classification) using inferred doc-topic proportions or document vectors, and compare to LDA and doc2vec baselines?
5) Do you have quantitative analogy results (e.g., 3COSMUL accuracy) and nearest-neighbor quality metrics on 20NG/HN?
6) How does runtime and memory scale with number of topics and documents, and how does it compare to LDA implementations?

Overall assessment
The idea is elegant, practical, and potentially useful, and the qualitative results are appealing. However, the paper currently lacks rigorous comparative evaluation and ablations necessary to establish novelty and significance at a conference venue. With added baselines, stronger quantitative evidence, and clearer positioning w.r.t. related work, the contribution could be compelling. As it stands, I lean toward rejection due to evaluation shortcomings, but I see promise if these are addressed.
