Summary and contributions
The paper proposes lda2vec, a neural topic modeling approach that augments the SGNS (word2vec) objective with document vectors expressed as simplex-constrained mixtures of topic vectors, and a Dirichlet-based sparsity regularizer on document–topic proportions. The context vector for predicting a target word is the sum of the pivot word vector and a document vector, tying words, topics, and documents into a shared embedding space. The model is trained end-to-end with negative sampling and Adam. The paper reports topic coherence (Cv) on 20 Newsgroups and qualitative topics and word analogies on a large Hacker News comments corpus.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple hybrid objective that yields sparse, interpretable document–topic mixtures while preserving word2vec-like word semantics, by combining SGNS with a Dirichlet-regularized document mixture over learned topic vectors.
2) Empirical demonstration that the joint space supports interpretable topics and meaningful word analogies, including on a large, specialized corpus (Hacker News), with an implementation in an autodiff framework.

Strengths
- Simple, elegant integration of interpretability into SGNS-style embedding learning: Replacing dense, unconstrained document vectors with a convex combination of learned topics (plus a sparsity prior) is a clear, principled design choice that encourages LDA-like interpretability while retaining distributional semantics. The additive context vector (pivot + document) is intuitive and compatible with known compositional properties of embeddings.
- Joint embedding space for words, topics, and documents: The shared space enables direct inspection of topics via nearest-neighbor words and meaningful analogical reasoning. The examples on 20NG and Hacker News are coherent and intuitive (e.g., “Encryption,” “Bitcoin,” “Gadget Hardware,” and word analogies such as Silicon Valley ≈ California + technology).
- Practicality and scalability: The method is easy to implement with standard autodiff and negative sampling, needs no model-specific variational inference, and scales to tens of millions of tokens. The use of GPUs and open-source code is a plus for reproducibility and adoption.
- Sparse document proportions via a Dirichlet regularizer: Encouraging sparsity in proportions addresses a common critique of neural document embeddings (opacity) and aligns with the interpretability of classic topic models. The qualitative claim that α < 1 produces more coherent topics is plausible and supported by example topics.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural document/embedding models: The core SGNS modification—using a document vector together with word vectors to predict targets—closely resembles PV-DM/doc2vec (document vector + context predicts target). The principal novelty is constraining the document vector to a simplex mixture of topics with a Dirichlet penalty. Related work on integrating topics and embeddings (e.g., topical word embeddings, Gaussian LDA, joint topic–embedding models) is not discussed or compared, making it hard to place the contribution precisely.
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and baselines: The only quantitative metric is average Cv on 20NG without comparisons to strong baselines such as LDA (with tuned hyperparameters), doc2vec variants, GloVe-based topic models, or contemporary neural topic models. No held-out likelihood, classification, retrieval, or clustering evaluations are provided. On Hacker News, coherence is omitted due to tokenization mismatch; however, coherence could be computed with an internal co-occurrence source or adapted to phrases. Without controlled comparisons, it is unclear whether lda2vec offers improvements over simpler baselines.
- Methodological clarity and rigor: The Dirichlet “likelihood” is used as a regularizer on softmax outputs rather than arising from a coherent generative model; implications for inference and calibration are not discussed. Important training details are underspecified: exact embedding dimension vs “hidden units,” dropout rates, frequency subsampling of frequent tokens, whether input/output embeddings are tied, topic vector normalization/regularization (to avoid scale pathologies in mixtures), and how the softmax is parameterized for pjk (the text suggests mapping R300 to a K-simplex, which seems inconsistent unless K=300 or there is a learned projection). Claims about ablations (e.g., α=1 degrades coherence) are asserted but not quantified.
- Use of pretrained embeddings on 20NG: Initializing with pretrained word vectors likely boosts coherence, but the paper neither ablates this choice nor controls for it in comparisons to non-embedding topic models, complicating fairness.
- Presentation and related work: Figures and references contain formatting issues and several citations are incomplete. The related work section omits important prior art at the intersection of topics and embeddings, which weakens the positioning.

Questions to authors
- How does lda2vec compare quantitatively to LDA and PV-DM/doc2vec on 20NG in terms of Cv, topic sparsity (e.g., average entropy or effective number of topics per document), held-out likelihood (if applicable), and a downstream task such as document classification or retrieval?
- Can you provide ablations for the Dirichlet term (vary α and λ) showing effects on coherence, sparsity (e.g., Gini or concentration), and stability across runs?
- Please clarify architecture/training details: embedding dimensionality; whether input and output embeddings are tied; frequency subsampling; dropout rates; negative sampling schedule; normalization or constraints on topic vectors; and the exact parameterization that maps document-specific parameters to the K-simplex (dimension mismatch in the description).
- On Hacker News, could you compute coherence using an internal co-occurrence matrix (e.g., NPMI on the same corpus) or adapt Palmetto to phrases, to provide at least relative comparisons across model variants?
- Do topic vectors’ norms drift during training, and if so, how do you guard against degenerate mixtures where scale dominates proportions? Did you consider unit-normalizing topics and using cosine similarity in the loss?

Additional comments
- The idea is promising and practically useful, but the paper needs stronger baselines and clearer methodological exposition. Cleaning up references and adding missing related work on topical word embeddings and neural topic models would improve the paper.
- Reporting runtime, memory, and training stability across seeds would aid reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak Reject. The approach is appealing and the qualitative results are encouraging, but the incremental novelty over PV-DM plus a sparsity constraint, the lack of rigorous comparisons and ablations, and clarity gaps currently fall short of a strong empirical NLP venue. With stronger evaluation and positioning, this could be a solid contribution.
