Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid between SGNS-style word embedding models and LDA-like topic mixtures. The context vector for predicting a target word is the sum of a pivot word vector and a document vector; the document vector itself is a convex mixture of K topic vectors, with mixture proportions p_j constrained to the simplex via softmax and regularized by a Dirichlet “likelihood” with small concentration to encourage sparsity. Training uses negative sampling and Adam in an autodiff framework (Chainer). Evaluation includes: (i) Twenty Newsgroups (20NG) topic coherence (Cv) and qualitative topics; (ii) a large Hacker News (HN) comments corpus with qualitative topics and word analogies.

My view of the primary contributions:
1) A simple neural objective that ties word, topic, and document vectors into a shared space, with document vectors parameterized as sparse mixtures of topic vectors. The summed doc+word context to predict targets is closely related to PV-DM, but the interpretable mixture structure over topics is a distinguishing feature.
2) An empirical demonstration that this model yields reasonably coherent topics on 20NG and learns corpus-specific semantic regularities on the HN corpus, with open-source implementation in an autodiff framework.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and implementability: The model is easy to reimplement in general autodiff frameworks, combining well-known components (SGNS, mixture over topics, sparsity regularizer). This lowers the barrier to experimenting with interpretable document representations in embedding spaces.
- Interpretable topic vectors in the embedding space: By forcing document vectors to be mixtures of shared topic vectors and embedding topics in the same space as words, the model supports intuitive diagnostics (nearest words to a topic vector), aligning interpretability (LDA-like mixtures) with distributional semantics (SGNS).
- Sparse document-topic mixtures without variational inference: Avoiding bespoke inference for each model variant is attractive. The Dirichlet-inspired regularizer is a pragmatic way to push p_j toward sparse mixtures while retaining end-to-end training.
- Qualitative results align with domain intuition: The 20NG topics correspond well to known groups (sci.space, sci.crypt, comp.windows.x, etc.). On HN, the topics and analogy structure reflect community-specific vocabulary (e.g., “DDG”, “Mt. Gox”, “Functional Programming”).
- Open-source code and practical details: Sharing code and preprocessing pipelines increases reproducibility and utility for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural/document-topic hybrids: PV-DM (Le & Mikolov, 2014) already sums document and word vectors to predict targets. Several contemporaneous works inject topic structure into embeddings or vice versa (e.g., TWE/Topical Word Embeddings; neural autoregressive topic models such as DocNADE; Gaussian LDA/Topic embeddings). The paper should position its contribution more clearly against these, emphasizing what is genuinely new (topic mixture parameterization of doc vectors plus Dirichlet sparsity in a SGNS objective) and empirically demonstrating advantages.
- Evaluation is narrow and lacks baselines: On 20NG, only Cv is reported; no direct comparisons to LDA, NMF, PV-DM/Doc2Vec, TWE, or LDA+embeddings methods. No document-level downstream evaluation (e.g., 20NG classification using p_j or doc vectors), no perplexity/held-out likelihood, and no topic diversity/human judgments. On HN, coherence is omitted; results are purely qualitative and analogies are shown without baseline comparisons. This makes it hard to assess the practical benefits over strong baselines.
- Use of pretrained embeddings confounds 20NG results: Initializing with pretrained word vectors likely boosts coherence and nearest-neighbor interpretability. There is no ablation contrasting pretraining vs. random initialization, so the contribution of the proposed training objective is unclear.
- Incomplete or imprecise mathematical exposition: The paper refers to a “Dirichlet likelihood” L_d but it is effectively a log-prior regularizer; constants and the softmax Jacobian are ignored. The parameterization of p_j via softmax is described as mapping “latent vectors initialized in R300” onto the simplex, which appears dimensionally inconsistent (the softmax should be over K topics, not the embedding dimensionality). Variable reuse (n as both number of topics and number of negatives) and index overloading (j) reduce clarity. These issues should be corrected.
- Sparse, but not truly sparse: Softmax guarantees p_jk > 0 for all k; the model cannot achieve exact sparsity. Alternatives (e.g., sparsemax/entmax, temperature scaling, L1 penalties on logits, or stick-breaking) may yield crisper mixtures. The paper does not discuss this limitation or explore alternatives.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity and training stability: The sparsity regularization weight λ and choice α=1/n are set heuristically; there is no sensitivity analysis. Dropout is mentioned but not systematically evaluated. The impact of β, negatives, and topic count on both coherence and embedding quality lacks thorough exploration with error bars.

Questions to authors
- Novelty and positioning: How does lda2vec differ empirically and methodologically from PV-DM (doc2vec) plus post-hoc topic discovery on doc vectors, or from TWE/other topical embedding models? Can you include direct comparisons (coherence, classification) to at least LDA, PV-DM, and one topical-embedding baseline?
- Pretraining ablation: On 20NG, what is the coherence and qualitative topic quality when starting from random embeddings? How much of the reported gains persist?
- Topic-word scoring: How are “top words” for a topic selected exactly? Is it nearest neighbors by dot product or cosine? Are these the words sent to Palmetto? If so, do you observe differences compared to scoring by a normalized p(word|topic) surrogate?
- Clarify parameterization: Please resolve the dimensional inconsistency: the softmax that yields p_j should be over K topics, not 300. Also, clarify the use of symbol n for topics vs. negative samples, and indexing for documents vs. pivot positions.
- Regularization details: How was λ selected? Any sensitivity analysis around λ, α, number of topics, β, negatives, dropout rate? Do you observe topic collapse or overly diffuse topics without L_d?
- True sparsity: Did you try sparsemax/entmax or temperature-annealed softmax to achieve crisper mixtures? Any empirical trade-offs?
- Downstream tasks: Can you report 20NG document classification using p_j or doc vectors, and word analogy performance compared to SGNS trained on the same data?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting topic diversity and intruder word evaluations in addition to Cv. Include standard deviations over multiple runs.
- Minor: fix reference formatting and notation, and define precisely how topic vectors are initialized and updated.
