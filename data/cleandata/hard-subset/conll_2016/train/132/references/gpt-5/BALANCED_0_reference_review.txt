Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that augments Skip-gram with Negative Sampling (SGNS) by adding document vectors that are constrained to be sparse mixtures of shared topic vectors, with sparsity encouraged via a Dirichlet prior. The context vector for predicting targets is the sum of the pivot word vector and a document vector, thereby coupling local and global semantics. The model is implemented in an autodiff framework (Chainer), supports GPU training, and is evaluated on 20 Newsgroups (with topic coherence) and a large Hacker News (HN) comments dataset (with qualitative topics, nearest neighbors, and analogies).

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) A simple neural topic model that jointly learns word, topic, and document embeddings in a shared space, where document vectors are constrained to be mixtures over topic vectors and trained with a Dirichlet-inspired sparsity regularizer. This yields interpretable topics while preserving SGNS-style semantic properties.
2) A practical formulation and implementation in an autodiff framework that is easy to optimize (no bespoke variational inference), with open-source code, and qualitative evidence on two corpora.
3) Empirical demonstration that adding doc+word context preserves standard embedding properties (nearest neighbors, analogies) while offering LDA-like interpretability.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and implementability: The model is an elegant modification of SGNS (context = word + doc mixture over topics) plus a simple Dirichlet log-likelihood term. It is easy to implement in modern autodiff frameworks and to train with standard optimizers. This lowers the barrier to experimentation compared to bespoke probabilistic inference.
- Interpretability via sparse doc-topic mixtures: The softmax simplex constraint with α<1 prior encourages sparse proportions. The reported topics on 20 Newsgroups are coherent and human-interpretable, and the shared embedding space lets one directly inspect topic–word similarities, a useful diagnostic not available in standard LDA.
- Qualitative evidence of semantic structure: On the HN corpus, the model learns meaningful nearest neighbors and analogies specialized to the domain, indicating that adding document-level signals did not degrade the desirable properties of SGNS-style embeddings.
- Reproducibility efforts: The authors provide open-source code, preprocessing details, and clear training defaults (negative sampling power, number of negatives, dropout usage), which is valuable for the community and supports adoption.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: The model is very close in spirit to Paragraph Vector (Le & Mikolov, 2014), where a document vector is added to word vectors to predict targets, with the main twist here being to constrain the document vector to be a mixture over learnable topic vectors with a sparsity prior. There is substantial related work on integrating topics and embeddings (e.g., topical word embeddings, neural topic models, LDA+embeddings variants) that is not compared against empirically or positioned clearly. The paper would benefit from a tighter differentiation and baselines against LDA, doc2vec, and topical embedding models.
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and baselines: On 20NG, only topic coherence is reported, with no baselines (e.g., LDA, NVDM/ProdLDA, ETM) to contextualize the numbers. On HN, coherence is omitted; however, coherence (e.g., NPMI) can be computed on the in-domain corpus rather than relying on Palmetto’s external corpus. There is no document-level downstream evaluation (e.g., 20NG classification using learned doc-topic mixtures or doc vectors), and the analogies are purely anecdotal without quantitative scores. Ablations (e.g., without Dirichlet term, without word+doc sum) are mentioned qualitatively but not quantified.
- Methodological clarity issues: Several details are confusing or possibly erroneous. For instance, the text claims “latent vectors initialized in R300” are mapped by softmax to doc-topic proportions; the dimension should be the number of topics, not the embedding size. The Dirichlet term is scaled by λ but not motivated from a principled posterior; numerical handling of α<1 near-zero probabilities is not discussed. It is unclear whether input/output embeddings are tied and whether the negative sampling distribution is updated during training. These issues affect reproducibility and interpretation.
- Missing comparisons and analysis of interpretability and sparsity: While the paper argues the Dirichlet prior improves interpretability, there are no quantitative sparsity metrics (e.g., effective number of topics per document, Gini/entropy) or topic diversity, nor user studies. The effect of λ and α is not systematically studied.
- Presentation and referencing problems: The paper contains formatting issues, broken references, and some incorrect bibliographic entries. Related work coverage is incomplete (e.g., topical word embeddings, neural variational topic models, embedding topic models), which weakens the positioning.

Questions to authors
1) Please clarify the parameterization of document proportions: what is the exact dimensionality before the softmax? If embedding size is 300 and number of topics is n, which space do the pre-softmax logits live in?
2) How do you handle numerical stability with α<1 and λ=200 when some pjk→0 (log pjk→−∞)? Do you add a floor or ε?
3) Are input and output word embeddings tied? If not, did you try tying, and how does it affect coherence and analogies?
4) Can you report baselines on 20NG: coherence and classification performance for LDA, doc2vec, and at least one neural topic model? Also, show ablations (no Dirichlet term; no word+doc summation; document vector unconstrained).
5) For the HN corpus, why not compute NPMI on the in-domain co-occurrence matrix instead of relying on Palmetto? Please provide coherence and topic diversity there.
6) How sensitive are results to λ, α, number of topics, and dropout rates? Please provide a brief sensitivity analysis.
7) What is the training cost (epochs, wall-clock time, GPU) and memory footprint for both datasets?
8) How stable are the learned topics across random seeds?

Additional comments
- Consider comparing against ETM (Embeddings Topic Model) or other neural topic models that jointly learn topic and word embeddings; if contemporaneous models are unavailable, include LDA and doc2vec as baselines.
- Improve reference formatting and fix broken citations; add missing related work on topical embeddings and neural topic models.
- Provide quantitative analogy results (3COSMUL) and standard word similarity benchmarks to complement qualitative examples.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form. The idea is appealing and practical, and the qualitative results are promising, but the paper falls short on novelty positioning and especially on quantitative evaluation and baselines. With stronger comparisons, ablations, clearer methodology, and in-domain coherence metrics, this work could be much more convincing.
