Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that augments the SGNS (word2vec) objective with document vectors constrained to be sparse mixtures of learned topic vectors. The context vector for predicting a target word is the sum of the pivot word embedding and the document embedding; document embeddings are parameterized as simplex-constrained mixtures over shared topic vectors, with a Dirichlet-inspired sparsity term. The model is trained end-to-end with negative sampling and Adam in an autodiff framework. Empirical results include topic coherence on 20 Newsgroups and qualitative analyses (topic word lists, nearest neighbors, analogies) on 20 Newsgroups and a large Hacker News corpus.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A simple, computationally convenient objective that jointly learns word, topic, and document embeddings in a shared space, while encouraging sparse, interpretable document-topic mixtures via a Dirichlet-style penalty.
2) Qualitative demonstrations that the learned topic vectors are interpretable and that the shared space supports word analogies and nearest-neighbor semantics.
3) An implementation leveraging automatic differentiation and negative sampling to scale to large corpora.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and elegance: The c = w + d construction is a natural way to inject document-level signal into SGNS while keeping word-topic-document embeddings in one space. The simplex constraint plus a Dirichlet log-likelihood term is a pragmatic route to sparse, interpretable doc-topic proportions without bespoke variational inference.
- Interpretability-by-design: Because topics and words inhabit the same space, the top words for each topic (by cosine similarity) are straightforward to compute and inspect. The Dirichlet penalty encourages sparse proportions, aligning with LDA-style interpretability goals.
- Practicality and scalability: Using negative sampling and autodiff is appealing for practitioners. The model appears easy to prototype, modify, and train on GPUs; the Hacker News experiment demonstrates feasibility on a large, domain-specific corpus.
- Evidence of coherent topics and meaningful semantics: On 20 Newsgroups, the reported mean Cv coherence is reasonable and the example topics align well with known newsgroups. The Hacker News analyses (nearest neighbors, analogies) suggest the shared space captures domain-specific relationships.
- Open-source implementation: Availability of code and preprocessing details improves reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and lack of baselines: There is no direct comparison against strong baselines such as LDA, GloVe+LDA, doc2vec (PV-DM/PV-DBOW), topical word embedding methods, or neural topic models (e.g., NVDM). Topic coherence is reported only for lda2vec, with no side-by-side coherence numbers for LDA or doc2vec+clustering on the same tokenization and training setup. There is no human topic quality evaluation.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses: The paper asserts the Dirichlet term and sparsity are important, but provides no ablation (e.g., α=1, λ=0) with coherence or qualitative comparisons. The impact of key choices—pretrained vs. random initialization, negative sampling exponent and count, dropout, number of topics, dimensionality, and normalization—are not systematically explored.
- Inconsistent and sometimes confounded setups: On 20 Newsgroups, word vectors are initialized from pretrained SGNS, which can inflate topic coherence and makes fair comparison to baselines ambiguous. On Hacker News, coherence is not reported due to tokenization differences; yet it is still possible to compute NPMI-based coherence from the corpus itself (or present alternative metrics), so the lack of any quantitative topic quality metric weakens the claim.
- Modeling clarity and rationale: While the approach is motivated, its probabilistic semantics are unclear—the Dirichlet “likelihood” acts only as a regularizer without a full generative story, and hyperparameters (α = 1/n, λ = 200) are heuristic with no justification or robustness check. The text also states that the softmax transforms “latent vectors initialized in R300 onto the simplex defined by pjk,” which is dimensionally confusing if the number of topics is n ≠ 300. More clarity on parameterization and shapes is needed. It is also unclear whether input/output embeddings are tied and whether embeddings are normalized during training.
- Limited downstream utility evaluation: The paper does not demonstrate that the learned document/topic representations help on downstream tasks (e.g., document classification/retrieval on 20 Newsgroups, clustering metrics, or transfer to related tasks). This makes it difficult to assess practical advantages over simpler baselines.

Questions to authors
- Parameterization: Please clarify the dimensionality of the logits for p_jk. Is the softmax taken over n topic logits per document? The text suggests R300 but n is 20–50 in experiments.
- Baselines: Can you report topic coherence (Cv and at least one other measure) compared to LDA and doc2vec on 20 Newsgroups with identical preprocessing? Also compare to a topical embedding baseline (e.g., TWE) if feasible.
- Ablations: Please provide quantitative evidence for the benefit of the Dirichlet sparsity term (vary α and λ, include α=1). Also, what is the effect of removing the document vector, or of not summing w and d?
- Initialization: How much do pretrained embeddings contribute on 20 Newsgroups? Please show runs from random init and report coherence and nearest-neighbor quality.
- Training details and stability: What are the negative sampling count, dropout rates, embedding normalization, and learning-rate schedules used? How stable are results across seeds? What are training time and memory footprints relative to LDA?
- Hacker News coherence: Could you compute coherence directly from the corpus counts (e.g., NPMI on the same tokenization) or report alternative intrinsic metrics?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is neat and practically appealing, and the qualitative results are promising. However, the paper as submitted lacks rigorous empirical comparison, ablations, and clarity on parameterization and modeling choices. Strengthening the evaluation with fair baselines, quantitative topic quality on both corpora, downstream tasks, and ablations would substantially improve the case for publication.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines (LDA, doc2vec, topical embeddings, and a neural topic model) with matched preprocessing and report multiple topic coherence measures with variance across runs.
- Include ablation studies for the Dirichlet term, topic count, negative sampling parameters, dropout, and initialization, and report document-topic sparsity (e.g., entropy) distributions.
- Provide downstream evaluations (e.g., 20NG classification using doc-topic proportions) and retrieval tasks to demonstrate utility.
- Clarify parameterization and training details, and report stability and complexity.
- For Hacker News, compute coherence from in-domain counts or add human evaluation.
