Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid objective that combines SGNS-style word embedding training with an LDA-inspired document representation. The core idea is to form the context vector as the sum of a pivot word vector and a document vector; the document vector is constrained to be a convex combination of shared topic vectors with simplex weights, and a Dirichlet “likelihood” term (α < 1) encourages sparse document–topic mixtures. The model is trained end-to-end with negative sampling and Adam. The authors evaluate on 20 Newsgroups (topic coherence) and a large Hacker News comments corpus (qualitative topics and analogies).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, differentiable objective that ties together SGNS embeddings with sparse document–topic mixtures, enabling interpretable topic vectors that live in the same space as words and documents.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach can yield coherent topics on 20 Newsgroups and preserves embedding regularities (analogies) on a large specialized corpus; open-source implementation.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and implementability: The architecture (c = w + d, d as a convex combination of shared topics, Dirichlet-inspired sparsity) is easy to reproduce in autodiff frameworks and GPU-friendly. The code release and training details (negative sampling, dropout, Adam) are pragmatic and valuable.
- Unified embedding space: Placing topics, words, and documents in the same space affords interpretable operations (e.g., nearest words to a topic vector) and exploits the additive compositionality noted in word2vec. This is a clear usability advantage over classical topic models.
- Sparse, normalized doc-topic mixtures: Enforcing the simplex via softmax and sparsity via a Dirichlet-like regularizer targets interpretability—mitigating a common criticism of dense doc vectors (e.g., doc2vec). The modeling choice is intuitively aligned with LDA while avoiding specialized variational inference.
- Qualitative results: On both datasets, the listed topics are plausible and aligned with known categories (e.g., sci.space, sci.crypt, Bitcoin, compensation, hardware). The analogy examples on Hacker News indicate the method captures corpus-specific semantics.
- Sensible training choices: Using tied embeddings for input/output, exploring negative sampling exponents (β ∈ {0.75,1.0}), and phrase-aware tokenization for Hacker News are reasonable design decisions that likely improve quality.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: The paper situates itself primarily against LDA and SGNS, but omits closely related lines that integrate embeddings and topics or learn interpretable document vectors. Prior works such as Topical Word Embeddings (TWE), TopicVec, Gaussian LDA, LFTM, or other joint topic–embedding models have explored coupling topics with embeddings and/or learning topic and word vectors in a shared space. Doc2vec (paragraph vectors) already learns document embeddings from context prediction. Framing the differentiator (sparse simplex doc-topic mixtures trained with SGNS) and positioning against these models is necessary to claim a clear advance.
- Weak empirical evaluation and absence of baselines: The paper reports mean Cv on 20 Newsgroups for lda2vec only, without comparisons to standard LDA (with matched preprocessing), CTM, or topical embedding baselines. For Hacker News, the evaluation is purely qualitative; coherence is omitted due to tokenization mismatch, and no alternative intrinsic metrics (e.g., internal NPMI on the corpus, topic diversity) are provided. No downstream tasks (document classification/retrieval using the doc-topic proportions; word similarity/analogy accuracy) are reported. As a result, it is difficult to assess how much the method improves topics or embeddings in practice.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses: The Dirichlet regularizer is central to interpretability, but there is no quantitative ablation showing its effect (λ, α) on sparsity and coherence. Similarly, there is no comparison of “free” document vectors vs. mixtures over topics, nor of c = w + d versus alternative compositions (e.g., gating or concatenation). The claim that topics become “incoherent” without sparsity needs quantified evidence. The dependence on pretrained word vectors for 20 Newsgroups vs. random init on Hacker News is also not analyzed.
- Probabilistic framing and interpretability claims: The “Dirichlet likelihood” on softmax outputs is effectively a regularizer; the model is not generative and does not provide posterior uncertainty or a likelihood for held-out evaluation (e.g., perplexity). While that can be acceptable in a discriminative embedding framework, the paper sometimes implies an LDA-like modeling status without the corresponding inferential properties. Clarifying the scope—an embedding model with a sparsity prior, not a probabilistic topic model—is important.
- Clarity and reporting details: Several implementation details are underspecified (dropout rate/formulation on vectors; subsampling of frequent words; learning rate schedule; normalization constraints on topics/embeddings; training times/hardware). Figures are not always easy to parse, and the references contain formatting issues. The choice α = 1/n is very aggressive; more justification is needed.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you report topic coherence (Cv) for standard LDA and a topical embedding baseline (e.g., TWE/TopicVec) on 20 Newsgroups with matched preprocessing? For document vectors, can you evaluate on document classification or retrieval against doc2vec and LDA?
- Ablations: Please provide quantitative ablations of λ and α (e.g., coherence vs. sparsity trade-off; distribution of doc-topic weights) and a comparison between (i) free doc vectors, (ii) mixtures without the Dirichlet regularizer, and (iii) the full model. Also, how critical is c = w + d relative to other compositions?
- Initialization: How much do pretrained word vectors contribute on 20 Newsgroups? Can you train from scratch and report the gap?
- Efficiency and settings: What are the training times, hardware, and key hyperparameters (dropout rate, subsampling of frequent words, windowing strategy) for both datasets? Did you tie input/output embeddings and apply any norm constraints?
- Topic redundancy/diversity: Do topics collapse or become redundant? Can you report topic diversity metrics or apply decorrelation/orthogonality penalties?
- Inference for new documents: How are doc-topic proportions inferred for unseen documents with fixed topics/word vectors, and what is the computational cost?

Overall, the idea is appealing and practically useful, but the paper needs stronger positioning w.r.t. prior work and substantially more rigorous empirical evaluation and ablations to justify acceptance.
