## Review of lda2vec: Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings

This paper presents lda2vec, a model aiming to combine the strengths of word embeddings (word2vec) and topic models (LDA) by jointly learning dense word vectors alongside sparse, interpretable document mixtures. The core idea is to integrate word embeddings into a probabilistic topic modeling framework, leveraging automatic differentiation for efficient model development. The authors demonstrate the model on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News corpus, showcasing topic coherence and the ability to learn semantic relationships between tokens.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the attempt to bridge the gap between dense distributed representations and sparse topic models within a unified, differentiable framework. While the individual components (word2vec and LDA) are well-established, the integration, even if conceptually straightforward, offers a potential pathway for more nuanced and interpretable document representations. A secondary contribution lies in the application of this combined model to the Hacker News dataset, demonstrating its ability to capture specialized vocabulary and relationships within a specific domain.

**Strengths:**

*   **Interesting Combination:** The idea of combining word embeddings and topic models is potentially valuable, offering a way to leverage the strengths of both approaches.
*   **Automatic Differentiation:** Utilizing automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer) is a practical approach, simplifying model development and enabling efficient training.
*   **Demonstration on Diverse Datasets:** Applying the model to both a standard benchmark (Twenty Newsgroups) and a more specialized corpus (Hacker News) provides some breadth to the evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Incomprehensibility:** Section 2 is severely lacking in clarity. The mathematical notation is presented without sufficient explanation, making it difficult for readers familiar with word2vec and LDA to understand the model's mechanics. A high-level overview and intuitive explanation are crucial.
*   **Unclear Motivation:** The rationale for using the *same* word vectors for pivot and target words, deviating from the standard word2vec approach, is not adequately explained. The benefit of this choice needs to be justified.
*   **Vague Terminology:** Phrases like "separate words from a marginal distribution" and "co-adaptation" are used without clear definitions, hindering understanding.
*   **Insufficient Evaluation:** The lack of quantitative evaluation is a major flaw. Relying solely on example clusters and the caption of Figure 2 is insufficient to demonstrate the model's effectiveness. Metrics beyond topic coherence are needed.
*   **Overstated Conclusion:** The claim that the model "solves word analogies" based on a few cherry-picked examples is a significant overstatement.
*   **Anonymity Breach:** Footnote 1 breaks the double-blind review process.
*   **Reference Error:** The Chang citation is missing the venue name.
*   **Ambiguous phrasing:** The phrase “If we only included structure up to this point” is unclear.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the specific motivation for using the same word vectors for both pivot and target words? How does this choice impact the learned representations?
2.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of the "separate words from a marginal distribution" concept?
3.  What quantitative metrics were considered for evaluating the model, and why were they ultimately excluded from the paper?
4.  Please clarify what "structure" is being referred to in the phrase “If we only included structure up to this point”.



Overall, while the core idea is promising, the paper suffers from significant clarity and evaluation issues. A substantial rewrite of Section 2, a more rigorous evaluation, and a more nuanced conclusion are necessary for this work to be considered for acceptance.
