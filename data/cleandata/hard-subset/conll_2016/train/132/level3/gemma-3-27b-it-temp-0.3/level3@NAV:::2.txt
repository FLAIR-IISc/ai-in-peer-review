## Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

This paper presents lda2vec, a novel neural-styled topic model that integrates word embeddings with Dirichlet topic distributions to produce sparse, interpretable document representations. The core idea – leveraging the strengths of both dense word vectors and sparse topic mixtures – is promising and the proposed model appears well-motivated, building upon existing work in both topic modeling and word embeddings. The authors successfully demonstrate the ability to jointly learn word and document representations within a unified framework using automatic differentiation.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the elegant combination of word2vec and topic modeling principles. The method offers a potentially valuable approach to generating document representations that are both semantically rich (due to the word embeddings) and interpretable (due to the sparse topic mixtures). The extension to noun-phrase tokenization in the Hacker News corpus is a secondary, but interesting, contribution demonstrating the model’s adaptability.

However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of empirical evaluation. While the authors provide illustrative examples from the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets, there is no quantitative comparison to standard topic models (like LDA) or other recent neural topic models (Cao et al. 2015; Nguyen et al. 2015; Shamanta et al. 2015, as rightly pointed out in the guidelines). Evaluation via supervised document classification using the learned topic representations, or through direct document similarity tasks, would significantly strengthen the paper. The claim of achieving high topic coherence on Twenty Newsgroups (with 20 topics) is unsurprising and lacks a more rigorous comparison to a baseline.

Several other concerns require attention. The inclusion of a GitHub link breaks anonymity and is a potential cause for rejection. The selection of examples in Figures 3-6 is unclear and raises concerns about potential cherry-picking. The computational feasibility of computing topic-word representations via pairwise comparisons with the entire vocabulary is questionable. Furthermore, the tokenization details using SpaCy are insufficiently described – specifically, which token types are extracted and how multiword terms are handled. The paper also lacks discussion on handling Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) terms when applying the trained model to new documents. Finally, there are minor issues with grammar, phrasing, and reference formatting that need to be addressed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide quantitative results comparing lda2vec to LDA and at least two other recent neural topic models on a standard document classification task?
2.  How was the selection of examples in Figures 3-6 determined? Can you provide statistics on the overall quality of topics generated across multiple runs?
3.  What is the computational complexity of computing topic-word representations, and is it scalable to large vocabularies?
4.  Please detail the specific SpaCy tokenization settings used, including the types of tokens extracted and how multiword terms are handled.
5.  How does the model handle OOV terms when applied to new documents?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please remove the GitHub link to maintain anonymity.
*   Correct the minor grammatical errors (e.g., "it's" vs. "its") and awkward phrasing.
