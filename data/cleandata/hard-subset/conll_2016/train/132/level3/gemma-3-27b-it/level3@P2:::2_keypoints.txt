- Presents lda2vec: joint learning of dense word vectors and sparse, interpretable document-topic mixtures via a neural network.  
- Main contribution: integrates dense embeddings and Dirichlet topic modeling into a single trainable framework.  
- Secondary contribution: shows semantic regularities in word vectors while providing interpretable document-level topic assignments.  
- Provides an implementation compatible with modern deep learning frameworks (Chainer) and promises open-source release.  

Strengths:
- Novel idea: blending Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings and enforcing sparse document representations for interpretability.  
- Clear, accessible presentation with helpful visual overview (Figure 1) and well-motivated approach.  
- Empirical demonstration that learned word vectors capture domain-specific semantic relationships (Hacker News analogies).  
- Practical implementation lowers adoption barrier.

Weaknesses:
- Lacks quantitative evaluation or comparisons to established topic models (LDA, NMF) and neural topic models.  
- Anonymity violation: GitHub link breaches double-blind review policy.  
- Risk of cherry-picking: selection criteria for qualitative examples are unclear.  
- Scalability concerns: computing topic-word representations via pairwise comparisons with full vocabulary may be impractical.  
- Missing implementation details: unclear SpaCy tokenization specifics, multi-word term handling, and OOV treatment for new documents.

Questions for authors:
- What quantitative metrics were considered and why were results omitted; please provide comparisons to LDA/NMF and neural topic models.  
- How were examples in Figures 3–6 selected—by metric or subjectively?  
- How does computational cost scale with vocabulary size and what mitigations exist?  
- Provide detailed SpaCy tokenization procedure, token types, and multi-word handling.  
- How are OOV terms handled when applying the model to unseen data?

Additional comments:
- Reference formatting is inconsistent and some entries are incomplete.  
- Minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasing need correction.  
- Figure 5 caption should use "terms" instead of "words."
