## Review for lda2vec (CONLL 2016)

**Summary:** This paper presents lda2vec, a model combining Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) word embeddings with a Dirichlet-distributed topic model. The goal is to learn dense word vectors alongside interpretable document representations, aiming to leverage the strengths of both distributional semantics and topic modeling. The model jointly trains word, document, and topic vectors within an automatic differentiation framework, producing sparse document-topic mixtures. The authors demonstrate lda2vec on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset and a Hacker News comments corpus, showcasing topic coherence and semantic relationships learned within the combined model.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Integration of Word Embeddings & Topic Modeling:** The core idea of seamlessly integrating word2vec-style training with a Dirichlet allocation framework is a potentially valuable direction. This offers a path towards combining the semantic richness of word embeddings with the interpretability of topic models.
2. **Demonstration on a Large Corpus:** Applying the model to the Hacker News corpus, with its specific vocabulary and conversational style, provides a useful case study. This shows the model's ability to learn domain-specific semantic relationships.
3. **Implementation & Framework Utilization:** The authors leverage automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer) for efficient implementation and training. This aspect contributes to the practicality and accessibility of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Potentially Valuable Combination:** The premise of integrating word embeddings and topic models is interesting and aligns with ongoing research trends in representation learning. The pursuit of models offering both semantic expressiveness and interpretability is worthwhile.
2. **Successful Application to Hacker News:** The experiments on the Hacker News dataset demonstrate that the model *can* learn meaningful representations in a specialized domain. The examples of learned word analogies (Figure 6) are suggestive, even if not rigorously evaluated.
3. **Framework Choice:** The utilization of Chainer (and the emphasis on automatic differentiation) is a positive aspect, promoting ease of implementation and experimentation.
4. **Clear Presentation of Model Architecture (Figure 1):** Figure 1 provides a high-level overview of the model, though the details are lacking (see weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Technical Details:** The primary and most significant weakness is the incomprehensibility of large portions of Section 2. The explanations are convoluted and assume an unrealistic level of pre-existing knowledge regarding the specific implementation choices. A complete rewrite is required to present a clear, high-level picture for readers familiar with word2vec and LDA. What specifically is being modified in SGNS? How exactly are document vectors integrated?
2. **Unclear Motivation & Justification:** Several design choices lack clear motivation. Why use the *same* word vectors for both pivot and target, contrary to the standard word2vec approach? What does it mean to “separate words from a marginal distribution”? The explanation is vague and unhelpful. The concept of “co-adaptation” is introduced without sufficient context.
3. **Ambiguity & Typographical Errors:** The phrase “If we only included structure up to this point” is ambiguous – what “structure” is being referenced? A typo exists: “it's similarity” should be “its.”
4. **Anonymity Breach:** Footnote 1 breaks anonymity, which is a violation of the conference's double-blind review policy.
5. **Insufficient Evaluation:** The lack of quantitative evaluation is a critical flaw. Relying solely on example clusters (Figures 3 & 4) and qualitative observations is insufficient. Figure 2 provides *some* quantitative data in the caption, but this is not presented in a structured and comprehensive manner. Standard topic modeling evaluation metrics (e.g., perplexity, topic diversity) should be included.
6. **Overstated Conclusion:** The conclusion overstates the model's capabilities by claiming it “solves word analogies” based on a few cherry-picked examples (Figure 6). This is a strong claim that requires rigorous quantitative validation. The presented examples are suggestive but not conclusive.
7. **Reference Formatting Error:** The citation for Chang et al. (2009) is missing the venue name.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Technical Clarity:** Can you provide a significantly simplified and more intuitive explanation of the model architecture, specifically Section 2? Please avoid jargon and focus on the core innovations and how they relate to standard word2vec and LDA.
2. **Pivot/Target Vector Choice:** What is the rationale for using the same word vectors for both pivot and target words, given that this deviates from the standard word2vec practice of learning distinct vectors?
3. **Marginal Distribution:** Could you elaborate on the meaning of “separating words from a marginal distribution”? What specific technique is employed, and why is it beneficial?
4. **Quantitative Evaluation:** Will you provide a more comprehensive quantitative evaluation of the model's performance, including standard topic modeling metrics and a more thorough assessment of its ability to capture word analogies?
5. **Coherence Metric:** While you report topic coherence, how sensitive are the reported coherence scores to variations in the preprocessing steps and hyperparameters?



**Additional Comments:**

* The use of noun phrases in the Hacker News corpus is a sensible preprocessing step, but the impact of this choice on the model’s performance should be discussed.
* A brief discussion of computational resources (e.g., training time, memory usage) would be helpful.
