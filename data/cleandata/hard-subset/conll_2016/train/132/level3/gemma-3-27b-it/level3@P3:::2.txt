This paper proposes lda2vec, a neural-styled topic model that extends word2vec to learn document embeddings while simultaneously encouraging sparsity to mimic traditional topic models. The core idea – jointly training word and document vectors with a Dirichlet prior on document topic mixtures – is appealing and potentially valuable for combining the strengths of distributed representations with interpretable topic modeling. The model is presented clearly and the preliminary results on the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets are promising, demonstrating the model's ability to learn coherent topics and capture semantic relationships.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Hybrid Neural-Topic Model:** The primary contribution is the lda2vec model itself, which bridges the gap between dense, distributed document representations and sparse, interpretable topic models. This novel combination leverages the strengths of both paradigms.
2.  **Joint Training Framework:** The paper demonstrates a practical approach to jointly training word and document embeddings within an automatic differentiation framework, streamlining development and enabling GPU acceleration.
3.  **Empirical Demonstration on Diverse Corpora:** The application of lda2vec to both the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets provides initial evidence of the model's effectiveness in diverse textual domains and highlights its ability to capture domain-specific semantics.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Intuitive Model Design:** The core idea of combining word2vec with a Dirichlet-distributed topic model is conceptually sound and offers a compelling approach to unsupervised document representation learning. The authors clearly articulate the motivation behind the model.
2.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and explains the model in a relatively accessible manner. The use of Figure 1 is particularly helpful in visualizing the model architecture.
3.  **Practical Implementation:**  The authors highlight the ease of implementation within automatic differentiation frameworks and mention the availability of open-source code, which is a significant benefit for reproducibility and further research.
4. **Demonstrated Semantic Capture:** The examples in Figures 3-6 are illustrative and suggest that the model does learn meaningful representations capable of capturing both topical structure and word-level semantics. The Hacker News examples, particularly Figure 5, are compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of quantitative evaluation. There are no comparisons to standard topic models (e.g., LDA, NMF) or other neural topic models. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess the model's performance or understand its advantages. The authors should evaluate their model using supervised document classification tasks leveraging the learned topic representations or by evaluating document similarity on appropriate datasets.
2.  **Potential Cherry-Picking of Examples:** The examples in Figures 3-6 appear selected; the criteria for selection are not stated. This raises concerns about potential cherry-picking, and a more systematic evaluation of topic quality is necessary. 
3.  **Computational Feasibility Concerns:** Computing topic-word representations via pairwise comparisons with the full vocabulary, as implied in the description of topic interpretation, is computationally expensive and potentially infeasible for large vocabularies. The authors need to address the scalability of this approach.
4.  **Implementation Details & OOV Handling:** The paper lacks detail regarding tokenization using SpaCy – specifically, what types of tokens or noun-chunks are extracted and how multiword terms are handled. Additionally, there's no discussion of how the model handles Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) terms when applying it to novel documents after the initial embedding phase.
5. **Unsurprising Result:** The finding that 20 topics fit the 20 Newsgroups dataset is unsurprising given the dataset's structure. A more informative analysis would involve comparison with the true labels using an information-theoretic metric (e.g., Normalized Mutual Information) and a direct comparison to LDA.
6. **Missing Related Work:**  The paper fails to compare against prior neural topic models, such as Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015).  A thorough discussion of these related works is essential.
7.  **Minor Errors:** There are some minor grammatical and phrasing errors (e.g., "it's" vs "its", awkward phrasing around “topic basis”). Additionally, the caption for Figure 5 should refer to "terms" instead of "words." The reference formatting is inconsistent, and entries like Roder et al. are missing conference information.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What quantitative metrics were considered for evaluating the model, and why were they ultimately omitted from the paper?
2.  What criteria were used to select the examples presented in Figures 3–6? Can you provide a more comprehensive evaluation of topic quality, perhaps using an automated metric like topic coherence across all learned topics?
3.  How does the computational cost of computing topic-word representations scale with vocabulary size? Are there any strategies for mitigating this cost?
4.  Could you provide more details on the tokenization process employed with SpaCy, including the specific token types and how multiword terms are handled?
5. How does the model handle OOV terms when applied to new documents?

**Additional Comments:**

The GitHub link provided in the paper breaks anonymity and should be removed before publication. Please ensure all references are properly formatted and complete with conference information.
