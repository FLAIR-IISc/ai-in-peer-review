## Review for CONLL 2016 – lda2vec: Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings

This paper presents lda2vec, a model that attempts to combine the strengths of distributed word representations (like word2vec) with the interpretability of topic models (like LDA). The core idea is to jointly learn word vectors and sparse, Dirichlet-distributed document mixtures of topic vectors, leveraging automatic differentiation frameworks for efficient implementation. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the *attempt* to bridge the gap between dense, distributed document representations and sparse, interpretable topic mixtures. While the concept is promising, the paper suffers from significant clarity and evaluation issues. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of applying modern automatic differentiation techniques to topic modeling, potentially easing development and experimentation. Finally, the application of the method to the Hacker News dataset and demonstration of learned semantic relationships is a positive, though limited, contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Potentially Valuable Combination:** The motivation for combining word embeddings and topic models is sound. The idea of leveraging the semantic richness of word vectors while retaining the interpretability of topic models is a compelling research direction. This blending has the potential to provide more nuanced and informative document representations.
2. **Leveraging Automatic Differentiation:** The use of automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer) is a practical and valuable aspect of this work. By removing the need for manual derivation of approximations, the authors aim to accelerate model development and experimentation – a crucial advantage in a rapidly evolving field.
3. **Demonstration on Hacker News:** The application to the Hacker News dataset and the examples demonstrating learned word relationships (Figure 5) offer a glimpse into the model’s ability to capture domain-specific semantic nuances. Showing that the model can learn meaningful relationships between tokens within this specialized corpus is encouraging.
4. **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the trade-offs between dense and sparse document representations and positions lda2vec as a hybrid approach aiming to capitalize on the benefits of both.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Technical Details:** The most significant weakness is the *severe* lack of clarity in the model description (Section 2). The explanations are dense, convoluted, and often lack sufficient context for readers familiar with both word2vec and LDA.  The mathematical notation is presented without adequate intuitive explanation. What exactly does “separating words from a marginal distribution” mean in the context of the loss function? What is “co-adaptation” and how does the proposed approach address it? A complete rewrite of Section 2 is essential for making the technical details accessible.
2. **Insufficient Evaluation:** The evaluation is remarkably weak.  Simply listing example topics (Figures 3 & 4) is insufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the model. While topic coherence is reported for the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, this is presented *only* in the figure caption. There's no discussion of the results or comparison to baseline models. A rigorous quantitative evaluation, including comparisons to established topic modeling techniques (LDA, NMF) and document embedding methods (doc2vec, sentence transformers), is crucial.
3. **Unclear Motivation and Design Choices:** Several design choices lack clear justification. Why use the same word vectors for pivot and target words, contrary to the standard word2vec approach? What is the rationale behind the specific parameters chosen (e.g., λ = 200, α = n-1)? The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of these choices.
4. **Anonymity Violation & Minor Issues:** Footnote 1 contains a link to the authors' code repository, breaking anonymity. The phrase “it's similarity” should be “its.” Additionally, the introduction's third paragraph is poorly written and unclear regarding the challenges of probabilistic topic model development.
5. **Overstated Conclusion:** The conclusion overstates the model's capabilities. Claiming the model "solves word analogies" based on a few cherry-picked examples is a significant exaggeration. The demonstrated relationships, while interesting, do not represent a systematic solution to the word analogy task.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of the loss function and the role of each component? Specifically, what is meant by "separating words from a marginal distribution," and how does this contribute to the learning process?
2. What were the motivations behind using the same word vectors for pivot and target words, as opposed to the standard word2vec practice of using separate vectors?
3.  Will you provide a quantitative evaluation of lda2vec compared to standard topic modeling (LDA, NMF) and document embedding (doc2vec, Sentence Transformers) techniques on multiple datasets? Please include standard evaluation metrics like perplexity, topic coherence, and downstream task performance.
4.  Can you elaborate on the choice of parameters (λ, α, β) and provide a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate the impact of these parameters on model performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper would benefit from a high-level diagram illustrating the overall architecture of lda2vec, making it easier for readers to grasp the core concepts.
*  The reference list contains a formatting error for the Chang et al. citation – the venue name is missing.
* The description of the Dirichlet likelihood term (Ld) is terse; a more detailed explanation would be helpful.
