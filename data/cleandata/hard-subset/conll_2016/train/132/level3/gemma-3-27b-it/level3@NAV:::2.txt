## Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

This paper presents lda2vec, a novel neural-styled topic model that aims to combine the strengths of word embeddings (like word2vec) with the interpretability of Dirichlet-distributed topic models. The core idea – jointly learning dense word vectors alongside sparse, interpretable document mixtures – is promising and the model itself appears well-designed, seamlessly integrating into automatic differentiation frameworks. The presented diagrams (Figures 1 & 6) effectively illustrate the model’s architecture and the learned relationships. The examples showcased in Figures 3-5 are compelling and demonstrate the model's ability to learn meaningful topics and semantic relationships.

However, the most significant weakness of this submission is the complete lack of empirical evaluation. While the authors demonstrate the *potential* of their model through qualitative examples, there’s no quantitative assessment or comparison to established topic modeling techniques (LDA, NMF) or other neural topic models (Cao et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2015; Shamanta et al., 2015). A clear evaluation path would be through supervised document classification using the learned topic representations, or by evaluating document similarity using a benchmark dataset. 

Several other concerns exist. Publishing the GitHub link in the submission compromises anonymity and could warrant rejection based on conference rules. The selection of examples in Figures 3-6 is unclear – it feels potentially cherry-picked and a more systematic evaluation of topic quality is needed.  The computational feasibility of computing topic-word representations via pairwise comparison with the full vocabulary is questionable and needs addressing. The paper lacks detail regarding tokenization with SpaCy – specifically, which token types (e.g., noun chunks) are extracted and how multiword terms are handled. The handling of Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) terms when applying the model to new documents is also absent. Finally, the claim that 20 topics fit the 20 Newsgroups dataset is unsurprising; a comparison to an information-theoretic baseline (e.g., entropy) or a direct LDA comparison would provide stronger validation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** Combining word embeddings with interpretable topic mixtures is a conceptually strong idea.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The model architecture and core concepts are presented clearly, and the diagrams are helpful.
*   **Promising Examples:** The qualitative examples suggest the model is capable of learning meaningful representations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The most critical flaw – no quantitative results or comparison to baselines.
*   **Anonymity Violation:** Including the GitHub link breaks conference anonymity.
*   **Potential Cherry-Picking:** The selection of examples is not justified and could be biased.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** Computing topic-word representations might be computationally prohibitive.
*   **Missing Implementation Details:** Tokenization and OOV handling are not discussed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What quantitative evaluations have been performed to demonstrate the effectiveness of lda2vec compared to existing topic modeling techniques?
2.  Please justify the selection of examples presented in Figures 3-6. Was a systematic evaluation of topic coherence or quality conducted?
3.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost of computing topic-word representations, and discuss potential strategies for scaling to larger vocabularies?
4.  Please provide details about the tokenization process used with SpaCy, including which token types are extracted and how multiword terms are handled.
5.  How does the model handle Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) terms when applied to new documents?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Minor grammatical errors exist throughout the paper (e.g., "it's" vs "its").
*   The caption for Figure 5 should state "terms" instead of "words."
*   Reference formatting is inconsistent and incomplete (e.g., Roder et al. lacks conference information).
