## Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model combining word embeddings (word2vec) with Dirichlet Topic Models (LDA) to learn both dense word vectors and sparse, interpretable document representations. The core idea of jointly training word and document representations within an automatic differentiation framework is potentially interesting, offering a path to more interpretable topic models leveraging recent advances in dense embeddings. The authors aim to overcome the difficulties of crafting new probabilistic topic models by leveraging modern frameworks for easier model development and evaluation.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity and evaluation issues. The technical details in Section 2 are largely incomprehensible. While the authors state they are building upon existing work in word2vec and LDA, the description lacks a clear, high-level overview accessible to readers familiar with these techniques. Concepts like “separate words from a marginal distribution” and “co-adaptation” are introduced without sufficient explanation. The justification for using the same word vectors for pivot and target, deviating from standard word2vec practice, is also lacking. Several phrases (e.g., “If we only included structure up to this point”) are ambiguous and require clarification. A minor typo ("it's similarity" should be "its") exists. Furthermore, Footnote 1 breaks anonymity.

The primary weakness is the complete absence of quantitative evaluation. The inclusion of example clusters (Figure 3 & 4) and a qualitative discussion of learned relationships (Figure 5) are insufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model. Figure 2’s quantitative content is relegated to the caption, hindering analysis. The conclusion overstates the model's capabilities, claiming it "solves word analogies" based on cherry-picked examples. The reference to Chang et al. is also improperly formatted, missing the venue name.

Despite these weaknesses, the *idea* of combining these approaches and the open-source release are potential strengths. If clarified and rigorously evaluated, this approach could offer a valuable contribution. The model's ability to learn both dense and sparse representations within a unified framework is promising. However, the current presentation and lack of validation prevent a positive assessment.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more accessible, high-level explanation of the model architecture in Section 2, clearly outlining how it builds upon existing word2vec and LDA techniques?
2.  What is the rationale behind using the same word vectors for pivot and target words, and how does this differ from the standard word2vec approach?
3.  Please provide a detailed quantitative evaluation of the model, including metrics beyond topic coherence.  Consider metrics for word similarity and downstream task performance.
4.  Could you elaborate on the meaning of “separate words from a marginal distribution” in the context of your model?
5.  Can you explain in more detail what is meant by “co-adaptation” and how your approach addresses it?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors should remove the anonymity-breaking footnote.
*   Correct the reference formatting error for Chang et al.
*   The phrase “If we only included structure up to this point” needs to be clarified: what “structure” is being referred to?
