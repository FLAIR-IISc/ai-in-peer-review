

# Review: "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
The paper introduces "lda2vec," a model attempting to combine word embeddings (from word2vec) with topic modeling (from LDA). The authors claim to create a model that jointly learns dense word vectors with Dirichlet-distributed latent document-level mixtures of topic vectors, producing sparse, interpretable document representations. The paper presents the model as a hybrid approach that preserves the semantic richness of word embeddings while maintaining the interpretability of topic models.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel architectural combination of word2vec and LDA that claims to produce interpretable document representations
2. A practical implementation within automatic differentiation frameworks
3. Some qualitative examples of topic discovery in two text corpora

## Strengths
1. The core idea of combining word embeddings with topic modeling is conceptually interesting and addresses a genuine gap between two major paradigms in NLP. The potential for creating interpretable representations that capture semantic relationships is valuable for domain scientists who need to understand their models.

2. The implementation appears to be straightforward within modern automatic differentiation frameworks, which is a practical advantage over traditional topic modeling approaches that require deriving custom approximations.

3. The paper provides some qualitative examples of topics discovered in the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets, which demonstrate the model's ability to identify meaningful themes.

## Weaknesses
1. **Technical incoherence and poor exposition**: The paper suffers from fundamental conceptual and technical flaws that prevent understanding. Section 2 is incomprehensible to readers familiar with word2vec and LDA. The key innovation - using the same word vectors for both pivot and target - contradicts the standard word2vec approach without explanation. The phrase "separate words from a marginal distribution" is undefined, as is "co-adaptation," and the ambiguous phrase "If we only included structure up to this point" leaves readers confused about what structure is being referred to. The paper needs a complete rewrite of Section 2 to provide a clear high-level picture.

2. **Lack of proper evaluation**: The paper claims strong results but provides no quantitative evaluation. The topic coherence results in Figure 2 are only mentioned in the caption without proper statistical analysis or comparison to baselines. The "word analogy" results in Figure 6 are based on a few cherry-picked examples and do not constitute a meaningful evaluation of the model's ability to solve word analogies. The conclusion overstates results by claiming the model "solves" word analogies, which is not demonstrated.

3. **Unclear motivation and introduction**: The third paragraph of the introduction is particularly confusing. It claims "crafting a new probabilistic topic model requires deriving a new approximation" but doesn't clarify what approximation is being derived, why prototype development is time-consuming, or why feature evaluation is easier. The paper fails to establish a clear motivation for the specific design choices.

4. **Critical technical errors**: The paper contains a clear typo ("it's similarity" should be "its") and a reference formatting error (Chang citation missing venue name). Footnote 1 breaks anonymity, which is unacceptable in a double-blind review.

5. **Misleading claims**: The paper claims to "solve word analogies" based on a few cherry-picked examples in Figure 6, which is misleading as no systematic evaluation is provided. The conclusion overstates the model's capabilities without proper evidence.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states "the same word vectors are used in both the pivot and target representations" (Section 2.1), which contradicts the standard word2vec approach. Please clarify the motivation for this design choice and provide evidence of its benefits over the standard approach.

2. Please explain what is meant by "separate words from a marginal distribution" and what "co-adaptation" refers to in the context of this model.

3. The phrase "If we only included structure up to this point" (Section 2.2.1) is ambiguous. Please clarify what structure is being referred to and why it's important.

4. The paper claims to "solve word analogies" but provides only a few cherry-picked examples. Please provide a quantitative evaluation of the model's ability to solve word analogies compared to standard word2vec and other methods.

5. The reference formatting error for Chang et al. (2009) should be corrected to include the venue name (Proceedings of the 2009 Conference on Empirical Methods in Natural Language Processing).
