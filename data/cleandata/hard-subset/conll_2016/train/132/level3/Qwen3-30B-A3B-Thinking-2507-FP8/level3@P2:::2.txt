

# Review for "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary
The paper proposes lda2vec, a model that extends word2vec's skipgram architecture to learn document representations that combine the semantic richness of word embeddings with the interpretability of topic models. The approach jointly learns word vectors, topic vectors, and document-topic proportions in a shared embedding space, using a Dirichlet likelihood with low concentration parameter to enforce sparsity in document-topic mixtures.

## Main Contributions
1. A unified framework that bridges word embeddings and topic models, enabling joint learning of word vectors, topic vectors, and sparse document-topic mixtures in a common embedding space.
2. A practical implementation that leverages automatic differentiation frameworks (like Chainer) for efficient training with GPU acceleration, significantly reducing the development time for probabilistic topic model variants.
3. Demonstrated ability to learn coherent topics and meaningful semantic relationships within specialized corpora, as evidenced by examples from the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets.

## Strengths
1. The model successfully integrates two complementary approaches (word embeddings and topic models) into a unified framework that preserves both semantic richness and interpretability. The examples in Figures 3-6 effectively demonstrate meaningful topic representations and semantic relationships within specialized domains.
2. The implementation simplicity and compatibility with automatic differentiation frameworks represent a significant practical contribution, lowering the barrier to adoption for researchers interested in hybrid topic modeling approaches.
3. The paper provides a clear motivation for combining these approaches, highlighting the interpretability advantages of topic models over dense vector approaches while leveraging the semantic richness of word embeddings.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper lacks any empirical evaluation or direct comparison to standard topic models (LDA) or neural topic models (Cao et al. 2015, Nguyen et al. 2015, Shamanta et al. 2015). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed model actually improves upon existing approaches.
2. The GitHub link is included in the paper, violating anonymity requirements for CONLL_2016 submission guidelines and potentially warranting rejection.
3. The examples in Figures 3-6 appear to be cherry-picked without clear explanation of selection criteria, raising concerns about the representativeness of the results. The paper does not address how the most coherent topics were selected for presentation.
4. The computational feasibility of the method for large vocabularies is questionable, as the paper claims to compute topic-word similarities via pairwise comparisons with the full vocabulary, which would be prohibitively expensive for large datasets.
5. The paper does not address how to handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms when applying the model to new documents, which is a critical consideration for practical deployment.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide a direct comparison of lda2vec's topic coherence scores with those of LDA and other neural topic models (Cao et al. 2015, Nguyen et al. 2015, Shamanta et al. 2015) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, including standard error estimates?
2. Please clarify the tokenization process using SpaCy - which specific token types or noun-chunks are extracted, and how multiword terms are handled.
3. How were the specific examples in Figures 3-6 selected, and what criteria were used to ensure they represent the typical output of the model?
4. What is the computational complexity of computing topic-word similarities as described in Section 2.2.1, and how does it scale with vocabulary size?
