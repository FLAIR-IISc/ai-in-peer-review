

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes lda2vec, a model that integrates Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings to learn joint document and word representations. The primary contribution is a method that produces sparse, interpretable document mixtures through a non-negative simplex constraint while jointly learning word vectors and topic vectors. The second contribution is a simple implementation approach compatible with automatic differentiation frameworks. The third contribution is the demonstration of the model on two datasets showing topic coherence and semantic relationships. However, the novelty of the model itself is questionable due to unclear technical details.

**Strengths**  
1. **Implementation Simplicity**: The model is straightforward to implement within modern automatic differentiation frameworks (e.g., Chainer), which could accelerate prototyping for future topic modeling research. The authors demonstrate this by providing open-source code, which is a significant practical contribution.

2. **Interpretable Document Representations**: The model successfully produces sparse document-topic mixtures (as shown in Figure 3) that align with human-interpretable topics, such as "Space" or "Encryption" in the Twenty Newsgroups dataset. This addresses a key limitation of dense neural document representations.

3. **Demonstrated Semantic Relationships**: The model learns meaningful word relationships specific to the Hacker News corpus (Figure 6), such as "Silicon Valley - California + Technology" and "Bitcoin - Digital Currency." This shows the model preserves semantic regularities in domain-specific language.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Technical Incomprehensibility**: Section 2 is critically unclear. The paper fails to provide a high-level explanation of the model's key innovations relative to existing work. Terms like "co-adaptation," "separate words from a marginal distribution," and "structure up to this point" are undefined and confusing. The model's core mechanism (e.g., why the same word vectors are used for pivot and target) is not justified, contradicting standard word2vec practice.

2. **Lack of Quantitative Evaluation**: The paper claims "high mean topic coherences" (Figure 2), but the coherence values are only reported in the caption without supporting analysis. No statistical comparisons to baselines (e.g., LDA, other topic models) are provided. The qualitative examples (Figures 3-6) are insufficient to validate the model's performance.

3. **Overstated Claims**: The conclusion claims the model "solves word analogies," but Figure 6 only shows a few cherry-picked examples without any systematic evaluation (e.g., accuracy on standard analogy datasets). This overstates the paper's contributions.

4. **Unclear Motivation**: The introduction's third paragraph claims that "crafting a new probabilistic topic model requires deriving a new approximation," but fails to explain *why* this is a problem or how automatic differentiation specifically resolves it. This weakens the paper's rationale for the proposed approach.

5. **Formatting and Anonymity Issues**: Footnote 1 references a GitHub repository, breaking anonymity. The Chang et al. citation is incomplete (missing venue name), and "it's similarity" should be "its."

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does lda2vec differ from existing models like LDA2VEC (Yao et al., 2016) or the "topic2vec" approach (Bolukbasi et al., 2016), which also combine topic models with word embeddings?  
2. Why does the model use the *same* word vectors for pivot and target tokens, contrary to standard word2vec practice? What evidence supports this design choice?  
3. What statistical significance tests were performed to confirm that the topic coherence values in Figure 2 are meaningfully better than baselines?  
4. How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary words during inference, given the use of domain-specific tokens (e.g., "DDG" in Hacker News)?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper's technical depth is insufficient for a conference like CONLL, where clarity and reproducibility are critical. The key innovation—using a Dirichlet likelihood to enforce sparsity—lacks novelty, as similar approaches exist (e.g., in LDA with sparse priors). The experimental evaluation is inadequate for a 2016 NLP conference, where rigorous quantitative comparisons are expected.
