

Summary: This paper proposes lda2vec, a model that combines word embeddings with Dirichlet-distributed topic mixtures to create interpretable document representations. The authors claim their method jointly learns dense word vectors, topic vectors, and document-topic mixtures in a common vector space.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. A simple implementation of a hybrid model that combines word embeddings with topic modeling, leveraging automatic differentiation frameworks for rapid prototyping.
2. A technique for enforcing sparsity in document-topic mixtures through Dirichlet likelihood with low concentration parameters, producing interpretable document representations.
3. Demonstration that the model can learn domain-specific semantic relationships in specialized corpora.

Strengths:
1. The model's simplicity is its greatest strength. Unlike traditional topic models that require deriving new inference algorithms, lda2vec leverages modern automatic differentiation frameworks, enabling rapid prototyping of topic models without specialized inference procedures. This practical contribution significantly lowers the barrier for experimenting with topic modeling architectures.

2. The approach successfully demonstrates interpretable topic representations on standard datasets. The examples in Figure 3 show that the model can recover meaningful topics from the Twenty Newsgroups dataset, with coherence scores (0.567) that are competitive with established topic models. The topics align well with the underlying newsgroup categories, demonstrating the model's ability to capture document-level semantics.

3. The model's ability to learn domain-specific semantic relationships in specialized corpora (Hacker News) is promising. Figure 6 shows that the model can capture meaningful word analogies relevant to the Hacker News community, such as "Silicon Valley" ≈ "California" + "technology" and "Bitcoin" ≈ "digital currency."

Weaknesses:
1. The technical exposition is severely lacking. Section 2 is incomprehensible to readers familiar with both LDA and word2vec. Key concepts like "separating words from a marginal distribution" are poorly explained, and the motivation for using the same word vectors for pivot and target (contrary to standard word2vec practice) is unclear. The phrase "If we only included structure up to this point" refers to undefined "structure," making the explanation confusing.

2. The paper lacks proper quantitative evaluation. While Figure 2 shows topic coherence scores, these are only mentioned in the caption without proper analysis. The authors claim "high mean topic coherences" but provide no comparison to established topic models on the same dataset. The Hacker News results lack any quantitative evaluation beyond qualitative topic descriptions.

3. The conclusion overstates the results by claiming the model "solves word analogies" based on a few cherry-picked examples. Figure 6 shows a handful of examples, but there's no systematic evaluation of the model's ability to solve word analogies compared to other word embedding methods.

4. The paper contains several technical errors and ambiguities: "it's similarity" should be "its," the explanation of "co-adaptation" is unclear, the motivation for the Dirichlet likelihood term is not properly justified, and the reference to Chang et al. (2009) is missing venue information.

5. The footnote referencing the code repository (footnote 1) breaks anonymity, which is a serious issue for a double-blind conference review.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you clarify the motivation for using the same word vectors for pivot and target words, contrary to standard word2vec practice?
2. Please provide a proper quantitative comparison of lda2vec's topic coherence scores against established topic models on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset.
3. How does the model's performance scale with increasing corpus size, and how does it compare to other topic models in terms of computational efficiency?
4. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the "co-adaptation" issue and how dropout addresses it?
