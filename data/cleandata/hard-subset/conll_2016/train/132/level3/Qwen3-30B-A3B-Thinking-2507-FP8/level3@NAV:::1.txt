

This paper proposes "lda2vec," a model combining word2vec and LDA to create interpretable document representations. The authors claim to jointly learn dense word vectors with Dirichlet-distributed latent document-level mixtures of topic vectors, claiming this yields sparse, interpretable document representations while preserving semantic regularities.

The main contribution claimed is a simple hybrid model that bridges the interpretability of topic models with the semantic richness of word embeddings. However, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate this contribution due to significant issues.

Strengths: The core idea of combining topic models with word embeddings has potential value, particularly for applications requiring human-interpretable document representations. The model's simplicity of implementation within automatic differentiation frameworks is a minor positive.

Weaknesses: 
1) The technical description in Section 2 is incoherent and requires a complete rewrite to provide a clear high-level picture for readers familiar with word2vec and LDA. Key concepts like "co-adaptation" and "separate words from a marginal distribution" are undefined.
2) The paper lacks quantitative evaluation - only example clusters are provided, with Figure 2's quantitative content limited to captions. The conclusion overstates results by claiming the model "solves word analogies" based on cherry-picked examples.
3) The introduction's third paragraph is unclear regarding what approximation is derived, why prototype development is time-consuming, and why feature evaluation is easier. The motivation for using the same word vectors for pivot and target (contrary to word2vec) is not justified.
4) Several critical errors exist: a typo ("it's similarity"), a footnote breaking anonymity, and a reference formatting error (Chang citation missing venue name).

The paper's claims about the model's capabilities are significantly overstated relative to the evidence provided. The model appears to be a straightforward extension of existing techniques without sufficient novelty or compelling evaluation to justify the claims made. The lack of clear technical exposition and quantitative results severely undermines the paper's contribution.
