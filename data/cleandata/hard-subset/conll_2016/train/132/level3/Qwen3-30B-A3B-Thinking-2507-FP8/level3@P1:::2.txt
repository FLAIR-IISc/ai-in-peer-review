

Summary: The paper proposes lda2vec, a model that extends word2vec's skipgram architecture to jointly learn dense word vectors and sparse, interpretable document-topic mixtures. The authors claim to produce document representations that are both semantically meaningful (through word vector relationships) and interpretable (through Dirichlet-constrained topic mixtures). The main contribution is the integration of neural word embedding techniques with topic modeling to create a unified representation space.

Strengths:
1. The model design is elegant in its simplicity, leveraging automatic differentiation frameworks to avoid the complex derivations typically required for new probabilistic models. This enables rapid prototyping of hybrid architectures and facilitates computational efficiency through GPU utilization.
2. The examples in Figures 3-6 demonstrate compelling topic coherence and semantic regularities in the Hacker News corpus, suggesting the model successfully captures both document-level themes and word-level relationships specific to the domain. The discovered topics (e.g., "Housing Issues," "Bitcoin," "Functional Programming") align well with the corpus content.
3. The approach to sparsifying document-topic mixtures through a Dirichlet likelihood with low concentration parameter (α = n⁻¹) effectively produces interpretable document representations, addressing a key limitation of dense neural document representations.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks empirical evaluation and direct comparison to standard topic models (LDA) and neural topic models (Cao et al. 2015; Nguyen et al. 2015; Shamanta et al. 2015). The topic coherence scores (Figure 2) are reported without comparison to baseline methods, making it impossible to assess whether the improvements are significant. A supervised document classification task using the learned topic representations would provide stronger evidence of utility.
2. The GitHub link in the paper (https://github.com/cemoody/lda2vec) violates CONLL's double-blind review policy, which would typically warrant rejection. The authors should remove this reference before resubmission.
3. The selection process for the example topics in Figures 3-6 is unclear, raising concerns about potential cherry-picking. Without a clear methodology for selecting these examples, the reader cannot assess whether they represent the typical performance of the model.
4. The computational feasibility of calculating topic-word similarities (by comparing each topic vector to the full vocabulary) is questionable for large corpora. The paper does not address the O(n²) complexity of this approach, which would become prohibitive for large vocabularies.
5. The paper fails to discuss how the model handles out-of-vocabulary terms when applied to novel documents, which is critical for real-world deployment. The tokenization procedure using SpaCy (Section 3.2) is described but lacks detail on how multiword terms are handled and how the vocabulary is constructed.

Questions to Authors:
1. How were the specific topics in Figures 3-6 selected? Please provide a clear methodology for topic selection to address concerns about cherry-picking.
2. What is the computational complexity of calculating topic-word similarities, and how does this scale to larger corpora? Can you provide evidence that this approach is feasible for practical applications?
3. How would the model handle out-of-vocabulary terms when applied to novel documents, particularly when word vectors are no longer updated during inference?
4. Can you provide direct comparisons to LDA and other neural topic models on standard benchmarks, including both topic coherence and downstream task performance?

Minor issues: The paper contains several grammatical errors (e.g., "it's" vs "its"), the phrase "topic basis" is awkwardly phrased, and Figure 5's caption should refer to "terms" rather than "words." Reference formatting is inconsistent, with some entries missing conference information (e.g., Röder et al. 2015). The claim that "20 topics fits 20 Newsgroups is unsurprising" is valid, but the paper should include an information-theoretic comparison to the true labels or a direct LDA comparison to justify this observation.
