- Proposes lda2vec to combine Dirichlet topic models with word embeddings for sparse, interpretable document mixtures and joint word/topic vectors, with simple autodiff-friendly implementation and demos on two datasets, but novelty is questionable due to unclear details.
- Strength: Simple to implement in automatic differentiation frameworks and authors provide open-source code.
- Strength: Produces sparse, human-interpretable document-topic mixtures (e.g., clear topics in Twenty Newsgroups).
- Strength: Learns domain-specific semantic relationships (examples from Hacker News show meaningful analogies).
- Weakness: Section 2 is unclear and lacks high-level explanation; key terms and core mechanisms (e.g., using same vectors for pivot/target) are undefined and unjustified.
- Weakness: Insufficient quantitative evaluation—coherence scores reported without analysis, baselines, or statistical tests.
- Weakness: Overstated claims about solving word analogies based on a few cherry-picked examples without systematic evaluation.
- Weakness: Unclear motivation for using autodiff to avoid deriving approximations and inadequate explanation of why that is a problem.
- Weakness: Formatting and anonymity problems (GitHub footnote breaks anonymity), incomplete citation, and minor grammar errors.
- Question: How does lda2vec differ from existing hybrid models like LDA2VEC or topic2vec?
- Question: Why use the same word vectors for pivot and target tokens, contrary to standard word2vec practice, and what evidence supports this choice?
- Question: What statistical significance tests were performed to validate the reported topic coherence improvements versus baselines?
- Question: How are out-of-vocabulary or domain-specific tokens handled during inference (e.g., "DDG" in Hacker News)?
- Additional comment: Technical novelty is limited (Dirichlet sparsity is not new) and experimental evaluation is inadequate for a conference-level contribution.
