- Summary: Proposes lda2vec, combining skip-gram word2vec with Dirichlet-based topic modeling to jointly learn word vectors, topic vectors, and sparse document-topic proportions.

- Contribution 1: A unified framework that bridges word embeddings and topic models, enabling joint learning of word, topic, and document-topic representations in a shared space.

- Contribution 2: A practical implementation using automatic differentiation frameworks (e.g., Chainer) for efficient GPU training, reducing development time for probabilistic topic-model variants.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates coherent topics and meaningful semantic relationships on Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News corpora.

- Strength 1: Successfully integrates semantic richness of embeddings with interpretability of topic models; example figures show meaningful topic representations.

- Strength 2: Implementation simplicity and autodiff compatibility lower the barrier to adoption for researchers.

- Strength 3: Clear motivation for combining interpretability of topic models with dense-vector semantic richness.

- Weakness 1: Lacks empirical evaluation or direct comparison to standard LDA or neural topic models, so improvement over existing methods is unverified.

- Weakness 2: Inclusion of a GitHub link violates CONLL_2016 anonymity requirements and could warrant rejection.

- Weakness 3: Figures 3–6 appear cherry-picked with no explanation of selection criteria, raising concerns about representativeness.

- Weakness 4: Computational feasibility is questionable because topic-word similarities are computed via pairwise comparisons with the full vocabulary.

- Weakness 5: No discussion of handling out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms when applying the model to new documents.

- Question 1: Please provide direct comparisons of lda2vec topic coherence with LDA and neural topic models on Twenty Newsgroups, including standard errors.

- Question 2: Clarify the SpaCy tokenization process—what token types or noun-chunks are extracted and how multiword terms are handled.

- Question 3: Explain how examples in Figures 3–6 were selected and what criteria ensure they represent typical model output.

- Question 4: What is the computational complexity of computing topic-word similarities as described, and how does it scale with vocabulary size?
