

# Review of "Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes "lda2vec," a model that extends word2vec to learn document embeddings while sparsifying them to mimic topic models. The authors claim to combine the interpretability of topic models with the semantic richness of word embeddings, producing sparse, interpretable document representations through a Dirichlet-optimized non-negative simplex constraint. The primary contributions are: (1) a novel framework for jointly learning document representations, topic vectors, and word vectors in a shared space, (2) a method for producing interpretable topic representations that can be directly compared to traditional topic models, and (3) an implementation that integrates seamlessly with automatic differentiation frameworks.

## Strengths
1. **Conceptual Innovation**: The idea of blending topic models with word embeddings is conceptually appealing and addresses a genuine gap between two complementary approaches to text representation. The model's ability to produce both dense word vectors and sparse document-topic mixtures offers a potentially valuable middle ground between neural and probabilistic approaches.

2. **Intuitive Examples**: The examples in Figures 3-6 demonstrate that the model can discover meaningful topics (e.g., "Space," "Encryption," "X Windows") that align with domain knowledge, suggesting the approach has merit for practical applications where interpretability matters.

3. **Implementation Simplicity**: The paper correctly notes that the model can be easily incorporated into existing automatic differentiation frameworks, which is a significant practical advantage for researchers wanting to experiment with hybrid approaches.

## Weaknesses
1. **Critical Lack of Empirical Evaluation**: The paper fails to provide any direct comparison to standard topic models (LDA) or neural topic models (Cao et al. 2015, Nguyen et al. 2015, Shamanta et al. 2015). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to evaluate whether the proposed method actually improves upon existing approaches in terms of topic coherence, interpretability, or utility. The authors merely report topic coherence scores without context or comparison.

2. **Anonymity Violation**: The paper includes a GitHub link (https://github.com/cemoody/lda2vec) in the text, which violates the conference's anonymity requirements and should be removed for review.

3. **Unsubstantiated Claims and Cherry-Picking**: The selection process for examples in Figures 3-6 is completely unclear, raising serious concerns about potential cherry-picking. The claim that "20 topics fits 20 Newsgroups" is unsurprising without an information-theoretic comparison to true labels or a direct LDA comparison.

4. **Computational Feasibility Concerns**: The method's reliance on pairwise comparisons with the full vocabulary to compute topic-word representations appears computationally infeasible for large vocabularies. The paper provides no discussion of how this is managed in practice.

5. **Technical Gaps**: The tokenization details using SpaCy are vague (which token types or noun-chunks are extracted, how multiword terms are handled), and the paper completely omits discussion of handling OOV terms when applying the trained model to novel documents.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you plan to address the critical lack of empirical comparison to standard and neural topic models? What specific metrics and baselines will you use for this comparison?

2. Could you clarify the tokenization procedure used for the Hacker News corpus, specifically how multiword terms are handled and how the resulting vocabulary differs from the Palmetto tool's requirements?

3. What is your approach to handling out-of-vocabulary terms when applying the trained model to novel documents?

4. Given the computational concerns about pairwise comparisons for topic-word representations, how do you address the scalability of your approach for large vocabularies?

5. Could you provide the anonymized GitHub link for your implementation (removing the actual URL) to verify your method's reproducibility while maintaining anonymity?

## Additional Comments
The paper contains several minor issues that need addressing: "it's" vs. "its" usage errors, an awkward "topic basis" sentence, and Figure 5 caption should say "terms" not "words." Reference formatting is inconsistent, with some entries (e.g., Roder et al.) missing conference information. The topic coherence results in Figure 2 are presented without comparison to LDA, making it difficult to assess the model's relative performance. Without addressing these critical weaknesses, particularly the lack of empirical comparison, the paper's claims cannot be substantiated.
