Summary
The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid between SGNS word embeddings and LDA-style document-topic mixtures. The model learns word vectors and a set of topic vectors in a shared space; each document vector is a simplex-constrained mixture of topic vectors, with a Dirichlet prior encouraging sparsity. The context vector for predicting targets is the sum of the pivot word vector and the document vector, thus combining local and global context. The paper presents qualitative topics on 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News, reports topic coherence (Cv) for 20NG, and shows word analogies specialized to the HN corpus. The implementation is built with an automatic differentiation framework.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, interpretable formulation that marries SGNS with LDA-like document mixtures: document vectors are convex combinations of shared topic vectors under a Dirichlet prior, while word, topic, and document vectors live in one space.
2) A practical recipe for training this hybrid end-to-end with standard autodiff and negative sampling, together with qualitative case studies on two corpora and some topic coherence reporting.
3) A design choice to use additive composition (document + pivot) for contexts, enabling topic-word similarity queries and domain-specific analogies in the shared space.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and interpretability: The simplex-constrained document-topic proportions and the Dirichlet sparsity provide familiar, LDA-like semantics. Embedding topics and words in the same space makes topic inspection straightforward via nearest neighbors.
- Practicality and simplicity: The approach requires only a small modification to the SGNS objective. It is easy to implement in modern frameworks, supports minibatching and GPUs, and leverages well-understood components (negative sampling, dropout, Adam).
- Cross-domain qualitative plausibility: On 20NG, example topics align with known newsgroups and achieve reasonable Cv coherences; on HN, the discovered topics look salient and the analogy examples suggest that domain-specific semantics are captured.
- Sensible modeling choices: The additive context (document + pivot) matches known additive behaviors of word vectors; the Dirichlet prior with α < 1 encourages sparse, interpretable mixtures; sharing the space between words and topics enables direct topic-word inspection.

Weaknesses
- Lack of quantitative evaluation and baselines: There is no systematic comparison against standard topic models (e.g., LDA, NMF), embedding-based document methods (e.g., doc2vec, simple averaged embeddings), or neural topic models (e.g., contemporary 2015 neural topic modeling work). Beyond per-model Cv on 20NG, there are no extrinsic evaluations. Suggested: (a) document classification on 20NG using learned document mixtures; (b) clustering agreement with ground-truth groups (NMI/AMI/ARI); (c) topic coherence/diversity vs LDA; (d) retrieval (document-document and document-word); (e) ablations (no Dirichlet, dense doc vectors, no word+doc additive context).
- Potential cherry-picking of qualitative results: Figures 3–6 show hand-picked topics and analogies without a pre-registered selection protocol. This risks selection bias. Provide full topic lists, random topic samples, aggregate coherence distributions (mean/median/variance), topic diversity measures, and analogy accuracy over a defined benchmark (or at least randomized test sets from the corpus).
- Anonymity violation: The submission includes a GitHub URL and dataset links that reveal authorship, which violates double-blind policy and may warrant desk rejection unless removed for review and replaced with anonymous links or an “available upon acceptance” statement.
- Missing related work and comparisons: The paper should position itself against prior neural/topic hybrid models and neural topic models (e.g., 2015-era neural topic modeling and topical embeddings) and compare empirically. It should also clarify how it differs from prior combinations of embeddings with topic modeling (e.g., using embeddings as priors or constraints).
- Inference and OOV/new-doc handling not specified: It is unclear how to infer document-topic proportions for unseen documents given a trained model (optimize p over the simplex with fixed word/topic vectors? one-step projection? amortized inference?). Handling of OOV terms at test time (especially for HN with a large, specialized vocabulary) is not described. Sensitivity to α and λ and stability across random initializations are also not reported.

Questions to Authors
- Quantitative comparisons: Can you report Cv and topic diversity versus LDA/NMF on 20NG, and a document classification or clustering evaluation using the learned mixtures? How does lda2vec compare to doc2vec and to neural topic models from 2015?
- Inference for new documents: What is your test-time procedure to infer p for unseen documents? Do you freeze word/topic vectors and optimize p via gradient steps on the simplex? How long does this take and how stable is it?
- OOV handling: How do you handle unseen tokens at test time? Do you use UNK, subword composition, or retrofitting?
- Selection protocol: How were the topics and analogy examples in Figures 3–6 chosen? Can you provide random samples and aggregate metrics?
- Computational complexity: For the HN vocabulary (~110k), how do you compute top tokens per topic and topic coherence efficiently? Any approximations (ANN, matrix multiplication with partial sorting)? What are training and inference runtimes vs LDA/doc2vec?
- Hyperparameters and ablations: Please report sensitivity to α and λ, the effect of tying input/output embeddings, dropout rates, and the negative sampling exponent β. What breaks if the Dirichlet term is removed?

Additional Comments
- Using 20 topics on 20NG is expected; please quantify alignment to ground-truth labels (e.g., mutual information, purity) across a range of topic counts and include an LDA baseline with identical preprocessing.
- Tokenization details need to be fully specified for reproducibility: spaCy version/settings, lowercasing, lemmatization, stopword removal, noun chunking rules, phrase vocabulary construction, and how multiword terms are handled across train/test.
- Minor writing issues: fix “it’s”→“its” where appropriate; clarify the “topic basis are also strongly affected” sentence; Figure 5 caption should refer to “terms” rather than “words.”
- References: Ensure consistent formatting and complete venue information (e.g., Röder et al., EMNLP/ACL venues as applicable), and verify all citations are correct and disambiguated.
- Ethics/policy: Remove de-anonymizing links for review; provide an anonymized code archive or defer code release until after acceptance.

Overall evaluation
The idea is appealing and practical, with nice qualitative demonstrations, but the current submission lacks the quantitative rigor and baselines expected for publication and violates anonymity. With thorough empirical comparisons, a clear inference story for new documents, and corrected anonymization, this work could be a strong contribution; in its current form, I lean toward rejection.
