This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that jointly learns word embeddings (via a skip-gram with negative sampling objective) and sparse, interpretable document representations (via simplex-constrained mixtures over a set of learned topic vectors). The context vector used to predict targets is the sum of a pivot word vector and a document vector; each document vector is a convex combination of topic vectors, with sparsity encouraged by a Dirichlet-inspired penalty. Experiments on 20 Newsgroups report average topic coherence (Cv), and a larger Hacker News corpus illustrates qualitative topics and word analogies.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A simple architectural formulation that ties SGNS word embeddings to LDA-style document-topic mixtures by summing word and document vectors, with document vectors constrained to the probability simplex and regularized to be sparse. This yields a single embedding space for words, topics, and documents.
- Practical recipe for training the hybrid objective in an automatic differentiation framework with negative sampling, dropout, and a Dirichlet-like sparsity penalty over document-topic proportions.

Strengths
- Clear and potentially useful idea: aligning interpretability (sparse document-topic mixtures) with the linear semantic structure of embeddings in a single space. The additive context vector is a straightforward and elegant mechanism to couple local lexical context and global document context.
- Interpretability affordances: because topics, words, and documents share a space, nearest-neighbor lists for topics are easy to compute and interpret, and document memberships are on the simplex, enabling percentage-style attributions. This addresses a common shortcoming of dense document representations.
- Empirical signals that the idea can work: on 20 Newsgroups, reported average topic coherence (Cv) is reasonable, and the qualitative topic examples (Space, Encryption, X Windows, Middle East) look plausible. On Hacker News, topic exemplars and nearest neighbors reflect corpus-specific semantics (e.g., Bitcoin, compensation, gadget hardware).
- Implementation feasibility: the training objective is compatible with standard autodiff/SGD tooling and GPUs and is described at a level that should be implementable by practitioners. The approach avoids bespoke variational inference derivations, which lowers the barrier to experimentation with hybrid models.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and exposition: Section 2 is hard to follow and needs a complete rewrite to provide a clean high-level picture for readers familiar with word2vec and LDA. Several terms are used imprecisely or without definition (e.g., “separate words from a marginal distribution,” “co-adaptation”), and phrases are ambiguous (e.g., “If we only included structure up to this point”—which structure?). The introduction’s paragraph about “deriving a new approximation” and prototype development time is unclear: what approximation is meant here (variational, sampling?), and how does autodiff change the trade-offs?
- Modeling choices insufficiently justified: using a single shared set of word vectors for both pivot and target departs from common SGNS practice; the motivation and empirical impact are not discussed. The “Dirichlet likelihood” applied to softmax outputs is nonstandard; the choice α = 1/n and λ = 200 appears ad hoc, and no sensitivity analysis is provided. Dropout on document and pivot vectors is mentioned but not motivated or ablated.
- Evaluation is too weak to support the claims: there are no baselines (e.g., LDA, SGNS+separate document embeddings, doc2vec) or ablations (no-Dirichlet, dense document vectors, separate input/output embeddings). Figure 2’s quantitative content is only in the caption, and there is no comparative coherence vs LDA or topic models trained under similar preprocessing. Hacker News results are entirely qualitative, and the claim that the model “solves word analogies” is overstated given a handful of cherry-picked examples and no standard analogy evaluation. No measures of document-topic sparsity, topic diversity, or stability across runs are reported.
- Reproducibility and details: important training details are missing (batch sizes, learning rates, early stopping, windowing and subsampling choices, handling of rare words, how negative sampling distribution is implemented). Mixing pretrained vectors (20NG) and random initialization (HN) introduces confounds without discussion. Inference for new documents (holding topics fixed) is not described.
- Presentation and policy issues: typo (“it’s similarity” → “its”); footnote with a non-anonymous code link breaks double-blind review; reference formatting errors (e.g., Chang et al. missing venue), and inconsistent citation styles.

Questions to Authors
- Please rewrite Section 2 to provide a concise, high-level overview before equations: what are the learnable parameters, what is optimized, and how does the document-topic simplex constraint interact with SGNS?
- Why use a single set of word vectors for both pivot and target? Did you try separate input/output embeddings, and how did it affect performance, coherence, or analogies?
- What exactly is meant by “separate words from the marginal distribution” and “co-adaptation” in this context? Provide formal definitions or intuitive explanations tied to the loss.
- Justify the Dirichlet-inspired regularizer: why α = 1/n and λ = 200? Provide sensitivity curves (coherence and sparsity vs α, λ) and an ablation without this term. How sparse do document-topic vectors become in practice?
- Provide quantitative comparisons: topic coherence vs LDA (same preprocessing), and embedding quality vs SGNS/doc2vec (e.g., standard analogy or similarity benchmarks). Include ablations on (i) no document vector, (ii) dense document vectors (no simplex), (iii) no dropout.
- How is inference performed for unseen documents given fixed topics and word vectors? Is optimization required, or is there an encoder?
- For Hacker News, can you compute coherence using an external corpus compatible with your vocabulary (e.g., Wikipedia) or map phrases to tokens to enable Cv, or alternatively report PMI-based internal coherence?

Overall, the core idea is appealing and practically motivated, but the current submission needs substantially clearer exposition and a much stronger, controlled empirical evaluation (with baselines and ablations) to support its claims.
