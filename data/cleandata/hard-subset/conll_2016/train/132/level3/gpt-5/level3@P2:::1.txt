Summary
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that jointly learns dense word embeddings (via a skip-gram with negative sampling objective) and interpretable document representations constrained to be sparse mixtures over a set of learned topic vectors (via a Dirichlet prior on simplex-constrained document-topic proportions). The context vector for predicting target words is the sum of the pivot word vector and the document vector, thereby tying local word co-occurrence with document-level themes. The paper presents qualitative topics on Twenty Newsgroups (20NG) and Hacker News, reports average topic coherence (Cv) for 20NG in a figure caption, and demonstrates a few corpus-specific word analogies.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple hybrid objective that combines SGNS word embeddings with Dirichlet-regularized, simplex-constrained document-topic mixtures, yielding sparse, interpretable document representations while co-training word/topic vectors.
2) An implementation-friendly formulation suitable for automatic differentiation frameworks and GPUs, with an available codebase (but see anonymity concern).
3) Qualitative demonstrations on 20NG and Hacker News; for 20NG, limited quantitative reporting of topic coherence.

Strengths
- Potentially useful hybrid of interpretability and distributional semantics: The model preserves SGNS-style linearities in word vectors while enforcing sparse, LDA-like document-topic mixtures. This addresses a long-standing trade-off between interpretability (LDA) and expressivity/transfer (embeddings).
- Conceptual simplicity and implementability: The objective is the sum of a standard SGNS loss and a Dirichlet log-likelihood on simplex-constrained document-topic proportions. This should be easy to implement and extend in AD frameworks, making it attractive to practitioners.
- Qualitative interpretability of learned topics: The 20NG examples (“Space,” “Encryption,” etc.) and Hacker News topics (“Bitcoin,” “Compensation,” etc.) are coherent and align with intuitive themes, suggesting the approach can discover salient structure in diverse corpora.
- Document–word vector space alignment: By tying document vectors (as topic mixtures) and word vectors in the same space, the method enables direct inspection of topic–word similarity and supports the intended interpretability story.
- Practical details provided: The paper specifies negative sampling settings, dropout, Dirichlet hyperparameters, and preprocessing/tokenization choices, which can help reproduction (modulo anonymity).

Weaknesses
- Clarity of the technical presentation (major): Section 2 needs a substantial rewrite. Several core notions are unclear or undefined for readers familiar with word2vec/LDA:
  - Motivation and implications of using the same embeddings for pivot and target (departing from word2vec’s two-matrix setup).
  - The phrase “separate words from a marginal distribution” is imprecise; what exact separation is meant relative to the negative sampling distribution u^β?
  - “Co-adaptation” is mentioned without definition or context; explain what failure mode dropout is preventing here.
  - “If we only included structure up to this point” is ambiguous—explicitly enumerate what components are included/excluded at each step.
  - The introduction’s third paragraph is confusing about “deriving a new approximation” (what approximation?), why prototyping is time-consuming, and why AD makes feature evaluation easier; connect this concretely to your model.
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation (major): The paper lacks comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., LDA, GloVe+LDA/DocNADE/NVDM, doc2vec, topic-embeddings hybrids). The only quantitative metric (Cv) is reported in a figure caption without baselines, variance, or significance. No downstream tasks (classification, retrieval), no perplexity/surrogate likelihood, no ablations (e.g., Dirichlet sparsity on/off, shared vs separate word matrices), and no topic interpretability user study. The qualitative clusters and a handful of analogies are insufficient to support claims.
- Overstated claims in the conclusion: The paper asserts the model “solves word analogies” based on cherry-picked examples without quantitative analogy benchmarks or comparisons to SGNS/GloVe; this should be toned down or substantiated.
- Anonymity violation (major): Footnote 1 links to the public code repository with author-identifying information, breaking double-blind review.
- Reference and presentation issues: At least one reference entry is malformed (e.g., Chang et al. missing venue). Minor typographical errors exist (“it’s similarity” → “its”). Figure 2’s numeric results appear only in the caption; place key quantitative results in the main text with discussion.

Questions to Authors
- Model clarity:
  - Why tie input and output embeddings (shared word vectors) rather than use separate matrices as in SGNS? Did you compare performance or coherence with separate matrices?
  - Precisely define “co-adaptation” in your context and explain how dropout mitigates it here.
  - Clarify the phrase “separate words from the marginal distribution” with a mathematical explanation tied to the negative sampling objective and u^β.
  - Explicitly list the model components present before adding the topic-mixture constraint when you say “structure up to this point.”
  - In the introduction, what “approximation” are you referring to (e.g., variational inference for LDA variants)? Explain concretely how AD removes that burden in your model.
- Evaluation:
  - Provide quantitative comparisons of topic coherence (Cv, NPMI) versus LDA and at least one neural topic model on 20NG, with multiple seeds and significance.
  - Add an ablation study: no Dirichlet term (α=1), no simplex constraint, separate vs shared embeddings, with/without dropout.
  - Report downstream performance (e.g., document classification on 20NG using document mixtures; retrieval using topic/word vectors).
  - For analogies, evaluate on standard benchmarks and compare to SGNS trained on the same corpora; include accuracy and any trade-offs due to the shared embedding.
- Practical aspects:
  - Complexity and scalability: How does training time/memory compare to SGNS + LDA trained separately? What is the cost of maintaining document-topic mixtures during SGNS training?
  - Sensitivity to λ and α: How robust are sparsity/Coherence/accuracy to these hyperparameters?

Additional Comments
- Fix anonymity (remove/reblind code link until camera-ready).
- Correct typos and clarify prose (e.g., “its” vs “it’s”), and move key numeric results into the main text with discussion.
- Improve references formatting and completeness (venues, years).
- Consider adding topic visualizations (top words, word–topic nearest neighbors) and quantitative topic diversity metrics.

Overall, the idea is promising and practically appealing, but the paper in its current form needs significant clarifications and substantially stronger quantitative evaluation and comparisons to meet the bar.
