Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that augments the SGNS/word2vec objective with document vectors constrained to be sparse mixtures over learned topic vectors. Word, topic, and document vectors share the same embedding space; document-topic proportions are encouraged to be sparse via a Dirichlet prior (α < 1). The model is implemented in an automatic differentiation framework and demonstrated qualitatively on 20 Newsgroups and a large Hacker News comments corpus.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple architectural formulation that joins SGNS with Dirichlet-regularized document-topic mixtures, yielding interpretable, sparse document representations while maintaining word-embedding linearities.
2) Qualitative case studies showing topics and token analogies on two corpora; limited intrinsic evaluation via topic coherence (Cv) on 20 Newsgroups.
3) An implementation suitable for autodiff frameworks (with code availability, though this currently breaks anonymity).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: The model is an elegant blend of SGNS and a Dirichlet-constrained topic mixture over a small set of topic vectors, making it easy to implement and likely easy to extend within modern autodiff frameworks. The design choice of sharing the space for words, topics, and documents is appealing for interpretability and downstream use.
- Interpretability via sparse mixtures: The simplex constraint and Dirichlet regularization directly target interpretable document-topic proportions. The qualitative topics on 20 Newsgroups appear sensible, and the idea to retrieve top tokens by cosine/dot similarity to topic vectors is intuitive and inspectable.
- Joint semantic space enables analysis: Because topics and words lie in the same space, one can do both topic inspection and word-analogy style probing. The Hacker News examples (e.g., tech, compensation, Bitcoin, fonts) illustrate that the space captures community-specific semantics.
- Potential for reproducibility and adoption: Positioning the method in a standard autodiff stack and releasing code (once anonymized) can facilitate replication and broader impact.

Weaknesses
- Lack of rigorous empirical evaluation and baselines: The paper does not compare against standard topic models (e.g., LDA) or recent neural topic models (e.g., Cao et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2015; related 2015-era neural topic variants). There is no extrinsic document-level evaluation (classification, clustering, retrieval) nor intrinsic comparisons beyond reporting a single coherence metric. For 20 Newsgroups, reporting that 20 topics work best is unsurprising and not informative without comparisons to LDA/variants on coherence, clustering metrics (e.g., NMI/ARI against newsgroup labels), or downstream classification using document-topic features.
- Anonymity violation: The inclusion of a GitHub repository and data links breaks double-blind review. This is a serious issue and, as-is, could warrant rejection independent of technical quality. The paper should be anonymized, and an anonymous supplemental link provided per policy.
- Evidence appears cherry-picked and incomplete: Only a small selection of topics is shown, and the criteria for selecting Figures 3–6 are not described. There is no distribution of topic coherences, no per-topic coherence table, and no comparison to baselines. Without comprehensive reporting (e.g., top-10 words for all topics in an appendix, mean/median/stdev of coherence, coverage of low-quality topics), the qualitative narratives risk selection bias.
- Missing computational and scalability details: The paper suggests computing similarity between each topic vector and all tokens to list “top tokens,” but does not discuss the computational method, complexity, or runtime/memory on the large vocabulary (110k tokens). There is no training time, hardware, or throughput report. Clarifying whether this is done via efficient matrix operations or ANN search matters for practical feasibility.
- Preprocessing and inference details are underspecified: The SpaCy-based tokenization for Hacker News (noun chunks, entities) is only briefly referenced. Important choices (case normalization, phrase detection criteria, filtering, handling of numbers/URLs) are not fully described. It is also unclear how out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms and new documents are handled at test time: how are document proportions inferred for unseen documents with fixed topics/embeddings, and are word vectors updated or kept fixed?

Questions to authors
- Baselines and tasks: Can you add quantitative comparisons to LDA and contemporary neural topic models on 20 Newsgroups? At minimum: topic coherence (Cv, UMass, UCI) with significance tests; clustering quality vs. ground-truth labels (NMI/ARI) using the MAP topic assignment; and a simple document classification task (train a linear classifier on document-topic proportions).
- Sensitivity and ablations: How sensitive are results to λ, α, number of topics, dropout, negative sampling exponent β, and number of negative samples? Please provide ablations (e.g., no Dirichlet term, dense document vectors without simplex, topics without sparsity).
- Inference for unseen documents and OOV: How do you infer pjk for new documents? Do you optimize p with fixed T and word embeddings, or use an encoder? How do you handle OOV words/phrases at inference?
- Computational considerations: What are the training times, hardware, and memory footprints for both corpora? How are top-k words per topic computed efficiently at large vocabularies? Any use of approximate nearest neighbor search?
- Tokenization details: Please specify exactly which SpaCy features are used (noun chunks, entities), phrase canonicalization/lemmatization rules, stopword handling, and whether multiword terms are merged deterministically or statistically.
- Anonymity: Will you provide an anonymized code archive and defer linking to identifiable repositories until after review?

Additional comments
- Writing: Several grammar/phrasing issues (e.g., “it’s” vs. “its”; the phrase “topic basis are also strongly affected” is awkward). Figure 5 caption should say “terms,” not “words.” Please proofread.
- References: Formatting is inconsistent; some entries are incomplete (e.g., missing venues for Röder et al. 2015 and others). Please standardize to the conference style and ensure all citations are accurate.
- Reporting: For 20 Newsgroups, please report full topic coherence distributions, not just means, and include LDA/CTM/neural baselines. Showing the top-10 words for all topics (appendix) will alleviate cherry-picking concerns.
- Scope: Perplexity is not directly applicable with SGNS; however, consider complementary intrinsic metrics (e.g., word intrusion/topic intrusion tests or human evaluation) to support the interpretability claim.

Overall: The modeling idea is neat and potentially impactful because of its simplicity and interpretability, but the current submission lacks the quantitative rigor and baseline comparisons expected for acceptance. With proper anonymization, stronger evaluations, and clearer experimental/methodological details, this work could be much more compelling.
