Summary
This paper proposes lda2vec, a neural topic model that augments the SGNS (word2vec) objective with document vectors constrained to be sparse mixtures over learned topic vectors on the probability simplex via a Dirichlet prior. The context vector is a sum of the pivot word vector and a document vector, so word and topic representations live in the same space. The paper presents qualitative topic examples on 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News, and reports Palmetto Cv coherence on 20 Newsgroups.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A simple neural architecture that enforces sparse, simplex-constrained document–topic mixtures while jointly learning word, topic, and document embeddings in a shared space.
- Empirical illustrations showing interpretable topics and corpus-specific word analogies; a practical recipe for implementing this in an autodiff framework.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: The additive context (word + document) is intuitive, maintains the linear compositionality of word2vec, and the Dirichlet prior is a straightforward way to induce sparse, interpretable topic proportions.
- Interpretability: The learned topics for 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News are coherent to the eye, and the shared embedding space allows natural inspection of topic–term similarity.
- Implementation accessibility: The model fits naturally into standard autodiff toolchains, making prototyping and extension straightforward for practitioners.
- Potential utility: If validated, this approach could offer a practical middle ground between dense doc embeddings and probabilistic topic models for applications requiring interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Lack of empirical evaluation and baselines: There is no quantitative comparison against standard topic models (e.g., LDA, CTM) or prior neural topic models (Cao et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2015; Shamanta et al., 2015). The sole metric (Cv on 20NG) is reported without baselines or statistical tests. This severely limits claims about effectiveness.
- Insufficient downstream assessments: No evaluations on supervised document classification using topic proportions, document retrieval/similarity benchmarks, clustering metrics (e.g., NMI/ARI with 20NG labels), or information-theoretic alignment (e.g., mutual information between topics and true labels). The finding that 20 topics fit 20 Newsgroups is unsurprising without comparison to LDA or other NTMs.
- Anonymity breach: Publishing the public GitHub and dataset links in the submission breaks double-blind review and may warrant rejection unless removed or anonymized.
- Selection bias concerns: The paper shows hand-picked examples (Figures 3–6) with no description of how they were selected or of overall distributional quality (e.g., proportion of coherent topics). This invites concerns about cherry-picking.
- Computational concerns: Computing topic–word similarity by pairwise comparisons with the full vocabulary can be costly (O(KVd)) and does not scale for large V or K. There is no discussion of practical top-k retrieval, approximate nearest neighbors, or batching on GPU.
- Preprocessing ambiguity: Tokenization via SpaCy is underspecified. It is unclear which token types are included, how noun-chunks/entities are extracted, how multiword terms are segmented and normalized, and how this differs across the two corpora.
- OOV and inference on new documents: The paper does not explain how to handle OOV terms or how to infer document proportions for unseen documents (e.g., fixing word/topic vectors and optimizing proportions only, learning an inference network, or using an EM-style procedure).
- Reference and presentation issues: References are inconsistently formatted and some entries lack venue information (e.g., Röder et al.). Several grammar/phrasing issues (“it’s” vs. “its”; the “topic basis” sentence is awkward). Figure 5’s caption should say “terms,” not “words.”

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Quantitative baselines: Can you report comparisons to LDA and prior neural topic models (Cao et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2015; Shamanta et al., 2015) on:
  - Topic coherence (Cv, UMass) on 20NG (same preprocessing).
  - Supervised classification using inferred document–topic proportions on 20NG and another dataset.
  - Document similarity/retrieval (e.g., 20NG, Reuters, or other public similarity sets).
  - Clustering alignment with ground-truth (NMI/ARI) and mutual information between topics and labels.
- Ablations: What is the effect of removing the Dirichlet term (α=1) or varying α and λ on sparsity and coherence? What is the impact of summation (word + doc) vs. using only doc vectors?
- Inference for new documents: With trained word and topic vectors fixed, how do you infer proportions for unseen documents? What optimization procedure, initialization, stopping criteria, and runtime do you observe? How are OOV tokens handled (UNK, subwords, hashing)?
- Tokenization specifics: Precisely define SpaCy-based tokenization:
  - Which token types are kept/filtered (stopwords, punctuation, numbers, URLs)?
  - For noun-chunks/entities, how are multiword terms normalized and de-duplicated? How do you handle overlapping spans?
  - Are lemmatization and casing consistent across corpora?
- Computational scalability: How do you compute top-k nearest terms for topics efficiently at scale? Do you use matrix multiplication with batched top-k, ANN libraries (e.g., FAISS), or inverted indices? What are the time/memory costs for V≈100k, K≥100?
- Anonymity: Can you remove or anonymize all identifying links (code, datasets) for the review version?

Additional Comments
- Please correct grammar and phrasing (“its” not “it’s”; revise the “topic basis” sentence for clarity). Update Figure 5 caption to “terms.” Ensure consistent reference formatting with full venue details (e.g., Röder et al., EMNLP 2015).
- Reporting: Provide the full distribution of topic coherences (mean, median, variance) and the proportion of coherent topics, not only selected examples. Include per-topic top terms with a non-cherry-picked sampling protocol.
- Practical guidance: Report training/inference runtimes, memory footprints, and sensitivity to hyperparameters (β, dropout, number of negatives).
- Reproducibility: For the camera-ready, release code and data with instructions—but ensure full anonymization for review.

Overall, the modeling idea is appealing and potentially useful, but the current submission lacks the empirical rigor and comparisons needed for publication. Substantial additions to evaluation, baselines, and clarity are required.
