Summary
The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that combines Skip-gram with Negative Sampling (SGNS) word embeddings and Dirichlet topic mixtures. A document vector is defined as a mixture over learned topic vectors (simplex-constrained via softmax) and is added to the pivot word vector to form the context vector used in the SGNS objective. A Dirichlet likelihood with low concentration is added to encourage sparse document–topic mixtures. The model is implemented in an autodiff framework (Chainer). Experiments on 20 Newsgroups report topic coherence scores; a large Hacker News corpus is used for qualitative topic and analogy examples.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple objective that fuses SGNS with a sparse, interpretable document–topic mixture, embedding words, topics, and documents in a shared space.
2) Qualitative evidence that topics inferred are interpretable via nearest-neighbor words; limited quantitative evidence via Cv on 20NG.
3) An implementation in an autodiff framework that purportedly eases prototyping (though it breaks anonymity in the submission).

Strengths
- Potentially interesting hybrid: Combining SGNS with a Dirichlet-constrained document mixture is conceptually appealing. The additive context (word + document) is a natural way to blend local and global semantics.
- Interpretability via shared space: Having topic vectors live in the same space as words enables intuitive inspection (nearest words to a topic), which is valuable for practitioners.
- Some quantitative topic coherence: Reporting Cv on 20NG, with sensitivity to the negative sampling exponent, is an appropriate first step toward quantifying interpretability.
- Scalable, simple training: The formulation should be easy to implement with negative sampling and autodiff; in principle this lowers the barrier to exploring topic–embedding hybrids.

Weaknesses
- Technical exposition is opaque: Section 2 needs a complete rewrite. For readers familiar with SGNS and LDA, the high-level picture is buried and key choices are unexplained. Specific issues:
  • The model uses the same word vectors for pivot and target, contrary to SGNS practice (distinct input/output embeddings). No motivation, theory, or ablation is provided.
  • “This loss is minimized when the observed words are completely separated from the marginal distribution.” The meaning of “separate words from a marginal distribution” is unclear; please formalize what separation means in the SGNS objective.
  • “Co-adaptation” is mentioned to justify dropout, but the phenomenon and its relevance here are not explained; dropout rates are not reported.
  • “If we only included structure up to this point” is ambiguous. Presumably “structure” refers to using context = word + document without the topic-mixture constraint; please state this explicitly.
- Insufficient empirical evaluation: There is no comparison to strong baselines (LDA with the same preprocessing; paragraph vectors; SGNS + post-hoc topic models; doc-topic neural models). The paper relies on a few example clusters and hand-picked analogies. Figure 2 provides only average Cv and no baselines, variance, or significance. No downstream evaluations (document classification, clustering purity, retrieval), no quantitative analogy accuracy, and no ablations (e.g., shared vs separate input/output embeddings; with/without Dirichlet sparsity; with/without document addition).
- Unclear claims in the Introduction: The third paragraph asserts that “deriving a new approximation” is time-consuming for probabilistic topic models, and that autodiff avoids this and “expedites” feature evaluation. It is unclear what approximation is being derived here (variational inference? sampling?) and how autodiff replaces it; the model does not present a probabilistic inference derivation. The link between autodiff and “easier feature evaluation” needs justification.
- Weak justification of key hyperparameters: α = 1/n and λ = 200 are asserted without principled motivation or sensitivity analyses. The minibatch scaling of the Dirichlet term is ad hoc; its impact on training dynamics is not evaluated.
- Overstatement of results: The conclusion claims the model “solves word analogies” based on a small set of cherry-picked examples; there is no quantitative analogy benchmark. This overstates the evidence.
- Anonymity and presentation issues: Footnote 1 links to a public repo, breaking anonymity. There is a typo (“it’s similarity” → “its similarity”). References have formatting errors; e.g., Chang et al. missing the venue name.

Questions to Authors
- Why use a single shared embedding matrix for pivot and target roles? Please justify theoretically or empirically, and provide an ablation comparing shared vs separate embeddings.
- What exactly is meant by “separating observed words from the marginal distribution” in the SGNS loss? Can you restate this in terms of maximizing log σ(c·w) and minimizing log σ(−c·w−) for negatives?
- What are the dropout rates for document and word vectors, and how sensitive is performance to these rates? What co-adaptation are you observing in practice?
- Please clarify “If we only included structure up to this point.” Does this refer to the model without the topic-mixture constraint and Dirichlet term?
- What “approximation” is being derived in traditional topic models that your approach avoids? Are you claiming to replace variational/sampling inference with direct optimization of point estimates? If so, please make that explicit.
- How are α and λ selected? Provide sensitivity analyses and guidance for practitioners. How do you scale Ld in minibatches, and does this produce a biased objective?
- Provide quantitative baselines: LDA topic coherence on the same preprocessed 20NG; doc2vec or SGNS+doc vectors; classification accuracy using document representations; standard word analogy accuracy.
