Summary
The paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid objective that augments SGNS with a simplex-constrained document representation: each document is a mixture over learnable topic vectors, with sparsity encouraged via a Dirichlet term. The model jointly learns word, topic, and document vectors in a shared space, aiming to combine word2vec’s linear regularities with LDA-like interpretability. Qualitative results are shown on 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Formulation of a simple hybrid objective that ties SGNS to sparse document–topic mixtures via a Dirichlet prior, placing topics in the same embedding space as words.
2) Practical recipe for implementing and optimizing the hybrid loss in autodiff frameworks.
3) Qualitative case studies suggesting interpretable topics and corpus-specific linear regularities.

Strengths
- Conceptual appeal: Bridging interpretable topic mixtures with distributional word embeddings is timely and potentially useful for exploratory analysis and downstream modeling.
- Interpretability mechanism: Simplex-constrained document mixtures and shared topic–word space enable inspecting “topic neighborhoods” via nearest-neighbor words, a clear usability advantage over dense doc vectors.
- Qualitative plausibility: Example topics on 20NG (e.g., Space, Encryption) look coherent; reported Cv values (~0.55–0.57) are within a reasonable range for this corpus.
- Implementation accessibility: The objective is easy to express with negative sampling and a Dirichlet regularizer, lowering the barrier for practitioners (though see anonymity concerns).

Weaknesses
- Clarity and exposition: Section 2 is very hard to follow. A clear high-level formulation is missing; key symbols (pivot/target/context, distributions) are under-defined. The phrase “If we only included structure up to this point” is ambiguous; please explicitly enumerate what structure is meant.
- Unclear/inaccurate statements: “separate words from a marginal distribution” is confusing (do you mean discriminate observed pairs from negatives drawn from u^β?). “Co-adaptation” is invoked without explanation. The Introduction’s third paragraph does not justify what approximation is avoided, why prototypes are time-consuming, or why feature evaluation becomes easier.
- Method choices lack motivation/ablations: Using the same word vectors for pivot and target departs from standard SGNS without justification; no ablation compares shared vs separate embeddings, the Dirichlet term (λ, α), dropout, or β.
- Evaluation is insufficient: No quantitative comparisons to LDA, GloVe+LDA, PV-DBOW, CTM, or neural topic models. Figure 2’s numbers appear only in the caption; no confidence intervals or statistical tests; no held-out likelihood, topic intrusion, classification tasks, or standard word-analogy/similarity benchmarks. The conclusion overstates claims (“solves word analogies”) based on cherry-picked examples.
- Anonymity and writing issues: Footnote 1 reveals a GitHub repo; remove for double-blind review. Typo: “it’s similarity” → “its similarity.” References have formatting errors (e.g., Chang et al. missing venue).

Questions to Authors
- Provide a precise objective with all terms and distributions defined, and a high-level diagrammatic explanation. What exactly is optimized for p_jk (softmax params) and how is the Dirichlet term scaled per minibatch?
- Why share pivot/target embeddings? What happens if you separate them as in SGNS?
- Please clarify “separate words from the marginal distribution” and “co-adaptation.”
- Add quantitative baselines (topic coherence, intrusion, held-out likelihood), ablations (λ, α, β, shared vs separate embeddings), and downstream tasks to substantiate claims.
- Ensure anonymity and fix reference formatting.
