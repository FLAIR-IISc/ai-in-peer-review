### **Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces lda2vec, a novel model that jointly learns dense word embeddings with interpretable, sparse document representations. The model cleverly extends the skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS) architecture by redefining the context vector. In lda2vec, the context vector for predicting a target word is the sum of the pivot word's vector and a document vector, `~cj = ~wj + ~dj`. This document vector `~dj` is itself constructed as a weighted mixture of a set of latent topic vectors, `~dj = Σ_k p_jk * ~t_k`. To achieve interpretability similar to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), the model incorporates a Dirichlet-likelihood term into the overall loss function, which encourages the document-topic proportions `p_jk` to be sparse. The entire model, including word, document, and topic vectors, is trained jointly within a single embedding space using an automatic differentiation framework. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities through qualitative analysis of topics and word analogies discovered in the 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Hybrid Architecture for Jointly Learning Word, Topic, and Document Embeddings:** The primary contribution is the elegant fusion of word embedding models (specifically, word2vec's SGNS) and topic models. By modifying the context vector to include a document-specific component and decomposing that component into a sparse mixture of topics, the model creates a unified vector space where words, topics, and documents coexist. This allows for direct comparison and interpretation across these different levels of semantic representation.

2.  **A Practical Framework for Prototyping Probabilistic Models:** A significant secondary contribution is demonstrating how a traditionally probabilistic model component (a Dirichlet prior for document-topic mixtures) can be successfully integrated as a loss term within a standard deep learning framework. This approach bypasses the need for complex, model-specific derivations for variational inference or Gibbs sampling, substantially lowering the barrier for researchers to prototype and extend such hybrid models.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Conceptual Elegance and Model Novelty:** The core idea of lda2vec is both intuitive and powerful. The additive composition of word and document vectors (`~wj + ~dj`) to form the context is a simple yet effective way to condition word predictions on both local (word-level) and global (document-level) context. The subsequent decomposition of `~dj` into topic vectors ensures that this global context is structured and interpretable. This unified approach is a novel and promising direction for combining the strengths of distributional word representations and probabilistic topic modeling.

2.  **Strong Qualitative Results and Interpretability:** The paper provides compelling qualitative evidence that the model works as intended. The topics discovered in both the 20 Newsgroups and Hacker News datasets (Figures 3 and 4) appear thematically coherent and meaningful. Furthermore, the model retains the ability of SGNS to learn semantic and syntactic regularities, as demonstrated by the word similarity (Figure 5) and analogy tasks (Figure 6). This combination of high-level thematic discovery and low-level word semantics in a single model is a key strength.

3.  **Potential for Broader Impact through Ease of Implementation:** By building the model within a modern automatic differentiation framework (Chainer), the authors showcase a practical and accessible methodology. This makes the work more easily reproducible and extensible for the research community. The provided open-source code further enhances its potential impact, although its inclusion in the submission is problematic (see Weaknesses).

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Complete Lack of Quantitative Evaluation and Comparison:** This is the most significant flaw of the paper. Despite proposing a new topic model, there is no empirical comparison to any baseline, such as standard LDA, other topic models, or even other neural document embedding methods like doc2vec. The evaluation is limited to topic coherence scores and qualitative examples. The paper would be substantially stronger with experiments on downstream tasks (e.g., document classification, information retrieval) or intrinsic evaluations (e.g., held-out log-likelihood/perplexity) against established models. Without this, it is impossible to gauge where lda2vec stands in terms of performance.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Prior Work:** The paper fails to discuss or compare itself against a body of relevant prior work on neural topic models that also aim to integrate word embeddings, such as A Joint Model of Topics and Topic-Specific Word Embeddings (Cao et al., 2015), An Interactive Topic Modeling (Nguyen et al., 2015), or A Neural Topic Model for Text (Shamanta et al., 2015). Situating lda2vec within the context of these related approaches is crucial for understanding its specific contributions and novelty.

3.  **Potential for Cherry-Picking and Unclear Experimental Details:** The qualitative examples presented in Figures 3-6 are convincing, but the authors provide no details on how these specific topics, words, and analogies were selected. This raises concerns about potential cherry-picking of the best results. Furthermore, several key implementation details are unclear. For instance, computing the top words for a topic by finding `argmax_i(~t_k · ~wi)` implies an exhaustive comparison against the entire vocabulary, which is computationally infeasible for large vocabularies. The tokenization process for the Hacker News corpus is also vaguely described.

4.  **Anonymity Violation:** The paper includes a footnote with a direct link to a GitHub repository, which violates the double-blind reviewing policy. This is a serious procedural issue that may warrant rejection on its own.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Can you provide a quantitative comparison of lda2vec against standard LDA and other relevant neural topic models on established benchmarks? Performance on downstream tasks like document classification or document similarity would be particularly valuable.
2.  Could you clarify the process used to select the examples shown in Figures 3, 4, 5, and 6 to address concerns about potential cherry-picking?
3.  How are the top N words for a given topic vector identified in practice? Does your method involve an expensive search over the entire vocabulary, and if so, how does this scale?
4.  The finding that n=20 topics works best for the 20 Newsgroups corpus is expected. Have you considered evaluating how well the learned document-topic distributions align with the ground-truth newsgroup labels, for instance, by using metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI)?
5.  How does the trained model generate representations for new, unseen documents? Is the entire network retrained, or is there a mechanism for inferring document-topic proportions for a new document while keeping word and topic embeddings fixed?
