**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a neural-styled topic model, lda2vec, that extends word2vec to learn document embeddings and sparsifies them to mimic topic models. The model combines the strengths of both word embeddings and topic models, allowing for interpretable document representations while capturing semantic regularities between tokens. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on the Twenty Newsgroups and Hacker News comments datasets.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. The paper proposes a novel neural-styled topic model that extends word2vec to learn document embeddings and sparsifies them to mimic topic models.
2. The model is able to capture semantic regularities between tokens while providing interpretable document representations.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on two datasets, showing that it can discover coherent topics and learn linear relationships between words.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The model is simple to implement and can be easily incorporated into existing automatic differentiation frameworks.
2. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their model and its components.
3. The paper includes a thorough discussion of related work and the strengths and limitations of the proposed model.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The paper lacks empirical evaluation or direct comparison to standard/neural topic models, making it difficult to assess the model's performance.
2. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the examples in Figures 3-6 were selected, which may indicate cherry-picking.
3. The paper does not discuss how to handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms when applying the trained model to novel documents after embedding updates.
4. The authors do not compare their model against prior neural topic models, which is a significant omission.
5. The paper contains minor issues, such as grammar and phrasing errors, and inconsistent reference formatting.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the examples in Figures 3-6 were selected, and whether they are representative of the overall performance of the model?
2. How do you plan to handle OOV terms when applying the trained model to novel documents after embedding updates?
3. Can you provide a comparison of your model against prior neural topic models, such as those proposed by Cao et al. (2015), Nguyen et al. (2015), and Shamanta et al. (2015)?
4. How do you plan to address the issue of computational infeasibility when computing topic-word representations by pairwise comparisons with the full vocabulary?
5. Can you provide more details on the tokenization process using SpaCy, including which token types or noun-chunks are extracted and how multiword terms are handled?

**Additional Comments**

The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing, but it requires more rigorous evaluation and comparison to existing models. The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their model.
