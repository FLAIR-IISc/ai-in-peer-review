**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel model, lda2vec, that combines the strengths of word2vec and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to learn dense word vectors and sparse, interpretable document representations. The model uses a hybrid approach to jointly train word, topic, and document vectors in a common representation space. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of lda2vec on two datasets: the Twenty Newsgroups corpus and the Hacker News comments corpus.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Hybrid model**: The paper proposes a novel combination of word2vec and LDA, which allows for the joint training of word, topic, and document vectors in a common representation space.
2. **Sparse document representations**: The model uses a Dirichlet-likelihood term to encourage sparse document representations, which are more interpretable than dense representations.
3. **Linear relationships between tokens**: The model learns linear relationships between tokens, which can be used to solve word analogies.

**Strengths**

1. **Interesting combination of word2vec and LDA**: The paper proposes a novel combination of two popular models, which can potentially lead to new insights and applications.
2. **Effective use of automatic differentiation frameworks**: The authors take advantage of modern automatic differentiation frameworks to quickly develop and train the model.
3. **Good qualitative results**: The paper presents good qualitative results on two datasets, including example topics and word analogies.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Technical details are unclear**: The technical details in Section 2 are incomprehensible and need a complete rewrite to provide a clear high-level picture for readers familiar with word2vec and LDA.
2. **Motivation for using the same word vectors for pivot and target is unclear**: The paper does not provide a clear motivation for using the same word vectors for pivot and target, which is contrary to word2vec.
3. **Lack of quantitative evaluation**: The paper does not provide a thorough quantitative evaluation of the model, relying instead on example clusters and a few cherry-picked word analogies.
4. **Conclusion overstates results**: The conclusion overstates the results by claiming that the model solves word analogies based on a few examples.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a clear motivation for using the same word vectors for pivot and target?
2. Can you provide more details on the Dirichlet-likelihood term and how it encourages sparse document representations?
3. Can you provide a more thorough quantitative evaluation of the model, including metrics such as perplexity and topic coherence?
4. Can you provide more examples of word analogies and demonstrate how the model can be used to solve them in a more systematic way?
