

**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model that jointly learns word embeddings and document representations through a hybrid approach combining Dirichlet-distributed topic mixtures with skip-gram word embeddings. The primary contributions are: (1) a simple framework that integrates topic modeling with word embeddings using automatic differentiation, producing interpretable document-topic mixtures; (2) demonstration that this approach yields coherent topics with high topic coherence scores comparable to state-of-the-art topic models; and (3) evidence that the model learns meaningful word relationships in specialized domains, as demonstrated through word analogy tasks.

**Strengths**

1. The paper successfully demonstrates a practical integration of topic modeling with word embeddings that preserves both the interpretability of topic models and the semantic richness of distributed word representations. The model achieves high topic coherence (0.567 on Twenty Newsgroups with 20 topics) that correlates with human evaluations, as validated by the Palmetto coherence measure. This is a significant contribution as it bridges two important paradigms in NLP without requiring complex model re-engineering.

2. The implementation is notably straightforward within automatic differentiation frameworks, which addresses a key limitation of traditional topic modeling approaches. The authors provide code and documentation, making the model accessible for replication and extension. The simplicity of incorporating the Dirichlet prior into the existing skip-gram framework is a major practical contribution that lowers the barrier to adoption.

3. The Hacker News experiments convincingly demonstrate the model's ability to capture domain-specific semantic relationships and topic structures. The word analogy results (e.g., "Silicon Valley" = "California" + "technology") show that the model learns meaningful linear relationships in specialized domains, which is a valuable capability for domain-specific applications.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper lacks a thorough comparison with closely related work that combines topic modeling and word embeddings. The authors cite some relevant work (e.g., LDA, word2vec), but fail to compare with approaches like "topic2vec" (Miao et al., 2016), "doc2vec" (Le & Mikolov, 2014), or "LDA2Vec" (Huang et al., 2016). Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the novelty and relative performance of lda2vec. The paper claims to be "simple to incorporate into existing automatic differentiation frameworks," but doesn't quantify the computational overhead compared to alternatives.

2. The paper asserts that the Dirichlet prior with α = n⁻¹ produces "sparse, interpretable document mixtures," but provides no quantitative evidence of sparsity (e.g., average non-zero entries per document) or interpretability (e.g., human evaluation of topic quality). The coherence scores are reported, but it's unclear whether these scores are significantly better than standard LDA with the same number of topics. Without this comparison, the claim of "interpretable" representations remains unsubstantiated.

3. The paper claims that "in contrast to continuous dense document representations, this formulation produces sparse, interpretable document mixtures," but doesn't clarify how this differs from other sparse topic modeling approaches (e.g., sparse LDA). The model's document representations are still dense in the vector space (they're sums of topic vectors), though the weights are constrained to be sparse. The paper should better clarify this distinction and demonstrate why their approach is superior to existing sparse topic modeling techniques.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does lda2vec's topic coherence compare to standard LDA (with the same number of topics) on the Twenty Newsgroups dataset? Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the coherence improvements are due to the model architecture or simply the word embeddings.

2. What is the computational cost of the Dirichlet term Ld compared to standard word2vec training? The paper mentions "dramatically improved computational speed" with GPUs, but doesn't provide training time comparisons.

3. The paper states "the document representations are similar to those in traditional LDA models," but doesn't explain how lda2vec's document representations differ from LDA's. In particular, how does the model handle documents that don't fit neatly into a small number of topics?
