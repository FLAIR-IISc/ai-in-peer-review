Summary and contributions
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that augments the SGNS objective with document-level vectors constrained to be mixtures over a small set of latent topic vectors. The document mixtures are mapped to the simplex via softmax and encouraged to be sparse with a Dirichlet log-likelihood term (α < 1). Word, topic, and document vectors are jointly trained and share a common embedding space; the context vector is the sum of the pivot word vector and the document vector. The model is demonstrated on 20 Newsgroups (with topic coherence measured by Cv) and on a large Hacker News comments corpus (qualitative topics and word analogies). Code is released and implemented in an autodiff framework.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, differentiable objective that combines SGNS with sparse, interpretable document-to-topic mixtures, embedding words, topics, and documents in one space.
2) Empirical demonstration that the learned topic vectors are interpretable (20NG coherence; qualitative topics on Hacker News) while preserving word-level linear regularities; open-source implementation in an autodiff/GPU setting.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and implementable: The model is easy to add to existing SGNS code, requires minimal probabilistic machinery, and leverages autodiff/GPUs. The Dirichlet-inspired regularizer and simplex parameterization are straightforward and likely to be reproducible. The released code is a practical plus for the community.
- Interpretability through sparse mixtures: Constraining document vectors to lie on the simplex over topics and adding an explicit sparsity-inducing term is a clear design choice targeting interpretability. The ability to retrieve nearest words to topic vectors (since topics live in the word space) is attractive for qualitative analysis.
- Joint space for words/topics/docs: Sharing the embedding space enables direct inspection of topic-word relations and supports vector arithmetic that appears to preserve known linearities, as illustrated on the domain-specific Hacker News corpus.
- Scalability and domain adaptation: The model scales to a substantially larger corpus (75M tokens, 110k vocab) and learns domain-specific semantics (e.g., font names, tech terms), suggesting viability beyond small benchmark datasets.

Weaknesses
- Missing quantitative baselines: The paper reports 20NG topic coherence (Cv) but does not compare against standard baselines (e.g., LDA, NMF, GloVe+LDA, doc2vec-based topics) under the same preprocessing and number of topics. Without these, it is hard to assess whether lda2vec improves interpretability or coherence relative to established methods.
- Limited evaluation of representation utility: There are no downstream document-level evaluations (e.g., 20NG classification using the learned doc/topic mixtures), nor quantitative word-level evaluations (e.g., standard analogy/similarity benchmarks, in- vs out-of-domain). The qualitative analogies are suggestive but not sufficient to support claims about preserving SGNS regularities.
- Ambiguity around “Dirichlet” treatment and sensitivity: The document proportions are parameterized via softmax (logistic-normal geometry), then regularized with a Dirichlet log-likelihood term. While this is a valid penalty, it is not a probabilistic Dirichlet generative model. The paper fixes α = 1/n and λ = 200 with minimal sensitivity analysis. The degree to which sparsity and coherence depend on α/λ is not established, nor is the trade-off with word embedding quality.
- Lack of ablations for key design choices: It is not shown whether summing document and pivot vectors (cj = wj + dj) is critical over alternatives (e.g., concatenation; gating; attention; doc-only context). The impact of tying pivot/target embeddings, dropout rate, and pretrained vs random initialization is not quantified. The claim that dropping Ld leads to incoherent topics is plausible but not demonstrated quantitatively.
- Topic quality on Hacker News only qualitative: Although phrase tokenization complicates Palmetto, some quantitative topic diagnostics (e.g., intrinsic coherence variants using an in-domain reference, PMI on held-out data, topic diversity) could still be provided. As is, conclusions rely on cherry-picked examples.

Questions to authors
- How does lda2vec’s topic coherence compare to LDA and doc2vec+clustering baselines on 20NG when controlling for preprocessing and topics (n = 20–50)? Please report Cv and topic diversity, with multiple random seeds.
- Can you report 20NG document classification using learned document mixtures (possibly with a simple linear classifier) and compare to LDA/doc2vec baselines?
- How sensitive are topic coherence and embedding quality to α and λ? Please include a small grid and report both coherence and an intrinsic word analogy score.
- Do you tie input/output embeddings for SGNS? If so, what is the impact on performance vs untied matrices?
- How are new/unseen documents handled at inference time? Can you fix topics/word vectors and optimize only document mixtures efficiently, and how stable are those mixtures?
- Do you apply any topic diversity/orthogonality regularization? Redundant topics can harm interpretability; what do you observe empirically?

Additional comments
- Clarify dropout rates and other training hyperparameters. Report training time vs LDA to support scalability claims.
- Consider reporting the norm distribution of topic vectors and whether you normalize vectors during training; large-norm topics can dominate mixtures.
- Some references and figures appear misformatted; please proofread.

Overall recommendation
The idea is appealing and practically useful, but the empirical evaluation is not yet strong enough for a clear assessment of novelty and significance. With solid quantitative comparisons to LDA/doc2vec baselines, downstream evaluations, and ablations/sensitivity analyses for the sparsity term and model design, this could become a compelling contribution.
