Summary and contributions
This paper proposes lda2vec, a hybrid model that augments the Skipgram Negative Sampling (SGNS) objective with document vectors constructed as sparse mixtures over a set of learned topic vectors. The key idea is to (i) embed words, topics, and documents in a shared space so that topics are interpretable via nearest-neighbor words, while (ii) encouraging sparse, nonnegative document–topic proportions via a Dirichlet regularizer on a simplex-constrained softmax. The model is implemented in an automatic differentiation framework and evaluated qualitatively and via topic coherence on 20 Newsgroups and qualitatively on a large Hacker News comments corpus.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, jointly trained architecture that couples SGNS with sparse, interpretable document–topic mixtures in a shared embedding space.
2) An implementation approach that leverages autodiff and GPU training and releases code, making the method accessible and reproducible.
3) Empirical evidence that the model yields coherent topics (on 20NG) and preserves linear regularities/analogies on a large, domain-specific corpus (HN).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: The model is an intuitive extension of SGNS where the context vector is a sum of a pivot word and a document vector, and the document vector itself is a convex combination of topic vectors. This yields a shared space where topics are interpretable by nearest words, and word analogies are preserved. The design is easy to understand and implement.

- Interpretability through sparsity: Enforcing document–topic proportions on the simplex with a Dirichlet regularizer (α < 1) is an effective and well-motivated mechanism for sparsity. The paper shows that removing this term degrades interpretability; with it, topic vectors correspond to coherent sets of words, and document mixtures are concise. This aligns with the stated goal of scientist-friendly, interpretable representations.

- Practicality and reproducibility: The model is implemented with autodiff (Chainer), uses standard optimizers (Adam) and negative sampling, and code is made available with unit tests. This lowers the barrier for adoption and extension by others.

- Evidence of topic quality and domain adaptation: On 20 Newsgroups, average topic coherence (Cv) is reported across several configurations, with best results at 20 topics and β=0.75; example topics align strongly with newsgroup themes. On Hacker News, topics reflect salient community themes and the learned embeddings support meaningful analogies specialized to the domain, indicating successful joint learning.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art combining topics and embeddings: While the coupling of SGNS with sparse document–topic mixtures is neat, similar ideas have appeared (e.g., doc2vec for document embeddings; topical word embedding variants that mix topic models and embeddings). The paper would be stronger with a clearer positioning against such work and discussion of what is new (e.g., the exact objective, shared-space topic interpretability, and Dirichlet-driven sparsity) and what is incremental.

- Evaluation lacks baselines and ablations: Topic coherence is only reported for lda2vec variants; there is no comparison to LDA, doc2vec+LDA, GloVe+LDA, or topical embedding baselines on 20NG. On HN, only qualitative results are shown; coherence is omitted due to vocabulary mismatch. No extrinsic evaluation (e.g., document classification, retrieval) is provided. Ablations (e.g., without Dirichlet term, varying λ and α, separate vs tied input/output embeddings, removing document vector) are discussed qualitatively but without quantitative evidence.

- Methodological clarity gaps: There is an apparent inconsistency in Section 2.2.1: the softmax is said to map latent vectors in R300 onto the simplex for pjk, but the document–topic proportion vector should be in R^n (n topics). This needs clarification. The choice to tie input/output word embeddings in SGNS deviates from common practice; a justification or empirical comparison would help. Hyperparameters (e.g., λ=200, α=1/n, dropout rates) are fixed with limited justification or sensitivity analysis.

- Claims about coherence and interpretability need stronger support: The paper states that removing the Dirichlet regularizer leads to incoherent topics and diffuse document mixtures, but provides no metrics or examples to substantiate the claim. Similarly, while example topics are convincing, averaged coherence values would benefit from variance across runs and comparisons to standard LDA on the same preprocessed data.

- Scalability and efficiency reporting: The model trains for 200 epochs; details about runtime, hardware, and throughput are not reported. The Dirichlet term is scaled to minibatches; a brief complexity analysis and empirical training time would help readers assess scalability to very large corpora and numbers of documents/topics.

Questions to authors
- Related work: How does lda2vec compare empirically and conceptually to topical word embeddings (e.g., TWE) and to pipelines that combine LDA with word2vec or doc2vec? What is unique to your objective and training that those approaches do not provide?

- Softmax/simplex dimensionality: In Section 2.2.1, please clarify the dimensionality of the latent variables passed through softmax to obtain pjk. Should this be R^n (number of topics), not R^300?

- Input/output embedding tying: You tie the word vectors for pivot and target. Did you compare to untied embeddings? How does tying affect performance (coherence, analogy accuracy)?

- Dirichlet hyperparameters: How sensitive are results to λ and α? Can you provide an ablation varying these, and quantitative evidence for the claim that α=1 (no sparsity) reduces coherence?

- Baselines on 20NG: Can you report topic coherence for LDA, GloVe+LDA, and doc2vec+topic models with identical preprocessing, and a simple extrinsic task (e.g., document classification using learned representations) to contextualize performance?

- HN coherence: Could you compute coherence using an intrinsic PMI/NPMI estimator over the HN corpus (rather than Palmetto) to provide quantitative topic quality?

- Training details: Please provide training time per epoch, total time, hardware specs, and negative sampling details (β sensitivity, number of negatives) for both datasets.
