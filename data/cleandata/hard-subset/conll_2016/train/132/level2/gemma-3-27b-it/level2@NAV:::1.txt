## Review of “Mixing Dirichlet Topic Models and Word Embeddings to Make lda2vec”

This paper introduces lda2vec, a model combining Skip-gram word embeddings with Dirichlet-distributed topic mixtures for document representation. The core idea is to learn dense word vectors alongside sparse, interpretable document-topic distributions, leveraging the strengths of both approaches. The authors motivate this by noting the interpretability of topic models versus the density of neural network document representations, and the potential to improve topic models with modern word embeddings.

The primary contribution is a method for jointly learning word and document representations within an automatic differentiation framework, enabling faster prototyping and experimentation. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of this approach on both the Twenty Newsgroups dataset (showing good topic coherence) and a Hacker News corpus (illustrating learned semantic relationships and analogies). Finally, the work presents a practical implementation and insightful analysis of learned representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Combination:** The fusion of Skip-gram with a Dirichlet process for document representation is a reasonable and interesting approach. It addresses a key limitation of purely dense document embeddings – interpretability.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The Twenty Newsgroups experiments demonstrate competitive topic coherence scores. The Hacker News results are compelling, illustrating the model's ability to capture specialized vocabulary and semantic relationships within that domain. The provided examples (word analogies) add a qualitative layer of validation.
3. **Practicality & Implementation:** The authors highlight the ease of implementation using automatic differentiation frameworks (Chainer) and provide open-source code. This facilitates reproducibility and further research.
4. **Clear Motivation & Related Work:** The introduction effectively sets the stage, outlining the limitations of existing methods and positioning lda2vec as a solution. The related work section is comprehensive and relevant.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the Twenty Newsgroups results are good, a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art topic models (e.g., NMF, LDA with more advanced inference techniques) would strengthen the claims.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions parameter tuning but lacks detailed discussion of sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., Dirichlet concentration parameter, learning rate). Exploring this could provide valuable insights.
3. **Scalability Considerations:** While the authors demonstrate the model on a sizable Hacker News dataset, a more thorough discussion of computational complexity and scalability to extremely large corpora would be beneficial. 
4. **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the *combination* is interesting, the individual components (Skip-gram, Dirichlet process) are well-established. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of what aspects are truly novel.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the Dirichlet concentration parameter (α)? Did you explore a wider range of values and analyze the impact on topic coherence and interpretability?
2. What is the computational cost of training lda2vec compared to traditional LDA, particularly as the number of topics and corpus size increase?
3. Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing Chainer specifically? Were other automatic differentiation frameworks considered, and if so, why was Chainer preferred?



Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of topic modeling. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
