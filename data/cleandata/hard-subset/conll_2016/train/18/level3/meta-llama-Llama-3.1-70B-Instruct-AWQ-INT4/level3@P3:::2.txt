This paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA and evaluates several methods, including a transition-based parser. The proposed parser, BSP, supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, making it a reasonable and potentially valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. The introduction of BSP, a transition-based parser that supports the three criteria of broad-coverage semantic structures.
2. The evaluation of several conversion-based parsing methods, including the use of existing parsers on converted representations.
3. The experimentation with UCCA-annotated corpora, which provides a unique opportunity to evaluate the effectiveness of transition-based methods for broad-coverage semantic parsing.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The introduction of a novel parser that supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, which is a significant contribution to the field of semantic parsing.
2. The thorough evaluation of several conversion-based parsing methods, which provides a comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
3. The use of UCCA-annotated corpora, which provides a unique opportunity to evaluate the effectiveness of transition-based methods for broad-coverage semantic parsing.

However, there are also some weaknesses to this paper:

1. The introduction and background sections overstate the importance of UCCA as the only sensible graph-based semantic representation to study. A more balanced discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of different semantic representations would be beneficial.
2. The authors' disagreement with the three proposed criteria for semantic representations (non-terminal nodes, grounded representations, and discontinuous units) is not fully justified. A more detailed discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of each criterion would be helpful.
3. The term "grounded" is overloaded, and the authors should consider alternative terms (e.g., "anchored" or "lexicalized") to avoid confusion.
4. The parser implementation seems acceptable, but the evaluation is unimpressive, with MaltParser outperforming BSP on primary edges and remote-edge F-scores being low.
5. The authors could have further improved the paper by discussing prior work on tree approximations and edge-reconstruction, which could have boosted dependency-tree baselines and further weakened the claimed strength of the BSP parser.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the advantages and disadvantages of the three proposed criteria for semantic representations?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of the overloaded term "grounded" and consider alternative terms?
3. Can you provide more details on the parser implementation and the features used in the BSP parser?
4. How do you plan to improve the evaluation of the BSP parser, particularly on remote-edge F-scores?
5. Can you discuss prior work on tree approximations and edge-reconstruction and how it could have impacted the results of the paper?

Additional comments:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work on semantic parsing and the advantages and disadvantages of different approaches.
* The authors could have provided more details on the UCCA annotation scheme and how it differs from other semantic representations.
* The paper could have benefited from a more comprehensive evaluation of the BSP parser, including an analysis of the errors made by the parser and potential ways to improve it.
