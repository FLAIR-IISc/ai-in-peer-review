

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes a transition-based graph parser for UCCA's semantic representations, arguing that semantic annotation should be graph-based with non-terminal nodes and discontinuity. It situates the work within broad-coverage semantic parsing and UCCA corpora. The authors present three experimental approaches: (1) converting UCCA to constituency trees and using UPARSE, (2) converting to dependency trees and using MaltParser/LSTM, and (3) a direct transition-based graph parser (BSP) supporting all three structural properties. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) the first parser supporting all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units), (2) development of conversion protocols between UCCA and standard representations, and (3) the first experimental exploration of UCCA as a semantic parsing target.

## Strengths
1. **Clear theoretical motivation**: The paper convincingly argues why the three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) are necessary for broad-coverage semantic parsing, with concrete examples illustrating limitations of current approaches.
2. **Practical evaluation on UCCA**: The paper provides the first experimental evaluation of UCCA as a parsing target, demonstrating that it can be parsed with ~70% primary edge F-score, which is promising given UCCA's inter-annotator agreement of 80-85%.
3. **Good data and code availability**: The authors plan to make all converters and parsers publicly available, which is valuable for reproducibility and future work in semantic parsing.

## Weaknesses
1. **Weak claim of novelty**: The paper's claim of being "the first semantic-oriented parser" is unsubstantiated. The transition set doesn't exploit UCCA-specific phenomena, and the parser is essentially a standard transition-based parser adapted for graph structures, not a fundamentally new semantic parsing approach.
2. **Inadequate baselines and evaluation**: The paper fails to compare with prior graph-parsing work (DAGParser, TurboParser) and doesn't use standard metrics from related SDP shared tasks. The conversion to dependency trees loses significant information (non-terminal nodes), and the paper doesn't properly address how this affects semantic interpretation.
3. **Lack of theoretical grounding**: The three proposed principles (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuity) are generic and somewhat ad-hoc. The paper lacks a clearer, broader introduction and fails to articulate whether the goal is a UCCA-specific parser, a general semantic parser, or a parsable semantic scheme.
4. **Ignoring UCCA's limitations**: The paper notes UCCA doesn't model quantifier scope (unlike MRS/DRT), but fails to provide an error analysis to judge how this affects the parser's informativeness and parsability.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide examples of UCCA implicit units that lack textual correspondents, and how these would be handled in the parser?
2. The paper claims UCCA doesn't model quantifier scope. How does this affect the parser's ability to handle sentences like "Every student read a book"?
3. Why didn't the authors compare with existing graph-parsing work (e.g., DAGParser, TurboParser) as baselines, and how would these approaches differ in handling UCCA structures?
4. The paper uses a custom evaluation metric. How does this compare to standard metrics used in semantic dependency parsing (e.g., from SemEval 2014-2015), and why weren't those metrics used?

## Overall Assessment
Despite these significant weaknesses, the paper addresses an important gap in semantic parsing and makes a valuable contribution by exploring UCCA as a parsing target. The work is interesting and would be worth discussing at CoNLL, though it requires substantial revisions to address the methodological concerns and strengthen the claims. The authors should clarify the theoretical motivation, provide more meaningful comparisons, and address UCCA's limitations in more depth. The paper would benefit from introducing "grounded semantic" earlier and considering richer SDP-style conversions (Schlüter et al., 2014) rather than surface dependency trees.
