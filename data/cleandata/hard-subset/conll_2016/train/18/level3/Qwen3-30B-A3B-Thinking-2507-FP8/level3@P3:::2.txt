

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation framework that supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors propose two approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA structures to dependency trees or constituency trees, and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports UCCA's structural properties. As I see them, the main contributions are:

1. First semantic parser for UCCA, addressing a significant gap in semantic parsing literature.
2. Comprehensive comparison between conversion-based approaches and a direct parser, providing practical insights for semantic parsing.
3. Public release of code and data, enabling reproducibility and future research.

**Strengths**

1. **First Semantic Parser for UCCA**: The paper fills a critical gap by introducing the first parser for UCCA, a representation framework that supports complex semantic structures like coordination and discontinuous units. UCCA's cross-linguistic applicability and semantic stability in translation (Sulem et al., 2015) make it a valuable framework for semantic parsing research.

2. **Thorough Comparative Evaluation**: The authors provide a comprehensive comparison between conversion-based approaches (to dependency trees and constituency trees) and a direct parser. This evaluation helps clarify trade-offs between different approaches and provides practical guidance for future work on semantic parsing.

3. **Open Science Commitment**: The authors make their code and data publicly available, which is a significant contribution to the community and enables reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Overstated Claims about UCCA**: The introduction overstates UCCA as "the only" sensible graph-based semantic representation to study, ignoring other frameworks like SDP (Broad-coverage Semantic Dependency Parsing) that support some of the proposed structural properties. This is misleading and needs to be balanced.

2. **Unconvincing Criteria for Semantic Representations**: The paper's three proposed criteria (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) are not convincingly argued. Prior work suggests non-terminal nodes may not add expressive power (e.g., dependency parsing often handles coordination through head selection), and the advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR are unclear. The authors should provide stronger evidence for why these properties are necessary.

3. **Overloaded Terminology**: The term "grounded" is overloaded and should be replaced with more precise terminology like "anchored" or "lexicalized" to avoid confusion with other uses of the term in NLP literature.

4. **Unimpressive Evaluation Results**: The evaluation results are underwhelming. MaltParser (a standard dependency parser) outperforms BSP on primary edges (73.2% vs. 62.4% F-score), and remote-edge F-scores are low (15.3% for BSP). This undermines the claimed significance of the BSP parser.

5. **Overlooked Prior Work**: The authors overlook prior work on tree approximations and edge-reconstruction (e.g., Agic et al. 2015) that could significantly boost dependency-tree baselines, further weakening the claimed strength of the BSP parser. Incorporating this work would strengthen the evaluation.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you respond to the criticism that non-terminal nodes may not add expressive power, as suggested by prior work on dependency parsing (e.g., Ivanova et al., 2012)?

2. Can you clarify the specific advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR, given that AMR has been successfully parsed with high accuracy (e.g., Flanigan et al., 2014)?

3. Why did you choose a simple rule-based inverse conversion for dependency trees rather than a more sophisticated approach that could better recover non-terminal nodes?
