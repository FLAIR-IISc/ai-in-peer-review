

This paper proposes a transition-based graph parser for UCCA's semantic representations, addressing three structural properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity. The authors present a direct transition-based parser (BSP) as their core contribution, alongside conversion-based approaches mapping UCCA to constituency and dependency trees. The work is significant as UCCA is the only semantic representation supporting all three properties, yet lacks a dedicated parser.

Strengths include the clear identification of important structural properties for semantic parsing that are often overlooked in existing frameworks. The paper provides the first parser for UCCA, demonstrating reasonable performance (nearly 70% F-score for primary edges), and makes all code publicly available. The conversion-based experiments also offer useful insights into the information loss when mapping between representation formalisms.

However, several weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution. The claim of being "the first semantic-oriented parser" is unfounded, as the transition set doesn't exploit UCCA-specific phenomena. The paper fails to properly compare against prior graph-parsing work (DAGParser, TurboParser, and SemEval 2014-2015 results), instead using only dependency and constituency parsers as baselines. The evaluation lacks standard metrics from semantic dependency parsing (SDP) shared tasks, weakening the comparison. The three proposed structural principles are generic and ad-hoc, requiring clearer articulation of the paper's goals (UCCA parser vs. general semantic parser).

The paper also omits important details: UCCA doesn't model quantifier scope (unlike MRS/DRT), and implicit units lack textual correspondents. The conversion to surface dependency trees loses significant information, and the paper should have used richer SDP-style conversions. Additionally, the paper should clarify whether it's proposing a UCCA-specific parser or a more general semantic parser approach.

Despite these issues, the work represents a meaningful step toward broad-coverage semantic parsing and would be valuable for discussion at venues like CoNLL. The authors should address these concerns in the revision, particularly by comparing against appropriate baselines, clarifying their contribution's scope, and providing better evaluation metrics.
