

Summary: This paper addresses the gap in semantic parsing by proposing transition-based techniques for parsing UCCA's broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS), which require multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The main contributions are: 1) A conversion-based approach mapping UCCA to constituency/dependency trees to leverage existing parsers; 2) The first transition-based parser (BSP) directly supporting all three BSS properties; 3) Experimental validation on UCCA-annotated corpora showing promising results (69.2% primary F-score).

Strengths: The paper identifies a meaningful gap in semantic parsing where existing parsers fail to support all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation. The conversion-based approach is practical and demonstrates that existing technology can be adapted to address the task, with the constituency conversion achieving 65.4% F-score on primary edges. The direct BSP parser provides a novel solution that supports all three structural properties, with out-of-domain results showing robustness (57.1% F-score). The work is significant as it enables parsing of UCCA, the only semantic annotation scheme supporting all three properties, advancing the field toward more comprehensive semantic representations.

Weaknesses: The claim of being "the first semantic-oriented parser" is overstated as the transition set does not exploit UCCA-specific phenomena beyond standard graph parsing techniques. The remote-edge handling could be achieved through label distinctions rather than introducing new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX/RIGHT-REMOTEX), reducing the novelty of the method. Crucially, the paper fails to compare against prior graph-parsing work from SemEval 2014-2015 tasks and existing DAG parsers (DAGParser, TurboParser), making it difficult to assess the true novelty and performance of BSP. The evaluation lacks metrics from related semantic dependency parsing (SDP) shared tasks, weakening the comparison to the broader semantic parsing literature.

The paper's three proposed structural principles (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) are presented as novel but are generic and somewhat ad-hoc, lacking clear theoretical grounding or justification for why these particular properties are necessary for broad-coverage semantic parsing. The introduction of "grounded semantic" should be clarified earlier, as the paper's goals are ambiguous between developing a UCCA-specific parser versus creating a general semantic parsing framework.

Questions to Authors: 1) Can you clarify the relationship between your BSP and prior DAG parsing work (e.g., Sagae and Tsujii 2008, Tokgöz and Eryiğit 2015)? Why weren't these used as baselines? 2) How does your approach handle implicit units that lack textual correspondents (mentioned in Section 2), and can you provide examples from the UCCA corpus? 3) Given UCCA's lack of explicit quantifier scope modeling (unlike MRS/DRT), what is the error analysis for such constructions? 4) Why was the Berkeley parser not considered as a baseline for constituency parsing, given its ability to handle discontinuous structures?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to semantic parsing by addressing a genuine gap in the field, but its claims of novelty and significance are undermined by insufficient comparison to existing graph parsing approaches. The conversion-based results are promising, but the direct BSP parser's advantages over existing DAG parsers need stronger justification. The evaluation metrics could be improved by incorporating SDP task metrics to allow better comparison to the broader semantic parsing community. Despite these issues, the work is interesting and relevant to CoNLL's focus on parsing, and the publicly available code will facilitate future work in this area. The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of goals (UCCA-specific parser vs. general semantic parser) and stronger engagement with the existing graph parsing literature.
