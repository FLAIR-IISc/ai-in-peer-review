

Summary: The paper introduces the first transition-based parser for UCCA, a semantic representation scheme supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units (BSS). It proposes two approaches: conversion-based parsing (mapping UCCA to dependency or constituency trees) and a novel direct parser (BSP) supporting all three BSS properties.

Main contributions: 
1. First parser for UCCA, addressing a gap in broad-coverage semantic parsing.
2. Demonstration that conversion-based approaches can be used for UCCA parsing, though with information loss.
3. Introduction of BSP, a transition-based parser directly supporting all three BSS properties.

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a genuine gap in semantic parsing literature: no existing parser supports all three BSS properties simultaneously, making this a meaningful contribution to the field. UCCA's unique combination of properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) is well-motivated and addresses limitations of existing semantic representations.
2. The evaluation design is solid, with in-domain and out-of-domain testing on UCCA-annotated corpora, providing a fair comparison between conversion-based methods and the direct BSP approach. The use of UCCA's inter-annotator agreement as an upper bound (80-85% F-score) provides useful context for the results.
3. The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that transition-based parsing can be adapted for complex semantic structures, with BSP achieving 69.2% primary edge F-score. This shows promise for future work in semantic parsing.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper overstates UCCA as the "only" scheme supporting all three BSS properties, ignoring other relevant work. For example, SDP (Oepen et al., 2015) supports multiple parents and discontinuous units, and while it lacks non-terminal nodes, it addresses similar semantic phenomena. The background section should be more balanced to acknowledge these alternatives.
2. The three proposed criteria for BSS are debatable. Non-terminal nodes may not add expressive power over bi-lexical dependency schemes (as seen in SDP), and the advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR are not convincingly argued. The paper fails to demonstrate why non-terminal nodes are necessary for semantic representation, as coordination structures could be handled with bi-lexical dependencies.
3. The evaluation results are underwhelming: the LSTM parser (73.2% primary edge F-score) outperforms BSP (62.4%), and remote edge F-scores are low (15.3% in-domain). The paper claims BSP "obtains comparable F-scores" to other parsers, but this is misleading given the significant margin (over 10% F-score) between the LSTM parser and BSP.
4. The paper overlooks relevant prior work on tree approximations and edge reconstruction (e.g., Agić et al. 2015), which could significantly boost the conversion-based baselines. The lack of comparison with such methods weakens the claim that BSP represents a substantial improvement.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do you respond to the argument that non-terminal nodes may not add expressive power over bi-lexical dependency schemes, given that SDP (which lacks non-terminal nodes) addresses similar semantic phenomena with comparable performance?
2. Can you clarify why "grounded" representations (like UCCA) are superior to non-grounded ones (like AMR) for semantic parsing, given that the paper does not provide empirical evidence for this claim?
3. How would incorporating recent advances in neural classifiers (e.g., using LSTM-based classifiers instead of the current perceptron) affect BSP's performance, particularly on remote edges?
4. Why did you choose to evaluate on UCCA rather than on a more widely used semantic representation like AMR, given that AMR has more established evaluation metrics and community support?
