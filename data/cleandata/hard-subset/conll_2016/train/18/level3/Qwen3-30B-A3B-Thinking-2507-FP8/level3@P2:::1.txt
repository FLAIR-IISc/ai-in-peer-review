

# Review for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a transition-based approach for parsing UCCA semantic structures, which support three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex units like coordination), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors present two complementary parsing strategies: (1) conversion-based approaches that map UCCA structures to dependency or constituency trees and leverage existing parsers, and (2) a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that supports all three structural properties. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are: (1) the first parser that supports all three structural properties for broad-coverage semantic parsing; (2) a formalization of the structural requirements for broad-coverage semantic representation; and (3) experimental validation of transition-based methods for parsing UCCA structures.

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a significant gap in the literature: no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing, despite UCCA being the only semantic representation that supports them. This is a meaningful contribution to the field.

2. The paper provides a clear and well-structured argument for why these three structural properties are necessary for broad-coverage semantic representation, with concrete examples illustrating the limitations of existing approaches.

3. The conversion-based approaches provide a practical way to leverage existing parsers for UCCA, which could be useful for immediate applications while more specialized parsers are developed. The evaluation demonstrates that conversion-based methods can achieve reasonable performance.

## Weaknesses
1. The core contribution (BSP) does not adequately exploit UCCA-specific phenomena. The remote-edge handling could have been achieved by label differences rather than introducing new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX and RIGHT-REMOTEX), which weakens the novelty of the approach. The transition set appears largely generic rather than tailored to UCCA's specific needs.

2. The paper fails to compare with relevant prior work in graph parsing, particularly SemEval 2014-2015 broad-coverage semantic dependency parsing (SDP) shared tasks and existing DAG parsers like DAGParser (Ribeyre) and TurboParser (André Martins). This lack of appropriate baselines significantly undermines the evaluation.

3. The evaluation metrics and baselines are not comparable to standard semantic parsing evaluations. The paper should have used metrics from the SDP shared tasks (SemEval 2014-2015) to allow direct comparison with prior work, rather than introducing ad hoc measures.

4. The claim of being "the first" semantic-oriented parser is problematic, as the transition set does not seem to exploit UCCA-specific phenomena, and the authors don't adequately compare with prior graph parsing work.

5. The paper lacks a clear statement of its goal: is it a UCCA parser, a general semantic parser, or a proposal for a more parsable semantic scheme? The three structural properties are somewhat ad-hoc and generic, without sufficient justification for why they constitute a meaningful framework for semantic parsing.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide examples of UCCA implicit units that lack textual correspondents (mentioned in Section 2), and explain how these units would be handled in a practical parsing system?

2. The paper states that UCCA doesn't model quantifier scope (unlike MRS/DRT). Could you provide an error analysis to judge whether this limitation affects the informativeness and parsability of UCCA structures?

3. Why were the conversion procedures from UCCA to dependency trees losing information (e.g., all non-terminal nodes lost), and how does this compare to richer SDP-style conversions (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014)?

4. The paper claims to be the first semantic-oriented parser, but the transition set appears generic. How does your parser specifically exploit UCCA's semantic properties beyond what would be possible with a generic graph parser?

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from an earlier introduction of "grounded semantic" concepts. The phrase-based experiments should have compared to Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015) and included a reference to the UPARSE parser. The conversion to surface dependency trees loses significant information, and richer SDP-style conversions would be preferable. The paper's claim of being the first semantic-oriented parser is overstated, and the lack of proper comparison with prior graph parsing work weakens the contribution. Despite these issues, the paper addresses a meaningful gap and would be worth discussing at CoNLL.
