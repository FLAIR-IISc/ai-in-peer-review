

# Review for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of parsing semantic representations that support three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word expressions), and discontinuous units (for phrasal verbs). The authors propose two approaches to parsing UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation), which they claim is the only semantic representation supporting all three properties. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. First parser for UCCA, enabling further research on this semantic representation
2. Development of a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three structural properties
3. Systematic evaluation of conversion-based approaches between UCCA and more standard representations (dependency trees, constituency trees)

## Strengths

1. **First parser for UCCA representation**: The paper provides the first parser for UCCA, which is a significant contribution since UCCA is the only semantic representation scheme supporting all three structural properties identified by the authors. This enables future research on UCCA-based applications and provides a foundation for further exploration of semantic parsing with these properties. The authors make their code and data publicly available, which enhances reproducibility and facilitates future work.

2. **Clear analysis of conversion approaches**: The paper provides a well-structured analysis of converting UCCA to dependency trees and constituency trees, along with the necessary inverse conversions. This systematic comparison of conversion-based approaches versus direct parsing offers valuable insights for the community on how to handle semantic representations with non-standard structural properties.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation on UCCA data**: The evaluation on the UCCA Wikipedia corpus provides a meaningful test case for the proposed approaches. The authors report results on both primary and remote edges, which is important given UCCA's structure. The out-of-domain results on the 20K Leagues corpus demonstrate some robustness to domain variation, suggesting practical applicability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated uniqueness of UCCA**: The paper claims UCCA is "the only such scheme supporting them" (all three structural properties), which is inaccurate. Several other semantic representations (e.g., certain dependency DAG approaches) support some of these properties, and the claim that UCCA is uniquely positioned to support all three is not convincingly argued. This overstatement weakens the paper's motivation.

2. **Questionable necessity of structural properties**: The three proposed criteria for "broad-coverage" semantic parsing are not sufficiently justified. The need for non-terminal nodes (to represent multi-word expressions) is questionable, as many semantic representations handle these through alternative mechanisms (e.g., head selection in dependency trees). The advantages of "grounded" representations over non-grounded ones (like AMR) are not clearly established, and the paper fails to provide evidence that these properties are truly necessary for broad-coverage semantic parsing.

3. **Underwhelming evaluation results**: The proposed BSP parser is outperformed by the LSTM-based dependency parser by 9.1% F-score on primary edges. The remote edge scores are particularly low (10-15% F-score), which undermines the claimed significance of the direct parsing approach. The paper fails to adequately address prior work on tree approximations and edge reconstruction (e.g., Agic et al. 2015), which could significantly boost the baseline dependency tree approaches and further weaken the claimed novelty of BSP.

4. **Overloaded terminology**: The term "grounded" is used inconsistently and without clear definition. The paper would benefit from considering alternatives like "anchored" or "lexicalized" to avoid confusion with other uses of the term in semantic parsing literature.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you respond to the criticism that the need for non-terminal nodes may be overstated, as many semantic representations handle multi-word expressions through other mechanisms (e.g., head selection in dependency trees)?

2. What specific evidence do you provide to support the claim that "grounded" semantic representations have clear advantages over non-grounded ones (like AMR), beyond the argument that they make alignment easier?

3. Given that the LSTM-based parser outperforms your BSP by 9.1% F-score on primary edges, what specific improvements would you make to BSP to achieve similar performance, and how would these relate to the unique properties of UCCA?

## Recommendation

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by providing the first parser for UCCA, but the claims about the necessity of the three structural properties and the uniqueness of UCCA are overstated. The evaluation results are underwhelming, with the proposed parser being outperformed by simpler approaches. I recommend rejection due to the significant weaknesses in the paper's claims and evaluation, though the work on UCCA parsing itself has potential value for future research. The authors should address the overstatement of UCCA's uniqueness, better justify their structural properties, and provide stronger evidence for the advantages of their approach over simpler alternatives.
