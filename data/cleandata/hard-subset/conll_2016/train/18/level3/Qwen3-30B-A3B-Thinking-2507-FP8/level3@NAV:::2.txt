

This paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parser for UCCA, a representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors propose a transition-based parser (BSP) and evaluate it against conversion-based approaches to dependency trees and constituency trees. The main contributions are: (1) the first parser for UCCA, enabling practical use of this representation; (2) a transition-based parser specifically designed for UCCA's structural properties; and (3) a thorough evaluation framework for assessing semantic parsers against UCCA's requirements.

Strengths include the timely contribution of addressing an important gap in semantic parsing, the clear exposition of structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation, and the comprehensive evaluation on a substantial UCCA corpus.

However, several weaknesses significantly undermine the paper's claims. First, the introduction overstates UCCA as the only sensible graph-based SR to study, failing to acknowledge other representations that might support similar structural properties. Second, the claimed advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR are unsubstantiated; the paper provides no concrete evidence that grounding improves parsing performance or downstream applications. Third, the evaluation is unimpressive: MaltParser outperforms BSP on primary edges (63.9% vs. 62.4%), and remote-edge F-scores are low (15.3% for BSP). Fourth, the term "grounded" is overloaded and should be replaced with a more precise term like "lexicalized" or "anchored." Finally, the authors overlook prior work on tree approximations (e.g., Agic et al. 2015) that could strengthen dependency-tree baselines and weaken the claimed advantage of BSP.

The paper's contribution is potentially valuable but is undermined by these weaknesses. The authors should address these issues before the work can be considered a meaningful advancement in semantic parsing. The claimed novelty of BSP is questionable given the modest performance improvements over existing methods, and the paper's theoretical claims about UCCA's superiority require stronger justification.
