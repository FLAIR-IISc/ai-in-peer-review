- Contribution: Introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, via conversion-based methods and a novel transition-based BSP parser.  
- Contribution: Provides a comprehensive comparison between conversion-based and direct parsing approaches.  
- Contribution: Releases code and data publicly for reproducibility.

- Strength: Fills a gap by offering the first parser for UCCA, handling multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities.  
- Strength: Thorough empirical comparison clarifying trade-offs between approaches.  
- Strength: Open-science commitment with public release of code and data.

- Weakness: Overstates UCCA as "the only" sensible graph-based framework, ignoring alternatives like SDP.  
- Weakness: The three proposed criteria (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) are not convincingly justified; non-terminals may not add expressive power and grounding advantages over AMR are unclear.  
- Weakness: Terminology issue—"grounded" is overloaded and should be replaced with clearer terms like "anchored" or "lexicalized."  
- Weakness: Evaluation results are unimpressive—MaltParser outperforms BSP on primary edges and remote-edge F-scores are very low.  
- Weakness: Overlooks prior work on tree approximations and edge-reconstruction (e.g., Agic et al. 2015) that could strengthen dependency-tree baselines.

- Question: How do authors respond to the claim that non-terminal nodes may not add expressive power (e.g., Ivanova et al., 2012)?  
- Question: What are the specific advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR given AMR's parsing success (e.g., Flanigan et al., 2014)?  
- Question: Why use a simple rule-based inverse conversion for dependency trees instead of more sophisticated reconstruction methods?
