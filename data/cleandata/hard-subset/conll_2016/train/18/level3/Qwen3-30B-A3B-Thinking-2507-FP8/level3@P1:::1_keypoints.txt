- Paper proposes transition-based techniques for parsing UCCA’s broad-coverage semantic structures, addressing multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities.
- Main contributions: conversion-based mapping to constituency/dependency trees to leverage existing parsers.
- Main contributions: first transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports the three BSS properties.
- Main contributions: experimental validation on UCCA corpora with promising results (69.2% primary F-score).
- Strength: Identifies a meaningful gap where existing parsers don’t support all three structural properties.
- Strength: Conversion-based approach is practical and yields competitive results (65.4% F-score for constituency conversion).
- Strength: BSP supports all three properties and shows out-of-domain robustness (57.1% F-score).
- Strength: Work advances parsing of UCCA and provides publicly available code for future research.
- Weakness: Claim of being “first semantic-oriented parser” is overstated; transition set uses standard graph-parsing techniques without UCCA-specific exploitation.
- Weakness: Remote-edge handling may be achievable with label distinctions instead of new transitions, reducing novelty.
- Weakness: Paper fails to compare against prior DAG parsers and SemEval/SDP work (e.g., Sagae & Tsujii 2008, Tokgöz & Eryiğit 2015, DAGParser, TurboParser).
- Weakness: Evaluation lacks SDP/shared-task metrics, weakening comparisons to broader semantic parsing literature.
- Weakness: The three structural principles are presented as generic/ad-hoc without clear theoretical grounding or justification.
- Weakness: “Grounded semantic” is unclear and goals are ambiguous between a UCCA-specific parser and a general semantic parsing framework.
- Questions for authors: Clarify relationship to prior DAG parsing work and why those baselines weren’t used.
- Questions for authors: Explain handling of implicit units without textual correspondents and provide UCCA corpus examples.
- Questions for authors: Provide error analysis for constructions requiring quantifier scope, given UCCA’s lack of explicit scope modeling.
- Questions for authors: Why wasn’t the Berkeley parser considered as a baseline for handling discontinuous constituency structures.
- Overall: Valuable and interesting contribution but undermined by insufficient comparisons, ambiguous goals, and need for stronger engagement with graph-parsing literature.
