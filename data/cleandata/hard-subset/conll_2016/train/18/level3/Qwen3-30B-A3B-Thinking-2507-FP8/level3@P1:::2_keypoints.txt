- Summary: Introduces the first transition-based parser for UCCA with two approaches — conversion-based parsing and a novel direct parser (BSP) that supports multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities (BSS).

- Contribution 1: First parser for UCCA, filling a gap in broad-coverage semantic parsing.

- Contribution 2: Shows conversion-based approaches can parse UCCA but incur information loss.

- Contribution 3: Proposes BSP, a transition-based parser directly supporting all three BSS properties.

- Strength 1: Identifies a genuine gap—no prior parser supports all three BSS properties—making UCCA a well-motivated target.

- Strength 2: Solid evaluation design with in-domain and out-of-domain tests and use of UCCA inter-annotator agreement (80–85% F) as an upper bound.

- Strength 3: Demonstrates transition-based parsing can handle complex semantic structures; BSP reported at 69.2% primary edge F-score (shows promise).

- Weakness 1: Overstates uniqueness of UCCA — ignores related work like SDP that supports multiple parents and discontinuities.

- Weakness 2: The three BSS criteria are debatable; non-terminal nodes’ necessity and advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR are not convincingly argued.

- Weakness 3: Evaluation is underwhelming — LSTM parser (73.2% primary F) outperforms BSP (62.4%), and remote edge scores are very low (15.3% in-domain).

- Weakness 4: Overlooks prior work on tree approximations and edge reconstruction (e.g., Agić et al. 2015) that could strengthen conversion baselines.

- Question 1: How do you respond to the claim that non-terminal nodes add no expressive power over bi-lexical dependency schemes like SDP?

- Question 2: Can you justify empirically why "grounded" representations (UCCA) are superior to non-grounded ones (AMR)?

- Question 3: How would using modern neural classifiers (e.g., LSTMs) affect BSP performance, especially for remote edges?

- Question 4: Why evaluate on UCCA rather than more widely used representations like AMR with broader community support?
