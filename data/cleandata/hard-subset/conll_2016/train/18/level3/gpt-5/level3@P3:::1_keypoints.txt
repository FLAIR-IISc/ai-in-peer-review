- Paper argues grounded semantic parsing needs multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminals, and discontinuity, using UCCA as example and comparing conversion-based and direct parsing approaches.  
- Authors present two lossy conversion pipelines from UCCA to discontinuous constituency and surface dependency formats to reuse existing parsers.  
- Authors introduce BSP, a direct transition-based graph parser with a dynamic oracle that natively supports DAGs, non-terminals, discontinuity, and predicts remote edges.  
- Experiments compare constituency-based (UPARSE), dependency-based (Malt and LSTM), and BSP on in-domain (Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) data.  
- Best primary-edge F1 was achieved by the LSTM dependency baseline (69.2), while BSP uniquely predicted remote edges (~13 F1).  
- Core technical contribution: direct transition-based UCCA parser (BSP) able to predict remote edges and trained with a dynamic oracle.  
- Empirical contribution: conversion procedures and comparative evaluation framework for UCCA parsing across domains.  
- Promised release of converters and parser to stimulate further research on grounded semantics.  
- Addresses an important problem beyond bilexical dependencies, capturing coordination, MWEs, and shared arguments.  
- Presents a clear, reproducible transition system with a dynamic oracle and a useful global “ratio” feature built on familiar shift–reduce principles.  
- Solid experimental design including in-/out-of-domain evaluation and informative finding that dependency LSTM outperforms others on primary edges.  
- Establishes initial empirical baselines and tools for UCCA work and for analyzing loss from tree/DAG conversions.  
- Timely and broadly interesting to CoNLL by linking syntactic and semantic parsing and exposing open design/evaluation gaps.  
- Missing comparisons to SDP literature and SemEval 2014/2015 parsers and metrics; should include SDP-style labeled/unlabeled metrics.  
- Related DAG parsers (e.g., Ribeyre’s DAGParser, TurboParser DAG extensions) are not empirically compared or discussed.  
- Claim of “first semantic-oriented parser” is overstated; transition inventory resembles existing discontinuous constituency and DAG parsers and may not exploit UCCA-specific phenomena.  
- Handling remote edges via dedicated transitions may be unnecessary; alternatives like label-level or edge-attribute distinctions could suffice.  
- Constituency conversion baselines underplay phrase-structure strength; should compare to strong parsers (e.g., Berkeley parser) with UCCA-informed labels and cite relevant reduction work.  
- Dependency conversion is intentionally lossy; stronger SDP-style graph conversions could preserve more structure and yield better comparisons.  
- The three proposed principles are generic and ad hoc; paper should clarify its primary goal (UCCA parser vs. general semantic parser vs. proposal for a scheme).  
- Deeper error analysis is missing, limiting assessment of informativeness and parsability of predicted graphs.  
- Question: What proportion of edges are remote, and how do false positives vs. missed links distribute? Could remote edges be modeled as primary edges with an attribute instead?  
- Question: How frequent are discontinuous units and SWAP actions, and what is BSP’s error profile on discontinuities vs. continuous structures?  
- Question: Provide examples of implicit UCCA units, discuss omission bias, and plans for joint modeling.  
- Question: Does UCCA’s lack of quantifier scope modeling cause systematic errors or representational gaps affecting parsing?  
- Question: Have the authors tried replacing the perceptron with an LSTM/MLP in BSP, and what gains are expected given dependency LSTM results?  
- Suggestion: Report SDP-style metrics, unlabeled scores, and per-category F1 (coordination, MWEs, linkage) alongside current metrics.  
- Suggestion: Include or discuss DAG/Turbo parser results or oracle conversions into SDP graphs; compare to SemEval systems.  
- Suggestion: Explore richer dependency conversions that retain predicate–argument structure and coordination, and document conversion loss thoroughly.  
- Suggestion: Define “grounded semantic” early, clarify the paper’s primary goal, and tone down the “first” claim to be precise.  
- Suggestion: Add qualitative and quantitative error analysis with breakdowns by structure type and label.  
- Suggestion: Add citations/links to UPARSE, DAGParser, and TurboParser repositories and ensure all tool names have bibliographic entries.  
- Overall: Despite gaps in methodology and comparisons, the paper addresses an important problem, proposes an extensible approach, and provides useful empirical findings warranting CoNLL discussion.
