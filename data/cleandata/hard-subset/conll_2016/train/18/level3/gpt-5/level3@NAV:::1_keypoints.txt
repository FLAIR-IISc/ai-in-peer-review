- Paper studies UCCA parsing, arguing three structural properties (reentrancy, non-terminals, discontinuity) and proposes conversion-to-other-formalisms and a direct transition-based graph parser (BSP).
- Experiments: strong dependency (LSTM) baselines via conversion achieve best primary-edge F1; BSP uniquely predicts remote edges but with low F1.

- Contribution: first systematic empirical study of UCCA parsing with conversion pipelines, evaluation protocol, code, and baseline landscape.
- Contribution: a transition system and dynamic-oracle training for DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, showing direct UCCA-style graph parsing is feasible.
- Contribution: comparison of conversion vs. direct parsing highlighting that classifier strength can matter more than transition inventory.

- Strength: timely and relevant problem—bringing grounded, graph-based semantics into a transition-based framework.
- Strength: clear implementation of a direct parser handling reentrancy and discontinuity, with dynamic oracle and feature ablations.
- Strength: empirical finding that conversion plus strong neural classifiers is competitive and practically useful.
- Strength: public resources and explicit conversions improve reproducibility and impact.

- Weakness: novelty claim weak—transition set is largely generic and doesn’t exploit UCCA-specific phenomena; remote edges might be modeled via labels instead.
- Weakness: missing important graph-parsing baselines (SemEval SDP systems, DAGParser, TurboParser), limiting external validity.
- Weakness: phrase-structure baselines incomplete; comparison to Fernández-González & Martins (2015) is essential and uparse reference is missing.
- Weakness: evaluation misaligned with related work—only yield-based labeled F reported; need SDP-style metrics on dependency conversions for comparability.
- Weakness: conversions are lossy (dependency conversion discards non-terminals); richer SDP-style or labeled constituent conversions should be explored.

- Question: justify new transitions for remote edges vs. label-based handling—what empirical gain do they provide?
- Question: why were established SDP/DAG parsers not used as baselines—were code or format barriers the issue?
- Request: add Fernández-González & Martins (2015) reduction baseline and a Berkeley parser baseline with rich node labels.
- Request: provide concrete examples of UCCA implicit units and explain whether/how BSP could handle them.
- Question: UCCA doesn’t model quantifier scope like MRS/DRT—how does this affect downstream utility; please include error analysis by phenomenon (coordination, remotes, discontinuities).

- Additional: three guiding principles feel generic and ad hoc; paper needs a clearer, broader introduction and crisper statement of goals (UCCA-specific vs. general semantic parser).
- Additional: introduce “grounded semantic” earlier; despite issues, the work merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.
