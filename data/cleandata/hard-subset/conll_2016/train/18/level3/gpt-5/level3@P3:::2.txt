Summary
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing of UCCA graphs, which allow multiple parents (remote edges), non-terminal units, and discontinuities. It proposes two strategies: (a) conversion-based pipelines that map UCCA to trees (constituency, dependency) so that existing parsers can be reused, and then convert outputs back; and (b) a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that supports the three structural properties. The paper defines an evaluation via labeled yield-matching, provides conversion upper bounds, and reports experiments on UCCA Wikipedia and out-of-domain 20K Leagues. Results show the best primary-edge F1 comes from an LSTM dependency parser with conversion (69.2), while BSP achieves 59.0 primary F1 and 13.3 remote F1 in-domain.

Claimed contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) First broad-coverage baselines for UCCA parsing, including the first direct transition-based parser that can predict UCCA’s non-terminals, remotes, and discontinuities, alongside competitive conversion-based baselines.
2) Practical conversion procedures and an initial, reproducible evaluation protocol and feature ablations for UCCA/BSS.
3) Minor technical elements (dynamic-oracle training for UCCA transitions; a “ratio” feature; remote-edge transitions) adapted to this setting.

Strengths
- Fills a gap: UCCA has had no broad-coverage parser; providing both direct and conversion-based baselines is valuable to the community and to future shared tasks.
- Careful engineering and clarity: the transition system, dynamic oracle, and feature templates are well specified; conversion algorithms are explicit, with upper bounds included.
- Broad empirical comparison: constituency (UPARSE), dependency (Malt, LSTM), and the new BSP are compared in-domain and out-of-domain, with a useful feature ablation. The out-of-domain results illustrate reasonable robustness.
- Insight via conversions: showing conversion loss and upper bounds helps calibrate expectations and may stimulate future work on better tree-to-graph reconstruction.

Weaknesses
- Overstated framing of representation choices: the introduction strongly implies UCCA is the only sensible graph-based SR worth studying and that the three criteria are necessary for “broad coverage.” This is not balanced. SDP covers two of three (multi-parents, discontinuities); the necessity of non-terminals per se (vs. bilexical graphs with compositional labels/latent structure) is not convincingly justified.
- “Grounded” terminology and claims: the term is overloaded in the literature. The purported advantages over AMR (anchoring/alignment) are asserted but not demonstrated empirically here. Consider “anchored” or “token-aligned,” and rephrase arguments more carefully.
- Technical contribution vs. results: the direct parser is outperformed on primary edges by a strong dependency-LSTM baseline by ~10 F1, and even by MaltParser baselines; remote-edge F1 is very low (~13). The transition system largely combines known operations; without a stronger local model (e.g., LSTMs, beam), the core technical novelty feels limited.
- Baselines could be stronger and may narrow the gap further: prior work on tree approximations plus edge reconstruction (e.g., Agić et al., 2015; SDP post-processing) could recover some remotes/non-terminals from dependencies. Such baselines are not evaluated. UPARSE’s beam is small; Malt settings are default; no attempt to reconstruct remotes for conversions, which biases comparisons.
- Limited analysis: little per-label breakdown (e.g., P/A/R/C/coordination), limited error typology, and no analysis of discontinuity handling or remote-edge recall errors. The effect of oracle, beam search, or training data size is not explored.

Questions to Authors (for author response)
- Necessity of criteria: What phenomena demonstrably require non-terminals beyond bilexical graphs with compositional labels or latent structure? Please provide concrete counterexamples where SDP-style representations provably fail.
- Terminology: What exactly do you mean by “grounded”? Would you consider adopting “anchored”/“token-aligned” and tempering comparative claims vs. AMR with evidence beyond alignment difficulty?
- Stronger BSP model: Can you report BSP with a stronger local model (e.g., LSTM features, character modeling, beam search)? Given the LSTM gains for the dependency baseline, this seems essential to test the promise of the direct approach.
- Stronger baselines: Can you add dependency-tree baselines augmented with edge reconstruction or latent non-terminal recovery (e.g., Agić et al., 2015-style post-processing), and report primary/remote F1 under these settings?
- Evaluation details: Please provide per-label F1 (both primary and remote), category-level breakdowns (coordination, PPs, MWEs, Scenes, linkers), and detailed error analysis for discontinuities. Where does BSP outperform conversions?
- Conversion sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the high-attach heuristic in inverse conversion? Could you report unlabeled scores and variance under alternative heuristics?

Additional comments
- Claims such as “the only scheme supporting them is UCCA” should be nuanced; relate more fairly to SDP, MRS/ERG analyses, and other anchored formalisms.
- Consider discussing inter-annotator agreement as a ceiling, but avoid implying that current numbers are close to that ceiling for the direct parser.
- The “ratio” feature is interesting; quantify its generality (e.g., does it help in other discontinuous settings?) and consider releasing it with code.
- Clarify data preprocessing choices (e.g., excluding linkage and implicit nodes) and their impact, and include learning curves.
