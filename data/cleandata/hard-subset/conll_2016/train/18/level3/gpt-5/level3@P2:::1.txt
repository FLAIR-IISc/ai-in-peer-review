Summary and Contributions
This paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing for grounded representations that require (i) multiple parents (DAGs), (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuous units. The authors use UCCA as a testbed and explore two approaches: conversion-based parsing (UCCA→constituency/dependency, then back) and a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that natively supports all three properties. Experiments on in-domain (Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) data report up to 69.2% labeled F1 on primary edges (no remotes) via an LSTM dependency baseline, and 59.0/13.3 F1 on primary/remote edges with BSP.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first):
1) An empirical study establishing baselines and upper bounds for UCCA parsing via conversions to constituency/dependency formalisms and a first direct transition-based graph parser for UCCA. The experimental design (two conversion pipelines + direct parser + OOD evaluation) is valuable.
2) A concrete transition system and feature set for parsing grounded, non-terminals-and-discontinuity DAGs, including a dynamic-oracle training setup and a simple but effective “ratio” regularizer feature.
3) An evaluation protocol for UCCA graphs (yield+label matching) and accompanying resources/converters, with insights into information loss under conversion.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and useful testbed: The paper motivates the need for BSS (DAGs, non-terminals, discontinuities) and leverages UCCA, a grounded, cross-linguistic scheme. Using both in-domain and out-of-domain data strengthens claims about robustness.
- Dual methodology illuminates trade-offs: The conversion pipelines with measured upper bounds help quantify how much information is lost by forcing BSS into trees. This is practically useful for the community considering representation choices.
- Direct parser that supports all three properties: BSP is, to my knowledge, the first transition-based parser explicitly targeting grounded DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuity in a single system. The system design is clearly described, reproducible, and employs a dynamic oracle.
- Strong comparative insight: The stack-LSTM dependency baseline’s sizable advantage over Malt (≈+9 F1) indicates that modern neural classifiers significantly help in this space; this points to obvious next steps for BSP and is a constructive takeaway.
- Promised resource release: Converters and code (upon publication) would materially benefit research on UCCA and related grounded semantic formalisms.

Weaknesses
- Novelty claims and UCCA specificity are overstated: The transition inventory is a relatively modest augmentation of known systems for discontinuous constituency and DAG dependencies. The “remote edge” handling could plausibly be captured via label distinctions without dedicated transitions; there is no ablation justifying the necessity of separate REMOTE transitions.
- Baselines and metrics are incomplete: Given the proximity to SDP and SemEval 2014/2015, the paper should compare against strong DAG/SDP parsers (e.g., TurboParser/TurboSemDep, DAGParser) or at least report results under SDP-style metrics on converted graphs. The current evaluation (yield+label) is bespoke; including standard SDP metrics would make results more comparable.
- Conversion baselines are underpowered: The dependency conversion discards non-terminals and reconstructs them with simple rules, likely underestimating what is achievable with richer conversions (e.g., SDP-style heads, smarter head rules, or methods like Fernández-González & Martins 2015; Kong et al. 2015). Similarly, on the constituency side, stronger parsers (e.g., Berkeley, neural constituency parsers) on enriched node labels should be tested.
- Limited analysis of semantic phenomena: There is little error analysis by construction type (coordination, phrasal verbs), remote edges, or discontinuities. UCCA’s handling of phenomena like implicit units and quantifier scope is not discussed; readers cannot assess whether what is easy to annotate is also easy to parse and sufficiently informative for downstream use.
- Clarity and positioning: The introduction weaves together “grounded semantics,” UCCA, and general BSS goals but does not clearly state whether the primary goal is an effective UCCA parser, a general-purpose BSS parser, or a methodology for designing parsable semantic schemes. Some editorial issues (late introduction of “grounded semantic,” missing reference for UPARSE) hinder readability.

Questions to Authors
- Please justify the design choice of separate LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX transitions versus encoding remoteness in labels. Can you provide an ablation showing whether distinct transitions materially improve precision/recall on remote edges?
- Can you include comparisons to SDP/DAG parsers or reproduce SemEval-style metrics by converting UCCA to SDP-like representations (even if lossy)? Alternatively, run TurboParser/TurboSemDep on an SDP-style conversion and report results.
- On the constituency track, can you try stronger baselines (e.g., Berkeley parser; recent neural constituency parsers) with enriched labels that preserve UCCA categories?
- Have you tried beam search and/or an LSTM-based classifier within BSP? Given the large boost observed for the LSTM dependency parser, this seems a natural next step.
- Please provide an error analysis by phenomenon: coordination, MWE/phrasal verbs, long-distance discontinuities, and remote edges (precision/recall distributions, typical confusions).
- Can you clarify and exemplify “implicit units” in UCCA, and outline how BSP could be extended to handle them?
- How does UCCA treat quantifier scope and similar scope-sensitive phenomena (vs. MRS/DRT)? What are the implications for downstream applications?

Additional Comments
- Consider richer conversions that better preserve UCCA structure (e.g., SDP-style heads, Schlüter et al. 2014; Kong et al. 2015), and report upper bounds for those.
- Try a “constituency with rich node labels” baseline (e.g., Berkeley) and report how much is recovered by stronger parsing versus conversion losses.
- Please introduce and define “grounded semantic” terminology earlier.
- Include or correct references for UPARSE (Maier 2015), Turbo/TurboSemDep, DAGParser (Ribeyre), and Fernández-González & Martins (2015).
- Reproducibility: if possible, provide anonymized links to converters/evaluation scripts during the review phase.

Overall, I find the problem important and the empirical study valuable. Despite the methodological and evaluation gaps, this paper would stimulate useful discussion at CoNLL and could become a strong contribution with the requested comparisons and analyses.
