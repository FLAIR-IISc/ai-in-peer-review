- Paper addresses broad-coverage UCCA semantic parsing (remote edges, non-terminals, discontinuities) with conversion pipelines and a direct transition-based parser (BSP); reports labeled yield-matching evaluation and conversion upper bounds; dependency-LSTM conversion yields best primary-edge F1 (69.2), BSP gets 59.0 primary / 13.3 remote F1 in-domain.
- Contribution: first broad-coverage UCCA baselines including the first direct transition-based parser handling UCCA’s non-terminals, remotes, and discontinuities.
- Contribution: provides practical conversion procedures, a reproducible evaluation protocol, and feature ablations for UCCA/BSP.
- Contribution: minor technical adaptations (dynamic-oracle training, a “ratio” feature, remote-edge transitions) for UCCA parsing.
- Strength: fills a gap by providing both direct and conversion-based UCCA baselines valuable for the community and shared tasks.
- Strength: careful engineering and clear specification of the transition system, dynamic oracle, feature templates, and explicit conversion algorithms with upper bounds.
- Strength: broad empirical comparison across constituency (UPARSE), dependency (Malt, LSTM), and BSP parsers in-domain and out-of-domain, plus feature ablation and robustness checks.
- Strength: conversion analysis (loss and upper bounds) offers useful insights and sets expectations for tree-to-graph reconstruction work.
- Weakness: framing overstates UCCA’s uniqueness and necessity of its three criteria; lacks balanced comparison to other formalisms (e.g., SDP) and justification for non-terminals vs. bilexical approaches.
- Weakness: “grounded” terminology is overloaded and claims of advantage over AMR (anchoring/alignment) are asserted without empirical support; suggests using “anchored” or “token-aligned.”
- Weakness: technical novelty limited because the transition system combines known operations and BSP is outperformed by dependency-LSTM (~10 F1) and even Malt; remote-edge F1 is very low (~13).
- Weakness: baselines could be stronger—prior tree-approximation plus edge reconstruction methods and better parser settings (larger beams, tuned Malt) are not evaluated and conversions do not attempt remote reconstruction.
- Weakness: limited analysis—no per-label breakdown, limited error typology, no detailed discontinuity or remote-edge error analysis, and no exploration of oracle/beam/training-data effects.
- Question: provide concrete phenomena that require non-terminals beyond bilexical graphs with compositional labels or latent structure; supply counterexamples where SDP-style representations fail.
- Question: clarify “grounded” terminology and consider adopting “anchored”/“token-aligned,” tempering comparative claims vs. AMR with evidence.
- Question: can BSP be reported with a stronger local model (LSTM features, char modeling, beam search) given LSTM gains seen in dependency baselines?
- Question: add stronger baselines that reconstruct edges/non-terminals from dependency trees (e.g., Agić et al., 2015) and report primary/remote F1.
- Question: provide per-label F1 for primary and remote edges, category-level breakdowns (coordination, PPs, MWEs, Scenes, linkers), and detailed error analysis highlighting where BSP outperforms conversions.
- Question: report sensitivity of inverse conversion to the high-attach heuristic, plus unlabeled scores and variance under alternative heuristics.
- Additional: temper claims like “the only scheme supporting them is UCCA” and relate more fairly to SDP, MRS/ERG, and other anchored formalisms.
- Additional: discuss inter-annotator agreement as a ceiling carefully and avoid implying current direct-parser scores approach it.
- Additional: quantify the generality of the “ratio” feature, consider releasing it, and test its usefulness in other discontinuous settings.
- Additional: clarify preprocessing choices (e.g., excluding linkage and implicit nodes), report their impact, and include learning curves.
