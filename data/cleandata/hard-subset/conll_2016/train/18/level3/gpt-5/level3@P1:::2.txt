Summary
The paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing for UCCA, emphasizing three structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal units, and discontinuities. It proposes two transition-based strategies: (a) conversion-based parsing by mapping UCCA to related formalisms (constituency and dependency) and using existing parsers, then inverting, and (b) a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that natively handles all three properties. Experiments on UCCA English (Wikipedia in-domain; 20K Leagues out-of-domain) show that conversion-based baselines, especially an LSTM dependency parser, outperform the direct parser on primary edges; BSP uniquely predicts remote edges but with low F-scores. The paper includes a new evaluation protocol for BSS and an ablation study.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength
1) Empirical evaluation showing that conversion-based pipelines are strong baselines for UCCA parsing. Notably, a stack-LSTM dependency parser achieves the best primary-edge score (≈69 F1) after conversion, surpassing both Malt and the proposed BSP. This is a valuable, evidence-based insight for the community.
2) Tooling for inter-representation conversion and an evaluation protocol for broad-coverage semantic structures, enabling comparison across formalisms and establishing upper bounds for lossy conversions.
3) A transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports multiple parents, nonterminals, and discontinuities, with a concrete transition set, dynamic oracle training, and some UCCA-specific features.

Strengths
- Clear, reproducible methodology and strong baselines: The paper carefully constructs conversion procedures to constituency and dependency formats and evaluates multiple established parsers (Malt variants, UPARSE, LSTM). Computing upper bounds for conversions is particularly useful to contextualize achievable performance and conversion loss.
- Practical finding that modern dependency parsers are competitive for UCCA via conversion: The LSTM parser’s ≈69 F1 on primary edges, substantially higher than Malt and UPARSE, is a meaningful result that guides future work. This also suggests a straightforward path to improvement for BSP by upgrading its local classifier.
- Direct parser design that covers challenging structure: The BSP transition set cleanly integrates DAG edges (primary and remote), nonterminal creation, and discontinuities (SWAP), with a dynamic oracle and a novel “ratio” regularization feature. The design is technically solid and extensible.
- Thoughtful evaluation setup and analysis: In-domain and out-of-domain testing, explicit handling of remote vs. primary edges, and a feature ablation study provide transparency into what drives performance and where the bottlenecks are.
- Potential impact via released resources: Public release of converters and parsers would materially benefit the community exploring UCCA and other anchored semantic formalisms.

Weaknesses
- Overstated positioning of UCCA and the three criteria: The introduction suggests UCCA is the only grounded graph-based SR supporting the required properties and implies these properties are necessary for broad-coverage semantics. This is not sufficiently balanced. Bi-lexical schemes (e.g., SDP formalisms) and anchored graph representations can encode many phenomena without nonterminals; whether nonterminals add essential expressive power versus convenience is not established empirically here.
- “Grounded” terminology and argumentation: “Grounded” is overloaded; consider “anchored” or “token-aligned.” The claimed advantages over AMR are asserted but not demonstrated (e.g., do alignment errors measurably dominate overall AMR parsing error? do downstream tasks benefit more from anchoring than from AMR’s abstraction?). The paper would benefit from careful, metric-based comparisons or at least a more nuanced discussion.
- Core results do not support superiority of the proposed parser: BSP’s primary-edge F1 (≈59) trails both conversion-based UPARSE (≈65) and especially the LSTM dependency baseline (≈69). While BSP is the only system predicting remote edges, its remote-edge F1 is low (≈13), limiting the practical advantage of its broader coverage.
- Baselines could be stronger with known reconstruction techniques: Prior work on tree approximation and edge reconstruction (e.g., Agić et al., 2015, among others) could likely boost dependency pipelines, making the conversion path even more competitive and further challenging the claimed value of a direct DAG+discontinuity+nonterminal parser. The paper should consider such baselines or discuss their expected impact.
- Limited analysis of where BSP gains or fails: Beyond aggregate F-scores, there is little error analysis by phenomenon (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, remote vs. primary edge types), nor an estimate of how much of UCCA’s advantage (nonterminals, remotes, discontinuities) is actually realized in predictions and useful for downstream interpretation.

Questions to Authors
- Can you quantify the frequency and distribution of remote edges and discontinuities in the corpora, and provide per-phenomenon performance? Where does BSP gain most relative to conversion-based systems?
- How sensitive are your results to the label-priority order used in dependency conversion and to the Label(u) heuristic during inverse conversion? Could learned head rules or methods like Kong et al. (2015) improve reconstruction and the dependency baseline?
- What proportion of the upper-bound loss for dependency conversion comes specifically from nonterminal removal vs. other factors? A decomposition would clarify where conversion hurts most.
- Have you tried beam search or neural scoring (e.g., BiLSTM features) in BSP? Given the LSTM baseline’s strength, a neuralized BSP seems a natural next step.
- Could you report speed and memory comparisons across systems? Transition-based methods are often favored for efficiency; quantifying this would be useful.

Additional comments
- Reframe the motivation to acknowledge alternative anchored formalisms (e.g., SDP frameworks) and clarify that the paper’s focus is on UCCA because it explicitly encodes nonterminals and discontinuities, not because alternatives are intrinsically inadequate.
- Consider adding a small downstream proxy (e.g., predicate-argument extraction quality) to substantiate claims about the utility of anchoring and nonterminals.
- Minor: replace “grounded” with “anchored/token-aligned” throughout, and sharpen the comparison to AMR to avoid implying that anchoring alone makes parsing easier or more useful without empirical support.
