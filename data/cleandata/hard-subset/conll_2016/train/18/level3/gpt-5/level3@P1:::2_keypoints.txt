- Paper tackles broad-coverage UCCA parsing, focusing on multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminals, and discontinuities, and proposes conversion-based pipelines and a direct BSP transition parser.  
- Experiments on in-domain (Wikipedia) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) UCCA show conversion-based pipelines, especially an LSTM dependency parser, outperform BSP on primary edges; BSP uniquely predicts remote edges but with low F1.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that conversion-based pipelines are strong baselines for UCCA parsing, with an LSTM dependency parser achieving ≈69 F1 on primary edges.  
- Contribution: released tooling for inter-representation conversion and a new evaluation protocol establishing upper bounds for lossy conversions.  
- Contribution: a transition-based BSP parser that natively supports DAGs, nonterminals, and discontinuities, with a concrete transition set, dynamic oracle, and regularization feature.  
- Strength: clear, reproducible methodology with careful conversion procedures and computed upper bounds to contextualize conversion loss.  
- Strength: practical finding that modern dependency parsers (stack-LSTM) are highly competitive via conversion, guiding future work and potential BSP improvements.  
- Strength: technically solid BSP design integrating primary/remote edges, nonterminal creation, and SWAP for discontinuities, plus dynamic oracle training.  
- Strength: thoughtful evaluation (in/out-of-domain, remote vs. primary, ablation study) providing transparency on performance drivers and bottlenecks.  
- Strength: potential community impact through released converters and parsers.  
- Weakness: introduction overstates UCCA’s uniqueness and necessity of the three criteria; alternative anchored/bi-lexical formalisms can encode many phenomena without nonterminals.  
- Weakness: “grounded” terminology is overloaded; claims of advantages over AMR are asserted without empirical support or downstream comparisons.  
- Weakness: BSP does not outperform conversion baselines on primary edges (≈59 vs. ≈69 F1), and remote-edge F1 is low (≈13), weakening claims of practical superiority.  
- Weakness: baselines could be stronger using known reconstruction techniques, which may further favor conversion approaches and challenge BSP’s value.  
- Weakness: limited error analysis by linguistic phenomenon; lacks breakdown (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, etc.) and assessment of how much UCCA’s extra structure helps downstream.  
- Question: please quantify frequency/distribution of remote edges and discontinuities and give per-phenomenon performance where BSP outperforms conversion.  
- Question: how sensitive are results to dependency conversion label-priority and Label(u) heuristics; could learned head rules or methods like Kong et al. (2015) improve reconstruction?  
- Question: what proportion of upper-bound conversion loss stems from nonterminal removal versus other factors—a decomposition would clarify conversion hurts.  
- Question: have you tried beam search or neural/BiLSTM scoring in BSP given the LSTM baseline’s strength?  
- Question: please report speed and memory comparisons across systems to substantiate efficiency claims for transition-based methods.  
- Additional: reframe motivation to acknowledge alternative anchored formalisms and clarify focus on UCCA’s explicit nonterminals/discontinuities.  
- Additional: consider adding a downstream proxy (e.g., predicate-argument extraction) to support claims about anchoring and nonterminals’ utility.  
- Additional: replace “grounded” with “anchored/token-aligned” and temper comparisons to AMR to avoid unsupported implications.
