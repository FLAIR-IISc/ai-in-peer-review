Summary and contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing with three structural properties: multiple parents (reentrancies), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. It explores two transition-based routes: (i) lossy conversion from UCCA graphs to trees (constituency and dependency) followed by off-the-shelf parsers, and (ii) a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that supports all three properties, including “remote” edges. Experiments on English UCCA (in-domain Wikipedia and out-of-domain 20K Leagues) show that an LSTM-based dependency parser on converted data achieves the best primary F1 (69.2), while BSP achieves lower primary F1 (~59) but can predict a small fraction of remote edges (~13 F1).

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) Establishing UCCA parsing as a benchmark task, including evaluation protocol, conversion procedures to/from trees, and a comparative study of conversion-based vs. direct parsing approaches.
2) A direct transition-based parser (BSP) for UCCA that supports non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents, with a dynamic oracle and UCCA-specific features.
3) Empirical findings that strong sequence models (stack-LSTM) on converted structures outperform the direct parser on primary edges, highlighting a practical path forward and an upper bound analysis quantifying conversion loss.

Strengths
- Clear task setup and evaluation protocol: The paper defines a reasonable labeled F1 measure over UCCA graphs (with and without remote edges), reports in-/out-of-domain results, and provides conversion upper bounds. This enables reproducibility and fair comparison among methods.
- Useful conversion pipelines: The constituency and dependency conversions are simple, implementable, and empirically effective. The stack-LSTM dependency baseline reaches 69.2 primary F1, establishing a strong reference point; the quantified loss through conversion (e.g., 88.4 primary F1 upper bound for dependency) is informative.
- Direct parser with coverage of key UCCA phenomena: BSP extends transition-based parsing with remote-edge transitions, SWAP for discontinuities, NODE for non-terminals, and a dynamic oracle. The “ratio” feature and UCCA-oriented feature set are thoughtful engineering contributions.
- Breadth of evaluation: Results include in-domain and out-of-domain setups, feature-ablation on development data, and comparisons to multiple transition systems (arc-standard/eager, constituency). This breadth strengthens the empirical picture.
- Potential impact on future parsers: The paper convincingly shows that transition-based techniques are viable for UCCA and that stronger local classifiers (e.g., stack-LSTMs) substantially help—suggesting an actionable path to improve direct UCCA parsers.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical advantage of the direct parser: BSP underperforms strong conversion-based baselines on primary edges (e.g., 59 F1 vs. 69.2 for the stack-LSTM dependency baseline), and its remote-edge F1 (~13) is very low in absolute terms. As a result, the central methodological contribution (BSP) is not yet competitive, which weakens the case for acceptance on performance grounds.
- Overstated representational claims and framing: The introduction asserts UCCA as the only scheme with the “required” three properties and heavily motivates non-terminals and grounding as essential. Several existing semantic frameworks support reentrancy and discontinuity (e.g., SDP formalisms), and the necessity of non-terminals for expressivity is debatable (many structures can be encoded bi-lexically, with trade-offs). The paper would benefit from a more balanced discussion and clearer separation between desiderata, expressivity, and practical utility.
- Ambiguous terminology (“grounded”): The term “grounded” is overloaded; here it seems to mean “anchored to input tokens.” In the AMR literature, “grounding” can imply external reference to world entities. The current argument comparing UCCA vs. AMR would be clearer if reframed using “anchored” or “lexicalized,” and the empirical benefits over AMR-style unanchored parsing should be demonstrated rather than asserted.
- Missing stronger tree-to-graph reconstruction baselines: The dependency/constituency conversions are evaluated without post-hoc reconstruction of non-terminals/reentrancies. Prior work on tree approximations and edge reconstruction (e.g., post-processing classifiers, factorization methods; see Agić et al., 2015, and related reconstruction lines) could substantially improve the conversion-based pipelines and might further diminish BSP’s relative advantage.
- Limited analysis of where BSP helps: The paper would be stronger with targeted evaluation on sentences exhibiting each phenomenon (reentrancy, discontinuity, coordination, MWEs) and a qualitative error analysis of remote-edge predictions. Without that, it is hard to see concrete benefits of BSP beyond a small remote-edge F1.

Questions to authors
- On the three “required” properties: Can you provide empirical evidence that non-terminals are necessary for UCCA parsing quality (e.g., compare performance when forcing bi-lexical structures with reconstruction), or at least a controlled analysis of constructions where non-terminals demonstrably help?
- Terminology: Would you consider replacing “grounded” with “anchored” or “lexicalized,” and revising the discussion contrasting UCCA and AMR to avoid conflating anchoring with other notions of grounding?
- Reconstruction baselines: Have you tried post-processing to recover remote edges and non-terminals from dependency parses (e.g., learning to predict remotes given a tree)? If not, can you estimate the potential based on your upper-bound gaps?
- Classifier choice and search: Since the stack-LSTM outperforms the perceptron-based pipeline by a large margin on primary edges, do you have results with an LSTM (or transformer) classifier inside BSP, and/or beam search? How much of BSP’s underperformance is due to greedy inference?
- Targeted evaluation: Can you report per-phenomenon metrics (e.g., performance on discontinuous constructions, coordination, linker relations) to identify where BSP is comparatively strong?

Additional comments
- Please soften claims that UCCA is the only sensible or only adequate representation supporting the needed properties; acknowledge trade-offs with bi-lexical DAGs (SDP) and other anchored formalisms.
- The conversion upper bounds are useful; consider also reporting the gap per edge label or per phenomenon to guide where better inverses are needed.
- Clarify whether linkage nodes/edges are dropped only for training or also for evaluation; discuss potential impact on Scene-level relations.
