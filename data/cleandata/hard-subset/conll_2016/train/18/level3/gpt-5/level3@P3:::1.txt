Summary
The paper argues that broad-coverage grounded semantic parsing should support three structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity. Using UCCA as the only existing scheme that exhibits all three, the authors (a) define two lossy conversion pipelines from UCCA to related formalisms—discontinuous constituency and surface dependency—and reuse off-the-shelf parsers; and (b) introduce a direct transition-based graph parser (BSP) that natively supports all three properties. Experiments on in-domain (UCCA Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) corpora compare three setups: constituency-based (UPARSE), dependency-based (Malt and an LSTM dependency parser), and the proposed BSP. The best primary-edge F1 comes from the LSTM dependency baseline (69.2), while BSP is the only model that predicts some remote edges (≈13 F1).

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A direct transition-based parser for UCCA-style DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuity, trained with a dynamic oracle, and able to predict remote edges. This is the core technical contribution.
- Empirical framework for UCCA parsing: conversion procedures to constituency and dependency formats plus comparative evaluation on in-domain and out-of-domain data.
- Release promise of converters and parser, which can catalyze work on grounded semantics.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and under-served problem: grounded, general-purpose semantic parsing that goes beyond bilexical dependencies to capture coordination, MWEs, and shared arguments.
- Clear, reproducible transition system with a dynamic oracle and a useful global “ratio” feature; the design extends familiar shift–reduce principles and is easy to build upon (e.g., swapping in stronger classifiers).
- Solid experimental design across three setups, including in-/out-of-domain evaluation; the finding that a strong dependency LSTM baseline outperforms conversion and direct DAG parsing on primary edges is informative and actionable.
- UCCA parsing results and converters fill a gap: they establish initial empirical baselines for future UCCA work and for analyzing loss incurred by tree/DAG conversions.
- The work is timely and of broad interest to CoNLL: it connects syntactic and semantic parsing and highlights open design/evaluation gaps in grounded semantics.

Weaknesses
- Baselines and metrics are incomplete relative to the SDP literature. There are no comparisons to SemEval 2014/2015 SDP parsers or metrics; evaluation should include SDP-style labeled/unlabeled metrics to enable cross-formalism comparison. Related DAG parsers (e.g., Ribeyre’s DAGParser, TurboParser DAG extensions) are neither compared nor discussed empirically.
- The “first semantic-oriented parser” claim is overstated. While the combination (DAG + non-terminals + discontinuity) for a grounded scheme is novel, the transition inventory largely mirrors existing discontinuous constituency and DAG parsers and does not exploit UCCA-specific phenomena. In particular, handling remote edges via dedicated transitions seems unnecessary; label-level or edge-attribute distinctions could suffice without expanding the transition set.
- Conversion baselines underplay phrase-structure strength: the constituency experiment should also compare against a strong phrase-structure baseline (e.g., Berkeley parser) with rich, UCCA-informed node labels, and to Fernández-González & Martins (2015) who study parsing-as-reduction between dependencies and phrases. The UPARSE reference/package details are missing or unclear.
- Dependency conversion is very lossy by design (non-terminals discarded, heuristic inverse). Stronger, SDP-style graph conversions (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014) could preserve more structure and yield more meaningful dependency results and comparisons to SemEval systems.
- Methodological clarity and scope need tightening. The three proposed principles are generic and somewhat ad hoc. The paper should more clearly state whether the goal is: (i) a UCCA parser, (ii) a general-purpose semantic parser, or (iii) a proposal for a parsable semantic scheme; this affects baselines, metrics, and claims. A deeper error analysis is missing, making it hard to assess informativeness and parsability of predicted graphs.

Questions to Authors
- Remote edges: What proportion of edges are remote in the corpora, and how do your errors distribute across false positives vs. missed links? Could you emulate remote edges as primary edges with a “remote” attribute and compare performance/complexity without new transitions?
- Discontinuity: How frequent are discontinuous units and SWAP actions? What is BSP’s specific error profile on discontinuities vs. continuous structures?
- Implicit units: Please provide concrete examples of UCCA implicit units and discuss how their omission might bias training/evaluation. Any plans for joint modeling?
- Scope phenomena: UCCA seems not to model quantifier scope (unlike MRS/DRT). Do you observe systematic errors or representational gaps related to scope that affect parsing?
- Classifier choice: Have you tried replacing the perceptron with an LSTM or MLP classifier in BSP? Given the large gains for the dependency LSTM, what increase do you expect for BSP?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Evaluation: Report SDP-style metrics alongside your yield-based metric to enable comparison with SemEval (2014–2015) systems. Add unlabeled scores and per-category F1 (e.g., coordination, MWEs, linkage).
- Baselines: Include (or at least discuss) results from DAG/Turbo parsers if feasible; otherwise, provide oracle conversions into SDP graphs and compare against published SDP systems. For phrase-structure, compare against the Berkeley parser with UCCA-informed labels, and add Fernández-González & Martins (2015).
- Conversions: Explore richer dependency conversions that retain predicate–argument structure and coordination (SDP-style); document conversion loss more thoroughly.
- Positioning/organization: Introduce and define “grounded semantic” in the first paragraph. Clarify the paper’s primary goal and reposition the “first” claim accordingly (e.g., first direct, transition-based parser for a grounded scheme supporting DAG + non-terminals + discontinuity).
- Error analysis: Add qualitative and quantitative error analysis, including breakdowns by structure type and label, to assess informativeness of predicted graphs.
- Resources/citations: Please add explicit references/links to UPARSE, DAGParser (Ribeyre), and TurboParser repositories. Ensure all in-text tool names have corresponding bibliographic entries.

Overall, despite methodological gaps and missing comparisons, the paper raises an important problem, proposes a concrete, extensible approach, and presents useful empirical findings. It is worth discussion at CoNLL.
