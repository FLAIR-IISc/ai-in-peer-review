Summary
The paper introduces broad-coverage parsing for UCCA, proposing two transition-based strategies: (i) conversion to more restrictive formalisms (dependencies, discontinuous constituency) followed by off-the-shelf parsing and inverse conversion, and (ii) a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that supports multiple parents, nonterminals, and discontinuities. Experiments on UCCA English corpora report primary-edge F1 around 59 for BSP, with an LSTM dependency baseline at 69.2; remote-edge F1 is low (~13).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A direct transition system for UCCA/BSS with support for DAGs and discontinuities, plus a dynamic-oracle training setup.
2) Practical conversion pipelines (with algorithms) between UCCA and constituency/dependency formalisms, together with an evaluation protocol for primary/remote edges.
3) Empirical benchmark of multiple methods on UCCA, including in-domain and out-of-domain tests, and feature ablations.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and engineering: The BSP transition set, dynamic oracle, and feature design adapt known techniques to UCCA’s demands (nonterminals, remotes, discontinuities) in a coherent framework.
- Useful conversion baselines and metrics: The bidirectional conversion procedures and the primary/remote evaluation clarify where information is lost and provide a reproducible yardstick for future work.
- Empirical breadth and transparency: Multiple parsers (Malt, UPARSE, LSTM) and in-/out-of-domain evaluations, plus feature ablations, give a relatively comprehensive picture of the landscape.
- Community value: First systematic study of UCCA parsing with released tools will likely catalyze further research on anchored/lexicalized semantic graphs.

Weaknesses
- Overstated framing: The introduction/background over-privileges UCCA as “the only sensible” graph-based SR; the discussion of alternatives (SDP, AMR) is not sufficiently balanced.
- Questionable criteria: The three proposed desiderata conflate necessity with convenience; nonterminals may not increase expressive power, and the advantages of “grounded” representations over AMR are argued but not demonstrated. The term “grounded” is overloaded; “anchored/lexicalized” would be clearer.
- Underwhelming results: BSP underperforms simple conversion baselines (Malt 60.1 F1, UPARSE 65.4 F1; LSTM 69.2 F1) on primary edges, and remote-edge F1 is very low (~13), limiting the practical impact of the “full” parser.
- Missing stronger baselines: Prior tree-approximation plus edge-reconstruction methods (e.g., Agić et al., 2015) could raise dependency baselines and further diminish BSP’s relative advantage; no comparison to modern DAG parsers or neuralized BSP (e.g., LSTM classifier, beam).
- Limited analysis: Little fine-grained error analysis by phenomenon (coordination, discontinuities, remotes), making it hard to connect design choices to outcomes.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Can you provide a more balanced argument for UCCA vs. SDP/AMR and adopt “anchored/lexicalized” instead of “grounded”?
- Do nonterminals add expressive power in UCCA, or mainly convenience? Please illustrate phenomena unrepresentable with bilexical dependencies.
- Can you report BSP with a neural local classifier and/or beam search?
- Can you include a tree-approximation + edge-reconstruction baseline (e.g., Agić et al., 2015) to contextualize BSP’s gains on remote edges?
