- Task and setup: tackles UCCA parsing requiring DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities, comparing conversion-based pipelines with a direct transition-based parser (BSP) on in-domain and out-of-domain data.  
- Contribution 1: Empirical baselines and upper bounds via two conversion pipelines plus the first direct transition-based parser for UCCA, with OOD evaluation.  
- Contribution 2: Concrete transition system and feature set (including dynamic oracle and a “ratio” regularizer) for parsing grounded DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities.  
- Contribution 3: Evaluation protocol (yield+label), converters/resources, and analysis of information loss under conversion.  
- Strength: Clear problem framing using UCCA and strong experimental design with in-domain and out-of-domain tests.  
- Strength: Dual methodology (conversion vs direct) clarifies trade-offs and quantifies conversion-induced information loss.  
- Strength: BSP is the first transition-based parser claiming native support for DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities, presented reproducibly.  
- Strength: Stack-LSTM dependency baseline significantly outperforms older baselines, highlighting neural gains and directions for BSP.  
- Strength: Authors promise to release converters and code, aiding the community.  
- Weakness: Novelty overstated—transition inventory is a modest extension of known systems and REMOTE handling lacks ablation justification.  
- Weakness: Incomplete baselines/metrics—no comparisons to SDP/DAG parsers and no SemEval/SDP-style metrics for comparability.  
- Weakness: Conversion baselines are underpowered—non-terminals are discarded and reconstructed with simple rules, underestimating stronger conversion possibilities.  
- Weakness: Limited error analysis—little breakdown by coordination, phrasal verbs, discontinuities, remotes, implicit units, or scope-sensitive phenomena.  
- Weakness: Clarity/positioning issues—unclear primary goal (UCCA parser vs general BSS parser) and editorial problems (late terminology, missing references).  
- Question: Justify separate LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE transitions versus encoding remoteness in labels; provide ablation on remote-edge performance.  
- Question: Compare to SDP/DAG parsers or report SemEval-style metrics on converted graphs (e.g., run TurboParser/TurboSemDep).  
- Question: Try stronger constituency baselines (Berkeley/neural parsers) with enriched node labels preserving UCCA categories.  
- Question: Have you tried beam search or an LSTM classifier within BSP, given the LSTM dependency gains?  
- Question: Provide error analysis by phenomenon (coordination, MWE/phrasal verbs, long-distance discontinuities, remotes) with precision/recall/confusions.  
- Question: Clarify and exemplify “implicit units” and how BSP could be extended to handle them.  
- Question: Explain how UCCA treats quantifier scope vs MRS/DRT and implications for downstream use.  
- Additional suggestion: Implement richer conversions (SDP-style heads, prior work) and report improved upper bounds.  
- Additional suggestion: Test a “constituency with rich node labels” baseline (e.g., Berkeley) to separate parser limitations from conversion loss.  
- Additional suggestion: Introduce “grounded semantic” earlier, correct/add missing references (UPARSE, Turbo/TurboSemDep, DAGParser, Fernández-González & Martins), and provide anonymized resource links for reproducibility.  
- Overall: Important and valuable empirical study that could become a strong CoNLL contribution if methodological comparisons, stronger baselines, and detailed analyses are added.
