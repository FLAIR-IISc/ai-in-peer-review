Summary
The paper studies broad-coverage, grounded semantic parsing with UCCA, arguing for three structural properties (reentrancy, non-terminals, discontinuity) and proposing two strategies: (i) conversion to related formalisms (constituency, dependency) and application of off-the-shelf parsers; (ii) a direct transition-based graph parser (BSP) that supports all three properties. Experiments on UCCA Wiki and 20K Leagues show that strong dependency baselines (LSTM) yield the best primary-edge F1 via conversion, while BSP uniquely predicts remote edges, albeit at low F1.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) First systematic empirical study of UCCA parsing, including conversion pipelines, evaluation protocol, and code, establishing a baseline landscape.
2) A concrete transition system and dynamic-oracle training for DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, demonstrating feasibility of direct parsing of UCCA-style graphs.
3) An informative comparison of conversion vs. direct parsing that highlights the importance of classifier strength over transition inventory.

Strengths
- Timely, interesting problem: bringing grounded, graph-based semantics into a transition-based framework is valuable and relevant to CoNLL audiences.
- Clear articulation and implementation of a direct parser that handles reentrancy and discontinuity; dynamic oracle and feature ablations are carefully executed.
- Empirical evidence that conversion plus strong neural classifiers is competitive, providing practical guidance to the community.
- Public resources and explicit conversions increase reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Novelty/“first semantic-oriented parser” claim is weak: the transition set is largely generic and does not exploit UCCA-specific phenomena; remote edges could be modeled by label distinctions rather than dedicated transitions.
- Missing graph-parsing baselines: prior SemEval 2014–2015 SDP systems and off-the-shelf DAG parsers (e.g., DAGParser/Ribeyre; TurboParser/TurboSemDep) are not compared, limiting external validity.
- Phrase-structure baselines are incomplete: comparison to Fernández-González & Martins (2015) is essential; uparse is used but the reference is missing.
- Evaluation misaligned with related work: only a yield-based labeled F is reported; SDP-style metrics should be included on dependency conversions to enable comparability.
- Conversions are lossy: dependency conversion discards non-terminals; richer SDP-style conversions (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014) should be explored; consider a constituent baseline with rich node labels using the Berkeley parser.

Questions to Authors
- Please justify new transitions for remote edges versus label-based handling; what empirical gain do they yield?
- Why were established SDP/DAG parsers not used as baselines? Were code or format barriers insurmountable?
- Can you add a Fernández-González & Martins (2015) reduction baseline and a Berkeley parser baseline with rich labels?
- Provide concrete examples of UCCA implicit units and discuss whether/how BSP could handle them.
- UCCA seems not to model quantifier scope (vs. MRS/DRT). How does this affect downstream utility? Please include an error analysis by phenomenon (coordination, remotes, discontinuities).

Additional comments
The three guiding principles are generic and somewhat ad hoc; the paper would benefit from a clearer, broader introduction and a crisper statement of goals (UCCA-specific parser vs. general semantic parser vs. parsable semantic scheme). Introduce “grounded semantic” earlier. Despite issues, the work merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.
