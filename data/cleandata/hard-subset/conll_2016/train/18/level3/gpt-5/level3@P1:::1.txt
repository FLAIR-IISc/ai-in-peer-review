Summary
The paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing by arguing that practical semantic representations should support three structural properties: multiple parents (reentrancies), non-terminal units (constituents without obvious heads), and discontinuity. It targets UCCA, the only available scheme that natively exhibits all three. The authors pursue two strategies: (i) conversion-based parsing, mapping UCCA graphs to constituency or dependency trees, applying off-the-shelf parsers, and converting back; and (ii) a direct transition-based graph parser (BSP) that supports non-terminals, discontinuities (via SWAP), and reentrancies (via REMOTE edges), trained with a dynamic oracle. Experiments on UCCA’s English Wikipedia corpus (in-domain) and 20K Leagues (out-of-domain) report a best primary-edge F1 of 69.2% using an LSTM arc-standard dependency parser after conversion, and BSP achieves around 59% primary F1 and 13% F1 on remote edges, with reasonable OOD robustness. The paper includes conversion upper bounds and feature ablations.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An empirical study demonstrating that transition-based parsers, via simple conversions, are strong baselines for UCCA, with clear conversion procedures and upper bounds that diagnose information loss.
2) A workable transition system and training setup (with dynamic oracle) for parsing DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, producing the first end-to-end UCCA results that include some remote edges.
3) Public converters and an evaluation protocol (yield- and label-based) that can seed further work on UCCA parsing.

Strengths
- Relevance and timeliness: UCCA is underexplored but attractive for grounded, cross-lingual semantics. Providing the first reproducible parsing results and tools for UCCA is a concrete community contribution.
- Sensible experimental design: in-domain and OOD evaluations; explicit conversion upper bounds; and informative feature ablations (e.g., the usefulness of the ratio feature and unigrams/bigrams).
- Clear, implementable conversion algorithms: the constituency and dependency conversions are transparent, and the reported upper bounds frame the attainable performance of conversion-based methods.
- Direct handling of reentrancy: unlike tree-only baselines, BSP predicts some remote edges, showing feasibility of DAG parsing for UCCA.
- Robustness: OOD results degrade modestly, suggesting the approach is not overfit to Wikipedia.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The transition set largely mirrors prior work on discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing. The REMOTE edges are handled by dedicating new transitions, but this design is not clearly superior to treating remoteness as an edge attribute. The paper’s “first parser” claim is weakened by not demonstrating clear UCCA-specific modeling gains (e.g., for coordination, MWEs).
- Missing baselines and metrics from related SDP work: There is no comparison to SemEval 2014/2015 SDP systems or to strong DAG/Turbo parsers. Results are not reported under SDP metrics, limiting comparability to a closely related literature that also targets broad semantic structures with reentrancies.
- Conversion choices are overly lossy: The dependency conversion discards non-terminals and remotes, then reconstructs with simple heuristics. Richer SDP-style conversions (preserving reentrancies and some latent structure) or a parsing-as-reduction approach (e.g., Fernández-González & Martins, 2015) could provide stronger baselines and a fairer assessment of the direct parser.
- Scope and framing: The three proposed principles (multi-parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) are general graph/phrase-structure desiderata rather than uniquely semantic. The paper needs a clearer positioning: is the main goal a UCCA parser, a general BSS parser, or an argument for a parsable semantic scheme? The relation to AMR, MRS, and DRT is underdeveloped; important semantic phenomena like quantifier scope appear out of scope for UCCA’s foundational layer.
- Insufficient qualitative analysis: Limited error analysis by UCCA category, discontinuity type, or remote-edge class; no discussion of implicit units (which are excluded) and their practical impact. This makes it hard to assess where the approach is informative vs. where it falls short.

Questions to Authors
- Why dedicate specific LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE transitions rather than predict a remoteness attribute with the same EDGE transitions? Please include an ablation comparing these designs.
- Can you re-evaluate with SDP metrics and include competitive SDP systems (e.g., Turbo-based, DAGParser) to situate performance relative to SemEval 2014/2015?
- Can you provide a richer conversion to semantic dependencies that preserves reentrancies and some non-terminal information (e.g., latent nodes or compound labels), and compare to parsing-as-reduction (Fernández-González & Martins, 2015)?
- Please add per-category F1 (Scenes, Participants, Linkers, Centers, coordination, MWEs), and specific analyses for discontinuities and remote edges. What are the dominant error sources for BSP vs. conversion baselines?
- How will implicit UCCA units be handled in future parsing (any preliminary experiments or examples)? What is the expected impact on evaluation and applications?
- Does UCCA model scope-sensitive phenomena (e.g., quantifier scope, control/raising) in this layer? If not, how should “broad-coverage” be interpreted?

Additional Comments
- Introduce “grounded semantic representation” early (tokens aligned to graph units) to orient readers.
- Add the missing UPARSE citation; consider a Berkeley parser baseline with richly labeled non-terminals. Also consider citing and, if feasible, including TurboParser/DAGParser repos as baselines.
- Report dataset statistics per split (rates of discontinuity and remotes) and release preprocessing details for reproducibility.
- The paper is interesting and valuable for CoNLL; strengthening baselines, metrics, and framing would significantly improve its impact.
