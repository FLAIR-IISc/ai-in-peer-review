- Paper addresses UCCA parsing with three structural properties (reentrancy, non-terminals, discontinuities) via conversion-based pipelines and a direct transition parser (BSP).
- Experimental finding: LSTM-based dependency parser on converted graphs achieves best primary F1 (69.2); BSP gets ~59 primary F1 and ~13 F1 on remote edges.
- Contribution: Establishes UCCA parsing as a benchmark with evaluation protocol, conversion procedures, and comparative study of approaches.
- Contribution: Proposes BSP, a direct transition-based parser supporting non-terminals, discontinuities, multiple parents, remote edges, a dynamic oracle, and UCCA-specific features.
- Contribution: Shows that strong sequence models on converted structures outperform the direct parser and provides upper-bound analysis quantifying conversion loss.
- Strength: Clear task setup and reasonable labeled F1 evaluation (with/without remotes), including in-/out-of-domain results and conversion upper bounds for reproducibility.
- Strength: Simple, effective constituency and dependency conversion pipelines; stack-LSTM dependency baseline at 69.2 primary F1 is a strong reference.
- Strength: BSP extends transition-based parsing with SWAP, NODE, remote-edge transitions, dynamic oracle, and useful UCCA-oriented features like the “ratio” feature.
- Strength: Broad evaluation including in-/out-of-domain tests, feature ablation, and comparisons to multiple transition systems.
- Strength: Demonstrates that transition-based techniques are viable for UCCA and that stronger local classifiers substantially help future direct parsers.
- Weakness: BSP underperforms conversion-based baselines on primary edges and achieves very low absolute remote-edge F1, weakening its empirical case.
- Weakness: Paper overstates representational claims (e.g., uniqueness of UCCA’s properties) and lacks balanced discussion of alternatives like SDP or bi-lexical encodings.
- Weakness: Terminology “grounded” is ambiguous and conflates anchoring with other notions used in AMR literature.
- Weakness: Missing stronger tree-to-graph reconstruction baselines (post-hoc reconstructions or classifiers) that could improve conversion pipelines and reduce BSP’s relative advantage.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of where BSP actually helps—no targeted evaluation or qualitative error analysis on remotes and specific phenomena.
- Question: Can authors provide empirical evidence that non-terminals are necessary, e.g., by comparing to bi-lexical reconstructions or controlled analyses?
- Question: Will authors replace “grounded” with “anchored/lexicalized” and clarify the UCCA vs AMR comparison?
- Question: Have authors tried post-processing to recover remote edges/non-terminals from trees, or can they estimate potential based on upper-bound gaps?
- Question: Do authors have results using stronger classifiers (LSTM/Transformer) or beam search inside BSP to test whether greedy inference causes underperformance?
- Question: Can authors report per-phenomenon metrics (reentrancy, discontinuity, coordination, MWEs) to show where BSP is comparatively strong?
- Additional: Soften claims that UCCA is uniquely adequate; acknowledge trade-offs with bi-lexical DAGs and anchored formalisms.
- Additional: Consider reporting conversion gaps per edge label/phenomenon to guide where inverse mappings need improvement.
- Additional: Clarify whether linkage nodes/edges are dropped only during training or also in evaluation and discuss impact on Scene-level relations.
