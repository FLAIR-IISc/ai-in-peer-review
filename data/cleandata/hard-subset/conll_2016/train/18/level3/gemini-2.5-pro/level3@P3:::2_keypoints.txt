1. Presents first broad-coverage UCCA parsers, comparing conversion-based (dependency/constituency) and direct (BSP) approaches; conversion LSTM tops primary-edge F1, BSP recovers some remote edges with low F1.  
2. Contribution: first empirical study and baselines for UCCA parsing, benchmarking conversion vs direct strategies.  
3. Contribution: introduces BSP, a novel transition-based parser combining discontinuous constituency and DAG dependency techniques to model UCCA.  
4. Strength: fills a clear community need by providing the first automatic UCCA parsers and benchmarks.  
5. Strength: sound, thorough methodology—evaluates direct parser against multiple strong baselines including an LSTM dependency parser.  
6. Strength: BSP design is well-motivated to handle non-terminals, reentrancies, and discontinuities.  
7. Weakness: empirical results are underwhelming—conversion LSTM outperforms BSP on primary edges and BSP’s remote-edge F1 is very low (13.3%); baselines may be incomplete.  
8. Weakness: introduction overstates UCCA’s indispensability and downplays alternatives (AMR, SDP) without sufficient justification.  
9. Weakness: terminology (e.g., “grounded”) is vague/overloaded and some arguments about alignment and parsing difficulty are contestable.  
10. Question: Have you tried incorporating LSTMs or other neural features into BSP to close the performance gap?  
11. Question: Can you analyze which types of remote edges BSP fails to recover—data sparsity or transition/feature limitations?  
12. Question: Why not compare to a two-stage pipeline (tree parsing then remote-edge reconstruction), a common strong baseline?
