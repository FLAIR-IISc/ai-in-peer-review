### **Summary of the paper**

This paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme. The authors argue that a broad-coverage semantic representation must support three key structural properties: multiple parents (reentrancy), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents, all of which are present in UCCA. They tackle the parsing problem with two complementary strategies: (1) a conversion-based approach, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted to dependency or constituency trees to be parsed by existing state-of-the-art parsers (MaltParser, UPARSE, and an LSTM-based dependency parser), and (2) a direct approach, for which they design a novel transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), that directly models all three structural properties of UCCA. Experiments on the English UCCA-annotated corpora show that a conversion-based LSTM parser achieves the highest F-score on primary edges (69.2%), while the direct BSP parser is able to recover some remote edges, albeit with a low F-score of 13.3%.

### **Main contributions**

1.  **The first empirical study of parsing the UCCA semantic representation.** As UCCA is a relatively new formalism, this work provides a valuable and necessary first step by establishing parsing baselines. The methodical comparison of conversion-based and direct parsing approaches provides an initial benchmark and a clear picture of the challenges involved.
2.  **The development of BSP, a novel transition-based parser for UCCA.** BSP is a well-designed parser that synthesizes techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and DAG dependency parsing to directly handle the specific graph structures of UCCA. While its performance is not state-of-the-art, the architecture itself is a useful contribution for researchers working on similar graph-based, text-anchored representations.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a clear need in the community.** The utility of any semantic representation scheme is greatly enhanced by the availability of an automatic parser. This paper is the first to address UCCA parsing, providing both baselines and a novel parser, which will be valuable for any future work involving UCCA.
2.  **Sound and thorough methodology.** The paper's two-pronged approach is commendable. Evaluating a novel, direct parser against strong baselines derived from mature, existing parsers is a rigorous way to assess its performance. The inclusion of multiple dependency parsers, including a modern LSTM-based one, provides a robust point of comparison.
3.  **Well-motivated parser design.** The BSP parser is a sensible and logical extension of existing transition-based parsing paradigms. The transition set and features are clearly motivated by the need to handle non-terminals, reentrancies (remote edges), and discontinuities, combining established techniques in a novel way for this specific task.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Underwhelming empirical results and potentially weak baselines.** The central claim that BSP is an effective parser for UCCA is undermined by the evaluation. The conversion-based LSTM dependency parser baseline significantly outperforms BSP on primary edges (69.2% F1 vs. 59.0% F1), suggesting that a powerful classifier is more important than a specialized transition system for this core task. Furthermore, the F1 score for remote edges is very low (13.3%), calling into question the practical utility of the direct approach for capturing the most complex aspects of the representation. The baselines could also be made stronger; a common approach in semantic dependency parsing is to use a two-stage pipeline (tree parsing followed by an edge-reconstruction step, e.g., Agic et al., 2015), which seems like a powerful and relevant baseline that is missing here.
2.  **Overstated claims and imbalanced framing in the introduction.** The paper frames UCCA's three structural properties as indispensable for any broad-coverage semantic representation, implicitly dismissing other formalisms like AMR or Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP). This is a strong and unsubstantiated claim. For instance, the necessity of non-terminal nodes over well-defined headship conventions in bi-lexical dependency schemes is debatable. Similarly, the advantages of a "grounded" representation over an ungrounded one like AMR are asserted rather than empirically demonstrated; both approaches involve well-understood trade-offs that are not acknowledged. The framing should be more nuanced, positioning UCCA as one of several valid approaches to semantic representation.
3.  **Vague and overloaded terminology.** The term "grounded" is used to differentiate UCCA from AMR, but this term is overloaded in NLP (e.g., grounding in vision). More precise alternatives like "anchored" (to the text spans) or "lexicalized" would improve clarity. The argument that ungrounded representations are inherently harder to parse due to the alignment problem is also contestable, as modern parsers often learn the alignment jointly with the structure. The arguments for UCCA's design principles should be rephrased more carefully to avoid ambiguity and strong, unsupported claims.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The LSTM-based dependency parser baseline substantially outperforms your direct BSP parser on primary edges. This suggests the performance gap may be due to the classifier's modeling power rather than the transition system itself. Have you considered incorporating LSTMs or other neural features into the BSP classifier to see if this would allow it to match or exceed the baseline's performance?
2.  The F1 score for remote edges is very low. Could you provide an analysis of the types of remote edges the parser fails to recover? Is this primarily a data sparsity issue, or does it point to limitations in the transition system or feature set for modeling these long-distance, non-local dependencies?
3.  Why did you not compare against a two-stage parsing pipeline, where a strong tree parser first predicts the primary edges and a separate classifier then predicts remote edges over that structure? This is a standard and often very effective technique in related tasks like semantic dependency parsing and could serve as a very strong baseline.
