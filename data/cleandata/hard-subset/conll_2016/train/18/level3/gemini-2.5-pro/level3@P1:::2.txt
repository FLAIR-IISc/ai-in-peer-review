**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents the first work on parsing for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a broad-coverage semantic representation. UCCA structures are directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) whose leaves are the words of a sentence, and which support non-terminal nodes, discontinuous constituents, and nodes with multiple parents. The authors propose and evaluate two main approaches. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted into either discontinuous constituency trees or dependency trees, which are then parsed using existing state-of-the-art parsers (UPARSE, MaltParser, and a stack-LSTM parser). The second approach is a novel, direct transition-based parser, Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), specifically designed to produce UCCA's complex structures. The systems are evaluated on the English UCCA-annotated corpora, establishing the first parsing benchmarks for this formalism.

**Contributions**

1.  **First Parsers for the UCCA Formalism:** The primary contribution of this work is the development and evaluation of the first systems for parsing UCCA structures. This includes both the creation of conversion methods that allow existing tools to serve as strong baselines, and the implementation of a novel direct parser (BSP). This is a valuable contribution that makes the UCCA representation accessible for automatic processing and downstream applications for the first time.
2.  **Empirical Analysis of Parsing Strategies for UCCA:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical comparison of different strategies for parsing UCCA. By evaluating conversion-based methods against a direct parsing method, the work offers valuable insights into the challenges of the task. The results serve as an important benchmark for future research on parsing this and other similar graph-structured semantic representations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the Task:** The paper addresses a new and challenging parsing problem. UCCA combines properties of both constituency and dependency formalisms (non-terminal nodes and DAG structures), and this work is a commendable first attempt at automatically producing these representations. Establishing baselines and a dedicated parser for a novel representation is a solid contribution to the field.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The authors have designed a thorough set of experiments. They test two distinct conversion-based approaches, employing multiple well-established and state-of-the-art parsers for each. This provides a strong and diverse set of baselines against which the performance of their proposed direct parser can be rigorously assessed. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation further strengthens the analysis.
3.  **Well-Motivated Parser Design:** The proposed direct parser, BSP, is well-designed and grounded in a solid body of work on transition-based parsing for non-standard structures, such as discontinuous constituency trees and dependency DAGs. The transition system is clearly specified, and the feature set is thoughtfully constructed by combining and extending features from several successful parsers.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Proposed Parser:** The central weakness of the paper is that the proposed direct parser, BSP, is empirically outperformed by a baseline. The stack-LSTM dependency parser, despite relying on a lossy conversion process, achieves a substantially higher F-score on primary edges (69.2%) than both BSP (59.0%) and its tree-only variant (59.9%). Furthermore, BSP's F-score on remote edges is very low (13.3%), which significantly weakens the argument for its main architectural advantage—the ability to natively produce DAGs. These results suggest that the conversion-based approach with a powerful neural classifier is currently superior, questioning the practical utility of the more complex BSP system.
2.  **Overstated and Unbalanced Motivation:** The paper's introduction and background sections argue that the three properties of UCCA (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) are essential requirements for any broad-coverage semantic representation. This claim is debatable and presented without sufficient balance. For instance, the argument against bi-lexical dependency representations (like SDP) rests on the difficulty of standardizing head-selection rules for phenomena like coordination or prepositional phrases. However, this is more of a practical annotation challenge than a fundamental limitation of the formalism's expressive power. The critique of AMR as "non-grounded" is similarly one-sided and overlooks the potential advantages of abstraction.
3.  **Ambiguous Terminology:** The term "grounded" is used in a non-standard way. In semantics and AI, "grounding" typically refers to connecting linguistic expressions to perception, action, or a real-world knowledge base. The authors use it to mean that semantic units are directly anchored to spans of text. This overloaded usage is confusing. Using a more precise term, such as "anchored" or "lexicalized," would clarify the claims and avoid ambiguity.
4.  **Missing Relevant Baselines and Related Work:** The dependency-conversion baseline, while strong, could be made even stronger, which would further highlight the performance gap with BSP. A standard pipeline approach would be to parse a dependency tree and then use a separate classifier to predict remote edges, thereby reconstructing the full DAG. The omission of such a baseline makes the comparison less conclusive. Related work on semantic dependency parsing (e.g., Agic et al., 2015, in *SemEval-2015 Task 18 Proceedings*) has successfully used such pipeline approaches and should have been discussed.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The stack-LSTM dependency parser baseline achieves the best performance on primary edges. Given that this parser's main innovation over MaltParser is its neural classifier, have you considered integrating a similar LSTM-based classifier into your direct BSP parser? Do you believe this would close the significant performance gap between your parser and the baseline?
2.  Your argument for including non-terminal nodes is largely based on avoiding arbitrary head-selection conventions required by dependency schemes. Could you further clarify why this constitutes a fundamental limitation in expressive power, rather than a practical challenge of annotation design that can be solved with consistent guidelines?
3.  The performance of BSP on remote edges is quite low. A plausible alternative approach would be a pipeline model: first, parse a high-accuracy primary-edge tree (perhaps using the LSTM parser), and second, use a separate classifier to predict remote edges on top of this tree structure. How do you see your integrated BSP approach comparing to such a pipeline, especially given the current results?
