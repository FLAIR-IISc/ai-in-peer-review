**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme. The authors explore two strategies: a conversion-based approach that maps UCCA graphs to tree structures to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers, and a direct approach using a novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed to handle UCCA's specific properties like non-terminal nodes and multiple parents.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **First Parsers for UCCA:** The paper presents the first empirical study of parsing UCCA, providing valuable baselines and a publicly available parser for a promising but under-resourced semantic formalism. This is a solid contribution that enables future work in this area.
2.  **Comparative Analysis of Parsing Strategies:** The work offers a useful comparison between directly parsing UCCA's graph structures and the simpler, lossy approach of converting them into trees for existing parsers.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Necessary Task:** Developing a parser for UCCA is a timely and worthwhile research direction. UCCA possesses unique structural properties not found in many other semantic representations, and providing a parsing model is a critical first step toward making the formalism usable in downstream applications and facilitating further research.
2.  **Sound Methodology:** The transition-based approach is a reasonable and well-established choice for this task. The design of the BSP parser, which extends existing techniques for discontinuous parsing and DAGs, is technically sound. The exploration of both direct and conversion-based methods provides a comprehensive initial investigation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unconvincing Empirical Evaluation:** The primary weakness is that the proposed BSP parser's performance is unimpressive. It is outperformed on the main metric (primary edge F-score) by a dependency-based baseline (MaltParser, and especially the LSTM parser). Furthermore, its F-score on remote edges is extremely low (around 13%), which undermines the central claim of developing a parser that can effectively handle UCCA's graph-based structures.
2.  **Overstated and Unbalanced Framing:** The introduction overstates the case for UCCA, framing its three design criteria (non-terminal nodes, multiple parents, "groundedness") as essential for any broad-coverage SR without sufficient justification. The advantages of UCCA's "grounded" nature over a representation like AMR are asserted rather than clearly demonstrated. The term "grounded" is also overloaded and potentially confusing; alternatives like "anchored" or "lexicalized" would be clearer.
3.  **Potentially Weak Baselines:** The dependency-based baselines might be stronger if they incorporated techniques from prior work on tree approximations and edge reconstruction (e.g., Agic et al., 2015). Improving these baselines could further diminish the relative performance of the proposed BSP parser, weakening the paper's claims about its efficacy.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The LSTM-based dependency parser achieved the highest F-score by a significant margin. Given that this model differs from MaltParser primarily in its classifier, have you considered integrating a similar neural classifier into your BSP model? This seems crucial for making a fair comparison of the underlying transition systems.
