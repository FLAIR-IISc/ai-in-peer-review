**Summary**

This paper presents a transition-based parsing approach for UCCA, a broad-coverage semantic representation formalism that uses directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) with non-terminal nodes and discontinuities. The authors explore two strategies: first, by converting UCCA graphs into constituency and dependency trees to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers; and second, by developing a novel, direct transition-based graph parser (BSP) that can handle UCCA's structural properties natively.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  The development and implementation of BSP, the first direct parser for the UCCA formalism. This is a significant engineering contribution that provides a valuable tool and a strong baseline for future work on this representation.
2.  An empirical study comparing direct versus conversion-based methods for parsing UCCA. This analysis offers useful insights into the challenges of parsing such rich structures and the information loss incurred when mapping them to simpler formalisms.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Novel and Challenging Task:** The paper tackles the difficult problem of parsing UCCA, a semantically rich but structurally complex formalism. Pushing the boundaries of parsing beyond trees and toward broad-coverage semantic DAGs is an important research direction, and this work is a valuable first step.
2.  **Solid Core Contribution:** The proposed direct parser, BSP, is a well-conceived system that intelligently combines and extends existing transition-based techniques for handling non-projectivity, DAGs, and non-terminals. It serves as a solid foundation and a reproducible benchmark for parsing UCCA.
3.  **Thoughtful Experimental Design:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation by comparing the direct parsing approach against two different conversion-based strategies. This design effectively highlights the trade-offs involved and demonstrates that transition-based methods are a viable approach for this task, achieving a respectable F-score.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Relevant Prior Work:** The paper’s most significant weakness is the omission of key baselines and evaluation metrics from the closely related field of broad-coverage semantic dependency parsing (SDP). There is no comparison against established graph-based parsers from the SemEval 2014/2015 SDP shared tasks (e.g., TurboParser, DAGParser). This makes it difficult to assess the performance of BSP in the broader context of semantic graph parsing. The evaluation would be much stronger if it included standard metrics from these shared tasks.
2.  **Weak Conceptual Framing and Novelty Claims:** The claim of proposing the first "semantic-oriented" parser is not well-supported, as the transition set appears to be a generic combination of techniques for handling graph structures rather than one that exploits UCCA-specific semantic properties. The three guiding principles (graph-based, non-terminals, discontinuities) are general structural properties, not unique to semantics, which weakens the paper’s conceptual motivation.
3.  **Methodological Concerns with Baselines:** The conversion-based baselines, while useful, could be stronger. The conversion to surface dependency trees loses significant information; a richer conversion inspired by the SDP tasks could have provided a more competitive baseline. For the constituency experiments, comparing against a powerful system like the Berkeley parser, potentially adapted for rich node labels, would have been more informative.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify what aspects of your parser's design (e.g., transition set, features) are specifically tailored to UCCA's *semantics*, as opposed to its generic graph-structured properties (which are also found in some syntactic formalisms)?
2.  Could you elaborate on why established graph parsers from the SDP shared tasks were not included as baselines? A discussion of how you expect BSP to perform against them, especially under standard SDP evaluation metrics, would be very helpful.
3.  The paper defers the handling of implicit units. Could you provide a concrete example of such a unit in UCCA and briefly discuss the challenges it poses for your transition-based framework?
