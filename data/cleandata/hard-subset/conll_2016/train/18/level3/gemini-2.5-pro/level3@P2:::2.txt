### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents the first parsing systems for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a broad-coverage semantic representation scheme. UCCA structures are directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) whose leaves are anchored to the text tokens. They are characterized by three key properties: non-terminal nodes representing complex semantic units, discontinuous units, and multiple parents for shared arguments. The authors pursue two main strategies for parsing UCCA. The first is an indirect, conversion-based approach that maps UCCA graphs to simpler, tree-like structures (constituency or dependency trees), allowing the use of existing state-of-the-art parsers. The second is a direct approach, for which the authors develop a novel transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), specifically designed to handle all three structural properties of UCCA. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that while the proposed BSP parser can handle the full graph structure, a conversion-based approach using an existing LSTM-based dependency parser achieves a substantially higher F-score on the primary tree structure.

### Contributions

1.  **First Parsers and Baselines for UCCA:** The primary contribution is the development and empirical evaluation of the first-ever parsers for the UCCA formalism. This includes a novel transition-based parser (BSP) tailored for UCCA's graph structures and, equally importantly, a systematic investigation of conversion-based methods that establish strong baselines using existing high-performance parsers. This work lays the foundation for future research on parsing this representation.
2.  **Empirical Analysis of Parsing Complex Semantic Graphs:** The paper provides a valuable analysis of the trade-offs involved in parsing UCCA. It contrasts the performance of a direct parsing method that models all structural properties simultaneously against indirect methods that incur information loss during conversion but can leverage more powerful existing parsing technology. The results highlight the significant challenge posed by UCCA's remote edges.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Novel and Worthwhile Problem:** To date, no parser has been available for the UCCA representation. This paper is the first to tackle this problem directly. Given the growing interest in different flavors of broad-coverage semantic parsing, developing a parser for a cross-linguistically motivated scheme like UCCA and establishing performance baselines is a timely and valuable contribution to the field.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Thorough Approach:** The authors' dual strategy of developing a novel parser while also thoroughly evaluating conversion-based approaches is a key strength. This allows for a comprehensive initial exploration of the task, providing a clear picture of what can be achieved with current technology (the conversion methods) versus a more bespoke but currently less performant approach (the direct parser).
3.  **Public Release of Code and Models:** The commitment to making all converters and the BSP parser publicly available is commendable. This will significantly lower the barrier for other researchers to work on UCCA parsing, ensuring the work is reproducible and encouraging follow-up research that can build directly upon these results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unconvincing Performance of the Proposed BSP Parser:** The central novel component of this work, the BSP parser, does not perform competitively. On the main task of predicting primary edges, it is substantially outperformed by a conversion-based baseline using an existing LSTM dependency parser (59.0% vs. 69.2% F-score). While the BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on this subtask is very low (13.3% F-score), limiting its practical utility. This performance gap undermines the argument for needing a specialized, direct parser over a simpler and more effective pipeline approach.
2.  **Overstated Motivation and Unbalanced Framing of UCCA:** The paper's introduction frames UCCA's structural properties (especially non-terminal nodes and "groundedness") as essential requirements for any broad-coverage semantic representation. This claim is not sufficiently substantiated. For instance, the concrete expressive power gained by non-terminal nodes over well-defined head-percolation rules in dependency schemes is not clearly argued. Similarly, the advantages of being "grounded" (anchored to text) over an abstract representation like AMR are asserted rather than demonstrated, ignoring the different goals of these formalisms. A more balanced positioning of UCCA as one of several valid approaches to semantic representation would strengthen the paper's framing.
3.  **Potentially Weak Baselines and Missed Related Work:** The dependency conversion baselines could be made significantly stronger. The current method simply discards remote edges, treating them as a complete loss. However, a large body of work exists on parsing graph-based formalisms by first predicting a spanning tree and then using a second-stage model to predict the remaining non-tree edges (e.g., Agić et al., 2015). Applying such an edge-reconstruction model on top of the high-performing LSTM dependency parser would constitute a much stronger baseline, which could potentially outperform BSP on both primary and remote edges.
4.  **Imprecise Terminology:** The term "grounded" is heavily overloaded in NLP and AI, often referring to grounding language in perception or action. The authors use it to mean that semantic units are directly anchored to spans of text. This choice of terminology is confusing. Using a more precise and less ambiguous term such as "anchored" or "text-based" would improve the paper's clarity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The conversion-based method using the LSTM parser outperforms your proposed BSP parser by over 10 F-score points on primary edges. Given this large performance gap, what is the core argument for continuing to develop the direct parsing approach? Have you considered integrating a more powerful classifier, such as an LSTM, into the BSP model to see if the gap can be closed?
2.  Your conversion-based baselines discard remote edges entirely. Have you considered a pipeline approach where you first predict a high-quality primary tree structure with the best parser (the LSTM model) and then apply a second-stage classifier to predict remote edges between the nodes of the predicted tree? This seems like a strong and natural baseline that is missing from the current evaluation. How do you expect BSP would compare against such a system?
