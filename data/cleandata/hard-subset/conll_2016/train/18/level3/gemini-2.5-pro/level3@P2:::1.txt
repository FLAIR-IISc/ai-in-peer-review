### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), defined as graph-based representations that accommodate multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents. Using the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a case study, the authors propose and evaluate two transition-based parsing strategies. The first strategy involves converting UCCA graphs into more restricted formalisms—constituency trees and dependency trees—and then applying state-of-the-art parsers (UPARSE, MaltParser, and a stack-LSTM parser). The second, and core, strategy is to develop a novel direct parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), which extends existing transition-based frameworks to directly generate the target BSS graphs. Experiments on UCCA-annotated corpora show that the conversion-based approach using a stack-LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest F-score on primary edges (69.2%), while the direct BSP parser is also effective and has the unique advantage of being able to predict remote edges.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **The first parser for the UCCA semantic representation.** The paper introduces a new and challenging parsing task to the community. The development of the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) provides a direct, native solution and a solid baseline for future research in this area.
2.  **A comparative study of direct vs. conversion-based parsing for complex semantic graphs.** The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis, including insightful upper-bound calculations for the conversion methods. This investigation highlights the trade-offs between leveraging existing, powerful parsing technology and designing specialized models for richer formalisms.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Novel and Important Task:** This paper is the first to tackle the problem of parsing UCCA, a cross-linguistically motivated and semantically rich representation scheme. By developing the first parsers for this formalism, the authors open up a new avenue of research and provide an essential tool for future work that aims to leverage UCCA in downstream applications. The provision of baselines is a significant contribution in itself.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The authors pursue two complementary and well-motivated parsing strategies. The first, a conversion-based approach, pragmatically assesses how well existing, highly-tuned parsers can handle this new task. The second, a direct parsing approach, addresses the limitations of the conversion methods head-on. This dual investigation provides a well-rounded and insightful perspective on the problem.
3.  **Strong Empirical Foundation:** The experiments are conducted on both in-domain and out-of-domain data, demonstrating the robustness of the proposed methods. The results are promising, with the best system reaching nearly 70% labeled F-score on primary edges, a respectable result for a new task, especially considering the inter-annotator agreement upper bound of 80-85%. The feature ablation study for the direct parser (BSP) also provides valuable insights into the contributions of different feature sets.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Relevant Baselines:** The paper's primary weakness is the omission of comparisons to established graph-based parsers. The task of parsing semantic structures with multiple parents and discontinuities is closely related to the Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) shared tasks at SemEval 2014 and 2015. Systems developed for that task, as well as general-purpose graph parsers like TurboParser (Martins et al.) or DAGParser (Ribeyre et al.), are designed to handle exactly these kinds of non-tree structures and would serve as much stronger baselines than the conversion-based approaches presented. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the true performance and novelty of the proposed BSP parser.
2.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Direct Parser:** The proposed BSP parser is presented as a novel contribution, but its transition system is largely a synthesis of existing techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing (e.g., SWAP from Maier, 2015) and dependency DAG parsing (e.g., not automatically reducing after an edge creation, similar to Sagae and Tsujii, 2008). The UCCA-specific additions, such as the `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX` transitions, feel like a minimal extension. A simpler design, such as adding a "remote" feature to the edge label within the standard `LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE` transitions, might have achieved the same result with less complexity. The parser does not appear to leverage any deeper semantic properties unique to UCCA.
3.  **Unclear Framing and Evaluation:** The motivation for BSS is based on three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuity) that are not exclusive to semantics and have been studied extensively in syntactic parsing. The paper's goals could be stated more clearly: is the main contribution a parser for UCCA, a general method for parsing semantic graphs, or an argument for UCCA as a parsable representation? Furthermore, the use of a newly defined evaluation metric makes it difficult to compare performance against related work (e.g., SDP). Adopting or including metrics from established shared tasks would have situated the work more firmly and improved its impact.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper mentions that UCCA includes "implicit units that have no correspondent in the text", which were excluded from this work. Could you provide a concrete example of such a unit and elaborate on the specific challenges they pose for the parsing models you explored?
2.  The brief comparison to MRS and DRT suggests UCCA is different, but it is unclear how it handles phenomena like quantifier scope. Could you comment on this? Additionally, an error analysis of the current parsers' outputs would be very helpful to understand which semantic phenomena are being captured correctly and which are not.
3.  Your constituency-conversion experiments use UPARSE. Have you considered using a more standard and powerful baseline for this, such as the Berkeley parser, with a scheme to enrich the non-terminal labels to encode some of the lost UCCA information?
4.  The conversion to dependency trees is quite lossy, as noted by the upper-bound analysis. Have you considered more sophisticated conversion procedures that aim to preserve more of the original graph structure, similar to the work done for the SDP shared task (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014)?
