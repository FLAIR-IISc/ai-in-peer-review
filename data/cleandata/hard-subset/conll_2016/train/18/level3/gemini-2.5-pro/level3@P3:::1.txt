### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents the first parsing approaches for UCCA, a broad-coverage semantic representation scheme that uses directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) to model phenomena like argument sharing (multiple parents), non-terminal semantic units, and discontinuities. The authors explore two main strategies: (1) converting UCCA graphs into simpler, tree-like structures (constituency and dependency) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers, and (2) developing a novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP) that can produce the full graph structure, including remote edges and discontinuities. The direct parser combines techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing. Experiments on the UCCA-annotated Wikipedia corpus show that a conversion-based approach using an LSTM-based dependency parser achieves the highest F-score (69.2%) on primary (tree) edges, while the direct BSP parser achieves a lower but respectable score (59.0%) while also being able to recover a portion of the remote edges that define the graph structure.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **The first parser for the UCCA representation.** This is a significant practical contribution that makes the UCCA formalism usable for downstream tasks and provides a crucial baseline for all future work on parsing this scheme.
2.  **A comparative study of direct vs. conversion-based parsing for a complex graph-based semantic formalism.** The paper's tripartite experimental setup provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between leveraging powerful existing parsers on lossy representations versus building a custom parser for the target structure.
3.  **The proposal of BSP, a transition system for UCCA.** While built on existing ideas, the specific combination of transitions for handling non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents in a single system is a solid piece of engineering.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a timely and important problem.** The field is moving towards richer, broad-coverage semantic representations that go beyond trees. UCCA is a promising cross-linguistically motivated formalism, and developing a parser for it is a necessary and valuable step. This work directly addresses a clear gap in the literature.
2.  **Solid and well-motivated direct parsing approach.** The core contribution, the BSP parser, is technically sound. It logically combines established mechanisms from the transition-based parsing literature—such as SWAP for discontinuities (Nivre, 2009; Maier, 2015) and delayed REDUCE for DAGs (Sagae & Tsujii, 2008)—to create a system tailored for UCCA's structural properties. The empirical results demonstrate its viability.
3.  **Thoughtful high-level experimental design.** The decision to evaluate three distinct parsing strategies (constituency conversion, dependency conversion, direct parsing) is a major strength. It provides a comprehensive view of the problem, highlighting the performance ceiling imposed by lossy conversions and showing that while existing tools can be adapted to achieve strong results on the tree-like backbone of the structure, a specialized parser is needed to recover the full graph.
4.  **High potential for impact and discussion.** As the first paper to tackle UCCA parsing, this work is poised to generate significant discussion and follow-up research. It establishes a benchmark and makes a new semantic resource computationally accessible. Despite some weaknesses, its novelty and the importance of the problem make it a very suitable paper for a venue like CoNLL.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Crucial baselines and comparisons are missing.** The paper's empirical claims are weakened by its failure to compare against or leverage highly relevant prior work.
    *   The SemEval 2014/2015 shared tasks on Broad-Coverage Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) are directly relevant, as they also deal with parsing sentences into semantic graphs with re-entrancies and discontinuities. The paper should have discussed this work more thoroughly and, ideally, used evaluation metrics from these tasks to allow for better comparison.
    *   The paper does not compare against established graph-based parsers like TurboParser (Martins et al.) or DAGParser (Ribeyre et al.), which are designed for similar structures and would serve as strong non-transition-based baselines.
    *   The phrase-based experiment using UPARSE should have been compared against other strong phrase-structure parsers, such as the one by Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015).
2.  **Claims of novelty are overstated and the motivation is unfocused.** The paper claims to be the first "semantic-oriented" parser of its kind, but the transition system does not appear to leverage any uniquely semantic information; it is a structural parser that combines known syntactic parsing techniques. Furthermore, the three guiding principles (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) are not unique to semantics and are found in various syntactic formalisms. The introduction would be stronger if it were framed more clearly as building a parser for UCCA, rather than making general claims about semantic parsing.
3.  **Methodological choices for conversion-based baselines are suboptimal.**
    *   The conversion to dependency trees is highly lossy (upper bound of 88.4 F-score) because it discards all non-terminal nodes. Stronger and more informative dependency baselines could have been created by using richer conversion schemes, such as those explored in the SDP literature (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014), which preserve more of the original graph structure.
    *   The constituency baseline is also weak. An alternative would have been to use a state-of-the-art parser like the Berkeley parser on trees where UCCA categories are encoded as rich non-terminal labels, which could provide a stronger point of comparison.
4.  **Justification for new transitions is unclear.** The parser introduces four new transitions (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE-EDGEX) specifically for remote edges. It is not clear why this is necessary. A simpler approach would be to use the existing LEFT/RIGHT-EDGEX transitions but with a distinguished label (e.g., `A-REMOTE`). This would not require extending the transition set and seems more parsimonious. The authors should justify their design choice.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper defers the parsing of UCCA's "implicit units that have no correspondent in the text". Could you provide a concrete example of such a unit and elaborate on the specific challenges they pose for a transition-based parser?
2.  An error analysis would greatly strengthen the paper. What are the most common error types for the BSP parser? For example, does it struggle more with correctly identifying remote edges, handling deep structures, or specific UCCA edge categories?
3.  Have you considered handling remote edges by simply modifying the edge label (e.g., creating `A-primary` and `A-remote` labels) instead of introducing four entirely new transitions? This seems like a simpler modification, and I would be interested to hear the rationale for choosing the more complex approach.
4.  Could you elaborate on why the standard metrics and strong baselines from the highly related SemEval SDP shared tasks were not incorporated into your evaluation? Comparing to this body of work would help situate your results within the broader context of semantic graph parsing.
