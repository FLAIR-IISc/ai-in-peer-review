### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of graph-based representations that support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The work focuses on the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of BSS. The authors propose and evaluate two main strategies for this task. The first is a conversion-based approach, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted into more standard formalisms—constituency trees and dependency trees—allowing existing state-of-the-art parsers (UPARSE, MaltParser, and a stack-LSTM parser) to be applied. The second, and the paper's core contribution, is a novel transition-based parser called Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) designed to directly produce the target graph structures. This parser extends existing transition systems for discontinuous constituency and dependency DAG parsing. Experiments are conducted on the English UCCA-annotated corpora, showing that while a strong dependency parser (stack-LSTM) achieves the highest F-score on primary edges, the direct BSP parser is competitive and uniquely capable of recovering remote edges (multiple parents).

### Main Contributions

1.  **The first parser and empirical evaluation for the UCCA formalism.** The primary contribution is the development of a dedicated parser (BSP) for the UCCA semantic representation. This is a significant practical contribution as it provides a baseline and an open-source tool for a representation scheme that, until now, lacked a parser. This enables further research and potential application of UCCA.

2.  **A systematic comparison of direct vs. conversion-based parsing for UCCA.** The paper presents a valuable comparative analysis by evaluating a direct graph parsing method against indirect methods that rely on converting the rich UCCA structure into simpler tree-based formalisms. This highlights the trade-offs between the approaches, particularly the information loss inherent in conversion versus the challenges of direct graph parsing.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a timely and challenging problem.** The field is increasingly moving towards richer, graph-structured semantic representations that go beyond trees. This paper tackles the difficult problem of parsing such structures, which can capture phenomena like argument sharing and discontinuous expressions more naturally than traditional syntactic representations. The focus on UCCA, a cross-linguistically motivated framework, makes the work relevant.

2.  **Provides a valuable resource for the community.** By developing and releasing the first parser for UCCA, the authors provide an essential tool for any researcher wishing to work with or build upon this semantic scheme. This is a strong, tangible contribution that lowers the barrier to entry for future work on UCCA.

3.  **Thorough experimental setup within its chosen scope.** The authors' evaluation is well-structured. They compare their direct parser not only against a variant of itself (BSPTree) but also against strong, publicly available parsers applied through two different conversion strategies. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments demonstrates the robustness of the proposed parser. The feature ablation study is also a welcome analysis of the BSP model's components.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art semantic graph parsers.** This is a major weakness. The paper frames its work within "Broad-coverage Semantic Dependency Parsing" but fails to compare against any systems or use evaluation metrics from the SemEval shared tasks on this topic (e.g., SemEval 2014 Task 8, SemEval 2015 Task 18). State-of-the-art graph parsers like TurboParser or other dedicated DAG parsers would have been far more appropriate and informative baselines than constituency or dependency tree parsers on converted data. The use of a custom evaluation metric, while necessary for UCCA, further isolates the work and makes it difficult to situate the reported performance relative to the broader field of semantic graph parsing.

2.  **Limited methodological novelty of the proposed parser (BSP).** The claim that this is the first "semantic-oriented" parser is not well-supported by the method itself. The transition system appears to be a skillful combination of existing techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing (Maier, 2015) and dependency DAG parsing (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008). The features and transitions do not seem to exploit any uniquely *semantic* properties of UCCA beyond its graph structure. For instance, the distinction between primary and remote edges is handled by introducing entirely new transitions (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX), whereas it could potentially be handled more simply by adding a feature or modifying the label set of standard EDGE transitions.

3.  **Weakness of the conversion-based baselines.** The conversion to dependency trees is particularly lossy, as it discards all non-terminal structure, which must be heuristically reconstructed. More sophisticated methods for converting phrase-based structures to semantic dependencies exist (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014) and would have provided a much stronger baseline. Similarly, the phrase-based experiments miss a comparison to relevant recent work like Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015). The fact that the stack-LSTM parser, even on this lossy dependency conversion, outperforms BSP on primary edges suggests that the direct parsing model has significant room for improvement.

4.  **Unclear framing and motivation.** The introduction presents three principles for BSS (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuity) as key requirements for semantic representation. However, these are general structural properties of graphs, not specific to semantics, and could apply to syntax as well. The paper's overall goal is not clearly articulated: is it to present a parser for UCCA, to propose a general architecture for parsing semantic graphs, or to demonstrate that UCCA is a parsable scheme? A clearer and broader introduction situating these structural properties within the landscape of semantic formalisms would strengthen the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you comment on why prominent graph-based semantic parsers (e.g., systems from the SemEval SDP tasks, or general-purpose graph parsers like TurboParser) were not included as baselines? Given that UCCA shares many properties with the representations used in those tasks, such a comparison seems essential for positioning your work.

2.  Your design introduces new LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX transitions to handle multiple parents. Could a similar effect be achieved by simply adding a 'remote' feature to the standard EDGE labels, thus simplifying the transition set? What is the rationale for the current design over this alternative?

3.  Section 2 mentions "implicit units that have no correspondent in the text". Could you provide an example of such a unit and elaborate on the specific challenges it poses for parsing, which led you to exclude them from this work?

4.  Could you provide a brief error analysis for the BSP parser? What are the most common categories of errors (e.g., attachment errors, label errors, errors in handling discontinuity)? This would offer valuable insight into both the current limitations of the parser and the inherent difficulties of parsing UCCA.
