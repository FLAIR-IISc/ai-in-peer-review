- Introduces Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) and the first parsers for UCCA, proposing conversion-based and direct (BSP) transition parsing strategies.
- Conversion-based approach maps UCCA to constituency/dependency form and applies UPARSE, MaltParser, and a stack-LSTM; stack-LSTM conversion achieves 69.2% labeled F on primary edges.
- The direct Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) can directly generate BSS graphs and uniquely predict remote edges.
- Main contribution: first native parser and baselines for UCCA, opening a new task for the community.
- Main contribution: comparative study of direct vs. conversion-based parsing, including upper-bound analyses and trade-offs.
- Strength: tackles a novel, semantically rich, cross-linguistic representation and provides essential baselines.
- Strength: comprehensive experimental design using two complementary parsing strategies.
- Strength: strong empirical evaluation on in-/out-of-domain data with ablation studies and results approaching inter-annotator agreement.
- Weakness: lacks comparison to established graph-based parsers (SDP systems, TurboParser, DAGParser), which would be stronger baselines.
- Weakness: limited technical novelty—BSP largely combines existing transitions (e.g., SWAP, non-reduction after edge) with minimal UCCA-specific extensions.
- Weakness: unclear framing (UCCA parser vs. general semantic graph method) and use of a new metric that impedes comparison to prior work like SDP.
- Question: provide examples of UCCA "implicit units" excluded here and explain the parsing challenges they pose.
- Question: clarify how UCCA handles phenomena like quantifier scope and include error analysis showing which semantic phenomena are/aren’t captured.
- Question: why not use a stronger constituency baseline (e.g., Berkeley parser with enriched non-terminal labels)?
- Question: consider less-lossy conversion procedures (as in SDP work, e.g., Schlüter et al. 2014) to better preserve original graph structure.
