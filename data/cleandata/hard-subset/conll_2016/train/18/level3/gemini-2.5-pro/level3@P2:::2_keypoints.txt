- Presents first parsers for UCCA, a DAG semantic scheme with non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents.  
- Proposes two parsing strategies: conversion-based mapping to trees and a direct transition-based Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP).  
- Experiments: BSP handles full graphs but a conversion-based LSTM dependency parser gets substantially higher primary-edge F-score.  
- Contribution: introduces the first UCCA parsers and strong conversion-based baselines, establishing a foundation for future work.  
- Contribution: provides an empirical analysis of trade-offs between direct graph modeling and conversion methods, highlighting remote-edge difficulty.  
- Strength: tackles a novel and timely problem by creating the first UCCA parsing systems.  
- Strength: methodologically thorough—develops a bespoke parser and systematically evaluates conversion baselines.  
- Strength: commits to public release of code and models, aiding reproducibility and follow-up research.  
- Weakness: BSP underperforms the conversion baseline on primary edges (59.0% vs. 69.2% F-score).  
- Weakness: BSP achieves very low remote-edge performance (13.3% F-score), limiting practical value.  
- Weakness: motivation and framing of UCCA are overstated; advantages of non-terminals and “groundedness” over other formalisms aren’t convincingly argued.  
- Weakness: baselines are potentially weak—conversion discards remote edges and omits stronger pipeline methods (spanning-tree + edge reconstruction).  
- Weakness: terminology is imprecise—“grounded” is overloaded; terms like “anchored” or “text-based” would be clearer.  
- Question: Given the >10 F gap, why continue direct parsing and have you tried integrating stronger classifiers (e.g., LSTMs) into BSP?  
- Question: Have you considered a pipeline that predicts a high-quality primary tree with the LSTM parser and then a second-stage model for remote edges, and how would BSP compare?
