## Review of “Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach”

This paper introduces the first semantic parsers specifically designed for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation. The authors explore both conversion-based methods leveraging existing parsers and a novel transition-based parser (BSP) directly supporting UCCA’s features – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. While the task is valuable and the initial exploration of BSP is a reasonable first step, the paper suffers from conceptual overreach and underwhelming empirical results.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the creation of a baseline parser for UCCA and the associated experimental setup. The authors successfully adapt transition-based parsing techniques to a novel semantic representation, providing a starting point for future research. A secondary contribution lies in highlighting the challenges of applying existing parsing technology to UCCA through the conversion-based approach, demonstrating the information loss inherent in forcing UCCA into more constrained formalisms.

However, the paper’s framing of UCCA as the *only* sensible graph-based semantic representation is overstated. The introduction presents a somewhat biased view, downplaying the strengths of alternatives like AMR. Furthermore, the authors’ justification for the three defining criteria of “broad-coverage” semantic representations is debatable. The necessity of non-terminal nodes for expressive power isn't convincingly argued, and the advantages of a “grounded” representation over AMR remain unclear, especially given the challenges of alignment. The repeated use of “grounded” is also problematic, as it's overloaded and could benefit from alternative terminology like “anchored” or “lexicalized.”

The empirical evaluation is a significant weakness. While the authors report reasonable performance for BSP on primary edges, the low F-scores on remote edges are concerning. The performance is not significantly better than existing parsers, and the lack of comparison to stronger baselines (e.g., dependency parsers augmented with tree approximation or edge reconstruction techniques like Agic et al., 2015) weakens the claim of BSP’s effectiveness. The out-of-domain results, while presented, don't substantially alter this assessment.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Task:** Addressing parsing for UCCA is a valuable contribution, opening up new avenues for semantic analysis.
*   **Baseline Implementation:** The BSP parser provides a functional starting point for future research on UCCA parsing.
*   **Exploration of Conversion Methods:** The investigation of converting UCCA to other formalisms highlights the challenges and trade-offs involved.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Biased Framing:** The paper overstates the uniqueness and superiority of UCCA compared to other semantic representations.
*   **Questionable Criteria:** The justification for the three defining criteria of “broad-coverage” semantic representations is not fully convincing.
*   **Unimpressive Evaluation:** The performance of BSP, particularly on remote edges, is not compelling, and the lack of strong baselines limits the conclusions.
*   **Overloaded Terminology:** The term "grounded" is used imprecisely and could benefit from clarification.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific motivations for choosing the three criteria (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity) as defining characteristics of “broad-coverage” semantic representations?
2.  How would the performance of BSP change if it were trained with a beam search instead of greedy decoding?
3.  Have you experimented with incorporating techniques for tree approximation or edge reconstruction into the dependency-tree baselines to strengthen the comparison?
4.  Could you provide a more precise definition of “grounded” as used in this paper, or consider alternative terminology to avoid ambiguity?
