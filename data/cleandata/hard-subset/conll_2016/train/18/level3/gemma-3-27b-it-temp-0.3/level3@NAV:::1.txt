## Review of “Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of parsing UCCA representations, arguing for the need for graph-based semantic parsing capable of handling multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity. The authors present two main approaches: conversion to existing parsable formalisms (dependency and constituency trees) and a novel transition-based graph parser (BSP). While the motivation and exploration of different parsing strategies are interesting, the paper suffers from methodological weaknesses and a lack of comprehensive empirical comparison.

The primary contribution, in my view, is the exploration of transition-based parsing for UCCA, and the identification of the challenges inherent in adapting existing parsing techniques to this richer semantic representation. The conversion-based approach serves as a useful baseline, demonstrating the information loss involved in flattening the UCCA structure. However, the core contribution – BSP – feels underdeveloped. The transition set, while extending existing models, doesn’t demonstrably exploit the unique characteristics of UCCA beyond adding transitions for remote edges. 

Several weaknesses hinder a strong recommendation. First, the claim of being the “first semantic-oriented parser” is overstated. The transition set lacks specific mechanisms for handling UCCA-specific phenomena, and the remote edge handling could be more elegantly addressed through edge labeling. Second, the experimental evaluation is insufficient. The lack of comparison to prior graph parsing work (SemEval 2014-2015, DAG/Turbo parsers) is a significant oversight. The phrase-based experiments should be compared to Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015), and the reference to uparse is missing. Furthermore, the evaluation metrics are limited; incorporating metrics from related SDP shared tasks would strengthen the analysis. Finally, the three principles (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuity) are generic to graph structures and not uniquely tied to semantics, weakening the paper’s core argument.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Parsing rich semantic representations like UCCA is crucial for advancing semantic understanding.
*   **Explores multiple approaches:** The paper investigates both conversion-based and direct parsing methods.
*   **Provides a baseline:** The conversion-based approach offers a useful benchmark for evaluating the direct parser.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Weak claim of novelty:** The proposed parser doesn't fully leverage UCCA-specific features.
*   **Insufficient baselines:** Missing comparisons to relevant prior work in graph parsing.
*   **Limited evaluation:** Lack of standard SDP metrics and a comprehensive empirical analysis.
*   **Generic principles:** The stated principles are not unique to semantic parsing.
*   **Unclear goals:** The paper lacks a clear statement of whether it aims to be a UCCA parser, a general semantic parser, or a demonstration of a parsable semantic scheme.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide examples of UCCA implicit units that lack textual correspondents, and explain how these are handled (or not handled) by your parser?
2.  UCCA appears not to model quantifier scope (unlike MRS/DRT). Can you provide an error analysis demonstrating the parser's behavior with sentences involving quantifier scope, and discuss the informativeness and parsability of such structures?
3.  Could remote edge handling be achieved more effectively through edge labeling rather than introducing new transitions?
4.  What is the motivation for the MINUPDATE and IMPORTANCE parameters used during training, and how were these values determined?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Introduce the concept of “grounded semantic” earlier in the paper.
*   Consider using constituent trees with rich node labels and the Berkeley parser as an alternative baseline.
*   Converting to surface dependency trees loses information. Recommend richer SDP-style conversions (Schlüter et al., 2014).
*   The paper would benefit from a clearer, broader introduction and a more focused statement of its goals.



Despite its shortcomings, the paper raises interesting questions and explores a relevant problem. I believe it is worthy of discussion, perhaps at a venue like CoNLL, but requires significant revisions to address the concerns outlined above.
