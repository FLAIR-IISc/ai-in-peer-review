## Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

This paper introduces the first semantic parsers for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation with properties—multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity—not fully supported by existing frameworks like AMR or standard dependency parsing. The authors explore both conversion-based parsing using existing tools and a novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed specifically for UCCA. 

The most significant contribution of this work isn’t necessarily the performance of BSP itself, but rather the **initial exploration and establishment of a benchmark for parsing UCCA**. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of applying transition-based parsing to this relatively unexplored representation, providing a foundation for future research. A second key contribution is the **creation and public release of code and data** for UCCA parsing, which will undoubtedly benefit the research community. Finally, the **analysis of the challenges inherent in UCCA parsing**—particularly handling discontinuities and non-terminal nodes—is valuable.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The authors’ framing of UCCA as *the* sensible graph-based SR is overstated; other representations have merits and warrant more balanced discussion. Furthermore, the theoretical justification for UCCA’s design choices—specifically the necessity of non-terminal nodes and the advantages of being “grounded”—is not entirely convincing. The term “grounded” is also overloaded, causing confusion, and could be better phrased as “anchored” or “lexicalized.”

The experimental results are somewhat underwhelming. While BSP achieves reasonable performance, it is only marginally better than simpler conversion-based approaches using MaltParser. Remote edge F-scores are particularly low, indicating a struggle with the core feature differentiating UCCA from tree-based representations. The evaluation also doesn't adequately address the impact of the conversion process on the performance of baseline methods. Prior work on tree approximations and edge reconstruction (e.g., Agic et al., 2015) isn't discussed and could potentially significantly improve baseline performance, diminishing the reported gains of BSP.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Task:** Addressing semantic parsing for UCCA is a valuable first step.
*   **Resource Creation:** Providing code and data is a significant contribution.
*   **Initial Benchmark:** Establishes a baseline for future UCCA parsing research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Biased Introduction:** Overstates UCCA’s advantages and ignores alternative SRs.
*   **Unconvincing Justification:** Arguments for UCCA’s design choices are weak.
*   **Modest Performance:** BSP's performance is not substantially better than baselines.
*   **Incomplete Evaluation:** Lack of comparison to more advanced baseline methods.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific linguistic phenomena that *require* non-terminal nodes in UCCA, beyond those already illustrated?
2.  What are the practical advantages of the “grounded” representation in UCCA compared to the more abstract AMR, specifically in downstream applications?
3.  How would the results change if you incorporated techniques from Agic et al. (2015) to improve the performance of the baseline dependency parsers?
4.  Why was beam search not used during inference for BSP, given that it is standard practice in many transition-based parsing systems?
