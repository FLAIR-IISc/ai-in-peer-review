- Introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for UCCA supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity.
- Explores conversion-based parsing strategies by converting UCCA to constituency/dependency structures.
- Reports experimental results on English UCCA reaching nearly 70% labeled F-score with the best parser.
- Correctly identifies a gap in existing parsers for handling complex semantic structures.
- Transition-based approach is reasonable given prior success in syntactic dependency parsing.
- The UCCA corpus is a valuable native resource for broad-coverage semantic representation.
- Evaluates both conversion-based and direct parsing methods, offering a comprehensive investigation.
- Code and data will be made available, promoting reproducibility.
- Claim of presenting “techniques for BSS parsing” is overstated since the transition set largely reuses existing types.
- Dedicated transitions for remote edges appear overly complex; simpler label-differentiation might suffice.
- Parser design shows limited exploitation of UCCA-specific features.
- Experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to relevant baselines from SDP shared tasks and established DAG/Turbo parsers (e.g., Ribeyre’s DAGParser, TurboParser).
- Phrase-based experiments omit the important Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015) baseline.
- Conversion schemes to trees are somewhat arbitrary and under-justified; more sophisticated SDP-style conversions should be considered.
- Evaluation focuses on primary edges and neglects thorough assessment of remote edge prediction.
- The three advocated properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuity) are presented ad hoc and need clearer semantic motivation and theoretical grounding.
- It is unclear whether the goal is a UCCA-specific parser, a general semantic parser, or a demonstration of scheme parsability.
- Questions for authors: provide examples of implicit units and parsing strategies; analyze quantifier scope errors and UCCA limitations; justify transition set design choices.
- Additional suggestions: introduce “grounded semantic” earlier, consider richer node labels or Berkeley parser baselines, and use SDP-style conversion/evaluation.
- Overall verdict: interesting and potentially valuable work worth discussion at CoNLL, but addressing the methodological, comparative, and conceptual weaknesses would strengthen the paper.
