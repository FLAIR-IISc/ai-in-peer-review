This paper introduces the problem of broad-coverage semantic parsing (BSS) using the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) framework and presents two approaches: conversion-based parsing leveraging existing parsers, and a novel transition-based parser (BSP). The core contribution lies in being the first to address parsing UCCA structures, which uniquely support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – properties argued to be crucial for broad semantic coverage. A secondary contribution is the exploration of conversion techniques to leverage established parsing technology.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Task:** Addressing parsing within the UCCA framework is a genuinely novel endeavor. UCCA, with its specific structural properties, hasn’t been tackled directly before, and the paper successfully establishes a baseline for future work in this area. This fills a gap in the semantic parsing landscape.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Approaches:** The paper thoughtfully explores two distinct parsing strategies – conversion-based and direct parsing – providing a comparative analysis. This demonstrates a well-rounded investigation and highlights the challenges inherent in the task. The conversion-based approach is a sensible starting point and provides a useful point of comparison for the more complex direct parser.
3. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivations behind broad-coverage semantic parsing, and the specific structural properties UCCA offers that other frameworks lack. This contextualizes the work and explains its potential impact. The detailed examples illustrating the three key properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) are particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated Claims Regarding UCCA’s Uniqueness:** The paper consistently positions UCCA as uniquely suited for broad-coverage semantic representation and implicitly criticizes alternatives like AMR. However, the arguments supporting this stance are weak. While UCCA is grounded, the benefits of this over AMR’s more abstract representation aren’t convincingly demonstrated. The assertion that non-terminal nodes definitively add expressive power requires stronger justification; they could be argued to simply represent a different level of abstraction. This framing diminishes the value of related work.
2. **Questionable Criteria for Broad Coverage:** The three criteria presented as essential for broad coverage (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuities) are debatable. The paper does not sufficiently explain *why* these properties are fundamentally necessary beyond simply stating that they appear in natural language phenomena. It’s unclear if these features provide a substantial advantage over existing approaches that handle semantic complexity differently.
3. **Unimpressive Performance of BSP:** The experimental results are not particularly strong. MaltParser, a relatively standard dependency parser, outperforms BSP on primary edge F-score. Furthermore, the F-score on remote edges, a key feature of UCCA, is low, indicating that the parser struggles to capture this crucial aspect of the representation. This raises concerns about the practical utility of the proposed parser. The comparison with the strong LSTM parser further highlights this issue.
4. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While conversion-based parsing is explored, the baselines used within that approach could be strengthened. The paper mentions prior work on tree approximations and edge reconstruction (e.g., Agic et al., 2015), but doesn’t incorporate these techniques to maximize the performance of the dependency-tree based baselines. This weakens the contrast between the conversion-based and direct approaches.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for why the three structural properties you highlight (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuities) are *necessary* for broad-coverage semantic representation, rather than merely *desirable*?
2. Could you elaborate on the advantages of a “grounded” representation like UCCA compared to more abstract frameworks like AMR, specifically in the context of downstream applications?
3. What specific modifications to the BSP architecture or training procedure might address the performance gap observed compared to the MaltParser and LSTM parser?
4. Would the relative performance of BSP and the conversion-based approaches change if more advanced techniques for tree approximation and edge reconstruction were employed in the conversion baselines?



**Additional Comments:**

The term "grounded" is used repeatedly, which can be confusing given its established meaning in other areas of NLP. Consider using alternative terms like “anchored” or “lexicalized” for clarity. The paper could also benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of UCCA and the potential trade-offs between different semantic representation formalisms.
