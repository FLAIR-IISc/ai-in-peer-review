This paper proposes techniques for parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme which uniquely supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors explore two strategies: converting UCCA structures to more restrictive formalisms (dependency and constituency trees) and training existing parsers, and constructing a novel transition-based parser (BSP) specifically for BSS. The primary contribution is an initial exploration of parsing UCCA structures, a relatively unexplored area given its support for the desired structural properties. A secondary contribution lies in the experimental setup and baseline results provided, offering a starting point for future work in this area. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a gap in existing parsing technology:** The paper tackles a genuine problem: the lack of parsers capable of handling the structural complexities required for truly broad-coverage semantic representation, as embodied by UCCA.  Existing parsers typically fall short in supporting features like multiple parents and non-terminal nodes.  The authors correctly identify this limitation and attempt to address it.
2. **Comprehensive approach:** The authors explore two distinct parsing strategies – conversion-based and direct parsing – providing a more nuanced evaluation of the challenges involved.  The conversion-based approach serves as a valuable baseline and demonstrates the feasibility (and limitations) of leveraging existing tools.
3. **Clear presentation of UCCA:**  The paper provides a well-structured explanation of the UCCA annotation scheme, its key properties, and its advantages over other semantic representation formalisms like AMR. The illustrative figures (Figure 1) are particularly helpful in understanding the structural properties being addressed.
4. **Implementation and Public Availability:**  The commitment to making the converters and parsers publicly available is a significant positive aspect, fostering reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated novelty and importance of BSS criteria:** The introduction strongly positions UCCA and its structural properties as essential for semantic parsing. However, the arguments for the necessity of each criterion (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuity) are not fully convincing.  The benefits of non-terminal nodes, in particular, seem debatable – they may not add significant expressive power and can introduce complexity. The advantages of a "grounded" representation over non-grounded representations like AMR are not clearly articulated and require stronger justification.
2. **Semantic Overload and Terminology:** The repeated use of the term “grounded” is problematic. It’s a loaded term with various meanings, and its application in this context is unclear. The authors should consider alternatives like "anchored" or "lexicalized" and carefully rephrase their arguments to avoid ambiguity. This ambiguity weakens the presented justification for UCCA.
3. **Evaluation results are not compelling:** The experimental results, while providing a baseline, are somewhat underwhelming.  The MaltParser, utilizing a simpler conversion-based approach, outperforms BSP on primary edge F-score. The remote edge F-scores are also relatively low, indicating a significant challenge in capturing the discontinuous nature of BSS. While acknowledging that more complex classifiers could improve results, the current performance doesn’t strongly support the claim of significant advancement.
4. **Limited comparison to related work:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly work on approximation techniques for converting between different syntactic/semantic representations.  For example, techniques for reconstructing edges after approximating to a dependency tree (like Agic et al., 2015) could potentially boost the performance of the conversion-based baselines and diminish the apparent advantage of the direct BSP parser.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific applications that would particularly benefit from the structural properties offered by UCCA and BSS, beyond the general goal of broad-coverage semantic representation? Providing concrete use cases would strengthen the motivation for this work.
2.  Given that the MaltParser outperforms BSP on primary edge F-score, what is your analysis of the reasons for this result? Is it related to the features used, the training algorithm, or other factors?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the MINUPDATE parameter during training? Have you experimented with different values to optimize performance?
4.  You mention that the conversion introduces lossy information. Could you quantify this information loss (e.g., using a metric comparing the gold UCCA structure to the converted structure and back again)?

**Additional Comments:**

- The discussion of the feature templates (Figure 3) is somewhat dense and could benefit from further explanation of the rationale behind each feature.
- Consider including an error analysis of the parser’s predictions to identify common failure cases and potential areas for improvement.
- It would be helpful to compare the computational complexity of BSP with that of the conversion-based approaches.
