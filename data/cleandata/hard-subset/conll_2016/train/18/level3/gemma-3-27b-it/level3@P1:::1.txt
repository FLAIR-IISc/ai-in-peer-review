The paper introduces a transition-based parsing approach for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a rich semantic representation scheme. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of existing parsers in capturing semantic structures requiring multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity – properties deemed essential for broad-coverage semantic representation. The core contribution lies in the development of a novel transition-based broad-coverage parser (BSP) designed to directly support these properties, alongside an exploration of conversion-based parsing strategies leveraging existing parsers on converted UCCA structures. The paper presents experimental results on the English UCCA corpus, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score with their highest-performing parser.

The authors correctly identify a gap in existing parsing technology regarding the representation of complex semantic structures. The proposal to address this gap with transition-based methods is reasonable, given their success in syntactic dependency parsing. The creation of the UCCA corpus, which natively supports the desired structural properties, is a valuable resource for the research community. The investigation of both conversion-based approaches and a direct parsing method offers a comprehensive exploration of potential solutions.  The availability of code and data upon publication is a significant strength, promoting reproducibility and further research.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall impact of the work. The claim of presenting "techniques for BSS parsing" as a primary contribution feels overstated. While the BSP parser is novel, the transition set largely reuses existing transition types. The introduction of dedicated transitions for remote edges seems unnecessarily complex; a more elegant solution could be achieved through differentiation of edge labels within existing transitions. This suggests a lack of deep exploitation of UCCA-specific features in the parser design. 

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient comparison to relevant baselines. The paper does not engage with existing graph-based parsing work from SemEval shared tasks (e.g., SDP tasks) which address similar challenges.  Established DAG/Turbo parsers like Ribeyre’s DAGParser or André Martins’ TurboParser are also absent from the comparison.  The phrase-based experiments lack a crucial reference to the work of Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015). The use of somewhat arbitrary conversion schemes (to constituency and dependency trees) without a strong justification and the absence of more nuanced evaluation metrics (such as those used in SDP shared tasks) further weakens the empirical analysis. The evaluation focuses heavily on primary edges, neglecting a thorough assessment of the remote edge prediction, which is crucial for capturing the full complexity of UCCA structures.

A fundamental conceptual concern lies in the somewhat ad-hoc nature of the three properties advocated for “broad-coverage semantic structures” (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity). These properties are not inherently semantic; they are structural features that can be found in various formalisms. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the motivations behind these properties and a more nuanced discussion of their theoretical underpinnings. A broader introduction contextualizing the work within the broader landscape of semantic representation would strengthen the paper. It’s unclear if the goal is a UCCA parser, a general semantic parser, or a demonstration of the parsability of a specific semantic scheme.

Specifically, I have the following questions for the authors:

1. Could you provide concrete examples of "implicit units" in UCCA that do not have direct textual correspondents? What parsing strategies are envisioned for these units in future work?
2. The paper does not explicitly address quantifier scope, a phenomenon often handled in formal semantics (e.g., MRS, DRT).  Could you provide an error analysis demonstrating instances where the parser struggles with quantifier scope, and discuss the limitations of UCCA in representing such phenomena?
3. The transition set seems somewhat conservative in its adaptation to UCCA. Could you elaborate on the design choices behind the transition set, and justify why more UCCA-specific transitions were not considered?



Additional Comments:

*   The term "grounded semantic" should be introduced earlier in the paper to provide context for the discussion of UCCA.
*   Exploring constituent trees with richer node labels, potentially leveraging the Berkeley parser, could serve as a valuable alternative baseline.
*   The conversion to surface dependency trees inevitably leads to information loss. Considering more sophisticated conversion schemes, such as those used in SDP (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014), may yield more informative results.



Despite these criticisms, the work presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. The paper is worth discussing at a venue like CoNLL, and addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen its impact.
