- Main contribution is applying transition-based parsing to UCCA, but the transition set does not exploit UCCA-specific properties and the novelty claim is overstated.  
- Methodological limitations: proposed transitions largely mirror dependency parsing rather than introducing mechanisms tailored to UCCA.  
- Experimental setup is weak: reliance on conversion-based baselines obscures the benefits of direct graph parsing.  
- Missing comparisons to prior graph-parsing work (e.g., SemEval SDP tasks, DAGParser, TurboParser) is a significant omission.  
- Converting UCCA to dependency trees loses substantial structural information and is insufficiently justified.  
- Evaluation metrics are not aligned with the broader SDP research community, hindering meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art.  
- Strength: addresses a relevant problem by building a UCCA parser, which is important for the representation’s usability.  
- Strength: provides a clear and helpful introduction to the UCCA annotation scheme.  
- Strength: offers an initial exploration of transition-based methods for graph-based semantic parsing.  
- Weakness: weak claim of novelty due to lack of UCCA-tailored transition types.  
- Weakness: inadequate baseline comparisons to relevant graph parsing systems.  
- Weakness: limited evaluation and lack of standard SDP metrics.  
- Weakness: questionable conversion strategies that discard important structure.  
- Weakness: claimed general principles (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuity) are not shown to be specific to semantics.  
- Question: justify omission of prior graph-parsing baselines in experiments.  
- Question: could remote edges be handled via edge labeling instead of separate transitions, and why wasn’t this explored?  
- Question: provide error analysis on handling complex quantifier scope and limitations of the parser.  
- Question: give concrete examples of how implicit UCCA units (without textual correspondents) are handled by the parser.  
- Additional comment: clarify the paper’s goals (UCCA parser vs general semantic parser vs demonstration of scheme) and consider introducing “grounded semantic” earlier.  
- Additional suggestion: explore constituent-tree-based baselines (e.g., Berkeley parser with rich node labels).
