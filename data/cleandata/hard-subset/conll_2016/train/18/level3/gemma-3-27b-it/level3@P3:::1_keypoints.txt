- Contribution: Introduces BSP, the first direct transition-based parser for UCCA handling multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities.
- Contribution: Provides an empirical comparison of conversion-based methods and a direct parser for UCCA parsing on in-/out-of-domain corpora.
- Contribution: Argues for semantic structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuity) beyond typical syntactic representations.

- Strength: Tackles a genuinely challenging problem of broad-coverage semantic parsing with a clear motivation.
- Strength: Well-designed experimental setup including conversion baselines and domain generalization tests.
- Strength: Clear, organized description of BSP’s transitions and feature templates, linked to prior transition-based methods.
- Strength: Plans to release converters and parsers to support reproducibility.
- Strength: Results point to meaningful directions for future work (remote edges, LSTM classifiers) and potential for thorough error analysis.

- Weakness: Claim of being the “first” semantic-oriented parser is overstated; transition set is largely similar to existing parsing transitions.
- Weakness: Remote-edge handling via new transitions is inelegant; using edge-label differences might be simpler and more standard.
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to prior graph-parsing work (SemEval 2014–2015) and DAG/Turbo parsers (e.g., Ribeyre’s DAGParser, Martins’ TurboParser).
- Weakness: Phrase-based experiments lack comparison to Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015); UPARSE capabilities (e.g., discontinuity) are not detailed.
- Weakness: Evaluation metrics are insufficient—omits common SDP/shared-task metrics, hindering direct comparison to prior work.
- Weakness: The three proposed principles are presented as generic/ad-hoc without situating them in a broader semantic-representation framework or clarifying the paper’s scope (UCCA parser vs general semantic parser).
- Weakness: UCCA-specific issues need clarification—treatment of implicit units without textual correspondents and whether UCCA models quantifier scope; lacking related error analysis.

- Question: Provide examples of UCCA implicit units with no textual correspondents and explain their handling in parsing.
- Question: Does UCCA model quantifier scope; if not, present error analysis for quantifier-related parsing failures and possible remedies.
- Question: How would BSP perform if remote edges were handled via edge-label differences instead of new transitions?
- Question: Why prioritize SWAP transitions during training, and how sensitive is performance to the IMPORTANCE value?
- Question: Explain the rationale for excluding DAGParser and TurboParser from baseline comparisons.

- Additional comment: Introduce the term “grounded semantic” earlier to establish context.
- Additional comment: Consider using constituent trees with rich node labels and the Berkeley parser as an alternative baseline.
- Additional comment: Conversion to surface dependency trees loses information—suggest richer SDP-style conversions (Schlüter et al., 2014).
- Additional comment: References are extensive and well-organized.

- Overall: Paper makes a valuable contribution and merits discussion at CoNLL, but addressing novelty claims, baseline/metric omissions, and UCCA-specific clarifications would substantially strengthen it.
