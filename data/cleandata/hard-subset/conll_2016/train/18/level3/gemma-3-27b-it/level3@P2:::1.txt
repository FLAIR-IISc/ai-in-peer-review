This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a richly-layered semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents – properties the authors argue are crucial for broad-coverage semantic representation. The authors present two main strategies: conversion to existing parsing formalisms (constituency and dependency trees) and a novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP). The primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of transition-based parsing as a viable approach to directly modeling UCCA’s graph structure, a relatively unexplored area. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation on UCCA-annotated corpora, providing a baseline for future work in this area. Finally, the paper offers a clear articulation of the structural properties needed for broad-coverage semantics, although these are arguably not unique to the UCCA framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Transition-Based Parsing:** The adaptation of transition-based parsing techniques to directly model UCCA graphs is a significant step. Transition-based methods have proven effective in various parsing tasks, and their application to the more complex task of broad-coverage semantic parsing is a promising direction. The work provides a concrete implementation (BSP) and demonstrates its feasibility.
2. **Empirical Evaluation on UCCA:** The paper provides a valuable empirical evaluation on the UCCA corpus, which, as the authors rightly point out, is currently the only widely available resource supporting all three desired structural properties. This provides a crucial baseline for future research and allows for a direct assessment of the proposed methods. The out-of-domain experiment also adds to the robustness evaluation.
3. **Clear Articulation of Structural Requirements:** The authors clearly articulate the structural properties needed for broad-coverage semantic representation: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity. While not entirely novel, the framing of these requirements within the context of UCCA and the motivation behind them is well-presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Transition System:** While applying transition-based parsing to UCCA is valuable, the core transition system lacks substantial novelty. The addition of transitions specifically designed to exploit UCCA's unique features (e.g., remote edges) feels somewhat ad-hoc. Exploring alternative methods for handling remote edges, such as utilizing different edge labelings within existing transition types, could have been investigated. The argument of being the “first” semantic parser doesn’t hold strongly in light of existing DAG parsers.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison against relevant baselines. Notably, previous work from SemEval shared tasks on broad-coverage semantic dependency parsing (2014-2015) is not mentioned or used as a comparison point. Furthermore, existing graph/DAG parsers (e.g., Ribeyre’s DAGParser, André Martins’ TurboParser) are not considered. The lack of these comparisons makes it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed methods.
3. **Evaluation Metrics and Conversions:** The proposed evaluation metrics, while reasonable, lack established grounding within the broader semantic parsing community. Furthermore, the conversion to dependency trees loses significant information. Exploring more sophisticated conversion strategies, such as those used in SDP (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014), would have provided a more informative comparison. The dependence on conversion procedures to evaluate the methods adds noise and obscures the true performance of each approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide specific examples of “implicit units” in UCCA that lack textual correspondents, and discuss how your parser addresses their representation (or avoids it)?
2. The paper mentions that UCCA does not model quantifier scope, unlike MRS/DRT. Could you provide an error analysis illustrating instances where this limitation affects parsing accuracy and discuss the potential for extending UCCA (or the parser) to address this issue?
3. The motivation for requiring multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity feels somewhat ad-hoc. Could you provide a broader justification for these principles and elaborate on how they relate to broader goals of semantic parsing beyond just modeling UCCA? A clearer articulation of the overall goal (UCCA parser vs. general semantic parser vs. parsable semantic scheme) would be beneficial.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The introduction would benefit from an earlier mention of “grounded semantic” representation to contextualize the work.
*   Consider citing the Berkeley parser and exploring its use with rich node labels as an alternative baseline for phrase-based experiments, as suggested in related literature (Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins, 2015).
*   The reference to UPARSE is missing, which is central to the constituency-based experiment.
*   Please provide links to the repos of DAGParser and TurboParser as suggested.



Despite these weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing and is worthy of discussion at a venue like CoNLL. Addressing the raised concerns, particularly regarding baseline comparison and the novelty of the transition system, would significantly strengthen the paper.
