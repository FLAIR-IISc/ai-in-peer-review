- Novelty: First work to tackle UCCA parsing, establishing a baseline for this underexplored semantic framework.
- Approach comparison: Thoughtful exploration of conversion-based parsing and a direct transition-based parser (BSP), enabling comparative analysis.
- Clear motivation: Well-articulated problem definition with helpful examples illustrating UCCA’s properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities).
- Overstated uniqueness: Claims that UCCA is uniquely suited for broad coverage are weakly justified and downplay related formalisms like AMR.
- Questionable criteria: The paper presents multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities as necessary for broad coverage without strong evidence they are essential.
- BSP performance issues: BSP is outperformed by MaltParser on primary edge F-score and shows low F-score on remote edges, undermining practical utility.
- Limited baselines: Conversion-based baselines omit stronger tree-approximation and edge-reconstruction techniques that could improve comparisons.
- Question — necessity: Request for stronger justification that the three highlighted structural properties are necessary rather than merely desirable.
- Question — grounding benefits: Ask for concrete advantages of a “grounded”/anchored UCCA representation over abstract frameworks like AMR for downstream tasks.
- Question — BSP improvements: Ask what architectural or training modifications could close the performance gap with MaltParser and LSTM parsers.
- Question — baseline impact: Would using advanced tree approximation and edge reconstruction change the relative performance of BSP versus conversion approaches?
- Terminology and scope: Recommend avoiding the ambiguous term “grounded” (use “anchored”/“lexicalized”) and adding a nuanced discussion of UCCA’s limitations and trade-offs.
