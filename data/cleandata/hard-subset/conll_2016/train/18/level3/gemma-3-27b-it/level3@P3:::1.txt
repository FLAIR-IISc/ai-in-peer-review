This paper proposes a transition-based graph parser for UCCA’s rich semantico-cognitive representations, arguing that broad-coverage semantic representation requires multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity—properties not fully supported by existing parsers. The authors present two approaches: conversion to existing formalisms (dependency and constituency trees) and a novel direct transition-based parser (BSP). They experiment with UCCA-annotated corpora, demonstrating the feasibility of their approach.

**Contributions:**

1. **First Transition-Based BSS Parser:** The core contribution is the development of BSP, a transition-based parser specifically designed to handle the complexities of UCCA’s broad-coverage semantic structures, including multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities.
2. **Empirical Exploration of UCCA Parsing:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of parsing UCCA structures using both conversion-based methods and a direct parser, offering valuable insights into the challenges and possibilities of this task.
3. **Identification of Key Structural Properties for Semantics:** The authors clearly articulate and motivate the need for structural properties beyond those commonly used in syntactic parsing for broad-coverage semantic representation, framing the motivation for UCCA and its parsing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Challenging Problem:** The paper tackles a genuinely difficult problem – parsing richly structured semantic representations like UCCA. The limitations of existing parsers regarding multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuity are well-articulated, justifying the need for new approaches.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed, including conversion-based baselines and a direct parsing approach, with both in-domain and out-of-domain testing. This provides a robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
3. **Clear Presentation of BSP:** The description of the BSP parser, including the transition set and feature templates, is clear and well-organized. The connection to existing transition-based parsing techniques is also effectively established.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:** The commitment to releasing the converters and parsers is a valuable contribution to the research community, enabling reproducibility and further research.
5. **Thorough Error Analysis (Potential):** The results suggest potential areas for future work, particularly regarding remote edge prediction and leveraging LSTM-based classifiers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Weak Claim of Novelty:** The claim of being the “first” semantic-oriented parser is overstated. While the parser is the first *direct* parser for UCCA, it doesn't fully exploit the UCCA-specific phenomena in its transition set. The transition set largely resembles existing dependency/constituency parsing transitions and feels somewhat generic.
2. **Suboptimal Remote Edge Handling:** Handling remote edges with entirely new transitions feels unnecessarily complex. Using edge label differences to distinguish primary/remote edges, as is common in other graph parsing approaches, could have been a more elegant solution.
3. **Missing Baselines:** The absence of comparisons to prior graph-parsing work from SemEval (2014-2015) and existing DAG/Turbo parsers (e.g., Ribeyre’s DAGParser, André Martins’ TurboParser) is a significant oversight. These would provide a more complete picture of the state-of-the-art.
4. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison:** The phrase-based experiments should be compared to Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015). The reference to UPARSE lacks context – detailing its capabilities (discontinuous constituents) is crucial.
5. **Insufficient Evaluation Metrics:** While the defined precision/recall/F-score are reasonable starting points, the evaluation lacks metrics commonly used in related SDP shared tasks. This hinders a direct comparison and assessment of progress in the field.  The lack of comparable baselines and metrics significantly weakens the empirical evaluation.
6. **Generic Principles:** The three proposed principles (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuity) are too generic and ad-hoc.  The paper lacks a broader introduction contextualizing these principles within a larger framework of semantic representation, and a clearer articulation of its goals. Is it a UCCA parser, a general semantic parser, or a proposal for a parsable semantic scheme?
7. **UCCA-Specific Issues:** Clarification is needed for UCCA implicit units lacking textual correspondents—examples would be helpful. The paper doesn’t address whether UCCA effectively models quantifier scope (unlike MRS/DRT), and an error analysis regarding this aspect would be informative.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide examples of UCCA implicit units that have no textual correspondents, and explain how these are handled during parsing?
2. Does UCCA explicitly model quantifier scope? If not, could you present an error analysis demonstrating cases where the parser struggles with quantifying structures, and discuss potential solutions?
3. How would the performance of BSP be affected by using edge label differences rather than new transitions to handle remote edges?
4. What is the rationale for prioritizing SWAP transitions during training?  How sensitive is the performance to changes in the IMPORTANCE value?
5. Could you elaborate on the reasons for not including DAGParser and TurboParser in the baseline comparisons?



**Additional Comments:**

* Introduce the term “grounded semantic” earlier in the paper to establish the context.
* Consider using constituent trees with rich node labels and the Berkeley parser as an alternative baseline.
* The conversion to surface dependency trees loses information. Suggesting richer SDP-style conversions (Schlüter et al., 2014) would be beneficial.
* The references section is extensive and well-organized.



Despite these weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing and is worthy of discussion at a venue like CoNLL. The research addresses a significant challenge, and the experimental results, while not groundbreaking, provide a solid foundation for future work. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above will strengthen the paper considerably.
