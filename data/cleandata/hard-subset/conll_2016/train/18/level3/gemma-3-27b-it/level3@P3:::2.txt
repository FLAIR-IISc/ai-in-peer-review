This paper introduces the first semantic parsers for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation. The authors explore both conversion-based approaches leveraging existing parsers and a novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed specifically for UCCA’s characteristics – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. While the task addressed is valuable and the initial implementation a reasonable starting point, the paper’s arguments are overstated, and the experimental results are not particularly compelling.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Initial UCCA Parsing Implementation:** The most significant contribution is the development and evaluation of BSP, a transition-based parser specifically designed to handle UCCA’s structure. This provides a baseline for future work in UCCA parsing and facilitates further research in this area.
2.  **Exploration of Conversion-Based Parsing:** The systematic investigation of converting UCCA structures into constituency and dependency trees, and then parsing those representations with established tools, offers a useful diagnostic. It demonstrates the information loss inherent in these conversions and highlights the challenges of adapting existing parsers.
3.  **Dataset & Task Introduction:**  The paper brings attention to UCCA and establishes it as a viable, though challenging, task for semantic parsing.  This is valuable for the community, providing a new benchmark and potentially driving further innovation in broad-coverage semantic representations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addressing a Novel & Important Task:** The paper tackles a critical gap in semantic parsing – the lack of robust parsers for graph-based representations like UCCA, which are designed to capture more complex semantic phenomena than traditional dependency or constituency trees. Broad-coverage semantic parsing is an important and challenging area.
2.  **Sound Methodology:** The use of transition-based parsing, given its recent successes in dependency and constituency parsing, is a reasonable and well-justified approach. The experimental setup is clear, and the inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation provides a more comprehensive assessment of the parser’s performance.
3.  **Detailed Implementation Description:** The paper provides a thorough description of the BSP parser, including the transition set and feature engineering.  This level of detail allows for reproducibility and facilitates further development by other researchers.
4.  **Clear Presentation of Conversions:** The explanations of the conversion procedures from UCCA to constituency and dependency trees are clear and well-defined. Showing the upper bounds of these conversions effectively highlights the difficulties of adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Overstated Claims & Biased Introduction:** The introduction presents UCCA as the *only* sensible graph-based semantic representation to study, which is a demonstrably false claim. AMR, SDP, and other graph-based formalisms exist and have substantial research communities. A more balanced discussion acknowledging these alternatives is necessary.
2.  **Questionable Theoretical Justification:** The paper’s three criteria for broad-coverage semantic representations (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity) are not adequately defended. The claim that non-terminal nodes inherently add expressive power is debatable; dependency trees can often encode similar information through alternative attachment strategies. The advantages of UCCA’s “grounded” representation over AMR are also unclear, and not convincingly demonstrated.
3.  **Ambiguous Terminology:** The repeated use of "grounded" is problematic, as the term is already established in other fields with different meanings. The authors should consider alternatives like "anchored" or “lexicalized” and carefully rephrase arguments to avoid confusion.
4.  **Unimpressive Empirical Results:** While BSP achieves reasonable performance on primary edges, the overall F-scores are not significantly higher than those of standard dependency parsers like MaltParser. The performance on remote edges is particularly low, indicating that the parser struggles with the aspects of UCCA that differentiate it from traditional dependency structures.
5.  **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:**  The paper does not thoroughly compare BSP to other state-of-the-art parsing techniques that could be adapted to handle graph structures. The LSTM parser comparison is a positive step, but exploring recent advances in graph neural networks (GNNs) could provide a more challenging baseline.
6.  **Limited Ablation Study:** While the ablation study is present, it lacks depth. More focused ablation studies examining the impact of specific feature sets or transitions would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific limitations of other graph-based semantic representations (e.g., AMR, SDP) that motivated your focus on UCCA?
2.  How do you justify the claim that non-terminal nodes inherently add expressive power beyond what can be achieved with alternative attachment strategies in dependency trees?
3.  Given the low performance on remote edges, what specific modifications to the BSP parser or feature set do you believe would most effectively improve performance on this aspect of UCCA?
4.  Have you explored the use of graph neural networks (GNNs) as an alternative to transition-based parsing for UCCA? If so, what were the results?
5.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the MINUPDATE and IMPORTANCE parameters used during training?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more rigorous error analysis to identify the most common types of errors made by the BSP parser.
*   The supplementary material should include more detailed information about the feature templates and the exact configurations used for each parser.
*   Consider including a discussion of the computational complexity of the BSP parser.
