- Introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA semantic graphs.  
- Proposes three principles for semantic annotation schemes: graph-based, non-terminals, and discontinuity.  
- Notes these principles are not unique to semantics and could apply to syntax or other layers.  
- Claims novelty as first parser for a semantically-oriented scheme, but this is only technically accurate and may be overstated.  
- Transition set does not explicitly handle UCCA-specific phenomena (underspecified arguments, semantic labels like Process/State).  
- Handling of remote edges could likely be integrated into existing transitions via label modifications, questioning novelty.  
- Existing transition-based graph parsers from SemEval-era work (e.g., Almeida & Martins, Ribeyre) were not used as baselines or compared.  
- Feature templates tie the model to UCCA, yet simpler features (unigrams, counts) seem more influential.  
- Phrase-based parsing experiments (UParse) lack comparison to other strong baselines (e.g., Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins); UParse is not referenced properly initially.  
- Converting UCCA to surface dependency trees causes substantial information loss; a richer conversion like Schluter et al. (2014) would be preferable.  
- Evaluation omits shared-task metrics and broader comparative baselines, weakening empirical contribution.  
- Ask to introduce the term "grounded semantic" earlier for clarity.  
- Suggest using a constituent-tree representation with rich node labels (e.g., Berkeley parser) as a strong baseline.  
- Request clarification and examples for "implicit units" in UCCA that have no text correspondent.  
- Questions whether UCCA can capture quantifier scope (compared to MRS/DRT) and how parsable/useful UCCA is in practice; calls for error analysis.  
- The three proposed principles appear ad hoc and need clearer positioning; the paper's primary argument (parser, model, or scheme) is unclear and dense.
