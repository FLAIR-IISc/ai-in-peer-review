This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling representations enriched with the Instantiated Semantic-Cognitive Annotation Scheme (UCCA) at UCCACorp. The authors first highlight what they believe should be included in a semantic-oriented scheme: (i) being graph-based (the possibility of multiple governors near a token/node), (ii) being non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures - syntactic -: coordination phrases, lexical: multi-word expressions), and (iii) allowing non-relatives (e.g., verbs + particles). Interestingly, none of these principles are bound by the semantic framework; they can also work for syntax or other representation layers. The authors quickly establish their work by first providing a broad overview of semantic parsing and then introducing their preferred annotation scheme (UCCA). They then present 3 sets of parsing experiments: (i) for component change using a Stanford parser and UCCCA dedicated to phrase-based parsing, (ii) dedicated to dependency change using UCCCA, and finally (iii) the core of their proposal, a set of experiments that shows that their transition-based graph parser is suitable for direct parsing of UCCCA graphs.

I found this work interesting, but before considering publication, I have several concerns about the methodology and empirical justification:

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantic-oriented scheme like theirs. Of course, they are. But with all due respect for the work behind this scheme, it is made up of various levels of under-specified structural arguments and a graph with semantic-oriented labels (process, state), and nothing in their transition set recognizes the specifics of such a graph. Distant edges related to the transition could also have been handled by others, assuming differences in the label set (for example, adding prefixes). If we limit the problem to graph parsing, many works from before 2014-2015 (Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribere et al, 2014-2015) proposed extensions or high model adaptations of transition-based graph parsers, and nothing prevents their use on this data set. This is mostly the use of a specific feature template that connects this model to this scheme (even if it is less impressive than Count features and UniGram). Anyway, because the graph-parsers mentioned above are available [1,2], I don't understand why they couldn't have been used as a baseline or source of comparison. Regarding phrase-based experiments using আপার্স, it could also have been validated by another parser by Fernandez-González and Martins (2015) that can produce parsing as good as LCFRS as আপার্স (the reference is missing when you first introduced আপার্স).

Because this scheme provides a more abstract view of syntactic-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, it would be important to use similar metrics in related shared work. At this point in this field, many systems, models, and datasets are competing, and I think the lack of comparison with other models and parsers is detrimental to this whole work. Still, I found it interesting, and because we are at the point where we move forward, I think this paper should be discussed at a conference like Conl.

Notes in random order
- Please introduce "grounded semantic" before page 2, you first use that phrase
- Why didn't you stick to the component-tree with rich node labels and propagator traces and then try to parse/train with the Berkeley parser? This could have been a good baseline.
- Converting to surface dependency trees is useless in my mind: you lose a lot of information, here a rich transformation like 'Shlutter et al, 2014, Semeval SDP) should be used.
- Can you elaborate on "UCCA graph can have an entity that has no correspondent in the text" or provide a reference or an example.
- You mentioned other representations like MRS and DRT, this increases the fact that your scheme does not allow modeling of quantitative scope information. It is thus completely comparable to other more syntax-oriented schemes. It is indeed more abstract than for example DM and probably less under-specified than the semantic level of PCEDT but how much? How informative is this scheme really and how "parseable" is it really? According to your score, it seems "difficult" but an error analysis would be useful.
- As I said before, the 3 principles you have prepared may apply to many things, they seem a little ad hoc to me and probably need to be in a much broader (and a little clearer) introduction. What are you trying to argue for: a parser that can parse UCCCA? A model suitable for semantic analysis? Or a semantic-oriented scheme that is really parseable? You are trying to say all of them in a very dense way and it is on the verge of being confusing.
