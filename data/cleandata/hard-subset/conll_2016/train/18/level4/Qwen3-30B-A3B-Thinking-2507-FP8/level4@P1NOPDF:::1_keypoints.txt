- Paper introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA and proposes three core principles for semantic annotation: graph structure, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities.  
- The stated claim of being the first parser for a "semantically-oriented scheme" is overstated given prior graph-parsing work.  
- The transition system does not sufficiently address UCCA-specific features like variable under-specification and semantic labels.  
- Remote-edge handling could be achieved via standard transitions and label adjustments rather than new operations.  
- Important prior graph-parsing baselines (e.g., Alameda & Martins 2014, Ribeyre et al. 2014–15, TurboParser, DAGParser) are missing from comparison.  
- Phrase-based experiments (Stanford conversion / U-Parser) lack comparison to LCFRS approaches (e.g., Fernandez-González & Martins 2015) and missing references hinder reproducibility.  
- Evaluation strategy is weak: results aren’t contextualized against standardized semantic dependency (SDP) benchmarks or Semeval shared-task systems.  
- Without standard benchmarks and metrics, claims that UCCA is harder to parse are unsupported.  
- The paper lacks a detailed error analysis to separate scheme-inherent difficulty from implementation issues.  
- Clarify terminology: introduce "grounded semantic" earlier and define it clearly.  
- Consider testing a strong constituent-tree baseline with rich node labels (e.g., Berkeley Parser) for fairer comparison.  
- UCCA-to-dependency conversion used is insufficient; richer conversions (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014) should be employed to preserve structure.  
- Provide clarification/examples for UCCA implicit units that lack textual correspondents.  
- Discuss how UCCA’s exclusion of quantifier scope affects expressiveness and comparability to schemes like DM or PCEDT.  
- The three proposed principles appear ad-hoc and the paper’s multiple claimed contributions (parser, model, parsable scheme) are not coherently centered around a single thesis.  
- Overall: parser is a valid technical contribution but novelty claims, missing baselines, inconsistent metrics, and weak theoretical framing require substantial revision.
