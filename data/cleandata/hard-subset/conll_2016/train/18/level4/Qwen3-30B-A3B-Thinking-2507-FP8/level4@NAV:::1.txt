

This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed for semantico-cognitive annotation schemes, specifically instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors first establish three core principles for semantic annotation schemes: (i) graph-based representation (allowing tokens/nodes to have multiple governors), (ii) non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures, e.g., syntactic coordinations or lexical multiword expressions), and (iii) support for discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs + particles). Notably, these principles are framework-agnostic and applicable beyond semantics. The work situates itself within broad-coverage semantic parsing, detailing the UCCA annotation scheme before presenting three experimental sets: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing via UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution—experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for direct UCCA graph parsing.

While the work is intriguing, several methodological and empirical concerns must be addressed prior to publication:  
The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme like UCCA. While UCCA’s graph structure includes under-specified structural arguments and semantic labels (e.g., Process, State), their transition system does not specifically address these graph characteristics. Transitions for remote edges could have been handled by existing transitions with label set modifications (e.g., affixes), rather than new transitions. Crucially, multiple post-2014-2015 Semeval shared task approaches (Almeda & Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) already extended transition-based or higher-model graph parsers, and their availability [1,2] makes them valid baselines—yet the authors omit them. The phrase-based experiments using U*parse could have been validated against Fernandez-Gonzales & Martins (2015), which produces comparable LCFRS-like parses (the reference for U*parse is missing upon introduction).  

Given UCCA’s abstract syntaxico-semantic representation—more abstract than most SDP treebanks—comparisons using shared-task metrics are essential. The absence of benchmarks against established parsers undermines the work’s significance in a competitive landscape. Despite these concerns, the paper is timely and merits discussion at a conference like CoNLL.  

Additional notes:  
- Introduce "grounded semantic" before page 2, as it is used prematurely.  
- Why not explore a constituent-tree baseline with rich node labels (e.g., Berkeley Parser)?  
- UCCA-to-surface-dependency conversion is suboptimal due to significant information loss; a richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014) should have been used.  
- Clarify or provide an example for "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units without textual correspondents."  
- Address whether UCCA’s lack of quantifier scope modeling (unlike MRS/DRT) limits comparability to other schemes. Quantify its abstraction level (e.g., vs. PCEDT) and assess its "parsability" via error analysis.  
- The three principles seem ad-hoc; clarify the paper’s core argument: is it a UCCA-specific parser, a model for semantic analysis, or a demonstration of a parsable semantic scheme? The current framing risks confusion.
