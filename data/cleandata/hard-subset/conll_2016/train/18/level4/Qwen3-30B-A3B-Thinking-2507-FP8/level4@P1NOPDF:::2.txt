

The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation formalism. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA allows "nonterminal" nodes that do not correspond to surface words. Unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded," meaning text tokens explicitly appear as nodes in the semantic structure. The authors propose several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

While developing a semantic parser for UCCA is reasonable given its existing annotation, the introduction and background section rings false by implying UCCA is the only viable graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism worth studying. This claim is unnecessary—the development of a high-quality UCCA parser would be a valuable contribution on its own.

I also question the three criteria for SR formalisms proposed in the introduction. The necessity of "nonterminal nodes" for expressive capacity is unclear. While they may simplify head assignment in coordinated structures, what specific information *requires* nonterminal nodes rather than richer edge labels? Similarly, the issue of discontinuity does not apply to non-grounded SRs, making the authors' emphasis on this point misleading. The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style formalisms remain unclear to me. Furthermore, the term "grounded" has been used inconsistently across semantic literature in the past decade; I recommend the authors adopt a more precise term (e.g., "lexicalized" or "token-anchored").

Consequently, the entire introductory argument needs significant revision to avoid overstating UCCA's uniqueness and to clarify its theoretical position.

The parser itself appears adequate, though I did not verify implementation details. However, the evaluation results are underwhelming. On "primary" edges, a standard MaltParser baseline outperforms the proposed BSP parser, and F-scores for "remote" edges (which dependency-tree parsers like Malt cannot directly compute) remain low. Crucially, the conversion of dependency graphs to trees has been extensively studied under "tree approximations" in CoNLL 2014/2015 shared tasks (without nonterminal nodes). Works like Agić et al. (2015) detail methods for reconstructing edges lost during graph-to-tree conversion. Incorporating such techniques could further improve MaltParser (and even LSTM-based variants) performance, diminishing the relative novelty and strength of the BSP parser. Given this, the paper's core contribution appears less compelling than presented.
