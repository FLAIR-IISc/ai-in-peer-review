

The paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation formalism. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs may include "nonterminal" nodes not corresponding to text tokens, and unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded" (i.e., text tokens appear as nodes in the representation). The authors propose multiple parsing methods, including a transition-based parser for direct UCCA construction, and evaluate them.  

While developing a semantic parser for UCCA is reasonable given its existence and annotated data, the introduction and background section presents a problematic claim: it asserts UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism worth studying. This overstatement is unnecessary and undermines the paper’s potential contribution, which could stand on its own merits.  

I also question the three criteria for SR formalisms proposed in the introduction. The utility of "nonterminal nodes" is unclear—they do not appear to provide additional expressive capacity, as head selection in coordinated structures could be handled via richer edge labels. The discontinuity issue is irrelevant for non-"grounded" SRs, and the advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR remain unconvincing. Furthermore, "grounded" is an overloaded term in semantics; I recommend alternatives like "anchored" or "lexicalized" to avoid confusion. Consequently, the entire introductory argument requires substantial revision for precision and balance.  

The parser methodology appears sound, though I did not scrutinize implementation details. However, the evaluation results are underwhelming. On "primary" edges, a standard MaltParser outperforms the presented BSP parser, and "remote" edge f-scores are modest. The conversion of dependency graphs to trees (via "tree approximations," studied in CoNLL 2014/2015 shared tasks) has been extensively addressed, including methods for reconstructing deleted edges (e.g., Agic et al., 2015). Incorporating such techniques could further boost MaltParser (and LSTM-based variants) performance, thereby diminishing the perceived novelty and advantage of the BSP approach.
