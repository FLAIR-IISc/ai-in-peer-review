

This paper introduces a transition-based approach for parsing broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS), which support three structural properties: multiple parents (to represent shared semantic arguments), non-terminal nodes (to represent complex multi-word units without clear heads), and discontinuous units (to handle phrasal verbs and multi-word expressions). The authors propose two complementary parsing strategies: (1) converting UCCA structures to dependency or constituency trees and using existing parsers on the converted representations, and (2) developing a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three properties. They evaluate their methods on UCCA-annotated corpora, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score for the best parser.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a gap in semantic parsing literature—no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation. UCCA is the only annotation scheme that satisfies these criteria, yet it lacked a dedicated parser until this work.
2. The authors provide a thorough and well-structured analysis of the structural requirements for semantic parsing, clearly demonstrating why current approaches (like AMR or SDP) fall short by omitting one or more of these properties.
3. The experimental design is robust, with both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluations, and the authors make all code and models publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
1. The claim of being "first to propose a parser for BSS" is overstated. Several transition-based graph parsers (e.g., Ribeyre et al., 2014; Sagae and Tsujii, 2008) could have been applied to UCCA data without modification, as they already handle non-projective structures. The authors should have included these as baselines rather than treating their approach as novel.
2. The conversion to dependency trees loses significant structural information (non-terminal nodes and remote edges), yet the authors present this as a viable alternative without comparing to more sophisticated conversion approaches (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014). This weakens their argument about the limitations of existing parsers.
3. The paper lacks meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art semantic parsers on standard evaluation metrics. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the reported 69.2% F-score represents progress or is consistent with existing approaches.
4. The three structural properties claimed as necessary for BSS seem somewhat arbitrary and lack justification beyond their utility for UCCA. The authors should clarify why these specific properties are required for "broad-coverage" semantic representation, rather than being specific to UCCA's annotation scheme.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper introduces "grounded semantic representation" without properly defining it before page 2. Please clarify this key concept early in the paper.
2. The authors mention that UCCA graphs may contain implicit units with no text correspondence. Please provide an example or reference to clarify this claim.
3. The paper states that the UCCA scheme "doesn't seem to allow for modeling quantifier scope information." How does this limitation affect the practical utility of UCCA for downstream applications, and how does it compare to other semantic representations like AMR or MRS?
4. The authors claim their parser is the first to handle all three structural properties, yet several graph parsers exist that could handle these properties. Why weren't these parsers evaluated as baselines, and how does the BSP's feature set specifically address UCCA's unique requirements beyond standard graph parsing?

Additional Comments:
- The conversion to dependency trees appears unnecessarily lossy; a richer conversion approach (e.g., from Semeval SDP) would better preserve semantic structure.
- The paper mentions that "UCCA's foundational layer covers predicate-argument structures" but doesn't clearly explain how this differs from other semantic representations like AMR or SDP in terms of practical utility.
- An error analysis would help clarify why UCCA parsing appears more challenging than other semantic parsing tasks, and whether the lower scores are due to the complexity of the representation or limitations in the parsing approach.
