- Introduces first parsers for UCCA, supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units via conversion and a new transition-based BSP.  
- Main contribution: first system that handles all three UCCA structural properties simultaneously.  
- Strength: Fills a clear gap by providing baseline parsers and benchmarks for UCCA with publicly released code and data.  
- Strength: Shows conversion-based approaches can handle UCCA reasonably well and offers a direct parser (BSP) that natively supports required structures.  
- Weakness: Overstates UCCA's uniqueness by claiming it's the only graph-based formalism with these properties without substantiating the claim.  
- Weakness: Unconvincing justification for non-terminal nodes; paper doesn't show why richer edge labels couldn't represent multi-word units.  
- Weakness: Misuses the term "grounded" to mean text tokens as nodes, conflicting with established semantic terminology.  
- Weakness: BSP underwhelms on evaluation (62.4% labeled F1 primary edges, 15.3% remote edges) and is outperformed by conversion + MaltParser.  
- Weakness: Insufficient engagement with prior work on tree approximations and reconstruction techniques from CoNLL shared tasks.  
- Question: Which other formalisms (e.g., AMR variants, SDP) could already support UCCA's three properties, and why were they excluded?  
- Question: Provide concrete examples where non-terminal nodes are necessary and edge labels would be insufficient.  
- Question: Why not use clearer terminology (e.g., "lexicalized" or "text-anchored") instead of "grounded"?  
- Question: What specific limitations cause BSP's lower performance versus MaltParser, and how can BSP be improved?
