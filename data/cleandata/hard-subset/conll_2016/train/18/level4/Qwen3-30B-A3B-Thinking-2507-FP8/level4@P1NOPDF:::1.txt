

This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed for UCCA, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme featuring rich graph-based representations. The authors first establish three core principles for semantic annotation schemes: (i) graph-based structure (allowing multiple governors per token/node), (ii) non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures like coordinate phrases or multiword expressions), and (iii) support for discontinuous elements (e.g., verb-participle constructions). Notably, these principles are framework-agnostic and could equally apply to syntax or other representation layers. The work positions itself within broad-coverage semantic parsing, focusing on UCCA as the annotation scheme of choice.

The authors present three experimental sets: (i) phrase-based parsing using Stanford Parser and UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing via UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) their core contribution—direct transition-based parsing of UCCA graphs. While the work is interesting, significant methodological and empirical concerns require addressing before publication.

The claim that this is the first parser for a "semantically-oriented scheme" is overstated. UCCA graphs feature variable structural under-specification and semantically oriented labels (e.g., Process, State), yet the transition system fails to address these specificities. For instance, transitions for remote edges could have been handled by standard transitions with adjusted label sets (e.g., affixes), rather than requiring new operations. Crucially, post-2014 graph-parsing work (e.g., Alameda & Martins, 2014; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015; and the Semeval shared tasks) already offers extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adapted higher-order models. These tools (e.g., Ribeyre’s DAGParser [1] and Martins’ TurboParser [2]) are publicly available and could serve as valid baselines or comparison points. Their absence weakens the paper’s empirical foundation. Similarly, the phrase-based experiments using U-Parser could have been validated against Fernandez-González & Martins (2015), whose LCFRS parser achieves comparable performance; the missing reference to U-Parser’s source undermines reproducibility.

The evaluation strategy also presents issues. UCCA’s abstract syntaxico-semantic representation differs significantly from standard SDP treebanks. To contextualize results, comparisons with Semeval shared-task systems (which use standardized metrics) would be essential. The lack of such benchmarks makes it difficult to assess whether UCCA’s parsing difficulty stems from the scheme itself or the evaluation methodology. Without these comparisons, the claim that UCCA is "harder" to parse lacks meaningful support. An error analysis would further clarify this.

Several specific concerns require clarification:  
1. The term "grounded semantic" appears before page 2—introduce it earlier.  
2. Why not test a constituent-tree baseline with rich node labels (e.g., using the Berkeley Parser)? This would provide a stronger point of comparison.  
3. The UCCA-to-dependency conversion is insufficient; a richer conversion (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014) should have been used to preserve structural information.  
4. Clarify the claim that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units without textual correspondents" with an example or reference.  
5. UCCA’s exclusion of quantifier scope (unlike MRS or DRT) limits its semantic expressiveness. How does this affect its comparability to schemes like DM or PCEDT? The paper’s scores suggest UCCA is "harder" to parse, but without an error analysis, it remains unclear whether this reflects inherent complexity or implementation issues.  
6. The three proposed principles (graph-based, non-terminal nodes, discontinuities) seem ad-hoc and lack a cohesive argument. The paper attempts to claim three distinct contributions (a UCCA parser, a semantic-parsing model, and a parsable semantic scheme) without clarifying the central thesis. This density risks confusion.

In summary, while the parser itself is a valid technical contribution, the paper’s claims of novelty, evaluation strategy, and theoretical framing require substantial revision. The absence of relevant baselines, inconsistent metrics, and underdeveloped justification for UCCA’s design principles undermine the work’s significance. The paper would benefit from a clearer contribution statement, expanded comparison to existing graph parsers, and alignment with established semantic parsing evaluation practices. Despite these issues, the work addresses an important gap in semantic parsing and merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.
