

This paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism. The authors introduce a transition-based parser and evaluate multiple approaches. While the development of a UCCA parser is warranted given the existence of UCCA-annotated data, the introduction and background section significantly undermine the contribution through problematic claims and insufficient justification.

The authors assert that UCCA is the *only* graph-based SR formalism worthy of study, which is both inaccurate and unnecessary. This framing distracts from a potentially valuable contribution: a robust UCCA parser. The paper would be stronger if it focused on UCCA’s merits without dismissing alternatives. Furthermore, the three criteria for SR formalisms proposed in the introduction lack convincing support. Specifically:

1.  **Nonterminal Nodes:** The authors claim nonterminal nodes (e.g., for coordinated structures) are essential for expressive capacity. However, it is unclear what information *requires* nonterminals rather than enriched edge labels (e.g., indicating coordination type or scope). Without evidence that nonterminals enable representations impossible otherwise, this criterion appears arbitrary.

2.  **"Grounded" Representations:** The authors define "grounded" as requiring text tokens to appear as nodes (contrasting with AMR). Yet, the advantages of this property over AMR are not compellingly argued. Discontinuity issues, which groundedness might mitigate, are irrelevant to non-grounded SRs like AMR. The term "grounded" itself is problematic, having been used ambiguously in semantics for over a decade. Alternatives like "lexicalized" or "anchored" would be clearer and less likely to confuse readers. The introduction should be rewritten to carefully justify UCCA’s specific design choices without overstating their necessity.

The parser itself appears technically sound, though the review does not delve into implementation details. The evaluation, however, is underwhelming. On primary edges (which dependency parsers like MaltParser can directly handle), the BSP parser is outperformed by a straightforward MaltParser baseline. For remote edges (where graph structure is critical), the f-scores are modest. Crucially, the comparison overlooks relevant prior work on graph-to-tree conversion for semantic dependency parsing. The CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks extensively studied "tree approximations" for converting semantic graphs to trees, including methods for reconstructing deleted edges (e.g., Agić et al., 2015). Incorporating such techniques could significantly improve MaltParser’s performance on remote edges, further diminishing the perceived advantage of the BSP parser. The paper’s evaluation does not account for this, making the comparative strength of their approach unclear.

In summary, the paper’s core contribution—a UCCA parser—is valuable but presented with excessive and unsupported claims in the introduction. The justification for UCCA’s design choices is weak, and the evaluation fails to adequately position the proposed parser against state-of-the-art graph-to-tree conversion techniques. The authors should:

1.  Remove the unsubstantiated claim that UCCA is the *only* viable graph-based SR.
2.  Re-evaluate and substantially revise the introduction to focus on UCCA’s specific merits without dismissing alternatives.
3.  Replace "grounded" with a more precise term (e.g., "lexicalized" or "anchored").
4.  Re-evaluate the results by comparing against MaltParser *enhanced with graph-to-tree reconstruction methods* (e.g., Agić et al.’s approach) to fairly assess the BSP parser’s relative strength.

With these revisions, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to semantic parsing. As currently written, the introduction’s overreach and the evaluation’s lack of context weaken the overall impact. The work is potentially interesting but requires significant refinement to be publishable.
