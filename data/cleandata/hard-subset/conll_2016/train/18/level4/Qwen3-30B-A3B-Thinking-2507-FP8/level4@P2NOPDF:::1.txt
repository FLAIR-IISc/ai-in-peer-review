

This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed for UCCA, a semantic-cognitive annotation scheme featuring rich graph structures. The authors first outline three core principles for semantic annotation: (i) graph-based representation (allowing multiple governors per token), (ii) non-terminal nodes (for complex structures like coordinate phrases or multiword expressions), and (iii) support for discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs + particles). Notably, these principles are framework-agnostic and could apply to syntactic or other representation layers. The work positions itself within broad-coverage semantic parsing, focusing on UCCA as the annotation scheme, and reports three experimental sets: (i) phrase-based parsing via Stanford parser and UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing using UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution—direct parsing of UCCA graphs using their transition-based parser.

While the work presents an interesting approach, significant methodological concerns undermine its contribution and empirical justification. The claim that this is the "first parser for a semantically-oriented scheme" is problematic. UCCA’s graph structure—featuring underspecified arguments and semantic labels (e.g., Process, State)—does not necessitate novel transition rules; existing graph parsers (e.g., Almeda & Martins, 2014; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) already handle similar structures. The authors’ model relies primarily on a scheme-specific feature template, not unique parsing innovations. Crucially, the readily available graph parsers cited (Ribeyre, 2014; Martins, 2014) should have been used as baselines for fair comparison, yet they are not. Similarly, the phrase-based experiments using UParse could have been validated against Fernandez-Gonzales & Martins (2015), whose LCFRS parser matches UParse’s capabilities (a reference is missing upon UParse’s introduction).

The paper also fails to adopt standard metrics from relevant semantic parsing shared tasks (e.g., Semeval SDP), despite UCCA’s abstract representation differing significantly from syntax-focused treebanks like Penn. Without such comparisons, the evaluation lacks contextual grounding. The reported scores suggest UCCA parsing is "harder," but without error analysis or benchmarking against established semantic parsers (e.g., those targeting MRS or DRT), this claim remains unsupported. The absence of such comparisons weakens the paper’s contribution, especially as the field increasingly relies on shared-task metrics for meaningful progress.

Several specific issues require resolution:  
1. **Novelty justification**: The three proposed principles appear somewhat ad hoc. They are broadly applicable (e.g., to syntax) but lack clear argumentation for why they are *essential* for semantic parsing. The introduction should clarify the paper’s precise aim: is it advocating for a UCCA-specific parser, a general semantic parsing model, or the viability of UCCA as a parseable scheme? The current conflation of these goals creates confusion.  
2. **Baseline omissions**: The conversion to surface dependency trees (used in experiments) discards critical semantic information. A richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014) would better preserve UCCA’s structure. Additionally, testing against the Berkeley Parser (with enriched constituent trees and label propagation) would provide a stronger baseline than dependency-based comparisons.  
3. **Technical ambiguities**: The claim that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units with no text correspondent" requires an example or reference. Similarly, the paper’s exclusion of quantifier scope modeling (unlike MRS/DRT) merits discussion: how does this limit UCCA’s applicability compared to more expressive semantic frameworks (e.g., PCEDT), and why is this acceptable for their parsing task?  
4. **Terminology**: "Grounded semantic" is used before definition (page 2). Define this term early to avoid confusion.  

The paper arrives at a pivotal moment in semantic parsing research, where frameworks like UCCA could bridge syntax and semantics. However, its current form risks being overlooked due to methodological gaps. With the proposed revisions—particularly adding relevant baselines, adopting shared-task metrics, clarifying the novelty argument, and resolving technical ambiguities—the work could make a meaningful contribution. As it stands, the lack of comparative evaluation and insufficient justification for the parser’s design hinder its publication suitability. I recommend major revision before consideration for CONLL.
