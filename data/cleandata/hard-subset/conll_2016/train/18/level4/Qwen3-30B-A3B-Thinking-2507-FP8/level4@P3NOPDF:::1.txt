

This paper presents a transition-based graph parser for UCCA, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors establish three design principles for semantic annotation schemes: (i) graph-based representation (allowing multiple governors per node), (ii) non-terminal nodes for complex structures (e.g., coordinate phrases, multiword expressions), and (iii) support for discontinuities (e.g., verb-participle pairs). While these principles are generalizable beyond semantics, the paper’s core contribution is a parser for direct UCCA graph parsing. The authors report three experimental sets: (i) phrase-based parsing via Stanford Parser and UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing via UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) their proposed transition-based graph parser for direct UCCA parsing.  

**Major Concerns:**  
The paper’s claim of novelty is problematic. The authors assert they are “the first” to propose a parser for a scheme like UCCA, yet prior work (e.g., Almeda & Martins, 2014; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) has extended transition-based graph parsers (e.g., TurboParser, DAGParser) to handle non-projective and graph-structured data. These systems are publicly available ([1], [2]) and could have served as baselines. The paper’s specific feature template (e.g., rich labels, unigram features) is less influential than the parser architecture itself, making the absence of these baselines a significant oversight. Similarly, for phrase-based parsing, the authors rely solely on U*parse but omit comparisons with Fernandez-González & Martins (2015), which produces comparable LCFRS-like parses.  

The metric choice further weakens the evaluation. UCCA’s abstract representation (e.g., richer than SDP treebanks) warrants using metrics from relevant shared tasks (e.g., Semeval SDP). Instead, the paper uses surface dependency conversions, which discard critical semantic information (e.g., non-terminal nodes, discontinuities). A richer conversion (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014) would have preserved structure. The lack of comparisons to state-of-the-art parsers (e.g., TurboParser) and standardized metrics makes it impossible to gauge the parser’s relative performance or contribution to the field.  

**Conceptual and Technical Gaps:**  
The paper’s three design principles are underdeveloped. While they are valid for graph-based representations, they are not uniquely semantic (e.g., applicable to syntax). This weakens the argument for UCCA’s necessity. The contribution remains ambiguous: Is the paper about parsing UCCA? A generalizable semantic parser? Or validating UCCA’s parsability? The dense, overlapping claims cause confusion.  

Additionally, UCCA’s limitations require explicit discussion. The scheme lacks explicit modeling of quantifier scope (unlike MRS/DRT), making it incomparable to more semantic frameworks. The paper’s assertion that UCCA is “more abstract” than DM or “less specified” than PCEDT needs quantification. Error analysis would clarify why UCCA parsing is “harder” (per results) and whether this stems from the scheme or parser. The claim that “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units with no text counterpart” (p. 3) lacks examples or references.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- “Grounded semantic” is used before introduction (p. 2).  
- The Berkeley Parser baseline (for constituent trees with rich labels) is unexplored.  
- The phrase “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units” needs elaboration or an example.  
- Missing reference for U*parse’s capabilities (cited as “ref missing” in §3.1).  

**Recommendation:**  
The work addresses a meaningful gap in semantic parsing but suffers from inadequate baselines, unclear contribution, and methodological weaknesses. The absence of comparisons to existing graph parsers (e.g., TurboParser, DAGParser) undermines the claimed novelty and performance assessment. While the parser design is technically sound, the paper fails to position itself within the broader literature or justify UCCA’s unique value.  

The paper’s timing—during a critical phase for semantic parsing—makes its publication relevant, but it requires substantial revision. I recommend **rejection** pending major revisions: (1) replace all baselines with state-of-the-art graph parsers; (2) adopt shared-task metrics and a richer conversion method; (3) clarify the contribution (UCCA parsing vs. general semantic parsing); (4) address UCCA’s limitations (e.g., quantifier scope, implicit units) with examples; and (5) integrate the three design principles into a cohesive argument. Without these, the paper’s impact is diminished, and its claims lack empirical support.
