

The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a specific graph-based semantic representation formalism. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs may contain "nonterminal" nodes that do not correspond to text tokens. Unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded," meaning text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors propose several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

While the existence of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data justifies developing a UCCA parser, the introduction and background section is problematic. It incorrectly asserts that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism worth studying, which is misleading and unnecessary—the development of a robust UCCA parser would constitute a valuable contribution on its own.

I also disagree with the three criteria for SR formalisms proposed in the introduction. The utility of "nonterminal nodes" in enhancing expressive capacity is unclear; while they may complicate head selection in coordinated structures, it is not evident what information *requires* nonterminal nodes rather than more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity does not arise in non-grounded SRs. The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones remain unconvincing, and the term "grounded" has been applied to multiple distinct concepts in semantics over the past decade—recommending alternatives such as "lexicalized" or "anchored" would improve precision.

Consequently, the entire introductory section requires substantial revision to be more carefully argued and phrased.

The parser itself appears technically sound, though I did not verify implementation details. However, the evaluation results are underwhelming. On "primary" edges, a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the proposed BSP parser, and the f-scores on "remote" edges (which dependency-tree parsers like Malt cannot directly compute) are modest. Additionally, the conversion of dependency graphs to trees has been extensively studied under "tree approximations" in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks (without nonterminal nodes). Prior work, such as Agić et al. (2015), "Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations," details methods for reconstructing edges deleted during graph-to-tree conversion. Incorporating such techniques would likely improve MaltParser (and LSTM-based MaltParser) performance further, diminishing the perceived strength of the BSP parser.
