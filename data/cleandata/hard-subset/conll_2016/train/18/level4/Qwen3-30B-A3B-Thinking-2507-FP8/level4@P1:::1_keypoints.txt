- Paper proposes a transition-based approach and conversion strategies for parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic structures (BSS), reporting ~70% labeled F-score on UCCA corpora.  
- Strength: Fills a gap by targeting a parser that supports multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuous units—properties UCCA requires and prior parsers did not jointly support.  
- Strength: Provides a clear, structured analysis of why existing semantic parsing formalisms (e.g., AMR, SDP) omit one or more required BSS properties.  
- Strength: Robust experimental design with in-domain and out-of-domain evaluations and publicly released code/models for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Claim of being the first BSS parser is overstated; earlier transition-based graph parsers could likely handle UCCA and should have been included as baselines.  
- Weakness: Dependency-tree conversion is lossy (drops non-terminals and remote edges), and the paper lacks comparison to richer conversion methods that better preserve structure.  
- Weakness: No meaningful comparison against state-of-the-art semantic parsers on standard metrics, making it hard to judge whether the reported F-score is real progress.  
- Weakness: The three structural properties are presented as necessary for BSS but lack broader justification beyond fitting UCCA’s design.  
- Question: "Grounded semantic representation" is introduced without a clear early definition—please define it before page 2.  
- Question: Authors claim UCCA graphs may contain implicit units without text correspondence—please provide an example or reference.  
- Question: UCCA reportedly cannot model quantifier scope—how does this limitation affect downstream utility compared to AMR or MRS?  
- Question: Why were existing graph parsers not evaluated as baselines, and what specific BSP features address UCCA beyond standard graph parsing?  
- Additional: The dependency conversion appears unnecessarily lossy; a richer SDP-style conversion should be considered.  
- Additional: The paper says UCCA’s foundational layer covers predicate-argument structure but doesn’t clearly explain practical differences from AMR or SDP.  
- Additional: An error analysis is needed to determine whether lower scores stem from UCCA complexity or parser limitations.
