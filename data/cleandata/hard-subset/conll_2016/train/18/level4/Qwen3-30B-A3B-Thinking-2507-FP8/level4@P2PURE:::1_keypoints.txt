- Introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA and proposes three principles for semantic annotation (graph structure, non-terminals, discontinuities), though these principles aren’t inherently semantic.  
- Reviewer finds the work interesting but raises significant methodological and empirical concerns needing resolution before publication.  
- Claim of being the first parser for a semantically oriented scheme is questionable because post-2014/2015 graph-parsing approaches could be applied to UCCA.  
- The transition set does not specifically address UCCA’s characteristics (underspecified structural arguments, semantically oriented labels); remote edges could be handled by existing transitions with label adjustments.  
- Important recent graph-parsing baselines (e.g., Almeida & Martins; Ribeyre et al.) are missing from comparisons.  
- The feature template appears to be UCCA-specific but is less influential than simple count/unigram features, weakening the claimed novelty.  
- Phrase-based experiments should be validated with another parser (e.g., Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins’ LCFRS-like parser) to strengthen analysis.  
- Surface dependency conversion used discards crucial structural information; a richer conversion (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014) should be employed.  
- Evaluation lacks use of shared-task metrics (e.g., SDP) and comparisons to competing models/parsers, undermining empirical contribution.  
- Introduce the term “grounded semantic” earlier in the paper, as its current placement is premature.  
- Test a constituent-tree baseline with rich node labels and trace propagation (e.g., Berkeley parser) as an additional baseline.  
- Clarify or exemplify the claim that “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units with no text correspondent.”  
- Discuss UCCA’s incompatibility with quantifier scope modeling (vs. MRS/DRT), compare its abstractness to schemes like DM or PCEDT, and include error analysis to contextualize lower parsing scores.  
- The three proposed principles seem ad-hoc and require stronger justification; the paper’s core aim (parsing UCCA vs enabling semantic analysis vs validating a parsable scheme) is unclear.  
- Despite these issues, the work is timely and merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.
