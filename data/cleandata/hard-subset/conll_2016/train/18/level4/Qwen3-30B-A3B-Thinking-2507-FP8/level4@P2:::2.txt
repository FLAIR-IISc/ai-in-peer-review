

Summary: The paper proposes the first transition-based parser for UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation), a semantic representation scheme that supports three structural properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors present two approaches: conversion-based methods that map UCCA structures to dependency trees or constituency trees, and a direct parser called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser) that directly constructs UCCA parses. The work is evaluated on UCCA-annotated corpora, with results showing approximately 69% labeled F-score for the best parser.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. First parser for UCCA that directly handles all three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units)
2. Practical conversion protocols between UCCA and two related formalisms (dependency trees and constituency trees)
3. First empirical exploration of UCCA parsing, demonstrating that transition-based methods can be effectively applied to this semantic representation

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a clear gap in semantic parsing research: no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as it addresses a limitation in current semantic parsing approaches.
2. The authors provide the first practical implementation of a UCCA parser, which is valuable given that UCCA is the only semantic representation scheme supporting all three required structural properties. This work enables further research on UCCA and semantic parsing.
3. The conversion-based approach is well-motivated and demonstrates that existing parsing technology can be adapted to handle UCCA structures with reasonable effectiveness, providing a useful baseline for future work.

Weaknesses:
1. The introduction unnecessarily claims UCCA is the only meaningful semantic representation to study, which is misleading and detracts from the paper's contribution. The paper could have focused on the specific merits of UCCA without dismissing other semantic representation schemes.
2. The justification for the three structural properties is weak, particularly regarding non-terminal nodes. The paper fails to demonstrate that non-terminal nodes provide expressive capacity that cannot be achieved with more informative edge labels. The claim that "standardization problems arise" with head selection for coordination structures is not adequately supported.
3. The evaluation results are underwhelming: the direct BSP parser does not outperform simpler conversion-based approaches (MaltParser) on primary edges (62.4% vs. 63.9% F-score), and the remote edge scores (15.3% for BSP) are quite low. This undermines the claim that the direct approach is superior.
4. The paper fails to address prior work on tree approximations for semantic dependency parsing (e.g., Agic et al. 2015), which has explored methods for reconstructing edges lost during graph-to-tree conversion. Incorporating such methods could have significantly improved the conversion-based results, making the contribution of the direct BSP parser less clear.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more concrete evidence for why non-terminal nodes are necessary rather than merely convenient for representing coordination structures? How would the semantic representation change if coordination structures were represented using edge labels instead of non-terminal nodes?
2. Given that the BSP parser underperforms MaltParser on primary edges, how do you account for this discrepancy? What specific features of BSP would you expect to improve its performance on primary edges?
3. How do you plan to address the low remote edge scores (15.3% F-score)? Would incorporating more sophisticated edge reconstruction methods from the tree approximation literature (e.g., Agic et al. 2015) significantly improve the conversion-based approach?
