

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which require three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared semantic arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex units like coordination), and discontinuous elements (for phrasal verbs). The authors present two complementary approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA structures to dependency/constituency trees and applies existing parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three BSS properties. The primary contribution is the first parser capable of handling all three structural properties simultaneously, with the secondary contribution being a systematic evaluation of conversion-based approaches. The third contribution is the demonstration that transition-based methods are viable for UCCA parsing, with nearly 70% labeled F1 on primary edges.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel Parser Architecture**: BSP is the first transition-based parser explicitly designed for BSS, addressing a gap in semantic parsing literature. The authors' extension of transition systems to handle remote edges (via LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX transitions) and discontinuous structures (via SWAP) is well-motivated and technically sound. This represents a significant step toward robust semantic parsing that accommodates linguistically rich phenomena.  
2. **Rigorous Evaluation Framework**: The paper establishes a clear evaluation metric for DAG structures by defining mutual edges based on yield sets, which is crucial for fair comparison in non-tree semantic parsing. The inclusion of both in-domain (Wikipedia) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) experiments demonstrates the parser's robustness, with BSP achieving 69.2% F1 on primary edges—a notable result given UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80–85%).  
3. **Practical Utility**: The authors provide concrete evidence that transition-based methods can be adapted to semantic parsing, with the LSTM-based classifier outperforming traditional parsers by 9.1% F1. This suggests a clear path for future improvements and establishes a benchmark for semantic parsing research.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of Comparative Baselines**: The paper fails to compare with existing graph parsing methods (e.g., TurboParser or DAGParser) that could have been adapted to UCCA. The authors cite these works but do not evaluate against them, weakening the claim of novelty. For instance, the conversion to dependency trees could have been compared against dependency DAG parsers (e.g., Sagae and Tsujii, 2008), which handle multiple parents.  
2. **Questionable Conversion Strategy**: The dependency tree conversion loses critical information (e.g., non-terminal nodes), as evidenced by the zero remote edge F1 scores. The authors acknowledge this loss but do not justify why a richer conversion (e.g., Semeval SDP's approach) was not explored, which would have preserved more structural details.  
3. **Underdeveloped Motivation for Structural Properties**: The three BSS properties are presented as novel, but they align with well-established concepts in semantic dependency parsing (e.g., SDP, AMR). The paper does not sufficiently distinguish UCCA from other schemes (e.g., AMR) or argue why these properties are uniquely necessary for semantic parsing. A clearer justification of UCCA's advantages would strengthen the contribution.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Why was the dependency tree conversion (which discards non-terminal nodes) chosen over a richer conversion method (e.g., Semeval SDP's) that preserves more structural information?  
2. How does UCCA's "grounded" semantic representation specifically address limitations of AMR (e.g., alignment errors) in practical applications like machine translation or summarization?  
3. The paper states that UCCA "may contain implicit units with no text correspondent" but provides no example or reference. Could you clarify this with a concrete example and explain how it affects parsing?  
4. The authors claim BSP is the first parser supporting all three BSS properties. How do they reconcile this with existing work on dependency DAG parsing (e.g., Sagae and Tsujii, 2008), which also handles multiple parents?  

**Additional Comments**  
- The term "grounded semantic" is used without definition on page 2; please clarify early in the paper.  
- The paper would benefit from an error analysis to explain why remote edges (13.3% F1) remain challenging despite BSP's design.  
- The claim that "no existing parser supports all three properties" is overstated; SDP parsers support multiple parents and discontinuities but not non-terminal nodes (Ivanova et al., 2012). A more precise comparison would strengthen the novelty argument.  

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to semantic parsing by providing the first parser for UCCA, a representation that supports linguistically rich structures. While the methodology requires refinement (particularly regarding baselines and conversion strategies), the work is technically sound and well-motivated. With minor revisions addressing the above points, it would be a strong candidate for publication.
