- Summary: Proposes the first transition-based parser for UCCA, compares conversion-based methods to a direct BSP parser, reporting best labeled F-score ≈ 69%.
- Contribution: First parser that directly handles UCCA's three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities).
- Contribution: Provides practical conversion protocols between UCCA and dependency/constituency formalisms.
- Contribution: Empirically explores UCCA parsing and shows transition-based methods can be applied to this representation.
- Strength: Identifies and addresses a clear gap—no prior parser supports all three UCCA structural properties.
- Strength: Delivers the first practical UCCA parser implementation, enabling further research on this semantic scheme.
- Strength: Conversion-based approach is well-motivated and offers a useful baseline by adapting existing parsing technology.
- Weakness: Overstates UCCA as the only meaningful semantic representation, which is misleading and detracts from the contribution.
- Weakness: Weak justification for the necessity of non-terminal nodes; paper fails to show they add expressive power beyond informative edge labels.
- Weakness: Direct BSP underperforms conversion-based MaltParser on primary edges and yields very low remote-edge scores (15.3% F), undermining claims of superiority.
- Weakness: Fails to engage relevant prior work on tree approximations and edge reconstruction (e.g., Agic et al. 2015), which could affect comparisons.
- Question: Can authors provide concrete evidence that non-terminal nodes are necessary rather than merely convenient, especially for coordination structures?
- Question: Why does BSP underperform MaltParser on primary edges, and which BSP features should improve this gap?
- Question: How will authors address low remote-edge scores and would incorporating tree-approximation/reconstruction techniques significantly improve conversion-based results?
