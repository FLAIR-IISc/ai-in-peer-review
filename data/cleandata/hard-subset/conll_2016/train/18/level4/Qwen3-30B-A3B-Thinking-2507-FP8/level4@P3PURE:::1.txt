

This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors first establish three core principles for a semantic annotation scheme: (i) being graph-based (allowing tokens/nodes to have multiple governors), (ii) incorporating non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures, e.g., syntactic coordinate phrases or lexical multiword expressions), and (iii) supporting discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs + particles). Notably, these principles are not inherently semantic—they could apply to syntactic or other representation layers. The work is positioned within broad-coverage semantic parsing, with the UCCA annotation scheme as the specific focus. Three experimental sets are then presented: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and an UCCA-to-constituency conversion; (ii) dependency parsing via UCCA-to-dependency conversion; and (iii) the core contribution, demonstrating that their transition-based graph parser directly handles UCCA graphs.  

While the work is interesting, several methodological and empirical concerns must be addressed before publication:  

1.  **Novelty Claim**: The authors correctly state they are the first to propose a parser for UCCA. However, the transition system does not specifically address UCCA’s unique graph features (e.g., semantically oriented labels like *Process* or *State*, or under-specified structural arguments). Remote-edge transitions could have been handled by existing transitions with label modifications (e.g., affixes), rather than new operations. Crucially, multiple post-2014–2015 Semeval shared-task works (Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) extended transition-based graph parsers or adapted higher-order models (e.g., DAGParser [1], TurboParser [2]), none of which were used as baselines. Given these publicly available implementations [1,2], their absence undermines the comparative value of the proposed system.  

2.  **Phrased-Based Baseline**: The phrase-based experiments using *uparse* could have been validated against Fernández-González and Martins (2015), whose LCFRS parser achieves comparable performance to *uparse* (the reference for *uparse* is missing upon first mention).  

3.  **Evaluation Metrics**: UCCA’s abstract syntaxico-semantic structures differ significantly from standard SDP treebanks. Using metrics aligned with related shared tasks (e.g., Semeval SDP) would have enabled meaningful comparisons with existing systems. The lack of such benchmarks weakens the empirical justification, especially as the field increasingly relies on cross-system evaluation.  

4.  **Theoretical Framing**: The three proposed principles appear somewhat ad hoc and require clearer justification within a broader introduction. The paper attempts to argue for (a) a UCCA-specific parser, (b) a model suitable for semantic analysis, and (c) a parsable semantic scheme—yet this conflation risks confusion. The core contribution should be explicitly clarified: is it a parser for UCCA, a generalizable semantic parsing framework, or a validation of UCCA’s parseability?  

5.  **Additional Concerns**:  
    - The term "grounded semantic" must be defined before page 2.  
    - Why not compare against a constituent-tree parser (e.g., Berkeley Parser) with rich node labels and trace propagation?  
    - The surface dependency conversion is unnecessarily lossy; a richer conversion (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014) should have been used.  
    - Clarify "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units with no textual correspondent" with an example or reference.  
    - Address whether UCCA’s lack of explicit quantifier scope modeling (unlike MRS/DRT) limits its comparability to more syntax-oriented schemes (e.g., DM) or richer semantic frameworks (e.g., PCEDT). An error analysis would clarify why UCCA parsing appears "harder" than other schemes.  

Despite these issues, the work is timely and merits discussion at a conference like CONLL, given the field’s current trajectory. However, without addressing the methodological gaps—particularly the missing baselines and inadequate metrics—the paper’s contribution remains under-justified.
