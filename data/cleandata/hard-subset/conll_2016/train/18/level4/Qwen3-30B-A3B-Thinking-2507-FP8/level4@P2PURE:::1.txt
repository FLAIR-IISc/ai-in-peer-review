

This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors first outline three principles they argue should define a semantic annotation scheme: (i) graph-based structure (allowing a token/node to have multiple governors), (ii) non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures, e.g., syntactic coordinate phrases or lexical multiword expressions), and (iii) support for discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs + particles). Notably, these principles are not inherently semantic; they could apply to syntactic or other representation layers. The authors position their work within broad-coverage semantic parsing, introducing the UCCA annotation scheme before presenting three experimental sets: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing via UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution—experiments demonstrating their transition-based graph parser’s suitability for direct UCCA graph parsing.

While I find the work interesting, several methodological and empirical concerns must be addressed before publication. The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme like UCCA. However, UCCA graphs feature underspecified structural arguments and semantically oriented labels (e.g., Process, State), yet their transition sets do not specifically address these graph characteristics. For instance, transitions handling remote edges could have been managed by existing transitions with adjusted label sets (e.g., via affixes). Crucially, numerous graph-parsing approaches post-2014–2015 Semeval shared tasks (Almeda & Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) extend transition-based or higher-model parsers for graph parsing, and nothing precludes their application to UCCA. The specific feature template primarily anchors the model to UCCA (though less influential than count/unigram features). Given the availability of these graph parsers [1, 2], their absence as baselines or comparison points is problematic.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments using U*parse, validation with another parser (e.g., Fernandez-Gonzales & Martins, 2015, which produces LCFRS-like parses comparable to U*parse) would have strengthened the analysis. Additionally, UCCA’s abstract syntaxico-semantic representation necessitates using metrics from related shared tasks (e.g., SDP), yet the paper lacks comparisons to competing models and parsers, undermining its empirical contribution. Despite these issues, the work is timely for the field’s current trajectory and merits discussion at a conference like CoNLL.

Specific concerns:  
- Introduce "grounded semantic" before page 2, as it appears prematurely.  
- Why not test a constituent-tree baseline with rich node labels and trace propagation using the Berkeley parser?  
- Surface dependency conversion discards critical structural information; a richer conversion (e.g., Schlüter et al., 2014) should have been used.  
- Clarify or provide an example for "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units with no text correspondent."  
- Address the scheme’s incompatibility with quantifier scope modeling (unlike MRS/DRT), and clarify how UCCA’s abstractness compares to schemes like DM or PCEDT. An error analysis would contextualize the "harder" parsing scores.  
- The three principles (graph-based, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous elements) seem ad-hoc and require clearer justification within a broader introduction. The paper’s core aim—parsing UCCA, enabling semantic analysis, or validating a parsable scheme—is unclearly articulated, risking confusion.
