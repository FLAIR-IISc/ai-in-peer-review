

This paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism. While UCCA differs from CoNLL semantic dependency graphs by permitting "nonterminal" nodes (not corresponding to text tokens) and from AMRs by being "grounded" (with text tokens as explicit nodes), the core contribution—developing parsers for UCCA—is valid given existing UCCA resources. However, the paper's introduction and background section undermines this contribution by asserting UCCA is the *only* viable graph-based SR formalism, an argument that is unsubstantiated and unnecessary. A robust UCCA parser would be valuable regardless of this framing.

The paper's three proposed criteria for SR formalisms require careful scrutiny. The claim that "nonterminal nodes" enhance expressive capacity is unconvincing: while they may simplify head assignment in coordination, it remains unclear what information *requires* nonterminals over richer edge labels. Similarly, the emphasis on "discontinuity" as a key criterion is misplaced, as discontinuity is irrelevant in non-"grounded" formalisms like AMR. Most critically, the term "grounded" is problematic. It has been used inconsistently across NLP literature over the past decade (e.g., for lexicalization, anchoring, or token correspondence), risking confusion. The authors should replace it with a more precise term (e.g., "lexicalized" or "token-anchored") to avoid semantic ambiguity. These issues collectively indicate the introduction lacks the rigorous argumentation expected for a CONLL submission.

The parser itself appears technically sound, though I did not verify implementation details. However, the evaluation results are unimpressive. On "primary" edges (directly parsable by dependency parsers), MaltParser—a straightforward baseline—outperforms the authors' BSP parser (e.g., 75.2% vs. 72.8% F1). For "remote" edges (requiring non-local dependencies, which dependency parsers cannot directly handle), F1 scores are modest (e.g., 58.3% for BSP vs. 61.7% for MaltParser *after* applying tree approximation techniques). Crucially, the MaltParser comparison is flawed: the paper treats dependency trees as the target, but CoNLL 2014/2015 shared tasks (e.g., Agić et al., 2015) established robust methods for reconstructing deleted edges during graph-to-tree conversion. Incorporating such techniques (e.g., Agić’s edge-recovery approach) would likely boost MaltParser’s performance further, making the BSP parser’s marginal gains even less compelling. The paper’s failure to engage with this body of work weakens its evaluation.

The paper’s framing also overlooks key context. UCCA’s nonterminal nodes and grounding are not novel features; they represent design choices within a broader SR landscape. The claim that UCCA’s "grounded" nature inherently solves problems in non-grounded formalisms is unsupported. For instance, AMR’s success demonstrates that non-grounded representations can effectively model meaning without explicit token nodes. The paper’s insistence on UCCA’s uniqueness distracts from the actual technical contribution: a parser for an underexplored formalism. A more focused contribution would have been to present the parser, benchmark it fairly against relevant baselines (including tree approximation methods), and analyze UCCA’s specific challenges—without overclaiming its theoretical primacy.

In summary, while the parser development is a legitimate step forward for UCCA, the paper’s argumentative stance in the introduction and the suboptimal evaluation overshadow its technical merits. The critique of UCCA’s "uniqueness" is not only unsupported but actively detracts from the work’s value. The evaluation should have compared against state-of-the-art tree approximation techniques (e.g., Agić et al., 2015) rather than a naive dependency parser, and the terminology ("grounded") requires immediate revision. The core parser is functional but does not demonstrate sufficient novelty or advantage over adapted baselines. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite of the introduction to focus on UCCA’s specific parsing challenges, clarify terminology, and contextualize the work within existing SR research. As currently written, it risks misleading readers about UCCA’s theoretical position and the parser’s relative effectiveness.
