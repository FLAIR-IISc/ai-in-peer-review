

Summary: This paper introduces a transition-based parser for UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation), a semantic representation scheme that supports three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared semantic arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex structures like coordination), and discontinuous units (like phrasal verbs). The authors propose two complementary approaches: conversion-based parsing (mapping UCCA to dependency trees or constituency trees) and a direct transition-based parser (BSP) supporting all three properties.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. First systematic exploration of parsing UCCA structures, which is significant given that UCCA is the only semantic annotation scheme supporting all three structural properties.
2. Demonstration that transition-based methods can effectively handle broad-coverage semantic parsing, with nearly 70% labeled F-score for the best parser.
3. Development of a novel parser (BSP) that directly supports all three required structural properties, going beyond existing conversion-based approaches.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a clear and thorough analysis of the structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors effectively demonstrate that no existing parser supports the combination of all three properties.
2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both conversion-based approaches and the direct parsing approach, with results showing promising performance on UCCA-annotated corpora.
3. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of semantic parsing by demonstrating that transition-based methods can be adapted to handle complex semantic structures, paving the way for future work in this area.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim is overstated. While the authors claim to be "first in proposing techniques for BSS parsing," there are existing graph parsing approaches (e.g., from SemEval 2014-2015) that could have been adapted to this task. The paper fails to acknowledge or compare with these approaches, making the novelty claim questionable.
2. The evaluation lacks meaningful comparisons with existing graph parsing methods. The authors use MaltParser and UPARSE as baselines, but these are not designed for graph parsing. The paper could have compared with approaches like those from Ribeyre et al. (2014-2015) or Almeda and Martins (2014, 2015), which specifically address graph parsing.
3. The paper fails to adopt standard evaluation metrics used in related semantic parsing tasks. The authors introduce custom metrics instead of using established measures from SemEval shared tasks, making it difficult to compare results with the broader field.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does your parser specifically address the semantic specificity of UCCA, beyond just handling graph structures? The paper mentions UCCA's semantic labels (e.g., Process, State), but it's unclear how these are integrated into the parsing process.
2. Why did you not compare with existing graph parsing approaches (e.g., those from SemEval 2014-2015) that could have been adapted to UCCA data? Given that the paper claims to be the first to propose techniques for BSS parsing, this comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.
3. The paper mentions that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text" (Section 2). Could you provide a specific example of such a unit and explain how it affects parsing?

Additional comments:
- The paper uses "grounded semantic" without definition before page 2. Please define this term early in the paper.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees appears to lose significant information (e.g., non-terminal nodes). A richer conversion approach (like the one from Schluter et al., 2014) would be more appropriate for this task.
- The paper could have explored using the Berkeley parser with rich node labels as a baseline for the constituency-based approach, which would have provided a more meaningful comparison.
- The three structural properties identified (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) are valuable, but the paper could better justify why these specific properties are necessary for broad-coverage semantic parsing. A more detailed discussion of how these properties relate to linguistic phenomena would strengthen the contribution.
