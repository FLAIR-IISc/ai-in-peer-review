- Valid contribution: first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, but introduction overclaims UCCA as the only viable graph-based SR formalism.  
- The claim that UCCA’s features make it uniquely viable is unsubstantiated and unnecessary.  
- The three proposed SR criteria need scrutiny; the argument that nonterminal nodes increase expressiveness is unconvincing.  
- It is unclear whether nonterminals are required versus using richer edge labels for coordination and related phenomena.  
- Emphasizing “discontinuity” is misplaced because it’s irrelevant for non-grounded formalisms like AMR.  
- The term “grounded” is used inconsistently in the literature and should be replaced with a precise term (e.g., “lexicalized” or “token-anchored”).  
- The parser seems technically sound, but the evaluation results are unimpressive on “primary” edges (MaltParser outperforms BSP).  
- “Remote” edge scores are modest and MaltParser with tree-approximation methods can equal or beat BSP.  
- The baseline comparison is flawed: the paper fails to incorporate established graph-to-tree edge-recovery techniques (e.g., Agić et al., 2015).  
- The paper overlooks broader SR context—UCCA’s nonterminals and grounding are design choices, not novel solutions, and AMR’s success counters claims that grounding is necessary.  
- Overall: argumentative framing, terminology issues, and weak baselines overshadow the technical contribution; rewrite to focus on the parser, fair comparisons, and clearer terminology.
