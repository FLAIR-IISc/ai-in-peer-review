

This paper introduces a transition-based parsing approach for UCCA, a semantic annotation scheme supporting three structural properties: multiple parents (allowing shared semantic arguments), non-terminal nodes (representing complex units like coordination and multi-word expressions), and discontinuous units (capturing structures like phrasal verbs). The authors present two complementary strategies: conversion-based parsing (mapping UCCA structures to constituency or dependency trees and using existing parsers) and direct parsing using their novel Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), which supports all three properties. The main contributions are: (1) the first parser for UCCA that directly supports all three structural properties, (2) a thorough analysis of conversion-based approaches to assess the feasibility of adapting existing technology, and (3) empirical validation demonstrating nearly 70% labeled F-score on UCCA-annotated corpora.

Strengths: The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a gap in semantic parsing technology for structures that require all three structural properties, which no existing parser supports. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including both in-domain and out-of-domain testing, and the authors provide detailed ablation studies of their feature sets. The work is well-positioned within the broader context of semantic parsing research, clearly differentiating UCCA from other semantic representation schemes like AMR and SDP.

Weaknesses: The claim of being "first to propose a parser" for a semantically-oriented scheme is problematic, as similar graph parsing techniques have been developed since the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (e.g., Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015; Almeida and Martins, 2014-2015). The authors fail to compare their approach with these existing graph parsers, which could have been adapted to UCCA with minimal effort. The conversion to dependency trees is particularly problematic as it loses substantial information, and a more sophisticated conversion (like that used in SemEval SDP) would have been more appropriate. The evaluation lacks comparison with standard metrics used in related semantic parsing tasks, making it difficult to assess the significance of their results relative to the field.

Questions to Authors: Please clarify why the conversion to dependency trees was used instead of a more sophisticated conversion method that preserves more information. How does UCCA compare to representations like MRS and DRT in terms of modeling quantifier scope and other semantic phenomena, and how does this affect parsing complexity? Provide an example of UCCA's implicit units (mentioned in Section 2) to clarify their nature and significance. Why not consider using the Berkeley parser with rich node labels as a baseline for the constituency parsing experiments?
