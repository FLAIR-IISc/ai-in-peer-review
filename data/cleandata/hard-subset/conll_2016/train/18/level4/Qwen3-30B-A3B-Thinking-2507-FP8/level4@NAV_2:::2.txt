

Summary: This paper presents the first semantic parser for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation that supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The main contributions are: (1) the development of a transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs UCCA structures while supporting all three structural properties, and (2) a conversion-based approach that maps UCCA into related formalisms (dependency trees and constituency trees) for parsing with existing technology. The authors evaluate their methods on UCCA-annotated corpora, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score for the best parser.

Strengths: The paper addresses a significant gap in semantic parsing literature by providing the first parser for UCCA, which has been used for annotation but lacked a dedicated parser. The conversion-based approach demonstrates practical utility, showing that existing parsers can be adapted to handle UCCA with reasonable performance. The work provides a concrete evaluation framework for broad-coverage semantic parsing, and the availability of the parsers and converters upon publication will facilitate further research.

Weaknesses: The introduction unnecessarily claims UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation worth studying, which is inaccurate and undermines the paper's contribution. The justification for the three structural properties is weak, particularly regarding the necessity of non-terminal nodes (which could be represented with better edge labels rather than requiring actual non-terminal nodes). The "grounded" terminology is confusing and not clearly distinguished from other semantic representation approaches, potentially causing confusion in the field. The evaluation results are not impressive, with the BSP parser performing worse than standard dependency parsers (MaltParser) on primary edges (62.4% vs. 63.9-73.2%), and remote edge scores being low (15.3% for BSP). The conversion approach to dependency trees has been studied before in the context of CoNLL semantic dependency parsing shared tasks, and the paper does not adequately address how their approach differs from or improves upon existing methods for tree approximation.

Questions to Authors: Could the authors clarify why non-terminal nodes are necessary for representing coordinated structures, rather than using more informative edge labels to encode the same information? How does the "grounded" representation provide advantages over AMR-style representations that the authors have not fully demonstrated in the paper?
