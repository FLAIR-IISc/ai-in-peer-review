

The paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a specific graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs may include "nonterminal" nodes that do not correspond to surface words. Unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded," meaning text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors propose several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that constructs UCCA parses directly, and evaluate them.

While developing a semantic parser for UCCA is reasonable given its existence and annotated data, the introduction and background section contains problematic assertions. Specifically, it incorrectly implies that UCCA is the only graph-based SR formalism worth studying, which is unnecessary and detracts from the contribution. A high-quality UCCA parser would constitute a valuable contribution independently.

The three criteria for SR formalisms proposed in the introduction are also questionable. The utility of "nonterminal nodes" in enhancing expressive capacity remains unclear: while coordinating structures may require head selection decisions, it is not evident that nonterminals are indispensable—more informative edge labels could potentially encode the same information. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant for non-"grounded" SRs. The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style formalisms are not convincingly established. The term "grounded" has been used ambiguously across multiple works in the past decade; the authors should adopt a more precise alternative (e.g., "anchored" or "lexicalized"). Consequently, the entire introductory section requires substantial revision for greater precision and rigor.

The parser itself appears sound, though the technical details are not evaluated here. However, the evaluation results are unimpressive. On "primary" edges, a basic MaltParser outperforms the presented BSP parser, and "remote" edge F-scores (which dependency-tree parsers like Malt cannot directly compute) are modest. The conversion of dependency graphs to trees has been extensively studied under "tree approximations" in CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks (without nonterminal nodes). Prior work, such as Agic et al. (2015) on semantic dependency graph parsing, details methods for reconstructing edges lost during graph-to-tree conversion. Incorporating such techniques could further improve MaltParser (and LSTM-based MaltParser) performance, thereby diminishing the relative novelty and strength of the BSP parser.
