

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces the first parsers for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation formalism that supports three structural properties: multiple parents (to represent shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (to represent multi-word units without clear heads), and discontinuous units (to represent phrasal expressions). The authors propose two approaches: (1) converting UCCA structures to dependency trees or constituency trees and using existing parsers on the converted representations, and (2) a novel transition-based parser, Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), that directly constructs UCCA graphs. Their main contribution is the first parsing system for UCCA that supports all three structural properties simultaneously.

**Strengths**  
The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a clear gap: UCCA is the only semantic representation supporting all three structural properties, but no parser exists for it. The authors' conversion-based approach demonstrates that existing parsers can handle UCCA's requirements with reasonable effectiveness, while the BSP parser directly supports all necessary features. The experimental evaluation on UCCA-annotated corpora provides a meaningful benchmark for future work in this space. The authors also make all code and data publicly available, which is highly commendable.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Overstated claims about UCCA's uniqueness**: The introduction incorrectly positions UCCA as the only reasonable graph-based semantic representation to study. The paper presents UCCA as the sole formalism supporting all three structural properties, but this claim is unsubstantiated. Other semantic representations (e.g., certain variants of AMR or SDP) might also support these properties with minor modifications, though the authors don't consider them. This framing unnecessarily narrows the paper's scope and relevance.

2. **Unconvincing justification for "non-terminal nodes"**: The paper asserts that non-terminal nodes are necessary for representing multi-word units without clear heads (e.g., coordination structures), but fails to demonstrate how they provide expressive capacity that couldn't be achieved with richer edge labels. For instance, instead of creating a non-terminal node for "John and Mary," the parser could simply use an edge label indicating coordination. The paper doesn't address why this alternative isn't sufficient, weakening the argument for this structural requirement.

3. **Misleading use of "grounded"**: The paper claims UCCA is "grounded" because text tokens appear as nodes, but this usage conflicts with established terminology in computational semantics (where "grounded" often refers to connections with external entities or real-world knowledge). The term is confusing and inconsistent with prior work. The authors should adopt clearer terminology (e.g., "lexicalized" or "text-anchored") to avoid confusion.

4. **Underwhelming evaluation results**: The BSP parser achieves only 62.4% labeled F1 on primary edges, which is surpassed by MaltParser (63.4–73.2%) on the same task. The remote edge results (15.3% F1) are particularly low, suggesting the parser struggles with the most challenging aspect of UCCA. The authors' claim that BSP "obtains comparable F-scores to MaltParser" is misleading since MaltParser (with conversion) significantly outperforms BSP on primary edges. The paper fails to address why a direct parser would be preferable to the conversion-based approach when the latter achieves better results.

5. **Inadequate engagement with prior work**: The conversion approach is not novel; the paper cites "tree approximations" from CoNLL 2014/2015 shared tasks (e.g., Agić et al., 2015), but doesn't discuss how their method differs or improves upon existing reconstruction techniques. Incorporating such methods could substantially improve the conversion-based approach, making the BSP's advantages even less apparent.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper claims UCCA is the "only" semantic representation supporting all three structural properties. What specific examples of other formalisms (e.g., AMR variants, SDP) could support these properties without modification, and why aren't they considered?

2. How do non-terminal nodes provide expressive capacity that cannot be achieved through richer edge labels? Can you provide a concrete example where edge labels alone would fail to represent a UCCA structure?

3. The authors use "grounded" to mean text tokens appear as nodes in the representation. Given the term's established usage in computational semantics, why not adopt a different term (e.g., "lexicalized" or "text-anchored") to avoid confusion?

4. The BSP parser underperforms MaltParser on primary edges. What specific limitations of BSP prevent it from matching MaltParser's performance, and how could these be addressed?
