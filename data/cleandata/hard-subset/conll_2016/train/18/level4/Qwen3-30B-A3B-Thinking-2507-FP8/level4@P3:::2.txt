

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents the first semantic parser designed for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a grounded semantic representation formalism. The authors identify three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex semantic units like coordination), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). While existing parsers support some of these properties, none support all three simultaneously, and UCCA is the only formalism that fully supports them. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) it develops conversion procedures to map UCCA structures to dependency trees and constituency trees, enabling the use of existing parsers; and (2) it introduces the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), a novel transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA graphs. The authors evaluate these approaches on UCCA-annotated corpora, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score on primary edges.

**Strengths**

1. **First parser for UCCA**: This is the first work to develop a parser for UCCA, a semantic representation formalism that supports all three structural properties. Given UCCA's growing adoption and its cross-linguistic applicability, this contribution fills a critical gap in semantic parsing literature. The authors' open-sourcing of all parsers and converters will enable further research on UCCA parsing.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation on UCCA data**: The paper provides the first systematic evaluation of semantic parsing for UCCA, a formalism with limited existing resources. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of parsing UCCA structures with transition-based methods, showing that conversion-based approaches can achieve reasonable performance (69.2% F-score with LSTM-based conversion). This evaluation establishes a baseline for future UCCA parsing research.

3. **Clear presentation of conversion procedures**: The paper provides detailed algorithms for converting between UCCA and other formalisms (constituency trees, dependency trees), which is valuable for researchers working with different semantic representations. The authors' analysis of the trade-offs between conversion-based and direct parsing approaches is particularly useful.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Overstated uniqueness of UCCA**: The paper's introduction unnecessarily claims UCCA is the only formalism supporting all three structural properties, which is inaccurate. For example, Broad-coverage Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) supports multiple parents and discontinuous units but uses bi-lexical dependencies (disallowing non-terminal nodes). This overstatement weakens the paper's argument for UCCA's necessity and creates an unnecessary debate about UCCA's uniqueness rather than focusing on the parsing contribution.

2. **Questionable justification of structural properties**: The authors' claim that non-terminal nodes are essential is not well-supported. It's unclear what expressive capacity they provide that couldn't be achieved with more informative edge labels or other structural features. The paper doesn't demonstrate how non-terminal nodes improve parsing performance or semantic representation quality compared to alternatives. This lack of justification makes the authors' structural criteria seem arbitrary rather than principled.

3. **Underwhelming performance results**: The BSP parser achieves 62.4% F-score on primary edges, significantly lower than the LSTM-based parser (73.2%) used in the conversion approach. This suggests that the direct parsing approach isn't superior to conversion-based methods, undermining the paper's main contribution. The authors also fail to address how their conversion-based methods could be improved by incorporating recent work on tree approximations (e.g., Agić et al., 2015), which could boost conversion-based performance further.

4. **Lack of clear advantages for UCCA**: The paper claims UCCA is "grounded" (text tokens appear as nodes), but doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this benefits semantic parsing compared to non-grounded representations like AMR. The authors use "grounded" without clear definition or justification, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that grounded representations lead to better parsing performance or more useful semantic representations.

**Questions to Authors**

1. The paper claims UCCA is the only formalism supporting all three structural properties. Can you clarify how SDP (which supports multiple parents and discontinuities) differs from UCCA in terms of the "non-terminal nodes" property, and why SDP can't be considered to support all three properties?

2. The paper doesn't demonstrate why non-terminal nodes are necessary for representing coordination structures. Could you provide a specific example where representing a coordination structure as a single node (with appropriate edge labels) would fail, while using a non-terminal node would succeed?

3. Given that the conversion-based LSTM parser outperforms the direct BSP parser, what specific advantages does the BSP parser offer that justify its development, particularly considering the additional complexity of handling remote edges and non-terminal nodes?

4. The paper uses the term "grounded" to describe UCCA, but this term has been used inconsistently in the literature. Could you clarify your definition of "grounded" and explain how it differs from other uses of the term in semantic parsing?
