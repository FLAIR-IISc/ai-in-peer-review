- Paper introduces a transition-based graph parser for semantico-cognitive annotation schemes, instantiated on UCCA.  
- Authors propose three core principles for semantic annotation: graph-based representation, non-terminal nodes, and support for discontinuous elements.  
- The three principles are claimed to be framework-agnostic and applicable beyond semantics.  
- Paper situates itself in broad-coverage semantic parsing and describes UCCA before presenting three experiment sets: phrase-based (Stanford + UCCA->constituency), dependency (UCCA->dependency), and direct UCCA graph parsing with their parser.  
- Major methodological concern: the claim to be first is questionable—post-2014/2015 SemEval works (Almeida & Martins; Ribeyre et al.) already extended transition-based/higher-order graph parsers and should be used as baselines.  
- The proposed transition system does not specifically address UCCA’s graph characteristics; remote edges might be handled by existing transitions plus label modifications rather than novel transitions.  
- Omission of relevant baselines and references (e.g., DAGParser, TurboParser SemEval data) weakens empirical evaluation.  
- Phrase-based experiments using U*parse lack validation against comparable LCFRS-like parsers (e.g., Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins 2015); U*parse reference is missing.  
- Given UCCA’s higher abstraction, comparisons using shared-task metrics and benchmarks against established parsers are essential; their absence undermines significance.  
- Despite issues, the work is timely and merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.  
- Paper should introduce the term “grounded semantic” earlier (before page 2) as it’s used prematurely.  
- Recommend trying a constituent-tree baseline with rich node labels (e.g., Berkeley Parser) as an additional comparison.  
- UCCA-to-dependency conversion used is suboptimal and loses information; a richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014) should be employed.  
- Clarify or exemplify “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units without textual correspondents.”  
- Address whether UCCA’s lack of quantifier scope modeling limits comparability to schemes like MRS/DRT; quantify abstraction level (e.g., vs. PCEDT) and perform error analysis to assess “parsability.”  
- The three principles/readable framing appear ad-hoc; clarify whether the contribution is a UCCA-specific parser, a general model for semantic analysis, or a demonstration that the scheme is parsable.
