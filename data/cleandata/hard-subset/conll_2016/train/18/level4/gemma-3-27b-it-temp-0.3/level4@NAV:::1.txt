This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the complex representations found in the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors begin by outlining characteristics they believe are essential for a semantic annotation scheme: (i) graph-based structure allowing multiple governors per token, (ii) non-terminal nodes representing complex structures (syntactic and lexical), and (iii) the ability to represent discontinuous elements. Notably, these principles aren’t inherently semantic and could apply to syntactic or other representational frameworks. The work is situated within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and the specific UCCA scheme. The authors then present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing utilizing the Stanford parser and a UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing with a UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution – experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for directly parsing UCCA graphs.

I find this work interesting, but several methodological and empirical concerns need addressing before publication. 

The authors claim novelty in proposing a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. While technically true, the UCCA scheme consists of graphs with varying degrees of structural under-specification and semantic labels, yet the proposed transition system doesn’t specifically address these characteristics. Even handling remote edges could potentially be achieved through existing transitions with modifications to the label set (e.g., using affixes). Considering this is a graph parsing problem, numerous works following the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al, 2014-2015) have extended or adapted transition-based graph parsers, and their application to this dataset should be considered. The model’s anchoring to the UCCA scheme primarily stems from the specific feature template used, though less significantly than count and unigram features. Given the availability of these existing graph parsers [1, 2], their use as baselines or for comparison is unclear.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments with Uparse, validation against another parser like that of Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015) – capable of producing comparable LCFRS-like parsing – would be beneficial (the reference for Uparse was missing upon its initial introduction). 

Given that the UCCA scheme offers a more abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structures compared to most SDP treebanks, employing the same evaluation metrics used in related shared tasks is crucial. The field currently features numerous competing systems, models, and datasets, and the lack of comparison points weakens the overall impact of this work. Despite these concerns, the work is interesting and timely, and I believe it would be valuable to discuss at a conference like ConLL.

**Specific points for consideration (in no particular order):**

*   Please introduce the term "grounded semantic" before page 2, as it is used prior to its definition.
*   Why wasn’t an approach involving constituent trees with rich node labels and trace propagation, followed by training/parsing with the Berkeley parser, explored as a baseline?
*   The conversion to surface dependency trees appears to lose valuable information; a richer conversion, such as that proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would be more appropriate.
*   Please elaborate on the statement that “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text,” providing a reference or example.
*   The mention of alternative representations like MRS and DRT raises the question of whether the UCCA scheme adequately models quantifier scope information, potentially limiting its comparability to other schemes. While more abstract than DM, how does its level of underspecification compare to the semantic level of PCEDT? A quantitative assessment of the scheme’s informativeness and parsability would be valuable, potentially through error analysis given the reported scores.
*   The three principles outlined by the authors appear somewhat ad-hoc and would benefit from a broader, clearer introduction. It’s unclear whether the primary goal is a parser for UCCA, a model for semantic analysis, or a parsable semantic-oriented scheme. The current presentation is dense and potentially confusing.
