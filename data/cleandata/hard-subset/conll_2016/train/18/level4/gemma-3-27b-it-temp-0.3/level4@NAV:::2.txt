The paper introduces novel broad-coverage semantic parsers for Universal Compositional Communication Analysis (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation. UCCA differs from other formalisms like CoNLL semantic dependency graphs by allowing nonterminal nodes—nodes not directly corresponding to input words—and from Abstract Meaning Representations (AMRs) by being “grounded,” meaning text tokens are represented as nodes within the semantic structure. The authors explore various parsing approaches, including a transition-based parser designed to directly generate UCCA parses, and present an evaluation of these methods.

While developing a semantic parser for UCCA is a logical step given the existence of the formalism and its associated data, the paper’s introduction and background section present a potentially problematic framing. The authors appear to position UCCA as the sole sensible graph-based semantic representation (SR) for study, which feels both unnecessary and weakens the argument; a successful UCCA parser would be a valuable contribution in its own right. 

I have reservations regarding the three criteria the authors establish for evaluating semantic representation formalisms. The purported benefit of “nonterminal nodes” is not fully convincing. While resolving head selection in coordinated structures can be challenging, it’s unclear what information *requires* a nonterminal node that couldn’t be captured through more informative edge labeling.  Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant for SRs that are not “grounded.” The advantages of “grounded” representations over AMR-style representations were not clearly demonstrated.  I also suggest the authors reconsider the term “grounded,” given its varied usage in semantic literature; alternatives like “anchored” or “lexicalized” might be more precise.  Therefore, the introductory section requires substantial revision and more nuanced argumentation.

The parser itself appears sound, though I haven’t verified implementation details. However, the reported evaluation results are not particularly compelling. The baseline MaltParser achieves better performance on “primary” edges than the presented Bilingual Shift-Reduce Parser (BSP).  Moreover, the f-scores on “remote” edges—those not directly computable by a standard dependency-tree parser—remain modest.  The conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees, relevant to this evaluation, has been extensively studied as “tree approximations” within the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing.  Existing work, such as Agic et al. (2015), details methods for reconstructing edges lost during graph-to-tree conversion. Incorporating such techniques could likely improve the MaltParser’s (and LSTM-based MaltParser’s) performance, further diminishing the relative strength of the BSP parser.
