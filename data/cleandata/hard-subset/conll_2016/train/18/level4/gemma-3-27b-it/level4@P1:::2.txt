The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain non-terminal nodes which do not represent words in the string. Unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are grounded, meaning that the text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present a number of parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

Given that UCCA and UCCA-annotated data exist, developing a semantic parser for UCCA seems a reasonable endeavor. However, the introduction and background section feel somewhat argumentative, suggesting that UCCA is the only sensible graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism to study. This framing doesn’t fully resonate, and also feels unnecessary – a successful UCCA parser could be a valuable contribution in its own right.

I don’t entirely agree with the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms the authors outline in the introduction. For instance, the benefit of “non-terminal nodes” in terms of expressive capacity isn't clear. While determining the head of a coordinated structure can be inconvenient, it’s not obvious what information could only be represented with a non-terminal node and not, for example, with more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity doesn't even apply to SRs that are not grounded. The advantages of grounded representations over AMR-style ones weren’t fully elucidated. I also suggest the authors consider alternative terminology to “grounded,” as this term has been used with various meanings in semantics in recent years ("anchored" or "lexicalized" might be alternatives). Therefore, I believe the introductory section of the paper needs more careful phrasing and argumentation.

The parser itself appears sound, although I haven’t checked the details exhaustively. However, the evaluation results aren’t particularly impressive. On primary edges, a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the presented BSP parser, and the F-scores on remote edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot directly compute) remain modest. Moreover, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees has been extensively studied as “tree approximations” within the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing – albeit without non-terminal nodes. Several researchers have proposed methods for reconstructing edges deleted during graph-to-tree conversion; for instance, Agic et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” thoroughly discusses the challenges involved in this reconstruction. Incorporating such methods could likely improve the F-score of the MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser!), potentially diminishing the distinctiveness of the BSP parser.
