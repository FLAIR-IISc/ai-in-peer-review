- Paper introduces the first broad-coverage UCCA semantic parsers and evaluates several methods, including a transition-based parser.  
- UCCA is described as allowing nonterminal nodes and being “grounded” (text tokens as nodes), distinguishing it from AMR and dependency graphs.  
- Authors’ framing of UCCA as uniquely valuable is problematic and unnecessary; a UCCA parser is a worthwhile contribution without grand claims.  
- The claimed benefit of nonterminal nodes is unconvincing; informative edge labels might capture the same information.  
- The discontinuity criterion is irrelevant for non-grounded semantic representations.  
- Claimed advantages of a “grounded” representation over AMR are not clearly demonstrated.  
- The term “grounded” is ambiguous; the reviewer suggests alternatives like “anchored” or “lexicalized.”  
- Recommend substantial revision of the introduction with more nuanced argumentation.  
- Parsing methods appear sound, though the reviewer did not deeply inspect implementation details.  
- Evaluation results are weak: MaltParser outperforms the proposed Beam Search Parser on primary edges.  
- F1 scores for remote edges are relatively low, indicating difficulty with non-treeable structures.  
- Prior work on tree approximations (e.g., Agic et al. 2015) could improve reconstruction of deleted edges and boost baseline performance; integrating such techniques (and LSTM-enhanced MaltParser) may reduce BSP’s advantage.
