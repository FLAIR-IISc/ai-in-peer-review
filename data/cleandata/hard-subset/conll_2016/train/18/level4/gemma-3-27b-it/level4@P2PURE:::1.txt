This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations found in the UCCA corpora, which utilize a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors begin by outlining characteristics they believe are essential for a semantic annotation scheme: (i) graph-based structure allowing multiple governors per token, (ii) the use of non-terminal nodes representing complex syntactic or lexical structures (coordinate phrases, multiword expressions), and (iii) the ability to represent discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs and particles). Notably, these principles are not inherently semantic and could apply equally well to syntactic or other representational layers. The authors then situate their work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce the specific annotation scheme they employ, UCCA. They subsequently present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing leveraging the Stanford parser and a conversion from UCCA to constituency, (ii) dependency parsing utilizing a UCCA to dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution – experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for directly parsing UCCA graphs.

I found this work interesting, but several methodological and empirical concerns must be addressed before publication. 

The authors claim to present the first parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. While this may be the case, the UCCA scheme consists of graphs with varying degrees of structural underspecification and semantically-oriented labels (Process, State), yet their proposed transition system does not specifically address these characteristics. Even transitions handling remote edges could potentially be accommodated by existing transitions given a carefully designed label set (e.g., through the use of affixes). Focusing on graph parsing, several works extending transition-based graph parsers, originating from the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al, 2014-2015), could be adapted to this dataset. The model’s adaptation to UCCA is primarily driven by the specific feature template employed, which appears less influential than count and unigram features. Given the availability of these existing graph parsers [1,2], it is unclear why they were not used as a baseline for comparison. 

Regarding the phrase-based experiments with Uparse, validation against another parser like that of Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015) – capable of producing comparable LCFRS-like parsing – would be beneficial. The reference to this parser is missing where Uparse is initially introduced. 

Because the UCCA scheme represents a more abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, employing the same evaluation metrics used in related shared tasks would be appropriate. The field currently features numerous competing systems, models, and datasets, and the lack of comparison points with existing work significantly weakens this contribution. Despite these concerns, I believe the work is valuable and warrants discussion at a conference like ConLL, particularly given the current need to explore new directions in semantic parsing.

Note (in random order):

- Please introduce the term “grounded semantic” before page 2, as it is used prior to its definition.
- Why was a constituent-tree approach with rich node labels and trace propagation, followed by parsing with the Berkeley parser, not considered as a baseline?
- The conversion to surface dependency trees appears unnecessarily lossy; a richer conversion, such as that proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would be preferable.
- Please elaborate on the statement “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text” or provide a reference or example.
- The mention of alternative representations like MRS and DRT raises the question of whether the UCCA scheme adequately models quantifier scope information, rendering it more comparable to syntax-oriented schemes. While more abstract than DM and potentially more underspecified than the semantic level of PCEDT, its actual informativeness and parsability require further investigation. An error analysis would be particularly useful, given the reported scores suggest a challenging task.
- As previously noted, the three principles outlined seem somewhat ad hoc and would benefit from a broader and clearer introduction. Is the goal a parser specifically for UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis, or a demonstration of the parsability of a semantic-oriented scheme? The current presentation attempts to address all these points simultaneously in a dense manner, bordering on confusion.
