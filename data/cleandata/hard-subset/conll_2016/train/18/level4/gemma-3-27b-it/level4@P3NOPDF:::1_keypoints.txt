- Presents a transition-based graph parser for UCCA but overclaims novelty as “first” for semantically-oriented schemes.  
- Fails to justify why existing graph/parsing systems (e.g., Almeda & Martins, Ribeyre et al., DAGParser, TurboParser) were not used as baselines or comparisons.  
- The transition system does not clearly address UCCA-specific challenges (e.g., handling of remote edges) that could be handled by existing approaches via label extensions.  
- The model appears driven more by feature templates (counts, unigrams) than by genuine modelling of UCCA structure.  
- Phrase-based experiments with Uparse lack validation against comparable constituency/LF parsers (e.g., Fernández-González & Martins 2015) and Uparse was not properly referenced.  
- Dependency conversion likely loses important information; a richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al. 2014 SemEval SDP) should be considered.  
- The paper lacks comparisons to other systems, models, datasets, and shared-task metrics (SDP metrics), weakening empirical claims.  
- Need to introduce the term “grounded semantic” earlier (before page 2) since it appears undefined initially.  
- Why not evaluate a baseline using richly labeled constituent trees parsed with the Berkeley parser and trace propagation?  
- The paper should elaborate on “implicit units with no correspondent in the text” with examples or references.  
- Raises the issue that UCCA omits phenomena like quantifier scope (unlike MRS/DRT), questioning its claimed abstractness and comparability to DM or PCEDT.  
- Recommend quantifying UCCA’s informativeness/parsability (e.g., via error analysis) to support claims about abstraction and utility.  
- The three principles motivating UCCA seem ad-hoc and need a clearer, broader justification.  
- The paper tries to be a parser, a semantic model, and a parsable annotation scheme simultaneously, leading to density and unclear goals.  
- Despite shortcomings, the topic is timely and relevant to semantic parsing and could be suitable for a venue like CoNLL after addressing methodological and empirical issues.
