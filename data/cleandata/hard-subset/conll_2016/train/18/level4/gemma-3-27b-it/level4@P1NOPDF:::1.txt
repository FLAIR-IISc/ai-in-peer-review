This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, as exemplified by the UCCA corpora. The authors begin by outlining what they believe constitutes a semantic-based annotation scheme: (i) a graph-based structure (allowing a token/node to have multiple governors), (ii) non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures – syntactic, such as coordinate phrases, and lexical, such as multiword expressions), and (iii) the ability to represent discontinuous elements (e.g., Verbs+particles). Interestingly, these principles are not inherently tied to semantics and could also be applied to syntactic or other representational layers. The authors then position their work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce their annotation scheme of choice, UCCA. They subsequently present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and a UCCA to constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing using a UCCA to dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution – experiments demonstrating that their transition-based graph parser is suitable for the direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

I found this work interesting, but several concerns regarding the methodology and empirical justification need addressing before publication.

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme such as theirs. While this is likely true, the scheme itself consists of graphs with varying degrees of structural underspecification and semantically-oriented labels (Process, state). However, the proposed transition set does not specifically address these characteristics. Even transitions related to remote edges could potentially be handled by existing transitions, given a difference in the label set itself (e.g., adding an affix). Focusing on graph parsing, numerous works following the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al, 2014-2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models, which could plausibly be applied to this dataset. The model’s anchoring to this specific scheme is largely due to the feature template used, though its influence appears less significant than count and unigram features. Given the availability of these graph parsers [1, 2], I question why they were not used as a baseline for comparison.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments using Uparse, validation against another parser, such as the one from Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015) – which can produce LCFRS-like parsing comparable to Uparse – would be beneficial (the reference to this parser is missing where Uparse was first introduced).

Because this scheme supports a more abstract view of syntactico-semantic structures than most Semantic Dependency (SDP) treebanks, it would have been important to employ the same metrics used in related shared tasks. The field currently sees competition between many systems, models, and datasets, and the lack of comparison points to existing models and parsers is a significant weakness of this work. Nevertheless, I find it interesting, and given the current crucial juncture in determining the future direction of the field, I believe this paper merits discussion at a conference like ConLL.

Note (in random order):

- Please introduce the term "grounded semantic" before page 2, as you use it before that point.
- Why didn’t you attempt to use a constituent-tree structure with rich node labels and propagated traces, and then train/parse with the Berkeley parser? This could have served as a strong baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees seems unnecessary, as it results in a loss of information. A richer conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would have been more appropriate.
- Could you elaborate on the statement that “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text,” or provide a reference or example?
- You mention other representations like MRS and DRT, which raises the question of whether your scheme allows for modeling quantifier scope information. Therefore, its comparability to other, more syntax-oriented schemes is limited. It is indeed more abstract than DM and potentially more underspecified than the semantic level of the PCEDT, but to what extent? How informative is this scheme, and how truly “parsable” is it? Your reported scores suggest it is challenging, and an error analysis would be valuable.
- As previously mentioned, the three principles you defined could apply to various scenarios and appear somewhat ad-hoc. They would benefit from being placed within a broader (and clearer) introduction. What are you arguing for: a parser that can parse UCCA? A model suitable for semantic analysis? Or a semantically-oriented scheme that is actually parsable? You attempt to convey all of these aspects in a very dense manner, which borders on being confusing.
