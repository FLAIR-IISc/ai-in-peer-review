This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors begin by outlining what they believe constitutes a comprehensive semantic-based annotation scheme: (i) graph-based structure (allowing tokens/nodes to have multiple governors), (2) non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures – syntactic – such as coordinate phrases, and lexical structures such as multiword expressions), and (3) support for discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs and particles). Notably, these principles are not necessarily tied to a semantic framework and could also apply to syntax or other representation layers. The authors then position their work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce their annotation scheme of choice, UCCA. They present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using a pre-existing parser and a UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing using a UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) the core of their proposal – experiments demonstrating that their transition-based graph parser is suitable for directly parsing UCCA graphs.

I find this work interesting, but several concerns regarding methodology and empirical justification must be addressed before publication.

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. While technically true, the proposed parser’s transitions do not fundamentally address the specificities of UCCA’s graph structure and semantically oriented labels. The transitions addressing remote edges could potentially be handled by existing transitions with a modified label set (e.g., using affixes). When considering graph parsing, numerous works following the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (e.g., Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models. It’s unclear why these existing parsers weren't utilized as baselines or sources for comparison. The reliance on a specific feature template is the primary factor anchoring the model to the scheme, though its influence appears less significant than that of count features and unigrams. Given the availability of these graph parsers [1, 2], their omission is notable.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments using UPARSE, validation against another parser, such as that of Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015) – capable of producing similarly high-quality LCFRS-like parsing – would be beneficial.

Because UCCA supports a more abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, employing the same metrics used in related shared tasks would be appropriate. Given the competitive landscape of systems, models, and datasets in this field, the lack of comparison points with other models and parsers is detrimental to the work as a whole. Nevertheless, given the current state of research and the need for exploration, this paper warrants discussion at a conference like CoNLL.

Further notes, in random order:

- Please define “grounded semantic” before its first use on page 2.
- Why wasn’t an approach involving constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, trained with the Berkeley parser, explored? It could have served as a valuable baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees seems of limited utility, as it results in significant information loss. A richer conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would be preferable.
- Can you elaborate on “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text” or provide a reference or example?
- The mention of other representations like MRS and DRT raises the question of whether your scheme allows for modeling quantifier scope information. It appears more closely aligned with syntax-oriented schemes than, for example, DM, and may be less specified than the semantic level of PCEDT. How informative is this scheme, and how “parsable” is it truly? An error analysis would be helpful, given that your reported scores suggest it's challenging.
- The three principles you propose could apply to various scenarios and appear somewhat ad-hoc. They would benefit from being situated within a broader (and more clearly articulated) introduction. What are you arguing for: a parser for UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis, or a parsable semantic-oriented scheme? The current presentation attempts to address all these points simultaneously in a dense manner, bordering on confusion.
