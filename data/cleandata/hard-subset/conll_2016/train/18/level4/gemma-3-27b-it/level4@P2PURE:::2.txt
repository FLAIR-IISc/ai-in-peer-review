The paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for Universal Compositional Semantics (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation formalism. UCCA graphs differ from CoNLL semantic dependency graphs in their allowance of “nonterminal” nodes, which do not correspond to individual words in the input string. Conversely, unlike Abstract Meaning Representations (AMRs), UCCA graphs are “grounded,” meaning they include text tokens as nodes within the semantic representation. The authors present and evaluate several parsing approaches, including a transition-based parser designed to directly construct UCCA parses.

While the development of a semantic parser for UCCA is a logical step given the existence of UCCA and its annotated data, the paper’s introduction and background section present a potentially misleading argument. The authors appear to suggest UCCA is uniquely positioned as a sensible graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism for study. This framing feels unnecessary and weakens the impact of an otherwise valuable contribution – a strong UCCA parser is a worthwhile achievement in its own right. 

I also question the validity of the three criteria the authors establish for evaluating semantic representation formalisms. The purported benefit of “nonterminal nodes” remains unclear; while they may offer a convenient solution to issues like head selection in coordinated structures, it’s not evident that they provide expressive power not achievable through more informative edge labels. Similarly, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant for SRs that are not “grounded.” The advantages of “grounded” representations over AMR-style representations are not convincingly demonstrated. Furthermore, the term “grounded” carries significant baggage within semantic literature, and I recommend the authors consider alternative terminology, such as “anchored” or “lexicalized,” to avoid ambiguity. Consequently, the introductory section requires substantial refinement and more careful argumentation.

The parser itself appears sound, though I haven't verified all implementation details. However, the reported evaluation results are not particularly compelling. The baseline MaltParser demonstrably outperforms the presented Beam-Search Parser (BSP) on “primary” edges, and the f-scores on “remote” edges – those inaccessible to a standard dependency-tree parser like Malt – remain relatively low. Moreover, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees, a process extensively studied as “tree approximations” within the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing, is relevant here. Existing work, such as Agic et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” thoroughly addresses the challenges of reconstructing deleted edges during graph-to-tree conversion. Incorporating such techniques would likely improve the performance of the MaltParser (and its LSTM-based variant), further diminishing the relative advantage of the BSP parser.
