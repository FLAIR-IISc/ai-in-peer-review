- Paper proposes a transition-based graph parser for UCCA and reports phrase-based, dependency-conversion, and direct-graph parsing experiments.  
- Reviewer finds the work interesting but raises significant methodological and empirical concerns needing resolution before publication.  
- Claim of being the “first” parser for such semantic schemes is questionable; the transition system does not specifically address UCCA’s underspecification or semantically-oriented labels.  
- Remote edges and other UCCA phenomena might be handled by existing transition designs via label engineering, so novelty is unclear.  
- Numerous existing transition-based graph parsers (SemEval 2014–2015 work) could be adapted to UCCA; these were not used as baselines.  
- The parser’s adaptation appears driven mainly by the feature template rather than substantive model changes; unigram/count features dominate.  
- Lack of comparison to existing graph parser implementations (e.g., DAGParser, TurboParser) weakens empirical claims.  
- Phrase-based experiments should be validated against other constituency/LT-grammar capable parsers (e.g., Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins 2015); Uparse is introduced without that comparison.  
- Evaluation should follow shared-task metrics used for SDP to enable comparison with competing systems and datasets.  
- Conversion to surface dependency trees is overly lossy; a richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al. 2014) is preferable.  
- Clarify or exemplify the claim that “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text.”  
- Discuss whether UCCA models quantifier scope and how it compares informativeness-wise to representations like MRS, DRT, DM, and PCEDT.  
- An error analysis is needed since reported scores suggest parsing UCCA is challenging.  
- The three principles motivating the scheme feel ad hoc and require a broader, clearer introduction and positioning—unclear whether goal is a UCCA-specific parser, a general semantic parser, or a parsability demonstration.  
- Editorial/request items: introduce “grounded semantic” earlier, and justify not using a constituent-tree approach with rich node labels and the Berkeley parser as a baseline.
