This paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for Universal Compositional Semantics for Chinese (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation formalism. UCCA graphs differ from CoNLL semantic dependency graphs in their inclusion of nonterminal nodes—nodes not directly corresponding to words in the input string. Conversely, unlike Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR), UCCA graphs are “grounded,” meaning all text tokens are represented as nodes within the semantic structure. The authors introduce and evaluate several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser designed to directly construct UCCA parses.

Developing a semantic parser for UCCA is a logical step given the existence of the formalism and UCCA-annotated data. However, the introduction and background section are positioned in a manner that feels unnecessarily critical of other semantic representation (SR) formalisms. The authors appear to argue for UCCA as the *only* sensible approach for study, a claim that is both unsubstantiated and detracts from the value of their contribution. A well-developed UCCA parser is a valuable result in its own right, and doesn’t require diminishing the merit of alternatives.

I have reservations regarding the three criteria the authors present for evaluating semantic representation formalisms. The purported benefit of “nonterminal nodes” is not convincingly demonstrated. While resolving the head of a coordinated structure can present challenges, it is unclear what information *requires* a nonterminal node for its representation, as opposed to being encoded within more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant for SRs that are not inherently “grounded.” The advantages of “grounded” representations, as opposed to AMR-style representations, were not clearly articulated. I also suggest the authors reconsider the term “grounded,” as it has been applied to diverse concepts in semantic research in recent years; alternative terminology such as “anchored” or “lexicalized” might offer greater clarity. Therefore, a substantial revision of the introductory section, with more nuanced argumentation, is warranted.

The parsing methods themselves appear sound, though I have not exhaustively examined the implementation details. However, I found the evaluation results somewhat underwhelming. On primary edges, even a baseline MaltParser achieves superior performance to the presented Bilingual Semantic Parser (BSP). The f-scores on remote edges—those not directly computable by a standard dependency-tree parser like Malt—are also not particularly high. 

Importantly, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees, and the subsequent reconstruction of lost edges, has been a significant area of research, particularly within the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. While these earlier works did not involve “nonterminal” nodes, they directly address the challenges of approximating graph structures with trees. Several studies, such as Agic et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” thoroughly discuss the difficulties inherent in this reconstruction process and propose methods for improving edge recovery. Incorporating such techniques could plausibly improve the performance of the MaltParser (and potentially an LSTM-enhanced MaltParser) and further diminish the apparent advantage of the BSP parser.

In summary, while the paper addresses an important task – parsing UCCA graphs – the framing of the work could be improved by a more balanced discussion of related formalisms, and the evaluation could be strengthened by considering relevant prior work on graph-to-tree approximation techniques.
