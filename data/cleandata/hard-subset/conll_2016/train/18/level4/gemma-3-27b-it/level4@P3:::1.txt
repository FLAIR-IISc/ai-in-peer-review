## Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations found in the UCCA corpora. The authors begin by outlining three properties they believe are essential for broad-coverage semantic annotation schemes: (1) graph-based structure allowing multiple parents, (2) non-terminal nodes representing complex structures, and (3) the ability to represent discontinuous elements. They then position their work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce UCCA as their chosen annotation scheme. The paper details three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using a conversion to constituency trees and the Stanford parser, (ii) dependency parsing using a conversion to dependency trees, and (iii) the core contribution: experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for directly parsing UCCA graphs.

I find this work interesting and potentially valuable to the community. However, several methodological and empirical concerns need to be addressed before publication.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Empirical Investigation of Transition-Based Parsing for UCCA:** The primary contribution lies in providing an initial empirical exploration of transition-based parsing applied directly to UCCA-annotated data. While the approach itself isn't entirely novel, applying it to this specific, structurally complex annotation scheme and achieving a labeled F1-score of around 60-70% demonstrates feasibility and provides a baseline for future work.
2.  **Identification of Key Challenges in Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing:** The paper highlights the difficulties inherent in parsing representations requiring multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. By attempting to directly parse UCCA, the authors illuminate these challenges and motivate further research into parsing techniques capable of handling such complexity.
3.  **Dataset and Baseline:** The work provides a publicly available implementation and evaluation on the UCCA dataset, which is crucial for the community. The conversion-based baselines, while not state-of-the-art, offer a starting point for comparison.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the need for semantic representations that go beyond traditional syntactic structures, and they convincingly argue for the importance of the three identified properties.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The inclusion of both conversion-based and direct parsing experiments provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach.  Exploring both constituency and dependency conversions is a valuable strategy.
3.  **Public Availability:** The commitment to making code publicly available is a significant strength, facilitating reproducibility and encouraging further research.
4.  **Solid Baseline Performance:** The achieved F1-score of ~60-70% for the direct parser demonstrates promising initial results, especially considering the complexity of the UCCA representation.
5.  **Novel Feature Incorporation:** The introduction of the ‘ratio’ feature appears to be a valuable addition to the feature set, demonstrating an understanding of the specific challenges posed by the UCCA annotation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Existing Graph Parsing Work:** The claim of being the “first” to propose a parser for such a scheme is misleading. Several existing graph parsers (e.g., Almeda & Martins, Ribeyre et al.) developed for SemEval shared tasks could serve as strong baselines, especially given their availability. The lack of comparison significantly weakens the argument for the novelty of the proposed approach.
2.  **Insufficient Justification for Feature Choices:** While the authors mention adapting features from related parsers, a more detailed rationale for the specific feature templates used, and the UCCA-oriented additions, would be beneficial. The relative importance of features could be more rigorously evaluated.
3.  **Inadequate Conversion Strategies:** The conversion to dependency trees appears overly simplistic, resulting in significant information loss. Employing a richer conversion method, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (SemEval SDP 2014), could improve the performance of the conversion-based baselines.
4.  **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the parser. Understanding the types of errors made by the system could inform future improvements and guide feature engineering efforts.
5.  **Ambiguous Overall Goal:** The introduction is dense and attempts to establish multiple arguments simultaneously (parser for UCCA, model for semantic analysis, validation of the annotation scheme). Clarifying the primary goal of the paper would improve its focus and clarity.
6.  **Unclear Motivation for the Three Principles:** The three defining principles for broad-coverage semantic parsing, while reasonable, appear somewhat *ad hoc*. A more thorough justification for their selection and a discussion of how they relate to broader theoretical frameworks would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the feature set used in BSP, particularly the UCCA-specific features?
2.  How did you choose the MINUPDATE and IMPORTANCE parameters for training, and did you explore other settings?
3.  Could you elaborate on the types of errors made by the parser, and how these errors relate to the specific challenges of UCCA parsing?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with training and running the BSP parser, compared to the conversion-based approaches?
5.  What are the limitations of UCCA that might necessitate alternative or complementary semantic representations (e.g., handling quantifier scope)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please introduce the term “grounded semantic” before its first use on page 2.
*   Consider exploring a constituent-tree approach with rich node labels and trace propagation as a baseline, potentially using the Berkeley parser.
*   Expand on the mention of "implicit units" in UCCA graphs, providing an example or a reference.
