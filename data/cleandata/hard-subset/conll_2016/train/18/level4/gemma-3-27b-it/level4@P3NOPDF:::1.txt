This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations found in the Universal Compositional Semantics for Analysis (UCCA) corpora. The authors begin by outlining characteristics they believe are essential for a semantic annotation scheme: (1) graph-based structure allowing multiple governors per token, (2) the inclusion of non-terminal nodes representing complex structures (both syntactic, such as coordinate phrases, and lexical, such as multiword expressions), and (3) the ability to represent discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs and particles). Notably, these principles are not inherently semantic and could equally apply to syntactic or other representational layers. The work is situated within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and then focuses on the specifics of the UCCA annotation scheme. The paper then details three sets of parsing experiments: (1) phrase-based parsing employing the Stanford parser and a conversion from UCCA to constituency, (2) dependency parsing via a UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (3) the core contribution – experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for directly parsing UCCA graphs.

I found this work interesting, but several concerns regarding methodology and empirical justification must be addressed before publication. 

The authors claim to present the first parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. While technically correct, this claim overlooks existing work in graph parsing. The UCCA scheme, as presented, consists of graphs with varying degrees of structural underspecification and semantic labels (e.g., Process, State). However, the proposed transition system does not specifically address the unique challenges posed by this structure. The handling of remote edges, for example, could potentially be accommodated within existing transition systems through label differentiation (e.g., using affixes). Numerous works following the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models. The authors should explain why these existing parsers were not used as baselines or for comparison. The model’s connection to the UCCA scheme appears largely driven by the specific feature template used, with count and unigram features likely having a greater impact. Given the availability of these graph parsers [1, 2], their inclusion in the evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments utilizing Uparse, validation against another parser like that of Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015) – capable of producing comparable LCFRS-like parsing – would be beneficial (the reference for Uparse was missing upon its initial introduction).

Because UCCA supports a more abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structures compared to most Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) treebanks, employing the same metrics used in related shared tasks would be crucial. The current lack of comparison to other systems, models, and datasets is a significant weakness. A more thorough comparison is essential to contextualize the contribution. Despite these concerns, I believe the paper’s exploration of parsing UCCA graphs is timely and warrants discussion, making it suitable for a conference like ConLL, particularly given the current need for direction in semantic parsing research.

Here are some additional points for consideration, presented in no particular order:

*   Please introduce the term “grounded semantic” before page 2, as it appears before being defined.
*   Why wasn't an approach involving constituent trees with richly labeled nodes and trace propagation, parsed using the Berkeley parser, explored as a baseline?
*   The conversion to surface dependency trees appears to lose valuable information. A richer conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would be more appropriate.
*   Please elaborate on the statement that “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text” – provide an example or a reference.
*   The authors mention alternative representations like MRS and DRT. This raises the issue of quantifier scope modeling, which appears to be absent in the UCCA scheme. Thus, its comparability to other schemes is questionable. Is it truly more abstract than Dependency Meta-representation (DM), and how much more underspecified is it than the semantic level of the PCEDT? Quantifying the informativeness and “parsability” of the UCCA scheme would be valuable. An error analysis could provide insights in this regard.
*   The three principles outlined by the authors seem somewhat ad-hoc and require a broader, clearer introduction to fully articulate their significance. Is the goal to present a parser for UCCA, a model for semantic analysis, or a parsable semantic annotation scheme? The current presentation attempts to achieve all three simultaneously, resulting in density and potential confusion.
