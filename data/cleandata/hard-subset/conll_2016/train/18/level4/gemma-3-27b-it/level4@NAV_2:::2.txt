The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes which do not represent words in the string. Unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded", which the authors take to mean that the text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present a number of parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

Given that UCCA and UCCA-annotated data exist, it seems reasonable to develop a semantic parser for UCCA. However, the introduction and background section raise concerns, as they appear to argue that UCCA is the only sensible graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism to study. This framing is unconvincing and feels unnecessary – a successful UCCA parser could be a valuable contribution in its own right.

I question the validity of the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms presented in the introduction. The necessity of "nonterminal" nodes for expressive capacity is unclear. While determining the head of a coordinated structure can be inconvenient, it is unclear what information could *only* be represented with a nonterminal and not, for example, with more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity does not even arise in SRs that are not "grounded." The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones were not clearly articulated. The term "grounded" is also overloaded in semantics, and I suggest the authors consider alternatives like "anchored" or “lexicalized.” Therefore, the introductory portion of the paper requires careful revision and more nuanced argumentation.

The parser itself appears sound, though I have not verified all details. However, the evaluation results are not compelling. Even a straightforward MaltParser achieves higher F-scores on "primary" edges than the presented BSP parser. Furthermore, the F-scores on "remote" edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot directly compute) remain relatively low. The conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees has been extensively studied as “tree approximations” in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing, even without "nonterminal" nodes. Several authors have proposed methods for reconstructing deleted edges during graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agic et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” details the challenges involved. Integrating such methods could likely improve the F-score of the MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser), further diminishing the advantage of the BSP parser.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty of Task:** This work represents the first attempt to develop parsers specifically for UCCA, a relatively new and potentially valuable semantic representation. The availability of UCCA-annotated data makes this a worthwhile endeavor.
2.  **Comprehensive Approach:** The authors explore multiple parsing strategies, including conversion-based methods and a novel transition-based parser tailored to the specific characteristics of UCCA.
3.  **Clear Presentation of UCCA:** The paper provides a concise and informative overview of the UCCA formalism, making it accessible to readers unfamiliar with the scheme.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Unconvincing Motivation:** The introductory arguments for the necessity and superiority of UCCA over other semantic representation formalisms are weak and potentially misleading. The framing of the work as a response to a lack of suitable representations is not well-supported.
2.  **Limited Evaluation:** The empirical results do not strongly support the claim of superior performance for the proposed transition-based parser. Existing, simpler dependency parsers perform comparably or better on primary edges.
3.  **Lack of Comparison with Related Work on Tree Approximation:** The authors do not adequately acknowledge or address the substantial body of work on tree approximation techniques in semantic dependency parsing, which could potentially improve the performance of the conversion-based baselines.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific advantages of UCCA's "grounded" nature compared to AMR-style representations, particularly in the context of downstream applications?
2.  What considerations led to the choice of the specific feature set for the transition-based parser, and how were the features selected and tuned?
3.  Have you experimented with incorporating tree approximation techniques (e.g., Agic et al., 2015) to improve the performance of the conversion-based baselines? If so, what were the results?
