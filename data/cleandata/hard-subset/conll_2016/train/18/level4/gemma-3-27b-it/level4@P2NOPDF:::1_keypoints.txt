- Introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA but the three motivating principles are not uniquely semantic and apply to syntactic frameworks as well.  
- Claiming the "first parser" for a semantically-oriented scheme is debatable given prior transition-based graph parser extensions (SemEval 2014–15 work).  
- The proposed transition system does not explicitly handle UCCA-specific characteristics (structural underspecification, semantic labels, implicit units).  
- Remote-edge handling could be achieved via label modifications rather than new transition types, questioning novelty.  
- The parser’s apparent UCCA-specificity seems driven mainly by simple count/unigram features rather than rich UCCA-aware modeling.  
- Authors failed to compare against existing graph-parsing baselines (e.g., Ribeyre’s DAGParser, TurboParser adaptations).  
- Phrase-based Uparse experiments should be compared to Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015) LCFRS-like parser.  
- Converting UCCA to surface dependency trees likely discards information; recommend richer conversions (e.g., Schluter et al. 2014).  
- Evaluation metrics should be aligned with related shared tasks (SDP) and include broader comparisons to other systems and datasets.  
- The term “grounded semantic” is used before being defined and should be introduced earlier.  
- Why not include a constituent-tree baseline with rich node labels and Berkeley parser training/parsing?  
- The paper should give an example/reference for “implicit units” in UCCA to clarify the claim.  
- Need clarification on UCCA’s relation to other formalisms (MRS, DRT, DM, PCEDT), especially regarding quantifier scope and overall informativeness/par­sability.  
- An error analysis is missing and would help interpret scores and practical parsability of UCCA.  
- The three principles appear ad hoc and the paper must clarify whether it argues for a UCCA-specific parser, a general semantic parser, or that semantically-oriented schemes are parsable.  
- Despite methodological and empirical weaknesses, the work is timely and could stimulate useful discussion at a venue like CoNLL.
