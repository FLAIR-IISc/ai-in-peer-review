- Paper introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA and situates desired characteristics of semantic schemes (graph structure, non-terminal nodes, discontinuities).  
- Reviewer finds the work potentially valuable but raises significant methodological and empirical concerns.  
- Novelty claim is questionable since existing transition systems could be adapted to UCCA with label adjustments.  
- Prior work (post-2014 SemEval) extended transition-based graph parsers and should be considered as relevant approaches.  
- Existing graph parsers (e.g., Ribeyre et al., TurboParser) must be included as baselines or comparative systems.  
- The parser’s performance seems driven largely by the feature template, especially count and unigram features.  
- Phrase-based experiments using Uparse lack validation against comparable parsers (e.g., Fernandez-Gonzalez and Martins 2015); a missing reference for Uparse should be added.  
- Dependency conversion appears to lose important information; richer conversions (e.g., Schluter et al. 2014) are recommended.  
- Evaluation metrics should align with those used in relevant shared tasks given UCCA’s abstract view of syntacto-semantic structure.  
- The paper lacks comparison with other systems and parsers, weakening its empirical impact.  
- Suggest exploring a constituent-tree approach with rich node labels and Berkeley parser as an additional baseline.  
- The claim about “implicit units” in UCCA needs elaboration or examples/references.  
- Questions raised about UCCA’s expressivity (e.g., quantifier scope) and its comparability to MRS, DRT, DM, PCEDT; a quantitative assessment would help.  
- Recommend an error analysis of parser outputs to assess informativeness and “parsability.”  
- The three principles for scheme design are broad/ad hoc and need clearer placement and framing; the paper’s goals (parser vs. semantic model vs. scheme) are unclear.  
- Minor editorial: introduce the term “grounded semantic” before its first use on page 2.  
- Overall, despite concerns, the reviewer believes the paper merits discussion at a conference like CoNLL.
