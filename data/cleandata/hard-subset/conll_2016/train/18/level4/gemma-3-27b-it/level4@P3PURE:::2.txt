The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain nonterminal nodes that do not directly correspond to words in the input string. Conversely, unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are “grounded,” meaning text tokens are represented as nodes within the semantic representation. The authors present and evaluate a number of parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses.

Given the existence of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, developing a semantic parser for this formalism is a reasonable endeavor. However, the introduction and background section struck a discordant note; the authors appear to argue that UCCA is the only sensible graph-based semantic representation (SR) to study. This argument is unconvincing and, moreover, seems unnecessary – a successful UCCA parser would represent a valuable contribution in its own right.

I do not fully agree with the three criteria the authors establish for semantic representation formalisms. For example, the contribution of nonterminal nodes to expressive capacity is unclear. While determining the head of a coordinated structure can be inconvenient, it is not evident what information could *only* be represented with a nonterminal node and not, for instance, with more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant for SRs that are not grounded. The advantages of grounded representations over AMR-style representations were also not sufficiently explained. Finally, the term “grounded” has been used to denote various concepts in semantics over the past decade, and I encourage the authors to consider an alternative term such as “anchored” or “lexicalized.” Consequently, I believe the introductory section of the paper would benefit from more careful phrasing and argumentation.

The parser itself appears sound, though I did not examine the implementation details. However, I found the evaluation results underwhelming. On primary edges, even a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the presented BSP parser, and the f-scores on remote edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot directly compute) remain relatively low. Moreover, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees – and the associated loss of information – has been extensively studied under the term “tree approximations” within the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (though not specifically with nonterminal nodes). Several authors have proposed methods for reconstructing edges lost during graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agic et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” discuss the challenges inherent in this reconstruction. Integrating such methods would likely improve the f-score of MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser), further diminishing the advantages of the BSP parser.
