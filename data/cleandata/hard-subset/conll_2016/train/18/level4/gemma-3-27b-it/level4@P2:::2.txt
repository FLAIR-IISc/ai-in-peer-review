This paper introduces the first parsers for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation that allows for multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features not all found together in existing semantic formalisms like AMR or standard dependency graphs. The authors present both conversion-based parsing methods, leveraging existing parsers, and a novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed specifically for UCCA.

The primary contribution of this work, in my assessment, is the creation and evaluation of a parsing system—BSP—for a relatively new and complex semantic representation (UCCA). While conversion-based methods are explored, the core novelty lies in tackling the challenges of parsing UCCA directly with a transition-based approach designed to handle its specific structural properties. The second significant contribution is the empirical exploration of UCCA parsing itself, providing a baseline for future work in this area. Finally, the paper highlights the unique characteristics of UCCA, prompting discussion about the design choices in semantic representation formalisms.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of UCCA Parsing:** The paper addresses a previously unaddressed problem: parsing UCCA structures. UCCA’s combination of structural properties necessitates new parsing approaches, and this work provides a first step in that direction. The transition-based parser, designed to directly handle these properties, is a valuable contribution.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach using multiple methods (conversion-based and direct parsing) and on both in-domain and out-of-domain data. This thoroughness allows for a nuanced assessment of the parser’s performance and robustness.
3. **Clear Presentation of UCCA:** The paper provides a concise and accessible introduction to UCCA, its motivations, and its unique characteristics. This is essential for readers unfamiliar with the formalism.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:** The commitment to release the code and models upon publication is a significant strength, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Framing of UCCA’s Uniqueness:** The paper’s initial framing of UCCA as uniquely suited for broad-coverage semantics feels overstated and potentially dismissive of other approaches. The arguments for the necessity of the three stated criteria (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuity) are not entirely convincing, especially concerning non-terminal nodes and their added expressive power. The paper would benefit from a more balanced discussion of the trade-offs involved in different semantic representation designs.
2. **Limited Performance Gains:** While BSP represents a significant initial effort, its performance on primary edges is currently surpassed by simpler, conversion-based approaches like MaltParser. The gains in handling remote edges, while notable, are not substantial enough to definitively demonstrate the advantages of a dedicated UCCA parser, especially considering the complexity involved.
3. **Lack of Engagement with Related Work on Tree Approximation:** The paper overlooks relevant work on reconstructing information lost in graph-to-tree conversions, particularly within the context of semantic dependency parsing (e.g., Agic et al., 2015). Incorporating such techniques could potentially improve the performance of the conversion-based methods and provide a stronger baseline for comparison.
4. **Terminology:** The term "grounded" is vague and overused in semantic representation research. The authors should consider adopting a more precise term to describe the textual anchoring of UCCA nodes.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the design choices behind the UCCA formalism that specifically motivated the need for features like non-terminal nodes? What concrete examples demonstrate the limitations of alternative representations (e.g., dependency trees with informative edge labels) in capturing the same semantic information?
2. Given the limited performance gains of BSP over conversion-based methods, what specific avenues for improvement do you believe are most promising (e.g., exploring more sophisticated features, incorporating pre-training techniques, or leveraging a more powerful classifier)?
3. Have you investigated incorporating techniques for reconstructing lost information during the conversion from UCCA graphs to trees, similar to the work on tree approximations in semantic dependency parsing? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
