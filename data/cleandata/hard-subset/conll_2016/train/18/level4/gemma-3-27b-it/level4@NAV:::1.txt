This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the complex representations found within the UCCA annotation scheme and corpora. The authors begin by outlining desired characteristics of a semantic annotation scheme: (i) graph-based structure allowing multiple governors per token, (ii) inclusion of non-terminal nodes representing complex syntactic and lexical structures (like coordinate phrases and multiword expressions), and (iii) support for discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs and particles). Notably, these principles are not inherently semantic and could apply to syntactic or other representation formalisms. The paper then contextualizes the work within the broader field of broad-coverage semantic parsing and specifically introduces the UCCA annotation scheme.  The core of the work presents three experimental sets: (i) phrase-based parsing utilizing the Stanford parser with a UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing through a UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) evaluation of the proposed transition-based graph parser for direct UCCA graph parsing.

I find this work to be a potentially valuable contribution, but several methodological and empirical concerns must be addressed before publication. 

The authors claim novelty in proposing a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. While technically true, the underlying graph structures, characterized by varying degrees of underspecification and semantic labels (Process, State), do not uniquely necessitate the proposed transition system. Existing transitions could potentially accommodate the scheme’s features with adjustments to the label set (e.g., affixes). Several works following the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al, 2014-2015) extended transition-based graph parsers or adapted higher-order models, and their applicability to this dataset should be considered. The model’s performance is primarily anchored by its specific feature template, though count and unigram features appear more influential. Given the availability of these existing graph parsers [1, 2], their inclusion as baselines or for comparative analysis is crucial.

Concerning the phrase-based experiments employing Uparse, validation against the parser from Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015) – capable of producing comparable LCFRS-like parsing – would strengthen the analysis (a reference was missing upon initial introduction of Uparse).  Furthermore, given that the UCCA scheme represents a more abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structure than most SDP treebanks, aligning evaluation metrics with those used in relevant shared tasks is important.  The current lack of comparison with other systems and parsers significantly weakens the overall impact of the work. Nevertheless, given the current juncture in the field, I believe this paper warrants discussion at a conference like ConLL.

**Additional Notes (in random order):**

- Please introduce the term “grounded semantic” before page 2, as it is used prior to its definition.
-  Exploring a constituent-tree approach with rich node labels and trace propagation, followed by training/parsing with the Berkeley parser, could provide a valuable baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees appears to lose valuable information; a richer conversion, such as that proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would be more appropriate.
- Please elaborate on the statement "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text," providing either a reference or a concrete example.
- The mention of alternative representations like MRS and DRT raises the issue of quantifier scope modeling, which appears to be unsupported by the current scheme. Consequently, its comparability to other schemes is limited.  Is it truly more abstract than DM and less specified than the semantic level of PCEDT? A quantitative assessment of its informativeness and “parsability” would be helpful, potentially through an error analysis of the parser’s outputs.
- The three principles articulated by the authors could apply broadly and appear somewhat *ad hoc*.  They require placement within a more extensive and clearly defined introduction.  It is unclear whether the primary goal is a parser for UCCA, a model for semantic analysis, or a parsable semantic-oriented scheme. The current presentation is dense and risks being confusing.
