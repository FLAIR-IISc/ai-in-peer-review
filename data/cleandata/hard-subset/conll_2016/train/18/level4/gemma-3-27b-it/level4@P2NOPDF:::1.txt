This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the complex representations found in the UCCA corpora, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors begin by outlining characteristics they believe are essential for a semantic annotation scheme: graph-based structure allowing multiple governors per node, the inclusion of non-terminal nodes representing both syntactic and lexical structures (like coordinate phrases and multiword expressions), and the ability to represent discontinuous elements (e.g., verb-particle constructions). However, it is noted that these principles are not intrinsically semantic and could apply equally well to syntactic or other representational frameworks. The paper then contextualizes the work within the broader field of broad-coverage semantic parsing and details the UCCA annotation scheme. The core of the work presents three sets of parsing experiments: phrase-based parsing utilizing the Stanford parser and a UCCA-to-constituency conversion; dependency parsing with a UCCA-to-dependency conversion; and, most importantly, experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for directly parsing UCCA graphs.

I find this work potentially interesting, but several methodological and empirical concerns need to be addressed before publication. 

The authors claim to present the first parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. While technically true, the UCCA scheme consists of graphs with varying degrees of structural underspecification and semantically-oriented labels (Process, State). Critically, the proposed transition system does not explicitly address these specific characteristics. Transitions for remote edges, for instance, could likely be accommodated by modifications to the label set (e.g., using affixes) rather than novel transition operations.  In the context of graph parsing, numerous works extending transition-based graph parsers—stemming from the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeda & Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015)—offer potential avenues for adaptation to this dataset. The model’s connection to the UCCA scheme is largely driven by the specific feature template used, which appears less influential than count and unigram features. Given the availability of these existing graph parsers [1, 2], it’s unclear why they were not employed as baselines for comparison.

The phrase-based experiments employing Uparse would benefit from comparison with the parser from Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015), which achieves comparable performance using LCFRS-like parsing. A reference to this work was missing when Uparse was initially introduced.

Given that the UCCA scheme provides a more abstract representation of syntactico-semantic structures than many Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) treebanks, aligning evaluation metrics with those used in related shared tasks would be beneficial. The current lack of comprehensive comparison to other systems, models, and datasets is a significant weakness. Despite these concerns, the work’s timeliness and potential contribution to ongoing discussions regarding the future direction of semantic parsing suggest it merits discussion at a conference like ConLL. 

Here are some additional points for consideration:

*   Please introduce the term “grounded semantic” before page 2, as it is used before being defined.
*   Why wasn't a constituent-tree approach with rich node labels and trace propagation, combined with training and parsing using the Berkeley parser, explored as a baseline?
*   The conversion to surface dependency trees seems to discard valuable information. A richer conversion, such as the one presented in Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would be more appropriate.
*   Please elaborate on the statement that “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text,” providing an example or a reference.
*   The mention of other representations like MRS and DRT raises the issue of quantifier scope, which appears to be absent from the UCCA scheme. Therefore, its comparability to other schemes – being more abstract than DM and potentially more underspecified than the semantic level of PCEDT – needs further clarification. How informative is this scheme, and how readily “parsable” is it? An error analysis, alongside the presented scores, would be particularly helpful.
*   The three principles outlined by the authors feel somewhat *ad hoc* and require a more extensive and clear introduction. It is unclear whether the paper argues for a parser specifically designed for UCCA, a model suited for semantic analysis generally, or a semantically-oriented scheme that is demonstrably parsable. These arguments are currently presented in a dense and potentially confusing manner.
