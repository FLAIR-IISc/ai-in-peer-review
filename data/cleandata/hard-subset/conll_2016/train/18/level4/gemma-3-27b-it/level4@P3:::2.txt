## Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation formalism. UCCA graphs allow for “nonterminal” nodes not directly corresponding to words in the input string, distinguishing them from many other semantic representations like dependency graphs. The authors explore both conversion-based parsing approaches leveraging existing parsers, and a novel transition-based parser designed to directly construct UCCA parses.

The primary contribution of this work is the development and evaluation of a transition-based parsing approach capable of handling the complexities of UCCA’s broad-coverage semantic structures, specifically multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. While conversion-based approaches are explored, the direct parsing method represents a significant step toward a dedicated and potentially more accurate parsing solution for UCCA. A secondary, valuable contribution lies in the experimental evaluation using UCCA-annotated corpora, providing a baseline for future work in this area and highlighting the challenges associated with parsing this richly structured representation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Direct Parsing Approach:** The development of a transition-based parser specifically designed for UCCA is a significant contribution. Existing parsers are not well-suited to handle the full expressive power of UCCA, and this work pioneers a dedicated solution. The adaptation of transition-based techniques, known for success in dependency parsing, to this more complex representation is a logical and promising direction.
2. **Resource Creation & Baseline:** The experimental results, even if not state-of-the-art, establish a crucial baseline for UCCA parsing. The authors provide a practical implementation and evaluation framework that will facilitate future research in this relatively unexplored area. The willingness to release the code and converters is commendable.
3. **Comprehensive Exploration of Parsing Strategies:** The paper explores both conversion-based and direct parsing methods, offering a comparative analysis of their strengths and weaknesses. This comparative approach provides valuable insights into the suitability of different parsing paradigms for UCCA. The identification of the limitations of conversion-based methods highlights the need for dedicated parsers capable of capturing the nuances of UCCA’s structure.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Questionable Framing of Motivation & Criteria:** The introductory framing, positioning UCCA as uniquely capable and defining “essential” properties of semantic representations, is overly assertive and potentially misleading. The justification for the three proposed criteria (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuities) is not fully convincing, particularly regarding the added expressiveness of non-terminal nodes. A more nuanced discussion acknowledging the strengths of alternative representations (e.g., AMR) would strengthen the paper.
2. **Subpar Performance & Limited Comparison:** The parser’s performance, especially compared to readily available tools like MaltParser, is not particularly strong. While the authors acknowledge this, they do not sufficiently address *why* the direct parsing method underperforms. A deeper error analysis and more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art parsers, possibly incorporating techniques for recovering lost information during graph-to-tree conversion (as noted by the reviewer), are needed.
3. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of parsing errors. Understanding *where* the parser struggles – for example, with specific UCCA categories or structural patterns – would provide valuable insights for future improvements. It would be helpful to see examples of incorrect parses and the reasons behind them.
4. **Terminology & Clarity:** The use of the term “grounded” is potentially confusing given its multiple meanings in semantics. A more precise and less ambiguous term is advisable. While the paper aims to be comprehensive, some sections (particularly the background) are dense and could benefit from increased clarity and conciseness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of UCCA structures where the BSP parser performs poorly? Specifically, what categories or structural features consistently lead to parsing errors?
2.  Have you experimented with incorporating techniques for reconstructing information lost during the conversion to dependency trees, such as those proposed by Agic et al. (2015), to potentially improve the performance of the dependency-based baselines?
3.  Regarding the three criteria for broad-coverage semantic representations, could you provide concrete examples of semantic phenomena that *require* non-terminal nodes or discontinuities and cannot be adequately represented using alternative approaches (e.g., edge labels in a dependency graph)?
4.  What are the computational costs (training and parsing time) of the BSP parser compared to the conversion-based approaches?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-maintained.
*   The figures are helpful in illustrating the UCCA structures and the parsing process.
*   Consider replacing the term “grounded” with a more precise alternative, such as “lexicalized” or “anchored.”
