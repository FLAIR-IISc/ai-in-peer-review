This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the complex representations found in the UCCA corpora, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors begin by outlining key characteristics they believe essential for broad-coverage semantic annotation: (1) graph-based structure allowing multiple dependencies for a single token, (2) non-terminal nodes representing complex phrases and multi-word expressions, and (3) support for discontinuous elements. Notably, these principles aren’t strictly semantic and could apply to syntactic or other representational layers. The paper positions this work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing, then focuses on the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using a Stanford parser with a UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing using a UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution – experiments demonstrating their transition-based graph parser’s ability to directly parse UCCA graphs.

I find this work interesting and believe it merits discussion at a conference like ConLL, but have several methodological and empirical concerns before recommending publication. 

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. While technically true, the novelty is overstated. The transitions developed don’t fully leverage the specificities of the UCCA graph structure and its semantic labels; the remote edge transitions, for example, could potentially be handled through more nuanced edge labeling. Considering the graph parsing work from the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (e.g., Almeda & Martins, Ribeyre et al.), which extended transition-based graph parsers or adapted higher-order models, it is unclear why these existing systems were not used as baselines or for comparison. The reliance on a specific feature template seems to be the primary factor anchoring the model to the UCCA scheme, and its influence compared to count features and unigrams remains unclear. The availability of these previous graph parsers ([1, 2]) makes their omission significant.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments with UPARSE, comparison with another parser like that of Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015), capable of producing comparable LCFRS-like parsing results, would strengthen the analysis. 

Because UCCA aims for a more abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structures than many SDP treebanks, utilizing evaluation metrics consistent with those from related shared tasks would be beneficial. The field currently lacks standardized comparisons, and the absence of benchmarks against existing models and parsers weakens the overall impact of this work.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation of Transition-Based Parsing on UCCA:** The paper provides a practical evaluation of transition-based parsing applied to a relatively novel and complex semantic annotation scheme (UCCA). This offers valuable insight into the feasibility of this parsing approach for such data.
2. **Implementation and Public Release of Parsing Tools:** The authors state their intention to release the converters and parsers, which would be a valuable resource for the research community working with UCCA or similar semantic representations.
3. **Identification of Challenges in Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing:** The work highlights the challenges of parsing representations requiring multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents, and motivates further research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors explore multiple parsing strategies, including conversion-based approaches and a direct transition-based parser, providing a well-rounded investigation.
2. **Clear Presentation of UCCA:** The paper provides a concise and accessible overview of the UCCA annotation scheme, making the work understandable to those unfamiliar with it.
3. **Robustness to Domain Shift:** The out-of-domain experiments demonstrate that the proposed parser exhibits some degree of robustness when applied to data from a different source.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Existing Work:** The lack of comparison to existing graph parsers and other relevant baselines undermines the assessment of the proposed method's novelty and effectiveness.
2. **Overstated Claims of Novelty:** The claim of being the “first” to propose a parser for this type of scheme is misleading, given prior work in graph parsing.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The absence of a detailed error analysis makes it difficult to understand the specific challenges and limitations of the proposed approach.
4. **Ad-hoc Nature of Defining Principles:** The three principles defining broad-coverage semantic structures feel somewhat arbitrary and lack a strong theoretical justification. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify whether the goal is a parser for UCCA, a model for semantic analysis, or a parsable semantic scheme.
5. **Useless Dependency Conversion**: The conversion to surface dependency trees loses too much information and offers limited benefit.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why existing graph parsers (e.g., those from SemEval 2014/2015) were not considered as baselines?
2.  What specific aspects of the UCCA annotation scheme necessitated the development of new transitions, as opposed to adapting existing transitions with modified features?
3.  Can you provide an example of an “implicit unit” in UCCA and explain how it might be handled in future work?
4.  How does the expressive power of UCCA compare to other semantic representations, such as DM and PCEDT, in terms of handling phenomena like quantifier scope?
5. What is the impact of the ratio feature, and how does it affect parsing performance?




**Additional Comments:**

- Please define "grounded semantic" before its first use on page 2.
- Consider exploring a constituent-tree parser with rich node labels to establish a stronger baseline.
- A richer conversion to dependency trees, such as the one from Schluter et al. (SemEval SDP, 2014), would be preferable to the current method.
