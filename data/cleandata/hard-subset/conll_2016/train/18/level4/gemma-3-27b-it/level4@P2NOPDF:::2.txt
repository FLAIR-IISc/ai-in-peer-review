This paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation formalism. UCCA differs from other approaches like CoNLL semantic dependency graphs by allowing “nonterminal” nodes that do not correspond to surface words, and distinguishes itself from Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR) through its “grounded” nature – the presence of text tokens as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present and evaluate several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser designed to directly construct UCCA parses.

While developing a semantic parser for UCCA is a logical step given the existence of the formalism and its annotated data, the paper’s introduction establishes a problematic framing. The authors appear to position UCCA as uniquely valuable among graph-based semantic representations, a claim that feels unnecessary and weakens their argument. A successful UCCA parser would constitute a valuable contribution in its own right, independent of broader claims about the superiority of the formalism.

I have concerns about the three criteria the authors propose for evaluating semantic representation formalisms. The supposed benefit of “nonterminal nodes” is not convincing. While resolving head selection in coordinated structures can be challenging, it’s unclear what information *requires* a nonterminal node that couldn’t be captured, for example, through more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant for non-grounded semantic representations. The advantages of a “grounded” representation over AMR-style representations are not clearly demonstrated. Finally, the term “grounded” carries a variety of meanings in semantic literature, and the authors should consider alternative terminology like “anchored” or “lexicalized” to avoid ambiguity. A substantial revision of the introductory section, with more nuanced argumentation, is recommended.

The parsing methods themselves appear sound, although I have not performed a detailed examination of their implementation. However, the evaluation results are not compelling. The baseline MaltParser outperforms the presented Beam Search Parser (BSP) on “primary” edges. Furthermore, the F1-scores on “remote” edges – those not directly representable in a standard dependency tree – remain relatively low. Importantly, the conversion of semantic dependency graphs into dependency trees, and the subsequent reconstruction of deleted edges (“tree approximations”), has been extensively studied, particularly within the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. Work such as Agic et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” details the challenges and methods for effectively reconstructing these lost edges. Integrating such techniques could significantly improve the performance of the MaltParser (and potentially an LSTM-enhanced MaltParser), thereby diminishing the relative advantage of the BSP.
