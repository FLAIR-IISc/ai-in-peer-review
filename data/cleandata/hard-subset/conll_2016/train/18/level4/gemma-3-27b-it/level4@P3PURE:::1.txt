This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, as exemplified by the UCCA corpora. The authors begin by outlining what they consider essential components of a semantic-based annotation scheme: (i) a graph-based structure (allowing tokens/nodes to have multiple governors), (2) non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures – syntactic, such as coordinate phrases, and lexical, such as multiword expressions), and (3) the accommodation of discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs and particles). Notably, these principles are not intrinsically linked to semantics and could equally apply to syntax or other representation layers. The authors then situate their work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce their chosen annotation scheme, UCCA. They subsequently present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing utilizing the Stanford parser and a UCCA-to-constituency conversion; (ii) dependency parsing via a UCCA-to-dependency conversion; and (iii) the core contribution, a set of experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

I find this work interesting, but several methodological and empirical concerns must be addressed before publication.

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme such as theirs. While this may be technically correct, the underlying scheme consists of graphs with varying levels of structural underspecification and semantically-oriented labels (Process, State), yet the proposed transition system does not specifically address these characteristics. Even the transitions handling remote edges could potentially be accommodated by existing transitions through variations in the label set (e.g., adding an affix). Focusing on graph parsing, numerous works following the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al, 2014-2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models. Nothing prevents their application to this dataset. The model’s anchoring to the scheme appears primarily driven by a specific feature template, which seems less influential than count and unigram features. Given the availability of these graph parsers [1, 2], it is unclear why they were not used as baselines or points of comparison.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments utilizing Uparse, validation against another parser like that of Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015), capable of producing LCFRS-like parsing comparable to Uparse, would strengthen the results (the reference for Uparse is missing upon its initial introduction). 

Because this scheme supports a more abstract representation of syntactico-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, it would have been crucial to employ the same metrics as used in the related shared tasks. In a field with numerous competing systems, models, and datasets, the lack of comparison points with existing approaches is a significant weakness. Despite these concerns, I find the work interesting, and given its relevance to current research directions, I believe it would benefit from discussion at a conference like ConLL.

Additional notes, in no particular order:

- Please introduce the term “grounded semantic” before page 2, as it is used prior to its definition.
- Why wasn’t an approach involving constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, trained and parsed with the Berkeley parser, explored? This could have served as a valuable baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees appears counterproductive, resulting in information loss. A richer conversion, such as that proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would be preferable.
- Can the statement “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text” be elaborated upon, with a reference or example provided?
- The mention of alternative representations like MRS and DRT raises the question of whether the scheme adequately models quantifier scope information. Consequently, its comparability to more syntax-oriented schemes is limited. It is more abstract than DM, and potentially more underspecified than the semantic level of PCEDT, but to what extent? How informative and “parsable” is this scheme in reality? Given the reported scores, it appears challenging, and an error analysis would be highly beneficial.
- As previously noted, the three principles you proposed are broadly applicable and appear somewhat ad-hoc. They would benefit from placement within a more extensive (and clearer) introduction. Is the aim to present a parser for UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis, or a parsable semantic-oriented scheme? The current presentation attempts to address all these points simultaneously, leading to density and potential confusion.
