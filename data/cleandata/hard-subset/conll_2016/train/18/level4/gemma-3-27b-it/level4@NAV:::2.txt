This paper introduces novel broad-coverage semantic parsers designed specifically for Universal Compositional Communicative Analysis (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation formalism. UCCA differs from other approaches like CoNLL semantic dependency graphs by allowing for nonterminal nodes – nodes not directly corresponding to words in the input – and from Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR) by being “grounded,” meaning all text tokens are represented as nodes within the semantic structure. The authors detail and evaluate several parsing techniques, including a transition-based parser built to directly generate UCCA parses.

While developing a semantic parser for UCCA is a logical step given the existence of the formalism and associated data, the paper’s introductory framing is problematic. The introduction positions UCCA as uniquely valuable among graph-based semantic representations, a claim that feels unnecessarily strong and weakens the overall argument. A successful UCCA parser constitutes a worthwhile contribution in its own right, independent of broader claims about the field.

I also question the three criteria presented by the authors for evaluating semantic representation formalisms. The asserted benefit of “nonterminal nodes” lacks clear justification; it’s unclear how they provide representational power beyond what could be achieved with more detailed edge labeling. Similarly, the concern with discontinuity seems irrelevant for non-grounded semantic representations. The purported advantages of a “grounded” approach over AMR-style representations are not convincingly demonstrated. Moreover, the term “grounded” carries considerable baggage in semantic literature, and the authors should consider alternative terminology like “anchored” or “lexicalized” to avoid ambiguity. Consequently, the introductory section requires substantial revision to present a more nuanced and well-supported argument.

The parser implementations appear sound, though I haven’t verified implementation details. However, the evaluation results are not particularly compelling. The baseline MaltParser achieves higher performance on primary edges than the presented Baseline Span Parser (BSP). Furthermore, f-scores on remote edges, which fall outside the capabilities of standard dependency tree parsers, remain modest. This evaluation overlooks a significant body of related work focused on approximating dependency graphs as trees – a topic extensively explored in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. Methods for reconstructing edges lost during graph-to-tree conversion, such as those proposed by Agic et al. (2015), could likely improve MaltParser performance, potentially diminishing the relative advantage of the BSP parser.
