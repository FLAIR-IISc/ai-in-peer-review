- Paper presents a transition-based graph parser for UCCA, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme supporting graphs, non-terminals, and discontinuities.
- Authors argue three principles for broad-coverage semantic annotation: graph structure, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous elements.
- Experiments include UCCA-to-constituency phrase-based parsing, UCCA-to-dependency parsing, and the direct transition-based graph parser (core contribution).
- Reviewer finds the work interesting and conference-worthy but raises methodological and empirical concerns before acceptance.
- Claim of being the “first” parser for this semantic-style scheme is overstated given prior graph-parsing work.
- New transitions do not fully exploit UCCA-specific structure or semantic labels; some transitions (e.g., remote edges) could be handled differently.
- Important prior graph parsers (SemEval 2014/2015) were omitted as baselines, which weakens comparative assessment.
- Reliance on a specific feature template ties the model to UCCA, but its advantage over simpler features is unclear.
- Phrase-based experiments should be compared to other capable parsers (e.g., Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins 2015).
- Evaluation lacks alignment with metrics and benchmarks from related shared tasks, reducing comparability and impact.
- Contribution: empirical evaluation showing transition-based parsing is feasible for UCCA.
- Contribution: planned release of converters and parsers as community resources.
- Contribution: highlights challenges in parsing representations with multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities.
- Strength: comprehensive experimental setup exploring conversion-based and direct parsing approaches.
- Strength: clear, accessible presentation of the UCCA annotation scheme for unfamiliar readers.
- Strength: demonstrated some robustness to domain shift in out-of-domain experiments.
- Weakness: limited comparison to existing graph parsers and baselines undermines novelty and effectiveness claims.
- Weakness: absence of detailed error analysis hampers understanding of specific failures and limitations.
- Weakness: the three defining principles feel ad-hoc and lack theoretical grounding; unclear overall goal (UCCA parser vs. semantic model).
- Weakness: the dependency conversion is of limited value because it loses too much information.
- Questions: why were SemEval 2014/2015 graph parsers not used as baselines?
- Questions: which UCCA aspects required new transitions instead of adapting existing ones with feature changes?
- Questions: request for example and handling plan for “implicit units” in UCCA.
- Questions: how does UCCA’s expressive power compare to representations like DM and PCEDT for phenomena such as quantifier scope?
- Questions: what is the impact of the ratio feature on parsing performance?
- Additional comments: define “grounded semantic” before first use; consider a constituent-tree parser with rich node labels as a stronger baseline.
- Additional comments: use a richer dependency conversion (e.g., Schluter et al., SemEval SDP 2014) instead of the current method.
