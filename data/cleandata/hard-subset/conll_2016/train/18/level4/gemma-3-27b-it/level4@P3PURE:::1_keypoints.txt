- Paper presents a transition-based graph parser for UCCA, addressing graphs with non-terminals, reentrancy (remote edges), and discontinuities, and compares phrase-based, dependency, and direct graph parsing.

- Novelty claim is questionable: existing transition-based graph parsers from SemEval-era work could likely handle UCCA’s phenomena and were not sufficiently considered.

- The proposed transition system does not specifically address semantic underspecification; remote edges might be handled by label changes rather than new transitions.

- The model seems tied to a feature template, but count and unigram features appear more influential, raising questions about scheme-specific modeling gains.

- Lack of baseline comparisons: established graph parsers (e.g., Ribeyre et al., TurboParser) were not used as baselines or points of comparison.

- Phrase-based experiments need validation against other constituency parsers (e.g., Fernández-González & Martins 2015) and the Uparse reference is missing on first mention.

- A Berkeley-parser baseline using constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces should have been explored as a meaningful comparison.

- Converting UCCA to surface dependency trees is counterproductive and causes information loss; a richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al. 2014) is preferable.

- Evaluation metrics should align with those used in related SemEval shared tasks to allow fair comparison across competing systems and datasets.

- The term “grounded semantic” is introduced before being defined and should be moved earlier or clarified.

- The statement about implicit units in UCCA needs elaboration with examples or references to clarify what has no textual correspondent.

- Questions remain about how well UCCA models scope phenomena (quantifiers) versus other semantic frameworks (MRS, DRT, DM, PCEDT); more analysis and an error analysis are needed to assess how parsable and informative the scheme is.

- The three proposed principles are broad and somewhat ad-hoc; the paper’s goals (parser for UCCA vs. general semantic parser vs. a parsable semantic scheme) need clearer framing and a less dense presentation.

- Overall: work is interesting and relevant but requires methodological, empirical, and comparative strengthening before publication; suitable for discussion at a venue like CoNLL after revisions.
