This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling the rich representations of a semantic-cognitive annotation scheme, exemplified by the UCCA corpora. The authors first reveal what they believe a semantic-based annotation scheme should include: (i) being graph-based (with the possibility of multiple governors), (ii) having non-terminal nodes (complex structures - syntactic -: coordinate phrases, lexical: multi-word expressions), and (iii) allowing discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs+particles). Interestingly, none of these principles are bound to a semantic framework; they can also work for syntax or other representation levels. The authors quickly establish their work by first presenting the broader context of extensive coverage semantic parsing and then their preferred annotation scheme (UCCA). They then present three sets of parsing: (i) dedicated to phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and UCCA, (ii) dedicated to dependency parsing using UCCA, and finally (iii) the core part of their proposal, a set of tests that show that their transition-based graph parser is suitable for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

I found this work interesting, but before considering a publication, I have several concerns about the methodology and empirical rationale:

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantic-based scheme like theirs. Certainly, they are the first. However, with due respect for the work behind this scheme, it is made up of graphs with various levels of less-specific structural logic and semantic-based labels (processes, states), and none of their transition sets consider the characteristics of such graphs. Even transitions related to distant edges could have been handled by others, if the difference in label sets could have been captured (e.g., adding an affix). If we limit the problem to graph parsing, many of the 2014-2015 Semeval shared tasks (Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribere et al., 2014-2015) proposed an extension of the transition-based graph parser or an adaptation of a higher-model, and nothing prevents their use in this data set. It is mainly the use of a specific feature template that connects this model to this scheme (even though it is less dominant than computational features and unigrams). However, since the graph-parsers mentioned above are available [1, 2], I don't understand why they couldn't have been used as a baseline or source of comparison.

In the case of phrase-based tests using Uppers, it could also have been validated by another parser by Fernández-González and Martins (2015) that can produce parsing similar to good LCFRS (the reference was missing when you first launched Uppers).

Since this scheme supports a more abstract view of syntactic-semantic structure than most SDP tribanks, it was important to use the same metrics as the related shared task. In this case, many systems, models, and datasets are competing, and I think the lack of comparison with other models and parsers is detrimental to this work. Yet I found it interesting, and since we are at the crossroads in terms of next steps, I think this paper should be discussed at a conference like CONLL.

Random notes:
- Please present "grounded semantic" before page 2, you used that phrase
- Why didn't you try to stick with the constituent-tree with rich node labels and propagator traces, and then train/parse with the Berkeley parser? This could have been a good baseline.
- Converting to surface-dependent tree seems useless to me: you are losing a lot of information, here it should have been used a more enriched conversion from 'Sluter et al., 2014, Semeval SDP)
- Can you elaborate on "there may be underlying units in the UCCA graph that have no consistency in the text" or provide a reference or example?
- You mentioned other representations like MRS and DRT, this raises the fact that your scheme does not allow the modeling of quantitative scope information. It is completely comparable to other syntax-based schemes. It is more abstract than DM for example and probably less specific than the semantic level of PCODIT, but how much? How informative is this scheme really and how "parseable" is it? According to your score, it seems "difficult" but an error analysis would have been useful.
- As I said before, the 3 principles you have created may apply to many things, they seem a bit ad-hoc to me and probably require a broader (and somewhat clearer) role. What logic are you trying for: a parser that can parse UCCA? A model suitable for semantic analysis? Or a semantic-based scheme that is actually parseable? You are trying to say all those things very frequently and are likely to be confused.
