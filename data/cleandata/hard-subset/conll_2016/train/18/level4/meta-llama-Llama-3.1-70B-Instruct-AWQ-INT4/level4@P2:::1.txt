This paper presents a transition-based graph parser able to cope with the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors start first by exposing what, according to them, should cover a semantic-based annotation scheme: (i) being graph-based (possibility for a token/node of having multiple governors) (2) having non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures – syntactic -: coordinate phrases, lexical: multiword expression) and (3) allowing discontinuous elements (eg. Verbs+particules). Interestingly, none of these principles is tied to a semantic framework, they could also work for syntax or other representation layers.

Summary of the paper: The paper proposes a transition-based graph parser for parsing UCCA graphs, which are semantico-cognitive annotation schemes. The authors argue that their parser is the first to tackle this type of representation. They present three sets of parsing experiments: phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and an UCCA to constituency conversion, dependency parsing using an UCCA to dependency conversion, and direct parsing of UCCA graphs using their transition-based graph parser.

Main contributions: 
1. The authors propose a transition-based graph parser for parsing UCCA graphs, which are semantico-cognitive annotation schemes. 
2. They present three sets of parsing experiments to evaluate their parser.
3. They argue that their parser is the first to tackle this type of representation.

Strengths:
1. The authors present a novel parser that can handle the rich representations of UCCA graphs.
2. They provide a thorough evaluation of their parser using three sets of parsing experiments.
3. They argue that their parser is the first to tackle this type of representation, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Weaknesses:
1. The authors claim that their parser is the first to tackle this type of representation, but they do not provide a thorough comparison with other existing parsers that could potentially handle UCCA graphs.
2. The authors do not provide an error analysis of their parser's performance, which would be useful in understanding its strengths and weaknesses.
3. The authors' three principles for a semantic-based annotation scheme (being graph-based, having non-terminal nodes, and allowing discontinuous elements) seem ad-hoc and could be clarified and expanded upon.

Questions to authors:
1. Can you provide a more thorough comparison with other existing parsers that could potentially handle UCCA graphs?
2. Can you provide an error analysis of your parser's performance?
3. Can you clarify and expand upon your three principles for a semantic-based annotation scheme?
4. How do you plan to address the issue of implicit units in UCCA graphs that have no correspondent in the text?
5. Can you provide more information on how your scheme models quantifier scope information and how it compares to other more syntax-oriented schemes?
