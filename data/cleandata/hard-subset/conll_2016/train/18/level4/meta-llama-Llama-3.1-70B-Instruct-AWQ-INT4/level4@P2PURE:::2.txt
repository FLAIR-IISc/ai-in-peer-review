The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes that do not represent words in the string. In contrast to AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded", which the authors interpret as the text tokens appearing as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors propose several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate their performance.

Given the existence of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, it is reasonable to develop a semantic parser for UCCA. However, the introduction and background section may be improved, as they seem to argue that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation formalism worth studying. This argument is unconvincing and unnecessary, as a good UCCA parser would be a valuable contribution in its own right.

I disagree with the three criteria for evaluating semantic representation formalisms presented in the introduction. Specifically, it is unclear whether "nonterminal nodes" provide any additional expressive capacity. While it can be inconvenient to determine the head of a coordinated structure, it is unclear what information can only be represented using nonterminal nodes and not, for example, through more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant in non-"grounded" semantic representations. The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones are not clearly explained. Additionally, the term "grounded" has been used to describe multiple concepts in semantics, and the authors may consider using alternative terms, such as "anchored" or "lexicalized". Therefore, the introductory part of the paper requires more careful phrasing and argumentation.

The parser itself appears to be sound, although I did not verify the details. However, I found the evaluation results underwhelming. On primary edges, a basic MaltParser outperforms the BSP parser presented here, and the f-scores on remote edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot compute directly) are not impressive. Moreover, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees has been extensively studied under the name "tree approximations" in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit without nonterminal nodes). Several authors have proposed methods for reconstructing the edges deleted during graph-to-tree conversion, such as Agic et al. (2015), who discuss the issues involved in this reconstruction in detail. By incorporating such methods, it is likely that the f-score of the MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser) could be improved, making the strength of the BSP parser even less clear.
