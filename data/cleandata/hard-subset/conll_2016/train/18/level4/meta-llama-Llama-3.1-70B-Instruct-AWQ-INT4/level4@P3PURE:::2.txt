The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes that do not represent words in the string. In contrast to AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded", which the authors define as having text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present a number of parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

Given the existence of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, it seems reasonable to develop a semantic parser for UCCA. However, the introduction and background section may benefit from rephrasing, as they appear to argue that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation formalism worth studying. This claim is not convincing, and a good UCCA parser could be a valuable contribution in its own right.

I do not entirely agree with the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms laid out in the introduction. For instance, the contribution of "nonterminal nodes" to expressive capacity is unclear. While it may be inconvenient to decide which word is the head of a coordinated structure, it is not evident what information can only be represented with a nonterminal node and not, for example, with more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity does not arise in semantic representations that are not "grounded". The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones are also not clear. Additionally, the term "grounded" has been used to describe various concepts in semantics in the past decade, and it may be beneficial for the authors to adopt a different term, such as "anchored" or "lexicalized". Therefore, I suggest that the introductory part of the paper be rephrased and argued more carefully.

The parser itself appears to be sound, although I did not verify the details. However, I found the evaluation results to be less impressive than expected. On the "primary" edges, a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the BSP parser presented here, and the f-scores on the "remote" edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot compute directly) are not very high either. Moreover, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees has been studied extensively under the name "tree approximations" in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit without "nonterminal" nodes). Several authors have proposed methods for reconstructing the edges deleted in the graph-to-tree conversion; for instance, Agic et al. (2015) discuss the issues involved in this reconstruction in detail. By incorporating such methods, it is likely that the f-score of the MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser) could be improved further, which would make the strength of the BSP parser even less clear.
