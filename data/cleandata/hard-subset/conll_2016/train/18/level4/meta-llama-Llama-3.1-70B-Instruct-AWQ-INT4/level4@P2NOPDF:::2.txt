The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA), a specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes that do not represent words in the string. In contrast to Abstract Meaning Representations (AMRs), UCCA graphs are "grounded," meaning that the text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate their performance.

Given the existence of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, developing a semantic parser for UCCA seems reasonable. However, the introduction and background section could be improved, as they appear to argue that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation formalism worthy of study. This argument is unnecessary, and a good UCCA parser could be a valuable contribution in its own right.

I do not entirely agree with the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms presented in the introduction. For instance, it is unclear whether "nonterminal nodes" contribute any expressive capacity. While it can be inconvenient to decide which word is the head of a coordinated structure, it is not evident what information can only be represented with a nonterminal node and not, for example, with more informative edge labels. Additionally, the issue of discontinuity does not arise in semantic representations that are not "grounded." The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones are also not clear. I suggest that the authors reconsider their use of the term "grounded," as it has been used for various concepts in semantics in the past decade, and instead opt for alternative terms such as "anchored" or "lexicalized."

The introductory part of the paper should be rephrased and argued more carefully. The parser itself appears to be sound, although I did not thoroughly examine the details. However, I found the evaluation results to be underwhelming. On the "primary" edges, a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the proposed parser, and the f-scores on the "remote" edges are not impressive either. Furthermore, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees has been studied extensively in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing, albeit without "nonterminal" nodes. Several authors have proposed methods for reconstructing the edges deleted in the graph-to-tree conversion, such as Agic et al. (2015), who discuss the issues involved in this reconstruction in detail. By incorporating such methods, it is likely that the f-score of the MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser) could be improved further, which would make the strength of the proposed parser even less clear.
