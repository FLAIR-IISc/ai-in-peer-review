This paper presents a transition-based graph parser able to cope with the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors start first by exposing what, according to them, should cover a semantic-based annotation scheme: (i) being graph-based (possibility for a token/node of having multiple governors) (2) having non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures – syntactic -: coordinate phrases, lexical: multiword expression) and (3) allowing discontinuous elements (eg. Verbs+particules). Interestingly, none of these principles is tied to a semantic framework, they could also work for syntax or other representation layers.

**Summary of the Paper**

The authors present a transition-based graph parser that can parse the rich representations of the UCCA corpora, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. They identify three key principles for a semantic-based annotation scheme: graph-based structures, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous elements. The authors then present three sets of parsing experiments: phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and an UCCA to constituency conversion, dependency parsing using an UCCA to dependency conversion, and direct parsing of UCCA graphs using their transition-based graph parser.

**Contributions**

1. The authors propose a transition-based graph parser that can parse the rich representations of the UCCA corpora.
2. They identify three key principles for a semantic-based annotation scheme: graph-based structures, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous elements.
3. They present a novel approach to parsing UCCA graphs using a transition-based graph parser.

**Strengths**

1. The authors present a novel approach to parsing UCCA graphs, which is a significant contribution to the field of semantic parsing.
2. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed parser, including comparisons with other parsers and conversion-based approaches.
3. The authors identify three key principles for a semantic-based annotation scheme, which can be applied to other representation layers.

**Weaknesses**

1. The authors claim that they are the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme such as theirs, but this claim is not entirely justified, as other works have proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-model ones.
2. The authors do not provide a clear justification for why they chose to use a specific feature template, which is less influential than the count features and the unigram one.
3. The paper lacks a clear comparison with other models and parsers, which makes it difficult to evaluate the significance of the proposed approach.
4. The authors do not provide an error analysis, which would have been useful in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed parser.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a clear justification for why you chose to use a specific feature template, and how it is related to the UCCA scheme?
2. How do you respond to the criticism that your claim of being the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme such as yours is not entirely justified?
3. Can you provide a comparison with other models and parsers, such as the ones mentioned in the references, to evaluate the significance of the proposed approach?
4. Can you provide an error analysis to understand the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed parser?

**Additional Comments**

1. The authors should consider providing a clearer introduction to the concept of "grounded semantic" before page 2.
2. The authors should consider using a richer conversion, such as the one from Schluter et al. (2014), instead of the surface dependency trees conversion.
3. The authors should provide more information about the implicit units in UCCA graphs that have no correspondent in the text.
4. The authors should consider providing a more detailed analysis of the quantifier scope information in the UCCA scheme and how it compares to other syntax-oriented schemes.
