The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA), a specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes that do not represent words in the string. In contrast to Abstract Meaning Representations (AMRs), UCCA graphs are "grounded," which the authors interpret as the text tokens appearing as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

Given the existence of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, developing a semantic parser for UCCA seems reasonable. However, the introduction and background section may benefit from rephrasing, as they appear to argue that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation formalism worthy of study. This assertion is not convincing and seems unnecessary, as a good UCCA parser could be a valuable contribution in its own right.

The three criteria for semantic representation formalisms presented in the introduction are not entirely convincing. For instance, it is unclear whether "nonterminal nodes" contribute any significant expressive capacity. While it may be inconvenient to decide which word is the head of a coordinated structure, it is not evident what information can only be represented with a nonterminal and not, for example, with more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity does not arise in semantic representations that are not "grounded." The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones are also not clearly demonstrated. The term "grounded" has been used to describe various concepts in semantics over the past decade, and it may be beneficial for the authors to consider alternative terms, such as "anchored" or "lexicalized." Therefore, the introductory part of the paper could be improved with more careful phrasing and argumentation.

The parser itself appears to be well-designed, although a detailed examination of its components was not conducted. However, the evaluation results are not particularly impressive. On the "primary" edges, a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the BSP parser presented in this paper, and the f-scores on the "remote" edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot compute directly) are not very high. Additionally, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees has been studied extensively in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit without "nonterminal" nodes). Several authors have proposed methods for reconstructing the edges deleted in the graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agic et al. (2015) discuss the issues involved in this reconstruction in detail. By incorporating such methods, it is likely that the f-score of the MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser) could be improved further, which would make the strength of the BSP parser even less clear.

Overall, while the paper presents a valuable contribution to the development of UCCA parsers, it could be improved with more careful argumentation and evaluation.
