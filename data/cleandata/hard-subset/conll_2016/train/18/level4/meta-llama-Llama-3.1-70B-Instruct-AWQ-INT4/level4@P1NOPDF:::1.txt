This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, as instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors begin by outlining the essential features of a semantic-based annotation scheme, which include: (i) being graph-based, allowing a token/node to have multiple governors; (ii) having non-terminal nodes that represent complex structures, such as syntactic coordinate phrases and lexical multiword expressions; and (iii) accommodating discontinuous elements, like verbs and particles. Notably, these principles are not specific to a semantic framework and could also be applied to syntax or other representation layers.

The authors position their work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce their chosen annotation scheme, UCCA. They then present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and an UCCA-to-constituency conversion; (ii) dependency parsing using an UCCA-to-dependency conversion; and (iii) a set of experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

I found this work interesting, but I have several concerns regarding the methodology and empirical justifications that need to be addressed before considering publication. The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. While this may be true, it is essential to acknowledge that their transition set does not specifically address the unique characteristics of such a graph, which features under-specified structural arguments and semantically-oriented labels (e.g., Process, State). The transitions related to remote edges could have been handled by other transitions, assuming a difference in the label set itself (e.g., adding an affix). 

When restricting the problem to graph parsing, numerous works published after the 2014-2015 Semeval shared tasks (e.g., Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models. These existing graph parsers could have been used as a baseline or source of comparison for this work. The use of a specific feature template is what anchors this model to the UCCA scheme, although it is less influential than the count features and the unigram feature.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments using uparse, it would have been beneficial to validate the results using another parser, such as the one proposed by Fernandez-Gonzalez and Martins (2015), which can produce LCFRS-like parsing comparable to uparse. Since the UCCA scheme supports a more abstract view of syntactic-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, it would have been crucial to use the same metrics as in the related shared task. At this point in the field, numerous systems, models, and datasets are competing, and the lack of comparison points with other models and parsers is detrimental to this work as a whole.

Despite these concerns, I found the paper interesting, and I believe it should be discussed at a conference like ConLL, as it represents a crossing point in the field.

Additional notes:

* Please introduce the concept of "grounded semantic" before page 2, as it is used without prior explanation.
* Why not use a constituent tree with rich node labels and propagate traces, and then train/parse with the Berkeley parser? This could have served as a good baseline.
* The conversion to surface dependency trees seems unnecessary, as it loses too much information. A richer conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, Semeval SDP), would have been more suitable.
* Please expand on or provide a reference/example for the statement "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text."
* The mention of other representations, such as MRS and DRT, raises the question of whether the UCCA scheme allows for modeling quantifier scope information. How does it compare to other syntax-oriented schemes in terms of abstractness and underspecification? An error analysis would have been useful in this regard.
* The three principles devised by the authors seem somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from a clearer introduction. What is the main argument being made: a parser that can parse UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis, or a semantic-oriented scheme that can be parsed? The current presentation is dense and potentially confusing.
