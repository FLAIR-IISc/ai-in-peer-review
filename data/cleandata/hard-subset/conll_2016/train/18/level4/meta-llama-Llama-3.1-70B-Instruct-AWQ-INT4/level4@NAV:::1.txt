This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations of the semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, as exemplified in the UCCA corpora. The authors outline the essential components of a semantic-based annotation scheme, which include: (i) graph-based representations allowing tokens/nodes to have multiple governors, (ii) non-terminal nodes representing complex structures, and (iii) support for discontinuous elements. Notably, these principles are not inherently tied to a semantic framework and could be applied to other representation layers.

The authors provide context by discussing broad-coverage semantic parsing and their chosen annotation scheme, UCCA. They present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing using UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) direct parsing of UCCA graphs using their transition-based graph parser.

While I found this work intriguing, I have several concerns regarding the methodology and empirical justifications. The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. However, the transition sets do not specifically address the unique characteristics of such graphs, including under-specified structural arguments and semantically-oriented labels. Other works, such as those by Almeda and Martins (2014, 2015) and Ribeyre et al. (2014-2015), have proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models that could be applied to this data set.

I question the decision not to use existing graph parsers as a baseline or comparison point. Additionally, the phrase-based experiments using Uparse could have been validated using another parser, such as the one by Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015). To better evaluate the scheme's abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structures, it would have been beneficial to use the same metrics as in related shared tasks.

I have several suggestions for improvement:

* Introduce the concept of "grounded semantic" before page 2.
* Consider using constituent trees with rich node labels and propagating traces, and train/parse with the Berkeley parser as a baseline.
* Instead of converting to surface dependency trees, use a richer conversion, such as the one by Schluter et al. (2014, Semeval SDP).
* Provide clarification or examples on "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text."
* Expand on the comparison to other representations, such as MRS and DRT, and discuss the scheme's ability to model quantifier scope information.
* Consider revising the introduction to clearly articulate the main argument: a parser for UCCA, a model for semantic analysis, or a semantic-oriented scheme that can be parsed.

Despite these concerns, I believe this paper is worth discussing at a conference like ConLL, as it presents an interesting work at a critical juncture in the field.
