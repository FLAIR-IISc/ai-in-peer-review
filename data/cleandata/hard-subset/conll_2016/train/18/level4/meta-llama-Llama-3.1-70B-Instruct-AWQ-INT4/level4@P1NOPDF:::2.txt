The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes that do not represent words in the string. In contrast to AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded", a term the authors use to indicate that the text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present a number of parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

Given the existence of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, developing a semantic parser for UCCA seems a reasonable pursuit. However, the introduction and background section strike a discordant note, as they appear to argue that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism worthy of study. This argument is unconvincing and unnecessary; a good UCCA parser would be a valuable contribution in its own right.

I disagree with the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms laid out in the introduction. For instance, it is unclear to me how "nonterminal nodes" contribute to the expressive capacity of a formalism. While it may be inconvenient to determine the head of a coordinated structure, it is not apparent what information can only be represented using a nonterminal node, as opposed to more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant in SRs that are not "grounded". The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones are also not evident. I suggest that the authors reconsider their use of the term "grounded", as it has been employed to describe various concepts in semantics over the past decade. Alternative terms, such as "anchored" or "lexicalized", might be more effective. As a result, I believe the introductory section requires more careful phrasing and argumentation.

The parser itself appears to be sound, although I did not scrutinize the details. However, I found the evaluation results underwhelming. The BSP parser's performance on "primary" edges is surpassed by a straightforward MaltParser, and the f-scores on "remote" edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot compute directly) are not impressive either. Additionally, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees has been studied extensively under the name "tree approximations" in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit without "nonterminal" nodes). Several authors have proposed methods for reconstructing the edges deleted in the graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agic et al. (2015) discuss the issues involved in this reconstruction in detail. By incorporating such methods, it is likely that the f-score of the MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser) could be improved further, which would make the strength of the BSP parser even less clear.
