This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling the rich representations of the UCCA corpora, which instantiate a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors begin by outlining the key principles that, in their opinion, a semantic-based annotation scheme should cover: (i) being graph-based, allowing a token/node to have multiple governors, (ii) incorporating non-terminal nodes that represent complex structures, both syntactic (e.g., coordinate phrases) and lexical (e.g., multiword expressions), and (iii) accommodating discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs and particles). Notably, these principles are not tied to a specific semantic framework and could be applied to syntax or other representation layers. The authors then situate their work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce their chosen annotation scheme, UCCA.

The paper presents three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and an UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing using an UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) the core of their proposal, a set of experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

I found this work interesting, but I have several concerns regarding the methodology and empirical justifications that need to be addressed before considering publication. The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme like theirs. However, with all due respect to the work behind this scheme, it consists of graphs with various levels of under-specified structural arguments and semantically oriented labels (e.g., Process, State), and nothing in their transition sets specifically addresses the unique characteristics of such graphs. Even the transitions related to remote edges could have been handled by other transitions, assuming a difference in the label set itself (e.g., adding an affix). If we restrict the problem to graph parsing, many works following the 2014-2015 Semeval shared tasks (e.g., Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models, and nothing precludes their use on this dataset. It is mostly the use of a specific feature template that anchors this model to this scheme, although it is less influential than the count features and the unigram one. Given that the aforementioned graph parsers are available [1, 2], I do not understand why they could not be used as a baseline or source of comparison.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments using uparse, it could have been validated by another parser, such as the one proposed by Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015), which can produce LCFRS-like parsing comparable to uparse (reference missing when introducing uparse). Since this scheme supports a more abstract view of syntactic-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, it would have been important to use the same metrics as in the related shared task. At this point in the field, many systems, models, and datasets are competing, and I believe that the lack of comparison points with other models and parsers is detrimental to this work as a whole. Despite these concerns, I found the paper interesting, and given the current state of the field, I think it should be discussed at a conference like ConLL.

Additional notes:

- Please introduce the concept of "grounded semantic" before page 2, as it is used before being explained.
- Why not attempt to stick to constituent trees with rich node labels and propagate traces, then train and parse with the Berkeley parser? This could have been a good baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees seems unnecessary, as it loses too much information. A richer conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, Semeval SDP), should have been used.
- Please expand on the statement "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text" or provide a reference or example.
- You mentioned other representations, such as MRS and DRT, which raises the fact that your scheme does not seem to allow for modeling quantifier scope information. This makes it less comparable to other more syntax-oriented schemes. While it is indeed more abstract than DM, for example, and probably more underspecified than the semantic level of the PCEDT, how informative is this scheme, and how "parsable" is it? According to your scores, it seems "harder," but an error analysis would have been useful.
- As I mentioned earlier, the three principles you devised could apply to many things and seem somewhat ad-hoc. They would likely benefit from a wider and clearer introduction. What are you trying to argue for: a parser that can parse UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis, or a semantic-oriented scheme that can actually be parsable? You are trying to make all these points in a very dense way, which is borderline confusing.
