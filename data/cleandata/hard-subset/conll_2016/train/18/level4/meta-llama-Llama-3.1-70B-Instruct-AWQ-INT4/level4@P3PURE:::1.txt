This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, as instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors begin by outlining what they believe a semantic-based annotation scheme should cover: (i) being graph-based, allowing a token/node to have multiple governors; (ii) having non-terminal nodes that represent complex structures, both syntactic (e.g., coordinate phrases) and lexical (e.g., multiword expressions); and (iii) accommodating discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs with particles). Notably, none of these principles is tied to a specific semantic framework, making them potentially applicable to syntax or other representation layers. The authors then position their work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce their chosen annotation scheme, UCCA. They present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and an UCCA-to-constituency conversion; (ii) dependency parsing using an UCCA-to-dependency conversion; and (iii) their core proposal, a set of experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

Although I found this work interesting, I have several concerns regarding the methodology and empirical justifications that must be addressed before considering publication. The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. However, with all due respect to the work behind this scheme, it consists of graphs with various levels of under-specified structural arguments and semantically-oriented labels (e.g., Process, State). The transition sets do not specifically address the peculiarities of such graphs. Even the transitions related to remote edges could have been handled by other transitions, assuming a difference in the label set itself (e.g., adding an affix). When restricting the problem to graph parsing, many works following the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models. These existing graph parsers could have been used as baselines or sources of comparison, yet the authors chose not to utilize them.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments using uparse, another parser from Fernández-González and Martins (2015) could have been used for validation, as it can produce LCFRS-like parsing comparable to uparse. Furthermore, since this scheme supports a more abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, it would have been beneficial to use the same metrics as in the related shared task. At this point in the field, many systems, models, and datasets are competing, and the lack of comparison points with other models and parsers is detrimental to this work as a whole. Despite these concerns, I found the paper interesting, and given the current state of the field, I believe it warrants discussion at a conference like ConLL.

Additional notes:

- Please introduce the concept of "grounded semantics" before page 2, as it is used prior to its explanation.
- Why not attempt to use constituent trees with rich node labels and propagate traces, then train and parse with the Berkeley parser? This could have served as a good baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees seems unnecessary, as it loses valuable information. A richer conversion, such as the one from Schlüter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would have been more suitable.
- Please expand on or provide a reference for the statement "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text."
- The mention of other representations, such as MRS and DRT, raises the issue that your scheme does not seem to allow for modeling quantifier scope information. This makes it less comparable to other syntax-oriented schemes. While it is more abstract than DM and likely more underspecified than the semantic level of the PCEDT, the extent of this difference is unclear. An error analysis would have been useful in this regard.
- The three principles devised by the authors seem somewhat ad-hoc and would benefit from a clearer introduction. It is unclear what the authors are arguing for: a parser that can parse UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis, or a semantic-oriented scheme that can be parsed. The current presentation is dense and potentially confusing.
