- Paper proposes a transition-based graph parser for UCCA and outlines three general principles for semantic annotation: graph-based structures, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous elements.  
- Authors evaluate three experiment sets: phrase-based (Stanford + UCCA-to-constituency), dependency (UCCA-to-dependency), and direct transition-based graph parsing of UCCA.  
- Reviewer finds the work interesting but raises significant methodological and empirical concerns that must be addressed before publication.  
- The claim of being the first parser for such a semantic scheme is disputed: the transition set does not seem to target unique semantic graph features.  
- Remote-edge transitions look arbitrary since remote edges could be encoded via label differences; nothing in transitions uniquely handles UCCA’s semantics.  
- Existing graph-parsing methods (post-2014 Semeval work) could be applied to UCCA, so lack of comparison to those parsers is problematic.  
- The model’s feature template ties it to the scheme, but count and unigram features dominate, reducing the template’s distinctiveness.  
- Publicly available graph parsers (Ribeyre, TurboParser, etc.) should have been used as baselines or comparisons.  
- The phrase-based experiments using uparse need validation with alternative parsers (e.g., Fernandez-Gonzales & Martins 2015); a reference for uparse was missing.  
- Because UCCA is more abstract than many SDP treebanks, the authors should use shared-task metrics and more comparable evaluation points; current comparisons are insufficient.  
- Despite criticisms, reviewer believes the paper merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.  
- Introduce the term “grounded semantic” earlier (before page 2) since it’s used before explanation.  
- Consider a baseline using constituent trees with rich node labels and the Berkeley parser instead of skipping that avenue.  
- Converting to surface dependency trees discards too much information; suggest using richer conversions (e.g., Schluter et al. 2014 SDP).  
- Clarify or exemplify the claim about implicit UCCA units that lack textual correspondents, or provide a reference.  
- Discuss limitations relative to formalisms like MRS/DRT: UCCA appears not to model quantifier scope, making it less comparable and raising questions about informativeness and “parsability”; include error analysis.  
- The three proposed principles are somewhat ad-hoc and broadly applicable; the paper needs a clearer framing of its main goal (UCCA parser vs. general semantic parser vs. claim of parsability).
