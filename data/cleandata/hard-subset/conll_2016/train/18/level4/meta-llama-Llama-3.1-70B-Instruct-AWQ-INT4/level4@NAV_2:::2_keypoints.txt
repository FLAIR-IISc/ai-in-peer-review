- Paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA and evaluates several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser.  
- UCCA differs from CoNLL semantic dependency graphs by having nonterminal nodes and from AMR by being "grounded" (text tokens appear as nodes).  
- Given existing UCCA data, building a semantic parser is a reasonable and valuable contribution.  
- Introduction/background overstates UCCA as the only sensible graph-based SR formalism and should be more balanced.  
- The three criteria for SRs (nonterminal nodes, discontinuity, groundedness) are not convincingly justified; the expressive role of nonterminals is unclear.  
- The advantage of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones is not clearly demonstrated; the term "grounded" is overloaded and should be replaced.  
- The introductory framing and arguments need careful rephrasing and stronger justification.  
- The proposed parser appears technically sound (not fully checked in detail).  
- Evaluation is unimpressive: a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the BSP parser on primary edges.  
- F-scores on remote edges are low, and the BSP parser’s gains are unclear given strong baseline performance.  
- Prior work on tree approximations and reconstruction (e.g., Agic et al. 2015) should be considered; incorporating such methods could boost baselines and challenge claimed gains.  
- Summary of contributions: development of a UCCA parser, multiple parsing methods including a transition-based parser, and an evaluation.  
- Strengths: novel UCCA parsing, thorough evaluation, clear organization.  
- Weaknesses/questions: clarify groundedness benefits, justify nonterminal expressivity, and provide details on edge-reconstruction methods used or considered.
