This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations of the UCCA corpora, which instantiate a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors first outline the essential features of a semantic-based annotation scheme, which they argue should be graph-based, allowing tokens or nodes to have multiple governors, include non-terminal nodes representing complex structures, and permit discontinuous elements. Notably, these principles are not specific to a semantic framework and could be applied to syntax or other representation layers.

The authors position their work within the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce the UCCA annotation scheme. They then present three sets of parsing experiments: phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and an UCCA to constituency conversion, dependency parsing using an UCCA to dependency conversion, and a set of experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

While I found this work interesting, I have several concerns regarding the methodology and empirical justifications that need to be addressed before considering publication. The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like theirs. However, I argue that their transition set does not specifically address the unique characteristics of UCCA graphs, which feature underspecified structural arguments and semantically-oriented labels. In fact, other transition-based graph parsers proposed after the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks could have been adapted for this dataset.

I am puzzled by the authors' decision not to use existing graph parsers as baselines or comparison points. For instance, the transition-based graph parsers by Almeida and Martins (2014, 2015) and Ribeyre et al. (2014-2015) could have been employed. Similarly, the phrase-based experiments using uparse could have been validated against another parser, such as the one by Fernandez-Gonzalez and Martins (2015), which can produce LCFRS-like parsing.

Given that the UCCA scheme supports a more abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, it would have been beneficial to use the same metrics as in the related shared task. The lack of comparison points with other models and parsers is detrimental to this work as a whole. Nevertheless, I believe that this paper should be discussed at a conference like ConLL, as it contributes to the ongoing exploration of semantic parsing.

Some additional notes and suggestions:

- The term "grounded semantic" is used before page 2, but it would be helpful to introduce it earlier.
- The authors could have explored using constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, training and parsing with the Berkeley parser as a baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees seems unnecessary, as it loses valuable information. A richer conversion, such as the one by Schluter et al. (2014), would have been more suitable.
- The authors mention that UCCA graphs may contain implicit units without corresponding text, but they should provide a reference or example to clarify this point.
- The comparison to other representations, such as MRS and DRT, raises questions about the scheme's ability to model quantifier scope information. A more in-depth analysis of the scheme's informativeness and parsability would be beneficial.
- The three principles outlined by the authors seem somewhat ad-hoc and could be clarified in a broader introduction. It is unclear what the authors are arguing for: a parser for UCCA, a model for semantic analysis, or a semantic-oriented scheme that can be parsed. A clearer focus would improve the paper's coherence.
