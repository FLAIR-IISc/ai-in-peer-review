The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, one specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes which do not represent words in the string. Unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded", which the authors take to mean that the text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present a number of parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a broad-coverage semantic parser for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation formalism. The authors present a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses and evaluate its performance on UCCA-annotated data.

**Main Contributions**

1. The development of a broad-coverage semantic parser for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation formalism.
2. The introduction of a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses.
3. The evaluation of the parser's performance on UCCA-annotated data.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to broad-coverage semantic parsing, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their parser's performance, including comparisons with other parsing methods.
3. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and methodology.

**Weaknesses**

1. The introduction and background section could be improved by providing a more nuanced discussion of the advantages and limitations of UCCA as a semantic representation formalism.
2. The evaluation results could be more impressive, particularly with respect to the parser's performance on "remote" edges.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other parsing methods, including those that use tree approximations.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Could you provide more information on the advantages and limitations of UCCA as a semantic representation formalism, particularly in comparison to other formalisms such as CoNLL semantic dependency graphs and AMRs?
2. How do you plan to improve the parser's performance on "remote" edges, and what challenges do you anticipate in this regard?
3. Could you provide more details on the comparison with other parsing methods, including those that use tree approximations, and how your parser's performance stacks up against these methods?
