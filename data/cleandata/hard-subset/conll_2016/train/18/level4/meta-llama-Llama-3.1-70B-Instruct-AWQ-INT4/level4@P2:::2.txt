The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, one specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes which do not represent words in the string. Unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded", which the authors take to mean that the text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present a number of parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation formalism that supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors propose two complementary parsing strategies: a conversion-based approach that leverages existing parsers and a novel transition-based parser, BSP, that directly supports the full set of UCCA properties. The main contributions of this work are:

1. The development of a transition-based parser that can handle UCCA's unique properties, such as non-terminal nodes and discontinuous units.
2. The proposal of a conversion-based approach that can leverage existing parsers to parse UCCA.
3. The evaluation of the proposed parsers on UCCA-annotated corpora, demonstrating their effectiveness in parsing UCCA.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to parsing UCCA, which is a challenging task due to its unique properties.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their proposed parsers, including a comparison with existing parsers.
3. The paper contributes to the development of semantic parsing technology, which has many applications in natural language processing.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The introduction and background section could be improved by providing a more balanced view of different semantic representation formalisms.
2. The evaluation results could be more impressive, particularly in comparison to existing parsers like MaltParser.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the advantages and limitations of UCCA and its "grounded" representation.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Could you provide more information on the advantages of UCCA's non-terminal nodes and how they contribute to its expressive capacity?
2. How do you plan to address the limitations of the BSP parser, particularly in terms of its performance on remote edges?
3. Could you discuss the potential applications of the proposed parsers in real-world natural language processing tasks?
