The paper proposes the first comprehensive semantic parsers for UCCA, a specific approach to graph-based semantic representations. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can include "nonterminal" nodes that do not correspond to words in the input string. In contrast to AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded," implying that the text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The authors present and evaluate several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses.

Given the existence of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, developing a semantic parser for UCCA seems justified. However, the introduction and background sections appear to imply that UCCA is the only viable graph-based semantic representation formalism, which is an unnecessary and unsubstantiated claim. A well-performing UCCA parser would be a valuable contribution in its own right.

I disagree with the authors' proposed criteria for evaluating semantic representation formalisms. The inclusion of "nonterminal nodes" does not necessarily contribute to expressive capacity. For instance, while it may be inconvenient to determine the head of a coordinated structure, it is unclear what information can only be represented using nonterminal nodes and not, for example, through more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant in non-grounded SRs. The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones are not clearly articulated. I suggest using alternative terminology, such as "anchored" or "lexicalized," to avoid confusion with existing concepts in semantics.

The introductory section requires more careful phrasing and argumentation. The parser itself appears to be well-designed, although I did not verify the details. However, the evaluation results are underwhelming. The BSP parser is outperformed by a basic MaltParser on "primary" edges, and the f-scores on "remote" edges are not impressive. Moreover, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees has been extensively studied under the name "tree approximations" in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. By incorporating methods for reconstructing deleted edges, such as those proposed by Agic et al. (2015), the f-score of the MaltParser could be improved, further diminishing the strength of the BSP parser.
