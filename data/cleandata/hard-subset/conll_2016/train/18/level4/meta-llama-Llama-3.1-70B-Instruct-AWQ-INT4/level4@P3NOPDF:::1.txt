This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations of the semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, as instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors begin by outlining the key principles that, in their view, a semantic-based annotation scheme should encompass: (1) being graph-based, allowing a token/node to have multiple governors; (2) incorporating non-terminal nodes that represent complex structures, both syntactic (e.g., coordinate phrases) and lexical (e.g., multiword expressions); and (3) accommodating discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs with particles). Notably, these principles are not specific to a semantic framework and could be applied to syntax or other representation layers.

The authors contextualize their work within the broader field of broad-coverage semantic parsing and introduce their chosen annotation scheme, UCCA. They then present three sets of parsing experiments: (1) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser and an UCCA-to-constituency conversion; (2) dependency parsing using an UCCA-to-dependency conversion; and (3) the core of their proposal, a set of experiments demonstrating the suitability of their transition-based graph parser for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

While I find this work interesting, I have several concerns regarding the methodology and empirical justifications that need to be addressed before considering publication. The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme like theirs. However, with due respect to the work behind this scheme, it consists of graphs with varying levels of under-specified structural arguments and semantically oriented labels (e.g., Process, State). Yet, their transition sets do not specifically address the peculiarities of such graphs. Even the transitions related to remote edges could have been handled by other transitions, assuming a difference in the label set itself (e.g., adding an affix).

If we restrict the problem to graph parsing, several works published after the 2014-2015 Semeval shared tasks (e.g., Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models. These could have been used on this dataset, and it is unclear why they were not used as baselines or sources of comparison. The use of a specific feature template anchors this model to this scheme, although it is less influential than the count features and the unigram feature.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments using Uparse, it would have been beneficial to validate them using another parser, such as the one proposed by Fernandez-Gonzalez and Martins (2015), which can produce LCFRS-like parsing as good as Uparse.

Given that this scheme supports a more abstract view of syntaxico-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, it would have been important to use the same metrics as in the related shared task. At this point in the field, many systems, models, and datasets are competing, and the lack of comparison points with other models and parsers is detrimental to this work as a whole. Nevertheless, I find the paper interesting, and since we are at a crossroads in terms of where to go next, I believe that this paper should be discussed at a conference like ConLL.

Some additional points to consider:

* Please introduce the concept of "grounded semantics" before page 2, as it is used earlier in the text.
* Why not use a constituent-tree with rich node labels and propagate traces, and then train/parse with the Berkeley parser? This could have been a good baseline.
* The conversion to surface dependency trees seems unnecessary, as it loses too much information. A richer conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014, Semeval SDP), would have been more suitable.
* Please provide more information on "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text" or provide a reference or example.
* You mention other representations, such as MRS and DRT, which raises the issue that your scheme does not seem to allow for modeling quantifier scope information. This makes it less comparable to other more syntax-oriented schemes. While it is more abstract than DM, for example, and probably more underspecified than the semantic level of the PCEDT, it is unclear how informative this scheme is and how "parsable" it is. An error analysis would have been useful in this regard.
* The three principles you devised seem somewhat ad-hoc and would benefit from a clearer introduction. What are you trying to argue for: a parser that can parse UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis, or a semantic-oriented scheme that can actually be parsable? You are trying to convey all these points in a dense and somewhat confusing manner.
