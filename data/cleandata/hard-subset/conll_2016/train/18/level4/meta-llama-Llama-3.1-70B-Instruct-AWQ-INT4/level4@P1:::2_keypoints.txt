- Presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA and evaluates several methods, including a transition-based parser.  
- Highlights UCCA differences: allows nonterminal nodes (unlike CoNLL dependency graphs) and is "grounded" with tokens as nodes (unlike AMR).  
- Introduction/background overstates UCCA’s uniqueness and implies other graph-based SRs are less sensible — this is unnecessary and misleading.  
- Questions the authors' three criteria for SR formalisms; unclear that nonterminal nodes add expressive power versus richer edge labels.  
- Challenges the claimed benefits of "grounded" representations over AMR-style ones and suggests avoiding the overloaded term "grounded" (e.g., use "anchored" or "lexicalized").  
- Recommends rephrasing and more careful argumentation in the introductory material.  
- The parser implementation appears reasonable, though details were not fully checked by the reviewer.  
- Evaluation is unimpressive: a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the BSP parser on primary edges, and remote-edge F1s are low.  
- Notes related work on graph-to-tree approximations and reconstruction (e.g., Agic et al. 2015) that could improve dependency-tree baselines and reduce BSP’s apparent advantage.  
- Main claimed contributions: novel broad-coverage UCCA parsing approach, a transition-based UCCA parser, and evaluation on UCCA-annotated data.  
- Strengths: introduces a new approach to broad-coverage semantic parsing, provides comparative evaluation, and emphasizes parser development for specific SR formalisms.  
- Weaknesses: weak/misleading introduction, unimpressive empirical results, and limited discussion of implications and applications.  
- Questions for authors: justify the advantage of nonterminal nodes; respond to BSP’s weaker performance versus baselines; detail potential real-world applications of the parser.
