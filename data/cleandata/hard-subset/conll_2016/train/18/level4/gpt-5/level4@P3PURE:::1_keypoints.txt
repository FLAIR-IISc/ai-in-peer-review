- Paper proposes a transition-based graph parser for UCCA and outlines principles (graph-based, non-terminals, discontinuities) that are, however, generic and not specific to semantics.
- Claim of novelty is weak: the transition set does not address scheme-specific phenomena and existing graph parsers from SemEval (2014–2015) could likely be applied.
- Remote-edge transitions seem unnecessary—remote edges could be handled by existing transitions plus label changes—so the formal contribution is unclear.
- The model appears tied to feature templates rather than novel parsing mechanics, reducing the perceived technical contribution.
- Missing important baselines and comparisons: existing graph parsers (e.g., Ribeyre et al., TurboParser) were not used as baselines or points of comparison.
- Phrase-based experiments should also have used Fernández-González & Martins (2015) as an additional baseline; the uparse citation is missing when first introduced.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees is lossy and a richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014) should have been used.
- Evaluation metrics are inconsistent with related SDP shared tasks; using the same metrics and comparison points is necessary for fair evaluation.
- UCCA’s implicit units need clarification or examples; the paper should expand on this claim or provide references.
- The scheme’s lack of quantifier-scope modeling (compared to MRS/DRT) raises questions about how semantically informative vs. syntax-like UCCA is; an error analysis is needed.
- The three design principles feel ad hoc and require a clearer, broader motivation: is the goal to present a UCCA parser, a semantic parsing model, or to argue that semantic schemes are parsable?
- Suggest adding a baseline using constituent trees with rich labels parsed by the Berkeley Parser.
- Despite concerns, the work is interesting and merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.
