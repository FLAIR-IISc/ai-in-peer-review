This paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation. In contrast to CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA allows “nonterminal” nodes that are not tied to surface words. Unlike AMR, UCCA is “grounded” in the sense that tokens are present as nodes in the representation. The authors present several parsing approaches, including a transition-based parser that builds UCCA graphs directly, and evaluate them empirically.

Developing a semantic parser for UCCA is reasonable given the availability of the formalism and annotated data, and a strong UCCA parser could be a valuable contribution on its own. However, the introduction and background sections strike the wrong tone: they appear to suggest that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation worth pursuing. That claim is not persuasive and distracts from the core contribution. A more balanced and precise motivation would strengthen the paper.

I also disagree with parts of the three design criteria for semantic representations proposed in the introduction. For example, it is not clear that allowing nonterminal nodes adds genuine expressive power. While nonterminals may sidestep head-selection issues in coordination, it is unclear what information cannot be equivalently encoded using richer edge labels or alternative dependency structures. Similarly, the discussion of discontinuity is orthogonal for representations that are not grounded. The advantages of grounding over AMR-style representations are not convincingly argued here. Moreover, the term “grounded” is overloaded in contemporary semantics; alternative terms such as “anchored” or “lexicalized” might reduce ambiguity. Overall, the introductory argumentation should be more careful and nuanced, distinguishing clearly between representational convenience, expressivity, and learnability.

Regarding methodology, the parsing approach appears sound, though I did not verify every algorithmic detail. The main concern lies in the evaluation. On “primary” edges, a straightforward MaltParser baseline outperforms the proposed BSP parser. On “remote” edges—where a standard dependency-tree parser like Malt does not directly apply—the F-scores are still not compelling. This weakens the empirical case for the proposed approach.

In addition, there is substantial prior work on converting semantic dependency graphs to trees and then reconstructing the removed edges, often discussed as “tree approximations,” particularly in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit without nonterminals). Several papers explore methods for recovering edges deleted during graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agić et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” provides a detailed treatment of these reconstruction techniques. Incorporating such methods could plausibly improve the performance of MaltParser baselines (including LSTM-augmented Malt), which would further narrow or even eliminate the observed advantage of the BSP parser. Without a stronger comparison against such enhanced baselines, the empirical benefit of the proposed parser remains unclear.

In summary:
- The core idea—building broad-coverage UCCA parsers—is timely and worthwhile.
- The introduction overstates the case for UCCA relative to other semantic representations and should adopt a more neutral, evidence-based framing.
- The theoretical claims about nonterminals, discontinuity, and grounding need clearer justification. If the goal is improved learnability or annotation consistency rather than expressivity, that should be stated explicitly.
- Empirically, current results are not convincing. Stronger baselines that exploit tree approximations and edge-reconstruction methods should be included, and the paper should report whether such techniques narrow the gap on both primary and remote edges.

Addressing these issues—especially a fairer framing, clearer representational arguments, and a more competitive baseline comparison—would substantially strengthen the paper.
