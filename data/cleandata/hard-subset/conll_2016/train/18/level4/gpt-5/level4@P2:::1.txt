Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing for representations that require three structural properties: (i) DAGs with multiple parents, (ii) non-terminal nodes for multiword and headless units, and (iii) discontinuities. The authors use UCCA as the testbed, since it exhibits all three properties.
- They pursue two strategies: (1) conversion-based parsing by mapping UCCA to related formalisms and applying off-the-shelf parsers, and (2) a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that supports all three properties. They evaluate on UCCA corpora, including out-of-domain data, and report competitive primary-edge performance, with some ability to predict remote edges.
- Primary contributions, in order of perceived strength:
  1) An empirical study showing that transition-based methods (and especially strong sequence models) can reach reasonable primary-edge F-scores on UCCA via both conversion-based and direct parsing approaches.
  2) A direct transition system (BSP) for DAG structures with discontinuities and non-terminals, plus feature design and a dynamic-oracle training setup for UCCA.
  3) Conversion protocols between UCCA and (discontinuous) constituency and dependency representations, enabling reuse of existing parsers.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and scope: The paper articulates three structural requirements for broad-coverage semantics and shows that UCCA is a suitable testbed that stresses all three. This framing is useful beyond UCCA and applies to other layers (syntax, MWEs).
- Solid empirical coverage: The authors present three experimental settings (constituency conversion, dependency conversion, direct parsing) and evaluate in-domain and out-of-domain. The LSTM-based dependency parser yields the strongest primary-edge scores, and BSP is robust across domains while also producing some remote edges.
- Transition-based approach is well justified: The paper leverages a line of work with strong results in syntax and semantics. The dynamic-oracle training and the feature set (including the ratio feature) are thoughtfully constructed and shown to matter via ablations.
- Public release: Converters and parsers are promised for release, which would be valuable to the community working on semantic parsing and UCCA.
- Clarity of the UCCA background: The explanation of Scenes, primary vs. remote edges, and representative phenomena (coordination, phrasal verbs, MWEs) is concise and helps readers understand the representational targets.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in the parsing method relative to existing DAG parsers: The transition set appears largely generic; there is little that is UCCA-specific beyond feature templates. Even remote-edge handling could arguably be folded into labels rather than dedicated transitions. Prior DAG/AMR/SDP parsing work (e.g., SemEval 2014/2015, Almeida & Martins; Ribeyre et al.) proposed DAG-capable transition systems or higher-order models; it is not clear why those systems were not used as baselines, especially since some are available [1,2].
- Incomplete baseline coverage for the conversion setups: For the constituency path, UPARSE is a reasonable choice, but related work such as Fernández-González and Martins (2015) (parsing as reduction to LCFRS-like structures) should be included for a fairer benchmark. The current comparison set may understate what conversion-based approaches can achieve.
- Evaluation mismatch with SDP conventions: Given the overlap with SDP-style tasks, aligning evaluation to established SDP metrics would facilitate comparison across models and datasets. The custom metric is reasonable for UCCA, but the lack of bridges to well-known metrics weakens situating the results within broader semantic parsing progress.
- Lossy dependency conversion: The surface dependency conversion is extremely lossy, eliminating all non-terminals and remote edges and requiring simplistic inverse heuristics. This makes the dependency track less informative; richer conversion strategies (e.g., as in SemEval SDP conversions) could better assess the potential of reuse.
- Insufficient analysis of representational payoff and difficulty: The paper briefly claims UCCA is abstract yet grounded but does not analyze what types of errors dominate, how much of the difficulty arises from discontinuities vs. remotes vs. non-terminals, or how UCCA compares in specificity vs. DM/PCEDT. An error analysis and discussion of scope phenomena would clarify how “parsable” and informative UCCA is in practice.

Questions to the authors
- Please define “grounded semantic” earlier in the paper, before first usage.
- Why not pursue a “rich constituency only” approach (e.g., enriched labels, propagated traces) and use a strong PCFG-based parser such as the Berkeley parser as a baseline?
- Can you elaborate on “implicit units with no textual correspondent” in UCCA? A concrete example and/or reference would help clarify their nature and why they are excluded here.
- How does UCCA handle quantifier scope or similar phenomena addressed by MRS/DRT? Does the scheme deliberately leave such semantics underspecified? If so, what are the implications for downstream tasks?
- Could you provide an error analysis breaking down where BSP fails (discontinuity, remotes, non-terminals, specific categories like coordination/MWEs)? This would illuminate whether the difficulty is representational or algorithmic.

Additional comments (minor)
- Please clarify the narrative focus in the introduction. Are you primarily arguing for: (a) a parser that can handle UCCA, (b) a general model for broad-coverage semantic analysis, or (c) a semantic scheme that is practically parsable? The current introduction attempts all three and becomes dense.
- Consider adding Fernández-González and Martins (2015) when first mentioning UPARSE, along with a brief rationale for parser choices.
- The surface dependency conversion seems of limited utility; consider richer conversions (e.g., those used in SemEval SDP) to retain more semantics.
- References: double-check spellings and years (e.g., Almeida & Martins). Ensure consistency and completeness, especially for works used as potential baselines.
- Reproducibility: since some DAG parsers are available, including them as baselines would significantly strengthen the empirical case.
- Links for external baselines:

  [2] https://github.com/andre-martins/TurboParser
      https://github.com/andre-martins/TurboParser/tree/master/semeval2014_data

Overall recommendation
The paper tackles an important and timely problem and provides useful empirical evidence that transition-based methods can handle UCCA reasonably well, with clear room for improvement via stronger local classifiers. However, the methodological novelty is limited relative to existing DAG parsers, and the baseline and metric choices make it difficult to situate the contribution within the broader semantic parsing landscape. I recommend acceptance for discussion at CoNLL contingent on strengthening the experimental comparisons and clarifying the representational and evaluation choices.
