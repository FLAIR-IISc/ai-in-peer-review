This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich, semantic-cognitive representations of UCCA. The authors motivate their work by outlining three desiderata for semantic annotation: (i) graph structure (allowing nodes to have multiple parents), (ii) non-terminal nodes (to capture complex units such as coordination and multiword expressions), and (iii) discontinuities (e.g., verb–particle constructions). Notably, these properties are not inherently semantic; they also apply to syntactic and other representational layers. The paper situates itself within broad-coverage semantic parsing and UCCA, then reports three experimental settings: (i) phrase-structure parsing via a Stanford parser after converting UCCA to constituency trees, (ii) dependency parsing after converting UCCA to dependencies, and (iii) direct UCCA parsing with the proposed transition-based graph parser, which is the main contribution.

I found the work promising, but I have several methodological and empirical concerns that should be addressed before publication:

- Novelty and specificity. The paper claims to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme like UCCA. While true in a narrow sense, the transition system itself does not appear to exploit properties unique to UCCA’s semantics. The handling of remote edges, for instance, could plausibly be absorbed into a labeling scheme without dedicated transitions. If the problem is framed as general graph parsing, there are existing approaches—post SemEval 2014–2015 (e.g., Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015)—that extend transition-based or higher-order parsers to DAGs. Nothing in the paper shows these methods would be unsuitable for UCCA, and they should be used as comparative baselines. The current anchoring to UCCA seems to stem mostly from the feature templates, with limited demonstrated impact beyond unigram and count features. Since implementations are available [1,2], it is unclear why they were not included for comparison.

- Baselines for phrase-structure experiments. The phrase-based experiments using UParse could be strengthened by including another strong LCFRS-capable parser, such as Fernández-González and Martins (2015). Also, the reference for UParse is missing at its first mention.

- Evaluation metrics and comparability. Given UCCA’s abstraction level relative to the SDP treebanks, it would be valuable to report results using metrics aligned with the SDP shared tasks to facilitate cross-framework comparison. With many datasets and models now competing, the lack of common evaluation points weakens the empirical narrative.

- Overall positioning. The paper is interesting and timely—especially as the field examines how to move beyond surface-syntactic dependencies—but the absence of strong baselines and shared metrics makes it hard to assess progress.

Additional comments (in no particular order):
- Introduce “grounded semantics” before first use (it appears prior to page 2 without definition).
- Consider a constituent-tree representation with rich node labels and propagated traces, and train the Berkeley parser as a baseline; this could offer a strong point of comparison.
- The conversion to surface dependencies seems too lossy for UCCA. A richer conversion, such as the one used for SemEval SDP (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014), would preserve more of the underlying structure.
- Please expand on “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units with no textual correspondent,” with a reference and/or an example.
- The discussion of MRS and DRT highlights that UCCA does not appear to model quantifier scope. This suggests comparability to more syntax-oriented schemes. It is likely more abstract than, say, DM, and perhaps more underspecified than the semantic layer of PCEDT—but by how much? Some quantification of underspecification and an error analysis would help assess how informative and “parsable” UCCA is. The reported scores suggest higher difficulty; understanding the sources of error would clarify whether this is due to annotation properties, modeling choices, or both.
- The three guiding principles (graphs, non-terminals, discontinuities) are plausible but feel ad hoc as presented. They could apply to many representational layers. A clearer, broader introduction should articulate what the paper is ultimately arguing for: (a) a parser for UCCA, (b) a general model for semantic analysis, or (c) evidence that a semantic-oriented scheme like UCCA is practically parsable. Currently the paper tries to argue all three, which risks confusion.

In sum, the paper tackles an important problem and presents a reasonable transition-based approach for UCCA. To strengthen the contribution, I recommend: adding strong graph-parsing baselines [1,2], incorporating competitive phrase-structure baselines, aligning evaluation with SDP-style metrics, and providing targeted error analysis and clearer framing of the core claims. With these additions, the work would make a solid contribution and merits discussion at CoNLL.
