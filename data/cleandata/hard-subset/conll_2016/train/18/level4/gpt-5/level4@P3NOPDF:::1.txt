Summary
This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representational demands of UCCA, a semantic-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors motivate three desiderata for a semantic annotation scheme—reentrancy (graph structure with multiple governors), non-terminal nodes (to capture complex constructions such as coordination and multiword expressions), and discontinuities (e.g., verb–particle constructions)—and argue these are framework-agnostic. They report three experimental settings: (i) phrase-structure parsing via an automatic conversion from UCCA to constituency and a standard phrase parser (Stanford), (ii) dependency parsing via a UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) direct UCCA graph parsing with their transition-based system. The central claim is that their transition-based parser is suitable for direct UCCA parsing and outperforms indirect conversion pipelines.

Strengths
- Timely contribution: addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing with properties (reentrancy, non-terminals, discontinuity) that are underexplored in mainstream syntactic parsing.
- Clear high-level motivation for why UCCA-like structures are needed for semantic analysis.
- Empirical evaluation across multiple parsing paradigms (constituency, dependency, direct graph parsing) provides a useful perspective on design choices.

Major concerns and suggestions
1) Novelty and positioning
- The paper claims to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme like UCCA. While likely true for UCCA specifically, the transition inventory itself appears largely generic to graph parsing. Even handling of “remote” edges seems reducible to label design rather than requiring bespoke transitions.
- Several existing graph parsers could plausibly be applied to UCCA (e.g., Almeida and Martins, 2014/2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014/2015), including transition-based DAG parsers and higher-order models. Public implementations exist and should be used as baselines or comparison points:
  [1] http://www.corentinribeyre.fr/projects/view/DAGParser
  [2] https://github.com/andre-martins/TurboParser and https://github.com/andre-martins/TurboParser/tree/master/semeval2014_data
- Currently, it is difficult to assess how much of the gains come from the transition system versus the feature templates tuned to UCCA. Please quantify feature contributions (e.g., ablations), especially given indications that count and unigram features dominate.

2) Phrase-structure baselines
- The phrase-based experiments (via UCCA→constituency conversion) would be strengthened by adding a competitive non-terminal parser beyond the Stanford parser. In particular, Fernández-González and Martins (2015) report LCFRS-like parsing performance competitive with UParse; including such a baseline would help validate the conversion approach. Also, ensure the UParse reference is provided on first mention.

3) Evaluation metrics and comparability
- Because UCCA provides a more abstract view than many SDP treebanks, it is important to anchor results to widely used metrics from the SemEval 2014–2015 Semantic Dependency Parsing tasks. Even if a perfect mapping is impossible, reporting labeled/unlabeled precision/recall/F1 over comparable edge sets (or a carefully justified subset) would facilitate cross-model and cross-corpus comparisons. As it stands, the lack of shared-task-aligned metrics weakens the empirical case.

4) Dependency conversion
- The conversion to surface dependency trees seems too lossy for UCCA’s abstractions. A richer conversion, akin to Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would likely preserve more semantic structure and yield a fairer test of dependency-based baselines. If you retain the current conversion, please justify its design and quantify information loss.

5) Error analysis and difficulty
- The reported scores suggest UCCA parsing is “harder” than some syntactic or SDP formalisms, but the paper stops short of explaining why. A targeted error analysis (by phenomenon: reentrancy, discontinuity, MWEs, remote edges, non-terminals) would clarify where the transition system struggles and where UCCA’s representational choices pose inherent difficulty.

6) Scope and semantic expressivity
- You mention relations to MRS and DRT, but UCCA appears not to encode quantifier scope, which places it closer to syntax-oriented schemes in terms of certain semantic distinctions. Please position UCCA more explicitly relative to DM and PCEDT: how much more abstract or underspecified is UCCA, and with what empirical consequences? Concrete examples and citations would help.

7) Clarity of goals and contributions
- The introduction compresses several intertwined claims: (a) UCCA is a useful semantic formalism, (b) it is parsable at scale, and (c) your transition-based system is a suitable general graph parser. Please clarify which of these is the primary contribution and streamline the narrative accordingly. The three desiderata (reentrancy, non-terminals, discontinuity) are plausible but currently read as ad hoc; framing them within a broader, clearer argument would help.
- Define “grounded semantics” before first use (currently appears after page 2). Early definitions will reduce confusion.

Minor comments
- Provide an example (or citation) for “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text,” and state how such units are handled during training/evaluation.
- Consider a constituent-tree baseline with rich node labels and propagated traces parsed by the Berkeley Parser; this could offer a strong point of comparison for the constituency conversion setting.
- Fix typos and style: “semantic-cognitive,” “verb–particle,” “may contain,” “particles,” consistent capitalization of UCCA, and consistent spelling of author names (e.g., Almeida; Fernández-González).
- Clearly state data splits, training details, and hyperparameters; report statistical significance where appropriate.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and promising paper that tackles a challenging and important problem. However, the empirical case would be substantially stronger with (i) comparisons to established graph parsers and competitive constituency baselines, (ii) metrics that allow alignment with the SemEval SDP literature, and (iii) a clearer articulation of the paper’s primary contribution alongside a focused error analysis. With these additions and clarifications, the work would make a valuable contribution. As it stands, I recommend acceptance for discussion (borderline/weak accept), contingent on improved positioning and comparative evaluation.
