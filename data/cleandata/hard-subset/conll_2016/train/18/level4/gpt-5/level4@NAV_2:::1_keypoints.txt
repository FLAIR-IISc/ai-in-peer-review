- Introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA, claiming support for multiple heads, non-terminals, and discontinuity.  
- Those three requirements are not intrinsically semantic and could apply to syntax or other layers.  
- Experiments report three tracks: UCCA→constituency + Stanford parser, UCCA→dependency conversion, and direct transition-based DAG parsing (core contribution).  
- Reviewer raises methodological and empirical concerns that must be addressed before publication.  
- The transition inventory appears generic for DAG parsing and does not exploit UCCA-specific properties (e.g., remote edges could be encoded in labels).  
- Prior post-SemEval literature (Almeda & Martins; Ribeyre et al.) already offers transition-based graph parsers—unclear why these were not used as baselines.  
- The model is mainly tied to UCCA via bespoke feature templates, but count features and unigrams seem more influential.  
- Publicly available graph parsers (references provided) should have been included as baselines or comparisons.  
- Phrase-structure experiments with uparse should be corroborated with Fernández-González & Martins (2015); uparse should be cited on first mention.  
- Because UCCA is more abstract than many SDP treebanks, evaluation should use comparable metrics from related shared tasks; lack of comparability is a major weakness.  
- The paper is timely and potentially impactful and merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.  
- Define “grounded semantic” before first use.  
- Consider a constituent-tree baseline with rich node labels (e.g., Berkeley parser) as a reasonable comparison.  
- The conversion to surface dependency trees loses too much information; a richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al., SemEval SDP) would be preferable.  
- Elaborate on “implicit units with no overt realization,” providing references and examples.  
- UCCA’s lack of quantifier scope encoding makes it comparable to syntax-oriented schemes; discuss how informative and “parsable” UCCA is and include an error analysis.  
- The three design principles feel broad/ad hoc; clarify whether the paper argues for (a) a parser for UCCA, (b) a semantic analysis model, or (c) that the scheme is parsable.  
- Explain why publicly available implementations cited in the review were not used as baselines.
