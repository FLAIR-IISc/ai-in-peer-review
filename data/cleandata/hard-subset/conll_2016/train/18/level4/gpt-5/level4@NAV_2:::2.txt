The paper introduces what the authors position as the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a particular graph-based semantic representation. In contrast to CoNLL-style semantic dependency graphs, UCCA allows “nonterminal” nodes that are not surface words. Unlike AMR, UCCA is “grounded,” i.e., the representation includes the text tokens as nodes. The authors propose several parsing strategies, notably a transition-based system that constructs UCCA graphs directly, and provide an empirical evaluation.

Given the availability of the UCCA formalism and annotated resources, building a UCCA parser is a reasonable and potentially useful goal. That said, the tone of the introduction and background reads as if UCCA were the only sensible graph-based semantic representation worth pursuing. I don’t find that persuasive, and it also seems unnecessary—demonstrating a strong UCCA parser would already be a solid contribution on its own.

I am also unconvinced by the three proposed desiderata for semantic representation in the introduction. In particular, it’s unclear that “nonterminal nodes” increase expressivity per se. While choosing heads in coordination can be inconvenient, it is not obvious what content requires nonterminals rather than, say, richer edge labeling. Moreover, discontinuity is not an issue at all for representations that are not “grounded.” The benefits of grounded (as opposed to AMR-style) representations are not made clear here. Finally, “grounded” has been used for several different concepts in recent semantics literature; I would suggest adopting a less overloaded term (e.g., “anchored” or “lexicalized”). Overall, the introductory argumentation would benefit from more careful framing.

The parser design appears reasonable (I did not audit every technical detail), but the empirical results are not particularly compelling. On “primary” edges, even a straightforward MaltParser setup outperforms the proposed BSP system, and the F-scores on “remote” edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot predict directly) remain quite low. Additionally, converting graphs to trees has been explored extensively under the label “tree approximations” in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit without nonterminals). There is a body of work on reconstructing edges dropped during graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agić et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” analyze these reconstruction issues in depth. Incorporating such reconstruction techniques would likely boost the MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser) further, which makes the relative advantage of BSP even less evident.
