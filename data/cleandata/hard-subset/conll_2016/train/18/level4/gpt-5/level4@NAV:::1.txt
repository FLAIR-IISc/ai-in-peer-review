This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations of a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors begin by outlining what, in their view, a semantic-based annotation scheme should include: (i) being graph-based (allowing a token/node to have multiple governors), (ii) having non-terminal nodes (encoding complex structures—syntactic: coordinated phrases; lexical: multiword expressions), and (iii) permitting discontinuities (e.g., verb–particle constructions). Interestingly, none of these principles is inherently tied to a semantic framework; they could also apply to syntax or other representation layers. The authors quickly situate their work by first introducing the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and then their annotation scheme of choice (UCCA). They then present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser with a UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing using a UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution, experiments showing that their transition-based graph parser is suitable for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

I found the work interesting, but before recommending publication I have several concerns about the methodology and empirical support:
The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme like theirs. Strictly speaking, yes. However, with all due respect to the effort behind this scheme, it consists of graphs with varying degrees of underspecified structural arguments and semantically oriented labels (Process, State), and nothing in the transition set appears to address the specificities of such graphs. Even the transitions for remote edges could arguably be subsumed by others by appropriately modifying the label set (e.g., adding an affix). If we frame this as graph parsing, many works following the 2014–2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models, and nothing precludes their application to this dataset. It is mostly the specific feature template that ties this model to this scheme (though it appears less influential than count and unigram features). Since the aforementioned graph parsers are available [1,2], I do not see why they were not used as baselines or points of comparison.
Regarding the phrase-based experiments using Uparse, they could also have been validated against the parser of Fernandez-Gonzalez and Martins (2015), which performs LCFRS-like parsing comparably to Uparse (and a reference is missing when Uparse is first introduced).
Because this scheme supports a more abstract view of syntax–semantics than most SDP treebanks, it would have been important to use the same metrics as in the related shared task. At this stage, many systems, models, and datasets compete, and the lack of comparison with other models and parsers weakens the work overall. Still, I found it interesting, and because we are at a crossroads regarding next steps, I think this paper should be discussed at a conference such as CoNLL.

Note in random order
- please introduce “grounded semantic” before page 2; you use that phrase earlier
- why didn’t you try sticking to constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, and then train/parse with the Berkeley parser? It could have been a good baseline.
- the conversion to surface dependency trees seems pointless: you lose too much information; a richer conversion such as Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP) should have been used.
- can you expand on “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text,” or provide a reference or example?
- you mention other representations such as MRS and DRT, which raises the point that your scheme does not seem to model quantifier scope. It is thus fully comparable to more syntax-oriented schemes. It is indeed more abstract than DM and likely more underspecified than the semantic layer of PCEDT, but by how much? How informative is this scheme, and how “parsable” is it? According to your scores, it seems “harder,” but an error analysis would help.
- as noted, the three principles you propose could apply to many things; they feel somewhat ad hoc and would benefit from a broader (and clearer) introduction. What are you arguing for: a parser that can parse UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis, or a semantically oriented scheme that is parsable? You seem to claim all of these rather densely, bordering on confusing.
