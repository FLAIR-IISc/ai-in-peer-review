Summary
The paper studies broad-coverage semantic parsing for representations that require (i) multiple parents (DAGs), (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuous units. It focuses on UCCA, the only grounded scheme claimed to support all three properties, and investigates two strategies: (a) lossy conversions from UCCA to constituency and dependency formalisms to leverage existing parsers (UPARSE, Malt, and an LSTM-based dependency parser), and (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs DAGs with discontinuities and non-terminals, and distinguishes primary vs. remote edges. The authors also define an evaluation measure for BSS and report in-domain and out-of-domain results on UCCA corpora, with the LSTM dependency baseline performing best on primary edges and BSP uniquely handling remote edges.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An empirical assessment of conversion-based parsing for UCCA, including practical converters and upper bounds that quantify information loss. This enables reuse of strong off-the-shelf parsers and offers a meaningful yardstick for UCCA parsing.

2) A direct transition-based parser (BSP) for broad-coverage DAGs with discontinuities and non-terminals, along with a dynamic oracle and targeted feature design, showing that transition-based methods are viable for UCCA (and that stronger local classifiers such as LSTMs likely help further).

3) An evaluation protocol for BSS (yield- and label-matching over DAG edges), coupled with the first reported parsing results on UCCA with both in-domain and out-of-domain settings.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and practical motivation: The paper isolates three structural properties necessary for broad semantic representations and shows how they are handled in UCCA. This framing is broadly relevant beyond UCCA.

- Breadth of experimental evaluation: The work compares conversion-based approaches (to constituency and dependency) with a direct DAG parser, reports upper bounds for the conversions, and includes both in-domain and out-of-domain tests. This breadth makes the empirical findings informative and actionable.

- Evidence that existing transition-based technology applies: The LSTM dependency parser substantially outperforms Malt on primary edges after conversion, suggesting that strong local classifiers matter. This result informs future work on BSP (e.g., replacing the perceptron with neural classifiers).

- First UCCA parsing results with explicit treatment of remote edges: BSP is the only evaluated model that predicts remote edges, demonstrating feasibility of direct parsing for multi-parent structures.

- Reusable infrastructure: Public release of converters and parsers will likely catalyze follow-up work on UCCA and related semantic schemes.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to existing DAG parsers: While BSP is tailored to UCCA, most transitions mirror prior work on discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing. The special handling of remote vs. primary edges could likely be encoded in labels. The paper would be stronger by situating BSP’s novelty against existing transition-based DAG parsers and AMR/SDP transition systems, and by clarifying what is genuinely new in the transition inventory, oracle, or features.

- Missing baselines and comparisons with available DAG parsers: Several open-source graph parsers from the SDP/AMR literature (e.g., TurboParser variants for SDP, DAG parsers by Ribeyre et al., and other transition-based DAG systems) appear applicable to UCCA (after appropriate conversions) but are not included. Their absence weakens claims about the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

- Conversion choices and metrics: The conversion to surface dependencies discards substantial structure (non-terminals, remotes), making the dependency results hard to interpret. More structure-preserving reductions (e.g., LCFRS-based reductions or enhanced dependencies) could yield stronger and fairer baselines. Likewise, alignment to standard SDP-style metrics, or reporting both the proposed metric and SDP metrics on a bi-lexical projection, would ease comparison with prior work.

- Lack of error analysis and limited qualitative insight: The paper reports overall F-scores but does not analyze where systems fail (e.g., remote edges vs. non-terminals vs. discontinuities; specific constructions such as coordination or MWEs). Without qualitative analysis, it is difficult to assess whether the UCCA formulation is parsable at scale, which distinctions drive errors, and how BSP should be improved.

- Positioning and clarity: The introduction mixes goals (a parser for UCCA, a generic model for broad semantic structures, and a case for UCCA’s utility) and uses key terms (e.g., “grounded semantic”) before defining them. The three guiding properties, while sensible, are not specific to semantics; the paper could better articulate why they are the right minimal set for broad semantic coverage and how they contrast with SDP, AMR, MRS, or DRT. The discussion on phenomena like quantifier scope and implicit units is too brief to clarify representational coverage.

Questions to Authors
- Novelty and scope: Which parts of BSP (transitions, oracle, features) are novel beyond prior DAG and discontinuous transition systems? Could remote vs. primary be modeled via labels without new transitions, and what would be lost?

- Baselines: Why were existing DAG parsers (e.g., SDP parsers, TurboParser variants, Ribeyre’s DAG parser) not included as baselines via appropriate conversions? Can you add these comparisons?

- Conversions and metrics: Can you evaluate with a more structure-preserving reduction (e.g., LCFRS-based parsing or enhanced dependencies) and/or report SDP-style metrics on a bi-lexical projection to allow cross-paper comparisons?

- Error analysis: Please provide an analysis of errors by construction (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, discontinuities) and by edge type (primary vs. remote). Where do conversion-based models fail vs. BSP?

- Implicit units and semantic coverage: Could you expand on implicit units (definition, frequency, examples) and discuss how/when they will be handled? What are UCCA’s limitations regarding scope and quantification, and how do these affect downstream utility?

- Additional baselines: Did you consider a strong constituency baseline (e.g., Berkeley parser) with enriched node labels and traces? Also, Fernández-González and Martins (2015) report competitive LCFRS-style parsing; can you include this baseline?

- Future improvements: Given the strong gains from LSTM classifiers in the dependency conversion setting, do you plan to replace the perceptron in BSP with a neural classifier? What bottlenecks do you expect?

Overall evaluation
This is a timely and useful empirical study with a solid experimental framework and promising initial results on UCCA. However, the paper would benefit significantly from stronger baselines, clearer positioning of methodological novelty, richer conversions/metrics for cross-comparison, and substantive error analysis. I recommend acceptance if these issues can be addressed, as the work is likely to stimulate discussion and progress on broad-coverage semantic parsing.
