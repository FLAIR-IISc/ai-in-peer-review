The paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a particular framework for graph-based semantic representation. In contrast to CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs may include “nonterminal” nodes that do not correspond to surface words. Unlike AMR, UCCA graphs are “grounded,” which the authors define as having text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic structure. The authors propose several parsing approaches, including a transition-based parser that constructs UCCA analyses directly, and provide an evaluation.

Given the availability of UCCA and UCCA-annotated corpora, building a semantic parser for UCCA is a reasonable goal. However, the introduction and background strike me as off-key, insofar as they appear to argue that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation worth serious attention. That does not persuade me, and it also seems unnecessary—producing a strong UCCA parser would already be a solid contribution.

I do not fully agree with the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms presented in the introduction. For example, I am not convinced that “nonterminal nodes” add expressive power. While choosing a head in coordination can be inconvenient, what exactly is the information that requires a nonterminal rather than, say, richer edge labels? In addition, the issue of discontinuity does not arise for SRs that are not “grounded.” The benefits of “grounded” representations over AMR-style ones remain unclear to me. I also note that “grounded” has been used for many different concepts in semantics over the last decade, and I encourage the authors to consider another term (“anchored”? “lexicalized”?). Overall, I feel the introductory section should be framed and argued much more carefully.

The parser design appears reasonable, though I did not verify all details. That said, the evaluation results are not especially compelling. On “primary” edges, even a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the BSP parser proposed here, and the f-scores on “remote” edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot directly produce) are not very high either. Moreover, converting dependency graphs to dependency trees has been examined extensively under “tree approximations” in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit without “nonterminal” nodes). Several works propose methods to reconstruct edges lost in the graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agic et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” provide an in-depth discussion of the reconstruction problem. Incorporating such techniques would likely raise the f-score of MaltParser (and the LSTM-augmented MaltParser!), making the advantage of the BSP parser even less apparent.
