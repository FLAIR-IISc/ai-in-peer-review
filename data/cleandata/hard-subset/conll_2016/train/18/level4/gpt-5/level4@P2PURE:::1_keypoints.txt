- Paper presents a transition-based graph parser for UCCA semantic graphs and reports comparisons to constituency and dependency conversions.  
- Authors claim novelty as first parser for this kind of semantic scheme, but novelty is questionable given prior graph-parsing work.  
- The transition set does not seem tailored to properties specific to semantically oriented UCCA graphs.  
- Remote-edge transitions may be unnecessary or representable via label adjustments.  
- Existing transition-based graph parsers (Almeida & Martins, Ribeyre et al.) could be applied to UCCA but were not used as baselines.  
- The model appears tied to feature templates, with count and unigram features dominating, undermining claims of scheme-specific modeling.  
- Phrase-structure experiments using UParse lack comparison to Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015) and omit the UParse reference.  
- Dependency conversion experiments risk losing information; richer conversion methods (e.g., Schluter et al., SemEval SDP) would be preferable.  
- The paper should report evaluation metrics consistent with related shared tasks to enable comparison.  
- No error analysis is provided to explain why UCCA appears harder to parse or which constructions cause errors.  
- The three proposed principles (graph-based, non-terminals, discontinuities) are not unique to semantic representations and need broader justification.  
- The paper fails to clarify its primary claim: a UCCA-capable parser, a semantic analysis model, or the parsability of the semantic scheme.  
- The manuscript uses “grounded semantics” before defining it and should introduce the term earlier.  
- Request to expand on or exemplify “implicit units” in UCCA graphs (missing reference/example).  
- The scheme does not seem to model quantifier scope, raising questions about its semantic depth and comparability to other formalisms.  
- Overall: interesting and timely work, but methodological and empirical shortcomings (missing baselines, metrics, conversions, and analyses) limit its impact.
