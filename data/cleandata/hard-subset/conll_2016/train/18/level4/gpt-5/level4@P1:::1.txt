Summary and contributions
This paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing under three structural requirements: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. The authors (a) propose conversion procedures from UCCA graphs to constituency and dependency formalisms to leverage existing parsers, and (b) introduce a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three properties. They evaluate on UCCA corpora (in-domain and out-of-domain), showing that transition-based methods are viable for this task.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A direct transition-based parser (BSP) that handles DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities, with UCCA-oriented features and a dynamic oracle.
2) A systematic conversion-based evaluation showing how far existing constituency and dependency parsers can go on UCCA when coupled with lossy conversions.
3) An empirical study on UCCA establishing initial performance baselines, including feature ablations and out-of-domain tests.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing around three structural properties. The paper articulates why DAGs, non-terminal units, and discontinuities are necessary for broad-coverage semantics and motivates UCCA as a testbed that exhibits all three.
- Two complementary strategies. The contrast between conversion-based parsing and a direct transition-based approach is informative. It shows both the utility and limits of reusing existing parsers via conversion versus building a parser that natively supports the target structures.
- Solid experimental setup on UCCA. The paper evaluates both in-domain and out-of-domain, reports primary vs. remote edge performance, and explores different baselines (Malt variants, UPARSE, an LSTM dependency parser), as well as a feature ablation for BSP.
- Practical insights and concrete paths forward. The results highlight that stronger classifiers (e.g., LSTM-based) substantially improve scores even with lossy conversions, suggesting similar gains for BSP. The new ratio feature is a neat regularizer for node creation and could be useful beyond this work.
- Public release. Making converters and parsers available is important for a nascent task like UCCA parsing.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in the parsing machinery and lack of comparisons to prior DAG parsers. While BSP supports the three structural properties, most transitions are standard, and even remote-edge handling could arguably be encoded via labels. Several graph parsers from the SemEval SDP line of work (e.g., Almeida and Martins; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) are available and relevant, yet are not used as baselines or comparison points. This weakens the empirical case for BSP’s novelty and effectiveness.
- Conversion baselines could be stronger. For constituency parsing, UPARSE is a reasonable choice, but Fernández-González and Martins (2015) present an alternative reduction to LCFRS-like parsing with competitive accuracy; this should be included or discussed. For dependencies, the chosen conversion seems too lossy; richer conversions (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014, SemEval SDP) may retain more structure.
- Evaluation metrics hinder comparability. Given the proximity to SDP-style parsing, it would be important to also report SDP metrics (or a compatible evaluation) to facilitate comparisons with a large body of recent work. The current measures, while sensible for UCCA, isolate the results from related literature.
- Ambiguity about what is new versus what is instantiated. The paper alternates between arguing for a general broad-coverage semantic parser, a UCCA-specific parser, and a conversion-based methodology. Because the three principles (DAGs, non-terminals, discontinuities) are not inherently semantic and apply equally to syntax and other layers, the positioning reads as somewhat ad hoc. A clearer statement of scope and novelty (general parser design vs. UCCA instantiation) would help.
- Missing analysis and semantic depth. The discussion of MRS/DRT raises the question of what semantic phenomena (e.g., quantifier scope) UCCA cannot capture. How informative is UCCA for downstream semantics, and how “parsable” is it? The relatively modest scores suggest difficulty, but the paper lacks an error analysis to clarify where the challenges lie (e.g., remote edges vs. discontinuity, specific categories, domain shift).

Questions for the authors
- Baselines: Why were existing DAG/graph parsers from SDP (e.g., Almeida and Martins; Ribeyre et al.) not used as baselines, given their availability [1][2]? Do you anticipate any technical impediment to applying them to UCCA graphs?
- Constituency parsing: In addition to UPARSE, could you evaluate the reduction approach of Fernández-González and Martins (2015) to LCFRS-like parsing? If not, please clarify why and discuss expected differences.
- Dependency conversion: Can you justify the choice of the simple dependency conversion over richer ones (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014)? How much performance do you estimate is lost due to the current conversion’s information loss?
- Metrics: Would you consider reporting SDP-style metrics to allow cross-paradigm comparison? If not, please explain why such a comparison is inappropriate for UCCA.
- Implicit units: Please expand on “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text,” with examples or references, and clarify how this impacts parsing and evaluation.
- Scope of semantics: Does UCCA model phenomena such as quantifier scope? How does its level of abstraction compare concretely to DM or the semantic layer of PCEDT? A brief qualitative comparison and error analysis would help interpret the scores.
- Novelty of transitions: Beyond the remote-edge transitions and the ratio feature, what aspects of BSP are specific to UCCA/BSS versus standard adaptations of discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing?

Additional comments
- Introduce “grounded semantics” before first use and define it clearly.
- Consider a constituent-tree representation with rich node labels and propagated traces and train a strong constituency parser (e.g., Berkeley) as an additional baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependencies appears extremely lossy; if retained, please justify its utility beyond serving as a lower bound.
- Minor: ensure UPARSE is properly cited when first introduced.
