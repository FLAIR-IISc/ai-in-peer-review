The paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a particular graph-based semantic representation. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs may include nonterminal nodes that do not correspond to surface tokens. Unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are “grounded,” meaning that text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic structure. The authors present several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA parses, and evaluate them.

Given the availability of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, developing a UCCA semantic parser is a reasonable goal. However, the introduction and background strike the wrong tone by seeming to suggest that UCCA is the only graph-based SR formalism worth studying. I find this unconvincing and also unnecessary—an effective UCCA parser would be a valuable contribution on its own.

I do not fully agree with the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms presented in the introduction. In particular, it is unclear that nonterminal nodes add expressive power. While choosing a head in coordination can be inconvenient, what information is there that truly requires nonterminals rather than, for example, richer edge labels? Moreover, discontinuity is a non-issue for SRs that are not grounded. The advantages of grounded representations over AMR-style ones are not made clear. The term “grounded” itself has been used for many different notions in semantics over the past decade; I encourage the authors to consider a different term (e.g., “anchored” or “lexicalized”). Overall, the introduction should be phrased and argued more carefully.

The parser itself appears sound, though I did not verify all details. I did not find the evaluation results particularly strong. On primary edges, even a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the proposed BSP parser, and F-scores on remote edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot compute directly) are also not very high. Furthermore, converting dependency graphs to trees has been studied extensively as “tree approximations” in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 Semantic Dependency Parsing shared tasks (albeit without nonterminal nodes). Several authors have proposed methods to reconstruct edges deleted in the graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agic et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” discuss these reconstruction issues in detail. Incorporating such methods would likely improve MaltParser’s F-score (and that of the LSTM-based MaltParser), making the strength of the BSP parser even less clear.
