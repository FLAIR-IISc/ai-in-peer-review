- Paper introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA and compares direct parsing to constituency and dependency conversion pipelines.  
- Strength: tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing properties (reentrancy, non-terminals, discontinuity) that are underexplored in syntactic parsing.  
- Strength: clear motivation for UCCA-like structures and empirical evaluation across multiple parsing paradigms.  
- Concern: Novelty unclear—transition inventory appears generic and remote edges may be handled via labels; prior graph parsers could apply to UCCA.  
- Concern: Missing comparisons to existing graph-parser implementations (e.g., Ribeyre, Almeida/Martins/TurboParser) as baselines.  
- Concern: Unclear how much gains stem from the transition system versus UCCA-specific feature templates; ablation needed, especially given dominance of count/unigram features.  
- Concern: Phrase-structure baselines are limited; should add competitive non-terminal parsers (e.g., Fernández-González & Martins) and cite UParse properly.  
- Concern: Evaluation metrics not aligned with SemEval SDP standards; report labeled/unlabeled P/R/F1 over comparable edge sets for cross-corpus comparison.  
- Concern: Dependency conversion is lossy for UCCA; either use a richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al.) or justify and quantify information loss.  
- Concern: Lack of targeted error analysis by phenomenon (reentrancy, discontinuity, MWEs, remote edges, non-terminals) to explain parsing difficulty.  
- Concern: Need clearer positioning of UCCA relative to MRS/DRT/DM/PCEDT and discussion of what semantic distinctions UCCA omits (e.g., quantifier scope).  
- Concern: Intro mixes multiple claims; clarify primary contribution (formalism utility, scalability, or transition-system generality) and define “grounded semantics” earlier.  
- Minor: Provide examples/citation and handling of implicit units in training/evaluation.  
- Minor: Consider a strong constituent-tree baseline (e.g., Berkeley Parser with rich node labels and traces).  
- Minor: Fix typos, naming/capitalization inconsistencies, and provide data splits, training details, hyperparameters, and statistical significance.  
- Overall: Interesting and promising work but requires stronger comparative evaluation, standardized metrics, clearer framing, and focused error analysis; reviewer recommends borderline/weak accept contingent on improvements.
