This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations of a semantic-cognitive annotation scheme, instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors first argue that a semantic annotation scheme should: (i) be graph-based (allowing a token/node to have multiple governors), (ii) include non-terminal nodes to represent complex structures—both syntactic (e.g., coordination) and lexical (e.g., multiword expressions), and (iii) allow discontinuities (e.g., verb–particle constructions). Notably, none of these principles is inherently tied to semantics; they could equally apply to syntax or other representation layers. The paper situates the work within broad-coverage semantic parsing, introduces UCCA, and then reports three sets of experiments: (i) phrase-structure parsing using the Stanford parser after converting UCCA to constituency trees, (ii) dependency parsing after converting UCCA to dependencies, and (iii) the core contribution, experiments showing that their transition-based graph parser can directly parse UCCA graphs.

I found the work interesting, but before publication I have several methodological and empirical concerns.

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme like theirs. Strictly speaking, yes; however, the scheme comprises graphs with various levels of underspecified structural arguments and semantically oriented labels (e.g., Process, State), and nothing in the transition set appears to address properties specific to such graphs. Even the transitions for remote edges could arguably be subsumed by other transitions if the label set were adjusted (e.g., using an affix). If we focus on graph parsing per se, several works following the 2014–2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models, and nothing prevents applying them to this dataset. It is largely the feature template that ties the model to this scheme (even though it seems less influential than the count features and the unigram feature). Since these graph parsers are publicly available [1,2], it is unclear why they were not used as baselines or points of comparison.

Regarding the phrase-based experiments using UParse, they could also have been validated with the parser of Fernandez-Gonzalez and Martins (2015), which can produce LCFRS-like parses comparable to UParse (also, the reference is missing when UParse is first introduced).

Because the scheme offers a more abstract view of syntactico-semantic structures than most SDP treebanks, it would have been important to use the same metrics as in the related shared task. At this stage in the field, many systems, models, and datasets compete; the lack of comparison points with other models and parsers is detrimental to the work as a whole. That said, the paper is interesting, and given that the community is at an inflection point regarding next steps, it merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.

Notes (in random order)
- Please introduce “grounded semantics” before page 2; you use the phrase earlier.
- Why not stick to constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, and then train/parse with the Berkeley parser? That would be a strong baseline.
- Converting to surface dependency trees seems unhelpful: too much information is lost. A richer conversion, such as in Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would be preferable.
- Please expand on “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text,” or provide a reference or example.
- You mention other representations such as MRS and DRT; this raises the issue that your scheme does not appear to model quantifier scope. It is therefore fully comparable to more syntax-oriented schemes. It is indeed more abstract than DM and likely more underspecified than the semantic layer of PCEDT—but by how much? How informative is this scheme, and how “parsable” is it? Your scores suggest it is harder; an error analysis would be useful.
- As noted, the three principles you propose could apply to many formalisms; they feel somewhat ad hoc and would benefit from a broader, clearer introduction. What is the main claim: a parser that can parse UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis, or a semantic-oriented scheme that is parsable? You appear to argue all three in a very dense way, which is borderline confusing.
