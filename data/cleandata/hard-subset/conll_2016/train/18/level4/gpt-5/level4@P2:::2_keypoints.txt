Paper tackles broad-coverage UCCA parsing via two approaches: conversion to trees plus off-the-shelf parsers, and a new transition-based direct parser (BSP); LSTM-based conversion yields best primary-edge F≈69% while BSP predicts some remote edges but has lower primary scores.

Primary contribution: a transition system (BSP) for directly building UCCA DAGs supporting multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, with UCCA-specific features and a dynamic oracle.

Secondary contribution: practical, often invertible conversion protocols between UCCA and constituency/dependency trees enabling use of existing parsers and providing baselines/upper bounds.

Tertiary contribution: empirical evaluation framework and UCCA-specific metrics on in-domain and out-of-domain corpora, with promised public release of tools and converters.

Strength: first direct parser tailored to UCCA, with motivated transition inventory (remote-edge transitions, SWAP) and dynamic oracle.

Strength: solid empirical setup including strong tree-based baselines (notably LSTM stack parser) and in-/out-of-domain evaluation.

Strength: clear, reusable conversion procedures that facilitate replication and cross-formalism work.

Strength: thoughtful UCCA-specific engineering (feature ablations, ratio regularizer, attention to discontinuity via SWAP).

Strength: valuable problem framing and resource/tool release for the community.

Weakness: paper overstates representational claims favoring UCCA (non-terminals, multiple parents, discontinuity, grounding) without convincingly ruling out alternatives or clarifying “grounded” terminology.

Weakness: BSP underperforms strong conversion baselines on primary edges, making the practical advantage of the direct approach unclear.

Weakness: missing stronger conversion baselines that reconstruct deleted edges after tree parsing (as in prior SDP work), so comparisons may underestimate tree-based pipelines.

Weakness: limited analysis of where BSP actually helps—lack of fine-grained error analysis isolating phenomena (coordination, discontinuities, multi-parent cases).

Weakness: evaluation reporting could be stronger (breakdowns by label/type/unit size/continuity/num-parents, and significance testing); comparisons to other DAG parsers or stronger neural BSP variants are missing.

Question: provide concrete examples where non-terminals are indispensable and cannot be captured by enriched dependency labels or compositional conventions?

Question: clarify terminology—would “anchored” or “token-aligned” be clearer than “grounded,” and can claims be softened to avoid implying UCCA is uniquely suitable?

Question: can the authors add post-parsing edge reconstruction baselines (e.g., Agić et al., 2015) to assess how close tree-based methods can get on remote edges?

Question: please include fine-grained breakdowns and an error taxonomy showing phenomena and labels where BSP surpasses conversion methods, and analyze remote-edge prediction errors.

Question: have the authors tried stronger neural components within BSP (stack LSTMs, bi-LSTM features, beam search) which might close the gap versus LSTM conversion baselines?

Overall: paper makes a useful contribution (first direct UCCA parser, conversions, metrics), but needs softened framing, stronger/more complete baselines, and deeper analysis to justify the advantages of the direct approach.
