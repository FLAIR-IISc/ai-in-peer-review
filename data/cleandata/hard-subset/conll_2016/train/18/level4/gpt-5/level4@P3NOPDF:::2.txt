This paper presents the first broad-coverage parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation in which graphs are grounded in text tokens and may include nonterminal nodes. The authors propose several methods, notably a transition-based parser that constructs UCCA graphs directly, and provide an empirical evaluation.

The topic is worthwhile. Given the availability of UCCA and annotated data, building practical parsers is a meaningful contribution. The paper is technically competent overall, and the transition-based approach is a natural fit for UCCA’s structure.

However, I have several concerns about positioning, argumentation, and evaluation.

- Positioning and claims. The introduction reads as if UCCA is the only sensible graph-based semantic formalism to study. This framing is not persuasive and, in my view, unnecessary. A strong UCCA parser can stand on its own without arguing that other formalisms (e.g., AMR, the CoNLL semantic dependency frameworks) are inadequate. I encourage the authors to recast the introduction to present UCCA as a complementary design point in the space of semantic representations, with clear, concrete motivations and trade-offs.

- Criteria for semantic formalisms. I am not convinced by the three criteria laid out in the introduction. In particular:
  1) The role of nonterminal nodes: It is not clear what semantic distinctions nonterminals enable that could not be captured with appropriately labeled edges among terminals (e.g., for coordination). If nonterminals contribute expressive power or learnability benefits, please provide explicit examples where they encode information that is otherwise lost or becomes impractically ambiguous with only terminal nodes and richer labels.
  2) Grounding vs. non-grounding: The advantages of “grounded” (token-anchored) graphs over AMR-style unanchored graphs are not clearly substantiated. Some arguments raised (e.g., discontinuity) do not apply to unanchored formalisms in the first place. If grounding is central, please define it precisely, justify its utility (e.g., alignment for downstream tasks, interpretability), and delimit where it matters.
  3) Terminology: “Grounded” has been used in multiple senses in semantics. Consider alternatives such as “anchored” or “lexicalized,” and ensure consistent usage throughout.

- Empirical evaluation and baselines. The reported results are not compelling as presented. On primary edges, a straightforward MaltParser baseline outperforms the proposed BSP parser; on remote edges—where dependency-tree parsers cannot predict links directly—the F1 scores remain low. Moreover, the baseline comparison appears incomplete. There is a substantial literature on converting graphs to trees and reconstructing deleted edges in the context of semantic dependency parsing (CoNLL 2014/2015 shared tasks) under the heading of “tree approximations.” In particular, Agić et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” detail methods that can substantially improve recovery of non-tree edges. Incorporating such reconstruction techniques would likely raise the MaltParser baseline (and a neural/LSTM-augmented Malt variant), making the empirical advantage of BSP less clear unless BSP improves as well. Without such strengthened baselines, it is difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach.

- Methodological clarity. While the overall parser design appears reasonable, the paper would benefit from:
  1) A sharper, example-driven explanation of how nonterminals are created and used during parsing, and what phenomena they primarily target (e.g., coordination, MWE grouping, shared arguments/modifiers).
  2) A careful comparison protocol: when converting UCCA graphs to trees for baselines, specify the conversion rules and evaluation alignment. Ensure that any information discarded in conversion is either recoverable or appropriately excluded from evaluation, and discuss potential biases.
  3) A more thorough error analysis, especially for remote edges: which relations fail most often, and why? Are errors due to data sparsity, feature design, or inherent ambiguity?
  4) Statistical significance testing where applicable, and per-relation breakdowns to illuminate differential strengths and weaknesses.

Suggestions for revision:
- Reframe the introduction to motivate UCCA on its merits, with concrete, linguistically grounded examples contrasting UCCA with AMR and CoNLL-style dependencies. Explicitly articulate the benefits and costs of nonterminals and grounding, and provide illustrative cases where UCCA’s design yields clearer or more learnable structures.
- Clarify the terminology around “grounded,” and avoid arguments that hinge on properties irrelevant to unanchored formalisms.
- Strengthen the experimental section:
  • Include baselines that perform graph-to-tree conversion followed by edge reconstruction (e.g., along the lines of Agić et al. 2015).  
  • Consider a MaltParser augmented with modern features (e.g., LSTM-based representations) to ensure a competitive baseline.  
  • Report comprehensive metrics (primary vs. remote edges; per-category F1; speed/complexity).  
  • Add ablations of the transition system and features to isolate the contributions of key design choices.
- Provide qualitative analyses showing where the proposed parser excels (e.g., specific constructions where nonterminals help) and where it fails, to guide future work.

Overall, I see the potential for a valuable contribution: a first, broad-coverage UCCA parser is of genuine interest. However, in its current form, the paper’s framing overreaches, and the empirical evidence does not yet convincingly establish the advantages of the proposed approach over strong, well-engineered baselines. I recommend substantial revision focused on tempering the claims, clarifying the theoretical motivations, and strengthening the evaluation before publication.
