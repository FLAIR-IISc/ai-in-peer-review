This paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a particular graph-based semantic representation. In contrast to CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain “nonterminal” nodes that do not correspond to tokens in the string. Unlike AMR, UCCA graphs are “grounded,” which the authors take to mean that the text tokens appear as nodes in the representation. The authors propose several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA graphs, and provide empirical evaluations.

Given the availability of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, developing a semantic parser for UCCA is a reasonable and worthwhile goal. However, the introduction and background read as if UCCA were the only graph-based semantic representation worth serious study. I do not find that argument persuasive, and it seems unnecessary: a strong UCCA parser would already be a valuable contribution.

I also disagree with parts of the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms presented in the introduction. In particular, it is unclear that “nonterminal nodes” add genuine expressive power. While choosing a head in coordination can be inconvenient, it remains unclear what information can only be captured with a nonterminal, rather than with richer edge labels. Moreover, questions of discontinuity do not arise in representations that are not “grounded.” The advantages of grounded representations over AMR-style ones are not made clear. The term “grounded” itself has been used for multiple concepts in semantics over the past decade; I encourage the authors to consider an alternative such as “anchored” or “lexicalized.” Overall, the introductory discussion should be framed and argued more carefully.

The parser design appears reasonable, although I did not verify all technical details. The evaluation results, however, are not very compelling. On “primary” edges, even a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the BSP parser presented here, and the f-scores on “remote” edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot compute directly) are not very high. Furthermore, the conversion of dependency graphs to dependency trees has been studied extensively under the heading of “tree approximations” in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit without “nonterminal” nodes). Several works propose methods to reconstruct edges deleted in graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agic et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” discuss these reconstruction issues in detail. Incorporating such methods would likely improve the f-score of MaltParser (and the LSTM-based MaltParser), making the strength of the BSP parser even less clear.
