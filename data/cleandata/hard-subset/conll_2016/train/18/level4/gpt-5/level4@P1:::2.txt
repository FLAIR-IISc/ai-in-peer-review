Summary
This paper presents the first suite of broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a grounded, graph-based semantic representation that permits multiple parents, non-terminal units, and discontinuities. The authors explore two strategies: (1) conversion-based approaches that map UCCA graphs to related formalisms (constituency and dependency trees) and leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers, and (2) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs UCCA structures, including remote edges. They evaluate on UCCA-annotated corpora in-domain and out-of-domain and report primary-edge F-scores up to ~69% via the stack LSTM dependency parser after conversion.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A practical evaluation showing that transition-based methods, especially a stack LSTM dependency parser via lossy conversion, are strong baselines for UCCA parsing, achieving the best primary-edge scores among tested systems.
- Conversion protocols between UCCA and constituency/dependency trees, enabling the reuse of established parsers and providing upper bounds for conversion-based methods.
- A direct transition-based parser (BSP) supporting multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, along with feature design and a dynamic oracle.

Strengths
- Timely and useful: Given the existence of UCCA and annotated corpora, building baselines and a direct parser is valuable for the community.
- Methodological clarity: The paper carefully specifies the conversion procedures and the transition system for BSP, including handling of remote edges and discontinuities, and defines a reasonable evaluation measure for DAGs grounded in token yields.
- Comprehensive empirical setup: Experiments cover both in-domain and out-of-domain settings, report upper bounds for conversions, and include feature ablations for BSP. The results are consistent across domains, suggesting robustness.
- Insightful empirical finding: The stack LSTM dependency parser, applied after UCCA→dependency conversion, outperforms all other approaches on primary edges, underscoring the effectiveness of transition-based neural classifiers and providing a clear path to improving BSP by upgrading its classifier.

Weaknesses
- Overstated motivation and framing: The introduction reads as if UCCA is the only graph-based semantic formalism worth studying. This framing seems unnecessarily dismissive of alternatives and distracts from the otherwise solid technical contribution of building UCCA parsers.
- Questionable necessity of the three stated criteria: The argument for “non-terminal nodes” as a required representational property is not compelling; it is unclear what semantic information cannot be captured by bi-lexical dependencies with richer edge labels, especially for coordination. Likewise, discontinuity is a design concern specific to grounded representations; for ungrounded SRs it does not arise. The paper does not convincingly articulate the advantages of grounding relative to AMR-style approaches.
- Terminology: “Grounded” is overloaded in the semantics literature. Alternative terms such as “anchored” or “lexicalized” would reduce ambiguity.
- Comparative performance: On primary edges, even a straightforward MaltParser baseline (after conversion) outperforms the proposed BSP, and BSP’s F-scores on remote edges are modest. The superiority of the stack LSTM dependency parser on primary edges further suggests classifier choice may dominate the architectural contribution of BSP.
- Missing engagement with relevant work on tree approximations: The CoNLL 2014/2015 semantic dependency parsing literature has studied graph-to-tree conversion and subsequent reconstruction (“tree approximations”). For example, Agić et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” discuss reconstructing deleted edges in detail. Incorporating such reconstruction could further improve the conversion-based baselines (including the stack LSTM parser), potentially narrowing or eliminating any advantage of BSP. The paper should either implement or more fully discuss this line of work.
- Incomplete baselines: The paper notes the lack of code for existing DAG dependency parsers as a rationale for excluding them. While understandable, the absence of stronger graph-parsing baselines weakens the empirical case for BSP.

Questions to Authors
- Please justify with concrete examples why non-terminal nodes are required for semantic representation beyond what enhanced dependency labels can provide (e.g., for coordination, MWEs, or PPs). What semantics is lost with purely bi-lexical graphs?
- What are the practical, measurable benefits of grounding for downstream tasks, compared to ungrounded formalisms like AMR? Can you provide empirical evidence or stronger arguments?
- Can you incorporate or simulate tree-approximation reconstruction methods (e.g., Agić et al. 2015) to strengthen the conversion-based baselines and clarify BSP’s added value?
- Since the stack LSTM dependency parser yields the best primary-edge scores, can you report results for BSP with an analogous LSTM classifier to separate representational/transition-system gains from classifier capacity?
- Terminology: would you consider adopting “anchored” or “lexicalized” instead of “grounded” to avoid confusion?

Overall, a useful step toward UCCA parsing with clear contributions in tooling and baselines; however, the introductory claims should be tempered, and the empirical comparisons strengthened—particularly by engaging with tree approximation and reconstruction work—before the advantages of the direct BSP approach become persuasive.
