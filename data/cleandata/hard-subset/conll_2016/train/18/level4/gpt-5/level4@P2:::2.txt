Summary
This paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing for UCCA, a grounded, graph-based semantic representation that supports multiple parents, non-terminal units, and discontinuities. The authors propose two approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA structures into (discontinuous) constituency or dependency trees and leverages existing parsers; and (2) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs UCCA graphs with primary and remote edges. They evaluate both approaches on UCCA-annotated corpora (Wikipedia and 20K Leagues), introduce evaluation measures for UCCA, and report that an LSTM-based dependency parser with conversion achieves the best primary-edge F-score (~69%), while BSP attains lower primary-edge scores but can predict some remote edges.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A transition system (BSP) for directly constructing UCCA DAGs that supports multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, together with UCCA-specific features and a dynamic oracle.
- Secondary: Practical conversion protocols between UCCA and constituency/dependency trees, enabling the use of off-the-shelf parsers and providing upper bounds and baselines.
- Tertiary: An empirical evaluation framework for UCCA parsing (including measures for primary vs. remote edges) on in-domain and out-of-domain data, with public release promised.

Strengths
- First direct parser for UCCA: The transition inventory and training setup (including remote-edge transitions, SWAP for discontinuity, and a dynamic oracle) are well motivated by UCCA’s structural requirements and prior transition-based work.
- Solid empirical setup and baselines: The paper evaluates both direct and conversion-based strategies, includes strong dependency baselines (notably the LSTM stack parser), and reports both in-domain and out-of-domain results, which adds credibility and utility for the community.
- Clear, reusable conversions: The conversion procedures between UCCA and trees are carefully defined (including invertible steps where possible), which should facilitate replication and further work on cross-formalism comparisons.
- UCCA-specific features and analysis: The feature ablation study, the introduction of a ratio feature as a regularizer, and attention to discontinuity via SWAP demonstrate thoughtful engineering tailored to UCCA’s characteristics.
- Problem framing and resources: The paper draws attention to UCCA as a grounded semantic formalism and provides an initial evaluation protocol; tools and converters being released would be valuable.

Weaknesses
- Framing and claims about representation design: The introduction argues strongly that only representations with non-terminals, multiple parents, and discontinuity are appropriate for “broad-coverage semantics,” privileging UCCA over alternatives. This overstates the case: for example, the necessity of non-terminals as opposed to richer edge labeling or post-hoc composition is not convincingly demonstrated; discontinuity is irrelevant for non-anchored formalisms; and the benefits of grounding vs. AMR-style approaches are asserted rather than empirically justified. The term “grounded” is also overloaded in the literature; consider “anchored” or “token-aligned.”
- Relative performance of BSP: On primary edges, BSP underperforms strong tree-based baselines (e.g., LSTM dependency with conversion). While BSP predicts some remote edges (which tree parsers cannot produce directly), the reported remote-edge F-scores are modest, making the overall advantage of the direct approach unclear.
- Missing stronger conversion baselines: There is extensive prior work on “tree approximations” for semantic dependency parsing (CoNLL 2014/2015). Methods that reconstruct deleted edges after tree parsing (e.g., Agić et al., 2015, “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations”) could likely improve the conversion baselines substantially. Without such reconstruction, the comparison may underestimate what tree-based pipelines can achieve, further weakening the case for BSP.
- Limited analysis of where BSP helps: The paper would benefit from a detailed error analysis identifying phenomena where BSP outperforms conversion approaches (e.g., specific coordination types, discontinuous MWEs, or multi-parent constructions), and ablations isolating contributions of remote-edge transitions and SWAP in practice.
- Evaluation scope and reporting: While the proposed UCCA metrics are reasonable, additional breakdowns (e.g., by label type, unit size, continuity, and number of parents) and significance testing would make the empirical case stronger. Also, comparing to existing DAG parsers (where feasible) or to more advanced neural classifiers within BSP would clarify headroom.

Questions to authors
- Representation design: Can you provide concrete cases where non-terminals are indispensable for semantic adequacy and cannot be captured via enriched dependency labels or compositional conventions? Similarly, can you quantify the practical benefits of grounding (token alignment) over non-anchored semantics (e.g., AMR) for parsing accuracy or downstream tasks?
- Terminology: Given the ambiguity of “grounded,” would you consider “anchored” or “token-aligned,” and can you refine the argument to avoid implying that UCCA is uniquely suitable among semantic formalisms?
- Baselines: Can you incorporate post-parsing edge reconstruction for the conversion pipelines (as in Agić et al., 2015, and related SDP work) to assess how close tree-based methods can get on remote/extra edges? If not, can you discuss expected gains based on prior literature?
- Where does BSP win? Please include fine-grained breakdowns showing phenomena and labels where BSP surpasses conversion-based methods, and an error taxonomy for remote-edge prediction.
- Learning setup: Have you tried neural classifiers (e.g., stack LSTMs, bi-LSTM features) within BSP or beam search? Given the large gap between LSTM and Malt under conversion, a neural BSP might change the conclusion.

Overall evaluation
The paper addresses an important gap by providing the first direct UCCA parser and a clear set of conversion protocols and baselines. However, claims motivating UCCA as the uniquely appropriate formalism should be softened and better justified; the current empirical results do not clearly demonstrate an advantage for the direct parser over stronger conversion pipelines, especially given missing reconstruction baselines. With reframed positioning, stronger baselines, and deeper analysis of where BSP helps, this would be a solid contribution.
