- Paper tackles broad-coverage UCCA parsing with two strategies: conversion-based (tree ↔ graph) and a new transition-based BSP that builds UCCA DAGs directly; reported primary-edge F ≈ 69% (conversion, LSTM) and ≈ 59% (BSP), remote edges ≈ 13% F.  
- Contributions: transition set and BSP implementation with dynamic oracle and ablation; conversion protocols with empirical upper-bound analysis; initial cross-domain UCCA benchmark and evaluation metric.  
- Strength: clear problem formulation and a concrete transition system (NODE, REMOTE, SWAP) with UCCA-specific constraints and dynamic-oracle training.  
- Strength: thorough baseline comparisons using multiple parsers and conversion round-trip upper bounds, providing useful reference points.  
- Strength: BSP directly models remote edges and reports non-trivial remote-edge scores, demonstrating feasibility of direct prediction.  
- Strength: commitment to reproducibility (release of converters/parsers), explicit evaluation measure, and feature ablation.  
- Strength: cross-domain evaluation on 20K Leagues gives early insight into domain sensitivity.  
- Weakness: framing overstates UCCA/BSS uniqueness and under-justifies the necessity of non-terminals and “grounded” representation vs. enriched bi-lexical dependency or AMR-style formalisms.  
- Weakness: direct BSP shows limited empirical advantage—lags strong conversion baselines on primary edges and yields low remote-edge F-scores.  
- Weakness: missing stronger conversion baselines—does not incorporate prior tree-approximation/reconstruction methods (e.g., Agić et al., 2015) that could recover dropped arcs.  
- Weakness: evaluation scope is limited (drops linkage/implicit units, sentence-level only), lacks detailed error analysis and statistical significance testing.  
- Weakness: methodological choices for BSP are underexplored—greedy linear model only; neural classifiers or beam search might substantially improve results.  
- Question: what semantic distinctions cannot be captured by enriched bi-lexical dependencies with virtual nodes—please give concrete failure examples.  
- Question: can “grounded” be replaced with a clearer term (e.g., token-anchored) and can authors better articulate downstream benefits with references or case studies?  
- Question: will authors incorporate tree-approximation reconstruction methods and test sensitivity to head-selection/label-priority choices in conversions?  
- Question: have authors tried beam search or neural classifiers for BSP, and can they provide ablations isolating the ratio and REMOTE-specific features?  
- Overall: paper opens an important line with tools and benchmarks but overclaims representational uniqueness and the direct parser underperforms; recommend revision to soften motivation, strengthen conversion baselines with reconstruction, and explore improved learning for BSP.
