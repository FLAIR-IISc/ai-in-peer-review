This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich structures found in a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme, instantiated here with the UCCA corpora. The authors first lay out what, in their view, a semantic representation must support: (1) graph structures allowing multiple heads (i.e., shared arguments/relations), (2) non-terminal units for complex constructs (syntactic: coordination; lexical: MWEs), and (3) discontinuity (e.g., verb–particle constructions). Notably, none of these requirements is intrinsically semantic; they could equally apply to syntax or other representational layers. The work is then situated within the broader landscape of broad-coverage semantic parsing, followed by an overview of UCCA as the target scheme. The paper reports three experimental tracks: (i) phrase-structure parsing via an UCCA→constituency conversion and the Stanford parser, (ii) dependency parsing via an UCCA→dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution—experiments demonstrating that their transition-based graph parser can directly parse UCCA DAGs.

I found the paper interesting, but I have several methodological and empirical concerns that should be addressed before publication. The authors state they are the first to propose a parser for a semantics-oriented scheme like UCCA. While that is technically true for this particular scheme, the transition inventory itself does not appear to exploit scheme-specific properties beyond generic DAG parsing; remote-edge handling, for instance, could plausibly be encoded within the label set (e.g., via an affix) rather than requiring dedicated transitions. If we abstract the problem to graph parsing, the post–SemEval 2014–2015 literature (Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) already presents extensions of transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models that should be applicable here. In practice, it is mainly the bespoke feature templates that tie the model to UCCA (and even those seem less influential than count features and unigrams). Given that the aforementioned graph parsers are publicly available [1,2], it is unclear why they were not included as baselines or points of comparison. Regarding the phrase-structure experiments with uparse, these could also have been corroborated with the approach of Fernández-González and Martins (2015), which delivers LCFRS-like parsing quality comparable to uparse (and uparse should be cited upon first mention). Because UCCA targets a more abstract syntactico-semantic layer than most SDP treebanks, employing the same evaluation metrics as in the related shared tasks would have been important. With many systems, models, and datasets now competing, the lack of comparability to existing parsers and metrics is a significant weakness. That said, the topic is timely and potentially impactful, and I believe it merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.

Notes in random order
- Please define “grounded semantic” prior to its first use (it appears before page 2 without introduction).
- Why not remain in a constituent-tree setting with rich node labels and propagated traces, and use the Berkeley parser? That would provide a reasonable baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees seems of limited value: too much information is lost. A richer conversion (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014, SemEval SDP) would be preferable.
- Please elaborate on “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units with no overt realization in the text,” and provide a reference and/or example.
- The mention of other representations such as MRS and DRT highlights that UCCA appears not to encode quantifier scope. In that sense, it is fully comparable to more syntax-oriented schemes—more abstract than DM, likely more underspecified than the PCEDT semantic layer—but by how much? How informative is the scheme, and how “parsable” is it in practice? The reported scores suggest it is harder; an error analysis would have been helpful.
- As noted, the three design principles you emphasize could apply broadly, which makes them feel somewhat ad hoc as presented. The introduction would benefit from a clearer framing: are you arguing for (a) a parser for UCCA, (b) a model for semantic analysis, or (c) a semantic scheme that is actually parsable? The current presentation leans toward all three in a very dense way, which risks confusion.
