This paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a particular graph-based semantic representation. In contrast to CoNLL-style semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs include “nonterminal” nodes that do not correspond to any surface token. Unlike AMR, UCCA graphs are “grounded,” by which the authors mean that text tokens appear as nodes in the representation. The paper introduces several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that constructs UCCA analyses directly, and provides an evaluation.

Given the existence of UCCA and UCCA-annotated data, developing a UCCA parser is a reasonable and useful goal. However, the introduction and background sections strike the wrong tone: they seem to imply that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism worth studying. This claim is unpersuasive and, in any case, unnecessary—delivering a strong UCCA parser would be a significant contribution on its own.

I do not fully agree with the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms proposed in the introduction. For example, it is unclear that “nonterminal nodes” add genuine expressive power. While choosing a head in coordination can be inconvenient, it is not obvious what information requires nonterminals rather than, say, richer edge labels. Similarly, the issue of discontinuity does not arise for SRs that are not grounded. The advantages of a grounded representation over AMR-style representations remained unclear to me. Moreover, the term “grounded” has been used for many different concepts in semantics over the past decade; I would encourage the authors to adopt a less overloaded term (e.g., “anchored” or “lexicalized”). Overall, the introductory argumentation would benefit from more careful framing and a more balanced discussion of alternative SRs.

The parser itself appears reasonable, although I did not verify all technical details. I was not impressed by the evaluation. On “primary” edges, even a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the BSP parser presented here, and the F-scores on “remote” edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot directly predict) are not very high either. In addition, conversion from dependency graphs to trees has been extensively studied as “tree approximations,” notably in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit without nonterminal nodes). Several authors have proposed methods to reconstruct edges deleted in graph-to-tree conversion; for instance, Agić et al. (2015), “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations,” discuss reconstruction strategies in detail. Incorporating such methods would likely raise the F-score of the MaltParser (and the LSTM-enhanced MaltParser), further obscuring the empirical advantage of the BSP parser.

In sum, the work is timely and the goal valuable, but the introduction should be reframed to avoid overclaiming about UCCA as a formalism, and the empirical case for the proposed parser needs to be strengthened relative to strong baselines augmented with known reconstruction techniques.
