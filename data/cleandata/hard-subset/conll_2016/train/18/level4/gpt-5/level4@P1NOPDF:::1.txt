This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations of the UCCA semantic–cognitive annotation scheme. The authors argue that a semantic annotation scheme should (i) be graph-based (allowing nodes with multiple governors), (ii) include non-terminal nodes to represent complex syntactic and lexical structures (e.g., coordination, multiword expressions), and (iii) allow discontinuities (e.g., verb–particle constructions). Notably, none of these principles is specific to semantics; they could just as well apply to syntax or other representational layers. The paper situates the work within broad-coverage semantic parsing, introduces UCCA, and reports three sets of experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing via the Stanford parser using a UCCA-to-constituency conversion, (ii) dependency parsing using a UCCA-to-dependency conversion, and (iii) the core contribution, direct parsing of UCCA graphs with a transition-based graph parser.

I find the work interesting, but before publication I have several methodological and empirical concerns.

First, the authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme like UCCA. Strictly speaking, yes—but UCCA graphs are characterized by various degrees of structural underspecification and semantically oriented labels (e.g., Process, State), and nothing in the proposed transition system appears to exploit properties unique to such semantics. Even the transitions for remote edges could arguably be handled by existing transitions if the label set were enriched (e.g., via label affixes). If we view this as a general graph-parsing problem, several works following the SemEval 2014–2015 shared tasks (e.g., Almeida and Martins, 2014/2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014/2015) extend transition-based graph parsing or adapt higher-order models, and nothing prevents their application to UCCA. In fact, it seems that what ties the proposed model to UCCA is largely the feature template—though this appears less influential than the count and unigram features. Given that the above graph parsers are publicly available [1,2], I do not understand why they were not used as baselines or points of comparison.

Second, for the phrase-based experiments using UParse, a stronger validation would include another LCFRS-capable parser, such as the system by Fernández-González and Martins (2015), which performs comparably to UParse (also, the reference is missing when UParse is first introduced).

Third, because UCCA aims at a more abstract syntactico-semantic representation than most SDP treebanks, it would be important to report the same metrics used in the related shared tasks. At this stage, with many systems, models, and datasets competing, the lack of direct comparison points is detrimental to the empirical claims. Despite this, I find the direction interesting; as the field is at a crossroads regarding where to go next, the paper merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.

Additional notes (in no particular order):
- Please introduce the term “grounded semantics” before page 2; you use it earlier without definition.
- Why not represent the data as constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, and then train/parse with the Berkeley parser? This would be a meaningful baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees seems unhelpful: too much information is lost. A richer conversion, such as that in Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), would be more appropriate.
- Please expand on the statement “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text,” or provide a reference or example.
- You mention other representations such as MRS and DRT. This raises the issue that your scheme appears not to model quantifier scope. In that sense it is comparable to more syntax-oriented schemes. It is indeed more abstract than DM and likely more underspecified than the semantic layer of PCEDT—but by how much? How informative is the scheme in practice, and how “parsable” is it? Given your scores, it seems harder; an error analysis would be valuable.
- As noted earlier, the three principles you present could apply very broadly and feel somewhat ad hoc; they would benefit from a wider and clearer introduction. What exactly are you arguing for: a parser that can parse UCCA, a model suitable for semantic analysis more generally, or a semantic-oriented scheme that is in fact parsable? The paper seems to argue all three at once, densely, which risks confusion.
