- Paper introduces the first broad-coverage UCCA parsers, including a transition-based parser, and reports an evaluation.  
- UCCA differs from CoNLL-style dependency graphs (has nonterminal nodes) and from AMR (is “grounded” / tokens appear as nodes).  
- Building a UCCA parser is a reasonable and useful research goal.  
- Intro/background overclaims UCCA’s uniqueness and strikes the wrong tone; it should not imply UCCA is the only SR worth studying.  
- The three proposed criteria for semantic formalisms are unconvincing: nonterminal nodes may not add real expressive power compared to richer edge labels.  
- The discontinuity argument and claimed advantages of “grounded” representations over AMR are unclear or not generally applicable; the term “grounded” is overloaded and a different term (e.g., “anchored” or “lexicalized”) is recommended.  
- The introduction needs more careful framing and a balanced comparison to alternative semantic representations.  
- The parser implementation appears reasonable, though the reviewer did not verify all technical details.  
- Evaluation is weak: a straightforward MaltParser outperforms the proposed BSP on primary edges, and remote-edge F-scores are low.  
- Prior work on tree approximations and reconstruction (CoNLL shared tasks, Agić et al. 2015) could strengthen baselines; incorporating those methods would likely narrow or remove the BSP’s empirical advantage.  
- Overall: work is timely and valuable, but the authors should reframe claims about UCCA and strengthen empirical comparisons to stronger baselines.
