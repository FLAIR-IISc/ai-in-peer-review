- Tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing supporting DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities using UCCA.
- Proposes conversion procedures from UCCA to constituency and dependency formalisms to reuse existing parsers.
- Introduces BSP, a transition-based parser that directly supports DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities with UCCA-specific features and a dynamic oracle.
- Evaluates on in-domain and out-of-domain UCCA corpora, establishing baselines and showing transition-based methods are viable.
- Contribution: direct BSP parser handling the three structural properties with feature ablations and a dynamic oracle.
- Contribution: systematic conversion-based evaluation measuring how far existing parsers reach via lossy conversions.
- Contribution: empirical UCCA study with baselines, out-of-domain tests, and initial performance numbers.
- Strength: clear framing of the three structural properties and motivation for using UCCA as a testbed.
- Strength: complementary comparison between conversion-based reuse of parsers and a native transition-based approach.
- Strength: solid experimental setup reporting primary vs. remote edges, in/out-of-domain performance, and feature ablations.
- Strength: practical insights showing stronger classifiers help even with lossy conversions and a useful ratio feature for node creation.
- Strength: public release of converters and parsers to support further work.
- Weakness: limited novelty in parsing machinery and lack of comparison to prior SDP/DAG parsers weakens the empirical claim.
- Weakness: conversion baselines could be stronger; alternative reductions (e.g., LCFRS-like) and richer dependency conversions are omitted.
- Weakness: evaluation metrics prevent easy comparison with SDP literature; SDP-style metrics should be considered.
- Weakness: unclear scope and novelty—paper oscillates between general semantic parsing, UCCA-specific parser, and conversion methodology.
- Weakness: missing error analysis and discussion of semantic coverage (e.g., quantifier scope, what UCCA cannot capture).
- Question: Why were existing SDP/DAG parsers (e.g., Almeida & Martins; Ribeyre et al.) not used as baselines and are there technical impediments to applying them to UCCA?
- Question: Could the Fernández-González and Martins (2015) LCFRS reduction be evaluated as a constituency baseline, or why not?
- Question: Justify the simple dependency conversion choice over richer conversions (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014) and estimate information loss.
- Question: Will the authors report SDP-style metrics to enable cross-paradigm comparison, or why is that inappropriate for UCCA?
- Question: Expand on implicit units in UCCA with examples and clarify their impact on parsing/evaluation.
- Question: Does UCCA model phenomena like quantifier scope, and how does its abstraction compare to DM or PCEDT?
- Question: Beyond remote-edge transitions and the ratio feature, what BSP aspects are genuinely novel versus standard discontinuous/DAG parsing adaptations?
- Additional: Define “grounded semantics” before first use.
- Additional: Consider training a strong constituency parser (e.g., Berkeley) on a constituent representation with rich labels as another baseline.
- Additional: Justify retaining the extremely lossy surface-dependency conversion if kept, and ensure UPARSE is properly cited on first mention.
