- First broad-coverage UCCA semantic parsing suite, exploring conversion-based and direct transition-based approaches.  
- Two strategies: conversion to constituency/dependency trees using existing parsers, and a new transition-based BSP that directly builds UCCA graphs (including remote edges).  
- Evaluation on in-domain and out-of-domain UCCA corpora; best primary-edge F-score ~69% using stack LSTM dependency parser after conversion.

- Contribution: practical evaluation showing transition-based methods (stack LSTM via lossy conversion) are strong baselines for UCCA parsing.  
- Contribution: defined conversion protocols between UCCA and constituency/dependency trees, providing reuse of parsers and upper bounds.  
- Contribution: introduced BSP — a transition system supporting multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities — with feature design and a dynamic oracle.

- Strength: timely and useful baseline and tooling for the UCCA community.  
- Strength: clear methodological specification of conversions and BSP, including remote edges and discontinuities, and DAG evaluation grounded in token yields.  
- Strength: comprehensive experiments (in-/out-of-domain, conversion upper bounds, feature ablations) showing robust results.  
- Strength: insightful finding that stack LSTM dependency parser after conversion outperforms other tested systems on primary edges.

- Weakness: framing overstates UCCA’s uniqueness and downplays alternative graph-based semantic formalisms.  
- Weakness: the necessity of “non-terminal nodes,” discontinuity, and grounding as required criteria is not convincingly justified.  
- Weakness: “grounded” terminology is overloaded and could be replaced by “anchored” or “lexicalized.”  
- Weakness: BSP is outperformed on primary edges by a simple MaltParser baseline after conversion, and BSP’s remote-edge F-scores are modest.  
- Weakness: classifier choice (stack LSTM) may dominate gains, calling BSP’s architectural advantage into question.  
- Weakness: insufficient engagement with tree-approximation/reconstruction literature (e.g., Agić et al. 2015) that could improve conversion baselines.  
- Weakness: incomplete baselines due to excluding existing DAG dependency parsers weakens the empirical case for BSP.

- Question: provide concrete examples showing non-terminal nodes encode semantic distinctions that enhanced dependency labels cannot.  
- Question: what measurable downstream benefits does grounding provide over ungrounded formalisms like AMR?  
- Question: can you incorporate tree-approximation/reconstruction methods to strengthen conversion-based baselines?  
- Question: can you run BSP with an LSTM classifier to isolate transition-system vs. classifier effects?  
- Question: will you consider renaming “grounded” to “anchored” or “lexicalized” to avoid confusion?

- Overall: useful contribution of tooling and baselines for UCCA parsing, but introductory claims should be tempered and empirical comparisons strengthened (esp. via tree approximation and stronger baselines) before BSP’s advantages are persuasive.
