This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations of a semantic-cognitive annotation scheme, instantiated in the UCCA corpora. The authors begin by laying out what, in their view, a semantics-based annotation scheme should cover: (i) being graph-based (allowing a token/node to have multiple governors), (ii) including non-terminal nodes (representing complex structures—syntactic, such as coordinate phrases; and lexical, such as multiword expressions), and (iii) allowing discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs + particles). Notably, none of these principles is inherently tied to a semantic framework; they could equally apply to syntax or other representational layers. The paper then positions the work by introducing the broader context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and the UCCA scheme. It presents three sets of parsing experiments: (i) a phrase-based parsing experiment using the Stanford parser with a conversion from UCCA to constituency; (ii) a dependency parsing experiment using a conversion from UCCA to dependencies; and (iii) the core contribution, a set of experiments showing that their transition-based graph parser is suitable for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

I find this work interesting; however, before considering publication, I have several concerns about the methodology and the empirical justification:

- The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically oriented scheme such as theirs. Of course, they are. But with all due respect to the work behind the scheme, the formalism consists of graphs with various levels of underspecified structural arguments and semantically oriented labels (Process, State), and nothing in the transition set appears to address any specificity of such graphs. Even the transitions related to remote edges could, in principle, be handled by the existing ones, assuming a difference in the label set itself (e.g., adding an affix). If we restrict the problem to graph parsing, many works following the 2014–2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014–2015) proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models, and nothing precludes their application to this dataset. It is mostly the use of a specific feature template that anchors this model to this scheme (even though it is less influential than the count features and the unigram one). Since the above-mentioned graph parsers are available [1,2], I do not understand why they were not used as baselines or points of comparison.

- Regarding the phrase-based experiments using uparse, these could also have been validated with another parser by Fernández-González and Martins (2015), which can produce LCFRS-like parses as good as uparse (the uparse reference is missing when it is first introduced).

- Because this scheme supports a more abstract view of syntactico-semantic structures than most of the SDP treebanks, it would have been important to use the same metrics as in the related shared task. At this point in the field, many systems, models, and datasets are competing; the lack of comparison points with other models and parsers is detrimental to the work as a whole. That said, I do find it interesting, and because we are at a crossroads in terms of where to go next, this paper merits discussion at a venue such as CoNLL.

Additional notes (in random order):
- Please introduce “grounded semantics” before page 2; you use the term earlier.
- Why not stick to constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, and then train/parse with the Berkeley Parser? This could have been a good baseline.
- The conversion to surface dependency trees seems useless: you lose too much information. A richer conversion, such as the one from Schluter et al. (2014, SemEval SDP), should have been used.
- Please expand on “UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text,” or provide a reference or example.
- You mention other representations such as MRS and DRT. This raises the issue that your scheme does not seem to model quantifier scope information. It is thus fully comparable to more syntax-oriented schemes. It is indeed more abstract than DM, and probably more underspecified than the semantic level of the PCEDT, but by how much? How informative is this scheme, and how “parsable” is it? According to your scores, it seems “harder,” but an error analysis would have been useful.
- As noted earlier, the three principles you propose could apply to many things; they feel a bit ad hoc and would benefit from being situated in a wider (and clearer) introduction. What are you trying to argue for: a parser that can parse UCCA? a model suitable for semantic analysis? or a semantic-oriented scheme that is actually parsable? You appear to be arguing all of these, in a very dense way, which is close to being confusing.
