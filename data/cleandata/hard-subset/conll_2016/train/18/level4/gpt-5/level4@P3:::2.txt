Summary and Contributions
This paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing for UCCA, a grounded, graph-based semantic representation that permits multiple parents, non-terminal units, and discontinuities. The authors propose two strategies: (a) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA graphs to more restrictive formalisms (constituency or dependency trees) and back, leveraging existing parsers; and (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs UCCA DAGs, including remote edges. Experiments on UCCA English Wikipedia (in-domain) and 20K Leagues (out-of-domain) report primary-edge labeled F-scores up to about 69% via the LSTM dependency parser after conversion, and around 59% for the direct BSP; BSP also predicts some remote edges (~13% F).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A transition set and implementation for a direct, transition-based UCCA parser that supports non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents (remote edges), with dynamic-oracle training and an informative feature ablation.
2) Conversion protocols between UCCA and constituency/dependency trees, with empirical evaluation and upper-bound analysis quantifying information loss.
3) An initial empirical benchmark for UCCA parsing across domains, including an evaluation metric for BSS and comparisons to strong baselines.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and technical design: The paper crisply specifies the three structural properties of interest and translates them into a concrete transition system (including NODE, REMOTE, and SWAP) with constraints appropriate for UCCA (e.g., one primary parent). The dynamic oracle and feature design are thoughtfully adapted from prior work.
- Thorough baseline comparisons and analysis: The conversion-based route is well-motivated and tested with multiple parsers (Malt, LSTM, UPARSE), reporting upper bounds from conversion round-trips. This establishes useful reference points for future UCCA work.
- Direct modeling of remote edges: Unlike conversion baselines (which drop remotes), BSP natively predicts remote edges and reports non-trivial scores. This is important for UCCA’s DAG nature and demonstrates feasibility of direct prediction.
- Reproducibility and resources: The promise to release converters and parsers, plus the explicit evaluation measure and feature ablation, will help establish UCCA parsing as a task and facilitate follow-up work.
- Cross-domain evaluation: Results on 20K Leagues show robustness trends and give an early sense of domain sensitivity for UCCA parsing.

Weaknesses
- Overstated positioning and insufficient justification of representational criteria: The introduction suggests that UCCA (or BSS with the specific three criteria) is uniquely appropriate for semantic parsing. The necessity of non-terminals as a representational primitive is argued largely from convenience (e.g., coordination head choice) rather than clear expressive inadequacy of bi-lexical dependencies augmented with labels. Similarly, the discussion of “grounded” representations lacks a precise argument about downstream advantages relative to non-anchored formalisms like AMR. The tone would benefit from tempering claims and acknowledging viable alternatives.
- Limited empirical advantage of the direct parser: On primary edges, BSP underperforms strong conversion-based baselines (notably the LSTM arc-standard parser achieving ~69% vs. BSP ~59% F), and remote-edge F-scores remain low (~13%). As presented, the direct approach’s empirical benefits are not yet compelling.
- Missing stronger conversion baselines: The paper evaluates only naive graph-to-tree conversions (which necessarily drop remote edges) and simple rule-based reconstruction of non-terminals. Prior work on tree approximations and reconstruction in semantic dependency parsing (e.g., Agić et al., 2015, “Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations”) proposes methods to recover deleted arcs; integrating such techniques would likely improve the conversion baselines and provide a fairer comparison to BSP.
- Evaluation scope and analysis: The work drops linkage and implicit units and evaluates at sentence level, which may understate the difficulty of UCCA’s inter-sentential phenomena. Error analysis is limited; more detailed breakdowns (by structure type, label, discontinuity, and sentence length) would clarify where BSP helps or fails, and how conversion losses distribute across phenomena. Statistical significance tests are not reported.
- Methodological choices not fully explored: BSP is trained greedily with a linear model; given the strong gains of LSTM features in the conversion baseline, a neural classifier or beam search for BSP could materially change conclusions. The paper hints at this but does not provide preliminary evidence.

Questions to Authors
- Representation and motivation:
  - What specific semantic distinctions cannot be captured adequately by bi-lexical dependencies with enriched labels and possible virtual nodes for coordination? Can you provide concrete, failure-mode examples beyond head-choice conventions?
  - Could you replace “grounded” with a more precise term (e.g., token-anchored) and more concretely articulate the downstream benefits (alignment-free extraction, span-level semantics), ideally with references or small case studies?
- Baselines and conversions:
  - Can you incorporate tree-approximation reconstruction methods (e.g., Agić et al., 2015) to recover dropped edges after conversion? How would this affect the LSTM and Malt baselines and your conclusions?
  - For dependency conversions, how sensitive are results to the label-priority order used to select heads? Could data-driven head selection (or learned per-label priorities) reduce conversion loss?
- Direct parser:
  - Have you tried beam search or a neural classifier for BSP? Even small beams or bi-LSTM features could alter performance, particularly for remote edges and discontinuities.
  - Can you provide ablations isolating the impact of the novel ratio feature and REMOTE-specific features on both primary and remote edges, and more analysis of typical remote-edge errors?

Overall Evaluation
This paper opens an important line of work by operationalizing UCCA parsing with both direct and conversion-based approaches, and by establishing baselines and tools. However, the introduction’s framing overstates the uniqueness of UCCA/BSS and underspecifies the practical advantages of non-terminals and token anchoring. Empirically, the direct parser currently lags strong conversion-based baselines, and the evaluation omits stronger reconstruction methods that could make the comparison more stringent. I recommend revision focused on (a) softening and sharpening the motivation; (b) strengthening baselines with reconstruction; and (c) exploring improved learning for BSP (beam/neural). With these changes, the paper would make a solid and well-substantiated contribution.
