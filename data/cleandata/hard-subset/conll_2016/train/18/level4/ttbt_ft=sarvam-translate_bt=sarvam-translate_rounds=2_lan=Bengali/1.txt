This paper presents a transition-based graph parser capable of handling rich representations of a semantic-cognitive annotation scheme, for example, a UCCA corpus. The authors first state that they believe a semantic-based annotation scheme should include: (i) graph-based (with the possibility of multiple governors), (ii) non-terminal nodes (complex structures - syntactic -: coordinate phrases, lexical: multi-word expressions), and (iii) discontinuous elements (e.g., verbs + particles). Interestingly, none of these principles are bound to a semantic structure; they can also work for syntax or other representation levels. The authors first establish their work quickly by presenting a broader coverage of the larger context of semantic parsing and then their preferred annotation scheme (UCCA). They then present three sets of parsing: (i) dedicated to phrase-based parsing using Stanford parser and UCCA, (ii) dedicated to dependency parsing using UCCA, and finally (iii) the core part of their proposal, a set of tests that show that their transition-based graph parser is suitable for direct parsing of UCCA graphs.

I found this work interesting, but before considering a publication, I have several concerns about the methodology and empirical arguments:

The authors first claim to propose a parser for a semantic-based scheme like theirs. Of course, they are the first. However, with due respect to the work behind this scheme, it is formed by graphs with various levels of less-specific structural arguments and semantic-based labels (state, process), and none of their transition sets consider the characteristics of such graphs. Even transitions related to distant edges can be handled by others, if the difference in label sets could be captured (e.g., adding an affix). If we limit the problem to graph parsing, many 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribere et al., 2014-2015) proposed an extension of the transition-based graph parser or an adaptation of a higher-model, and this data set does not hinder their use. It is mainly the use of a specific feature template that connects this model to this scheme (even though it is less dominant than computational features and unigrams). However, since the graph-parsers mentioned above are available [1, 2], I don't understand why they couldn't have been used as a baseline or source of comparison.

In the case of phrase-based testing using Upper, it could also be validated by other parsers by Fernandes-González and Martins (2015) that can produce similar parsing to good LCFRS (the reference was missing when you first launched Upper).

Since this scheme supports a more abstract view of syntactic-semantic structure than most SDP tribanks, it was important to use the same metrics as the related shared task. In this case, many systems, models, and datasets are competing, and I think the lack of comparison with other models and parsers is detrimental to this work. Yet I found it interesting, and since we are at a crossroads in terms of next steps, I think this paper should be discussed at a conference like Conl.

Random Note:
- Please present "grounded semantic" before page 2, you used that phrase
- Why didn't you try to stick to the component-tree with rich node labels and propagator traces and then train/parse with the Berkeley parser? This could have been a good baseline.
- Converting to a dependency-based tree on the page seems useless to me: you are losing a lot of information, here it should have been used a more enriched conversion from 'Sluter et al., 2014, SemEval SDP)
- Can you elaborate on "UCCA graphs may have underlying units that have no consistency with the text" or give a reference or example?
- You mentioned other representations like MRS and DRT, this raises the fact that your scheme does not allow the modeling of quantitative opportunity information. It is completely comparable to other syntax-based schemes. It is more abstract than DM for example and probably less specific than the semantic level of POCODIT, but how much? How informative is this scheme really and how "parsable" is it? According to your score, it seems "difficult" but an error analysis would have been useful.
- As I said before, the 3 principles you have created may apply to many things, they seem a bit ad-hoc to me and probably need a broader (and somewhat clearer) role. What logic are you trying for: a parser that can parse UCCA? A model suitable for semantic analysis? Or a semantic-based scheme that is actually parsable? You are trying to say all those things very frequently and are likely to be confused.
