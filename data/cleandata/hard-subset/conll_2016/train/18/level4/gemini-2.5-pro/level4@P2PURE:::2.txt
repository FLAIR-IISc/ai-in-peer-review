This paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA), a graph-based semantic representation. The authors position UCCA in relation to other formalisms, noting that its graphs can contain 'nonterminal' nodes not anchored to words (unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs) and are 'grounded' in the text tokens (unlike AMRs). The core of the paper introduces and evaluates several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser designed to directly construct UCCA graphs.

While the development of a UCCA parser is a well-motivated and valuable goal, the paper's introduction is unconvincing in its attempt to establish UCCA's superiority over other graph-based semantic representations. A strong UCCA parser would be a significant contribution on its own, without needing to diminish other formalisms. The three criteria for semantic representations proposed by the authors are not entirely persuasive. For instance, it is unclear whether 'nonterminal' nodes add expressive capacity that could not be achieved through other means, such as more informative edge labels to handle phenomena like coordination. Similarly, the challenge of representing discontinuity is presented as a key motivator, yet this issue primarily arises for representations that are 'grounded' in the first place. The advantages of such 'grounded' representations over AMR-style approaches are not clearly articulated. Furthermore, given that the term 'grounded' has been used for many different concepts in semantics, a more specific term like 'anchored' or 'lexicalized' might be preferable. Consequently, the entire introductory section requires a more careful and nuanced framing of UCCA's role and contributions.

Regarding the proposed parser, while the model itself appears sound, the empirical evaluation is not compelling. The results show that a standard MaltParser outperforms the proposed BSP parser on 'primary' edges. While the BSP parser can handle 'remote' edges, which a dependency-tree parser cannot produce directly, the reported F-scores for these edges are modest.

More significantly, the comparison against MaltParser is incomplete. The task of converting dependency trees back into dependency graphs (often called 'tree approximations') was extensively studied in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (albeit for formalisms without 'nonterminal' nodes). Researchers have developed methods to reconstruct non-tree edges that are lost in the graph-to-tree conversion; for example, Agic et al. (2015) in "Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations" discuss these reconstruction techniques in detail. Applying such methods to the MaltParser and LSTM-based MaltParser baselines would likely improve their graph-level scores significantly. This omission weakens the evidence for the proposed parser's effectiveness, as it is unclear whether it offers a substantial advantage over a strong, appropriately adapted baseline.
