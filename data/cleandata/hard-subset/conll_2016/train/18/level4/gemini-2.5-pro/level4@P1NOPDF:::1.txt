This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich representations of the UCCA semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors begin by outlining their criteria for a comprehensive semantic annotation scheme: (1) being graph-based, allowing tokens/nodes to have multiple governors; (2) including non-terminal nodes to represent complex structures like coordinate phrases or multi-word expressions; and (3) allowing for discontinuous elements (e.g., verb-particle constructions). Interestingly, these principles are not inherently semantic; they could also apply to syntactic formalisms or other layers of representation. The paper situates this work within the broader context of semantic parsing before introducing its chosen annotation scheme, UCCA. The authors then present three sets of parsing experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing using the Stanford parser after converting UCCA to a constituency format; (ii) dependency parsing after converting UCCA to a dependency format; and finally, (iii) the core contribution, experiments showing that their transition-based graph parser can directly parse UCCA graphs.

While I found this work interesting, I have several concerns regarding the methodology and empirical justification that should be addressed before the paper can be considered for publication.

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like UCCA. While this may be technically true for UCCA itself, the argument is weakened by the fact that the proposed parser does not appear to handle any of the unique *semantic* properties of the formalism. The UCCA scheme consists of graphs with some under-specified structural arguments and semantic labels (e.g., Process, State), yet nothing in the parser's transition set is designed to treat these specificities. From a technical standpoint, the parser is a general graph parser. Even the transitions for remote edges could arguably be handled by standard transitions, simply by enriching the label set (e.g., by adding an affix to denote remoteness).

From the perspective of general graph parsing, numerous works, particularly following the SemEval 2014-2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (e.g., Almeida and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015), have proposed extensions to transition-based graph parsers or adaptations of higher-order models. Nothing precludes the application of these existing parsers to the UCCA dataset. The main component anchoring this model to the UCCA scheme seems to be a specific feature template, although the paper suggests this is less influential than the count and unigram features. Given that these state-of-the-art graph parsers are publicly available [1,2], it is a significant omission not to use them as baselines or for comparison.

This lack of comparison to relevant state-of-the-art systems extends to the other experiments. The phrase-based experiments using Uparse would have been strengthened by comparison with the parser from Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015), which achieves comparable performance on LCFRS-style parsing. Furthermore, since UCCA provides a more abstract view of syntactic-semantic structure than most Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) treebanks, it would have been particularly valuable to use evaluation metrics from the related SemEval shared tasks to facilitate comparison. The field currently has many competing systems, models, and datasets. The lack of clear comparison points with other state-of-the-art parsers and established evaluation metrics is a major weakness of this paper.

Despite these criticisms, I find the work interesting. The field is at a crossroads regarding the future of semantic representation and parsing, and this paper raises important questions that would be valuable to discuss at a conference like CoNLL.

**Additional Points:**

*   The term "grounded semantics" is used before it is formally introduced. Please define it upon its first use.
*   A stronger baseline for the constituency-based approach could have been established by converting UCCA to constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, which could then be parsed with a well-established tool like the Berkeley parser.
*   The conversion to surface dependency trees seems overly simplistic and lossy. A richer conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014) for the SemEval SDP task, would have provided a more meaningful dependency-based baseline.
*   The paper states that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text." Please expand on this point with an example or a reference.
*   The comparison to representations like MRS and DRT highlights that UCCA does not appear to model quantifier scope. This arguably makes it more comparable to syntax-oriented graph schemes than to deeper semantic formalisms. The paper would benefit from a more precise positioning of UCCA relative to other frameworks (e.g., DM, the semantic layer of PCEDT). How much more abstract or under-specified is it? How informative is the scheme, and how "parsable" is it in practice? The reported scores suggest it is a challenging task, but a detailed error analysis is needed to shed light on this.
*   Finally, the three motivating principles for the semantic scheme feel somewhat ad-hoc and could apply to many formalisms. This connects to a larger clarity issue: the introduction is dense and tries to argue for multiple things at once. Is the main contribution a parser for UCCA, a general model for semantic analysis, or a demonstration that a semantically-oriented scheme like UCCA is parsable? Clarifying the central argument of the paper would significantly improve its focus and impact.
