This paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA), a specific formalism for graph-based semantic representation. The authors highlight key distinctions from other formalisms: unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA structures incorporate "nonterminal" nodes not tied to specific words, and in contrast to AMR, they are "grounded," meaning the text's tokens serve as nodes in the graph. The work presents and evaluates multiple parsing techniques, including a transition-based parser for the direct construction of UCCA graphs.

While the goal of creating a UCCA parser is certainly valid, particularly with the availability of UCCA-annotated corpora, the paper's introductory framing is concerning. The introduction and background seem to position UCCA as the only sensible graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism worth pursuing. This argument is not persuasive and feels superfluous, as a robust UCCA parser would constitute a valuable contribution in its own right.

I find myself in disagreement with the three criteria for SR formalisms presented in the introduction. For instance, it remains unclear whether "nonterminal nodes" provide any additional expressive capacity. While they may simplify the handling of structures like coordination, it is not demonstrated why this information could not be encoded via more informative edge labels instead. Moreover, the issue of discontinuity is largely irrelevant for SRs that are not "grounded." The paper also does not convincingly explain the advantages of its "grounded" representations over AMR-style alternatives. The term "grounded" itself is ambiguous, having been used for various concepts in semantics recently; alternatives like "anchored" or "lexicalized" might be clearer. As a result, the entire introductory argument needs to be rephrased with greater care.

While the parser itself seems technically reasonable (though not examined in detail), the evaluation results are unconvincing. On "primary" edges, the proposed BSP parser is surpassed by a standard MaltParser. The F-scores for "remote" edges, which are beyond the direct capability of a dependency-tree parser, are also not particularly strong. A more significant issue is that the comparison to tree-based parsers is incomplete. The field has extensively studied the conversion of dependency graphs to "tree approximations," especially within the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. This research (e.g., Agic et al., 2015, on reconstructing deleted edges) provides established methods for improving graph-based scores from tree-based parsers. By incorporating such techniques, the performance of the MaltParser baseline could likely be enhanced, making the relative contribution of the authors' BSP parser even less clear.
