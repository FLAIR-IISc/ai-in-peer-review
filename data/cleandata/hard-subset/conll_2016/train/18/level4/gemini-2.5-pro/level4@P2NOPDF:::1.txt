This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors frame their work by outlining three desirable properties for a semantic representation: (i) a graph-based structure allowing multiple parents, (ii) non-terminal nodes for complex phrases, and (iii) the ability to handle discontinuous constituents. Notably, these principles are general and could apply to syntactic representations as well. After positioning UCCA within the broader context of semantic parsing, the authors present three sets of experiments: (1) a phrase-based approach using a UCCA-to-constituency conversion parsed with the Stanford parser; (2) a dependency-based approach using a UCCA-to-dependency conversion; and (3) the core proposal, a direct transition-based parser for UCCA graphs.

While the paper addresses an interesting and relevant problem, I have several significant concerns regarding the methodology and empirical evaluation that should be addressed before publication. My primary criticisms relate to the lack of comparison with relevant existing work and the justification for some of the experimental choices.

**Novelty and Comparison to Existing Work**

A central claim of the paper is that it presents the first parser for a semantically-oriented graph-based scheme like UCCA. While this is true for UCCA specifically, the broader claim requires more careful justification. The proposed transition system does not appear to incorporate mechanisms specifically tailored to the *semantic* aspects of UCCA (e.g., labels like 'Process' or 'State'). The system's novelty seems to lie primarily in its feature templates rather than the transition algorithm itself. The transitions for remote edges, for instance, could arguably be handled by standard transitions with a modified label set (e.g., using an affix to denote remoteness).

This brings up a more critical issue: the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art graph parsers. Given that several transition-based and higher-order graph parsers have been developed following the SemEval 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (e.g., Ribeyre et al., 2014; Martins et al., 2015), and are publicly available, their absence as baselines is a significant omission. Nothing in the paper suggests these systems would be incompatible with the UCCA data, and comparing against them is crucial for contextualizing the proposed parser's performance. The same concern applies to the phrase-based experiments; the work of Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015) presents a parser capable of producing LCFRS-style analyses comparable to U-Parse and would have served as a valuable point of comparison.

**Experimental Design and Framing**

The paper's central thesis is somewhat ambiguous. It is unclear whether the main contribution is the specific parser for UCCA, a generalizable model for semantic parsing, or an argument for the parsability of the UCCA scheme itself. The introduction attempts to argue for all three, which makes the paper's focus diffuse. The three principles laid out for semantic schemes feel somewhat ad-hoc and would benefit from being situated within a broader, more clearly articulated theoretical context.

Furthermore, some of the experimental design choices weaken the paper's conclusions:

1.  **Dependency Conversion:** The conversion to surface dependency trees seems overly simplistic and results in significant information loss. A more sophisticated conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014) for the SemEval SDP task, would have provided a more meaningful and challenging baseline.

2.  **Evaluation Metrics:** The paper would be strengthened by adopting metrics used in related shared tasks (e.g., SemEval SDP). This would allow for a more direct comparison of the difficulty of parsing UCCA relative to other semantic graphbanks and help position the results within the wider field.

3.  **Error Analysis:** The results suggest that parsing UCCA is a difficult task. An error analysis is essential to understand the primary challenges. Are errors concentrated on particular phenomena, such as re-entrancy, discontinuity, or non-terminal structures? Such an analysis would provide valuable insights into both the parser's limitations and the scheme's complexities.

**Minor Points and Suggestions**

*   The term "grounded semantics" is used on page 1 before it is formally introduced on page 2. Please define it upon first use.
*   A strong constituency-based baseline could have been established by converting UCCA to constituent trees with rich node labels and traces for discontinuous elements, and then parsing them with a powerful tool like the Berkeley parser. Why was this approach not considered?
*   Please clarify the statement that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text." An example or a reference would be very helpful.
*   The comparison to other formalisms like MRS and DRT highlights that UCCA does not appear to model quantifier scope. This suggests it is more comparable to abstract syntactic schemes than to representations of logical form. It would be useful to more precisely characterize UCCA's expressive power relative to other graphbanks like DM or the semantic layer of the PCEDT.

In summary, the paper tackles an important problem and presents a functional parser for a novel representation. However, its contribution is undermined by a lack of comparison to highly relevant and available state-of-the-art systems and by a lack of clarity in its overall argumentation. While I find the work interesting and believe it addresses topics worthy of discussion at CoNLL, the aforementioned issues should be thoroughly addressed to substantiate the paper's claims and contribution to the field.
