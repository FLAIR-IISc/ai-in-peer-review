- Introduces the first broad-coverage UCCA semantic parsers and evaluates multiple parsing techniques, including a transition-based parser.  
- Emphasizes key distinctions from other formalisms: UCCA has nonterminal nodes (not tied to words) and is “grounded” with tokens as graph nodes.  
- The goal of building a UCCA parser is valid given available UCCA-annotated corpora.  
- Introductory framing is problematic: it positions UCCA as the only sensible graph-based SR formalism, which is unpersuasive and unnecessary.  
- Disagrees with the three proposed SR criteria; unclear that nonterminal nodes add expressive power versus richer edge labels.  
- Claims discontinuity is largely irrelevant for non-grounded SRs, so that criterion is questionable.  
- The term “grounded” is ambiguous and the benefits over AMR-style (non-grounded) representations are not convincingly argued; alternatives like “anchored” or “lexicalized” might be clearer.  
- The entire introduction/background should be rephrased more carefully and with stronger justification.  
- The parser appears technically reasonable but is not examined in depth.  
- Evaluation is unconvincing: the BSP parser is beaten by a standard MaltParser on primary edges.  
- F-scores for remote edges (beyond dependency-tree parsers) are weak.  
- Comparison to tree-based baselines is incomplete—prior work (CoNLL 2014/2015, Agic et al. 2015) on reconstructing deleted edges could improve MaltParser, potentially narrowing or removing BSP’s advantage.
