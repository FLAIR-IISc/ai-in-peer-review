### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a transition-based parser for UCCA, a broad-coverage semantic representation scheme. The authors motivate their work by identifying three key structural properties required for broad-coverage semantics: the ability to represent multi-parent structures (graphs), non-terminal nodes for complex units, and discontinuities. To tackle this parsing task, the paper explores two main strategies: first, converting UCCA structures to more standard formalisms (constituency and dependency trees) and applying existing state-of-the-art parsers; and second, developing a novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP) capable of handling UCCA's specific graph structures.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The first parser developed specifically for the UCCA semantic annotation scheme.
2.  A systematic experimental comparison of two approaches to parsing UCCA: a direct approach using a novel parser, and an indirect, conversion-based approach using existing parsers.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackling a New, Broad-Coverage Representation:** The paper addresses the important and timely problem of parsing broad-coverage semantic representations. As the community explores various semantic formalisms beyond traditional dependency structures (e.g., AMR, SDP), this work on UCCA is a valuable and relevant contribution to the conversation. It introduces a new formalism and dataset to the parsing community, which could spur further research.
2.  **Systematic Comparison of Methods:** The systematic comparison between direct parsing (BSP) and conversion-based approaches provides useful insights into the trade-offs involved and the current capabilities of existing tools when applied to this new task.
3.  **Relevance to the Field:** The challenges posed by UCCA (graphs, non-terminals, discontinuities) are central to current research in parsing. This paper provides a good test case and a solid baseline for a representation that combines these features, making it a paper worthy of discussion at a conference like CoNLL.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty and Missing Baselines:** The central claim of proposing the *first* parser for UCCA, while technically true, overlooks the fact that the proposed transition system is largely a synthesis of existing techniques for graph and discontinuous parsing. The transition set does not appear to contain operations that are uniquely motivated by the *semantic* properties of UCCA, but rather by its graph structure. Crucially, the paper does not compare its performance against several existing and publicly available transition-based graph parsers developed in the context of the SemEval SDP shared tasks (e.g., Almeida and Martins, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014), which would be the most natural and informative baselines. Nothing appears to preclude their application to the UCCA data.
2.  **Sub-optimal Conversion Baselines:** The choice of parsers and conversion methods for the baseline experiments could be stronger. The conversion to surface dependency trees is highly lossy, as acknowledged by the upper-bound scores, making it a relatively weak baseline. A more sophisticated conversion, such as those used in the SemEval SDP tasks (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014) to create richer dependency graphs, would have provided a much stronger and more informative comparison. Furthermore, for constituency-based parsing, other recent parsers that can handle similar structures, such as the one by Fernández-González and Martins (2015), could have been included.
3.  **Lack of Comparative Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation is performed in a vacuum, using a custom metric. While this is necessary for a new representation, the lack of comparison points with other models and schemes is detrimental to the work. By not reporting scores using established metrics from related tasks, such as the SemEval SDP shared task (on a converted version of the data), the authors miss a critical opportunity to situate their results within the broader field of broad-coverage semantic parsing. This makes it difficult to assess how "hard" UCCA parsing is relative to other formalisms.
4.  **Unclear Framing of the Core Argument:** The introduction and motivation feel somewhat ad-hoc. The three principles for broad-coverage semantic structures are presented as general requirements but could equally apply to various syntactic formalisms. This makes the paper's primary argument unclear: is it advocating for a new parser (BSP), a new semantic scheme (UCCA), or demonstrating that this scheme is parsable? The attempt to argue for all three simultaneously weakens the focus and makes the introduction dense and potentially confusing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide an example of an "implicit unit" in UCCA and elaborate on why parsing them is deferred to future work?
2.  The paper mentions other formalisms like MRS and DRT, which handle phenomena like quantifier scope. How does UCCA handle such phenomena, and where would you place it on a spectrum from syntax-oriented representations (like SDP) to more abstract logical forms (like AMR)? Your scores suggest the task is difficult, but an error analysis would be helpful to understand if this is due to structural complexity or semantic ambiguity.
3.  For your conversion-based experiments, did you consider a baseline that converts UCCA to a constituency tree with rich node labels and traces (to handle re-entrancies/discontinuities) and then uses a powerful constituency parser like the Berkeley parser?
4.  Given the significant information loss in your conversion to CoNLL-style dependency trees (as shown by the 88.4% upper bound), can you justify this choice over more structure-preserving conversion methods that have been developed for the SDP tasks?
