- Introduces the first parsers for UCCA, a grounded semantic formalism with non-terminal nodes and discontinuous constituents.  
- Uses two parsing strategies: conversion to/from related formalisms (discontinuous constituency/dependency trees) and a novel transition-based parser (BSP).  
- Evaluates these approaches on English UCCA-annotated corpora.  
- Contribution: provides the first UCCA parsers, enabling downstream use of UCCA resources.  
- Contribution: gives an empirical comparison between direct parsing and conversion-based baselines as a foundation for future work.  
- Strength: addresses a novel and necessary task, opening UCCA to the research community.  
- Strength: systematically explores two distinct parsing strategies, offering comprehensive analysis and baselines.  
- Strength: authors will release parsers and converters publicly, aiding reproducibility.  
- Weakness: motivation/framing overstates UCCA’s superiority and is unnecessary for the parser contribution.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification for the necessity of non-terminal nodes versus richer edge labels.  
- Weakness: benefits of grounding/anchoring vs ungrounded formalisms (e.g., AMR) are not clearly articulated; “grounded” terminology is ambiguous.  
- Weakness: BSP’s empirical performance is underwhelming compared to conversion-based baselines (primary-edge F-score: 59.0 vs. 69.2 for LSTM dependency baseline).  
- Weakness: BSP performs poorly on remote edges (13.3 F-score), indicating difficulty with a core graph challenge.  
- Weakness: comparison to conversion baselines may be unfair—converters naively drop remote edges and omit established graph-reconstruction techniques that could strengthen baselines (e.g., approaches from SemEval/Semantic Dependency Parsing).
