- Paper introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA and outlines three design principles for a semantic scheme (graphs, non-terminals, discontinuities).  
- Authors run three experiment series: constituency conversion + Stanford parser, dependency conversion, and their direct transition-based graph parser.  
- Major methodological concern: claim of novelty is misleading because the transition set does not clearly address UCCA-specific properties (under-specified arguments, semantically-rich labels).  
- Handling of remote edges seems ad-hoc and could be achieved by alternative label strategies rather than novel transitions.  
- The paper overlooks a substantial body of post-2014 transition-based graph parsers (e.g., Almeda & Martins; Ribeyre et al.) that could be adapted and used as baselines.  
- The parser’s UCCA-specificity appears to come mainly from feature templates, not from a fundamentally new parsing mechanism.  
- Omission of available advanced graph parsers (public implementations) as baselines is a major weakness.  
- Phrase-based experiments lack comparison to other LCFRS-style parsers (e.g., Fernandez-Gonzales & Martins 2015) and Uparse is not properly referenced.  
- Evaluation is not comparable to the broader field: standard SemEval SDP metrics and benchmarks were not used, reducing impact.  
- Converting UCCA to surface dependencies likely discards important structure; richer conversions (e.g., Schluter et al. 2014) should be considered.  
- Several presentation and clarity issues: "grounded semantics" is introduced before definition; the claim about implicit units needs example or citation.  
- Need for deeper analysis: compare UCCA’s informativeness and parsability to frameworks like DM, PCEDT, MRS, DRT, and provide a detailed error analysis to support claims that UCCA is harder.  
- Guiding principles feel ad-hoc and the paper lacks a clear central argument—unclear whether the main contribution is the parser, a general model for semantics, or a demonstration of UCCA’s parsability.  
- Despite criticisms, the reviewer finds the problem interesting and believes the work merits discussion at CoNLL if methodological concerns are addressed.
