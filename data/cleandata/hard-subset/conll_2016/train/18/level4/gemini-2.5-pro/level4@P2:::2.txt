### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents the first parsing methods for Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA), a broad-coverage semantic representation scheme. UCCA structures are directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) characterized by three key properties that distinguish them from other common formalisms: they are "grounded" (i.e., tokens are leaves in the graph), they permit non-terminal nodes that do not correspond to single words, and they can represent discontinuous constituents.

The authors explore two distinct approaches to parsing UCCA:
1.  A **conversion-based approach**, which maps UCCA graphs to more restrictive formalisms (dependency trees or discontinuous constituency trees). This allows the use of existing, high-performance parsers. The resulting parses are then converted back to the UCCA format for evaluation.
2.  A **direct approach**, for which the authors develop a novel transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), specifically designed to directly generate UCCA graphs with all their structural properties.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **The first parser and empirical benchmarks for the UCCA formalism.** By developing and evaluating methods for this task, the paper provides a crucial tool and a set of baselines that can enable the broader use of UCCA-annotated data and facilitate future research into this semantic representation.
2.  **A systematic comparison of direct vs. conversion-based parsing strategies for UCCA.** The paper's exploration of both a bespoke parser and the adaptation of existing tools provides valuable insights into the challenges of the task and the trade-offs between different modeling approaches.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Task Formulation.** This work addresses a clear gap in the literature by tackling the previously unaddressed task of parsing UCCA structures. The creation of the first parser for an existing, annotated semantic representation is a significant and valuable contribution. This research paves the way for UCCA to be used in downstream NLP applications.
2.  **Comprehensive Methodological Exploration.** The authors do not limit themselves to a single solution. Instead, they present a thorough investigation by comparing a novel, direct parsing algorithm (BSP) against several strong baselines derived from leveraging state-of-the-art parsers for related formalisms (MaltParser, UPARSE, and an LSTM-based dependency parser). This dual approach strengthens the paper's conclusions and provides a more complete picture of the problem space.
3.  **Publicly Available Resources.** The authors' commitment to releasing their converters and parsers is a commendable contribution to the community. This will ensure the reproducibility of their results and lower the barrier to entry for other researchers wanting to work with UCCA.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Underwhelming Empirical Performance of the Novel Parser.** The primary motivation for developing the novel BSP parser is to handle the full complexity of UCCA graphs, including remote edges. However, the experimental results raise questions about its practical advantages. On primary edges, the BSP is significantly outperformed by the LSTM-based dependency parser (69.2% vs. 59.0% F-score), even though the latter operates on a lossy conversion of the original data. Furthermore, BSP's performance on the remote edges it is specifically designed to capture is very low (13.3% F-score), which limits the claims about its effectiveness.
2.  **Insufficient Engagement with Related Work on Graph-to-Tree Conversion.** The conversion-based approach is presented as a straightforward, lossy process. However, the problem of parsing semantic graphs via tree approximations, including the reconstruction of non-tree edges, has been explored extensively in the context of the SemEval shared tasks on Semantic Dependency Parsing (e.g., Oepen et al., 2015). For instance, Agić et al. (2015, "Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations") discuss methods for recovering graph edges after tree parsing. By not attempting to reconstruct remote edges for the baseline parsers, the paper presents a potentially weakened version of the conversion-based approach. A stronger baseline that includes an edge-reconstruction step would provide a more meaningful comparison and might further diminish the relative performance of the BSP parser.
3.  **Weak Motivation for the UCCA Formalism's Design.** The introduction argues for the necessity of UCCA's specific structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) for any broad-coverage semantic representation. However, the justification feels overstated and could be more nuanced. For example, it is not made clear what expressive capacity non-terminal nodes provide that could not be captured in a bi-lexical dependency graph through other mechanisms, such as more informative edge labels or re-entrant structures. The paper would be more convincing if it focused on the merits of providing a parser for UCCA as an existing formalism, rather than positioning UCCA as the uniquely correct way to represent semantics.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the conversion-based baselines: Could you discuss how your method relates to the "tree approximation" techniques used in the SemEval SDP shared tasks? Did you consider implementing a post-processing step to reconstruct remote edges after dependency parsing? Such a step would create a much stronger baseline and allow for a more direct comparison of performance on remote edges.
2.  Given that the LSTM-based parser substantially outperforms BSP on primary edges, what are the key advantages of the direct parsing approach? Are there specific types of structures that BSP is better equipped to handle, which may not be fully captured by the current evaluation metrics?
3.  Could you provide a more concrete example of a semantic distinction that can only be captured by a non-terminal node in UCCA, and which cannot be represented in a bi-lexical formalism like SDP (e.g., through the use of special labels or additional relations)? This would help clarify the motivation for this structural feature.
