This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser designed for the complex representations of the UCCA semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors begin by outlining their three core principles for a semantic annotation scheme: (1) support for graph structures, allowing nodes to have multiple parents; (2) inclusion of non-terminal nodes to represent complex syntactic or lexical units (e.g., coordinate phrases, multiword expressions); and (3) accommodation of discontinuous constituents (e.g., verb-particle constructions). Notably, these principles are not exclusive to semantics and could apply equally to syntactic or other representational layers. The work is situated within the broader context of wide-coverage semantic parsing, with a specific focus on the UCCA framework. They present three series of parsing experiments: (i) a phrase-based approach using the Stanford parser after converting UCCA to a constituency format; (ii) a dependency-based approach using a similar conversion to dependencies; and (iii) their primary contribution, a set of experiments demonstrating the suitability of their proposed transition-based graph parser for parsing UCCA graphs directly.

While the work is interesting, I have several significant concerns regarding the methodology and empirical validation that should be addressed before publication.

The claim to be the first to parse such a semantically-oriented scheme is, while technically correct, somewhat misleading. The proposed parser's transition set does not seem to address the unique properties of the UCCA scheme, such as its under-specified structural arguments or its semantically-rich labels (e.g., Process, State). The specific transitions for remote edges, for instance, could arguably be handled by more general transitions if the edge labels were augmented (e.g., with a special affix). More importantly, the paper frames the problem as graph parsing, yet it overlooks a significant body of work on transition-based graph parsing that emerged following the 2014-2015 SemEval shared tasks (e.g., Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015). These existing parsers could have been adapted for this task. The model's specificity to UCCA seems to derive primarily from its feature templates rather than its core parsing mechanism. Given that several of these advanced graph parsers are publicly available [1,2], their omission as baselines or points of comparison is a major weakness. Similarly, the phrase-based experiments with Uparse could have been strengthened by comparison with the parser by Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015), which also produces LCFRS-style parses. A reference for Uparse is also missing upon its first mention.

Given that UCCA offers a more abstract representation than many semantic dependency parsing (SDP) treebanks, it is crucial to evaluate using the standard metrics from related shared tasks (e.g., SemEval SDP) to ensure comparability with the broader field. The absence of such comparative evaluation points against other models and parsers significantly diminishes the paper's impact. Despite these criticisms, the paper addresses an interesting problem at a pivotal moment for the field, and I believe it presents a valuable contribution that merits discussion at a venue like CoNLL.

Additional Points:
*   The term "grounded semantics" is used on page 2 before it is formally introduced. Please define it earlier.
*   A valuable baseline would be to convert UCCA to constituent trees with rich node labels and traces, and then apply a state-of-the-art constituency parser like the Berkeley parser. Why was this approach not considered?
*   The conversion to surface dependencies appears to discard a significant amount of structural information. A richer conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014) for the SemEval SDP task, would have been more appropriate.
*   Please clarify the statement that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text" by providing an example or a reference.
*   The mention of MRS and DRT highlights that UCCA does not appear to model quantifier scope, which makes it more directly comparable to syntax-oriented graph schemes. How does UCCA's informativeness and "parsability" truly compare to frameworks like DM or the semantic layer of the PCEDT? Your results suggest it is a "harder" task, but a detailed error analysis is needed to substantiate this.
*   As noted earlier, the three guiding principles feel somewhat ad-hoc. The introduction should more clearly articulate the paper's central argument. Is the primary contribution the parser itself, the model's suitability for semantic analysis in general, or a demonstration that this particular semantic scheme is parsable? The current version attempts to argue for all three, which results in a lack of focus.
