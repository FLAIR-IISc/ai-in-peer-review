### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents the first parsing systems for the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) framework, a graph-based semantic representation that is "grounded" (i.e., its nodes correspond to text tokens or spans) and allows for non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units, and multiple parents. The authors propose and evaluate two distinct approaches: 1) converting UCCA graphs to more standard formalisms (constituency and dependency trees) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers, and 2) a novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP) specifically designed to handle the structural properties of UCCA.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Transition-Based Parser (BSP) for Complex Semantic Graphs:** The primary contribution is the BSP parser, which integrates mechanisms from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing to directly generate UCCA structures. It is, to my knowledge, the first parser designed to handle the combination of non-terminal nodes, discontinuity, and multiple parents in a single, grounded framework.
2.  **The First Empirical Benchmark for UCCA Parsing:** The paper establishes the first set of parsing results on UCCA-annotated data. By evaluating both direct and conversion-based methods, it provides an initial benchmark and analysis that will be valuable for any future work on parsing this semantic representation.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of the Task and Approach:** This is the first paper to tackle the problem of parsing UCCA structures. The problem is non-trivial due to the combination of structural properties that are not jointly handled by standard parsers. The development of a specialized transition-based system (BSP) is a solid and well-motivated approach to addressing this new task.

2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Strategy:** The authors do not merely propose their own parser; they also wisely investigate an alternative strategy based on converting the UCCA formalism into formats for which high-quality parsers already exist. This dual approach provides a much richer analysis, allowing for a comparison between leveraging powerful existing tools and building a bespoke system. This is a strong methodological choice.

3.  **Potential for Community Resource:** The authors state their intention to publicly release the code for their parser and the conversion scripts. Given that this is a new research area, providing these resources would be a significant contribution to the community, enabling reproducibility and fostering future research on UCCA parsing.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overstated Motivation and Framing:** The introduction and background sections argue for the advantages of UCCA by critiquing other formalisms (e.g., AMR, SDP) in a way that feels one-sided and, at times, unconvincing. For instance:
    *   The necessity of "non-terminal nodes" is not clearly justified in terms of expressive power. While handling coordination without choosing a lexical head is convenient, it is not clear what semantic information can be expressed that is impossible to capture with, for example, more informative edge labels in a dependency graph.
    *   The discussion of "grounded" vs. un-grounded representations does not fully explore the trade-offs. The advantages of grounding are mentioned, but the corresponding advantages of un-grounded representations (e.g., abstraction from syntactic detail, canonicalization) are not acknowledged. Furthermore, the term "grounded" has been used for many different concepts in semantics; a more precise term like "anchored" or "span-based" might reduce confusion.
    *   A more balanced positioning of UCCA within the landscape of semantic formalisms would strengthen the paper's motivation without diminishing the value of developing a parser for it.

2.  **Underwhelming and Potentially Misleading Experimental Results:** The central claim of the paper is the effectiveness of the proposed BSP parser, but the results do not strongly support this.
    *   The proposed BSP parser is outperformed on its primary evaluation metric (primary edge F-score) by a conversion-based approach using a standard LSTM dependency parser (59.0% vs. 69.2%). This raises questions about the practical utility of the more complex, specialized BSP parser when a simpler, indirect method works significantly better.
    *   The performance of BSP on "remote edges"—the very structures that dependency parsers cannot directly produce—is very low (13.3% F-score). While this is a difficult sub-task, such a low score suggests the model is struggling to learn these phenomena effectively.

3.  **Weakness of the Conversion-Based Baselines:** The comparison to the conversion-based methods is undermined by the simplicity of the conversion process. The paper treats the conversion from a graph to a tree as a solved problem where edges are simply dropped and not recovered. However, the problem of "tree approximation" and subsequent reconstruction of graph edges has been a central topic in the SemEval shared tasks on Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP). Methodologies exist for intelligently predicting which edges to retain during tree conversion and, more importantly, for reconstructing the deleted edges from the parsed tree output (e.g., Agić et al., 2015, "Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations"). By not incorporating any such reconstruction techniques, the reported scores for the MaltParser and LSTM baselines are likely lower bounds. A stronger baseline might close the gap on remote edges and further challenge the performance of the BSP parser.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The comparison to the dependency parsers relies on a lossy conversion that discards all remote edges and does not attempt to reconstruct them. Could you comment on why more sophisticated graph-to-tree approximation and reconstruction methods, such as those explored in the SemEval 2014/2015 SDP shared tasks, were not considered for the dependency-based baseline? How might incorporating these techniques affect the comparison with your proposed BSP parser?

2.  The F-score for remote edges, a key phenomenon that distinguishes UCCA from trees and motivates your direct parsing approach, is quite low (around 13%). Do you have an error analysis that sheds light on why performance is so low? Is this primarily a data sparsity issue, a limitation of the transition system, or a feature engineering problem?

3.  Could you elaborate on the claim that non-terminal nodes are a necessary component for a broad-coverage semantic representation? Specifically, what semantic distinctions can be made with a non-terminal node that could not be made in a purely bi-lexical dependency graph, perhaps by using more complex edge labeling schemes or re-entrant structures to model phenomena like coordination?
