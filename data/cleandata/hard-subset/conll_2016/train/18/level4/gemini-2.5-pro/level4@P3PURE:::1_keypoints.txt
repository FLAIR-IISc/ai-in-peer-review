Paper introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA and argues three semantic annotation properties (graph structure, non-terminals, discontinuity).  
Experiments include constituency conversion (Stanford parser), dependency conversion, and direct UCCA parsing with the proposed parser.  
Major concern: insufficient comparison to existing graph-parsing work, making the novelty claim tenuous.  
Transitions do not appear specifically tailored to UCCA’s semantic properties; remote edges could be encoded in edge labels.  
Omission of important baselines (e.g., Almeida & Martins 2015; Ribeyre et al. 2014) and SemEval SDP systems is a major weakness.  
Parser appears tied to UCCA via feature templates rather than presenting a fundamentally new algorithm.  
Phrase-based experiments are incomplete—should include Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015); UPARSE was not properly referenced.  
Evaluation lacks standardized SemEval SDP metrics, preventing meaningful comparison to broader SOTA.  
Conversion to surface dependency trees is overly simplistic and lossy; should use more sophisticated conversions (e.g., Schluter et al. 2014).  
Claim that UCCA contains implicit units without text correspondents is unsubstantiated and needs example or reference.  
Comparison to MRS/DRT is superficial; UCCA omits quantifier scope and seems closer to syntax-oriented schemes—needs detailed positioning versus DM/PCEDT.  
Paper lacks a thorough error analysis to explain parsing difficulty and typical error patterns.  
The three guiding principles for semantic annotation feel ad-hoc and need stronger theoretical motivation.  
Central contribution is unclear—uncertain whether the paper’s main claim is the parser, model generality, or demonstrating UCCA’s parsability.  
Minor editorial issue: “grounded semantics” is used before it is defined and should be introduced earlier.  
Suggested stronger baseline: convert UCCA to constituency trees with rich node labels/traces and use a powerful parser like Berkeley as a comparison.
