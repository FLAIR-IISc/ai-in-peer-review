- Paper presents first parsing methods for UCCA, a DAG semantic formalism with grounded tokens, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents.  
- Two parsing approaches: conversion-based (map to dependency/constituency trees, parse, convert back) and a direct transition-based parser (BSP).  
- Main contributions: (1) first UCCA parser and empirical benchmarks, (2) systematic comparison of direct vs. conversion-based strategies.  
- Strength: novel task formulation—fills a gap by providing the first parser and baselines for UCCA, enabling downstream use.  
- Strength: comprehensive methodological exploration—compares BSP to strong baselines (MaltParser, UPARSE, LSTM dep parser).  
- Strength: authors release converters and parsers publicly, supporting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: BSP’s empirical performance is underwhelming—LSTM dependency parser outperforms BSP on primary edges and BSP has very low remote-edge F1 (~13.3%).  
- Weakness: conversion-based baselines do not attempt reconstruction of non-tree/remote edges, despite prior SemEval SDP work on tree-approximation and edge recovery.  
- Weakness: motivation for UCCA’s design (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) is weakly justified; unclear what non-terminals add beyond bi-lexical graphs.  
- Question: How does the conversion method relate to SemEval SDP tree-approximation techniques, and did authors try post-processing to reconstruct remote edges?  
- Question: Given the LSTM parser’s superior primary-edge performance, what concrete advantages does direct parsing (BSP) provide and which structures does it handle better?  
- Question: Can authors provide a concrete example where a UCCA non-terminal captures a semantic distinction that cannot be represented in a bi-lexical formalism like SDP?
