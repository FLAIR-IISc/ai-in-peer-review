This paper introduces a transition-based graph parser for UCCA, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme featuring graph-based structures, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous elements. The authors position their work within the context of broad-coverage semantic parsing and present three sets of experiments: (i) phrase-based parsing via a conversion to constituency trees, (ii) dependency parsing via a conversion to dependency trees, and (iii) their primary contribution, a direct transition-based parser for UCCA graphs.

While the paper addresses an interesting and important problem, I have several significant concerns regarding the methodology, empirical evaluation, and framing of the contribution that preclude a recommendation for acceptance in its current form.

**1. Claims of Novelty and Lack of Competitive Baselines**

My primary concern is the lack of comparison to existing, relevant work, which makes it difficult to assess the paper's contribution.

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for a semantically-oriented scheme like UCCA. While this may be true for this specific scheme, the core parsing problem is that of parsing directed acyclic graphs. The proposed transition set does not appear to handle any uniquely *semantic* phenomena of UCCA; rather, it handles its *structural* properties (e.g., re-entrancies, non-terminals, remote edges). These structural challenges are not unique to UCCA.

Several powerful, publicly available graph parsers have been developed, particularly following the SemEval 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on Semantic Dependency Parsing (e.g., Almeida and Martins, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014). Nothing in principle prevents these systems from being applied to the UCCA dataset, likely with adaptation of the feature templates. The authors do not provide a compelling reason for omitting these systems as crucial baselines. Without such a comparison, the claim that a new parser is necessary or superior remains unsubstantiated.

Similarly, for the phrase-based parsing experiments, a comparison with other strong parsers that can produce LCFRS-style analyses, such as the one by Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015), would have provided a more rigorous validation of that experimental branch.

**2. Experimental Design and Evaluation**

The paper's evaluation methodology could be significantly strengthened.

*   **Lossy Conversions:** The conversion to surface dependency trees seems particularly problematic, as it discards much of the rich structural information (e.g., non-terminal nodes, shared arguments) that motivates the UCCA scheme in the first place. This makes the dependency parsing baseline a weak point of comparison. A more meaning-preserving conversion, such as those developed for the SemEval SDP shared task (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014), would have provided a more informative result.
*   **Missed Baselines:** A valuable constituency-based baseline would be to convert UCCA to constituent trees with enriched node labels and co-indexed traces for remote dependencies, and then apply a state-of-the-art parser like the Berkeley parser. This would provide a strong point of comparison for how well existing technology can approximate UCCA structures.
*   **Standardized Metrics:** To situate this work within the broader field, it is crucial to use evaluation metrics that allow for comparison with other systems and formalisms. Adopting metrics from related shared tasks, such as SemEval SDP, would help the community gauge the relative difficulty of parsing UCCA compared to other semantic graphbanks (e.g., DM, PAS, PCEDT).

**3. Clarity, Framing, and Justification**

The paper's framing and several key concepts would benefit from greater clarity.

*   **Guiding Principles:** The three principles outlined for a semantic scheme (graph-based, non-terminals, discontinuities) are general enough to apply to many syntactic formalisms as well. They feel somewhat ad-hoc and should be better motivated within a broader discussion of the requirements for semantic representation.
*   **Central Argument:** The paper's central thesis is unclear. Is the primary contribution the parser itself, a general model for semantic analysis, or a proof-of-concept for the parsability of the UCCA scheme? The paper seems to argue for all three simultaneously, which makes the introduction dense and potentially confusing.
*   **Error Analysis:** The results indicate that parsing UCCA is a challenging task. A detailed error analysis is essential. What are the most common error types? Are they related to specific UCCA phenomena like remote edges, implicit units, or particular semantic categories? This analysis would provide crucial insights for the community and for future work.
*   **Undefined Concepts:** Several terms and claims need clarification. For instance, the phrase "grounded semantics" is used on page 1 before being defined later. The assertion that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text" is intriguing but requires an example or a reference to be understood.
*   **Semantic Depth:** The mention of MRS and DRT raises the question of UCCA's expressive power. UCCA does not appear to model phenomena like quantifier scope, which makes it more comparable to syntactically-oriented graph representations than to deep logical-form semantics. A clearer positioning of UCCA on the spectrum from syntax to semantics would be beneficial.

**Conclusion**

The development of parsers for richer, graph-based semantic representations is an important direction for our field. This paper tackles this challenge head-on and presents an interesting first step. However, in its current form, the work is undermined by a lack of comparison to relevant state-of-the-art systems, methodological weaknesses in the evaluation, and a lack of clarity in its overall framing.

While I believe the topic is highly relevant to the CONLL community, a substantial revision is required to address these major points before the paper can be considered for publication. A revised version with appropriate baselines, a more robust evaluation, and a clearer articulation of its contribution could represent a valuable addition to the literature.
