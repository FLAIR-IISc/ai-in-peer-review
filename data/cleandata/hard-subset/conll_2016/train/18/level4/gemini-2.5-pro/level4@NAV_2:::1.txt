### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the first parsing approach for the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme, a representation that supports complex graph structures including multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. The authors propose and evaluate two transition-based strategies: first, by converting UCCA graphs into more restricted formalisms (constituency and dependency trees) and applying existing state-of-the-art parsers; and second, by developing a novel direct parser, BSP, that extends existing transition-based frameworks to handle the full structural properties of UCCA. Experiments are conducted on the English UCCA-annotated corpora.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  The development of BSP, a novel transition-based parser designed to directly parse Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) like UCCA, which can model multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units within a single framework.
2.  The first empirical investigation into the automatic parsing of the UCCA formalism. This includes establishing performance benchmarks using both the proposed direct parser and indirect, conversion-based methods leveraging existing high-performance parsers.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles a Novel and Important Problem:** This is the first work to address the parsing of UCCA, a broad-coverage semantic representation scheme. By tackling the challenges posed by UCCA's graph-structured, multi-layered representations, the paper opens up a new and valuable direction for research in semantic parsing.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Approach:** The paper presents a thorough empirical study. Rather than solely focusing on their proposed model, the authors provide valuable context by exploring conversion-based approaches that leverage well-established parsers (UPARSE, MaltParser, a stack-LSTM parser). This provides a clear picture of how existing technology fares on this new task and establishes strong baselines for future work.
3.  **Well-Grounded Model Design:** The proposed direct parser, BSP, is a methodologically sound extension of well-understood transition-based parsing techniques. It successfully integrates ideas from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing to create a system capable of handling the specific structural properties of UCCA.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Relevant Graph Parsers:** The central claim is that this is the first parser for such a scheme. However, several transition-based and higher-order graph parsers have been developed, particularly following the SemEval 2014/2015 Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) shared tasks (e.g., Ribeyre et al., 2014; Almeida and Martins, 2015). Many of these are publicly available and could, in principle, be applied to the UCCA data. The lack of comparison or even discussion of these highly relevant systems as potential baselines weakens the claims of novelty and makes it difficult to situate the performance of the proposed BSP parser within the broader context of semantic graph parsing.
2.  **Limited Novelty in the Transition System:** The proposed transition set appears to be a standard combination of actions for parsing discontinuous graphs, rather than being specifically tailored to the *semantics* of UCCA. For instance, the distinction between primary and remote edges is handled by separate `LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE` and `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE` transitions, but this could arguably be handled by a single set of edge-creation transitions with a richer label set (e.g., using a `*-remote` affix). The parser's adaptation to UCCA seems to rely more on the feature templates than on a fundamentally new parsing mechanism designed for this type of semantic representation.
3.  **Questionable Choices in Baselines and Evaluation:** The conversion to dependency trees is extremely lossy, discarding all non-terminal nodes, which are a key feature of the representation. The reported upper bound of 88.4 F-score confirms the information loss. Using more sophisticated conversion techniques (e.g., those from the SDP shared tasks) might have produced a much stronger and more informative baseline. Furthermore, the use of a custom evaluation metric makes it difficult to compare these results to performance on other broad-coverage semantic parsing tasks. Adopting or comparing against the official SemEval SDP metrics would have been beneficial for the field.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper frames the task as distinct from prior work in semantic graph parsing. Could you clarify why existing publicly available dependency graph parsers (e.g., TurboParser, Ribeyre's DAGParser) were not considered as baselines for the direct parsing approach?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the implicit units that are mentioned to exist in UCCA graphs but were excluded from this work? An example would be helpful to understand the full scope of the parsing task.
3.  The conversion to constituency trees involved simply dropping remote edges. An alternative approach could be to represent the graph structure using rich node labels and traces, which could then be handled by a powerful constituency parser like the Berkeley parser. Was this or any other richer conversion strategy considered as a baseline?
4.  The paper positions UCCA as a Broad-coverage Semantic Structure, but notes that phenomena like quantifier scope (present in MRS or DRT) are not handled. How do you characterize the expressive power and parsing difficulty of UCCA compared to the formalisms used in the SemEval SDP tasks? An error analysis comparing the types of structures that BSP struggles with versus those that are challenging for SDP parsers would be very insightful.
