This paper introduces the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) framework, a graph-based semantic representation. The authors position UCCA by contrasting it with other formalisms: unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs include "nonterminal" nodes that do not correspond to words in the input string, and unlike Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR), UCCA graphs are "grounded" in the text, meaning the sentence tokens serve as nodes in the graph. The paper presents and evaluates several parsing methods, including a novel transition-based parser designed to directly construct UCCA graphs.

While the development of a semantic parser for an existing, annotated formalism like UCCA is a well-motivated and reasonable goal, the framing in the introduction and background sections could be improved. The paper seems to argue, at times implicitly, that UCCA is the most, or perhaps only, sensible graph-based semantic representation to pursue. This strong positioning seems both unnecessary and difficult to substantiate. The contribution of a high-performing UCCA parser would be significant on its own merits without needing to diminish other established formalisms.

My main concerns relate to the justification for UCCA's design, as presented in the paper. The authors lay out three criteria for semantic representation formalisms, but the arguments are not entirely convincing.
First, the paper does not clearly articulate the added expressive power of "nonterminal nodes." While they may offer a convenient way to handle phenomena like coordination without committing to a specific lexical head, it is not obvious that this information could not be captured by other means, for instance, through more expressive edge labels on a purely lexicalized graph.
Second, the advantage of handling discontinuity is presented as a key feature, yet this is primarily a challenge for "grounded" (or lexicalized) representations. The issue is less relevant for formalisms like AMR that are not directly anchored to the surface string in the same way.
Third, the benefits of "grounded" representations over AMR-style approaches are not made clear. Furthermore, the term "grounded" has been used to describe many different concepts in semantics over the last decade. To improve clarity and avoid ambiguity, the authors might consider adopting a more precise term, such as "anchored" or "lexicalized."
Overall, the introductory section would be much stronger if its arguments were presented more carefully and with greater nuance.

Regarding the proposed parser and the evaluation, while the technical approach seems sound, the empirical results are not compelling. On the "primary" edge prediction task, the proposed BSP parser is outperformed by a standard MaltParser baseline. For "remote" edges—the very structures that motivate moving beyond a tree-based parser—the reported F-scores are not particularly high.

More importantly, the comparison against dependency-based baselines appears incomplete. The paper overlooks a significant body of work on "tree approximations" for semantic dependency graphs, explored extensively in the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. This line of research focuses on converting semantic graphs to trees for parsing and then reconstructing the non-tree edges that were removed. For instance, Agic et al. (2015, "Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations") detail methods for this reconstruction process. By incorporating such established post-processing techniques, the performance of the MaltParser and LSTM-based parser baselines could likely be improved significantly. If the baselines were made more competitive in this way, the practical advantages of the proposed BSP parser would become even less clear.
