- Presents a transition-based graph parser for UCCA with three experiments: constituency via conversion, dependency via conversion, and direct UCCA graph parsing (core claim).
- Major methodological concern: parser is a general graph parser and does not handle UCCA’s unique semantic properties (e.g., semantic role labels, under-specified arguments).
- Remote-edge handling appears trivializable (could be encoded in labels), undermining claim of UCCA-specific transitions.
- Fails to compare against existing state-of-the-art graph parsers from SemEval 2014–2015 (e.g., Almeida & Martins; Ribeyre et al.) despite public implementations.
- The proposed feature template may be the main UCCA-specific component, but counts/unigram features seem more influential.
- Phrase-based experiments lack comparisons to strong baselines like Fernandez-Gonzalez & Martins (2015) and to established LCFRS-style parsers.
- Evaluation misses the opportunity to use SemEval SDP metrics for broader comparability with related semantic parsing work.
- The dependency conversion is overly simplistic and lossy; recommend richer conversions like Schluter et al. (2014) for a meaningful baseline.
- Terminology issue: “grounded semantics” is used before being defined—define at first mention.
- A stronger constituency baseline could be created by converting UCCA to richly-labeled constituency trees and using a robust parser (e.g., Berkeley parser).
- Paper claims UCCA may contain implicit units without textual correspondents but provides no example or reference—expand and exemplify.
- UCCA appears not to model quantifier scope, making it closer to syntax-oriented schemes than deeper semantic formalisms; the paper needs clearer positioning against MRS, DRT, DM, PCEDT.
- Reported scores suggest UCCA parsing is challenging; a detailed error analysis is needed to explain what makes it hard and how informative the scheme is.
- The three motivating principles for the scheme feel ad-hoc and broadly applicable; the introduction is dense and unclear about the paper’s central contribution.
- Overall verdict: work is interesting and timely, but methodological omissions, lack of baselines/comparisons, and clarity issues must be addressed before acceptance.
