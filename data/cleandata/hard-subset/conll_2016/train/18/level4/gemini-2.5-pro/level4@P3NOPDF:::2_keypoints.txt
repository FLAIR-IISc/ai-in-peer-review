- Presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based representation, and introduces several parsing methods including a transition-based parser.
- Highlights UCCA features distinguishing it from other formalisms: nonterminal (non-word) nodes and grounded (tokens as nodes) graphs.
- Building a parser for UCCA is a valuable contribution given available annotated data.
- Introduction overstates UCCA’s uniqueness and should focus more on motivating the parser rather than claiming formalism superiority.
- The three design-criterion claims need more careful argumentation rather than broad assertions.
- Nonterminal nodes: benefit claimed (e.g., coordination) but trade-offs and alternative approaches (e.g., richer edge labels) are not fully discussed.
- Groundedness: concrete benefits over AMR-style ungrounded representations are not clearly articulated; the term “grounded” is ambiguous and could be replaced with “anchored” or “lexicalized.”
- Revise framing to cautiously present UCCA design principles and emphasize the parser contribution.
- Experimental evaluation is weak due to unconvincing baseline comparisons.
- MaltParser outperforms the proposed BSP on primary edges; BSP only modestly handles remote edges.
- Paper fails to compare against tree-approximation and graph-reconstruction methods from recent semantic dependency parsing literature (e.g., Agic et al. 2015).
- Recommend adding stronger, state-of-the-art baselines (including reconstruction methods) to make empirical advantages of BSP clear.
