This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed for the rich representations of UCCA, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors begin by outlining their desiderata for a semantic annotation scheme, proposing that it should be: (1) graph-based, allowing nodes to have multiple governors; (2) inclusive of non-terminal nodes to represent complex structures (e.g., coordinate phrases, multiword expressions); and (3) supportive of discontinuous elements (e.g., verb-particle constructions). Notably, these principles are not specific to a semantic framework and are equally applicable to syntax or other levels of representation. The paper situates its contribution within the broader context of semantic parsing before focusing on UCCA. The authors then present three sets of experiments: (1) phrase-based parsing via a conversion of UCCA to constituency structures, parsed with the Stanford parser; (2) dependency parsing via a conversion of UCCA to dependency trees; and (3) the core contribution, a direct parsing of UCCA graphs using their proposed transition-based graph parser.

While the work is interesting, I have several significant concerns regarding the methodology and empirical justification that should be addressed before publication.

The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser for this specific semantically-oriented scheme. While this is technically true, the claim is somewhat misleading. The UCCA scheme consists of graphs with various levels of under-specified structural arguments and semantic labels (e.g., Process, State), yet nothing in the proposed transition system is tailored to these specific semantic properties. For instance, the transitions for remote edges could be handled by a standard transition set if remote dependencies were simply encoded differently in the label set (e.g., using an affix).

When viewed purely as a graph parsing problem, numerous works following the SemEval 2014-2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing (e.g., Almeda and Martins, 2014, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014-2015) have proposed extensions to transition-based parsers or adaptations of higher-order models. There is no clear reason why these existing parsers could not be applied to the UCCA dataset. The primary element anchoring this model to the UCCA scheme appears to be a specific feature template, which the authors note is less influential than the count and unigram features. Given that several of these state-of-the-art graph parsers are publicly available [1,2], their absence as baselines or points of comparison is a significant omission.

Similarly, the phrase-based experiments using Uparse (for which a reference is missing upon its first mention) would be strengthened by a comparison with the parser from Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015), which achieves comparable performance on LCFRS-style parsing.

Given the similarities between UCCA and the representations in the Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) shared tasks, it is crucial to evaluate using the same metrics to provide a direct point of comparison. In the current landscape of semantic parsing, where numerous systems, models, and datasets co-exist, the lack of direct comparison points with established parsers and metrics is a major weakness of this work. Despite these shortcomings, the paper addresses an interesting problem. The field is currently at a juncture regarding the future of broad-coverage semantic representation, and this work could spark valuable discussion at a venue like CoNLL.

**Specific Points:**

*   The term "grounded semantics" is used before it is formally introduced on page 2. Please define it upon first use.
*   Could a stronger baseline have been established by converting UCCA to constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, and then parsing with a state-of-the-art constituency parser like the Berkeley parser?
*   The conversion to surface dependency trees seems overly simplistic and loses too much structural information. A more meaning-preserving conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014) for the SemEval SDP task, would have provided a more informative baseline.
*   Please expand on the statement that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondence in the text." An example or a reference would be helpful for clarification.
*   The mention of MRS and DRT highlights a key distinction: UCCA does not appear to model quantifier scope. This makes it more comparable to syntax-oriented graph representations than to these deeper semantic formalisms. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with other schemes. For instance, while it is more abstract than DM and perhaps more underspecified than the tectogrammatical layer of the PCEDT, the precise trade-offs are unclear. How informative is the UCCA scheme, and what are the primary challenges in parsing it? The reported scores suggest it is a difficult parsing task, but a thorough error analysis is needed to understand the specific sources of difficulty.
*   As mentioned earlier, the three guiding principles for semantic representation feel somewhat ad-hoc and would be better motivated within a clearer, broader introduction. This relates to a larger clarity issue: the paper's central argument is ambiguous. Is the main contribution (a) a parser for UCCA, (b) a general model for semantic analysis, or (c) a demonstration that the UCCA scheme is parsable? The paper attempts to argue for all three simultaneously, which makes the narrative dense and at times confusing.
