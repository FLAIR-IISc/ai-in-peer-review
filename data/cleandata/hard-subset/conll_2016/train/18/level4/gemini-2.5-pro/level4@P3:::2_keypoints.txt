- Proposes BSP, a novel transition-based parser to directly handle UCCA's non-terminals, discontinuities, and reentrancies.
- Evaluates conversion-based approaches (to constituency/dependency) alongside BSP to leverage existing parsers.
- Establishes the first empirical benchmark for UCCA parsing with both direct and conversion methods.
- Novelty: first work to tackle parsing of UCCA's combined structural challenges with a specialized parser.
- Experimental design: dual strategy (direct parser vs. conversion + existing parsers) gives richer analysis and comparison.
- Community impact: authors plan to release code and conversion scripts, aiding reproducibility and future research.
- Framing/motivation is overstated and one-sided; claims about UCCA advantages over other formalisms (e.g., need for non-terminals, grounding) are not fully justified.
- BSP is outperformed by a conversion-based LSTM dependency parser on primary edge F-score (59.0% vs. 69.2%), questioning BSP's practical utility.
- BSP performs very poorly on remote edges (≈13.3% F-score), suggesting it struggles with the structures motivating a direct parser.
- Conversion-based baselines are weak because the conversion drops remote edges and no reconstruction techniques (from SDP literature) were used.
- The omission of tree-approximation/reconstruction methods likely underestimates conversion baselines and could narrow the BSP advantage.
- Questions: why were SDP-style graph-to-tree approximation/reconstruction methods not used; what explains BSP’s low remote-edge F-score (data, model, or features); justify necessity of non-terminal nodes versus enriched dependency representations.
