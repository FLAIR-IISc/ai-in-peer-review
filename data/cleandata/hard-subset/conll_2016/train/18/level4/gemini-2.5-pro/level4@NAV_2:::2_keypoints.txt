- Introduces first UCCA parsing methods: conversion-to-tree approaches and a novel transition-based BSP parser handling non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents; evaluated on English UCCA corpora.

- Contribution: first empirical study and benchmark results for UCCA parsing on existing annotated data.

- Contribution: BSP, a new transition-based parser that directly models UCCA’s non-terminal nodes, reentrancy, and discontinuities.

- Contribution: comparative analysis of direct (BSP) versus conversion-based approaches, providing baselines for future work.

- Strength: addresses a clear, previously unaddressed need by building parsers for the available UCCA formalism and data.

- Strength: comprehensive methodological exploration by evaluating both conversion-based baselines and a direct parser.

- Strength: authors commit to releasing converters and parsers, aiding reproducibility and future research.

- Weakness: motivation and framing are overstated—claims about the necessity of UCCA’s structural properties (non-terminals, grounding, etc.) are insufficiently justified.

- Weakness: BSP’s performance is underwhelming—outperformed on primary edges by tree-approximation parsers and low F-score (~13%) on remote edges.

- Weakness: baselines are not state-of-the-art—the conversion is simplistic and neglects established graph-to-tree reconstruction techniques from semantic dependency parsing.

- Question: would integrating a stronger classifier (e.g., LSTM) into BSP be a straightforward way to close the performance gap?

- Question: why were advanced graph reconstruction methods from the Semantic Dependency Parsing literature not used to strengthen the conversion-based baselines?

- Question: what unique semantic information do UCCA non-terminal nodes capture that cannot be represented in a purely bi-lexical dependency graph with expressive edge labels?
