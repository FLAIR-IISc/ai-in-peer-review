- Paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA and proposes several methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA graphs.
- Authors emphasize UCCA features: nonterminal nodes (not tied to words) and that UCCA is “grounded” (text tokens appear as nodes).
- Motivation and introductory framing are unconvincing and overly dismissive of other graph-based semantic representation frameworks.
- The three criteria for SR formalisms are not persuasive; the added expressive power of nonterminal nodes is not demonstrated.
- The paper does not show what cannot be represented with alternative mechanisms (e.g., richer edge labels) instead of nonterminal nodes.
- The discussion of discontinuity is misleading: discontinuity issues UCCA addresses do not arise in ungrounded formalisms like AMR.
- The claim that UCCA is “grounded” vs. AMR is insufficiently justified, and the term “grounded” is overloaded—“anchored” or “lexicalized” would be clearer.
- Parsing architecture seems technically sound but empirical results are weak: MaltParser baseline outperforms BSP on primary edges.
- F-scores for remote edges (which tree parsers cannot directly produce) are only modest.
- Baseline comparison is incomplete: prior work on graph-to-tree approximations and post-processing (e.g., Agić et al. 2015) could substantially improve MaltParser baselines.
- Because stronger adapted baselines are possible, the claimed advantages of the proposed BSP parser over baselines remain unclear.
