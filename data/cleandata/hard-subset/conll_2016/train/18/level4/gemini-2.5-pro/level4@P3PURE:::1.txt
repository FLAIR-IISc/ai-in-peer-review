This paper presents a transition-based graph parser designed to handle the rich, graph-based representations of UCCA, a semantico-cognitive annotation scheme. The authors begin by outlining three properties they argue are essential for a semantic annotation scheme: (1) a graph-based structure, allowing nodes to have multiple parents; (2) the inclusion of non-terminal nodes to represent complex structures like coordinate phrases or multi-word expressions; and (3) support for discontinuous elements (e.g., verb-particle constructions). Notably, these principles are not unique to semantics; they are also relevant to syntactic representations and other layers of linguistic analysis. After situating their work within the context of broad-coverage semantic parsing, the authors present three sets of experiments: (1) phrase-based parsing via a conversion from UCCA to constituency trees, using the Stanford parser; (2) dependency parsing via a conversion from UCCA to dependencies; and (3) their core contribution, a set of experiments demonstrating that their novel transition-based graph parser can parse UCCA graphs directly.

While the work is interesting, I have several significant concerns regarding its methodology and empirical justification that should be addressed before it can be considered for publication.

My primary criticism concerns the lack of comparison to existing, relevant work. The claim to be the first to parse such a semantically-oriented scheme is tenuous. While the parser is new for UCCA specifically, the paper does not demonstrate that UCCA's structural properties (graphs with under-specified arguments and semantic labels like 'Process' or 'State') require a fundamentally new parsing approach. Nothing in the proposed transition set appears specifically designed to handle the unique semantic aspects of these graphs. For instance, the transitions for remote edges could arguably be handled by existing mechanisms, simply by encoding the 'remote' property within the edge label itself (e.g., using an affix).

More importantly, the field of graph parsing has seen significant advances, particularly following the SemEval 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. Several transition-based and higher-order graph parsers have been developed (e.g., Almeida and Martins, 2015; Ribeyre et al., 2014) that could, in principle, be applied to UCCA. Given that these parsers are publicly available, their omission as baselines or points of comparison is a major weakness. The current model seems to be tied to UCCA primarily through its feature templates, rather than a fundamentally novel parsing algorithm.

Similarly, the phrase-based parsing experiments using UPARSE are incomplete. The authors should have also compared against the parser by Fernandez-Gonzales and Martins (2015), which is reported to perform comparably on similar tasks. (A reference for UPARSE was also missing upon its first mention).

The lack of standardized evaluation is another concern. UCCA represents a more abstract level of structure than many Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) treebanks. To properly situate this work, the authors should have reported scores using the standard metrics from the SemEval SDP shared tasks. Without these common comparison points, it is difficult to assess the performance of the proposed parser relative to the broader state of the art in semantic parsing.

Despite these criticisms, the paper addresses an interesting problem at a crucial juncture for the field. For this reason, I believe the work is worth discussing at a venue like CoNLL, provided the authors can address the methodological issues raised.

**Additional Points:**

*   The term "grounded semantics" is used on page 2 before it is formally introduced. Please define it earlier.
*   The baseline experiments could be strengthened. For instance, why not convert UCCA to constituent trees with rich node labels and propagated traces, and then use a powerful standard tool like the Berkeley parser?
*   The conversion to surface dependency trees seems overly simplistic and lossy. A more sophisticated conversion, such as the one proposed by Schluter et al. (2014) for the SemEval SDP task, would have provided a more meaningful dependency parsing baseline.
*   The claim that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text" is intriguing but unsubstantiated. Please provide an example or a reference to clarify this point.
*   The comparison to MRS and DRT highlights that UCCA does not appear to model quantifier scope, making it more comparable to syntax-oriented graph schemes than to these deeper semantic formalisms. A more detailed comparison is needed to position UCCA relative to other graph-based schemes like DM or the semantic plane of the PCEDT. How does it differ in terms of abstractness and underspecification? The parsing scores suggest UCCA is challenging, but a thorough error analysis is required to understand *why* it is difficult to parse and what the typical error patterns are.
*   The three guiding principles for semantic annotation presented in the introduction feel somewhat ad-hoc and need to be better motivated within a broader theoretical context. The paper's central argument is also unclear. Is the main contribution the parser itself, the model's suitability for general semantic analysis, or a demonstration that UCCA is a parsable scheme? The introduction attempts to argue for all three, which results in a dense and confusing narrative.
