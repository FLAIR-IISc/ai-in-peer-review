**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces the first parsing methods for UCCA, a grounded, graph-based semantic representation that, unlike other formalisms, includes non-terminal nodes. The authors explore two main strategies: (1) converting UCCA graphs to tree structures (constituency or dependency) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers, and (2) developing a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed to directly generate UCCA's specific structures, including non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents. The methods are evaluated on the English UCCA-annotated corpora.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  The first empirical study of parsing for the UCCA formalism, providing benchmark results on existing UCCA-annotated data.
2.  The development of BSP, a novel transition-based parser that directly models the key structural properties of UCCA (non-terminal nodes, multiple parents, and discontinuities) by integrating techniques from different parsing paradigms.
3.  A comparative analysis of direct versus conversion-based approaches for UCCA parsing, which serves as a baseline for future work in this area.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Clear Need:** The paper tackles a well-defined and previously unaddressed problem: building a parser for the existing UCCA semantic representation scheme. Given the availability of UCCA-annotated data, developing tools to automatically produce these representations is a valuable and necessary step for the community.
2.  **Comprehensive Methodological Exploration:** The authors present a thorough investigation by exploring two distinct and complementary approaches. The conversion-based method provides strong baselines by leveraging powerful existing parsers, while the direct BSP parser is a technically interesting attempt to model the unique challenges of the UCCA formalism. This dual approach provides a solid foundation for future research.
3.  **Publicly Available Resources:** The commitment to release all converters and parsers is a significant strength. These resources will be highly valuable for any future research on UCCA parsing or the formalism itself, enabling reproducibility and extensions.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unconvincing Motivation and Framing:** The introduction argues that the three structural properties of UCCA (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity) are necessary for any broad-coverage semantic representation. This claim is not sufficiently justified and feels overstated. For instance, the necessity of non-terminal nodes over, say, more expressive edge labeling in a purely lexicalized graph is not clearly established. Similarly, the advantages of a "grounded" representation over an ungrounded one like AMR are asserted rather than demonstrated. The paper would be more compelling if it focused on the practical contribution of parsing an existing formalism, rather than making broad, debatable claims about the nature of semantic representation.
2.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Proposed Parser:** The experimental results do not demonstrate a clear advantage for the proposed BSP parser. On primary edges, which constitute the bulk of the structure, BSP is outperformed by a standard MaltParser and significantly so by the LSTM-based parser, both operating on a simple tree approximation of the data. Furthermore, the F-score on remote edges—the one aspect that the conversion-based methods cannot handle—is very low (around 13%), which calls into question the practical effectiveness of the direct approach at this stage.
3.  **Baselines Are Not State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to conversion-based methods is weakened by the simplicity of the conversion. The problem of converting semantic graphs to trees and reconstructing the original graph has been extensively studied under the name "tree approximations" in the SemEval 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on Semantic Dependency Parsing. There are established methods for reconstructing edges deleted during the graph-to-tree conversion. By not incorporating these reconstruction techniques, the paper's baselines are likely weaker than they could be. A stronger baseline might further reduce the perceived effectiveness of the BSP parser.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance of the LSTM-based dependency parser on primary edges is substantially higher than all other methods, including your proposed BSP parser. This suggests the classifier is a major factor. Could you comment on whether incorporating a more powerful classifier like an LSTM into the BSP transition system would be a straightforward way to close this performance gap?
2.  Regarding the conversion-based baseline: Why did you not explore more sophisticated graph reconstruction techniques from the Semantic Dependency Parsing literature to create a stronger baseline? It seems likely that such methods could recover some of the remote edges, or at least improve the overall score, providing a more challenging point of comparison for your direct parser.
3.  Could you elaborate on the unique semantic information that is captured by UCCA's non-terminal nodes that cannot be represented in a purely bi-lexical dependency graph, for instance by using designated edge labels for phenomena like coordination?
