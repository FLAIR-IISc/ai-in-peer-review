### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces the first parsing systems for the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme, a broad-coverage semantic representation. The authors define Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) as those supporting three key properties: multiple parents (enabling graph structures), non-terminal nodes (for complex units like coordinations), and discontinuous constituents (for phenomena like phrasal verbs).

The paper explores two distinct parsing strategies. The first involves converting the UCCA graphs into more restricted formalisms—constituency trees and dependency trees—and then applying existing state-of-the-art parsers (UPARSE, MaltParser, and a stack-LSTM parser). The second, and the paper's core proposal, is a novel transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), designed to directly generate the full range of UCCA structures. Experiments on the English UCCA-annotated corpora demonstrate the feasibility of parsing this representation, with the strongest system (a conversion-based LSTM parser) achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score on primary edges.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The first empirical study on parsing the UCCA semantic framework.** This work establishes initial performance benchmarks and provides a valuable analysis of the challenges associated with parsing this representation.
2.  **A comparative analysis of direct vs. conversion-based parsing for UCCA.** By implementing both approaches, the authors provide useful insights into the trade-offs between leveraging mature parsing technology through lossy conversions and developing a specialized parser that can model the target structures directly.
3.  **The development of BSP, a transition-based parser that integrates techniques for handling graphs and discontinuities.** While its components are based on previous work, their combination into a single system tailored for a rich semantic representation is a valuable engineering contribution.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Timely and Important Problem:** Broad-coverage semantic parsing is a crucial area of research, and this paper tackles a new, richly structured representation. By focusing on a scheme like UCCA, which aims to be cross-linguistically applicable and cognitively grounded, the work pushes the boundaries of current parsing technology and explores promising directions for semantic representation.
2.  **Thorough Exploration of Methodological Alternatives:** The authors do not commit to a single solution but instead present a comprehensive study comparing two fundamentally different strategies: adapting existing parsers via conversion and building a new, direct parser. This comparative framework is a major strength, as it illuminates the inherent challenges of the task (e.g., the information loss in conversion, confirmed by the upper-bound analysis) and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.
3.  **Establishes Strong Performance Baselines:** The paper successfully demonstrates that parsing UCCA is a feasible, albeit difficult, task. The results, particularly the ~70% F-score achieved by the LSTM-based conversion approach, provide a strong and encouraging baseline for a completely new parsing task. The robustness of the BSP parser in the out-of-domain setting is also a noteworthy finding.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Novelty and Missing Comparisons to Relevant Parsers:** The paper's primary technical contribution, the BSP parser, appears to be a skillful integration of existing transition-based techniques for DAG parsing and discontinuous constituency parsing, rather than a fundamentally new algorithm. The claim of being the "first" to propose techniques for BSS parsing is technically true for UCCA, but overlooks highly relevant prior work. Specifically, the SemEval 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on Broad-Coverage Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) spurred the development of several transition-based and higher-order graph parsers capable of handling many of the same structural phenomena (e.g., multiple parents, re-entrancies). Many of these systems are publicly available (e.g., Ribeyre et al., 2014; Almeida and Martins, 2015) and should have been considered as direct baselines for parsing UCCA graphs. Their absence makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and relative performance of the proposed BSP model.
2.  **Suboptimal Choices in Conversion-Based Experiments:** While the conversion-based approach is a valuable part of the paper, the chosen methods could have been stronger. The dependency conversion is extremely lossy, as confirmed by the upper bound of 88.4 F-score. More sophisticated and structure-preserving conversion methods, such as those developed for the SemEval SDP task (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014), could have provided a much stronger and more informative baseline. Furthermore, for the constituency conversion, other powerful parsers capable of handling discontinuities, such as the one by Fernández-González and Martins (2015), could have been included for comparison.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The paper presents overall performance scores but lacks a qualitative error analysis. To better understand the challenges of parsing UCCA, it would be immensely helpful to know what kinds of structures (e.g., specific coordination types, deeply nested relations, particular discontinuity patterns) are most difficult for the parsers. This analysis is crucial for interpreting the results and guiding future improvements. Additionally, using a non-standard evaluation metric, while necessary for a new formalism, makes it hard to compare the difficulty of this task with related work like SDP.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the key algorithmic innovations in BSP that distinguish it from prior transition-based DAG parsers developed for tasks like SDP? Given their public availability and relevance, could you comment on why these systems were not used as direct baselines for comparison?
2.  The conversion to dependency trees loses significant structural information. Have you considered using more sophisticated, structure-preserving conversion algorithms to create a stronger dependency-based baseline? Similarly, have you considered other baselines, such as training a powerful constituency parser like the Berkeley parser on trees where discontinuities are handled with traces?
3.  You mention that "UCCA graphs may contain implicit units that have no correspondent in the text." Could you provide an example of such a unit and elaborate on the challenges these pose for parsing?
4.  In your discussion of related work, you mention formalisms like MRS and DRT. Could you comment on UCCA's capacity (or lack thereof) to represent phenomena like quantifier scope, and how this positions it relative to these other semantic representations?
5.  Could you provide a brief error analysis for the BSP parser? Identifying the most common types of errors would greatly enhance the reader's understanding of the primary challenges that remain for parsing UCCA.
