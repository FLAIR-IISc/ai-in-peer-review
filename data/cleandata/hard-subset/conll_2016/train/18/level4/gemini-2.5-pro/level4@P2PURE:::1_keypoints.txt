- Claims novelty for a UCCA parser but the transition system is not specifically tailored to UCCA’s semantic properties, making the claim misleading.
- Remote-edge handling could be encoded in labels and managed by standard transition systems, so the approach isn’t uniquely necessary for UCCA.
- Fails to compare against existing semantic graph parsers from SemEval (e.g., Almeda & Martins, Ribeyre) and available DAG/TurboParser implementations.
- The proposed UCCA-specific feature template appears secondary to simple unigram/count features, reducing the claimed UCCA-specificity.
- Phrase-based baseline is weak; should compare to strong constituency parsers (e.g., Berkeley parser) or Fernandez-Gonzales & Martins (2015).
- Conversion to surface dependency trees is overly simplistic and discards structure; should use meaning-preserving conversions like Schluter et al. (2014).
- Evaluation should include SDP shared-task metrics to enable direct comparisons with prior semantic dependency work.
- Missing citation for Uparse at first mention.
- Define “grounded semantics” at first use rather than introducing the term without definition.
- Clarify the claim about UCCA’s “implicit units” with concrete examples or references.
- UCCA does not model quantifier scope, making it closer to syntax-oriented graphs than deeper formalisms like MRS/DRT; require detailed scheme comparisons.
- Need a thorough error analysis to explain why parsing scores are low and identify main sources of difficulty.
- The three guiding principles for representation feel ad-hoc and should be better motivated within a broader introduction.
- Paper’s central contribution is ambiguous (UCCA parser vs general semantic model vs demonstration of UCCA’s parsability), weakening the narrative.
- Despite methodological and evaluation shortcomings, the topic is interesting and could provoke useful discussion at a venue like CoNLL.
