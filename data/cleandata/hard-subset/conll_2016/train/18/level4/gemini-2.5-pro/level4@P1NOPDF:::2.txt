This paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation framework. The authors highlight several features of UCCA: unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes that do not correspond to words in the input sentence; and unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are "grounded," which the authors define as having text tokens appear as nodes in the representation. The paper proposes and evaluates several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA graphs.

Developing a parser for UCCA is a reasonable goal, given the existence of the formalism and its associated annotated data. However, the motivation presented in the introduction and background sections is unconvincing. The authors appear to argue that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism worthy of study, a position that is difficult to defend. This line of argumentation seems unnecessary, as a robust UCCA parser would be a valuable contribution on its own merits without needing to diminish other well-established SR frameworks.

The three criteria for SR formalisms laid out in the introduction are not entirely persuasive. For instance, it is unclear whether "nonterminal nodes" add significant expressive power. While they may offer a convenient way to handle phenomena like coordination without designating a specific head, the authors do not demonstrate what information can be represented by a nonterminal that could not also be captured by other mechanisms, such as more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the problem of handling discontinuity, which UCCA addresses, does not arise in ungrounded representations like AMR in the first place. The paper also fails to clearly articulate the advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style representations. On a terminological note, I would caution against the term "grounded," as it is overloaded in the semantics literature; "anchored" or "lexicalized" might be more precise alternatives. Consequently, the entire introductory section requires more careful phrasing and argumentation to effectively motivate the work.

While the proposed parsing architecture appears technically sound, the empirical results are not compelling. On "primary" edges, a standard MaltParser baseline outperforms the proposed BSP parser. The F-scores for "remote" edges—which a tree-based parser like Malt cannot produce directly—are also modest.

More importantly, the comparison to the MaltParser baseline is incomplete. The task of converting dependency graphs to trees and then reconstructing the original graph has been studied extensively under the heading of "tree approximations," particularly in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. Several methods have been proposed for reconstructing the edges that are lost in the graph-to-tree conversion. For example, Agić et al. (2015, "Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations") discuss this reconstruction problem in detail. By incorporating such post-processing techniques, it is highly likely that the performance of the MaltParser baselines could be substantially improved. This possibility further weakens the case for the proposed BSP parser, making its advantages over a strong, appropriately adapted baseline unclear.
