This paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation framework. The authors highlight several features of UCCA that distinguish it from other formalisms. Unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain "nonterminal" nodes that do not correspond directly to words in the sentence. Unlike AMR, UCCA graphs are "grounded," meaning the text tokens appear as nodes in the semantic representation. The paper introduces several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser designed to directly construct UCCA graphs, and provides an empirical evaluation of these methods.

The development of a parser for an existing, annotated semantic representation like UCCA is a valuable and welcome contribution. Given the availability of UCCA-annotated data, building effective parsers is a crucial next step for the formalism's utility and adoption by the community.

However, the paper could be strengthened in two main areas: the framing of the work in the introduction and the experimental evaluation.

**Clarity of Motivation and Framing**

The introduction and background sections seem to argue that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism with a sensible set of design principles. This strong positioning feels unnecessary, as a high-performing UCCA parser is a significant contribution in its own right. The paper would be more compelling if it focused on motivating the parser's development within the context of UCCA, rather than making broad, and potentially contestable, claims about the formalism's superiority.

Specifically, the three criteria for SR formalisms laid out by the authors could be argued more carefully:

1.  **Nonterminal Nodes:** The paper suggests that nonterminal nodes are a key advantage, for example, in handling coordination without forcing an arbitrary head choice. However, it is not entirely clear what expressive capacity is gained that could not be achieved through other mechanisms, such as more informative edge labels. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between these representational choices would strengthen the authors' argument.

2.  **Groundedness:** The advantages of "grounded" representations over AMR-style representations are not fully elucidated. While the paper mentions discontinuity as a challenge for grounded formalisms, this issue does not arise in un-grounded ones. A clearer articulation of the concrete benefits that groundedness provides would help the reader appreciate this design choice. Furthermore, the term "grounded" has been used to describe many different concepts in semantics over the past decade. To avoid ambiguity, the authors might consider using a more specific term, such as "anchored" or "lexicalized."

Revising the introduction to more cautiously frame UCCA's design principles and focus on the core contribution—the parser itself—would make the paper's claims more robust.

**Experimental Evaluation and Baselines**

While the proposed parser appears technically sound, the evaluation results are not entirely convincing. The primary concern is the comparison to the baseline systems. On "primary" edges, a straightforward MaltParser implementation outperforms the proposed BSP parser. While the BSP parser can handle "remote" edges, which a standard dependency-tree parser cannot, its F-scores on these edges are modest.

More importantly, the comparison could be made more rigorous by engaging with recent literature on parsing graph-based dependency structures. The task of converting dependency graphs to trees for parsing and then reconstructing the graph structure has been studied extensively under the name "tree approximations," particularly in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. Several methods have been proposed for reconstructing the edges that are lost in the graph-to-tree conversion. For instance, Agic et al. (2015, "Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations") discuss this problem in detail.

By incorporating such reconstruction methods, the performance of the MaltParser baseline (and the LSTM-based version) could likely be improved significantly. A stronger, state-of-the-art baseline for graph-based parsing would provide a much more informative benchmark. Without this comparison, the practical advantages of the proposed BSP parser over a strong, pipeline-based approach remain unclear.

In summary, this paper addresses the important problem of parsing UCCA representations. However, its contribution would be significantly strengthened by a more carefully framed introduction and a more thorough experimental evaluation against stronger, state-of-the-art baselines from the semantic dependency parsing literature.
