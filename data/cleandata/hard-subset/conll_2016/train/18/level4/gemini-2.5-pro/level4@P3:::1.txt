### **Summary**

This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a formalism characterized by multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The work is instantiated on the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme. The authors propose and evaluate two approaches: (1) converting UCCA structures to more standard dependency or constituency formats to leverage existing parsers, and (2) a novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP) designed specifically for these structures. Experiments are conducted on UCCA-annotated corpora, establishing the first benchmarks for this parsing task and achieving a primary F-score of 69.2% with the best-performing conversion-based model.

### **Contributions**

1.  **First Empirical Investigation of UCCA Parsing:** The primary contribution of this work is the introduction and first empirical investigation of parsing for the UCCA semantic representation. It establishes initial performance benchmarks on a novel and challenging parsing task, providing a foundation for future research.

2.  **Analysis of Conversion-Based vs. Direct Parsing:** A secondary contribution is the comparative analysis of two distinct strategies for this task. The exploration of conversion-based approaches provides insight into how much of the UCCA structure can be captured by existing parsing technology, highlighting the structural gaps between UCCA and more conventional formalisms. This is contrasted with a direct parsing approach, which, while not outperforming the best conversion method, can handle the full range of UCCA structures.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Novel and Relevant Problem:** The paper addresses the important and timely problem of parsing broad-coverage semantic representations that go beyond standard trees. By focusing on UCCA, which incorporates complex phenomena like multiple parentage, non-terminals, and discontinuities simultaneously, the authors push the boundaries of current parsing technology and introduce a new challenge to the community.

2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Approach:** The authors' two-pronged approach—exploring both conversion-based methods and a direct parser—provides a comprehensive initial study. This allows for a clear comparison between leveraging existing, highly-tuned parsers on a lossy representation versus building a bespoke parser for the full representation. The inclusion of in-domain and out-of-domain experiments further strengthens the empirical evaluation.

3.  **Public Release of Resources:** The commitment to release all converters and parsers is a valuable contribution to the community. This will enable reproducibility and foster further research on this new task, which is crucial for a paper introducing a novel problem.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art Graph Parsers:** The primary weakness is the lack of comparison with existing, highly relevant transition-based graph parsers. The proposed BSP parser is presented as a novel contribution, but its transition set is largely a combination of techniques from prior work on discontinuous and DAG parsing. Several state-of-the-art graph parsers, particularly those developed for the SemEval 2014/2015 Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) tasks (e.g., Almeida and Martins, 2014; Ribeyre et al., 2014), are publicly available and could have been directly applied to the UCCA data as strong baselines. Without these comparisons, it is impossible to assess the true novelty or effectiveness of the proposed BSP model relative to the state of the art in graph parsing.

2.  **Weak Conversion Baselines and Lack of Standard Metrics:** The conversion-based experiments, while conceptually interesting, are methodologically weak. The dependency conversion discards significant structural information, as evidenced by the low upper bound (88.4 F-score), making the results difficult to interpret. More sophisticated conversion methods (e.g., Schluter et al., 2014 from SemEval SDP) could have provided a much stronger and more informative baseline. Furthermore, the decision to introduce a new evaluation metric instead of adapting existing, widely-accepted metrics from the SDP shared tasks hinders direct comparison with the broader semantic parsing literature, making it difficult to situate the reported scores in a wider context.

3.  **Unclear Framing of Contributions and Motivation:** The motivation and framing of the paper could be significantly improved. The three "principles" for BSS are presented as novel requirements but are well-established challenges in the parsing community. The introduction is dense and does not clearly articulate whether the main contribution is the UCCA formalism itself, the specific BSP parser, or the general task of parsing such structures. This ambiguity makes it difficult for the reader to pinpoint the central argument of the paper and assess its contributions accordingly.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify why existing transition-based graph parsers from the SemEval SDP tasks were not used as baselines, given their direct applicability to parsing directed acyclic graphs?

2.  Could you please provide an example or a reference for the "implicit units that have no correspondent in the text" mentioned in Section 2? This would help clarify the full scope of the UCCA parsing task that is deferred to future work.

3.  The paper positions UCCA as a broad-coverage semantic representation. However, it does not seem to model phenomena like quantifier scope, which are central to formalisms like MRS and DRT. Could you comment on the limitations of the UCCA scheme in this regard and how it compares to the semantic depth of other representations like the Prague tectogrammatical layer used in the SDP tasks?
