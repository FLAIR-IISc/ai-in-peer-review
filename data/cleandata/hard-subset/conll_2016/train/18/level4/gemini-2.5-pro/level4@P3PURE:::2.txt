The paper presents the first broad-coverage semantic parsers for UCCA, a graph-based semantic representation. UCCA differs from other formalisms; for example, unlike CoNLL semantic dependency graphs, UCCA graphs can contain 'nonterminal' nodes that do not correspond to words in the text. Unlike AMRs, UCCA graphs are 'grounded', meaning that text tokens appear as nodes in the representation. The authors present and evaluate several parsing methods, including a transition-based parser that directly constructs UCCA graphs.

While developing a semantic parser for UCCA is a reasonable goal given the existence of the formalism and annotated data, the framing in the introduction and background sections is problematic. The authors seem to argue that UCCA is the only graph-based semantic representation (SR) formalism worthy of study. This line of argumentation is unconvincing and, in my view, unnecessary; a high-performing UCCA parser would be a valuable contribution on its own merits, without needing to diminish other formalisms.

I also found the three criteria for semantic representation formalisms laid out by the authors to be unconvincing. For instance, the paper does not clearly demonstrate that 'nonterminal nodes' add expressive capacity. While it can be inconvenient to select a head for coordinated structures, the authors do not explain what information is captured by a nonterminal node that could not also be represented using, for example, more informative edge labels. Furthermore, the issue of discontinuity is irrelevant for SRs that are not 'grounded' in the authors' sense, and the paper fails to clearly articulate the advantages of these 'grounded' representations over AMR-style ones. Additionally, the term 'grounded' is overloaded in the semantics literature; the authors should consider a more precise term, such as 'anchored' or 'lexicalized', to avoid confusion. For these reasons, the introductory sections of the paper require a much more careful and nuanced presentation of the motivation.

The proposed parser appears technically sound, although I did not verify all details of the implementation. However, the evaluation results are not compelling. On 'primary' edges, a straightforward MaltParser baseline outperforms the authors' BSP parser, and the F-scores on 'remote' edges (which a dependency-tree parser like Malt cannot compute directly) are also not very high.

Furthermore, the comparison to the MaltParser baseline is incomplete. The problem of converting dependency graphs to trees has been extensively studied as 'tree approximations,' particularly in the context of the CoNLL 2014 and 2015 shared tasks on semantic dependency parsing. While these tasks did not involve 'nonterminal' nodes, they developed methods for reconstructing edges deleted during the graph-to-tree conversion (e.g., Agic et al., 2015, "Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations"). By incorporating such reconstruction methods, it is likely that the performance of the MaltParser-based baselines could be significantly improved. This makes the reported advantage of the proposed BSP parser on 'remote' edges less convincing and potentially overstated.
