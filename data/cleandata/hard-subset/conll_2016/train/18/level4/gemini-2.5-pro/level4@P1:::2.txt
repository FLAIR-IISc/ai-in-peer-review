This paper introduces the first parsers for Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA), a broad-coverage, graph-based semantic representation. UCCA graphs are distinguished from other formalisms by three main properties: they are "grounded" (i.e., terminals correspond to text tokens), they permit non-terminal nodes that do not correspond directly to words, and they can represent discontinuous constituents. The authors pursue two main strategies for parsing UCCA: 1) converting UCCA structures to and from related formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers, and 2) developing a novel transition-based parser, BSP, that directly constructs UCCA graphs. These approaches are evaluated on the English UCCA-annotated corpora.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The development and evaluation of the first-ever parsers for the UCCA formalism. This is a necessary first step towards making UCCA-annotated resources useful for downstream NLP applications.
2.  An empirical comparison of two distinct parsing strategies for this task: a direct approach versus a conversion-based one. This provides an important baseline and a point of departure for future work on UCCA parsing.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles a Novel and Necessary Task:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to address the task of parsing UCCA structures. Given that the formalism and its associated annotated corpora exist, developing a parser is a logical and valuable contribution to the field. It opens the door for other researchers to work with and build upon this semantic representation.

2.  **Systematic Exploration of Parsing Strategies:** The paper does not simply propose a single parser but instead investigates two different families of approaches. The conversion-based method provides a strong indication of what can be achieved with current state-of-the-art technology, while the direct parsing approach explores a more bespoke solution. This dual strategy provides a more comprehensive and insightful analysis of the problem.

3.  **Publicly Available Resources:** The authors' commitment to making their parsers and converters publicly available is a significant strength. This will facilitate reproducibility and enable other researchers to easily build on this work, which is especially important for a new parsing task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unconvincing Motivation and Framing:** The introduction and background sections seem to argue for the inherent superiority of UCCA's design principles over other semantic representations, which I found unconvincing and unnecessary. The contribution of a novel parser stands on its own merits without needing to claim the underlying formalism is uniquely correct.
    *   **Non-terminal nodes:** The paper does not make a clear case for why non-terminal nodes are a crucial feature for a semantic representation. It is not obvious what expressive power they add that could not be achieved through other means, such as more descriptive edge labels in a purely lexicalized graph (as in Semantic Dependency Parsing).
    *   **Discontinuity and Grounding:** The issue of discontinuity is primarily a consequence of using a "grounded" representation. The paper does not sufficiently articulate the advantages of this grounded approach over ungrounded formalisms like AMR, where discontinuity is not an issue. Furthermore, the term "grounded" has been used for many different concepts in semantics; a more precise term like "anchored" or "lexicalized" might be clearer. The paper would be stronger if it focused on the practical need for a UCCA parser rather than trying to establish the superiority of the UCCA formalism itself.

2.  **Underwhelming Empirical Results and Weak Baselines:** The performance of the proposed BSP parser is not compelling, especially when compared to the conversion-based methods.
    *   On primary edges, the baseline LSTM-based dependency parser outperforms the proposed BSP parser by a significant margin (69.2 vs. 59.0 F-score). The F-score on remote edges—the one component that BSP can model directly and the dependency parser cannot—is very low (13.3 F-score), suggesting that the direct model struggles with this core challenge.
    *   More importantly, the comparison to the conversion-based methods is potentially unfair to the baselines. The conversion from dependency trees back to UCCA is simplistic and makes no attempt to reconstruct the remote edges that were deleted during the initial conversion to a tree. The problem of recovering a full graph from a tree approximation has been studied extensively, for instance in the context of the SemEval shared tasks on Semantic Dependency Parsing (e.g., Agic et al., 2015, "Semantic dependency graph parsing using tree approximations"). By incorporating more sophisticated graph reconstruction techniques, the conversion-based baselines would likely become much stronger, further diminishing the perceived advantage of the direct BSP parser.
