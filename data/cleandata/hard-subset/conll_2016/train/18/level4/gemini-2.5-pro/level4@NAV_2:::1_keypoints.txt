- Introduces the first parsing approach for UCCA, handling multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities.
- Proposes two strategies: convert UCCA to constituency/dependency trees for existing parsers, and a novel direct parser (BSP) extending transition-based frameworks.
- Main contributions: the BSP direct parser and the first empirical benchmarks for UCCA parsing on English corpora.
- Strength: tackles a novel and important problem by opening semantic parsing research to UCCA's graph-structured representations.
- Strength: comprehensive experiments including conversion-based baselines and state-of-the-art parsers, establishing useful baselines.
- Strength: BSP is a well-grounded extension of transition-based methods combining ideas from discontinuous constituency and dependency DAG parsing.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison with existing graph/semantic dependency parsers (e.g., SDP-era systems), weakening novelty and contextualization.
- Weakness: transition system shows limited novelty—uses standard actions for discontinuous graphs and relies on features rather than a UCCA-specific mechanism.
- Weakness: conversion to dependency trees is highly lossy (reported upper bound 88.4 F-score), and better conversion techniques could yield stronger baselines.
- Weakness: use of a custom evaluation metric limits comparability to other broad-coverage semantic parsing tasks; adopting SDP metrics would help.
- Question: why were existing dependency graph parsers (e.g., TurboParser, Ribeyre's DAGParser) not used as baselines for direct parsing?
- Question: please clarify and exemplify the implicit UCCA units excluded from this work.
- Question: was a richer conversion strategy (e.g., rich node labels/traces handled by a parser like Berkeley) considered as a baseline?
- Question: how does UCCA's expressive power and parsing difficulty compare to SDP formalisms, and can you provide error analysis contrasting BSP and SDP parser failures?
