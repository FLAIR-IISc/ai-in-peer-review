

**Summary and Contributions**  
The paper introduces the concept of Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which require three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units. It proposes two approaches for parsing BSS: (1) conversion-based methods that map BSS into dependency/constituency trees and use existing parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three properties. The primary contribution is the first parser capable of handling all three BSS properties simultaneously, with the conversion protocols serving as a secondary contribution. The third contribution is the experimental demonstration that transition-based methods can be effectively applied to BSS parsing using UCCA-annotated data.

**Strengths**  
1. **Clear identification of a significant gap**: The paper compellingly argues that existing semantic parsers fail to support all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation. This is well-motivated by examples and careful comparison to prior work (UCCA, SDP, AMR), making a strong case for the need for BSS parsing. The paper successfully demonstrates that UCCA is the only scheme supporting all three properties, which justifies the focus on UCCA as a test case.

2. **Practical and principled conversion protocols**: The conversion procedures between UCCA and dependency/constituency trees are well-defined and carefully reasoned. The authors acknowledge the lossy nature of conversion but show that it remains effective in practice. This provides a valuable bridge for leveraging existing parsers and could inform future work on semantic representation conversion.

3. **Strong empirical results for a novel task**: The BSP parser achieves nearly 70% labeled F-score on primary edges (the highest among direct approaches), which is impressive given the complexity of the task. The results demonstrate that transition-based parsing is viable for BSS, and the out-of-domain robustness (comparable performance on 20K Leagues data) is particularly noteworthy.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Evaluation metrics lack standardization**: The paper defines new metrics for evaluating BSS parsing, but these are not sufficiently justified or compared to standard parsing metrics. The metric's collapse to PARSEVAL for tree structures is mentioned, but the paper doesn't explain how these metrics compare to those used in other semantic parsing work (e.g., AMR). This makes it difficult to assess the significance of the reported scores relative to prior work.

2. **Insufficient comparison to alternative semantic representations**: While the paper claims UCCA is the only scheme supporting all three properties, it doesn't thoroughly compare with other representations that might partially support them. For example, the paper cites SDP as supporting discontinuous units and multiple parents but disallowing non-terminal nodes, yet it doesn't explore whether SDP could be adapted to handle non-terminal nodes (e.g., via conversion or extension) to make a more nuanced comparison.

3. **Limited analysis of error patterns**: The paper reports F-scores but doesn't analyze where errors occur, which is critical for understanding the limitations of the approach. For instance, it's unclear whether the parser struggles more with remote edges, non-terminal nodes, or discontinuous units. Without this, it's difficult to assess the paper's claims about the strengths and weaknesses of the two approaches.

4. **Underdeveloped discussion of scalability and cross-linguistic applicability**: While the paper mentions UCCA's cross-linguistic applicability, it doesn't discuss how the parser would scale to other languages or larger datasets. The evaluation is limited to English, and the paper doesn't address potential challenges in adapting BSP to other languages with different structural properties.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How do the proposed evaluation metrics compare to those used in standard semantic parsing tasks (e.g., AMR or SDP)? Could the authors provide a brief comparison to contextualize their results?
2. The paper claims UCCA is the only scheme supporting all three properties, but how do other representations like MRS or DRT handle these properties? Could a similar conversion approach be applied to them?
3. The BSP parser achieves 15.3% F-score on remote edges. What are the most common error patterns for remote edges, and how might they be addressed in future work?
