

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper addresses the gap in semantic parsing by proposing techniques for parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which require three structural properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors identify UCCA as the only semantic annotation scheme supporting all three properties, but note it lacks a dedicated parser. Their main contributions are: (1) developing conversion procedures to transform UCCA structures into dependency trees and discontinuous constituency trees, enabling the use of existing parsers; and (2) introducing the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), a transition-based parser that directly supports all three BSS properties. The authors evaluate their approach on UCCA-annotated corpora, demonstrating that BSP achieves nearly 70% labeled F-score for primary edges.

**Strengths**

1. The paper identifies a clear and important gap in semantic parsing: no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation. This is a significant contribution to the field, as the authors correctly argue that many existing semantic parsers either lack one or more of these properties or avoid grounding semantic units altogether.

2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of UCCA and its relationship to other semantic representations, which helps contextualize their work. Their justification for focusing on UCCA is well-supported, particularly regarding its cross-linguistic applicability and semantic stability across translations.

3. The conversion-based approach is practical and demonstrates that existing technology can be adapted to handle UCCA structures with minimal loss. The detailed conversion procedures (Algorithms 1 and 2) are well-explained and provide a useful framework for future work.

4. The BSP parser is a novel contribution with well-designed transition set and features. The paper provides sufficient technical details for replication, including the feature templates (Figure 3) and training procedures. The ablation study (Table 3) effectively demonstrates the contribution of different feature sets.

5. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with both in-domain and out-of-domain testing. The authors provide a clear analysis of the results, including the performance of conversion-based methods versus direct parsing, and the impact of removing remote edge transitions.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper does not sufficiently justify why UCCA was chosen over other semantic representations that might also support the three structural properties. While the authors mention AMR and SDP, they do not provide a thorough comparison of why UCCA is uniquely suited for this work, particularly given that SDP already supports discontinuous units and multiple parents (though not non-terminal nodes).

2. The evaluation metrics are custom and not well-justified. The authors define their own measures for comparing DAG structures, but do not adequately explain why these are superior to existing metrics or how they relate to standard evaluation practices in semantic parsing. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the metric's theoretical grounding.

3. The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the conversion-based approach. While the authors note that conversion is lossy, they don't provide sufficient detail on how much information is lost or how this impacts the results. The upper bounds for conversion-based methods (Table 2) are presented but not thoroughly analyzed in terms of practical implications.

4. The paper lacks comparison with recent semantic parsing approaches that might have addressed similar challenges. For instance, the authors mention AMR parsing work (Wang et al., 2015), but do not discuss how their approach relates to or differs from these methods, which would help contextualize their contribution.

5. The paper does not sufficiently discuss the practical implications of their work for downstream applications. While the conclusion mentions machine translation and summarization, the paper does not provide concrete examples of how their parser would improve these applications or what specific challenges it addresses.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors justify UCCA as the best representation for this work, given that other semantic representations (like SDP) support two of the three required properties? Could similar conversion techniques be applied to other representations?

2. Could the authors provide more detail on how the custom evaluation metrics relate to human judgment of semantic structure? How do these metrics correlate with the UCCA inter-annotator agreement (80-85% F-score)?

3. The paper notes that the LSTM parser outperforms MaltParser by 9.1% F-score. Given that the LSTM parser differs only in its classifier, could the authors discuss how this finding impacts their approach to BSP, particularly regarding the potential benefits of using a more sophisticated classifier for BSP?

4. How does the performance of BSP compare to the upper bound provided by the conversion-based methods (88.4% F-score)? What specific aspects of the conversion process prevent the parsers from reaching this upper bound?
