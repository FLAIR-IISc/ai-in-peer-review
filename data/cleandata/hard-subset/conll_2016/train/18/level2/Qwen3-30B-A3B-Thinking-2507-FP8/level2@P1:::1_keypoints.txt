- Proposes techniques for parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) requiring multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuous units; identifies UCCA as unique in supporting all three but lacking a parser.
- Main contributions: conversion procedures from UCCA to dependency and discontinuous constituency trees, and a new transition-based Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) that directly supports all BSS properties, achieving ~70% labeled F-score for primary edges.

- Strength: Clearly identifies a gap—no existing parser supports all three BSS structural properties—and motivates the need for this work.
- Strength: Provides a thorough analysis of UCCA and situates it relative to other semantic representations, arguing for its cross-linguistic and semantic stability.
- Strength: Conversion-based approach is practical and well-documented (Algorithms 1 & 2), showing existing parsers can be adapted with minimal loss.
- Strength: BSP is a novel, well-specified transition-based parser with detailed features and training procedures; ablation study shows feature contributions.
- Strength: Evaluation is comprehensive (in-domain and out-of-domain) with clear analysis of conversion vs. direct parsing and the effect of remote-edge transitions.

- Weakness: Insufficient justification for choosing UCCA over other representations that might support the required properties (e.g., SDP).
- Weakness: Custom evaluation metrics are introduced without sufficient justification or connection to standard semantic parsing metrics.
- Weakness: Limitations and information loss in the conversion-based approach are noted but not quantified or deeply analyzed.
- Weakness: Lacks comparison to recent semantic parsing methods that may address similar challenges, limiting contextualization of contributions.
- Weakness: Practical implications for downstream applications (e.g., MT, summarization) are mentioned but not concretely demonstrated.

- Question: Why is UCCA the best representation choice given others (like SDP) already support some required properties, and could conversion techniques apply to those?
- Question: How do the custom evaluation metrics relate to human judgments and UCCA inter-annotator agreement (80–85% F-score)?
- Question: Given the LSTM parser’s 9.1% F-score advantage over MaltParser, how might more sophisticated classifiers affect BSP performance?
- Question: How does BSP’s performance compare to the conversion-based upper bound (88.4% F-score), and what conversion aspects prevent reaching that bound?
