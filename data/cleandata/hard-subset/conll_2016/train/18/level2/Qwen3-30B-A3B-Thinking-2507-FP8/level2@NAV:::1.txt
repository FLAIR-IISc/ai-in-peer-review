

This paper addresses a significant gap in semantic parsing by proposing the first techniques for parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which require three structural properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The main contributions are: (1) the first parser supporting all three BSS properties, (2) conversion-based approaches mapping UCCA structures to dependency/constituency trees, and (3) experimental validation on UCCA-annotated corpora.

Strengths: The paper identifies a clear, well-motivated gap in semantic parsing that has been overlooked in prior work. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including both in-domain and out-of-domain testing with multiple approaches. The results are promising, achieving 69.2% labeled F-score on primary edges, which is a solid baseline for future work. The paper also demonstrates practical relevance by connecting UCCA parsing to applications like machine translation and summarization.

Weaknesses: The paper doesn't sufficiently analyze why the parser's performance (69.2% F-score) lags significantly below UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80-85%), which is a critical concern for a parsing task. The conversion-based approach's information loss (particularly regarding remote edges) is acknowledged but not thoroughly evaluated in terms of downstream impact. The paper also fails to adequately compare their approach with other semantic parsing frameworks beyond brief mentions of AMR and SDP, making it difficult to assess relative novelty.

The authors should address the performance gap between parser results and inter-annotator agreement, providing error analysis to identify specific failure modes. They should also expand the comparison with existing semantic parsing approaches to better establish the contribution's novelty. While the conversion-based methods are interesting, the paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of when and why these approaches fail to preserve the full semantic structure.

Questions: How do you plan to address the performance gap between your parser (69.2% F-score) and UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80-85%)? Can you provide specific examples of where the BSP parser fails, particularly regarding remote edges? How might your approach be extended to handle implicit units, which the paper mentions as deferred to future work?
