

Summary of the paper: The paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS), which require three structural properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors propose two complementary parsing strategies: (1) conversion-based approaches that map BSS into related formalisms (dependency trees and discontinuous constituency trees) for using existing parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser called Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) that directly supports all three properties. The work is evaluated on UCCA-annotated corpora, the only semantic representation scheme supporting all three properties.

Main contributions: The paper makes three significant contributions: (1) it identifies and defines the structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing, (2) it introduces the first parser capable of handling all three structural properties simultaneously, and (3) it demonstrates effective conversion procedures between UCCA structures and more commonly used formalisms.

Strengths: 
1. The paper clearly identifies a meaningful gap in semantic parsing research by establishing the three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation. The motivation is well-justified through examples and references to existing work, making the problem well-defined.
2. The paper provides a thorough technical description of the conversion procedures and the BSP parser, including the transition set and feature templates. This level of detail is valuable for reproducibility and further research.
3. The evaluation on UCCA-annotated corpora is well-executed, with both in-domain and out-of-domain testing. The results demonstrate that BSS parsing is feasible with transition-based methods, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score on primary edges.
4. The paper makes all code and models publicly available, which is a significant contribution to the community and enables further research in this area.
5. The discussion of the conversion-based approach provides useful insights into the trade-offs between using existing parsers versus developing new specialized parsers.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't provide sufficient comparison with other semantic parsing approaches that handle subsets of the required structural properties. Without this, it's difficult to assess the true novelty and significance of the work relative to existing literature.
2. The evaluation metrics, while explained, are custom-defined and not standard in the field. This makes it challenging for the community to directly compare results with other semantic parsing approaches.
3. The paper doesn't adequately address why the LSTM-based parser (73.2% F-score) outperforms the BSP (62.4% F-score) for primary edges, despite both using transition-based approaches. This gap needs further explanation to understand the limitations of the BSP.
4. The paper lacks a detailed analysis of failure cases, which would help identify specific challenges and guide future improvements to the parser.
5. The conversion-based approach loses significant information when mapping to dependency trees, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient examples or discussion of the most problematic structures.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the performance of the BSP parser compare to other semantic parsing approaches that handle some of the structural properties (e.g., SDP parsers or AMR parsers)?
2. Could the authors provide more details about the inter-annotator agreement scores for the UCCA corpus to help contextualize the reported performance?
3. The paper mentions that conversion to dependency format loses more information than conversion to constituency trees. Could the authors provide specific examples of structures that are particularly challenging to convert?

The paper presents a meaningful contribution to semantic parsing by addressing a well-defined gap in the literature. The technical approach is sound, and the results demonstrate that BSS parsing is feasible with transition-based methods. The main limitations lie in the lack of comparative analysis with related work and the under-explained performance gap between BSP and the LSTM-based parser. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and impact.
