Summary and contributions
The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing with three key structural properties: multiple parents (reentrancy), non-terminal units, and discontinuities. It presents two complementary strategies:
1) Conversion-based parsing: procedures to map UCCA graphs to (i) discontinuous constituency trees and (ii) dependency trees, enabling the use of existing transition-based parsers and subsequent inverse conversion to UCCA.
2) A direct transition-based parser (BSP) that natively supports reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuity via an extended transition set, dynamic oracle training, and UCCA-oriented features.

Experiments on UCCA English corpora (Wikipedia in-domain and 20K Leagues out-of-domain) evaluate both strategies with a yield-based labeled F1 metric for primary and remote edges. The strongest primary-edge result is obtained via conversion with an LSTM dependency parser (69.2 F1), while BSP uniquely predicts remote edges (13.3 F1 in-domain).

As seen here, the primary contributions are:
- A transition system and learning setup for directly parsing grounded semantic DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities in UCCA.
- Conversion protocols between UCCA and dependency/constituency formats, with empirical upper bounds and baselines demonstrating what current technology can recover.
- An evaluation protocol and first published parsing results for UCCA, including in-/out-of-domain comparison and feature ablations.

Strengths
- Well-motivated task and precise problem formulation. The need to handle reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuity in grounded representations is clearly argued, with concrete UCCA examples (e.g., coordination, phrasal verbs; Fig. 1).
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility. Transition set, training (averaged perceptron, dynamic oracle), and feature templates are detailed; conversion algorithms are explicit (Algorithms 1–2); evaluation measure is defined and related to PARSEVAL. Upper bounds quantify conversion loss (e.g., 88.4 primary F1 for dependency conversion).
- Comprehensive empirical study with competitive baselines. The comparison spans UPARSE (discontinuous constituency), Malt (arc-standard/eager), and an LSTM dependency parser. Results are contextualized with upper bounds, making it clear where information is lost due to conversion versus parsing (e.g., LSTM 69.2 primary F1 vs. 88.4 upper bound).
- First evidence for UCCA parsing and remote-edge prediction. While conversion methods achieve 0 F1 on remotes by construction, BSP predicts remotes with 13.3 F1 in-domain and 13.6 F1 out-of-domain, establishing a baseline for reentrancy in grounded semantics.
- Useful analysis and insights. Feature ablations highlight impactful components (e.g., unigram/bigram features; the “ratio” feature improving primary recall). Out-of-domain results are close to in-domain (e.g., BSP primary 59.0 vs. 57.1), suggesting robustness.

Weaknesses
- Primary-edge accuracy of direct parsing lags behind conversion with strong neural baselines. BSP’s primary F1 (59.0) is substantially below the LSTM conversion result (69.2), limiting the appeal of the direct approach in its current form. The paper itself suggests replacing the local classifier with LSTMs; this seems essential to realize the promise of BSP.
- Remote-edge performance remains low. Although unique among baselines, remote-edge F1 is modest (13.3 in-domain; LR 11.8), and the paper offers limited error analysis or label-wise breakdown to guide improvements (e.g., which remote relations are hardest, typical confusions).
- Evaluation scope and comparisons are somewhat constrained. Only English UCCA is considered; inter-sentential links and implicit units are excluded (reasonable for scope, but important for completeness). No comparison to SDP-style systems via a dedicated conversion is attempted, and existing DAG parsers are omitted due to code availability; a simplified reimplementation or alternative publicly available graph parsers could strengthen the empirical case.
- Decoding and modeling choices limit performance. Greedy inference without beam or global scoring can be brittle, especially with SWAP and REMOTE transitions. The perceptron and sparse features are dated relative to neural transition-based parsers; this is reflected in the large gap to the LSTM dependency baseline.
- Some details deferred or missing. The Label(·) function for inverse conversion is in supplementary material; runtime/complexity and memory footprint are not reported; significance testing is not presented. These omissions make it harder to assess practical utility and robustness.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- What is the full definition and empirical impact of the Label(·) function used in dependency→constituency inverse conversion? How sensitive are results to this heuristic?
- For remote edges, which labels dominate the errors? Can confusion matrices or per-label F1 be provided?
- How often is SWAP used, and what is the distribution of discontinuity types in errors (e.g., “pass” vs. “gap” in Maier & Lichte’s taxonomy)?
- What are decoding speed and memory statistics for BSP vs. conversion pipelines? Any indications of scalability to longer sentences?
- Was beam search tried for BSP? If so, what was the impact; if not, are there constraints that made it impractical?
- Are there constraints in BSP to prevent cycles or duplicate primary parents beyond the stated conditions? Any observed violations during training/inference?

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Strengthen BSP with a neural scoring model (e.g., BiLSTM features or stack LSTMs) and/or beam search; the large margin to the LSTM dependency baseline suggests significant gains are likely.
- Provide targeted error analysis for remote edges and discontinuities (label-wise F1, typical structural mistakes), and report ablations specific to the REMOTE feature set, to guide future work.

Optional:
- Add statistical significance tests for main comparisons.
- Report efficiency metrics and include the conversion heuristics inline (not only in supplementary material).
- Explore an SDP-style conversion and comparison to public SemEval SDP parsers to triangulate the performance landscape for reentrancy.
- Consider a small multilingual or cross-domain test to further substantiate the claim of cross-linguistic applicability of UCCA (even if only for zero-shot transfer).

Overall, the paper tackles an important and underexplored problem with clear technical contributions and a careful empirical setup. The conversion study is informative, and the direct parser establishes a baseline for full BSS parsing in UCCA. Enhancing the learning/inference components and deepening the analysis of remote edges would significantly strengthen the work.
