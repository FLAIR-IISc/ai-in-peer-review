Summary and contributions
This paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing (BSS) where target graphs must support three structural properties often required by grounded semantic representations: multiple parents (reentrancy), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. The authors focus on UCCA, the only available scheme in this space exhibiting all three properties, and present two complementary strategies: (i) conversion protocols from UCCA to constituency and dependency formats enabling the reuse of existing parsers, with inverse mapping for evaluation; and (ii) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three properties via an extended transition set and feature design. They introduce an evaluation protocol for DAGs based on labeled yields, conduct in-domain and out-of-domain experiments on UCCA corpora, and analyze feature contributions.

Perceived main contributions (in order of strength):
1) A transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuity in grounded semantic structures, with a dynamic oracle and novel features (including a “ratio” regularizer).
2) Systematic conversion protocols between UCCA and (dis)continuous constituency and CoNLL-style dependency trees, enabling benchmarking with off-the-shelf parsers and establishing informative upper bounds.
3) A practical evaluation setup for UCCA/BSS using labeled yields, plus empirical results on in-domain and out-of-domain data with ablation.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and scope: The paper articulates why grounded semantic parsing requires all three structural properties, with concrete UCCA examples (coordination, phrasal verbs, shared arguments). This frames a meaningful gap: existing grounded parsers do not jointly support all three.
- Solid engineering of a direct parser: BSP extends transition-based parsing with dedicated transitions (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE, SWAP, NODE) and a dynamic oracle suited to DAGs with discontinuity. The feature design is thoughtful, including UCCA-specific remote-edge features and the simple yet effective “ratio” feature acting as a node-creation regularizer.
- Comprehensive experimental setup: The study evaluates (a) direct parsing with BSP, (b) constituency conversion with UPARSE, and (c) dependency conversion with Malt (arc-standard/eager) and LSTM Stack-LSTM. Upper bounds quantify conversion loss (e.g., for dependency, 88.4 F1 on primary edges vs 100 for constituency). Both in-domain (Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) settings are reported, showing robustness (BSP primary F1 59.0 in-domain; 57.1 OOD; remote F1 ~13 in both).
- Insightful empirical findings: The LSTM dependency parser via conversion achieves the highest primary F1 (69.2), while conversion-based methods score 0 on remote edges by construction; BSP predicts remote edges (F1 13.3 in-domain), demonstrating the value of direct modeling. Feature ablations are informative (e.g., unigrams/bigrams and ratio notably aid primary recall).
- Reproducibility intent: The authors plan to release converters and parsers, which will benefit the community working on grounded semantics and UCCA.

Weaknesses
- Limited superiority of the direct method on core metrics: The strongest primary-edge result comes from the conversion to dependency trees with an LSTM classifier (69.2 F1), not from the proposed BSP (59.0 F1). While BSP uniquely predicts remote edges, their F1 remains low (~13). The paper could better analyze why BSP lags on primary edges and how to close this gap beyond the general suggestion to swap in an LSTM classifier.
- Conversion/inverse-conversion bias and evaluation confounds: The dependency inverse conversion is heuristic (e.g., always high-attach), which can both obscure or penalize structures when mapping back to UCCA. This may favor or disadvantage specific baselines in ways not fully quantified. The upper bounds (e.g., 88.4 for dependency) show substantial irrecoverable loss, but the practical impact on different phenomena (coordination, MWE, PP attachment) is not analyzed.
- Limited qualitative/error analysis: Results would be more actionable with a breakdown by phenomenon (e.g., coordination vs. phrasal verbs, long-distance discontinuities, frequency/magnitude of reentrancies) and typical error patterns for remote vs. primary edges. This is especially relevant given UCCA’s rich label set and the low remote edge F1.
- Scope and generality: Experiments are restricted to English UCCA foundational layer, with linkage and implicit nodes excluded. It remains unclear how BSP generalizes to other grounded schemes (e.g., SDP variants allowing reentrancy but not non-terminals) or to multi-layer UCCA (linkage, implicit). Some discussion is given, but evidence is limited.
- Methodological gaps: Inference is greedy; no beam or global scoring is attempted. No significance testing is reported. The dynamic oracle and training details are described but hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., MINUPDATE, IMPORTANCE, SWAP upweighting) and efficiency metrics (speed, memory) are absent.

Questions to authors
- Can you report a variant of BSP with a neural (e.g., Stack-LSTM) local classifier to directly test your claim that architecture is the main bottleneck? Even a small-scale comparison would be informative.
- How sensitive are results to the inverse conversion heuristics, particularly the high-attach assumption? Could a stronger inverse conversion (e.g., learned reconstruction à la Kong et al., 2015) improve fairness of conversion-based baselines?
- What is the distribution and length of discontinuities in the datasets, and how does SWAP usage correlate with errors? Any ablation of SWAP or analysis by gap type/length?
- Could you provide per-label F1 and phenomenon-based breakdowns (e.g., Center, Participant, Linker; coordination; MWEs; remote edge types)? This would help identify where BSP helps most relative to conversion pipelines.
- Did you explore beam search for BSP? If so, how does it trade off accuracy vs. speed?

Additional comments
- The labeled-yield evaluation is reasonable and reduces to PARSEVAL on trees; however, it would help to complement it with unlabeled scores and alternative graph metrics to assess structural vs. labeling errors.
- Reporting inter-annotator agreement (80–85 primary F1) is helpful; placing system results relative to this bound per phenomenon would further contextualize difficulty.
- Releasing code and converters will be valuable; consider including scripts for computing upper bounds and reproducing conversions to facilitate cross-representation studies.

Overall recommendation
This is a well-motivated and thoughtfully executed paper that advances grounded semantic parsing by (a) proposing the first direct transition-based parser supporting reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuities, and (b) establishing informative conversion pipelines and baselines. While the best primary-edge scores come from conversion with a strong neural classifier, BSP uniquely predicts remote edges and provides a foundation that could benefit significantly from neural scoring and/or beam search. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening analysis and clarifying the impact of conversion heuristics and model choices.
