Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing where structures require (i) multiple parents, (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuities. Using UCCA as the target formalism, the authors present two complementary approaches: (a) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA graphs into more restrictive formalisms (dependency and discontinuous constituency) and back; and (b) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three structural properties, with an accompanying training procedure and feature set. They define an evaluation measure suitable for DAGs and report in-domain and out-of-domain results.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A transition system (BSP) that directly parses into DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including explicit transitions for remote (reentrant) edges.
2) Practical conversion procedures between UCCA and dependency/constituency representations (with clear algorithms and upper bounds), enabling strong baselines with off-the-shelf parsers.
3) The first empirical study of UCCA parsing, including evaluation metrics, datasets, and analyses of feature impacts.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and relevance: The paper isolates three structural properties required for broad semantic coverage and argues convincingly that grounded parsing needs them. UCCA is an appropriate testbed. This situates the work well within ongoing efforts beyond SRL and AMR.
- Technical soundness and completeness of the transition system: The BSP transition inventory is well-motivated (NODE, SWAP, LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE vs. LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE), enforces UCCA constraints (single primary parent), and uses a dynamic oracle. Feature design is carefully adapted from related parsers with UCCA-specific additions (e.g., ratio regularizer, remote-aware counts).
- Conversion methodology and baselines: The constituency and dependency conversion algorithms are explicit and reproducible, with informative upper bounds (100% primary for constituency conversion; 88.4% for dependency). This yields strong, competitive baselines; notably, the LSTM dependency parser achieves 69.2 primary F1, highlighting the effectiveness of conversion-based pipelines.
- Robustness and generalization: The out-of-domain (20K Leagues) results are comparable to in-domain for both BSP and BSPTree, suggesting some domain robustness of the proposed approach.
- Reproducibility intent: The authors commit to releasing converters and parsers, and provide enough algorithmic detail to facilitate replication.

Weaknesses
- Limited performance of the direct parser vs. conversion baselines: BSP’s primary F1 (59.0) lags the best conversion-based system (69.2) by a large margin. While BSP is the first to produce remote edges, remote F1 is low (13.3 in-domain; 13.6 OOD), calling into question its practical utility in its current form. The claim that transition-based methods are effective is better supported by the conversion+LSTM setup than by BSP itself.
- Evaluation metric and analysis limitations: The yield-based labeled edge matching is reasonable for UCCA DAGs, but the paper lacks detailed breakdowns by phenomenon (e.g., coordination, MWEs, discontinuities, remotes) and by error type. Given the low remote scores and the importance of non-terminals, finer-grained analyses would clarify where BSP fails and where conversion pipelines lose information.
- Missing strong DAG baselines: The paper does not compare against existing SDP parsers or other DAG-capable systems, arguing code unavailability. This leaves a gap in understanding how well bi-lexical DAG parsers (with suitable mappings) might fare, even if they lack non-terminals natively. A partial comparison, alternative implementations, or post-hoc reproduction for a subset would strengthen the empirical story.
- Training/inference design likely underpowered: BSP uses greedy inference and an averaged perceptron. The experiments convincingly show that a stronger classifier (stack-LSTM) yields sizable gains in the conversion setting; it is highly plausible that replacing BSP’s local classifier and/or using beam/global training would close much of the gap. As is, the direct method underperforms due to choice of learning/inference rather than transition design.
- Conversion upper bound interpretation: The 100% primary upper bound for constituency conversion (due to only dropping remotes) is trivial and less informative about representational loss than the dependency case. A richer analysis of how much linguistic content is lost in each conversion (beyond the numeric upper bound) would be useful.

Questions to authors
- How do you ensure acyclicity during BSP parsing? Are there explicit constraints or checks preventing cycles when creating remote edges?
- What is the prevalence of remote edges, discontinuities, and coordination in the datasets? Please provide counts and per-phenomenon F1 to contextualize the 13% remote F1.
- Did you try beam search or global training for BSP? Given the strong effect of LSTMs in the conversion setting, what results do you get with an LSTM-based classifier inside BSP?
- How sensitive are results to the SWAP weighting and MINUPDATE/IMPORTANCE hyperparameters? Any ablation on those?
- For the dependency inverse conversion, the Label(·) heuristic is a major source of loss (upper bound 88.4). Can you quantify how much each heuristic choice contributes, or try alternative mappings (e.g., learned reconstruction)?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include runtime and model size for BSP vs. conversion pipelines; transition-based methods are often chosen for efficiency.
- Provide an error analysis on a few representative constructions (e.g., coordination heads, PP relators, phrasal verbs) to illustrate typical BSP errors vs. conversion artifacts.
- Consider multi-task or pipeline variants that exploit strong syntactic parses, or joint training with SDP/AMR-style supervision, to improve generalization on remote edges.
- The references and citation formatting contain multiple minor typos; please proofread.
- The commitment to release code and converters is valuable; ensure trained models and scripts for reproducing conversions/evaluations are included.

Overall, this is a timely, well-motivated paper with clear algorithms and a useful empirical study. While the direct parser underperforms the best conversion baseline at present, the contribution of a transition system that supports all three structural properties, coupled with open resources and solid baselines, is significant. Strengthening BSP with modern neural classifiers and adding detailed analyses would make the work more compelling.
