Summary
The paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing for grounded representations that require (i) multiple parents (DAGs), (ii) non-terminal units, and (iii) discontinuities. It introduces two approaches: (a) conversion-based pipelines that map UCCA graphs to constituency/dependency trees for use with existing parsers and then invert the mapping, and (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports the three structural properties. Experiments on UCCA show that a stack-LSTM dependency parser plus conversion reaches 69.2% F on primary edges, while BSP attains comparable primary scores and uniquely recovers some remote edges.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A direct transition system (BSP) for semantic DAGs with discontinuities and non-terminals, including explicit REMOTE-edge operations, SWAP, a dynamic oracle, and a useful global “ratio” regularization feature.
2) Practical conversion protocols between UCCA graphs and constituency/dependency formats, with upper-bound analyses, enabling reuse of strong syntactic parsers and revealing where information is lost.
3) First systematic evaluation and metrics for UCCA parsing across in-domain and out-of-domain settings, plus ablations that illuminate impactful features.

Strengths
- Importance and scope: The work squarely addresses key structural needs for semantic parsing beyond bi-lexical dependencies, and uses UCCA—one of the few grounded schemes supporting all three properties.
- Sound methodology: Both direct and conversion paradigms are explored, with clear algorithms for forward/inverse conversions, dynamic-oracle training, and principled evaluation including upper bounds.
- Empirical depth: Comparisons across multiple parsers, in/out-of-domain tests, feature ablations, and careful discussion of primary vs. remote edges provide a well-rounded picture.
- Practical insight: The strong performance of the LSTM classifier in the conversion pipeline gives a concrete path to upgrade BSP’s local model.

Weaknesses
- Limited performance on remote edges: BSP’s remote F-scores are low (~13%), and conversion pipelines cannot predict them at all, leaving a central objective only partially addressed.
- Method competitiveness: The best primary scores come from the converted LSTM pipeline; the direct parser lags and uses a linear perceptron without beam/global search or neural features.
- Generality and coverage: Experiments focus on English UCCA and omit implicit units and most inter-sentential phenomena; cross-lingual or other formalisms (SDP/AMR) are not evaluated.
- Evaluation/reporting: No statistical significance tests; limited error analysis by phenomenon/label; inverse conversion heuristics may bias conclusions without detailed diagnostics.

Questions to Authors
- Can BSP benefit from a neural classifier (e.g., BiLSTM/stack-LSTM) or beam/global training, and do you expect this to raise remote-edge recall?
- How transferable is BSP to other semantic DAG formalisms (SDP, AMR with alignment) with minimal transition changes?
- Could you provide a breakdown of errors by UCCA label and by phenomenon (coordination, phrasal verbs, discontinuities)?
- How sensitive are results to the high-attachment heuristic in inverse conversion, and have you tried learned attachment reconstruction?
