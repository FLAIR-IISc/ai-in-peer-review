- Paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing (BSS) for graphs with reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuities, focusing on UCCA.
- Proposes two strategies: conversion protocols between UCCA and constituency/dependency formats, and a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three properties.
- Introduces a labeled-yield evaluation protocol for DAGs and runs in-domain and out-of-domain experiments with ablations.
- Main contribution 1: BSP — a transition-based parser with extended transitions, a dynamic oracle, novel features, and a “ratio” regularizer.
- Main contribution 2: Systematic conversion and inverse-conversion protocols enabling benchmarking with off-the-shelf parsers and upper-bound estimation.
- Main contribution 3: Practical evaluation setup for UCCA/BSS and empirical results (in-domain, OOD) with feature analyses.
- Strength: Clear motivation and examples showing the necessity of supporting all three structural properties in grounded semantics.
- Strength: Solid engineering of BSP with dedicated transitions (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE, SWAP, NODE) and thoughtful feature design including remote-edge features.
- Strength: Comprehensive experimental design comparing direct parsing and conversion-based pipelines, with upper-bound quantification.
- Strength: Insightful findings — conversion + LSTM dependency achieves highest primary F1; BSP uniquely predicts remote edges but with low F1 (~13).
- Strength: Authors plan to release code and converters, supporting reproducibility.
- Weakness: BSP underperforms on primary edges compared to conversion + LSTM (59.0 vs 69.2 F1), with remote-edge F1 remaining low.
- Weakness: Inverse-conversion heuristics (e.g., high-attach) may bias evaluation and are not fully analyzed for different phenomena.
- Weakness: Limited qualitative/error analysis and lack of per-phenomenon breakdowns (coordination, MWEs, discontinuities, reentrancies).
- Weakness: Limited scope — experiments only on English UCCA foundational layer; unclear generalization to other schemes or multi-layer UCCA.
- Weakness: Methodological gaps — greedy inference only, no beam/global scoring, no significance testing, and missing hyperparameter sensitivity and efficiency metrics.
- Key question: Can BSP be evaluated with a neural local classifier (e.g., Stack-LSTM) to test if architecture is the main bottleneck?
- Key question: How sensitive are results to inverse-conversion heuristics and could learned reconstruction improve fairness?
- Key question: What is the distribution/length of discontinuities and how does SWAP usage correlate with errors; any SWAP ablation?
- Key question: Can the authors provide per-label F1 and phenomenon-based breakdowns to clarify where BSP helps?
- Key question: Was beam search explored for BSP and how does it trade off accuracy vs. speed?
- Additional comment: Complement labeled-yield evaluation with unlabeled scores and other graph metrics to separate structural vs. labeling errors.
- Additional comment: Place system results relative to inter-annotator agreement per phenomenon for better context.
- Additional comment: Release scripts for computing upper bounds and reproducing conversions to aid cross-representation studies.
- Overall recommendation: Lean toward acceptance — paper is well-motivated and advances the field, but needs stronger analysis and clarification of conversion/modeling impacts.
