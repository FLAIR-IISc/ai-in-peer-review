## Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of parsing broad-coverage semantic structures, specifically those represented by UCCA, which allows for multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features absent in many existing parsers. The authors identify this gap and propose two approaches: conversion to simpler formalisms for use with existing parsers, and the development of a novel transition-based parser (BSP) directly supporting these complex structures.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of BSP, a transition-based parser designed specifically for UCCA structures. While transition-based parsing is a well-established technique, adapting it to handle the complexities of UCCA, including the novel remote edge transitions, represents a significant step. The secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation of both conversion-based and direct parsing approaches on UCCA data, demonstrating the feasibility of parsing these structures and providing a baseline for future work. The authors also provide a useful analysis of feature importance for BSP.

The strengths of this paper are several. The problem addressed is important and well-motivated. The experimental setup is sound, with clear evaluation metrics and a comparison to existing parsing techniques (through conversion). The authors also acknowledge limitations and make their code publicly available, promoting reproducibility. The results, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score, are encouraging given the complexity of the task and the lack of prior work in this area. The inclusion of out-of-domain experiments demonstrates some robustness.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The feature set, while comprehensive, relies heavily on existing features from other parsing tasks and could benefit from more UCCA-specific features beyond the remote edge indicators. The ablation study, while present, doesn't provide a deep dive into the impact of each feature. The comparison to the LSTM parser, while showing its superior performance, isn't fully integrated into the analysis – the authors don't explore incorporating LSTM features into BSP. The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (UCCA) and a single language (English).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the design choices behind the ratio feature and provide a more detailed analysis of its impact on performance?
2.  Given the strong performance of the LSTM parser, have you considered incorporating LSTM layers or features into the BSP architecture?
3.  What are the primary challenges in extending this work to other languages with UCCA annotations?



Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. Despite some limitations, the proposed approach is promising, and the results provide a strong foundation for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions above.
