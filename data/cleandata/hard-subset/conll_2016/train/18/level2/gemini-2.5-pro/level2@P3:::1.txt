**Summary & Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations characterized by multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents. It introduces the first parsing systems for the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme, a formalism that exhibits all three of these properties. The work makes the following primary contributions:

1.  **Introduction and Formulation of the UCCA Parsing Task:** The paper is the first to formalize and tackle the problem of parsing UCCA structures. By doing so, it opens up a new and challenging task for the semantic parsing community, providing a framework for parsing a rich, cross-linguistically motivated representation.

2.  **A Systematic, Dual-Strategy Investigation:** The work presents a comprehensive exploration of two distinct approaches. The first is a conversion-based method that leverages existing, state-of-the-art parsers by converting UCCA graphs into more restrictive formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees). The second is a direct method involving a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed specifically to generate the full set of BSS properties, including remote edges for multiple parentage.

3.  **Strong Empirical Baselines and Analysis:** The paper establishes a solid set of experimental results on the English UCCA corpus. It demonstrates that an indirect approach using an LSTM-based dependency parser can achieve a high F-score on the primary tree structure of UCCA. Concurrently, it shows the feasibility of the direct parsing approach, which, while not yet matching the performance on primary edges, is uniquely capable of predicting the full graph structure.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The work addresses a clear and important gap in semantic parsing. To date, no parser has existed for UCCA, a promising broad-coverage semantic scheme. By providing the first parsers and a thorough empirical study, this paper lays the essential groundwork for future research in this area.

2.  **Methodologically Sound and Thorough:** The decision to explore both conversion-based and direct parsing methods is a significant strength. This dual approach provides a multifaceted view of the problem. The conversion-based track effectively assesses how well existing technology can be adapted, while the direct track proposes a principled, native solution. The inclusion of upper-bound calculations for the conversion methods is particularly valuable, as it clearly quantifies the inherent information loss and helps contextualize the parser performance.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem is clearly motivated, the technical details of the conversion algorithms and the novel transition system are explained with clarity (e.g., Algorithms 1 and 2, Figure 2), and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently. This high level of clarity makes the contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

4.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The plan to release the developed converters and parsers is a commendable aspect of the work. This will lower the barrier to entry for other researchers and is likely to stimulate follow-up work on UCCA parsing and related BSS tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Performance Gap of the Direct Approach:** The novel BSP parser, a central contribution, currently underperforms the best conversion-based method (the stack-LSTM parser) by a substantial margin on primary edges (59.0% vs. 69.2% F-score). The paper correctly identifies the potential of incorporating an LSTM-based classifier as a path to improvement. However, in its current state, the performance gap raises questions about the immediate efficacy of the direct approach compared to adapting existing SOTA tools. A more detailed analysis of the sources of error for BSP (e.g., search errors vs. feature limitations) could strengthen the paper by providing more concrete insights beyond the choice of classifier.

2.  **Limited Analysis of Remote Edge Prediction:** While the BSP parser's ability to predict remote edges is its key advantage, the analysis of this capability could be deepened. The reported F-score of 13.3% is modest, and a qualitative error analysis would be highly beneficial. Understanding the types of multi-parent phenomena that the parser succeeds or fails on would provide a clearer roadmap for future improvements in modeling these complex graph structures.

3.  **Heuristic Nature of Inverse Conversion:** The procedure for converting dependency trees back to UCCA-style structures (Algorithm 2) is based on a simple heuristic for reconstructing non-terminal nodes. The upper bound of 88.4% F-score indicates that this step introduces significant information loss. While a perfect conversion is not expected, the paper could be improved by discussing the limitations of this heuristic more explicitly. For example, are there specific constructions where the high-attachment assumption consistently fails? A brief discussion of potential avenues for a more sophisticated reconstruction process would be a welcome addition.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance difference between the MaltParser (arc-standard, 60.1% F1) and the stack-LSTM parser (also arc-standard, 69.2% F1) is very large. The paper attributes this to the classifier. Is it certain that the LSTM-based classifier is the sole reason for this 9.1% F1 improvement, or could there be other contributing factors, such as differences in feature representations (e.g., pre-trained word embeddings) or implementation details between the two systems?

2.  The BSP parser uses a dynamic oracle during training. Given that inference is greedy, were any experiments conducted with a static oracle? It would be interesting to know if the dynamic oracle was critical for achieving the reported performance, especially for learning to correctly apply the SWAP and remote-edge transitions, which add considerable complexity to the search space.
