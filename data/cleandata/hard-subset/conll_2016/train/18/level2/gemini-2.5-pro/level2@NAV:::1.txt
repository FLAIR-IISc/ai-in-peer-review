**Summary of the paper**
This paper addresses the challenge of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which incorporate multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. Focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme, the authors propose and evaluate two distinct transition-based strategies. The first is an indirect, conversion-based method that maps UCCA graphs to simpler constituency or dependency trees, enabling the use of existing state-of-the-art parsers. The second is a direct approach, for which the authors develop the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), a novel transition-based system designed to natively handle all structural properties of BSS. The work establishes the first set of empirical results and strong baselines for the task of UCCA parsing.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The paper's primary contribution is the design and implementation of BSP, the first direct transition-based parser for UCCA. This system provides a unified framework for handling the scheme's complex graph-structured, non-terminal, and discontinuous properties.
2.  A second key contribution is the establishment of a comprehensive benchmark for UCCA parsing. By systematically comparing the direct parsing method against several strong, conversion-based baselines using state-of-the-art parsers, the authors provide a valuable point of reference for future work.
3.  The clear formalization of the conversion algorithms between UCCA and standard tree-based representations is a useful contribution that facilitates comparison across different semantic formalisms.

**Strengths**
1.  This work tackles a novel and important problem, presenting the first dedicated parsing solution for the UCCA semantic scheme. The methodology is well-conceived, and the paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
2.  The experimental evaluation is thorough and rigorous. Comparing multiple systems across both in-domain and out-of-domain datasets provides a robust and reliable assessment of the proposed methods.
3.  The analysis of the results is insightful. The authors correctly identify that while conversion-based methods lose structural information (e.g., remote edges), the representational power of modern classifiers like LSTMs can yield superior performance on the simplified task, which points to a clear path for future improvements.
4.  The authors' commitment to making their code and converters publicly available is a significant strength that will encourage reproducibility and foster further research in this new area.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The primary weakness is the significant performance gap between the proposed direct parser (BSP) and the best-performing conversion-based baseline. BSP's F-score on primary edges is over 10 points lower than the stack-LSTM dependency parser (59.0 vs. 69.2). This disparity reduces the practical appeal of the direct approach in its current form.
2.  While the ability to predict remote edges is a key motivation for the BSP model, its performance on this sub-task is very low (around 13 F1) and is not accompanied by a detailed error analysis. It is unclear whether this is due to data sparsity, feature inadequacy, or fundamental task difficulty.
3.  The comparison between BSP, which uses a linear perceptron classifier, and the baseline stack-LSTM parser is not entirely equitable. The choice of classifier is a major confounding variable, and the observed performance difference may be more attributable to the power of the classifier than to the merits of the direct vs. indirect parsing strategy.
