### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents the first parsing results for the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme, a broad-coverage semantic representation. The authors identify three key structural properties of such representations—multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents—which are not jointly handled by existing parsers. They propose and evaluate two distinct transition-based approaches. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA structures are lossily converted into dependency or discontinuous constituency trees, allowing the use of existing state-of-the-art parsers. The second is a novel direct parser, Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), which uses a new transition system designed to produce the full UCCA structures, including multiple parents and discontinuities. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a conversion-based approach using a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser achieves the highest F-score on primary edges (69.2%), while the direct BSP parser is the only method able to predict remote edges, which form the DAG structure.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **The first comprehensive study of parsing UCCA structures.** This work introduces a new and challenging parsing task to the community. By establishing multiple strong baselines and proposing a novel direct parser, the paper provides a solid foundation for future research on parsing this rich, cross-linguistically motivated semantic formalism.
2.  **A novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP) for complex semantic graphs.** The paper presents a principled parser design that elegantly combines and extends existing transition-based techniques to simultaneously handle non-terminal nodes, discontinuous spans, and multiple parents within a single framework.
3.  **A thorough comparative analysis of direct versus conversion-based parsing.** The paper provides a valuable comparison between directly parsing the complex UCCA graphs and parsing simplified, converted representations. This analysis, which includes upper-bound calculations for the lossy conversions, sheds light on the trade-offs involved and offers important insights, such as the potential impact of more powerful classifiers on this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Task:** The paper tackles an important and timely problem. As the field moves towards richer semantic representations, there is a clear need for parsers that can handle structures more complex than projective trees. The authors clearly define the structural challenges and systematically address them using a well-motivated formalism (UCCA), for which this paper provides the first parser.
2.  **Comprehensive and Well-Designed Experiments:** The evaluation is thorough and insightful. The authors compare their novel parser against several strong baselines derived from state-of-the-art systems for related tasks. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain test sets demonstrates the robustness of the methods. A particularly commendable aspect is the calculation of upper bounds for the conversion-based methods, which clearly quantifies the information loss inherent in the conversion and helps to contextualize the performance of the parsers.
3.  **Principled Design of the Direct Parser (BSP):** The BSP parser is a thoughtful synthesis of ideas from prior work on transition-based parsing for discontinuous constituency, dependency DAGs, and standard constituency parsing. The transition set is a logical extension of these works, and the feature engineering is well-motivated, including the introduction of a novel and apparently effective global `ratio` feature for regularizing the creation of non-terminal nodes.
4.  **Clear and Insightful Analysis of Results:** The discussion of the results is excellent. The authors correctly identify that the superior performance of the LSTM dependency parser (on a converted task) points towards the limitations of the linear classifier used in BSP, rather than a fundamental flaw in the direct transition-based approach. This provides a clear and promising direction for future work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Performance on Remote Edges:** While BSP is the only proposed model capable of predicting remote edges (which create the DAG structure), its performance on this aspect of the task is very low (13.3 F-score). This is a key challenge in parsing UCCA, and the current results suggest that the model struggles significantly to capture these re-entrant structures. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed error analysis of these predictions. For instance, are failures concentrated on specific linguistic phenomena or structural configurations? This would provide more insight into the remaining challenges for this task.
2.  **The Comparison Between BSP and the LSTM Parser is Indirect:** The paper's strongest result comes from an LSTM-based parser operating on a simplified, converted dependency representation. While the authors' hypothesis that a stronger classifier is the key reason for its success is plausible, the comparison to BSP is not entirely direct. The LSTM parser is solving what may be an inherently simpler task (parsing a dependency tree) than BSP, which must directly contend with the full complexity of the UCCA graphs. This makes it difficult to fully disentangle the effect of the classifier from the effect of the task representation. The argument would be more conclusive if, for example, BSP had also been evaluated on the converted dependency structures.
3.  **Clarity of the Inverse Dependency Conversion:** The procedure for converting dependency trees back to UCCA-style structures (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics, such as assuming all attachments are high-attaching and using a rule-based function `Label(u)` whose details are in supplementary material. Given that the dependency conversion is lossy (88.4 F upper bound) and that this inverse conversion is crucial for the dependency-based systems, a more detailed discussion of these heuristics and their impact on the final performance would be beneficial.

### Questions to Authors

-   The performance on remote edges is a clear area for improvement. Could you elaborate on any error analysis you have performed for these edges? Are there particular UCCA categories (e.g., Participants shared across different Scenes) or syntactic configurations that are especially difficult for BSP to predict?
-   The novel `ratio` feature appears to be quite effective. It serves as a global regularizer on the creation of non-terminal nodes. Have you considered whether this feature could be beneficial for other transition-based constituency parsers in general, including the UPARSE baseline used in your experiments?
-   For this initial work, you understandably simplify the problem by discarding inter-sentence relations and implicit nodes. Could you briefly comment on the main challenges you foresee in extending the transition-based framework to handle these phenomena, particularly parsing across sentence boundaries?
