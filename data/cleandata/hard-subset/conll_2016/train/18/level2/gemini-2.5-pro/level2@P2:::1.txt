### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the first methods for parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations that feature non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units, and multiple parents (DAGs). The authors use the UCCA annotation scheme as a concrete instantiation of BSS. Two distinct approaches are proposed and evaluated: (1) a conversion-based approach, where UCCA structures are lossily converted to dependency or discontinuous constituency trees, allowing the use of existing state-of-the-art parsers; and (2) a direct approach, for which the authors develop a novel transition-based parser (BSP) capable of generating all three properties of BSS directly. Experiments on UCCA-annotated corpora show that a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser, when applied to a converted representation, achieves the highest performance on the core tree structure (69.2% F-score on primary edges), while the proposed direct parser is the only one capable of recovering multi-parent remote edges, albeit with lower accuracy.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **Pioneering the task of UCCA parsing.** This is the first paper to tackle parsing for the UCCA formalism. It defines the problem, proposes evaluation metrics, establishes strong baselines, and provides a new parser for the task. This is a significant contribution that opens up a promising semantic representation for use in downstream applications.
2.  **A thorough comparative study of parsing strategies for complex semantic graphs.** The dual-strategy experimental design is highly effective. By comparing a bespoke direct parser against strong existing parsers on converted data, the authors provide valuable insights into the trade-offs involved. The finding that a conversion-based approach with a powerful classifier can outperform a more structurally faithful direct approach is an important result for the field.
3.  **The development of BSP, a transition system for direct BSS parsing.** The paper proposes a novel transition-based parser that unifies techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing to directly model the complex properties of UCCA.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear motivation and novelty.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for parsers that can handle the combination of properties found in BSS. By identifying a gap in existing parsing technology and targeting a well-defined and increasingly relevant semantic formalism (UCCA), the work addresses a novel and important problem.
2.  **Excellent experimental design and analysis.** The decision to pursue both conversion-based and direct parsing approaches is a major strength. It provides a multi-faceted view of the problem, yielding not only a set of strong baselines but also an analysis of the capabilities and limitations of existing technology. Using a state-of-the-art LSTM-based parser (Dyer et al., 2015) as one of the baselines makes the comparison particularly strong and relevant. The inclusion of upper bounds for the conversion procedures is also a very good practice that helps contextualize the results.
3.  **Solid and reproducible methodology.** The proposed conversion algorithms are simple and clearly described. The BSP transition system is a sensible and well-grounded combination of existing ideas from related parsing paradigms, extended to handle the specifics of UCCA. The authors' plan to release all converters and parsers will be a valuable resource for the community and will facilitate future work on this task.
4.  **Robustness and insightful conclusions.** The evaluation on both in-domain and out-of-domain data demonstrates the robustness of the proposed methods. The authors provide a clear-eyed analysis of their results, correctly identifying that the choice of classifier (LSTM vs. perceptron) is a major factor in performance and pointing towards a clear path for future improvement: integrating a more powerful classifier into their direct BSP parser.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The proposed direct parser (BSP) is significantly outperformed on its primary task by a baseline.** The central technical contribution of the paper is the BSP parser. However, on the core task of predicting the primary tree structure, it achieves an F-score (59.0%) that is over 10 points lower than the score of the LSTM dependency parser applied to a converted representation (69.2%). While BSP is the only method that can predict remote edges, its much weaker performance on the backbone structure makes the direct approach appear less viable than the conversion approach, at least with the current implementation. This weakens the central claim about the effectiveness of the proposed direct parser.
2.  **Limited analysis of remote edge prediction.** A key selling point of BSS and the BSP parser is the ability to handle multiple parents (remote edges). However, the performance on this subtask is very low (13.3% F-score), and the paper provides almost no analysis of these results. What types of multi-parent phenomena are being captured? What are the primary sources of error? Is the issue data sparsity, inadequate features, or limitations of the transition system? Given that this is a novel capability of the parser, a more in-depth error analysis would be crucial to understanding the current limitations and guiding future work.
3.  **The novelty of the BSP system itself is somewhat incremental.** The paper is transparent that BSP builds on existing work in discontinuous constituency parsing and DAG dependency parsing. The transition set is largely a combination of known transitions (SHIFT, REDUCE, NODE, SWAP) with the addition of specific `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX` actions. While combining these in a novel context is a valid contribution, the paper could do a better job of positioning the specific algorithmic novelty. As presented, it feels more like a careful engineering effort to piece together existing components for a new formalism rather than a fundamental advance in transition-based parsing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between the LSTM-based dependency parser and your perceptron-based BSP on primary edges is substantial. To what extent do you believe this gap is attributable to the classifier versus the feature set or the transition system itself? Have you considered integrating an LSTM-based classifier into the BSP parser, and what challenges would you anticipate in doing so?
2.  Could you provide more qualitative or quantitative analysis on the prediction of remote edges? For instance, does performance correlate with the distance of the remote dependency, or with the specific UCCA categories of the parent and child? Understanding the nature of the errors on this novel aspect of the task would be very helpful.
