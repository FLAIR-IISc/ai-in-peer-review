- First formalization and introduction of UCCA parsing, opening a new broad-coverage semantic parsing task.  
- Dual-strategy approach: conversion-based methods (to discontinuous constituency/dependency) and a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that can produce remote edges.  
- Strong empirical baselines: LSTM-based dependency parser attains high F on primary UCCA structure; BSP can predict full graphs though lags on primary edges.  
- High novelty and impact: provides the first parsers for UCCA and foundational work for future research.  
- Methodologically thorough: explores both adaptation of existing parsers and a native transition system, including upper-bound analyses for conversion loss.  
- Clear presentation: algorithms, transition system, and experiments are well explained and easy to follow.  
- Commitment to reproducibility: authors plan to release converters and parsers.  
- BSP performance gap: BSP scores much lower on primary edges than the best conversion-based method (59.0% vs. 69.2% F), with insufficient error analysis to pinpoint causes.  
- Limited analysis of remote-edge prediction: low F-score (13.3%) and no qualitative error analysis to show what multi-parent phenomena fail or succeed.  
- Heuristic inverse conversion: reconstruction of non-terminals is simplistic, causing information loss (upper bound 88.4%); paper should discuss where the heuristic fails and possible improvements.  
- Question: Is the stack-LSTM’s 9.1% F advantage over MaltParser solely due to the LSTM classifier, or do feature representations/implementation differences (e.g., embeddings) contribute?  
- Question: Was a static oracle tried for BSP, and how critical is the dynamic oracle given greedy inference and the complexity of SWAP and remote-edge transitions?
