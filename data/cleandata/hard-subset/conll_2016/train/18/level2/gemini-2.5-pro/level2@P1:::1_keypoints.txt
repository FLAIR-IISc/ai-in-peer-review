- First parsing results for UCCA, addressing multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminals, and discontinuities with two transition-based approaches.
- Conversion-based method: lossily convert UCCA to dependency/discontinuous constituency trees to use existing parsers; LSTM dependency parser on converted data achieves 69.2% F on primary edges.
- Direct parser (BSP): new transition system designed to produce full UCCA structures, and the only method able to predict remote edges forming the DAG.
- Main contributions: first comprehensive UCCA parsing study, novel direct transition-based BSP, and thorough direct vs conversion-based comparative analysis.
- Strength: tackles an important, timely problem by defining structural challenges and introducing the first UCCA parser.
- Strength: comprehensive, well-designed experiments including in- and out-of-domain tests and upper-bound calculations for conversion loss.
- Strength: principled BSP design combining prior transition-based techniques and introducing a novel global `ratio` feature for non-terminal creation.
- Strength: clear, insightful analysis that attributes conversion-based success to classifier power and points to improving BSP’s classifier.
- Weakness: BSP’s performance on remote edges is very low (13.3 F), indicating struggle with re-entrant/DAG structures and needing deeper error analysis.
- Weakness: comparison between BSP and LSTM parser is indirect because the LSTM solves a simpler, converted task, making it hard to isolate classifier vs representation effects.
- Weakness: inverse dependency-to-UCCA conversion uses heuristics (e.g., high attachment assumption, rule-based Label(u)) and is lossy (88.4 F upper bound), requiring more discussion of heuristics’ impact.
- Question: request for error analysis on remote edges—are specific UCCA categories or syntactic configurations particularly problematic for BSP?
- Question: could the `ratio` feature benefit other transition-based constituency parsers (e.g., UPARSE)?
- Question: what are the main challenges in extending the transition-based framework to handle inter-sentence relations and implicit nodes, especially across sentence boundaries?
