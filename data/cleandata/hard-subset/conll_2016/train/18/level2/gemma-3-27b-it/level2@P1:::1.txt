This paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors identify the need for parsers capable of handling multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features not commonly supported by existing semantic parsers. They propose two strategies: converting UCCA structures to simpler formalisms (dependency trees and constituency trees) and leveraging existing parsers, and constructing a novel transition-based parser (BSP) directly supporting BSS properties. The experimental results on UCCA corpora demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, reaching nearly 70% labeled F-score.

**Contributions:**

1. **Identification of BSS properties & Gap in Existing Parsers:** The paper clearly articulates the structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuity) required for broad semantic coverage and convincingly argues that existing parsers fall short in supporting all three simultaneously. This framing of the problem is a significant contribution.
2. **Novel Transition-Based BSS Parser (BSP):** The development of BSP, a transition-based parser specifically designed for BSS, is a core contribution. Extending existing transition-based techniques with new transitions and features to handle the complexities of UCCA is a valuable step towards practical BSS parsing.
3. **Empirical Evaluation on UCCA Data:** The thorough experimental evaluation on UCCA corpora, both in-domain and out-of-domain, provides valuable insights into the performance of the proposed methods and establishes a baseline for future research in this area. The inclusion of both conversion-based and direct parsing approaches enables a comparative analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper effectively motivates the need for BSS parsing by highlighting the limitations of existing approaches and clearly defining the desired properties. The explanation of UCCA and its relevance is well-presented.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The transition-based approach is well-justified given its success in related parsing tasks. The description of the transition system and feature set is thorough and understandable. The two-pronged approach (conversion vs. direct parsing) is a sensible strategy for tackling a new parsing challenge.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental setup is well-defined, with clear evaluation metrics and a reasonable split of the UCCA corpus. The inclusion of out-of-domain testing is a strength, demonstrating the robustness of the approach.
4. **Public Availability of Resources:**  The commitment to making the converters and parsers publicly available is a crucial aspect, fostering reproducibility and further research.
5. **Careful Analysis and Discussion:** The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the results, acknowledging the limitations and suggesting avenues for future improvement (e.g., LSTM-based classifiers).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the paper compares to existing parsers via the conversion-based approach, the direct comparison to state-of-the-art dependency DAG parsers (which could potentially handle multiple parents directly) is missing. This limits the ability to fully assess the advantages of the proposed BSP. The note that code for such parsers was unavailable is understandable but ideally acknowledged as a limitation.
2. **Complexity of Features:** The feature set, while comprehensive, appears rather complex and could benefit from feature selection or ablation studies to identify the most impactful features. The results presented in Table 3 address this partially, but a more systematic analysis would be beneficial.
3. **Evaluation Metric limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, while practical given the lack of standard measures, might not fully capture the nuances of BSS parsing. A more fine-grained evaluation considering different types of errors would provide a more complete picture.
4. **Remote Edge Prediction:** The performance on remote edges (13.3% F-score) is significantly lower than that on primary edges, suggesting that the parser struggles with capturing long-range dependencies. Further investigation into improving remote edge prediction is warranted.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are presented, a detailed error analysis (e.g., identifying common error patterns) would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the parser and guide future development efforts.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific reasons why existing dependency DAG parsers were unavailable for comparison?
2.  What are the computational costs associated with the BSP parser compared to the conversion-based approaches?
3.  Do you have any preliminary experiments exploring different classifier architectures (e.g., LSTMs) within the BSP framework?
4.  Could you provide examples of common errors made by the parser, particularly related to remote edge prediction?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The inclusion of figures illustrating the UCCA representation is helpful.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the potential applications of BSS parsing beyond the research context.
