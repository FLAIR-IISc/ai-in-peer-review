## Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of parsing broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS), specifically using the UCCA annotation scheme, which allows for multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors identify a gap in existing parsers and propose two strategies: conversion to simpler formalisms followed by parsing, and direct parsing with a novel transition-based parser (BSP). The primary contribution lies in the development and evaluation of BSP, the first parser designed to directly handle the complexities of BSS as defined by UCCA. A secondary contribution is the empirical analysis of conversion-based parsing as a baseline and diagnostic tool.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The problem addressed is important and well-motivated. The UCCA corpus provides a unique, valuable resource for studying these semantic structures. The experimental setup is solid, with clear evaluation metrics and a comparison to relevant baselines, including conversion-based methods and existing parsers applied after conversion. The reported results (around 70% labeled F-score) demonstrate the feasibility of transition-based parsing for BSS and show competitive performance with existing techniques. The inclusion of novel features, particularly the `ratio` feature, is a promising addition.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The ablation study (Table 3) lacks sufficient detail regarding statistical significance. While improvements are reported, it’s unclear if these are robust. The LSTM parser comparison, while showing better performance, isn’t integrated into the BSP framework, leaving the potential for further improvement unexplored. The description of the conversion procedures, while clear, feels somewhat cursory; a more detailed discussion of information loss during conversion would be beneficial. Finally, while the authors acknowledge limitations, a deeper analysis of error types and potential avenues for addressing them (e.g., handling long-range dependencies) would strengthen the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide statistical significance tests for the results presented in Table 3?
2.  What were the main error types observed during evaluation with BSP? Were there specific UCCA structures that consistently caused errors?
3.  Have you investigated incorporating an LSTM-based classifier within the BSP framework, instead of solely relying on a linear classifier?
4.  Could you elaborate on the specific information loss during the conversion to dependency trees and how this impacts the subsequent parsing performance?
