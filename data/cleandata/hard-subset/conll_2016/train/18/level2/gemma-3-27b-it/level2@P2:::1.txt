This paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), as exemplified by the UCCA annotation scheme. The key observation is that existing parsers struggle with the structural properties required for BSS – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – and that UCCA is currently the only annotation scheme exhibiting all of these. The authors propose two strategies: converting UCCA to simpler formalisms (dependency trees and constituency trees) and leveraging existing parsers, and constructing a novel transition-based parser (BSP) specifically designed for BSS.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction and initial evaluation of a transition-based parser (BSP) capable of handling the complexities of BSS, specifically addressing the challenges of multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units directly within the parsing process. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating the feasibility of conversion-based parsing, showcasing that existing technology can be adapted, albeit with some loss of information, to tackle this task. Finally, the provision of a UCCA corpus for experimentation represents a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper tackles a critical gap in semantic parsing – the lack of parsers capable of representing complex semantic structures as found in natural language. Existing parsers are often limited by tree-based constraints, hindering their ability to capture phenomena like shared arguments and discontinuous constituents.  The focus on BSS is well-motivated and fills a significant need.
2. **Novelty of BSP:** The proposed BSP parser represents a novel extension of transition-based parsing techniques to accommodate the specific requirements of BSS. The introduction of new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features demonstrates a thoughtful approach to adapting established methods. The consideration of a `ratio` feature, acting as a regularizer for node creation, is a particularly interesting idea.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments. The comparison against conversion-based methods (using both UPARSE and MaltParser) provides a valuable baseline. The detailed ablation study helps to identify the most important features for BSP's performance. The inclusion of upper bounds on conversion-based methods provides a useful benchmark.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methods. Figure 1 effectively illustrates the structural properties of BSS, and the diagrams of the transition set and feature templates are helpful. The detailed explanation of the conversion algorithms is also appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains with BSP:** While BSP is a novel approach, its performance (around 62.4% F-score on primary edges) is not dramatically superior to the conversion-based methods, especially considering the increased complexity. The LSTM parser, leveraging a different architecture, achieves a significantly higher F-score (73.2%), suggesting that BSP may not be optimally exploiting the available data. The impact of adding remote edges is relatively small, raising questions about the effectiveness of the added transitions.
2. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set, while extensive, relies heavily on features borrowed from existing dependency and constituency parsing models. It's unclear if these features are truly optimal for BSS parsing. The feature ablation study doesn't offer significant insight into which features are *most* crucial, and the rationale behind some feature choices (e.g., the head word features given that UCCA doesn't explicitly represent heads) could be better explained.
3. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, based on edge matching and yield, is somewhat simplistic and doesn't fully capture the nuances of semantic parsing accuracy. A more fine-grained evaluation that considers the quality of the semantic relationships and the coherence of the resulting graph would be beneficial. 
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors BSP makes (e.g., incorrect attachment of remote edges, misidentification of non-terminal nodes) would be invaluable for guiding future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the strong performance of the LSTM parser using the converted representations, have you considered exploring an LSTM-based architecture for BSP itself? Would replacing the linear classifier with an LSTM layer be a viable direction?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis of BSP, identifying the most common types of errors it makes? Are there specific linguistic phenomena that BSP struggles with particularly?
3.  The ratio feature is an interesting idea. Can you elaborate on the intuition behind it and provide more empirical evidence of its effectiveness? How sensitive is the performance to the specific value used for the ratio threshold?
4.  The head word features are borrowed from dependency parsing. Given that UCCA does not explicitly represent head words, how crucial are these features to BSP's performance, and could they be replaced with more UCCA-specific features?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors mention the possibility of extending the parser to handle implicit units. This is an interesting direction for future work and could further enhance the parser's broad coverage.
*   The provided code and data will be crucial for the reproducibility and further development of this work. Ensure that the code is well-documented and easily accessible.
*   Consider comparing the results to a simple baseline, such as a random parser, to provide a more complete picture of BSP's performance.
