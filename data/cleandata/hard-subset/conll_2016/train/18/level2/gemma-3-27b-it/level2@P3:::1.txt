The paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The key contribution is the exploration of techniques for parsing structures demanding multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, characteristics lacking in existing parsers. The work investigates two strategies: converting UCCA structures into more readily parsable formalisms (dependency trees and constituency trees) and constructing a dedicated transition-based parser (BSP) directly supporting BSS properties. The paper demonstrates the feasibility of transition-based methods for this task, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score on the English UCCA corpus.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a significant gap:** The work tackles a crucial problem in semantic parsing – the limitations of current representations and parsers in capturing complex semantic phenomena. Existing parsers often struggle with structures requiring multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, or discontinuities, and this paper directly addresses those limitations with BSS and UCCA. This identification of the problem and its framing as broad-coverage semantic parsing is a substantial contribution.
2. **Comprehensive exploration of approaches:** The investigation of both conversion-based and direct parsing methods is a strength. Exploring conversion allows leveraging existing parsing technology, while the development of BSP demonstrates a commitment to directly addressing the challenges of BSS.  The comparison of these approaches provides valuable insights into the trade-offs involved.
3. **Solid experimental evaluation:** The experimental setup on the UCCA corpus is well-defined, with clear evaluation metrics. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments adds robustness to the findings.  The ablation study provides insights into the importance of different feature sets.  Providing code publicly is also a very positive aspect.
4. **Clear motivation and background:** The paper provides a thorough and accessible introduction to the challenges of broad-coverage semantic parsing, the UCCA annotation scheme, and related work. This background is essential for understanding the contributions of the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the transition system:** The core of BSP builds heavily on existing transition-based parsing techniques. While extending these methods to handle BSS properties is valuable, the novelty of the transition system itself is incremental.  More details regarding the rationale behind the choice of specific transitions (e.g., LEFTREMOTEX/RIGHTREMOTEX) and their interactions would strengthen the contribution.
2. **Performance gap compared to state-of-the-art:** While 69.2% F-score is a reasonable starting point, it falls short of the performance achievable with more sophisticated models like the LSTM parser reported in the evaluation (73.2% primary edge F-score). The paper acknowledges this but doesn't fully explore avenues to bridge the gap within the BSP framework.
3. **Evaluation metric limitations:** The evaluation metric used, based on edge matching, might not fully capture the semantic correctness of the parsed structures. A more nuanced evaluation, potentially involving downstream application performance, could provide a more comprehensive assessment.
4. **Insufficient discussion of error analysis:** While the paper reports results, a detailed error analysis identifying the types of structures that BSP struggles with would be highly beneficial. This would help guide future research efforts to improve the parser's performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the design choices behind the LEFTREMOTEX and RIGHTREMOTEX transitions? What specific linguistic phenomena motivated their inclusion, and how do they differ in their behavior from other transitions?
2. The paper mentions that removing remote edge transitions only slightly affects primary edge F-score. Does this suggest that correctly identifying remote edges is particularly challenging, or that they contribute relatively little to overall semantic accuracy?
3. Could you provide examples of parsing errors made by BSP, and discuss the potential reasons for those errors?
4. Considering the strong performance of the LSTM parser, have you explored incorporating LSTM-based features into the BSP framework?  If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The discussion of related work could be expanded to include recent advances in graph-based semantic parsing, which might offer alternative approaches to handling multiple parents and discontinuities.
*  The feature ablation study could be made more comprehensive by systematically varying combinations of feature sets.
* The abstract mentions the code will be released upon publication. Please ensure this is done promptly to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
