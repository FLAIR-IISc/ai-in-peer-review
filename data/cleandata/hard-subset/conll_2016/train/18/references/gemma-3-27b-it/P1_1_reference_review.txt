## Peer Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

**Summary:**

This paper presents a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors argue that existing parsers fail to support the key properties of BSS – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. They explore both conversion-based parsing (converting UCCA to dependency/constituency trees) and a novel, custom-built transition-based parser (BSP) designed to handle BSS directly. They achieve promising results on the English UCCA corpus, reaching nearly 70% labeled F-score with BSP.

**Main Contributions (as claimed by authors, in order of strength):**

1.  **First BSS Parser:** The development of BSP, the first reported parser explicitly designed to parse BSS, supporting all three key properties.
2.  **Exploration of Conversion-Based Parsing:** An investigation into the feasibility of leveraging existing parsing technology via conversion to more restrictive formalisms (dependency/constituency trees).
3.  **UCCA Parsing Experiments:**  Demonstration of the applicability of transition-based parsing to the UCCA annotation scheme with a concrete implementation and evaluation.

**My Assessment of Contributions (in order of strength):**

1.  **Novel Transition-Based Parser for BSS (BSP):**  The primary contribution is indeed the BSP parser. Directly addressing the challenges of BSS with a tailored model is a significant step forward.
2.  **Empirical Evaluation on UCCA:** The experimental results on the UCCA dataset provide a crucial benchmark and demonstrate the feasibility of the proposed approach. This is valuable for the community.
3.  **Insightful Exploration of Conversion-Based Approaches:**  The analysis of conversion-based parsing provides valuable insights into the limitations and trade-offs when attempting to utilize existing tools for a more expressive formalism.



**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses an Important Gap:** The paper tackles a significant gap in the field of semantic parsing – the lack of parsers capable of handling the complexities of truly broad-coverage semantic representations like UCCA.
2.  **Sound Technical Approach:**  The adoption of a transition-based approach is well-motivated, given its success in related parsing tasks. The extensions to handle multiple parents and discontinuities appear reasonable and well-explained.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, including both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments, and compare their approach with several baselines (conversion-based parsers).
4.  **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. Figure 1 effectively illustrates the core challenges addressed by BSS.
5. **Public Availability of Resources:** Promising to make the converters and parsers publicly available is a strong positive, fostering reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Performance Gains over Conversion-Based Methods:** While BSP achieves a respectable 62.4% F-score, the gains over the conversion-based methods (especially those using more sophisticated classifiers like LSTM) are not dramatic. This raises questions about the effectiveness of the specific design choices within BSP and suggests further optimization is needed. The gains in predicting remote edges are more noteworthy.
2.  **Feature Engineering Dependency:** The parser relies heavily on hand-crafted features. While this is common in transition-based parsing, it limits the potential for generalization and scalability. Exploring learned feature representations (e.g., using neural networks) could be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis to pinpoint the specific types of structures where BSP struggles. Understanding the types of errors made would be valuable for identifying areas for improvement.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, while reasonable given the lack of established alternatives, feels somewhat coarse-grained and may not fully capture the quality of the parsed structures. It prioritizes exact edge matching, which may penalize near-misses.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Feature Ablation Analysis:** You present a feature ablation study in the paper.  Can you discuss which features were *most* impactful in improving performance, and any surprising findings from the ablation?  Were there any features that appeared redundant or detrimental?
2.  **LSTM Integration:** You mention the strong performance of the LSTM-based dependency parser after conversion. Have you explored integrating LSTM layers directly into the BSP framework (e.g., for state representation or transition selection)?  What challenges did you encounter?
3.  **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a qualitative error analysis, highlighting specific examples where BSP fails and the underlying reasons for those failures? Are there specific UCCA structures or edge labels that are particularly challenging for the parser?
4. **Scalability:** How does the computational cost of BSP scale with sentence length, and what are the limitations in terms of processing very long sentences or documents?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of related work on parsing other non-projective dependency formalisms (beyond AMR).
*   The choice of MINUPDATE=5 seems somewhat arbitrary. Was this value optimized, or chosen based on prior work?
*   The supplementary material referenced regarding the Label function should be provided with the submission.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. While the performance gains over conversion-based methods are not overwhelming, the development of BSP represents a significant step toward parsing truly broad-coverage semantic structures. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides a strong foundation for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
