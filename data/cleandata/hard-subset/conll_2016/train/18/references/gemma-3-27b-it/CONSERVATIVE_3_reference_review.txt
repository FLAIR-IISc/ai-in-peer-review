This paper proposes a transition-based parsing approach for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The core contribution is a novel parser, BSP, designed to handle the complexities of BSS – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units.  A secondary contribution lies in exploring conversion-based parsing, utilizing existing constituency and dependency parsers after transforming UCCA structures into more conventional formats. Finally, the authors provide a public release of their code and datasets, which is a positive step for reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Challenging Problem:** The paper tackles a genuinely difficult parsing problem. UCCA's requirements for broad coverage – allowing multiple parents and non-terminal nodes – push beyond the capabilities of standard dependency and constituency parsing.  The motivation for a parser supporting these features is well articulated.
2. **Sound Methodological Foundation:** The use of a transition-based framework is sensible, given its success in related parsing tasks. The authors appropriately leverage existing work in discontinuous parsing and adapt it for the BSS context.  The detailed description of the transition set and feature engineering demonstrates a clear understanding of the underlying principles.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental setup, including both in-domain and out-of-domain testing, is rigorous. Reporting results with both conversion-based methods *and* the proposed BSP allows for a clear comparison and demonstrates the value of the dedicated BSS parser. The inclusion of feature ablation studies further strengthens the analysis.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:**  The commitment to releasing code and datasets is a significant contribution to the field, enabling further research and development in BSS parsing. This is particularly valuable given the relative novelty of the task.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement, Not a Breakthrough:** While the results are respectable, achieving around 70% labeled F-score doesn’t represent a dramatic leap forward.  The improvements over the conversion-based methods, while statistically significant, are not substantial enough to clearly demonstrate the superiority of the bespoke BSS parser. The LSTM results from converting to dependency format are particularly strong and suggest further exploration of more sophisticated classifiers within the BSP framework could yield larger gains. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t fully address the implications.
2. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** The core transition system and feature set are largely adaptations of existing techniques. The primary novelty lies in the *application* of these techniques to the UCCA framework rather than in fundamentally new parsing algorithms. The contribution feels more like an engineering effort to adapt established methods than a groundbreaking innovation.
3. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metrics, based on edge matching, are somewhat limited. They don't fully capture the quality of the overall BSS structure, and the dependence on exact edge label matches is potentially overly strict. A more holistic evaluation metric might provide a more nuanced assessment of parsing accuracy. While the authors acknowledge that UCCA inter-annotator agreement serves as an upper bound, the evaluation seems to be missing another benchmark, for example, a human-level baseline.
4. **Remote Edge Performance:** The performance on remote edges remains relatively low (around 13-20% F1). While the authors do explore ablation, the limited performance warrants further investigation. Is this inherent to the UCCA scheme, or can improved feature engineering or transition design address this limitation?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the strong performance of the LSTM dependency parser after conversion, what steps are being taken to integrate a similar classifier (e.g., LSTM) into the BSP framework?  What challenges are anticipated in doing so?
2.  The evaluation metrics focus on edge matching.  Could you elaborate on potential alternative evaluation metrics that might better capture the overall quality of the parsed BSS structure?
3.  What is the primary source of errors identified in analyzing the BSP output? Specifically, are there consistent patterns in mispredicted transitions, and if so, what features might be added to address them?
4. The ablation study shows that ratio features improve recall, but decrease precision. Can you explain this trade-off?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is thorough, but could benefit from more critical engagement with the limitations of existing approaches.
*   The supplementary material should include a detailed list of all features used in the parser.
*   Consider including a more qualitative error analysis with example parses illustrating common failure cases.
