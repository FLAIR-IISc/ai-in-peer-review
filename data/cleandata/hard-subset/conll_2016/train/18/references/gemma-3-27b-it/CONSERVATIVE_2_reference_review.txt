This paper proposes a transition-based parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The core contribution is the first attempt at directly parsing UCCA graphs using a transition-based approach. A secondary contribution lies in exploring conversion-based parsing, utilizing existing constituency and dependency parsers as a baseline and diagnostic tool. Finally, the authors provide a publicly available implementation, which could be valuable to the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Clear Gap:** The paper tackles a genuinely under-explored problem – parsing UCCA, a semantic representation with desirable properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) but lacking a dedicated parser. This addresses a practical need within the UCCA community.
2. **Sound Methodology (for an initial attempt):** The adoption of a transition-based approach is reasonable, given its success in related parsing tasks. The authors acknowledge the limitations of relying on existing parsers through conversion and correctly identify the information loss inherent in that process. The experimentation with feature sets is also a positive aspect.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their parser on both in-domain and out-of-domain data, providing a more robust assessment of its generalization capability. Reporting precision, recall, and F-score for both primary and remote edges provides a nuanced view of performance.
4. **Public Availability:** The commitment to releasing the code is essential for reproducibility and further research. This allows the community to build upon their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Parsing Method:** While applying transition-based parsing to UCCA is novel *in this context*, the core parsing methodology itself lacks significant innovation. The transition set is largely borrowed from existing work, and the feature set, while comprehensive, doesn't demonstrate a particularly insightful adaptation to the challenges of UCCA. It feels like applying established techniques rather than developing truly novel parsing mechanisms.
2. **Performance is Not Competitive:** While reaching ~70% labeled F-score is a starting point, it doesn’t demonstrate a substantial leap forward compared to the conversion-based methods, particularly when considering the complexity of developing a dedicated parser. The LSTM baseline demonstrates significantly better performance suggesting that improvements are available using existing, albeit more complex, approaches. A more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art semantic parsers (even if they don't directly handle UCCA's structural properties) is needed to justify the effort invested in a dedicated parser.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *why* the parser fails on certain structures (e.g., specific types of discontinuities or complex coordination) would be crucial for guiding future improvements. Simply reporting aggregate scores is not sufficient. What types of remote edges are particularly problematic? What errors are introduced by the conversion processes?
4. **Lack of Ablation Study Depth:** The ablation study is somewhat superficial. While the authors demonstrate the impact of certain feature sets, a more systematic ablation, exploring interactions between features and the contribution of individual transitions, would be more informative.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The LSTM parser consistently outperforms BSP. Can you elaborate on potential reasons for this discrepancy? What specific aspects of the LSTM architecture contribute to its superior performance?
2. Provide a detailed error analysis. What types of UCCA structures does BSP struggle with the most? Provide concrete examples from the test set.
3. What is the computational complexity of BSP compared to the conversion-based approaches? Is the overhead of direct parsing justified by the potential gains in accuracy?
4. The ratio feature seems unusual. What motivated its inclusion, and can you provide evidence (beyond the reported results) that it’s genuinely helpful?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper’s writing is clear and well-organized. However, the introduction could benefit from a more compelling motivation for directly parsing UCCA, emphasizing the limitations of conversion-based approaches more forcefully. The references are comprehensive. The choice of evaluation metrics, while sensible, should be clearly justified and compared to alternative evaluation schemes used in similar tasks.
