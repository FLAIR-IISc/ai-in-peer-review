## Review for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a transition-based parser (BSP) for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – properties the authors term “Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures” (BSS). The authors explore both direct parsing with BSP and a conversion-based approach leveraging existing constituency and dependency parsers. They achieve near 70% labeled F-score on in-domain data and demonstrate reasonable performance on out-of-domain data.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First Transition-Based Parser for UCCA:** This is the most significant contribution. The paper provides a concrete implementation and evaluation of a parser capable of handling the complexities of UCCA’s BSS, opening the door for further research in this area.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of Conversion-Based Approaches:**  The work provides a valuable baseline and error analysis for assessing the limitations of adapting existing parsing technology to UCCA, highlighting the information loss inherent in conversion to simpler formalisms.
3. **Introduction of UCCA-Specific Features:**  The addition of features tailored for remote edges and the ratio of terminals to nodes show a good understanding of the challenges of parsing UCCA and a thoughtful attempt to address them.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Importance of the Task:**  Parsing UCCA and, more generally, handling BSS is a crucial step towards more expressive and nuanced semantic representations. The lack of existing parsers for this scheme makes this work particularly valuable.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The use of a transition-based approach is well-motivated, given its success in related parsing tasks. The exploration of both direct parsing and conversion-based baselines is a strong methodological choice, providing context for the BSP results.
3. **Clear Presentation and Thorough Experiments:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of BSP. The experimental setup is reasonably thorough, including both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation and feature ablation studies.
4. **Strong Baseline Performance:** Achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score on a complex semantic parsing task is a respectable result, especially as a first attempt.  The comparison with conversion-based methods demonstrates the benefits of a specifically designed parser.
5. **Publicly Available Resources:** The authors’ commitment to releasing the code and models is a significant positive aspect, enabling reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Improvement over Conversion-Based Methods:** While BSP outperforms the direct conversion-based approaches, the margin of improvement isn't substantial. This raises questions about the effectiveness of the specific features and transitions introduced.  The LSTM parser performed remarkably well, and the potential benefits of applying similar techniques to BSP are acknowledged but not explored.
2. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, based on edge overlap, feels somewhat crude. It doesn't fully capture the structural differences between predicted and gold-standard graphs, particularly regarding the relationships between remote edges and the overall graph coherence. A more nuanced metric, perhaps incorporating graph edit distance, could provide a more informative evaluation.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of BSP’s predictions. Understanding *where* the parser struggles (e.g., with specific UCCA categories, long-distance dependencies, or coordination structures) would be crucial for guiding future development.
4. **Feature Engineering Focus:**  The focus on feature engineering feels somewhat traditional. Exploring end-to-end learning approaches (e.g., using pre-trained language models and graph neural networks) could potentially yield more substantial improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the potential for improvement with an LSTM-based classifier. Could you elaborate on the challenges of integrating such a classifier into the BSP framework?
2.  The evaluation metric seems to focus heavily on edge-level accuracy.  Do you have plans to explore more sophisticated graph-based evaluation metrics, such as those based on graph edit distance?
3. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the common errors made by BSP and the UCCA categories where it struggles the most?
4.  Given the performance of the LSTM parser with converted trees, do you believe the current feature set in BSP is a bottleneck preventing further performance gains, and if so, what types of features would you prioritize exploring next?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive, effectively situating this work within the broader context of semantic parsing.
*  The explanation of the UCCA scheme is clear and well-motivated.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. While the improvements over conversion-based methods are moderate, the development of the first transition-based parser for UCCA is a significant step forward. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and offers a strong foundation for future research. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
