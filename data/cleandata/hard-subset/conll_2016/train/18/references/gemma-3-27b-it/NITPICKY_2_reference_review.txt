This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – collectively termed Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS). The authors explore both conversion-based parsing (converting UCCA to dependency and constituency trees) and a novel, dedicated transition-based parser (BSP). They report results on the English UCCA corpus, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score.

The primary contribution of this work is the initial exploration of parsing techniques tailored for UCCA and, more broadly, BSS. While transition-based parsing is well-established, adapting it to handle the complexities of UCCA (discontinuity, non-terminals, multiple parents) is a non-trivial undertaking. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation of conversion-based methods, providing a baseline and highlighting the information loss inherent in simplifying UCCA to more traditional formalisms.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Gap:** The paper tackles a clear gap in semantic parsing – the lack of parsers for representations that fully capture complex semantic structures as defined by BSS. UCCA, with its rich representational power, has lacked a practical parser until this work.
2. **Thorough Exploration:** The authors comprehensively explore two distinct parsing strategies: conversion-based and direct parsing. This comparative approach provides valuable insights into the challenges and potential of each method. The conversion-based approach serves as a reasonable baseline and establishes the difficulty of the task.
3. **Well-Defined Evaluation:** The evaluation metrics, while novel, are clearly defined and appropriate for assessing the performance of BSS parsing. The use of both in-domain and out-of-domain datasets adds robustness to the evaluation.
4. **Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a thorough description of the transition system, features used in BSP, and training details. This level of detail is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvements, Not Breakthrough Results:** While achieving nearly 70% F-score is a solid starting point, it isn't a significant breakthrough. The performance is comparable to existing parsers when simplified to tree structures, suggesting that the current BSP implementation hasn't fully leveraged the benefits of directly modeling BSS. The LSTM comparison is promising, but only reported as a single number and lacks detail.
2. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The success of BSP appears heavily reliant on carefully engineered features, particularly those adapted from existing parsers. The novel "ratio" feature, while reportedly beneficial, lacks a clear theoretical justification. This makes the approach less generalizable and potentially brittle.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the parser fails – which structural properties are most challenging to predict – would be crucial for guiding future improvements.  Are errors concentrated in handling discontinuities, multiple parents, or non-terminal nodes?  Without this, it's difficult to assess the direction of future research.
4. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper presents results without statistical significance testing. Differences in F-score, especially between different configurations of BSP, should be supported by statistical analysis to demonstrate their reliability.
5. **Overly Detailed Presentation:** The paper, particularly in Section 3 (Conversion-Based Parsing), includes a level of detail in the conversion algorithms that seems unnecessarily verbose and detracts from the core contribution of the novel parser. The algorithmic descriptions feel more like code dumps than focused explanations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a detailed error analysis, categorizing errors by the type of structural property (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuities) that is incorrectly parsed?
2.  What is the computational cost of the additional transitions introduced in BSP compared to a standard transition-based dependency parser?
3.  The LSTM parser’s superior performance is mentioned but lacks detail. Can you provide more information about its implementation and features? Were any UCCA-specific features employed?
4.  Can you provide a justification for the “ratio” feature? What is the intuition behind its usefulness in capturing BSS structure?
5.  What experiments were conducted to determine the optimal value for the MINUPDATE parameter?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are very thorough. The formatting is generally good, though some equation numbering appears inconsistent. The figures are clear and helpful. The paper would benefit from streamlining the conversion algorithm descriptions and focusing more on the insights gained from the error analysis.
