This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), aiming to address the challenges of broad-coverage semantic parsing – specifically, supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors explore both conversion-based approaches (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP). The primary claimed contributions are (1) the first attempt at parsing UCCA structures, (2) the introduction of a novel transition-based parser capable of handling the complexities of broad-coverage semantic structures, and (3) empirical results on English UCCA data, demonstrating nearly 70% labeled F-score.

The most significant contribution, in my view, lies in the *formalization of the UCCA parsing problem* as a transition-based task. While the initial results are not groundbreaking, framing UCCA parsing within a well-established and actively researched paradigm is valuable. The detailed description of the transition system and features, adapted for UCCA's specific characteristics, provides a solid foundation for future work. The exploration of conversion-based approaches, while ultimately limited by information loss, serves as a useful baseline and diagnostic tool. The accessibility of the code is a positive aspect, enabling reproducibility and further research.

A key strength is the *thoroughness of the experimental setup*. The paper provides clear descriptions of the data, evaluation metrics, and training procedures. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments demonstrates a consideration for generalizability. The ablation studies on feature sets, while brief, offer some insight into the importance of different features.

The paper’s attention to detail regarding the formalisms (UCCA, dependency trees, constituency trees) and the conversion procedures is commendable. The clear explanation of the algorithms and the comparison of different approaches is a strong point.

However, the paper suffers from numerous weaknesses, many stemming from a focus on meticulousness over impactful results. First, the *novelty of the proposed approach is questionable*. The core transition system is largely an extension of existing work in discontinuous parsing, and the UCCA-specific features appear relatively minor. The authors acknowledge this, but fail to adequately justify why these modifications are sufficient to warrant a novel parser.

Second, the *evaluation is limited*. While the 70% F-score is respectable, it is not significantly higher than baseline approaches (as indicated by the conversion experiments) and doesn't definitively demonstrate the superiority of BSP over more complex models (e.g., those leveraging LSTM networks, which are shown to perform better with conversion based approach). The evaluation focuses solely on primary and remote edges, neglecting other potentially important aspects of parsing quality.

Third, the paper is *overly verbose and repetitive*. The background section, while comprehensive, is excessively detailed and could be significantly condensed. Similar concepts are repeatedly explained in different sections, leading to a lack of conciseness. The description of the conversion procedures is also unnecessarily lengthy and could be presented more succinctly.

Fourth, there is a *lack of error analysis*. The paper doesn’t delve into the types of errors made by the parser, hindering a deeper understanding of its limitations and potential areas for improvement. Specifically, what types of remote edges are most often missed? What kinds of non-terminal structures are challenging for BSP? Without this analysis, it is difficult to assess the practical significance of the results.

Finally, the minor formatting issues throughout the paper (inconsistent spacing, unusual capitalization, and occasional grammatical errors) detract from its overall professionalism. These errors, while individually minor, collectively create a negative impression.

1.  **What is the primary bottleneck preventing BSP from achieving higher F-scores? Is it the transition system, the feature set, the training data size, or some other factor?**
2.  **The paper mentions the ratio feature as being beneficial. Can you provide more detail on why this feature is effective and whether it exhibits any negative side effects?**
3.  **What specific types of errors are most frequent in the parser's output? Provide examples of incorrectly parsed sentences and explain the reasons for the errors.**
4.  **Regarding the LSTM parser performing well on converted dependency trees, have you considered incorporating LSTM layers directly into the BSP architecture?**

Additional Comments:
*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., some citations include years, others don’t).
*   Figure 1 could benefit from more descriptive labels.
*   The paper could benefit from a table summarizing the key differences between UCCA, AMR, and SDP.



I recommend **rejection** due to the limited novelty, insufficient evaluation, and excessive verbosity, despite the potentially valuable contribution of formalizing UCCA parsing as a transition-based task. While the work demonstrates a meticulous approach, it lacks the substantial impact necessary for publication in a competitive venue like CoNLL. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly conducting a thorough error analysis and exploring more advanced model architectures, could significantly improve the paper's quality and justify its acceptance in a future iteration.
