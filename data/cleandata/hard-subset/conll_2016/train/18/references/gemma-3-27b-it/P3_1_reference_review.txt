## Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

**Summary:** This paper presents a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors address the challenges of parsing representations requiring multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features lacking in existing parsers. They explore both conversion-based methods (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel, dedicated BSS parser (BSP), evaluating on English UCCA data in both in-domain and out-of-domain settings.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First dedicated parser for BSS:** The development and evaluation of BSP represents a significant step forward. While conversion-based methods are explored, the core contribution is a parser specifically designed to handle the complexities of BSS, demonstrating the feasibility of this approach.
2. **Comprehensive exploration of transition-based methods:** The paper systematically investigates transition-based parsing for BSS, building on existing techniques and introducing new transitions and features tailored to UCCA. This provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.
3. **Empirical evaluation with UCCA data:**  The evaluation on the UCCA dataset offers a valuable resource and benchmark for the broader semantic parsing community. The in-domain and out-of-domain experiments provide insights into the robustness of the proposed methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing semantic parsing approaches in capturing the full complexity of natural language semantics, motivating the need for BSS parsing. The explanation of UCCA and its properties is thorough and accessible.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The adoption of a transition-based approach is well-justified, leveraging the successes of this paradigm in syntactic parsing.  The exploration of both conversion-based methods and a dedicated parser provides a robust evaluation framework.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:**  The feature set presented is comprehensive and draws on established techniques from related parsing tasks. The inclusion of the “ratio” feature is a particularly insightful addition, demonstrating a thoughtful consideration of model regularization.
4. **Solid Empirical Results:**  The results demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing parsers when converted to the BSS format, and a respectable F-score for the dedicated BSP parser. The out-of-domain evaluation provides a crucial assessment of generalization ability.
5. **Code Availability:**  The commitment to publicly releasing the code is a significant benefit, facilitating reproducibility and future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Improvement over Conversion Methods:** While BSP achieves a reasonable F-score, the gap between its performance and that of the strong baseline (LSTM parser applied to converted dependency trees) is substantial. This raises questions about the effectiveness of the specific transitions and features implemented in BSP, suggesting that further refinement is needed.  The authors acknowledge this, but the degree of the gap is concerning.
2. **Evaluation Metric limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, based on matching edge yields, is a reasonable starting point, but may not fully capture the semantic correctness of the parsed structures. Specifically, it doesn’t heavily penalize for incorrect edge labels, which are crucial for semantic interpretation. A more nuanced evaluation that considers the semantic roles encoded by the edges would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Detailed Ablation Study:** While a feature ablation study is presented, it lacks depth.  It would be valuable to systematically investigate the impact of individual transitions (e.g., the remote edge transitions) and feature groups on performance, providing a clearer understanding of their relative contributions.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Semantic Parsers:** The comparison is largely limited to parsers converted to BSS format.  A direct comparison with state-of-the-art semantic parsers for other representations (e.g., AMR) – even if requiring some adaptation – would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Analysis of Errors:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by BSP? Specifically, what types of structures (e.g., coordination, discontinuities) are most challenging for the parser, and what are the common sources of errors (e.g., incorrect edge labels, incorrect attachment points)?
2. **Remote Edge Transitions:**  What is the impact of including the remote edge transitions on parsing speed and memory usage?  Are there strategies to optimize these transitions?
3. **Potential for LSTM integration:** You mention the promising results of the LSTM parser on the converted dependency trees.  Have you explored integrating LSTM layers into the BSP framework to potentially improve performance? If so, what were the results?
4. **Semantic Role Evaluation:** Do you have plans to explore more sophisticated evaluation metrics that explicitly assess the semantic correctness of the parsed structures, considering edge labels and semantic roles?




**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and concise.
* The related work section provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
* The figures are helpful in illustrating the concepts and examples.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing, providing the first dedicated parser for BSS and a comprehensive evaluation of transition-based methods in this context. While the performance gap between BSP and strong baselines is a concern, the novelty of the approach, the thoroughness of the experimentation, and the commitment to code availability justify acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. I recommend acceptance.
