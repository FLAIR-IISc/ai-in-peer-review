## Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation scheme supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – characteristics deemed crucial for broad-coverage semantic parsing. The authors explore both conversion-based parsing using existing constituency and dependency parsers and a novel, dedicated transition-based parser. They demonstrate competitive performance on English UCCA data, achieving around 70% labeled F-score, and highlight avenues for further improvement.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First dedicated parser for UCCA:** The primary contribution is the development of BSP, the first publicly available parser capable of directly handling the complexities of the UCCA representation, specifically its support for multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities.
2. **Empirical evaluation of conversion-based approaches:** The authors provide a valuable empirical investigation into the feasibility of repurposing existing parsing technologies (constituency and dependency) for UCCA parsing via conversion.  This highlights the information loss inherent in such approaches and establishes a baseline for comparison.
3. **A robust baseline for future UCCA parsing:** By releasing the code and presenting a well-defined experimental setup, the authors provide a solid baseline for future research in UCCA parsing and broad-coverage semantic representation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a crucial gap in tooling:**  UCCA, despite its theoretical advantages, lacked a dedicated parser. This work directly addresses this limitation, making UCCA more accessible for research and applications. The availability of the code is a significant positive.
2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation, comparing their novel parser with conversion-based approaches and performing experiments on both in-domain and out-of-domain data. This demonstrates the robustness of their approach.
3. **Well-motivated design choices:** The extension of existing transition-based parsing techniques is logically sound, building upon a successful paradigm in syntactic and semantic parsing.  The inclusion of novel features specifically tailored for UCCA (remote edge handling) demonstrates a thoughtful approach.
4. **Clear presentation of the UCCA scheme:**  The paper provides a concise and accessible introduction to UCCA, which is beneficial for readers unfamiliar with the representation.  The use of Figure 1 effectively illustrates the key structural properties.
5. **Analysis of feature importance:** The ablation study (Table 3) provides valuable insight into the contribution of different feature sets, guiding future model development.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited performance gains over conversion methods:** While BSP represents a significant step forward in having a dedicated parser, the performance improvement over carefully tuned conversion-based parsers (especially the LSTM dependency parser) isn’t substantial. This raises the question of whether the complexity of a dedicated parser is fully justified at the current stage.
2. **Lack of detailed error analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth error analysis. Identifying specific types of errors made by BSP (e.g., incorrectly predicting remote edges, difficulties with coordination structures) would provide valuable insights for improvement.
3. **Suboptimal hyperparameter tuning:** The use of default settings for many components (e.g., MaltParser) and a relatively limited hyperparameter search for BSP suggest potential for further performance gains.  Exploring a wider range of configurations, especially for the linear classifier, could yield significant improvements.
4. **Limited exploration of alternative transition systems:** The transition system, while reasonable, is based on existing models. Investigating alternative transition systems specifically designed for DAG structures and non-projectivity might be beneficial.  
5. **Evaluation Metric Limitations**: The proposed evaluation metric, while a reasonable starting point, isn't ideal. It treats all edge types equally and doesn't capture the importance of correctly identifying primary edges versus remote edges.  A more nuanced evaluation metric that reflects the UCCA hierarchy could be more informative.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the most common types of errors made by BSP? Specifically, where does BSP struggle compared to the best conversion-based method?
2.  You mention the ratio feature.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind this feature and why it proved to be effective? Did you explore other regularization techniques?
3.  Were any specific challenges encountered when adapting existing transition-based parsing techniques to handle the DAG structure of UCCA? How were these challenges addressed in the design of BSP?
4.  Given the limited performance gain over the LSTM-based dependency parser, what are the key advantages of having a dedicated UCCA parser (e.g., potential for more tailored features, better control over the parsing process)?
5.  Would you consider developing a more nuanced evaluation metric that distinguishes between the importance of primary and remote edges?  



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clear.
*   The related work section is comprehensive.
*   The inclusion of Supplementary Material is helpful.
*   It might be beneficial to compare the computational cost of BSP versus the conversion-based approaches.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a valuable contribution that addresses a significant gap in the tooling available for UCCA parsing. While the performance gains over conversion-based methods are not dramatic, the development of BSP provides a crucial foundation for future research in this area.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly through a more detailed error analysis and optimized hyperparameter tuning, could further strengthen this work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
