This paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing, tackling the challenge of broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS) as defined by the UCCA annotation scheme. The primary contribution is the first transition-based parser capable of handling the complexities of BSS – specifically, multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. A secondary contribution lies in exploring conversion-based parsing as a baseline and diagnostic tool, and a third, more modest, contribution is the presentation of a comprehensive experimental setup using the UCCA corpus.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Boldness:** The core idea of directly tackling BSS parsing with a transition-based method is genuinely novel. Existing semantic parsing work largely avoids the full complexity of BSS, or doesn’t ground the representation. This work courageously addresses these challenges head-on, even if the resulting performance isn’t state-of-the-art (yet). The approach is ambitious and pushes the boundaries of current semantic parsing techniques.
2. **Addressing a Key Gap:** UCCA, while a theoretically appealing semantic representation, lacked a practical parser. This work directly addresses that gap, providing a foundation for further research and applications leveraging UCCA. The lack of existing tools makes this a significant contribution to the field.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors have meticulously set up a comprehensive experimental framework, including conversion-based baselines and a clear evaluation methodology. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation, and ablation studies, demonstrate a thorough approach to evaluating the proposed parser.
4. **Insights from Conversion Methods:** The exploration of conversion-based parsing reveals valuable insights into the information loss associated with simplifying BSS into more traditional formalisms. This helps to contextualize the performance of the direct parsing approach and informs future representation design.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Performance Gaps:** While the reported 69.2% F-score is respectable, it is not dramatically superior to existing methods when considering only primary edges, and the performance on remote edges remains relatively low. This suggests the current model struggles to fully capture the complexities of BSS, particularly discontinuous units and multiple parentage.
2. **Limited Exploration of LSTM Integration:** The authors acknowledge that incorporating an LSTM-based classifier could potentially improve performance. This feels like a significant missed opportunity, as LSTM-based methods have demonstrably advanced performance in related parsing tasks. The lack of such an experiment weakens the claim of a fully explored approach.
3. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The parser's performance heavily relies on carefully engineered features. While comprehensive, this reliance on feature engineering feels somewhat dated in the era of end-to-end neural models. A more end-to-end approach, perhaps leveraging pre-trained language models, might yield more robust and generalizable results.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, while a reasonable starting point, lacks nuance. It treats all edges equally and doesn’t account for the hierarchical structure of the BSS. A more sophisticated evaluation metric that considers the overall structural accuracy of the parse is needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What were the primary challenges encountered when attempting to directly model discontinuous units and multiple parentage within the transition system? Can you provide specific examples where the parser consistently fails?
2. What steps would be required to integrate an LSTM-based classifier into the BSP framework? Do you anticipate significant engineering challenges, or are the primary hurdles related to feature representation?
3. How sensitive is the parser's performance to the MINUPDATE and IMPORTANCE parameters? Have you explored a wider range of parameter settings, and if so, what were the results?
4. Can you elaborate on the design choices behind the novel "ratio" feature? What hypothesis drove its inclusion, and how does it interact with other features in the model?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The figures are helpful in illustrating the concepts. The related work section is comprehensive. Minor grammatical issues are present but do not significantly detract from the overall readability. The code availability is also a definite plus.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the performance gaps and the reliance on feature engineering, I recommend **accepting** this paper. The work is genuinely novel, addresses a critical gap in the field, and presents a compelling approach to tackling a challenging problem. The boldness of the approach and the potential for future advancements outweigh the current limitations. The paper represents a significant step forward in broad-coverage semantic parsing and deserves to be published to stimulate further research in this area.
