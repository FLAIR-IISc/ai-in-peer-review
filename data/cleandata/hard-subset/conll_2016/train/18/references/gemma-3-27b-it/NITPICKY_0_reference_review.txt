The paper introduces a transition-based parser for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – collectively termed Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS). The authors present both conversion-based parsing approaches utilizing existing constituency and dependency parsers, and a novel BSS parser (BSP). They achieve around 70% labeled F-score on an English UCCA corpus.

The primary contribution is the initial exploration of parsing techniques tailored for BSS, specifically UCCA. While transition-based parsing has been successful in other semantic and syntactic tasks, adapting it to handle the complexities of UCCA (especially discontinuities and multiple parenthood) represents a worthwhile step. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive evaluation of conversion-based methods, demonstrating the information loss inherent in restricting BSS to simpler formalisms like dependency trees. Finally, the public release of the code, as promised, would be valuable for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Challenging Problem:** The paper tackles a genuinely difficult parsing problem – handling broad-coverage semantic structures. UCCA’s expressive power demands parsing approaches that move beyond traditional tree-based representations, and the authors appropriately focus on transition-based methods known for flexibility.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The paper presents a well-defined experimental setup, including both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation. The comparison with conversion-based baselines offers a useful benchmark and highlights the limitations of simplifying UCCA into more conventional structures. The ablation study also provides valuable insights into feature importance.
3. **Clear Presentation of Technical Details:** The transition system and feature set are described with sufficient detail, allowing for replication. The inclusion of algorithms for conversion between different representations is a helpful addition.
4. **Good Baseline Performance:** Achieving ~70% labeled F-score on a relatively complex parsing task is commendable, especially as a first attempt. The performance is comparable to existing parsers when considering only primary edges, indicating a solid foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvements, Not Breakthrough:** While the work is solid, the improvements over conversion-based methods are marginal. The best performing parser, the LSTM-based one (not implemented within BSP), significantly outperforms BSP, suggesting the primary contribution is demonstrating feasibility rather than state-of-the-art performance. The lack of a detailed error analysis hinders understanding where the parser struggles most.
2. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The performance heavily relies on carefully engineered features. While the feature set is comprehensive, it feels ad-hoc and lacks a strong theoretical justification. The reliance on head-word based features in a scheme that explicitly avoids head-selection seems particularly questionable. The addition of the 'ratio' feature feels similarly tacked on.
3. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single corpus (Wiki) and a single language (English). While out-of-domain results are provided, extending the evaluation to other languages and potentially different UCCA-annotated datasets would strengthen the findings. The lack of comparison to other semantic parsers beyond dependency and constituency parsers (e.g., AMR parsers) is a missed opportunity.
4. **Minor Technical Imprecision:** The description of the dependency conversion algorithm (Algorithm 1) is somewhat ambiguous and potentially inefficient. Specifically, the `h(u)` function could be optimized. Similarly, the conversion back from dependency trees (Algorithm 2) relies on a heuristic assumption (high-attaching) that may not always hold. This lack of rigor impacts confidence in the conversion baselines.
5. **Presentation & Redundancy:** The paper suffers from redundancy. The background section is excessively long and could be significantly condensed. The numerous references to existing work, while demonstrating awareness of the field, sometimes interrupt the flow of the core argument. The repeated emphasis on the three BSS properties feels unnecessarily verbose.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a detailed error analysis of BSP's predictions, identifying common error types and potential areas for improvement?
2.  What is the computational complexity of the proposed transitions, and how does it scale with sentence length?
3.  Given the superior performance of the LSTM-based parser, do you plan to integrate LSTM layers into the BSP architecture? If not, what are the limitations preventing this integration?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the MINUPDATE parameter, and have you explored other regularization techniques?
5.  The description of remote edge transitions seems to lack clarity. Can you elaborate on the reasoning behind the separate `LEFTREMOTEX` and `RIGHTREMOTEX` transitions, and provide examples of situations where each would be preferred?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of the algorithms (Algorithms 1 & 2) is suboptimal, making them difficult to read.
*   The figure captions could be more descriptive. Specifically, Figure 3 needs more explanation of what the feature templates actually represent.
*   Please clarify the definition of “grounded” in the context of semantic parsing. The usage in the paper is somewhat vague.



I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a competent and well-executed investigation, it lacks the significant novelty or performance improvements necessary for acceptance at a competitive conference like CoNLL. The incremental nature of the contribution, combined with the reliance on feature engineering and limited scope of evaluation, makes it fall short of the expected standards. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly a more rigorous analysis of error sources and exploration of more advanced architectures, would substantially strengthen the work.
