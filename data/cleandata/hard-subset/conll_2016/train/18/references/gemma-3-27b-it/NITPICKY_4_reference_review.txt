The paper presents a transition-based parser for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – collectively termed Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS). The authors explore both conversion-based approaches using existing parsers and a novel, dedicated BSS parser (BSP). The primary claimed contribution is the first parser capable of handling all three BSS criteria. A secondary contribution is the exploration of conversion techniques to leverage existing parsing technology. A tertiary claim is the demonstration of the approach on English UCCA data, achieving ~70% labeled F-score.

I view the primary contribution as the attempt to apply transition-based parsing to a genuinely complex semantic representation. While the reported performance isn’t groundbreaking, the very act of tackling UCCA’s complexities with this paradigm is noteworthy. The investigation of conversion strategies provides a useful baseline and insight into the challenges involved. However, the overall quality of the work is surprisingly low, riddled with methodological inconsistencies, superficial analysis, and questionable claims.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Challenging Problem:** The attempt to parse UCCA structures directly is valuable. UCCA's expressiveness, particularly its support for discontinuities and non-terminals, presents a significant hurdle for traditional parsing approaches.
2. **Exploration of Conversion Methods:** The initial investigation into converting UCCA to constituency and dependency trees offers a pragmatic way to evaluate the potential of existing technology. The observation that conversion inevitably leads to lossy representations highlights the need for a dedicated parser.
3. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing semantic parsing frameworks and motivates the need for BSS parsing. The description of UCCA's properties is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Superficial Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is alarmingly weak. The use of a single, relatively small dataset (Wiki) and a simple, greedy decoding strategy limits the generalizability of the findings. The out-of-domain experiment is merely a single run on another dataset without parameter tuning. There's no ablation study on the features *within* BSP, beyond the cursory mention in the conclusion.  The comparison to the LSTM parser is also insufficient; it’s not clear if the LSTM parser was fine-tuned for UCCA or used a pre-trained model.
2. **Methodological Flaws in Conversion:** The dependency conversion procedure (Algorithm 1) is overly simplistic and lacks justification. The assumption of "high-attaching" in the inverse conversion is arbitrary and likely introduces errors. A more thorough evaluation of the information loss during conversion is needed, going beyond the stated “error resulting from this back and forth conversion is discussed in Section 6” which is never done.
3. **Lack of Rigorous Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. What types of errors does BSP make? Are there specific UCCA constructions that are particularly challenging? The authors simply report F-scores without providing any insights into the underlying causes of errors. The claim that the ratio feature is a “regularizer” is unsubstantiated; it simply correlated with performance.
4. **Inconsistent Feature Usage and Reporting:** The feature description (Figure 3) is poorly organized and somewhat vague.  The novel "remote" features are briefly mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed. There is no justification for the choice of features or their specific engineering.
5. **Questionable Claims of Novelty:** While the authors claim this is the "first" parser for BSS, this claim feels overstated. The BSP parser largely builds upon existing transition-based parsing techniques. The novelty lies in the *application* of these techniques to UCCA, not in the techniques themselves. The authors do not adequately acknowledge or differentiate their work from prior work in discontinuous parsing or dependency DAG parsing.
6. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, uneven spacing) and grammatical errors, distracting from the content. The figures are low-resolution and difficult to interpret.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the justification for the "high-attaching" assumption in the dependency conversion (Algorithm 2)?  Have you experimented with alternative strategies?
2.  Provide a detailed error analysis of BSP, identifying the most frequent types of errors and the UCCA constructions that pose the greatest challenges.
3.  Please elaborate on the LSTM parser used for comparison. Was it pre-trained? Was it fine-tuned on the UCCA data? Provide details on its architecture and training procedure.
4.  What is the concrete effect of adding the ratio feature? What happens if it is removed? Provide statistical evidence to support the claim that it acts as a “regularizer”.
5.  How does the performance of BSP compare to a simpler baseline, such as a non-projective dependency parser trained directly on the UCCA data without any UCCA-specific features?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's references are poorly formatted. Some citations are incomplete. The use of abbreviations (e.g., "BSS") without prior definition is jarring. Section 6 is referenced in the text but contains no discussion of conversion errors.



**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the problem addressed is interesting, the methodological shortcomings, superficial evaluation, and lack of rigorous analysis outweigh the potential contributions. The paper feels rushed and lacks the polish expected of a publication. Significant revisions, including a more comprehensive experimental setup, a detailed error analysis, and a clearer articulation of the contributions, would be required for reconsideration.
