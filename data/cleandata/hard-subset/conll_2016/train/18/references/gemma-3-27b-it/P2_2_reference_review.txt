## Review for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – characteristics deemed essential for "broad-coverage" semantic parsing. The authors identify a gap in existing parsers, as UCCA lacks a dedicated parser despite its advantageous structural properties. They explore both conversion-based approaches (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel transition-based parser designed specifically for UCCA.  The paper demonstrates the feasibility of UCCA parsing, achieving around 70% labeled F-score on in-domain data and showing reasonable performance on out-of-domain data.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First dedicated parser for UCCA:** This is the primary contribution. The paper addresses a significant gap by creating a parser for a relatively unexplored, but potentially valuable, semantic annotation scheme.
2. **Exploration of transition-based parsing for broad-coverage semantic structures:**  The paper demonstrates that transition-based methods, already successful in syntactic parsing, can be effectively adapted to handle the complexities of UCCA, including discontinuities and multiple parents.
3. **Empirical evaluation and baseline establishment:** The paper provides a solid empirical evaluation of both conversion-based and direct parsing approaches, establishing a baseline for future research on UCCA parsing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a clear and important problem:** The lack of a parser for UCCA hinders its adoption and practical application. This work directly tackles that issue. The motivation for broad-coverage semantic structures is clearly articulated.
2. **Sound methodology:** The combination of conversion-based approaches and a novel, specifically designed parser is a sensible and thorough approach.  The use of transition-based parsing is well-justified given its recent successes in related areas.
3. **Solid experimental setup and results:** The evaluation is well-designed, using standard metrics and a reasonable split of the data.  The results are promising, demonstrating that UCCA parsing is feasible and that BSP achieves respectable performance. The inclusion of out-of-domain evaluation is a definite strength.
4. **Clear and well-written:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the UCCA scheme, the challenges of parsing it, and the proposed approach. The figures are helpful in illustrating the concepts.
5. **Code availability:** The commitment to making the code publicly available upon publication significantly increases the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited improvement over conversion-based baselines:** While BSP achieves a decent F-score, the improvement over the conversion-based approaches (especially the constituency-based conversion) is not substantial. This suggests that a significant amount of information is lost during the conversion process, and that BSP isn’t yet fully exploiting the richness of UCCA.  The authors acknowledge this, but it's a key area for improvement.
2. **Feature set limitations:** The feature set appears relatively standard and lacks exploration of more sophisticated UCCA-specific features. The addition of the "ratio" feature is noted, but more targeted feature engineering tailored to the nuances of UCCA could likely yield significant gains.
3. **Classifier choice:** The use of a linear classifier is a potential limitation. The authors note the potential for improvement with an LSTM, and this feels like a crucial next step, as the LSTM parser performed well in the conversion-based experiments.
4. **Evaluation metric limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, while reasonable, doesn’t fully capture the complexity of semantic parsing. A more nuanced evaluation that considers different types of errors or semantic relations could provide a more complete picture of the parser's performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Feature Engineering:** Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the chosen feature set? Were other UCCA-specific features considered and discarded? If so, why?
2. **LSTM Integration:** What are the specific challenges to integrating an LSTM-based classifier into the BSP framework? Do you anticipate significant engineering effort or are there fundamental reasons why an LSTM might not be suitable?
3. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of errors made by BSP? This would help guide future improvements.
4. **Conversion Loss:** What is your assessment of the information loss during the conversion to constituency trees? Does this loss primarily affect the recall or precision of the final parsed structure?
5. **Scalability:**  How does the performance of BSP scale with sentence length and the complexity of the UCCA graph?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work on semantic parsing, beyond AMR and SDP.
*   A brief comparison of UCCA to other semantic representations (e.g., FrameNet, PropBank) in terms of its strengths and weaknesses would be helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. While there are areas for improvement, the work addresses a significant gap, proposes a sound methodology, and provides a promising initial evaluation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above and responding to the questions posed. The potential for future work building on this foundation is substantial.
