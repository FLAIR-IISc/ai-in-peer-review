This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors address the lack of existing parsers capable of handling the complexities of BSS – namely, multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. They present two main approaches: conversion-based parsing utilizing existing constituency and dependency parsers, and a novel transition-based parser designed specifically for BSS. The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulates the challenges of BSS parsing, and provides a thorough experimental evaluation.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **First Transition-Based Parser for BSS:** The primary contribution lies in the development of BSP, the first parser designed to directly handle the complexities inherent in BSS as defined by UCCA. This is a significant step forward in addressing a gap in the NLP landscape.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation with Conversion Baselines:** The paper provides a robust evaluation framework by comparing BSP against conversion-based approaches using existing, well-established parsers (UPARSE, MaltParser, LSTM). This allows for a clear assessment of the benefits of a dedicated BSS parser.
3. **Analysis of Feature Importance:** The ablation study investigating the contribution of various feature sets to BSP’s performance provides valuable insights into the key factors driving parsing accuracy, which could inform future parser development.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper convincingly argues for the need for BSS parsing and provides a strong rationale for the UCCA scheme's suitability for representing broad semantic coverage. The examples illustrating the three structural properties are helpful and well-chosen.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experimental design is rigorous, including both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation, and provides a fair comparison between the proposed method and strong baselines. The use of established datasets (Wiki, 20K Leagues) strengthens the reproducibility of the results.
3. **Well-Explained Method:** The description of the transition system and feature set is detailed and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain how BSP extends existing transition-based parsing techniques to accommodate the specific requirements of BSS.
4. **Significant Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to conversion-based parsers is particularly valuable, demonstrating that while existing tools can be adapted, a dedicated BSS parser achieves competitive performance.
5. **Public Availability:** The authors commitment to releasing the code and models upon publication will greatly benefit the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains over Strong Baselines:** While BSP is a valuable contribution, the performance gains over the LSTM-based dependency parser (especially after conversion) are relatively modest. This raises questions about the extent to which the gains justify the complexity of the new parser. A deeper analysis of the error types could clarify where BSP excels and where it falls short.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by BSP and the baselines would provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, are errors concentrated on remote dependencies, non-terminal node prediction, or specific syntactic structures?
3. **Feature Engineering:** While the ablation study is helpful, the feature set itself appears somewhat ad-hoc and relies heavily on features borrowed from related parsing tasks. A more principled feature selection or learning approach could potentially improve performance.
4. **Overly Complex Transition System:** The transition system, with its inclusion of both primary and remote edge transitions, feels somewhat complex. A simplified transition system might be easier to train and interpret, while still achieving comparable performance.  The justification for separating these transitions isn’t fully explored.
5. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, while reasonable given the lack of established metrics, is somewhat limited. A more comprehensive evaluation that considers different aspects of parsing accuracy (e.g., dependency accuracy, constituent recall) could provide a more nuanced assessment of the parser's performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of errors that BSP makes most frequently?
2.  The LSTM-based parser, after dependency conversion, consistently outperforms BSP. What do you attribute this performance difference to, and what specific aspects of BSP could be improved to close the gap?
3.  What is the computational cost of BSP compared to the baseline parsers, particularly the LSTM-based parser?
4.  Regarding the remote edge transitions, can you provide more insights into their impact on performance and whether a simpler transition system (without explicitly distinguishing primary/remote edges) would lead to a significant performance degradation?
5.  How sensitive is BSP's performance to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., MINUPDATE, IMPORTANCE)? Did you perform a systematic hyperparameter tuning process?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of UCCA and potential future directions for BSS parsing.
* The notation used throughout the paper is generally clear, but could be made more consistent.
*  The references are comprehensive and well-organized.



Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. While the performance gains over strong baselines are modest, the development of a dedicated BSS parser is a significant step forward. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly through a more detailed error analysis and exploration of feature engineering techniques, would further strengthen this work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
