This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically targeting the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors acknowledge the lack of existing parsers for UCCA and propose a novel approach alongside conversion-based baselines utilizing existing constituency and dependency parsers. The primary contribution is the development and evaluation of a transition-based parser capable of handling multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units—features central to BSS. Secondary contributions include an analysis of the feasibility of converting UCCA structures to more conventional representations (dependency and constituency trees) for leveraging existing parsing technology, and the release of code for both conversion methods and the novel parser.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Significant Gap:** The lack of a parser for UCCA is a substantial problem, and this work directly addresses it. UCCA, with its ability to represent complex semantic structures, has potential for various NLP applications, and the availability of a parser is a crucial step towards realizing this potential.
2. **Novel Parser Design:** The BSP parser’s extensions to existing transition-based frameworks, specifically the novel transitions for remote edges, demonstrate a thoughtful approach to handling the complexities of BSS. The inclusion of a `ratio` feature, attempting to regulate node creation, is a potentially useful innovation.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a robust evaluation, including comparisons to conversion-based baselines, in-domain and out-of-domain performance assessment, and ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different features. The evaluation metrics, despite being newly defined for BSS, are clearly explained and appropriate for the task.
4. **Public Code Release:** The planned release of code for the converters and BSP parser promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gain over Baselines:** While BSP achieves competitive performance, the gains over strong conversion-based baselines (particularly the LSTM parser) are relatively modest. The primary edge F-score is comparable, and the ability to predict remote edges, though valuable, doesn’t translate into a dramatic overall performance improvement. This raises questions about the novelty and effectiveness of the specifically designed transitions in BSP.
2. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The ablation studies reveal a strong dependence on feature engineering, particularly unigrams and bigrams. The contribution of the novel `ratio` feature appears limited. This suggests that the current parser may be overly reliant on handcrafted features and could potentially benefit from more sophisticated feature learning approaches.
3. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, while reasonable, lacks the maturity and widespread acceptance of established parsing metrics like LAS/UAS. The metric focuses on edge matching, potentially overlooking differences in the overall structural quality of the parse. Further, the metric does not penalize incorrect edge *labels*, only the existence of a matching edge.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the BSP parser. Understanding the types of errors made by the parser would provide valuable insights for future improvements. A qualitative examination of parsing failures would be highly beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The LSTM parser consistently outperforms BSP on primary edge F-score. Can you elaborate on the specific architectural differences between the two systems and discuss potential avenues for incorporating similar LSTM-based components into BSP?
2.  The ablation study shows that the `ratio` feature has a limited impact. Was this feature intended to address a specific issue in BSS parsing, and if so, can you explain why it didn’t perform as expected?
3.  Could you provide a few examples of predicted parses from BSP that demonstrate the advantages of handling multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units compared to the output of the conversion-based parsers?
4. Given the limitations of the proposed evaluation metric, do you plan to explore alternative metrics or conduct a human evaluation of the parse quality?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of related work in semantic parsing, particularly approaches that handle non-projective dependencies or discontinuous constituents.
*   Clarifying the relationship between UCCA and other semantic representation schemes (e.g., AMR, SDP) in terms of their expressiveness and suitability for different applications would be helpful.
*   The use of beam search in inference could potentially improve the performance of BSP.
