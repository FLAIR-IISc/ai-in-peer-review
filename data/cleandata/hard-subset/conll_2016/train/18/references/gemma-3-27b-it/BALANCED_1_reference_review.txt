This paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors identify a gap in existing parsing technology – the lack of a parser capable of handling the complexities of BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units) – and propose a novel parser (BSP) designed to address these challenges. They also explore a conversion-based approach to leverage existing parsers.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Parser for BSS:** The development of BSP, a transition-based parser explicitly designed to handle the structural properties of BSS as defined by UCCA, is the primary contribution. This parser extends existing transition-based frameworks with new transitions and features tailored for UCCA’s characteristics.
2. **Empirical Evaluation on UCCA:**  The thorough experimental evaluation of BSP on both in-domain and out-of-domain UCCA corpora provides a valuable benchmark and demonstrates the feasibility of transition-based parsing for BSS. Reaching nearly 70% labeled F-score is a significant initial result for this challenging task.
3. **Analysis of Conversion-Based Approaches:** The investigation of converting BSS to simpler representations (dependency and constituency trees) and utilizing existing parsers, though ultimately less effective than BSP, provides useful insights into the information loss inherent in such conversions and offers a baseline for comparison.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Clear Gap:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important problem – the lack of parsers for BSS, a representation considered valuable for its broad coverage and cross-linguistic applicability.
2. **Solid Technical Approach:** The choice of a transition-based approach is well-justified, given its success in related parsing tasks and its natural fit for the UCCA framework. The extensions to existing transition systems (new transitions, features) are clearly explained.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, including in-domain and out-of-domain testing, as well as comparison to conversion-based baselines.  The inclusion of both precision and recall provides a balanced view of performance.
4. **Availability of Resources:** The commitment to publicly releasing the converters and parser is a significant strength, fostering reproducibility and further research.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the approach, the UCCA representation, and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains over Baselines:** While achieving nearly 70% F-score is good, the performance gain of BSP over the conversion-based baselines (particularly the LSTM parser leveraging dependency conversion) is relatively modest. This raises questions about the extent to which BSP truly capitalizes on the unique features of BSS.
2. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The performance of BSP heavily relies on carefully designed features. While the authors describe these features, the process of feature selection and optimization isn’t fully detailed. The impact of individual features isn't sufficiently analyzed beyond the ablation study.
3. **Incomplete Remote Edge Analysis:** While the authors include transitions for handling remote edges, the consistently lower F-score on remote edges compared to primary edges suggests that this aspect of the parser requires further refinement.  A more detailed error analysis focusing specifically on remote edge prediction is needed.
4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Semantic Parsers:** The paper focuses heavily on comparing to dependency and constituency parsers, but lacks comparison to other state-of-the-art semantic parsers (e.g., AMR parsers) – even if a direct comparison is imperfect due to differences in representation.  This makes it difficult to assess the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach in the broader landscape of semantic parsing.
5. **Limited Ablation Study:** The ablation study is helpful but somewhat limited. A more comprehensive ablation study varying the transition sets and feature combinations would provide further insight into the contributions of different components of the system.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The LSTM parser using dependency conversion achieves a higher primary edge F-score. Could you elaborate on why BSP does not significantly outperform this baseline, despite being designed specifically for UCCA?  What specific challenges remain in accurately capturing UCCA structure with the current transition system and features?
2.  The remote edge F-score is consistently lower than the primary edge F-score. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis focusing specifically on the types of errors made in predicting remote edges?  Are there specific linguistic phenomena or structural patterns that are particularly challenging for the remote edge transitions?
3.  How sensitive is BSP’s performance to the MINUPDATE and IMPORTANCE hyperparameters? Did you explore a wider range of values for these parameters during development?
4.  You mention that the ratio feature serves as a regularizer for node creation. Can you provide more details on how this feature was designed and its impact on parsing performance, particularly in preventing overly complex or fragmented graphs?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation and descriptions of the transition system are generally clear, but could benefit from more visual examples.
*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in semantic parsing, particularly those focusing on discontinuous or non-projective structures.
*   Consider including a qualitative example of a correctly and incorrectly parsed UCCA structure to illustrate the strengths and weaknesses of BSP.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. While the performance gains over baselines are modest, the development of a dedicated parser for BSS and the thorough evaluation on UCCA data represent a significant step forward. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
