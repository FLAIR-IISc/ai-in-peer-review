This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically targeting the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors address the lack of existing parsers for UCCA, which supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features not commonly found in traditional parsers. They propose a novel parser (BSP) and also explore conversion-based approaches utilizing dependency and constituency trees.

The primary contribution of this work is the development and evaluation of a novel transition-based parser, BSP, capable of handling the complexities of BSS as defined by UCCA. While transition-based parsing is a well-established technique, adapting it to accommodate multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, particularly within the UCCA framework, represents a significant step. The exploration of new transitions and features tailored for UCCA further strengthens this contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation of both the direct BSP approach and conversion-based methods. The results provide a baseline for future work in UCCA parsing and offer insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different parsing strategies. Finally, making the converters and parsers publicly available is a valuable contribution for the research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Parser:** BSP directly addresses a gap in existing tools for parsing UCCA structures. The introduction of specialized transitions (LEFT-EDGEX, RIGHT-EDGEX, LEFTREMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) to handle the unique characteristics of UCCA graphs (multiple parents, remote edges) is a key strength. This is not a simple adaptation of existing parsers, but a thoughtful extension designed for a specific, challenging semantic representation.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation, comparing BSP against conversion-based approaches using established parsers (MaltParser, UPARSE). This allows for a nuanced assessment of the performance and limitations of each method. The use of both in-domain and out-of-domain datasets enhances the robustness of the findings.
3. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing semantic parsing approaches and motivates the need for parsers that can handle BSS. The detailed explanation of UCCA and its properties establishes a solid foundation for understanding the challenges involved.
4. **Feature Engineering:** The incorporation of a ‘ratio’ feature – the ratio of terminals to nodes – is a novel approach that potentially regularizes node creation and could be beneficial for other transition-based constituency parsers. The blending of established feature sets from related parsing tasks with UCCA-specific features demonstrates careful consideration of relevant linguistic properties.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains Over Conversion-Based Methods:** While BSP is novel, its F-score improvements over the conversion-based methods (especially those using the LSTM classifier) are relatively modest. The paper acknowledges this and suggests further improvements using LSTM within BSP, but the current performance difference doesn’t strongly justify the complexity of the direct BSS parser.
2. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, based on edge matching and yield comparison, seems simplistic and may not fully capture the quality of the parsed BSS. The metric relies heavily on exact matches and might not adequately reward partial correctness in graph structure. A more sophisticated metric, potentially considering graph edit distance or semantic similarity, could provide a more informative evaluation.
3. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis to pinpoint the specific types of errors BSP makes. Understanding the common error patterns would be crucial for guiding future development efforts. For instance, are errors concentrated on remote edges, non-terminal nodes, or specific syntactic constructions?
4. **Dependency on Existing Parsers for Conversion:** The conversion-based approach relies on the performance of established dependency and constituency parsers. The quality of the conversion directly impacts the final results, introducing a dependency on external components.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the LSTM baseline: You mention that applying an LSTM-based approach to BSP is likely to improve results. Could you elaborate on the practical challenges of integrating an LSTM-based classifier within the BSP framework?
2.  Evaluation Metric: Could you discuss the limitations of the proposed evaluation metric and explore potential alternatives that might better capture the quality of BSS parsing?
3.  Error Analysis: Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the common types of errors made by BSP and discussing potential strategies to address them?
4.  Remote Edge Prediction: The results suggest that predicting remote edges remains a challenge. What specific features or transition modifications are being considered to improve the accuracy of remote edge prediction?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a visual comparison of the parsed BSS structures from BSP and the gold standard, illustrating both correct and incorrect predictions.
*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include recent advances in graph neural networks for semantic parsing, which may offer alternative approaches to BSS parsing.
*   The description of the feature templates could be more concise and focused on the novel features introduced in BSP.
