## Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging task of broad-coverage semantic parsing, specifically targeting the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme. The authors identify three key properties lacking in existing parsers – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – and propose a transition-based approach, Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), to address them. They also investigate conversion-based methods, translating UCCA structures into dependency and constituency trees to leverage existing parsing technology. The results demonstrate competitive performance, reaching nearly 70% labeled F-score in-domain.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First Transition-Based Parser for BSS:** The development of BSP is a significant contribution. It's the first attempt to directly tackle the complexities of UCCA parsing with a transition-based model, laying a foundation for future research.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Conversion Approaches:** The thorough investigation of converting UCCA to more standard representations (dependency and constituency trees) provides valuable insights into the information loss inherent in such conversions and establishes a baseline for comparison.
3. **Identification and Formalization of BSS Properties:**  The clear articulation and formalization of the requirements for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity – is a useful contribution to the field, providing a framework for evaluating semantic parsing approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The transition-based approach to parsing UCCA is genuinely novel. While transition-based parsing is well-established, applying it to this specific type of semantic structure is a significant step forward.  The extensions to the transition system to handle non-terminal nodes and discontinuities are well-motivated and sound.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The paper provides a well-defined experimental setup with clear evaluation metrics (precision, recall, F-score) and utilizes both in-domain and out-of-domain testing. The comparison against conversion-based methods is particularly valuable.
3. **Strong Baseline Results:** Achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score is a commendable result for a novel parsing approach, demonstrating the viability of the proposed method. The results show that BSP is competitive with existing parsers when applied after conversion.
4. **Clear and Comprehensive Background:** The introduction and background sections are well-written and provide a clear understanding of the motivation, related work, and the UCCA annotation scheme itself. This makes the paper accessible to a broad audience.
5. **Open-Source Commitment:** The authors’ commitment to making the code publicly available is a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Improvement over Conversion-Based Methods:** While BSP achieves competitive results, the improvement over the conversion-based approaches (particularly those using dependency parsing) is not dramatic.  This suggests that a significant portion of the information is lost during the conversion process, but BSP doesn’t fully recover it.  More substantial gains are needed to justify the complexity of a dedicated parser.
2. **Feature Engineering & LSTM Potential:** The reliance on relatively standard features (as described in Figure 3) feels somewhat conservative. Given the success of LSTMs in similar parsing tasks (as highlighted in the results section), exploring LSTM-based features within the BSP framework would likely yield significant performance improvements. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, but it's a substantial limitation of the current work.
3. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:**  The proposed evaluation metric, while reasonable, might not fully capture the nuances of semantic parsing.  The focus on edge-level F-score could overlook errors in the overall structure and semantic relationships. Exploring alternative or complementary evaluation metrics might provide a more comprehensive assessment.
4. **Limited Analysis of Errors:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the parser fails (e.g., specific UCCA categories, long-distance dependencies, ambiguous cases) would be crucial for guiding future improvements. 
5. **Scaling to Larger Corpora:** The reported experiments are conducted on relatively small datasets.  The scalability of BSP to larger corpora and more complex linguistic phenomena remains an open question.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the potential for improvement with LSTM-based features. Could you elaborate on how you envision integrating LSTMs into the BSP framework? Specifically, which features would you encode with LSTMs, and how would this affect the transition system?
2.  The ablation study shows that the 'ratio' feature significantly improves recall. Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why this feature is so effective?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on specific UCCA categories or grammatical constructions where the parser consistently performs poorly?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with BSP compared to the conversion-based methods?  Is the increased complexity of BSP justified by the performance gains?
5.  The conversion-based methods show variations in performance depending on the target formalism (dependency vs. constituency). What insights does this give you regarding the inherent suitability of different representations for capturing UCCA’s semantics?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more visual representation of the parse trees generated by BSP, alongside the gold-standard UCCA structures.
*   It would be helpful to provide a clearer distinction between the contributions of the original work and the adaptations from existing parsing literature.



Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. While there are areas for improvement, the paper presents a promising new approach to tackling a challenging problem. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
