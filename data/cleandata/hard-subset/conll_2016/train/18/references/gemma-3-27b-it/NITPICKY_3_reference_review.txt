The paper presents a transition-based parser for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features lacking in existing parsers. The authors explore both a conversion-based approach utilizing existing parsers and a novel, dedicated parser (BSP). The primary claimed contribution is the first parser capable of handling the complexities of Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS). A secondary contribution is the exploration of conversion methods to leverage existing parsing technology. A third, more modest, contribution is the release of code and data.

I perceive the main contribution as the exploration of applying transition-based parsing to the UCCA framework, providing a baseline and a testbed for future research. The conversion experiments are useful for understanding limitations, but are not groundbreaking. The code release, while valuable, is standard practice and not a primary contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Transition-Based Parsing:** The application of transition-based parsing to UCCA, given the representation's inherent complexity, is a worthwhile endeavor. This provides a solid starting point for further exploration and establishes a benchmark for future work in broad-coverage semantic parsing.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors demonstrate a thoughtful approach to feature engineering, adapting existing successful features and introducing a novel ‘ratio’ feature. This suggests a strong understanding of the task and the potential for feature-based improvements.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper includes both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation, providing a more robust assessment of the parser's performance. The comparison with conversion-based approaches provides a baseline for evaluating the benefits of a dedicated parser.
4. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of broad-coverage semantic parsing and motivates the need for a parser capable of handling multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains over Conversion Methods:** While BSP achieves comparable primary edge F-scores to conversion-based methods, the gains are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of a dedicated parser, particularly given the relatively low performance on remote edges. The performance is far from state-of-the-art dependency parsing numbers, suggesting there is considerable room for improvement.
2. **Inadequate Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the parser fails – specific linguistic phenomena, edge label prediction errors, etc. – is crucial for guiding future development. Without such analysis, improvements are likely to be ad hoc.
3. **Questionable Experimental Setup Details:** The training/dev/test split is not clearly explained, and the lack of detail surrounding the MINUPDATE and IMPORTANCE parameters used during training raises concerns about reproducibility and potential overfitting. The arbitrary doubling of weights for SWAP transitions feels particularly unsupported and requires justification.
4. **Overstated Claims of Novelty:** The authors claim to introduce the “first” parser for BSS, which is technically true given the specific UCCA annotation scheme. However, the core transition mechanisms are heavily based on existing work, and the modifications are incremental. The novelty lies in the *application* rather than the fundamental approach.
5. **Formatting inconsistencies and minor errors:** Throughout the paper, I noticed several inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, spacing issues) and minor errors (e.g., references not formatted consistently). While not critical, they detract from the professionalism of the submission. In section 3, algorithm 1's line "n(t)← h(u);" should be "n(t) ← h(Parentc(u));"

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the specific types of errors the parser makes? Specifically, what types of remote edges are most frequently missed or incorrectly predicted?
2.  What is the justification for doubling the weight updates for SWAP transitions? Did you perform an ablation study to confirm its benefit?
3.  Please clarify the training/dev/test split used for evaluation. Provide the exact sentence indices used for each set.
4.  Why was a greedy search used for BSP instead of beam search, given that UPARSE uses beam search and obtains higher performance?
5. Can you provide more details on the specific limitations of the conversion-based methods, and explain how these limitations motivate the need for a dedicated parser?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are somewhat difficult to read, especially Figure 1. Larger, clearer figures would improve comprehension.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough but could benefit from a more critical assessment of existing approaches.
*   Please review the paper carefully for formatting inconsistencies and minor errors before submitting the final version.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a reasonable approach, the limited performance gains, lack of detailed error analysis, and minor technical issues outweigh the contributions. The work represents a solid starting point, but requires significant further development and refinement before it can be considered a substantial contribution to the field. The minor flaws detract from an otherwise acceptable paper.
