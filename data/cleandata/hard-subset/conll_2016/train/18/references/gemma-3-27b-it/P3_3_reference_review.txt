## Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

**Summary:** This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – collectively termed Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS). The authors explore both conversion-based parsing (converting UCCA to dependency and constituency trees) and a novel, directly trained BSS parser.  They demonstrate competitive performance with existing parsers on the English UCCA corpus, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First Direct Parser for BSS:** The most significant contribution is the development of BSP, the first parser specifically designed to handle the complexities of UCCA and, more broadly, BSS. This moves beyond relying on approximations through conversion to simpler formalisms.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of BSS Parsing:** The paper provides a valuable empirical evaluation of BSS parsing, both with the novel parser and through conversion techniques, establishing a baseline for future research in this area.  The out-of-domain experiments are a plus.
3. **Exploration of Transition System Extensions:** The authors successfully extend transition-based parsing techniques to accommodate the requirements of UCCA, introducing new transitions and features, providing a practical demonstration of the method's adaptability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing semantic parsing approaches and convincingly motivates the need for BSS and a parser capable of handling them. The examples illustrating the three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) are particularly helpful.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is well-defined, using a standard dataset (UCCA) and establishing clear evaluation metrics. The comparison with both conversion-based methods and existing parsers provides a strong basis for assessing the performance of BSP. The inclusion of out-of-domain testing is commendable.
3. **Thoughtful Transition System Design:** The design of the transition system is well-reasoned, effectively extending existing techniques to support the unique requirements of UCCA. The explanation of the new transitions (LEFT-EDGEX, RIGHT-EDGEX, LEFTREMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) is clear.
4. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The feature set is extensive and incorporates relevant information from related parsing tasks, suggesting a good understanding of the factors influencing parsing accuracy. The introduction of the "ratio" feature is a particularly interesting addition.
5. **Public Availability of Resources:** The authors' commitment to making the converters and parsers publicly available is a significant benefit to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains:** While BSP achieves competitive performance, the gains over conversion-based methods, especially given the complexity of the system, are relatively modest. The 62.4% F-score, while respectable, doesn't represent a substantial leap forward, and the LSTM parser outperforms BSP.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study Depth:** The ablation study (Table 3) is somewhat superficial. While it demonstrates the importance of feature groups, it doesn't provide a detailed understanding of which specific features contribute the most to performance.  A more granular analysis would be helpful.
3. **Simplified Conversion Procedures:** The conversion procedures to constituency and dependency trees are somewhat simplistic. More sophisticated conversion methods might yield better performance for the conversion-based baselines, providing a stronger point of comparison.  The loss of information during conversion is acknowledged but could be explored further.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, while reasonable, doesn't fully capture the quality of the parsed BSS. It primarily focuses on edge labels and yields, potentially overlooking important structural differences.  Exploring alternative evaluation metrics, potentially incorporating semantic similarity measures, would be beneficial.
5. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying common error patterns in BSP's predictions would provide valuable insights for future improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The LSTM parser significantly outperforms BSP. Could you elaborate on the architecture and training details of the LSTM parser used for comparison, and discuss potential avenues for incorporating LSTM-based components into BSP?
2.  The ablation study shows that adding remote edge transitions slightly *decreases* performance on the in-domain set.  What is the explanation for this counterintuitive result? Is it related to data sparsity or other factors?
3.  You mention the possibility of improving the conversion procedures.  Have you considered using more sophisticated alignment techniques or graph transformation algorithms to minimize information loss during conversion?
4.  Could you provide some example predictions from BSP where it succeeds and fails, illustrating the types of errors it commonly makes?  A qualitative error analysis would be very insightful.
5. You claim that the ratio feature serves as a regularizer. Could you elaborate on this and provide some evidence (e.g., learning curves) to support this claim?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The inclusion of the UCCA figures is helpful for visualizing the concepts.




Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. While the performance gains are modest, the development of the first direct parser for BSS is a significant achievement. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly through a more in-depth error analysis and a deeper investigation of the LSTM baseline, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
