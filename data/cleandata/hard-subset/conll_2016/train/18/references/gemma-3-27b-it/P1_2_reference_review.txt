## Peer Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

**Summary:**

This paper presents a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically targeting the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors identify a gap in existing parsers for representations requiring multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, and propose a novel parser (BSP) along with conversion strategies from more common formalisms like dependency and constituency trees. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on English UCCA data, achieving around 70% labeled F-score and showing reasonable robustness to out-of-domain data.

**Main Contributions (as claimed by authors, in order of strength):**

1.  **First BSS Parser:** The primary contribution is the development of the first parser specifically designed for parsing BSS, overcoming the limitations of existing parsers.
2.  **Novel Transition System & Features:** The paper introduces a novel transition system and UCCA-specific features, extending existing transition-based parsing techniques to handle the complexities of BSS.
3.  **Conversion-Based Baseline & Analysis:** The authors explore conversion protocols to leverage existing parsers for a baseline, providing insights into the challenges of BSS parsing and its relation to more common representations.

**My Assessment of Contributions:**

1.  **Novel Parser for a Specific Representation:** The most significant contribution is genuinely the development of a parser *capable* of handling the BSS formalism.  The limitations of existing tools for UCCA (and similar representations) are real, and addressing this is valuable.
2.  **Exploration of Transition-Based Methods:** The adoption of a transition-based framework is a sensible choice given its success in related parsing tasks.  The extension with remote edge handling and the introduction of new features are incremental contributions, demonstrating adaptation of existing techniques.
3.  **Insightful Conversion Experiments:** The conversion experiments are less a novel contribution and more a helpful investigative step. They effectively demonstrate the information loss inherent in attempting to fit BSS into simpler formalisms, justifying the need for a dedicated parser.


**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Clear Gap:** The paper directly tackles a known limitation in semantic parsing: the lack of tools capable of handling complex, non-projective structures like those in UCCA. This makes the work relevant and timely.
2.  **Solid Experimental Setup:**  The evaluation is well-defined with appropriate datasets (Wiki and 20K Leagues) and a clear evaluation metric (labeled F-score on primary and remote edges).  The inclusion of out-of-domain testing is also a strength, showing some level of generalizability.
3.  **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work, and the technical details are explained in a reasonably accessible manner. The inclusion of Figure 1 is particularly helpful for understanding the BSS representation.
4. **Practical Contribution:** The commitment to releasing the code upon publication is a substantial benefit to the community, enabling further research and development in this area.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Conversion:**  The conversion experiments are not merely a baseline, but a helpful demonstration of *why* a dedicated parser is necessary, highlighting the information lost when forcing BSS into simpler structures.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Performance Gains:** While 70% F-score is a reasonable starting point, the performance of BSP isn't dramatically better than the conversion-based approaches, especially considering the complexity of the proposed system. The relatively small performance difference raises questions about the effectiveness of the novel features and transitions.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to MaltParser and UPARSE, while useful, doesn't represent the state-of-the-art in semantic parsing. Comparing to stronger, existing semantic parsers (even if they require adaptation) would better contextualize the performance of BSP. Specifically, an LSTM-based transition-based parser trained directly on UCCA data (mirroring the approach taken with dependency parsing) would be a much more informative baseline.
3.  **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The success of the parser heavily relies on carefully crafted features. While the features are well-motivated, their reliance on head-word identification (derived from the conversion experiments) feels somewhat contradictory to the goal of avoiding head-selection problems inherent in dependency-based representations.  More exploration of end-to-end learning approaches or learned features would be valuable.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the parser fails (e.g., handling specific types of remote edges, coordination structures) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
5. **Remote Edge Performance:** Performance on remote edges remains low. While they acknowledge this, a deeper dive into the difficulties of predicting remote edges and potential solutions is needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The LSTM parser achieved significantly higher scores with dependency conversion. Have you explored applying an LSTM-based architecture *directly* to the BSS parsing task (i.e., building a transition-based BSS parser with LSTM features)?  If not, why?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the types of errors made by the parser?  Are there specific UCCA structures that consistently cause problems?
3.  You mention that the ratio feature improved recall.  Can you explain the intuition behind why this feature is effective?
4.  The use of head-word features seems to contradict the BSS goals. Can you elaborate on why you included them and whether you considered alternative feature representations?
5.  How sensitive is the performance of BSP to the MINUPDATE parameter? Have you explored a wider range of values?



**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be beneficial to include a table summarizing the number of different edge labels in the UCCA corpus to give a sense of the complexity of the parsing task.
*   A brief discussion of the computational cost of BSP compared to the conversion-based methods would be useful.
