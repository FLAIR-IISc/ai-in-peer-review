This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a semantic representation aiming to support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – properties lacking in existing parsers. The authors address this gap by proposing BSP and exploring conversion-based parsing using dependency and constituency trees.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **First Transition-Based BSS Parser:** The development of BSP represents a significant contribution, providing an initial framework for parsing UCCA structures, a specific instantiation of BSS. This work establishes a baseline and opens avenues for future research in this area.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of Conversion-Based Approaches:** The thorough evaluation of converting UCCA structures to constituency and dependency trees provides valuable insights into the information loss associated with these transformations. This analysis clarifies the limitations of relying solely on existing parsing technology and underscores the need for BSS-specific parsers.
3. **Novel Features for UCCA Parsing:** The introduction of UCCA-oriented features, particularly the “ratio” feature, demonstrates an understanding of the specific challenges of BSS parsing and offers a potential avenue for performance improvement.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Clear Gap:** The paper tackles a well-defined problem – the lack of parsers for BSS – and proposes a solution that directly addresses the key requirements of broad coverage semantics.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed, including both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluations, and comparisons with relevant baselines (including conversion-based approaches). The use of both Wiki and 20K Leagues datasets provides a robust assessment of the proposed method.
3. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The description of the transition system and feature set is clear and comprehensive, facilitating reproducibility. The inclusion of algorithms for conversion procedures further enhances the clarity.
4. **Public Availability of Resources:** The commitment to making the converters and parser publicly available is a positive aspect, promoting further research and adoption of the proposed approach.
5. **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a good overview of related work in semantic parsing, including AMR, SDP, and UCCA, contextualizing the contribution within the broader research landscape.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains over Baselines:** While BSP achieves comparable performance to existing parsers on primary edges, the F-score (around 62-63%) is not significantly higher.  The performance on *remote* edges, while non-zero, is considerably lower, suggesting challenges in capturing non-projective dependencies. The LSTM parser significantly outperforms BSP, suggesting a potentially more fruitful direction.
2. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The large number of features, while potentially effective, introduces complexity. A more targeted feature selection process or the exploration of automated feature learning techniques could simplify the model and potentially improve performance.
3. **Simplistic Evaluation Metric:** The evaluation metric, while straightforward, may not fully capture the nuances of BSS parsing.  Consideration of more sophisticated metrics, potentially focusing on higher-order dependencies or semantic coherence, could provide a more comprehensive assessment.
4. **Limited Analysis of Errors:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by BSP (e.g., incorrect edge labels, incorrect attachment points) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
5. **Lack of Ablation Study Depth:** While feature ablation is presented, the analysis lacks depth. Identifying *which* features contribute most to remote edge prediction, for example, would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The LSTM parser outperforms BSP considerably. Could you elaborate on why you chose to focus on a transition-based approach, despite the stronger performance of the LSTM-based model?  What were the computational costs involved in exploring the LSTM model more extensively?
2.  The paper mentions the ratio feature as novel.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind this feature and why it is particularly useful for BSS parsing?  What impact does varying the weight of this feature have on overall performance?
3.  Could you provide a detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by BSP (e.g., incorrect edge labels, incorrect attachment points, failure to predict remote edges)?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of BSP to the MINUPDATE and IMPORTANCE parameters?  Did you explore a wider range of values for these parameters?
5. How do the performance metrics correlate with human annotation agreement on UCCA?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used throughout the paper is generally clear, but could be made more consistent.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between BSS, AMR, and SDP to further clarify the unique contributions of the proposed approach.
*   The phrase “grounded semantic representation” appears frequently. It could be beneficial to define this term more precisely in the introduction.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing by addressing the challenge of parsing BSS. While the performance of BSP is not yet state-of-the-art, the work lays a solid foundation for future research and provides valuable insights into the challenges and opportunities of broad-coverage semantic parsing. Despite the weaknesses, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions provided above.
