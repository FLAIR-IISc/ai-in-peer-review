## Peer Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. Recognizing the lack of existing parsers for UCCA, the authors explore two strategies: converting UCCA structures to simpler formalisms (dependency trees and constituency trees) and training existing parsers on these conversions; and developing a new transition-based parser (BSP) specifically designed to handle the complexities of BSS – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. They demonstrate the efficacy of BSP, achieving around 70% labeled F-score on in-domain data and provide initial results on out-of-domain performance.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First Transition-Based Parser for BSS/UCCA:** This is the primary contribution. The authors successfully develop a functional transition-based parser capable of handling the complexities inherent in UCCA’s broad-coverage semantic representation, a significant step towards enabling wider adoption and application of this formalism.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of Conversion-Based Approaches:** The thorough examination of converting UCCA to dependency and constituency trees, and evaluating existing parsers on these converted structures provides a valuable baseline and highlights the information loss associated with such conversions.
3. **Novel Features for UCCA Parsing:** The introduction of features specifically tailored to UCCA’s structure, particularly the ‘ratio’ feature, demonstrates a thoughtful approach to adapting existing parsing techniques to this specific semantic representation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Gap:** The paper tackles a significant problem – the lack of a parser for the promising UCCA annotation scheme. The availability of a parser is essential for widespread adoption and application of UCCA.
2. **Sound Methodological Approach:** The combination of conversion-based approaches and the development of a dedicated parser provides a robust evaluation strategy. Starting with established methods and then building a novel solution is a well-reasoned approach.
3. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the experimental setup is detailed and reproducible. The explanation of the transition system and features is comprehensive.
4. **Promising Results:** Achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score is a strong result, especially considering this is a novel parser for a complex representation. The results demonstrate the feasibility of the transition-based approach.
5. **Availability of Resources:** The authors’ commitment to making the code and converters publicly available is a significant positive aspect, fostering reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparative Analysis of BSP:** While the paper compares BSP to parsers trained on converted UCCA data, it lacks a thorough comparison with more recent and advanced semantic parsing techniques, particularly those leveraging neural networks (e.g., AMR parsers using LSTMs or Transformers). A more direct comparison would better position the contribution of BSP.
2. **Transition System Complexity:** The transition system appears somewhat complex, particularly with the introduction of separate transitions for primary and remote edges. The justification for this complexity, while explained, could be further strengthened with an ablation study demonstrating the impact of these specific transitions on performance.
3. **Feature Engineering Dependency:** The performance of BSP relies heavily on feature engineering. While the features are well-motivated, it would be valuable to explore the potential of automatically learning features using neural network architectures, potentially simplifying the transition system.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The defined evaluation metrics, while providing a starting point, are relatively simple. More comprehensive evaluation metrics, perhaps inspired by AMR parsing evaluation, could provide a more nuanced assessment of parser performance.
5. **Out-of-Domain Performance Gap:** While the out-of-domain results are reasonable, the drop in performance (particularly on remote edges) suggests that the parser is sensitive to domain shifts. Further investigation into domain adaptation techniques could be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Neural Network Exploration:** Have you explored incorporating neural network-based features or architectures (e.g., LSTMs, Transformers) into BSP? If so, what were the results? If not, what were the primary reasons for not pursuing this direction?
2.  **Transition System Ablation:** Can you provide an ablation study demonstrating the impact of the separate `LEFTREMOTEX` and `RIGHTREMOTEX` transitions on parsing performance? What is the performance drop when these transitions are removed or combined with other transitions?
3. **Remote Edge Prediction:** The significant drop in remote edge F-score in the out-of-domain experiment is concerning. Can you analyze the types of errors being made in remote edge prediction and discuss potential strategies for improving robustness?
4. **Comparison to AMR Parsers:** Can you discuss the key differences between UCCA and AMR, and how these differences might impact the applicability of techniques developed for AMR parsing to UCCA parsing? Is it possible to adapt state-of-the-art AMR parsers to UCCA?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material mentioned in the paper (regarding the definition of `Label`) should be readily available with the submission.
*   Consider discussing the computational complexity of the proposed parser.



Overall, this is a strong paper addressing a significant problem with a well-reasoned and executed approach. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly with a more thorough comparative analysis and exploration of neural network-based approaches, would further strengthen the contribution and impact of this work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
