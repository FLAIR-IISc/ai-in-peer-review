This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features lacking in existing parsers. The authors present a novel parser (BSP) and explore conversion-based parsing as a baseline. The main claimed contributions are: 1) the first parser designed for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) as defined by UCCA; 2) a novel transition system and feature set tailored for UCCA parsing; and 3) empirical evaluation on English UCCA data, demonstrating nearly 70% labeled F-score.

I view the primary contribution as the *attempt* to tackle a challenging parsing problem – that of UCCA – with a well-established methodology (transition-based parsing). The UCCA representation itself is interesting and the lack of a parser is a genuine gap. However, the work falls short of demonstrating significant advancement in parsing technology itself.  The secondary contribution lies in the creation of a dataset and evaluation setup for this specific task, which will be useful for future research, although the dataset size remains relatively modest.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Clear Gap:** The lack of a parser for UCCA is a genuine issue, and the authors correctly identify this.  The motivation for supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units is well-articulated and the importance of broad-coverage semantics is clear.
2. **Sound Methodological Foundation:** The choice of a transition-based approach is reasonable given its success in related tasks (dependency parsing, AMR parsing). Leveraging existing techniques as a starting point is a prudent approach.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide results on both in-domain and out-of-domain data, along with comparisons to conversion-based baselines. This provides a reasonable, although not exhaustive, evaluation of the proposed system.
4. **Public Availability:** The promise to release the code and data is commendable and will facilitate reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Parsing Methodology:** While the transition system is extended, the core ideas (SHIFT, REDUCE, NODE, and edge creation) are largely borrowed from existing work. The UCCA-specific features, while helpful, don’t represent a fundamental breakthrough in parsing techniques. The paper lacks a convincing argument *why* this transition system is particularly well-suited for UCCA beyond simply being able to represent the desired structures.
2. **Performance is Not State-of-the-Art:**  A 69.2% F-score, while respectable, isn’t dramatically higher than existing parsers on other semantic tasks, particularly given the relatively limited size of the UCCA corpus. The LSTM-based parser, used as a comparison via conversion, significantly outperforms BSP, suggesting that the key bottleneck is the local classifier, not necessarily the transition system itself. This strongly indicates a need for more sophisticated modeling of the parser state.
3. **Inadequate Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The conversion-based experiments, while insightful, utilize relatively simple dependency parsers (arc-standard/eager MaltParser).  Comparing against more powerful, state-of-the-art dependency or constituency parsers would provide a stronger baseline and a more nuanced understanding of the performance gains (or lack thereof) achieved by BSP.  The use of beam search in UPARSE, while noted, further highlights the lack of such exploration within BSP itself.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the errors made by BSP.  Understanding *where* the parser struggles (e.g., handling discontinuities, multiple parents, specific edge labels) is crucial for guiding future improvements.  Simply reporting overall F-score provides limited insight.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Why was beam search not employed during inference with BSP?  Given the success of beam search in related transition-based parsing tasks, what were the reasons for using a greedy approach?
2. The LSTM parser significantly outperforms BSP. Do you have hypotheses as to why this is the case?  Would incorporating an LSTM-based classifier into BSP be a promising direction for future work?
3. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of errors that BSP makes most frequently (e.g., incorrect edge labels, missed attachments, incorrect handling of discontinuities)?
4. The ratio feature is described as a “novel” regularization. Can you elaborate on the intuition behind this feature and provide some ablation study results demonstrating its impact on performance?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are extensive and well-formatted. The figures are helpful in illustrating the UCCA representation. The discussion of related work (AMR, SDP) is adequate. The supplementary material should contain a complete list of features used.
