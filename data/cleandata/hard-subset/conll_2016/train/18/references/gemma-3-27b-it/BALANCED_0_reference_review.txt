This paper presents the first parser for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), aiming to support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units—properties lacking in existing parsers for grounded semantic representations like UCCA. The authors adopt a transition-based approach, exploring both conversion-based parsing (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel BSS parser (BSP). They achieve nearly 70% labeled F-score on the English UCCA corpus, demonstrating a promising initial step.

**Contributions:**

1. **First BSS Parser:** The primary contribution is the development of BSP, the first parser explicitly designed to handle the complexities of BSS as defined by the UCCA scheme. This represents a significant step towards parsing semantic structures with richer expressiveness than currently available tools allow.
2. **Empirical Evaluation on UCCA:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation of their approach, and conversion-based baselines, on the UCCA corpus. This establishes a baseline for future research in BSS parsing and provides valuable insights into the challenges and opportunities of the task.
3. **Analysis of Conversion-Based Approaches:** The exploration of conversion-based parsing, while not the central focus, offers a valuable analysis of the information loss involved in mapping BSS to more restricted formalisms, providing a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs involved.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Importance of the Problem:** The problem of parsing BSS is inherently novel and important. Existing semantic parsing approaches often fall short in representing the full complexity of natural language semantics. The UCCA scheme and the corresponding parsing task address this gap, and the paper successfully identifies and tackles this challenge.
2. **Sound Methodological Approach:** The transition-based approach is well-motivated, building on successful techniques in syntactic and semantic parsing. The exploration of both direct parsing with BSP and conversion-based methods provides a comprehensive evaluation strategy.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, using appropriate datasets and evaluation metrics. The inclusion of out-of-domain results demonstrates the robustness of the proposed approach. The ablation study provides further insight into the contribution of different features.
4. **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed approach. The authors’ commitment to releasing the code upon publication is commendable and will facilitate reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Improvement over Baselines:** While the 70% F-score is a good starting point, the performance of BSP is not dramatically better than the conversion-based baselines, particularly given the complexity of the proposed approach. This suggests that the current features and transition system may not be fully capturing the intricacies of BSS.
2. **Overly Complex Feature Set:** The feature set described in Figure 3 is quite extensive and potentially redundant. The ablation study partially addresses this, but a more systematic feature selection or reduction process could lead to improved performance and model interpretability.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Semantic Parsers:** While the authors compare to parsers converted *to* the UCCA format, a direct comparison to state-of-the-art semantic parsers for representations like AMR (using a conversion to AMR from UCCA, for example) would provide valuable context and highlight the specific advantages of the BSS approach.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, based on edge overlap, may not fully capture the quality of the parsed BSS. A more holistic evaluation metric that considers the overall structural similarity between the predicted and gold-standard graphs would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis of BSP, identifying the common types of errors made by the parser and suggesting potential improvements to address them?
2.  What is the computational cost of BSP compared to the conversion-based approaches? Is the performance gain worth the increased computational complexity?
3.  Have you explored different transition systems or feature representations beyond those presented in the paper? If so, what were the results?
4.  How does the performance of BSP vary across different sub-types of UCCA structures (e.g., coordination structures, discontinuous units)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of the potential applications of BSS parsing and how it might enable new capabilities in areas like machine translation or information extraction.
*   The references are comprehensive and well-organized.
*   The conversion to dependency format seems particularly lossy. Perhaps exploring conversion to a more expressive dependency variant (e.g. a higher-order dependency format) would yield better baselines.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. While the performance gains over baselines are modest, the authors have successfully demonstrated the feasibility of parsing BSS using a transition-based approach. The thorough evaluation, clear presentation, and commitment to reproducibility make this a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, a more detailed error analysis, a comparison to relevant state-of-the-art semantic parsers (even via conversion), and consideration of a more nuanced evaluation metric would significantly strengthen the paper.
