This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically targeting the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors identify a gap in existing parsers’ ability to handle multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units—properties crucial for comprehensive semantic representation. They explore both conversion-based approaches (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel, direct parsing strategy with BSP.

The primary contribution of this work lies in being the first to propose a transition-based parsing method specifically designed for BSS as defined by the UCCA scheme. While transition-based parsing is established for syntactic and semantic tasks, applying it to the complexities of UCCA, with its DAG structure and non-terminal nodes, is a non-trivial extension. The defined transition set, incorporating novel transitions for remote edges, and the feature engineering tailored for UCCA, represent a substantial effort. A secondary contribution is the empirical evaluation of conversion-based parsing as a baseline and diagnostic tool.  Demonstrating the information loss inherent in converting BSS to simpler representations provides valuable insight into the challenges of UCCA parsing. Finally, the public release of code and data (upon publication) is a beneficial contribution to the research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a critical gap:** The paper tackles a clear and important problem: the lack of parsers capable of handling the full expressive power of BSS, as exemplified by UCCA. Existing parsers often fall short in representing complex semantic relationships accurately.
2. **Novel transition system:** The proposed transition set is thoughtfully designed to accommodate the core properties of BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuities).  The introduction of `LEFTREMOTEX` and `RIGHTREMOTEX` transitions is particularly relevant and demonstrates an understanding of the specific challenges of UCCA parsing.
3. **Comprehensive experimental setup:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation, including both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments, conversion-based baselines, and ablation studies.  This allows for a nuanced assessment of the proposed approach and provides insights into the importance of different features.
4. **Clear and detailed methodology:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of the BSP architecture, feature set, and training procedure.  This makes the work reproducible and facilitates further research. The inclusion of algorithms for conversion procedures is also helpful.
5. **Strong baseline and comparison:** The conversion-based parsing experiments, alongside the comparison to existing parsers (UPARSE, MaltParser, LSTM), provide a solid baseline for evaluating the performance of BSP and understanding the benefits of directly modeling BSS.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited performance gain over baselines:** While BSP demonstrates the feasibility of directly parsing BSS, the performance gains over strong baseline parsers (especially the LSTM parser after dependency conversion) are modest. The F-score difference, while statistically significant, is not dramatic.  This raises questions about the effectiveness of the current feature set and transition system.
2. **Lack of detailed error analysis:** The paper presents quantitative results but lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Understanding *where* BSP struggles (e.g., specific types of remote dependencies, coordination structures) would be valuable for guiding future improvements.
3. **Potential for overly complex features:** The feature set, while comprehensive, appears somewhat complex. It's unclear if all features are equally important, and simplifying the feature set might improve generalization and reduce overfitting.  The ablation study helps, but a more targeted analysis of individual feature contributions would be beneficial.
4. **Reliance on existing parsers for conversion baselines:** The performance of the conversion-based baselines depends heavily on the quality of the underlying parsers (UPARSE, MaltParser, LSTM).  If these parsers are not optimal for the specific characteristics of UCCA, the results might be misleading.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting specific error types made by BSP and comparing them to the errors made by the baseline parsers?
2. What is the computational cost of BSP compared to the conversion-based approaches, particularly given the increased complexity of the transition system and feature set?
3. You mention applying a beam search during inference could further improve results. Have you experimented with beam search, and if so, what impact did it have on performance?
4. The ratio feature seems novel. Can you elaborate on the intuition behind this feature and provide a more detailed analysis of its impact on performance?

**Additional Comments:**

- The notation used throughout the paper is generally clear, but some of the symbols (e.g., `h∗`) could benefit from more explicit definitions in the main text.
- Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the UCCA annotation scheme itself.  Are there any inherent ambiguities or inconsistencies in the scheme that might affect parsing performance?
-  The paper could benefit from a visualization of a UCCA graph and the corresponding BSP parse tree to help readers better understand the relationships between the two representations.
