## Review for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to parsing Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation designed to capture complex structures like multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. The authors propose a transition-based parser (BSP) capable of handling these complexities, alongside conversion-based methods leveraging existing parsers for simpler representations. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the English UCCA corpus, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First Transition-Based Parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures:** The primary contribution is the development of BSP, the first parser specifically designed for UCCA and capable of handling its key features. This provides a crucial tool for working with this rich semantic representation.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of UCCA Parsing:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation of both a direct parsing approach (BSP) and conversion-based methods, establishing a baseline and demonstrating the viability of UCCA parsing.
3. **Insightful Exploration of Conversion Strategies:**  The investigation of converting UCCA structures to dependency and constituency trees offers valuable insights into the information loss inherent in these simplifications, and provides a useful comparative framework.


**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Gap:** The paper tackles a significant problem: the lack of parsers for UCCA, a theoretically strong but practically under-served semantic representation. Providing a parsing solution is essential for wider adoption and application of UCCA.
2. **Solid Technical Approach:** The adoption of a transition-based framework is well-justified, building upon successful paradigms in both syntactic and semantic parsing. The extensions to handle UCCA’s specific requirements (remote edges, non-terminals) appear sensible and technically sound. 
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is well-designed, including in-domain and out-of-domain testing, and comparison to conversion-based baselines.  The detailed analysis of feature ablation is particularly valuable for understanding the factors contributing to performance.
4. **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details. The promise of public release of code and data is a significant strength, fostering reproducibility and further research.
5. **Thoughtful Exploration of Trade-offs:** The conversion-based experiments effectively highlight the information loss involved in simplifying UCCA, justifying the need for a dedicated, broad-coverage parser.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains Over Baselines:** While the 70% F-score is a good starting point, the performance gain of BSP over the conversion-based methods (especially the LSTM parser applied *after* conversion) is relatively modest. This raises questions about the effectiveness of the novel UCCA-specific features and the overall design of the transition system.  More significant performance improvements are needed to firmly establish the superiority of direct UCCA parsing.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the errors made by BSP. Understanding the types of errors (e.g., incorrect remote edge attachments, difficulties with coordination structures) would provide crucial insights for future improvement.  Where does BSP struggle, and why?
3. **Simplified Evaluation Metric:** The proposed evaluation metric, while straightforward, is relatively coarse-grained. It doesn't fully capture the nuances of UCCA structure. Considering more sophisticated evaluation measures that assess the quality of semantic relations, or the completeness of the semantic graph, would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Exploration of Advanced Techniques:**  The use of a linear classifier in BSP seems somewhat dated.  Exploring more powerful classifiers (e.g., neural networks, including the LSTM approach successfully used after conversion) could potentially yield substantial improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of BSP's performance? Specifically, what types of errors are most frequent, and what features seem to be most crucial for correcting them?
2.  The LSTM parser, when applied after dependency conversion, outperforms BSP.  Have you investigated incorporating LSTM layers into the BSP framework itself? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the primary challenges in doing so?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of BSP to the MINUPDATE parameter?  Have you explored a wider range of values for this parameter, and what impact did they have on performance and training time?
4.  The paper mentions the potential for using this framework with other languages.  Have you experimented with any other UCCA-annotated corpora, and what challenges did you encounter?




**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be helpful to include a more visual example of a UCCA structure alongside its dependency/constituency conversion, highlighting the information loss.
*   The discussion of related work is good, but could be expanded to include more recent advances in semantic parsing with graph-based neural networks.
*   Clarify whether the ratio feature is normalized in any way (e.g. by corpus size).



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing, providing the first dedicated parser for UCCA.  While the performance gains over baselines are modest, the work is well-motivated, technically sound, and provides a solid foundation for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, and a commitment to publicly releasing the code and data.
