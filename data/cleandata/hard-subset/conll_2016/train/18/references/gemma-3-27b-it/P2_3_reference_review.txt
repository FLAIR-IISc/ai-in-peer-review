## Review for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation scheme supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – characteristics lacking in existing parsers. The authors explore both conversion-based parsing (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel, direct transition-based approach. They achieve around 70% labeled F-score on in-domain data and demonstrate reasonable performance on out-of-domain data, releasing their code for reproducibility.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First Transition-Based Parser for BSS/UCCA:** The primary contribution is the development and evaluation of the first parser specifically designed to handle the complexities of broad-coverage semantic structures as represented in UCCA.
2. **Empirical Exploration of Conversion-Based Parsing:** The paper provides a useful empirical analysis of the effectiveness (and limitations) of converting UCCA structures to simpler, more established representations (constituency and dependency trees) for parsing with existing tools.
3. **Baseline for Future Research:** This work establishes a solid baseline for future research on UCCA parsing, providing a functional parser and a detailed experimental setup.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Gap:**  The paper tackles a real gap in semantic parsing – the lack of parsers capable of handling the structural complexities needed for broad-coverage semantic representation like UCCA.  Existing tools often force compromises that limit expressive power.
2. **Comprehensive Approach:** The authors explore both conversion-based and direct parsing methods, demonstrating a thorough investigation of the problem.  The conversion experiments provide valuable insights into information loss and the limitations of existing parsers when applied to UCCA.
3. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is well-defined, using appropriate evaluation metrics (even defining new ones for BSS evaluation) and including both in-domain and out-of-domain testing. The inclusion of ablation studies further strengthens the evaluation.
4. **Code Release:**  Making the code publicly available significantly enhances the reproducibility and impact of the research. This is crucial for fostering further work in this area.
5. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed approach, the UCCA scheme, and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains over Conversion Methods:** While BSP achieves comparable results to parsers using converted structures (specifically regarding primary edges), the gains aren't substantial.  This raises questions about the added complexity of the direct parsing approach. The paper acknowledges the LSTM potential, but a comparison *within* the direct parsing framework against a more sophisticated classifier is missing. This is the primary weakness.
2. **Limited Exploration of Remote Edge Prediction:**  The performance on remote edges is significantly lower.  While the authors acknowledge this, more investigation into specific features or transition designs tailored to capturing remote dependencies would be beneficial.  The observation that removing remote edge transitions doesn’t dramatically worsen primary edge performance is concerning – it suggests the model isn’t effectively learning to predict them.
3. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metrics (precision/recall on edge labels) are a reasonable starting point, but may not fully capture the quality of the parsed semantic structures.  A more holistic evaluation that considers the overall graph structure or downstream application performance would be valuable.
4. **Feature Engineering Focus:** While the feature set is extensive, it relies heavily on hand-engineered features. Exploring learned feature representations (e.g., using neural networks) could potentially improve performance and reduce reliance on domain expertise.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the potential for improvements with an LSTM-based classifier.  Could you elaborate on the planned implementation and expected performance gains?  Would you be able to include preliminary results with an LSTM-based classifier in the author response?
2.  The results indicate that predicting remote edges is significantly harder.  What specific challenges do you believe contribute to this difficulty?  Have you experimented with any specialized features or transition designs to address this issue?
3.  Could you discuss the limitations of your evaluation metric, and potential alternative metrics that might provide a more comprehensive assessment of parsing quality?
4.  The ratio feature appears to be helpful. Could you explain the intuition behind this feature, and why you believe it improves performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-curated and demonstrate a good understanding of the related work.
*   The diagrams illustrating the UCCA structures are very helpful for understanding the key concepts.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. While the performance gains over conversion-based methods are modest, the development of the first transition-based parser for UCCA and the thorough experimental evaluation make this a worthwhile submission.  Addressing the weaknesses regarding remote edge prediction and classifier sophistication would further strengthen the work. **I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.**
