This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors identify a gap in existing parsers' ability to handle multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features deemed crucial for comprehensive semantic representation. They explore both conversion-based parsing (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel, direct BSS parser (BSP).

The primary contribution is the implementation and evaluation of BSP, the first parser explicitly designed for BSS, demonstrating reasonable performance (around 62-63% labeled F-score on primary edges) and a framework for future work. A secondary contribution is the exploration of conversion-based approaches, providing a baseline and revealing the information loss inherent in reducing BSS to simpler formalisms. A third, more minor contribution, is the release of code and resources, though this is stated as future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a clear gap:** The paper correctly identifies the limitations of existing parsers with respect to BSS characteristics. UCCA’s expressive power necessitates a dedicated parsing approach, and the authors accurately frame this need.
2. **Sound methodological approach:** The transition-based parsing paradigm is well-established and justifiable, providing a solid foundation for the proposed parser. The exploration of conversion methods provides a valuable comparative element and highlights the trade-offs involved.
3. **Empirical evaluation:** The authors provide experimental results on both in-domain and out-of-domain data, demonstrating some degree of robustness. The evaluation metrics, while admittedly ad-hoc, are clearly defined and appropriate given the novelty of the task. The ablation study offers some insights into feature importance.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts of BSS and UCCA, making it accessible to readers unfamiliar with these frameworks. The figures illustrating the semantic representation are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the parsing method:** While the combination of features and transitions is adapted for BSS, the core transition-based approach itself is not novel. The paper leans heavily on established techniques from dependency and constituency parsing, with relatively minor modifications. The claim of "first parser" is somewhat misleading; it’s the first *dedicated* parser, but builds heavily on prior work.
2. **Performance is modest:** The reported F-scores, while promising for a first attempt, are not exceptionally high, especially compared to state-of-the-art results in related parsing tasks (e.g., dependency parsing).  The gap between BSP and the LSTM-based conversion method is substantial, suggesting significant room for improvement.
3. **Insufficient comparison to strong baselines:** The comparison to conversion-based methods is useful, but the parsers used for conversion (MaltParser, UPARSE) are not the strongest available. The authors acknowledge the potential of an LSTM-based approach for BSP, but do not implement it, leaving a crucial comparison missing. Evaluating against a more sophisticated baseline would better contextualize BSP’s performance.
4. **Limited analysis of errors:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the parser struggles (e.g., specific UCCA categories, sentence structures) is critical for guiding future development. The brief mention of difficulty predicting remote edges is insufficient.
5. **Lack of statistical significance testing:** Results are presented without statistical significance testing, making it difficult to assess the reliability of the observed differences.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the computational complexity of BSP compared to the conversion-based approaches?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting common error patterns and the UCCA categories where the parser performs poorly?
3.  Why was an LSTM-based implementation of BSP not included in the evaluation? Given the significant performance gap with the conversion-based LSTM parser, is this a planned future direction?
4.  You mention that "ratio" is a novel feature. Could you elaborate on the intuition behind this feature and explain why it’s particularly beneficial for BSS parsing?
5.  How sensitive is BSP to the MINUPDATE parameter during training? Was this parameter optimized on the development set?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper’s reliance on the averaged structured perceptron algorithm feels somewhat dated given the prevalence of deep learning methods in parsing. A more modern training approach could potentially yield substantial improvements. The authors should clarify the criteria for selecting the “best” headword when applying features based on head words, as this choice can significantly impact performance.
