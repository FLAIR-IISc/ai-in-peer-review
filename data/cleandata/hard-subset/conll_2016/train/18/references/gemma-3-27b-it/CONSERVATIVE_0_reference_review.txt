This paper presents a transition-based parser for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a semantic representation scheme supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features absent in existing parsers. The authors explore both conversion-based parsing using existing technology and a novel, dedicated transition-based parser (BSP). The primary claimed contribution is the first parser capable of handling the complexities of BSS as defined by the UCCA annotation scheme, achieving around 70% labeled F-score. A secondary contribution is the exploration of conversion-based parsing as a baseline and diagnostic tool. Finally, the authors contribute a publicly available implementation of their parser and converters.

I believe the core contribution lies in the attempt to tackle a genuinely challenging parsing problem—handling the complexities of UCCA’s BSS. The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing parsers and motivates the need for a parser capable of representing richer semantic structures. The experimental setup, while limited, is reasonable and includes out-of-domain evaluation. The public release of code is a valuable addition to the community.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. First, the performance, while presented as promising, is not convincingly demonstrated as state-of-the-art. The comparison to conversion-based approaches is useful but these approaches themselves don’t represent the ceiling on performance—an LSTM-based parser substantially outperforms them. A more rigorous comparison against strong baselines specifically tailored for similar semantic parsing tasks (e.g., AMR parsing with transition-based methods) is missing. The authors acknowledge this and suggest LSTM integration, but this is merely a future direction, not part of the present work.

Second, the novelty of the transition system itself appears incremental. The authors build upon existing transition systems for discontinuous parsing and dependency parsing, adding transitions for remote edges. While this is a logical extension, the paper lacks a detailed justification for *why* these specific transitions are crucial for BSS parsing and how they differ in principle from existing approaches. The feature set also seems largely borrowed from prior work with only a single novel feature (ratio), whose impact isn't thoroughly analyzed.

Third, the evaluation metrics are limited. While the proposed precision/recall/F-score are reasonable, they only focus on edge-level accuracy. A more nuanced evaluation considering the correctness of entire semantic subgraphs or the impact of parsing errors on downstream applications would be more informative. The evaluation does not explore error analysis beyond simply reporting the F-score. What types of errors are most common? Are remote edges particularly problematic?

Finally, the connection to linguistic theory, while present in the introduction, feels underdeveloped throughout the paper. The paper introduces UCCA as a theoretically motivated representation but doesn't deeply engage with the implications of UCCA for parsing methodology. How does the theoretical foundation of UCCA guide the design of the parser? 

Here are some questions for the authors:

1.  The LSTM parser significantly outperforms BSP. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* this is the case? What specific limitations of the transition-based approach are being exploited by the LSTM?
2.  The “ratio” feature is presented as novel. Please provide a more detailed ablation study demonstrating its impact on performance, and discuss the intuition behind why it improves parsing accuracy.
3.  Can you provide an error analysis of the BSP parser? What are the most common types of errors, and how do they relate to the specific challenges of BSS parsing (e.g., remote edges, discontinuous units)?
4.  How does the UCCA framework specifically inform the design choices of your transition system and feature set? Could other semantic frameworks benefit from the same techniques?



Additional Comments:

The formatting of the tables is somewhat inconsistent. The conversion procedures are described with algorithmic detail, which is good, but the algorithms themselves are not central contributions and could be relegated to the appendix. Consider a more detailed discussion of related work on discontinuous parsing and its relevance to UCCA.
