This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically targeting the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors identify a gap in existing parsers, as UCCA supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, features not simultaneously present in other parsers. They propose a novel parser (BSP) and also explore conversion-based approaches, leveraging existing technology for constituency and dependency parsing. The primary contribution lies in the first attempt to build a dedicated parser for BSS, demonstrating a functional system and providing a baseline for future research. A secondary contribution is the exploration of conversion-based parsing, offering insights into the information loss associated with simplifying BSS into more restrictive formalisms. Finally, the publicly available code and dataset will be valuable resources for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Significant Gap:** The paper tackles a genuinely underexplored problem. Existing semantic parsing frameworks often compromise on expressiveness to simplify parsing. UCCA provides a rich representation, but lacked a dedicated parser, making this work timely and relevant.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The authors adopt a well-established transition-based parsing approach, building on recent advances in the field. This choice is justified by the structural similarities between dependency schemes and the distinctions represented in UCCA. The exploration of both direct parsing (BSP) and conversion-based methods is a strength, providing a comprehensive evaluation.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, with clear evaluation metrics and a comparison to both baseline conversion approaches and a stronger LSTM-based dependency parser. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain results provides a more robust assessment of the parser's performance.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivations behind the work, the challenges of BSS parsing, and the design choices made in the proposed approach. Figures effectively illustrate the structural properties of BSS and the conversion processes.
5. **Resource Contribution:** Making the code and datasets publicly available significantly enhances the impact of the research, facilitating reproducibility and further development in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Performance Gap:** While the BSP parser achieves reasonable performance (around 60-70% F-score), it lags behind the LSTM-based parser, particularly concerning remote edges. This suggests that the proposed features or the transition system may not be fully capturing the complexities of BSS, and there’s substantial room for improvement.
2. **Limited Feature Exploration:** The feature set, while comprehensive, draws heavily from existing parsers. There’s limited exploration of features specifically tailored to the nuances of UCCA, such as features encoding the semantic roles of the UCCA categories. The ablation study does not deeply investigate the impact of individual features or combinations.
3. **Simplified Evaluation Metrics:** The proposed evaluation metrics, while necessary given the lack of established measures, are relatively simplistic. They primarily focus on edge-level accuracy and don’t capture the overall structural quality of the parsed graphs. A more holistic evaluation method, potentially incorporating graph similarity measures, would be beneficial.
4. **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to other parsers is limited. While the LSTM-based dependency parser provides a strong baseline, comparing against more recent and sophisticated semantic parsers, even if they don’t directly support all BSS properties, could provide a more nuanced understanding of BSP’s strengths and weaknesses.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by the parser (e.g., incorrect edge labels, incorrect attachment points, failure to identify discontinuous units) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The LSTM parser significantly outperforms BSP.  Could you elaborate on the specific architectural differences and training details that contribute to this performance gap? What modifications to BSP might yield similar results?
2.  You mention that the ratio feature improves recall on primary edges. Can you provide further intuition on why this feature is effective and whether it has any negative impacts on precision?
3.  The conversion-based approach highlights information loss when simplifying BSS.  Could you quantify this loss more precisely, perhaps by analyzing the types of UCCA structures that are most frequently lost during conversion?
4.  Are there any specific UCCA categories or relations that the parser consistently struggles with? If so, what features or transition rules could be added to address these challenges?
5.  The paper mentions that remote edges are difficult to predict. Is this a general phenomenon, or specific to the dataset, and what modifications can be implemented to solve the problem?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The section on related work provides a useful overview of the landscape of semantic parsing.
*   The description of the transition set and features is clear and well-organized.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing, laying the groundwork for future research on BSS parsing. While the performance of the proposed parser is not yet state-of-the-art, the work is well-motivated, methodologically sound, and accompanied by a valuable resource contribution. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly regarding feature engineering and error analysis, would significantly strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the reviewer's questions.
