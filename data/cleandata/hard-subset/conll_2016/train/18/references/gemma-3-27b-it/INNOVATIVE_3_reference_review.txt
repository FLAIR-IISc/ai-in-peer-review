This paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The core contribution is a novel parser, BSP, capable of handling multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features absent in existing parsers for grounded semantic representations. The authors also explore conversion-based methods as a baseline and a means of leveraging existing parsing technology. A secondary contribution is the public release of the code and resources, facilitating further research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Task:** Addressing BSS parsing is genuinely novel. Existing semantic parsing approaches typically compromise on one or more of the crucial properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuity) that UCCA and thus BSS require. This paper tackles a clear gap in the field, offering a first attempt at a comprehensive solution.
2. **Bold Approach with Transition-Based Parsing:** Choosing a transition-based approach is a smart move. Transition-based methods are efficient and have proven successful in various parsing tasks. Adapting this framework to handle the complexities of BSS represents a significant conceptual step. The extension of existing parsers with new transitions is a pragmatic and promising direction.
3. **Empirical Validation:** Despite the novelty, the authors provide initial empirical results demonstrating the feasibility of their approach. Achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score, given the complexity of the task and lack of prior work, is encouraging. The out-of-domain experimentation adds credibility.
4. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing semantic representations and provides a compelling motivation for BSS and the UCCA scheme. The illustrative examples (Figure 1) effectively highlight the need for the features supported by BSS.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the conversion-based results serve as a baseline, the comparison to state-of-the-art methods *specifically* designed for related tasks (e.g., AMR parsing with strong baselines) is lacking. A more thorough comparison would better position the contribution of BSP. The claim of "comparable" results to MaltParser/UPARSE feels weak without directly addressing the stronger LSTM parser's performance in a similarly tailored manner.
2. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The performance is heavily reliant on feature engineering, with a substantial number of features borrowed from existing parsers. While understandable as a starting point, the paper doesn’t explore the potential for more UCCA-specific or learned features. The ratio feature, while promising, feels somewhat ad-hoc without a strong theoretical justification.
3. **Incomplete Validation:** The experimentation is limited to the English UCCA corpus. Demonstrating the generalizability of the approach to other languages, given UCCA’s cross-linguistic applicability, would significantly strengthen the work. The out-of-domain experiment is a step in the right direction, but further investigation across languages is needed.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metrics, while necessary given the lack of established standards, are relatively simplistic and may not fully capture the quality of the parsed structures. More nuanced evaluation methods could provide a more accurate assessment of BSP's performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by BSP? What types of BSS structures are particularly challenging for the parser?
2. What are the computational costs associated with BSP compared to the conversion-based methods and other state-of-the-art semantic parsers?
3. Do you have any preliminary plans for exploring more UCCA-specific features or learned feature representations?
4. Have you considered incorporating techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing more directly into BSP, given the similarities between the tasks?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The inclusion of the UCCA annotation scheme explanation is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the framework. The decision to release the code is commendable and will undoubtedly benefit the research community. The supplementary material should include a full list of feature templates for clarity.
