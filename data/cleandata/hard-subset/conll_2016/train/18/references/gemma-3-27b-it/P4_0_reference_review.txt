This paper introduces a transition-based parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), aiming to address the limitations of existing parsers in representing complex semantic relationships like multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. The authors propose a novel parser (BSP) and explore conversion-based approaches to leverage existing parsing technology.

The primary contribution of this work is the development and evaluation of BSP, a transition-based parser specifically designed to handle the complexities of UCCA-formatted BSS. This includes extending existing transition systems with new transitions to accommodate multiple parents and discontinuous units, as well as incorporating UCCA-specific features. The reported F-score of around 70% demonstrates the feasibility of this approach. A second significant contribution is the exploration of conversion-based parsing as a baseline and diagnostic tool. Converting BSS to simpler representations (constituency and dependency trees) and using existing parsers provides valuable insights into the challenges of BSS parsing and the information loss inherent in the conversion process. Finally, the provision of the codebase will facilitate future research in the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Parser:** The BSP parser directly addresses the challenges posed by BSS, which demand the capacity to model semantic structures beyond traditional constituency or dependency trees. The tailored transition system and feature set are well-motivated and appear crucial for tackling the complexities of UCCA annotation.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation, including both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments. The inclusion of conversion-based parsing provides a valuable comparison point and highlights the benefits of the direct BSS parsing approach. The analysis of feature ablation further clarifies the importance of different components of the parser.
3. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing semantic parsing methods and provides a compelling motivation for the development of BSS parsing. The introduction effectively explains the three key properties of BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuities) and their importance.
4. **Practical Contribution:** The commitment to releasing the code and models is a significant strength, enabling reproducibility and facilitating further research by the community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While the conversion-based experiments offer a baseline, a more direct comparison to recent state-of-the-art semantic parsers (e.g., those for AMR) – even if requiring adaptation – would strengthen the paper. The comparison primarily focuses on simpler dependency parsers and a constituency parser, which may not fully represent the competitive landscape for semantic parsing.
2. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set, while extensive, appears somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic investigation of feature importance or the exploration of learned feature representations (e.g., using neural networks) could potentially yield further improvements. The reliance on head word features, while justified, seems somewhat at odds with the non-terminal nature of BSS.
3. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, while reasonable given the lack of established standards for BSS evaluation, has limitations. It may not fully capture the nuances of semantic correctness, especially in cases where partial or approximate matches are possible. A more sophisticated evaluation protocol might consider semantic similarity or downstream task performance.
4. **Focus on English:** The work is currently limited to the English language. Investigating the transferability of the proposed approach to other languages, given UCCA’s cross-linguistic applicability, would be a valuable extension.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the specific choice of features, particularly the head word features? How do these features interact with the non-terminal nature of UCCA structures?
2. You mention the potential for using LSTM-based classifiers. Have you conducted preliminary experiments with LSTMs, and if so, what were the results?
3. What are the main sources of error in the BSP parser? Could you provide examples of errors that are particularly challenging for the model?
4. Given the computational cost of the conversion approaches, can you comment on the scalability to larger datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

The figure captions are generally helpful, but could benefit from more detailed explanations of the UCCA labels (e.g., "P," "A," "R"). The discussion of related work is comprehensive, but could be streamlined to focus more on the most relevant approaches.
