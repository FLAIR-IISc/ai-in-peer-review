## Review for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a transition-based parser (BSP) for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – characteristics absent in existing parsers. The authors explore both conversion-based parsing (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel, direct parsing approach with BSP. They achieve competitive results, reaching ~70% labeled F-score in-domain and demonstrating reasonable performance out-of-domain.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **First Transition-Based Parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures:** The primary contribution is a functional parser designed specifically for UCCA, tackling the challenges of its richer structure compared to simpler dependency or constituency representations. This provides a baseline and a starting point for future work in this area.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of Conversion-Based Approaches:** The exploration of converting UCCA to simpler representations for parsing provides useful insights into the information loss and potential benefits of these approaches. The quantitative results demonstrate the limitations of relying solely on existing, more constrained parsers.
3. **Identification of Key Features for BSS Parsing:** The feature analysis highlights the importance of both established features and a novel ratio feature for achieving good performance, offering guidance for future model development.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Significant Gap:** The paper tackles a crucial gap in semantic parsing: the lack of parsers capable of handling complex, broad-coverage structures like those found in UCCA. This is a well-motivated and important problem.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The authors employ a reasonable experimental setup, including both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation, and comparison against conversion-based baselines. The use of existing parsers (MaltParser, UPARSE, LSTM) as baselines is sensible.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the motivations and challenges, and the methodology is explained in a straightforward manner.  Figure 1 effectively illustrates the key characteristics of BSS.
4. **Reproducibility:** The commitment to making code publicly available is a strong point, fostering reproducibility and further research.
5. **Insightful Feature Analysis:** The ablation study (Table 3) provides valuable information about the relative importance of different feature sets, guiding future improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gain over Baselines:** While the BSP parser achieves competitive results, the performance gain over the conversion-based approaches (especially the LSTM parser) is not substantial. This raises questions about the advantages of the direct parsing approach over leveraging existing, well-optimized parsers after conversion. The performance of 70% F-score is respectable, but leaves room for significant improvement.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the parser fails (e.g., specific types of discontinuities, coordination structures) would be crucial for identifying avenues for improvement.  Qualitative examples would be beneficial.
3. **Simplifications in Conversion Procedures:** The conversion to dependency trees seems overly simplistic. More sophisticated head percolation or graph transformation techniques might lead to better conversion results and, consequently, better performance of the downstream parsers. The lack of comparison with more complex conversion methods is a missed opportunity.
4. **Limited Exploration of Transition System:** The transition system itself could benefit from further exploration.  Investigating alternative transition operations specifically designed to handle the complexities of UCCA could potentially improve performance. The choice of transitions appears relatively standard.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a detailed error analysis, including examples of common errors made by the BSP parser? This would help understand the remaining challenges in BSS parsing.
2. **LSTM Performance:** The LSTM parser significantly outperforms BSP.  Can you elaborate on the differences in feature sets and training procedures that might account for this performance gap?  Could some of the LSTM features be incorporated into BSP?
3. **Conversion Refinement:** Did you explore more sophisticated methods for converting UCCA structures to dependency trees? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
4. **Transition System Exploration:** Were alternative transition systems considered during the development of BSP?  If so, what were the trade-offs that led to the final choice?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The notation is generally clear, although some definitions could be slightly more precise.
*   Consider providing a visual example of a predicted parse alongside a gold-standard parse to illustrate the types of errors the parser makes.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing by introducing the first transition-based parser for broad-coverage semantic structures. While the performance gains over conversion-based approaches are modest, the work establishes a solid baseline and identifies important research directions. The paper is well-written and clearly presented. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the need for a more detailed error analysis and a discussion of potential improvements to the conversion procedures.
