This paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The primary contribution is the first parser capable of handling the complexities of BSS – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – simultaneously. A secondary contribution lies in the exploration of conversion-based parsing, using constituency and dependency trees as intermediate representations to leverage existing parsing technology, alongside the novel transition system. Finally, the authors release both code and a new evaluation setup for UCCA parsing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Problem & Approach:** The core idea – tackling full BSS parsing – is genuinely novel. Existing parsers fall short in supporting the complete set of required features. The transition-based approach, while building on established techniques, represents a courageous attempt to directly address the problem instead of relying on simplification or approximation. The inherent difficulty of the task makes this a valuable endeavor.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set & Transition System:** The extension of standard transition-based parsing with specific transitions to handle UCCA’s unique requirements (remote edges, discontinuities) demonstrates insightful design. The inclusion of the ratio feature appears to be a particularly interesting attempt at regularization, and the analysis of feature ablation (presented in the paper, though not central to the core argument) suggests a well-considered feature engineering process.
3. **Practical Contribution: Code & Evaluation:** Making the code and resources publicly available is a significant positive.  The establishment of a baseline and evaluation setup for UCCA parsing is itself a valuable contribution to the community, providing a foundation for future work.
4. **Robustness:** The relatively consistent performance between the in-domain and out-of-domain experiments indicates a degree of robustness in the proposed method, which is encouraging for a novel parsing approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Performance Gap:** While the approach *works*, the F-score (around 62-69%) on primary edges, while respectable, is not groundbreaking. The LSTM baseline demonstrates that there’s significant room for improvement, and the current implementation doesn't yet surpass readily available, albeit less directly applicable, parsers by a large margin. This suggests the current feature set and transition system might not be fully exploiting the potential of the approach.
2. **Limited Analysis:** The ablation study is brief, and there's a lack of detailed error analysis.  Understanding *where* the parser fails – specific UCCA structures it struggles with, common error types – would be invaluable for guiding future improvements.  The paper states that the remote edge transitions do not improve performance substantially, but deeper analysis into why this is the case is missing.
3. **Conversion-Based Parsing Limited Scope:**  The conversion-based approach feels somewhat perfunctory. While it’s useful to establish a lower bound, the analysis doesn't fully explore the information loss inherent in the conversions or provide strong insights into the relative difficulty of different UCCA features. It’s more of a proof-of-concept than a significant exploration.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the most frequent types of errors made by BSP? Specifically, what UCCA structures (e.g., complex coordination, deeply nested scenes) consistently lead to errors?
2. The LSTM baseline significantly outperforms BSP. What specific aspects of the LSTM architecture or training procedure do you believe contribute to this performance difference? Have you explored incorporating LSTM layers *within* the transition-based framework of BSP?
3.  You mention the ratio feature as a regularization technique. Can you elaborate on the intuition behind this feature, and provide further evidence (beyond the ablation study) of its effectiveness?
4.  The paper briefly mentions implicit units. How do you envision extending BSP to handle these units, and what new challenges would this introduce?

**Additional Comments:**

The introduction provides a good overview of the motivation and challenges of BSS parsing. The related work section is comprehensive. While the presentation could be more concise in places, the paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details. The figures are helpful.



I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the performance isn't state-of-the-art, the novelty of the problem tackled, the boldness of the approach, and the practical contribution of code and an evaluation setup outweigh the current performance limitations. This work lays a crucial foundation for future research in broad-coverage semantic parsing and offers a promising direction for handling complex semantic structures. I am willing to overlook the relatively modest performance gains given the inherent difficulty of the task and the innovative nature of the solution.
