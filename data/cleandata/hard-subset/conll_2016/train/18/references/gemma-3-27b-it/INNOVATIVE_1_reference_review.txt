This paper tackles the challenging problem of broad-coverage semantic parsing, specifically focusing on parsing into the UCCA framework, which allows for multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – features lacking in many existing parsers. The authors propose a transition-based approach, acknowledging the absence of a dedicated UCCA parser and the novelty of their work in this area. They explore both conversion-based methods (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP).

The primary contribution is the initial exploration of transition-based parsing for UCCA, a genuinely novel direction given the complexity of the representation. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation of conversion-based methods, providing a baseline and insight into information loss during the conversion process. Finally, the publicly releasing of code and data (stated intention) will be a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Boldness:** The core idea of tackling UCCA parsing with a transition-based approach is highly commendable. UCCA is a rich representation, and attempting a direct parse is a significant step beyond simply adapting existing tools through conversion. This represents a bold move toward a more comprehensive semantic parsing solution.
2. **Comprehensive Representation:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing semantic parsing frameworks and convincingly motivate the need for broad-coverage structures as captured by UCCA. Their focus on multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities is crucial for representing complex semantic relationships.
3. **Thorough Exploration:** The paper doesn’t rely solely on the novel parser. The investigation of conversion-based approaches provides a valuable comparison point and reveals the challenges inherent in shoehorning UCCA into more restrictive formalisms.
4. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of both in-domain and out-of-domain datasets, along with clear evaluation metrics, strengthens the findings. Reporting ablation studies (even briefly) adds credibility.
5. **Potential Impact:** A working UCCA parser has the potential to impact a range of NLP applications, including machine translation, information extraction, and dialogue systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains over Conversions:** While the direct parser (BSP) achieves comparable primary edge F-scores to the conversion-based methods, the lack of substantial improvement is a concern. The reported 62.4% F-score, while respectable, isn't a dramatic leap, particularly considering the added complexity of the direct approach.
2. **Sparse Remote Edge Prediction:** The relatively low F-score on remote edges (15.3%) suggests that the model struggles to capture these crucial aspects of UCCA structure. This is a significant limitation, as remote edges are a defining characteristic of the representation. The improvement with the "ratio" feature is a step in the right direction, but further work is needed.
3. **Lack of Detailed Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth error analysis. Identifying the types of errors made by BSP (e.g., incorrect attachment, missed edges) would provide valuable insights for future development.
4. **Limited Scope of Ablation:** The ablation study is brief. More comprehensive ablation studies examining the contribution of individual features and transition types would provide a clearer understanding of the model’s behavior.
5. **Classifier Choice:** The reliance on a linear classifier seems potentially limiting. Given the success of LSTMs in other parsing tasks, the authors should explore incorporating a more powerful classifier as suggested by their analysis of the LSTM parser performance with the converted trees.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically focusing on the types of errors made in predicting remote edges?
2.  What are the computational costs associated with the novel transition types introduced in BSP, and are there any opportunities for optimization?
3.  Given the success of the LSTM parser with converted trees, what are the main challenges in adapting this approach directly to UCCA parsing within the BSP framework?
4.  You mention the use of a dynamic oracle. Have you explored other oracle strategies, such as a static oracle, and if so, how did they affect performance?



**Additional Comments:** The paper’s strength lies in its ambition and exploration of a largely uncharted territory. Despite the relatively modest performance gains, the work represents a valuable first step towards a truly broad-coverage semantic parser. The potential impact is significant, and the authors’ willingness to publicly release their code and data is commendable. I lean towards acceptance, prioritizing the novelty and direction-setting nature of this work.
