## Peer Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA), a semantic representation scheme supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units—characteristics lacking in existing parsers. The authors explore both conversion-based parsing (to constituency and dependency trees) and a novel, direct parsing approach with a transition system extended to handle UCCA’s complexities. The results demonstrate competitive performance on English UCCA data, achieving around 70% labeled F-score, and suggest avenues for improvement.

**Main Contributions (as claimed by authors, ordered by strength):**

1.  **First Transition-Based Parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS):** The authors present the first parser designed to directly handle UCCA, a BSS scheme with challenging structural properties.
2.  **Exploration of Conversion-Based Parsing:** The paper investigates leveraging existing parsing technology through conversion to more constrained formalisms (dependency and constituency trees), providing a baseline and insights into the feasibility of this approach.
3.  **Novel Transition System and Features:** BSP introduces new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features tailored for UCCA’s unique characteristics, extending existing transition-based parsing techniques.

**Contributions (as I see them, ordered by strength):**

1.  **Demonstration of Feasibility of Direct UCCA Parsing:** The core contribution is showing that direct parsing of UCCA structures *is* feasible with a transition-based approach, opening the door for further research.  The success, even at 70% F1, is significant given the complexity of the representation.
2.  **Comprehensive Exploration of Parsing Strategies:** The paper provides a solid comparative study of both conversion-based and direct parsing strategies, offering valuable insights into their strengths and limitations in the context of BSS.
3.  **Establishment of a Baseline:** The work establishes a baseline for UCCA parsing that can be used for comparison with future work.  The publicly available code is a very positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addressing a Significant Gap:** The paper tackles a genuinely important problem—parsing semantic representations that go beyond traditional dependency or constituency structures. The lack of parsers for UCCA makes this work especially valuable.
2.  **Sound Methodology:** The combination of conversion-based and direct parsing approaches provides a strong empirical foundation. Exploring both strategies allows for a nuanced understanding of the challenges.
3.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the UCCA formalism, the proposed transition system, and the experimental setup. The figures illustrating the UCCA structures are very helpful.
4. **Robustness Demonstration:** The results on the out-of-domain data indicate a reasonable degree of robustness to domain shift.
5.  **Open Source Commitment:** Making the code publicly available is a crucial contribution for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Performance Improvement with Novel Transitions:** While the novel transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) are a logical extension, the performance gains from including them are relatively modest (15.3% F-score on remote edges), raising questions about their effectiveness and whether further refinements are needed.  The paper doesn't deeply analyze *where* these transitions fail.
2.  **Feature Engineering – Potential for Improvement:** The feature set, while drawing from established techniques, appears somewhat conservative. The ablation study shows individual features are helpful, but the combination might not be optimal. Exploring more sophisticated features (e.g., incorporating word embeddings, richer contextual information) could significantly improve performance.
3.  **Evaluation Metric Limitations:**  The proposed evaluation metric, while understandable given the lack of standard measures for BSS, is relatively simple and doesn't fully capture the complexity of UCCA structures. A more fine-grained evaluation, potentially considering different edge types or sub-graph structures, would provide a more comprehensive assessment.
4.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to conversion-based parsers is useful, a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art semantic parsers (even those operating on different representations like AMR) would provide a stronger context for the achieved results.  The LSTM result *is* a good comparison, but could be explored further.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis of the BSP parser, specifically identifying common errors and the types of UCCA structures that are most challenging to parse?
2.  Have you experimented with more sophisticated feature engineering techniques (e.g., word embeddings, contextual features) beyond the ones presented in the paper? If so, what were the results?
3.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the proposed evaluation metric and discuss potential alternatives for a more comprehensive assessment of UCCA parsing performance?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of MINUPDATE=5 and IMPORTANCE=2? Did you try different values and if so, how did they affect performance?
5.  Why did you choose a greedy inference approach? Would beam search improve performance and is it computationally feasible?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between UCCA and other semantic representations like AMR and SDP.
*   The notation is generally clear but could be slightly improved for consistency.




Overall, this is a valuable contribution that addresses an important challenge in semantic parsing. While there are areas for improvement, the work demonstrates the feasibility of directly parsing complex semantic structures and establishes a solid baseline for future research.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses highlighted above.
