**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces the task of Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), aiming to parse natural language into semantic representations that simultaneously support three complex structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents. Using the UCCA formalism as a testbed, the authors present the first parsing solutions for such a representation. The work follows two complementary strategies: first, it establishes strong baselines by converting UCCA structures into simpler formalisms (dependency and constituency trees) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers. Second, it proposes BSP, a novel transition-based parser specifically designed to directly generate BSS graphs by integrating techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Pioneering a New Parsing Task:** The most significant contribution is the formulation and first-ever attempt at solving the BSS parsing problem. By tackling UCCA, the authors address a gap in the semantic parsing literature for grounded, structurally rich representations that are arguably more faithful to linguistic reality than simpler tree-based schemes. This work effectively opens up a new and challenging research direction.
2.  **A Novel, Direct Parser for Complex Semantic Graphs:** The paper presents BSP, a coherent and principled transition-based system capable of handling the combined challenges of BSS. While its components draw from prior work, its synthesis into a single model that can produce grounded DAGs with non-terminal nodes and discontinuities is a substantial and novel architectural contribution.
3.  **A Rigorous Baseline Evaluation:** The conversion-based approach is a methodologically sound and insightful way to benchmark the new task. It not only provides strong performance numbers to contextualize the results of the novel parser but also serves as an excellent analysis of the limitations of existing parsing technology when faced with more complex graph structures.

**Strengths**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Ambition:** This paper is a breath of fresh air. Instead of offering an incremental improvement on a well-trodden task, it bravely charts a new course. The problem of parsing directly into representations as complex as UCCA has been largely unaddressed, and this paper provides the first comprehensive treatment. This kind of ambitious, foundational work is crucial for advancing the field beyond its current paradigms.
2.  **A Well-Designed and Coherent System:** The proposed BSP parser is thoughtfully designed. The transition set is a clever combination of mechanisms from different parsing traditions, logically extended to handle the specific requirements of UCCA (e.g., the distinction between `LEFT-EDGE` and `LEFT-REMOTE`). The system is a complete solution that directly addresses the core challenges, rather than relying on complex pipelines or post-processing hacks. The introduction of the `ratio` feature is also a simple but elegant idea for controlling the proliferation of non-terminal nodes.
3.  **Methodologically Sound and Insightful Experiments:** The two-pronged experimental strategy—conversion vs. direct parsing—is a major strength. It allows for a clear-eyed assessment of where things stand. The finding that a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser can achieve high performance on the tree backbone of the structure (after conversion) is an important result in itself. This grounds the work and clearly delineates the specific challenge that the novel parser, BSP, is designed to solve: the prediction of remote edges that create the DAG structure.
4.  **Robustness and Public Contribution:** The evaluation, including both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments, demonstrates the practical viability and robustness of the proposed methods. The authors' commitment to releasing their code and converters is commendable and will be invaluable for fostering future work in this nascent area.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The Performance of the Novel Parser:** The central weakness is that the novel BSP parser is significantly outperformed on primary (tree) edges by the conversion-based approach that uses an LSTM dependency parser (59.9% vs. 69.2% F-score). While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its core performance on the simpler backbone structure is lagging. The authors acknowledge that integrating a more powerful classifier like an LSTM would likely close this gap, but as presented, the paper's main technical contribution is not its best-performing model. For a practitioner wanting to parse UCCA today, the recommendation would be to use the conversion approach, which slightly undermines the case for BSP in its current form.
2.  **Limited Analysis of the Core Challenge:** The key advantage of BSP is its ability to predict remote edges, but this capability is not deeply analyzed. The F-score is low (~13%), which is understandable for a first attempt at a difficult problem, but the paper offers little insight into *why*. An error analysis is missing: Are certain categories of remote edges harder than others? Are the errors due to attachment, labeling, or both? A more detailed analysis of the parser's failures on this front would have been more valuable than, for example, the feature ablation study, and would provide a much clearer roadmap for future improvements.
3.  **Incremental Nature of the Parser's Components:** While the *synthesis* is novel, the individual components of the BSP parser are largely direct adaptations of pre-existing work. The transition set is a combination of known techniques for constituency (Sagae & Lavie, 2005), discontinuities (Maier, 2015), and DAGs (Sagae & Tsujii, 2008), and the feature set is similarly derived. This is a reasonable and effective engineering approach, but it means the paper's technical novelty lies more in system-building than in the invention of fundamentally new parsing algorithms or mechanisms.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance gap between the perceptron-based BSP and the LSTM-based dependency parser is substantial. You rightly suggest that using an LSTM classifier in BSP is a promising path for future work. Could you elaborate on why this was not explored here? Are there non-trivial technical hurdles to integrating stack-LSTMs with the more complex state (non-terminals, graph structure, buffer with non-terminals) required by your system?
2.  The conversion from constituency to dependency (Algorithm 1) relies on a predefined priority order of edge labels to identify heads. How was this order determined, and how sensitive are the dependency conversion upper bound and the final parser performance to this choice?
3.  The `ratio` feature is an interesting idea. Have you performed any analysis to confirm its role as a regularizer? For example, does it primarily penalize `NODE` transitions, and how does its removal affect the total number of non-terminals predicted by the parser?
