### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the task of Broad-coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), which aims to produce grounded semantic representations that simultaneously support three key structural properties: multiple parents (forming DAGs), non-terminal nodes for multi-word units, and discontinuous constituents. The paper proposes the UCCA annotation scheme as a suitable formalism for this task and presents the first parsing approaches for it. Two complementary strategies are explored. The first is a conversion-based approach, which assesses the capability of existing technology by converting UCCA structures into dependency or discontinuous constituency trees, parsing them with state-of-the-art tools, and converting the results back. The second is a novel, direct transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), specifically designed to handle all three BSS properties by extending existing transition systems with new operations for creating remote edges. The methods are evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, where a conversion-based approach using an LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest score on the core tree structure (69.2% F1), while the direct BSP parser is the only method able to predict the full DAG, including remote edges.

### Contributions

1.  **Establishment and Benchmarking of UCCA Parsing:** The paper defines and provides the first empirical results for the task of parsing the UCCA formalism. By evaluating both conversion-based methods using existing parsers and a novel direct parser, it establishes a comprehensive set of initial benchmarks. This provides a valuable foundation and a clear starting point for future research on parsing this and other broad-coverage semantic representations.
2.  **A Direct Transition-Based Parser for Complex Semantic Graphs:** The paper presents BSP, a novel parser that integrates mechanisms for handling non-terminal nodes, discontinuities, and multiple parents within a single transition-based framework. The system is a technically sound combination and extension of prior work on discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing, successfully adapted to the specific requirements of UCCA, such as the distinction between primary and remote edges.
3.  **A Systematic Comparison of Direct vs. Conversion-Based Parsing:** The work provides a well-designed empirical study that directly compares the trade-offs between simplifying a complex graph for existing parsers versus building a specialized parser. The analysis, particularly the calculation of upper bounds for the conversion methods, effectively quantifies the information loss inherent in the conversion approach and highlights the necessity of direct parsing for recovering the full semantic structure.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the need for parsers that can handle the combination of DAG structures, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. It clearly defines the properties of "Broad-coverage Semantic Structures" and correctly identifies UCCA as an appropriate testbed, successfully framing a new and relevant parsing problem for the community.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is comprehensive and well-executed. It compares two fundamentally different strategies (direct vs. conversion), employs multiple strong, state-of-the-art systems for the conversion baselines (including MaltParser, UPARSE, and a stack-LSTM parser), and validates the robustness of the proposed parser on both in-domain and out-of-domain data. The inclusion of upper-bound calculations for the conversion pipelines is a key strength, providing a clear-eyed view of their inherent limitations.
3.  **Technically Sound Parser Design:** The proposed BSP parser is a well-grounded extension of established transition-based parsing techniques. Its transition set is a logical synthesis of operations from parsers for discontinuous constituency and dependency DAGs. The feature set is similarly well-constructed, incorporating standard features from related work alongside novel, task-specific features like the `ratio` regularizer, whose effectiveness is demonstrated in an ablation study.
4.  **Insightful Analysis and Clear Future Directions:** The discussion of the results is insightful. The authors correctly identify that the superior performance of the LSTM-based conversion approach is likely attributable to the power of its classifier rather than the conversion strategy itself. This observation leads to a concrete and promising path for future work: integrating more powerful classifiers like LSTMs into the direct BSP model. The commitment to releasing code and data further strengthens the paper's contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The Proposed Direct Parser is Outperformed on Primary Edges:** The main technical contribution, the BSP parser, achieves an F-score of 59.0% on primary (tree) edges. This is over 10 percentage points lower than the 69.2% F-score achieved by the conversion-based approach that uses an existing LSTM dependency parser. While BSP is unique in its ability to parse remote edges, its significantly weaker performance on the core structure, which constitutes the vast majority of the graph, is a notable limitation of the current work.
2.  **Unbalanced Comparison of Models:** The BSP parser, which uses a linear perceptron classifier and greedy inference, is directly compared with a dependency parser using a much more powerful stack-LSTM classifier. This makes it difficult to isolate the source of the performance gap and fairly assess the merits of the proposed transition system. It is unclear whether BSP's lower score is due to limitations in its transition system and features, or simply the comparative weakness of its classifier and inference algorithm. Including a beam search implementation for BSP would have provided a more direct comparison with UPARSE and strengthened the evaluation.
3.  **Limited Novelty in the Transition System Components:** While the complete BSP system is novel for the task of UCCA parsing, its core mechanisms are adaptations of existing work. The handling of discontinuities with a SWAP transition is adopted from parsers like Maier (2015), and the general strategy of adding edges to form a DAG without immediate reduction follows prior work in dependency DAG parsing (e.g., Sagae and Tsujii, 2008). The primary novelty lies in the synthesis of these components and their specific application to UCCA, rather than in fundamentally new parsing transitions.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap on primary edges between BSP (59.0 F1) and the LSTM dependency parser (69.2 F1) is substantial. Could you provide further analysis on the source of this gap? To what extent do you attribute it to the classifier (perceptron vs. LSTM), the feature representation, or potential limitations of the BSP transition system itself when modeling tree structures?
2.  The inverse conversion from dependency to constituency (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics to reintroduce non-terminal nodes. Given the non-trivial information loss shown by the 88.4 F1 upper bound for this conversion path, could you provide a qualitative analysis of the primary error types introduced by this back-conversion? For instance, does it struggle with specific constructions like coordination, or does it tend to produce overly flat or deep structures?
3.  The `ratio` feature is a novel and effective contribution. Could you offer more intuition on its function? Does it primarily act as a regularizer to prevent the parser from generating an excessive number of non-terminal nodes, and if so, how does this manifest in the final parse structures?
