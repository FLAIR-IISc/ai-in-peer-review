**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces the task of broad-coverage semantic parsing for formalisms that support three key properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents. It focuses on the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of such a formalism. The authors present two main approaches: 1) a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed specifically to produce UCCA-style structures, and 2) a conversion-based approach that transforms UCCA graphs into more restricted formalisms (dependency trees and discontinuous constituency trees) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers. The methods are evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, establishing the first parsing results for this representation.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Pioneering the UCCA Parsing Task:** The most significant contribution is the formalization and establishment of the UCCA parsing task. By developing parsing methodologies, evaluation metrics, and providing the first benchmark results, this paper opens up a new and valuable direction for research in semantic parsing. UCCA's cross-linguistic motivation and rich structural properties make it a compelling target for parsing.
2.  **An Insightful Comparative Analysis via Conversion Baselines:** The authors' decision to create strong baselines by converting UCCA to other formalisms is a major strength. This experimental design provides a clear picture of the task's difficulty and reveals that a state-of-the-art dependency parser with an LSTM-based classifier, even after a lossy conversion, can set a very high performance bar. This finding is itself a valuable contribution, guiding future efforts in this area.
3.  **Development of a Novel, Full-Coverage Parser (BSP):** While its empirical performance is not state-of-the-art, the development of the BSP parser is a solid contribution. It provides a direct, integrated model for parsing BSS structures and is the only method presented that can handle all structural properties of UCCA, including remote edges, within a single framework.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Importance of the Problem:** The paper tackles a novel and well-motivated problem. While semantic parsing is a crowded field, parsing for a grounded, cross-linguistically applicable representation like UCCA that elegantly handles coordination, MWEs, and argument sharing without resorting to bi-lexical dependencies is an important research gap. The introduction clearly lays out the limitations of existing schemes (SDP, AMR) and justifies the need for BSS parsing.
2.  **Strong and Rigorous Experimental Design:** The two-pronged experimental approach is excellent. Comparing a purpose-built parser (BSP) against strong, off-the-shelf parsers adapted via conversion provides a much richer analysis than simply presenting a new model in isolation. The inclusion of multiple dependency parsers, particularly the high-performing LSTM-based parser from Dyer et al. (2015), demonstrates a thorough and up-to-date evaluation. The in-domain and out-of-domain experiments further strengthen the empirical claims.
3.  **Clarity and Methodological Detail:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The transition system for BSP is clearly specified (Figure 2), the feature sets are detailed (Figure 3), and the conversion algorithms are explicitly provided (Algorithms 1 & 2). This level of detail is commendable and crucial for reproducibility and for future work to build upon these methods.
4.  **Valuable Resource Contribution:** The authors' commitment to releasing the parsers and converters is a significant strength. This will lower the barrier to entry for other researchers interested in UCCA parsing and facilitate direct comparisons in future work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The Proposed Novel Method is Substantially Outperformed:** The main weakness is that the central methodological proposal, the BSP parser, is significantly outperformed by one of the conversion-based baselines. The LSTM dependency parser achieves 69.2% F-score on primary edges, a full 9-10 points higher than BSP. While the paper correctly concludes that this points to the promise of using LSTMs, it does undermine the practical utility of BSP in its current form. The paper would be stronger if it were framed less as "we present BSP" and more as "we explore two methods for UCCA parsing and find that a conversion-based approach with modern neural classifiers is currently superior."
2.  **Limited Technical Novelty in the Transition System:** The BSP transition system is a thoughtful combination of techniques from prior work on discontinuous constituency parsing (Maier, 2015), dependency DAG parsing (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008), and standard transition-based constituency parsing. While the application to UCCA is new, the core parsing actions (SHIFT, REDUCE, NODE, SWAP, remote edges) are not fundamentally novel algorithmic contributions. The primary novelty lies in the feature engineering and the specific combination for this new task.
3.  **Underdeveloped Analysis of Remote Edge Prediction:** A key advantage of BSP is its ability to predict remote edges, which the conversion methods cannot. However, performance on this subtask is very low (around 13% F-score), and the paper offers little analysis as to why. Are these edges simply too sparse in the training data? Are there specific linguistic constructions that prove most difficult? Is the feature set inadequate for capturing the long-distance dependencies required for remote edge prediction? A brief error analysis would have added significant value and offered a clearer path forward for improving this aspect of the parser.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance gap between the perceptron-based BSP and the LSTM-based dependency parser is striking. Your conclusion rightly points to this as a promising direction. Could you elaborate on the challenges you foresee in integrating stack-LSTM or similar neural features into the more complex BSP transition system, which handles non-terminal creation and remote edges, compared to a standard arc-standard dependency parser?
2.  The dependency-to-constituency conversion (Algorithm 2) seems to rely on several heuristics, such as the `Label(u)` function and the high-attaching assumption for reconstructing non-terminals. Could you quantify the error introduced by this inverse conversion? Specifically, what is the F-score loss when applying the full dependency conversion round-trip (UCCA -> dep -> UCCA) to the gold data? This would help disentangle parsing errors from conversion errors in the 69.2% F-score result.
3.  Regarding the low performance on remote edges: Can you provide any insight into the types of remote edges that BSP fails to predict? For instance, does it struggle more with argument sharing across verbal predicates versus nominalizations, or are there specific UCCA edge labels that are particularly problematic?
