### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the new task of broad-coverage semantic parsing (BSS), focusing on the UCCA formalism, which uniquely combines three challenging structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors propose and evaluate two main approaches. The first is a conversion-based strategy that maps UCCA structures to more restrictive formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers. The second, and more significant, contribution is a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed from the ground up to directly produce these complex semantic graphs. The work is evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, establishing the first parsing results for this representation.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **Pioneering a new and challenging parsing task.** This is the first work to formalize and attempt to parse a grounded semantic representation that simultaneously handles DAG structures, non-terminals, and discontinuities. This is a bold and important step towards parsing richer and more general semantic structures.
2.  **The design of a novel, direct parser (BSP) for these complex structures.** The paper proposes a new transition system that synthesizes and extends techniques from dependency, constituency, and discontinuous parsing to directly model all properties of BSS. This provides a principled and extensible framework for this new task.
3.  **A strong and insightful comparative analysis.** By benchmarking their novel parser against strong baselines derived from converting the task to more standard parsing problems, the authors provide valuable context for their results and highlight clear avenues for future work.

### Strengths

This is an excellent, forward-looking paper that I am enthusiastic about. Its primary strength lies in its ambition and novelty.

1.  **Tackling a Fundamental Challenge:** The authors are not content with incremental gains on well-trodden ground. Instead, they define and attack a new problem that pushes the boundaries of semantic parsing. Formalisms like UCCA that capture phenomena such as argument sharing ("John" in "After graduation, John moved...") and headless structures ("John and Mary") are clearly important for deep language understanding. By being the first to develop a parser for such a representation, this work opens up a new and promising research direction. This willingness to venture into new territory is precisely what the field needs.

2.  **A Conceptually Elegant Direct Model:** The proposed BSP parser is a novel and well-motivated solution. Rather than relying on complex post-processing or pipeline hacks, it directly models the generation of DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities within a single, unified transition-based framework. The combination of NODE, SWAP, and the new REMOTE-EDGE transitions is a thoughtful synthesis of existing ideas applied to a new domain. This direct approach is more elegant and likely more robust in the long run than indirect conversion methods.

3.  **Insightful Experimental Design:** The decision to compare BSP against conversion-based approaches is very effective. It not only provides strong baselines but also leads to a key insight: the underlying classifier technology is crucial. The fact that an LSTM-based dependency parser, even with the information loss from conversion, outperforms the perceptron-based BSP on primary edges strongly suggests that the BSP transition system is sound and that its performance can be substantially boosted by integrating a more powerful classifier. This is not a failure of the model, but a clear and valuable roadmap for future improvement.

4.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** Beyond the main system, the introduction of the `ratio` feature is a clever, simple, and likely generalizable idea for transition-based constituency parsers. It provides a global structural constraint to a locally-driven process, which is an interesting way to regularize the creation of non-terminal nodes.

### Weaknesses

While the paper is strong, there are areas for improvement. However, in my view, these are acceptable limitations for a paper breaking new ground.

1.  **Sub-SOTA Performance of the Novel Parser:** The most significant weakness is that the novel BSP parser is outperformed on primary edges by the conversion-based LSTM dependency parser (59.0% vs. 69.2% F-score). While BSP is the only system capable of predicting remote edges, its performance on them is quite low (13.3% F-score). In a paper focused on incremental improvement, this would be a major flaw. Here, it is an acceptable result for a first-generation system in a new task. The authors correctly identify the likely cause (the classifier) and the path forward, which mitigates this weakness substantially.

2.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Transitions:** The transition set of BSP is largely a combination of transitions from prior work (e.g., SWAP from Maier 2015, non-reducing edges from Sagae & Tsujii 2008). The primary additions are the REMOTE-EDGE transitions. While the synthesis is novel and applied to a new problem, the underlying components are not entirely new. This is a minor point, as the novelty lies in the successful application and integration for this new, more complex task.

3.  **Uncertainty of the Evaluation Metric:** The authors propose a new evaluation metric based on matching the yields of labeled edges. While this seems sensible and is a necessary step for a new task, its properties are not yet well understood. It is difficult to fully interpret the meaning of a ~70% F-score without a broader context of how this metric behaves on different types of errors. Establishing a standard evaluation for such structures is a hard problem in itself, so this is an understandable limitation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between BSP and the LSTM-based dependency parser is substantial. You suggest this is due to the classifier. Could you elaborate on why you believe the transition system itself is sound and that the gap can be closed primarily by replacing the perceptron with an LSTM? Are there any specific error types that suggest a limitation in the transition system or oracle rather than just the classifier's predictive power?

2.  The `ratio` feature is an interesting contribution. Could you provide a brief analysis or intuition on how this feature impacts the parser's behavior? For example, does it primarily prevent the creation of spurious non-terminals, and how does it interact with the NODE transitions during decoding?
