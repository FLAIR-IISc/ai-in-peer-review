### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces the task of Broad-coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), defined as parsing into semantic structures that simultaneously support multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The paper presents UCCA as a representative BSS formalism and proposes the first parsing methods for it. Two main approaches are explored: 1) a conversion-based method, where UCCA structures are lossily converted to dependency or constituency trees, parsed with established parsers, and converted back; and 2) a novel direct approach, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), which is a transition-based system designed to produce BSS structures directly. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a conversion-based approach using a state-of-the-art LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest performance on the tree-like backbone of the structures (primary edges), while the proposed BSP parser performs worse on this subtask but is uniquely capable of predicting the non-tree parts (remote edges), albeit with low accuracy.

### Contributions
1.  **A strong baseline for UCCA parsing:** The paper's most significant contribution is demonstrating that an established, state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser (Dyer et al., 2015) can be effectively adapted to parse the core tree structure of UCCA annotations. By converting the UCCA graphs to dependencies, this existing parser achieves the highest reported score (69.2% F1 on primary edges), setting a rigorous and challenging baseline for this new task.
2.  **Introduction of the UCCA parsing task:** The work successfully frames UCCA parsing as a concrete task for the community, providing the necessary data splits, evaluation metrics, and initial benchmark results. This lays the groundwork for future research on parsing such complex, graph-structured semantic representations.
3.  **A proof-of-concept direct parser:** The paper proposes a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that can directly generate the full UCCA graph structure. While its empirical performance is not competitive with the baseline, it serves as a valuable proof-of-concept for a direct parsing architecture.

### Strengths
1.  **Tackles a novel and complex problem:** The paper addresses the challenging task of parsing into a rich semantic representation that unifies characteristics of constituency, dependency, and graph structures. This is a worthwhile direction for the field as it moves towards more comprehensive semantic analysis.
2.  **Methodologically sound comparison with established methods:** The inclusion of the conversion-based approach is a major strength. It grounds the new problem in the context of well-understood formalisms and allows for the use of powerful, established parsers. Comparing the novel direct parser against a strong baseline like the Dyer et al. (2015) parser provides a clear and honest assessment of where the new method stands.
3.  **Clear and informative results:** The experimental results are presented transparently. The fact that the established LSTM-based method outperforms the novel BSP parser is a crucial, if sobering, finding. This result is valuable in its own right, suggesting that powerful sequence models are highly effective at capturing the primary predicate-argument structure, and that this might be the most practical approach for now.
4.  **Thorough empirical evaluation:** The authors evaluate their models in both in-domain and out-of-domain settings, demonstrating the robustness of the parsers. Furthermore, the feature ablation study for the BSP parser provides useful insights into the contributions of its different components.

### Weaknesses
1.  **The novel method is significantly outperformed by the baseline:** The primary technical contribution of the paper is the BSP parser. However, it achieves an F-score of 59.0% on primary edges, which is more than 10 F1 points lower than the 69.2% achieved by the conversion-based LSTM parser. While the BSP parser is the only one that can predict remote edges, its performance on that task is very low (13.3% F1). A new method should ideally offer a compelling advantage, but as it stands, one would be better off using the established baseline and ignoring remote edges entirely. The paper essentially presents a new, complex parser that is inferior to a simpler, stronger baseline.
2.  **Limited novelty in the proposed parser design:** The BSP parser is presented as a novel system, but its design is largely an integration of existing techniques from different transition-based parsers. It combines transitions for constituency parsing (NODE), discontinuous parsing (SWAP from Maier, 2015), and DAG parsing (not reducing after edge creation, similar to Sagae and Tsujii, 2008). The primary novelties are the specific REMOTE transitions and a few features. This feels more like an engineering effort to combine known components than a fundamental advance in parsing algorithms.
3.  **Ad-hoc conversion methodology:** The conversion from UCCA to dependency trees, which underpins the strongest baseline, is described as heuristic. The inverse conversion relies on rules like "attachments are always high-attaching" and a non-specified `Label(u)` function. The reported upper bound of 88.4% F1 shows this conversion is very lossy. This significant information loss complicates the comparison; the LSTM parser might perform even better with a more principled conversion scheme. The paper lacks a sufficient analysis of what information is lost in this process and how it affects the parser's performance.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The performance gap between your BSP parser and the LSTM baseline appears to be due to both the transition system and the classifier (linear perceptron vs. LSTM). To isolate the contribution of your proposed transition system, have you considered replacing BSP's linear classifier with an LSTM-based one, as in Dyer et al. (2015)? This seems like a critical experiment to demonstrate that the proposed transition system itself is sound, rather than being held back by a weaker classifier.
2.  Given that the conversion-based LSTM parser is the strongest model for primary edges, have you considered a pipeline or multi-task approach? For example, one could first predict the primary-edge tree with the LSTM parser and then use its output as features for a second-stage classifier that predicts only the remote edges. This seems like a more direct and potentially more effective path to parsing the full UCCA graph than building an entirely new parser that is weaker on the primary subtask.
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the dependency conversion process? What specific UCCA structures are responsible for the ~12% F1 loss in the upper bound? Understanding this would clarify the limitations of the baseline approach and better motivate the need for a direct parsing model.
