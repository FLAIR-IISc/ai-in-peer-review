### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper introduces the novel task of Broad-coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), which aims to produce semantic representations that are directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) capturing three key properties: multiple parents for shared arguments, non-terminal nodes for multi-word units, and discontinuous constituents. The paper uses the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instantiation of BSS.

The authors propose two main approaches to tackle this new task. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted into more restricted formalisms (dependency trees or discontinuous constituency trees), allowing existing state-of-the-art parsers to be applied. The second is a direct approach, for which the authors develop BSP, a novel transition-based parser specifically designed to produce BSS graphs by extending existing systems with new transitions for remote edges and UCCA-specific features. These approaches are evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, both in- and out-of-domain.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **Introduction and formalization of a new parsing task:** The paper is the first to systematically address the problem of parsing into a rich, grounded semantic representation (UCCA) that jointly models DAG structures, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. By framing this as BSS parsing and developing initial methodologies, the authors open a new and promising research direction.
2.  **A thorough empirical investigation with strong baselines:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation that not only introduces their novel parser but also establishes strong baselines by cleverly adapting existing high-performance parsers. This rigorous comparison provides crucial context and reveals that an adapted LSTM-based dependency parser currently sets the state-of-the-art for the tree-like backbone of the structures.
3.  **Development of a novel, direct BSS parser:** The Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) is a valuable contribution as a proof-of-concept system that can directly generate all structural properties of BSS. While its performance is not yet state-of-the-art, it serves as a foundational model upon which future work in direct BSS parsing can be built.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and timely problem:** The paper tackles a significant gap in semantic parsing. While representations like semantic dependencies and AMR have gained traction, UCCA offers a unique, cross-linguistically motivated perspective that is grounded in the text and handles phenomena like coordination and multi-word expressions in a more integrated way. Introducing a parsing methodology for this formalism is a valuable and timely contribution to the field.
2.  **Rigorous and insightful experimental design:** The decision to compare a bespoke direct parser against strong conversion-based baselines is a major strength. This allows for a nuanced analysis of the problem. It highlights that while existing powerful models (like the stack-LSTM parser) can be successfully adapted to handle the core tree structure, the full complexity of the task (i.e., remote edges) requires specialized mechanisms. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation further demonstrates the robustness of the findings.
3.  **Constructive analysis and clear future directions:** The discussion of the results is balanced and insightful. The authors correctly identify the superior performance of the LSTM-based parser on primary edges and astutely suggest that integrating a similar classifier into their own more expressive transition system is the most promising path forward. This self-critical analysis strengthens the paper and provides a clear roadmap for future research.
4.  **Contribution to reproducibility:** The commitment to release the code for the converters and the BSP parser is commendable. As this is the first work on UCCA parsing, providing these resources is essential for fostering further research in this area, allowing others to build upon, improve, and compare against this foundational work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming performance of the novel parser (BSP):** The main technical novelty of the paper, the BSP parser, is significantly outperformed by the conversion-based LSTM baseline on the primary task of predicting the core tree structure (59.0% vs. 69.2% F1 on primary edges). Furthermore, while BSP is the only system that can predict remote edges, its performance on this subtask is very low (13.3% F1). This raises questions about the current practical utility of the direct approach and makes the "conversion-based" approach seem like the more significant practical contribution of the paper.
2.  **Lack of detail on the parsing oracle:** The paper states that a dynamic oracle is used for training BSP, but provides little detail on its implementation. Given the complexity of the target structures (DAGs with discontinuities), defining the set of "optimal" transitions at any given state is non-trivial. How are ambiguities handled if multiple transition sequences can lead to the gold structure? What is the strategy for ordering the creation of primary vs. remote edges? Without these details, it is difficult to fully assess the learning setup and for others to faithfully reimplement the system.
3.  **Potential issues with the conversion-based evaluation:** The inverse conversion from dependency trees back to UCCA structures relies on heuristics (e.g., assuming high-attachments, rule-based labeling). The information loss is significant, with the conversion upper bound at only 88.4% F1. This makes it difficult to ascertain whether the LSTM parser's superior performance is due to its better parsing ability or its ability to produce dependency structures that align well with the specific inverse conversion heuristics. A more detailed error analysis of the conversion process itself would help disentangle these factors.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between the LSTM dependency parser and BSP on primary edges is substantial. You suggest that integrating an LSTM classifier into BSP is a key next step. Could you elaborate on the potential challenges in doing so? Is it a straightforward engineering task, or do you foresee research challenges in combining a stack-LSTM architecture with your more complex transition system, which includes SWAP and multiple types of edge-creation actions?
2.  The performance on remote edges is currently very low (13.3 F1). Based on your error analysis, what are the primary failure modes? Is the model failing to identify which nodes should have multiple parents, or is it identifying the correct nodes but attaching them to the wrong secondary parent? Is this primarily a feature representation problem or a search problem inherent to predicting non-local dependencies in a local model?
3.  Could you provide more detail on how the dynamic oracle handles the scheduling of transitions? For example, for a node that is both a child via a primary edge and a child via a remote edge, is there a canonical order in which the oracle tries to create these edges? How does this interact with the creation of the node's own children?
