**Summary and Contributions:**

This paper introduces the task of Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), aiming to parse natural language into semantic representations that are Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) and can natively represent non-terminal and discontinuous semantic units. As the first work to tackle this problem, it focuses on parsing into the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme. The authors pursue two strategies: first, they establish strong baselines by converting UCCA structures to and from simpler tree formalisms (constituency and dependency) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers. Second, they propose a novel, direct transition-based parser, BSP, designed specifically to produce BSS structures by incorporating new transitions to handle multiple parents (remote edges) and discontinuities within an integrated framework. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that while a conversion-based approach using an LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest F-score on primary edges, the proposed BSP is the only method capable of parsing the full DAG structure, including remote edges.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel and principled transition-based parser (BSP) for a new class of rich semantic structures.** The key contribution is the design of a system that directly models and produces DAGs with non-terminal and discontinuous nodes. This is a bold and conceptually significant step beyond standard tree parsing, opening a new avenue for research in semantic parsing.
2.  **The establishment and comprehensive analysis of the UCCA parsing task.** The paper effectively frames a new shared task for the community. By defining an evaluation metric and, crucially, providing a thorough analysis of strong conversion-based baselines, the authors rigorously situate their novel approach and clearly delineate the limitations of existing technology, thereby justifying the need for new methods like BSP.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Ambition:** The paper's primary strength is its novelty. It tackles a new, challenging, and important parsing problem. To my knowledge, this is the first work to propose a direct parser for a grounded semantic representation that simultaneously handles multiple parentage, phrasal units, and discontinuities. This is precisely the kind of ambitious, foundational work that pushes the field forward. The problem itself is well-motivated, and the proposed BSP system is a compelling first solution.
2.  **Elegant and Direct Modeling Approach:** The design of the BSP transition system is a significant strength. Rather than shoehorning a complex representation into a simpler, lossy format, BSP is built from the ground up to handle the target formalism. The introduction of distinct transitions for primary (LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE) and remote (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE) edges is a clean and principled way to generate DAGs within a transition-based framework. This direct approach is far more promising in the long term than conversion-based methods, which will always be limited by the expressivity of their intermediate representation.
3.  **Insightful Experimental Design and Analysis:** The authors present a very strong experimental setup. The inclusion of conversion-based baselines is not just a formality; it provides a crucial reference point. The fact that an off-the-shelf LSTM dependency parser can achieve a high score on the tree-like backbone of the structures is a fascinating result in itself. However, this finding powerfully underscores the contribution of BSP, as it simultaneously reveals the fundamental inability of tree parsers to handle the full scope of the problem (i.e., remote edges). This comparative analysis provides a clear and honest picture of the current state of affairs.
4.  **Laying the Groundwork for Future Research:** This paper effectively opens up a new research direction. By providing the first parser, evaluation metrics, and strong baselines for UCCA, the authors have created the necessary foundation for future work to build upon. This is a substantial contribution to the community, and the promise to release all converters and parsers further enhances its value.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Significant Performance Gap on Primary Edges:** The most notable weakness is that the proposed BSP parser is outperformed on primary edge F-score by a substantial margin (~10 points) by the LSTM dependency parser baseline. While BSP's ability to parse remote edges is a clear advantage, the large performance discrepancy on the structural backbone is a concern. The paper speculates that using an LSTM classifier in BSP would help, but it would be stronger with a more in-depth analysis of this gap. It is unclear whether the performance deficit is solely due to the classifier (perceptron vs. LSTM) or if the more complex search space of the direct DAG parser leads to greater error propagation in a greedy model.
2.  **Dated Classifier Technology:** The novelty of the paper lies in the transition system, not its machine learning backend. However, the choice of a linear perceptron model feels somewhat dated, especially when the authors' own baseline results demonstrate the immense power of modern neural classifiers (LSTMs) on a related task. This makes it difficult to assess the true potential of the proposed transition system. While I appreciate prioritizing the novel parsing model over a perfectly tuned SOTA implementation, incorporating a stronger classifier would have made the paper's claims about the viability of the direct approach much more conclusive.
3.  **Analysis of Conversion Lossiness:** The upper bound for the dependency conversion is 88.4%, indicating a significant loss of information. The paper attributes this to the removal of non-terminal nodes, but the analysis is brief. A more detailed error analysis of what is lost in the conversion process (e.g., specific constructions, attachment ambiguities) would provide deeper insight into the challenges of the task and further motivate the direct parsing approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The performance of the LSTM dependency baseline is very strong. Could you further elaborate on your hypothesis for the large performance gap between it and BSP on primary edges? Do you believe this is primarily attributable to the choice of classifier (perceptron vs. LSTM), or do you suspect that the search space for the direct DAG parsing task is fundamentally harder, leading to more search errors in your greedy model compared to the arc-standard dependency parser?
2.  The `ratio` feature is presented as a novel and generally useful idea. Could you provide a bit more intuition on its effect? For instance, what was the change in precision vs. recall when this feature was added/removed? Does it primarily prevent the parser from over-generating non-terminals?
3.  The SWAP transition is used to handle discontinuities in English. UCCA is designed to be cross-linguistically applicable. Do you foresee any limitations of this mechanism for parsing languages with much freer word order and more frequent or complex discontinuous structures than English?
