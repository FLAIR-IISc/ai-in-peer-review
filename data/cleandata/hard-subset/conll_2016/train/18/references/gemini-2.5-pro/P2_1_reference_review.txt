### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of semantic representations that support (1) multiple parents (DAGs), (2) non-terminal nodes for multi-word units, and (3) discontinuous constituents. The work focuses on the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of BSS. The authors propose and evaluate two main approaches: a conversion-based method that transforms UCCA into simpler formalisms (dependency or constituency trees) to leverage existing parsers, and a direct approach using a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed specifically for BSS.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **Introduction of the UCCA parsing task and a novel direct parser (BSP):** The primary contribution is the formulation of the UCCA parsing problem and the development of the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP). BSP is the first parser designed to handle the specific combination of DAG structures, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities required by UCCA, extending existing transition-based parsing techniques with new transitions and features.
2.  **A thorough empirical baseline using conversion-based methods:** A significant secondary contribution is the systematic evaluation of existing state-of-the-art parsers on this new task via conversion. This provides strong baselines, contextualizes the performance of the novel parser, and offers valuable insights into the relationship between UCCA and more standard syntactic formalisms.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a well-motivated and novel problem:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing semantic representations (e.g., bi-lexical dependencies in SDP, lack of grounding in AMR) and makes a compelling case for parsing formalisms like UCCA that handle a broader range of semantic phenomena. To my knowledge, this is the first work to present a parser for the UCCA formalism, opening up a new and valuable direction for broad-coverage semantic parsing.

2.  **Methodologically sound and comprehensive approach:** The authors' two-pronged strategy is excellent. The conversion-based approach is a smart and necessary first step, establishing how far one can get with existing technology. The design of the direct parser, BSP, is a logical extension of well-established and successful transition-based parsing paradigms (e.g., from Maier, 2015; Sagae & Tsujii, 2008). The new transitions for remote edges and the UCCA-specific features are well-motivated additions.

3.  **Thorough and insightful evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong. The authors define a suitable evaluation metric for BSS, test on both in-domain and out-of-domain data, and provide a feature ablation study that clarifies the contribution of different components of their BSP model. Crucially, they also report the upper bounds for the conversion-based methods, which is essential for correctly interpreting the performance of the baseline parsers by disentangling parser error from conversion loss.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The proposed novel parser (BSP) is outperformed by a baseline:** The most significant weakness is that the main technical contribution, the BSP parser, achieves a considerably lower F-score on primary edges (59.0% in-domain) than the baseline using a dependency conversion and an existing LSTM-based parser (69.2%). While BSP has the unique advantage of being able to predict remote edges, its performance on the core tree structure is 10 F-score points lower than the baseline. This raises questions about the practical utility of the more complex, direct parsing approach compared to a simpler, high-performing pipeline, at least in its current form.

2.  **Limited analysis of the performance gap and error types:** The paper notes the superior performance of the LSTM parser and suggests that incorporating a similar classifier into BSP would likely improve results. While this is a reasonable hypothesis, the paper would be much stronger with a more detailed analysis of *why* BSP underperforms. An error analysis comparing the output of BSP and the LSTM-based dependency parser could reveal specific structural phenomena (e.g., attachment decisions, node creation) where the direct transition system struggles relative to the dependency-based approach. Without this, the path to closing the performance gap is speculative.

3.  **Potential oversimplification in the dependency-to-constituency conversion:** The inverse conversion from dependency trees back to UCCA-like structures (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics, such as assuming high attachments and using a rule-based function for labeling new non-terminals. This process is inherently lossy and may be a source of error that unfairly penalizes the dependency-based parsers. The impressive results from the LSTM parser are achieved *despite* these heuristics, suggesting its underlying parsing quality might be even higher. This slightly complicates the interpretation of the comparison between the direct and conversion-based approaches.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the significant performance gap on primary edges between BSP (~59 F1) and the LSTM-based dependency conversion parser (~69 F1)? Beyond suggesting an LSTM classifier for BSP, have you performed any error analysis that reveals specific structural types where the direct transition-based approach underperforms compared to the simpler, tree-based parsing approach?

2.  The ability to predict remote edges is a key advantage of BSP, but the F-score is quite low (~13 F1). Could you comment on the primary sources of error for these remote edges? Are they mostly attachment errors (wrong parent), labeling errors, or failures to posit an edge at all? What are the most promising avenues for improving their prediction?
