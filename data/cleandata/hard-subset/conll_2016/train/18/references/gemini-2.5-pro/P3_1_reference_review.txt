### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations that support (1) multiple parents (DAGs), (2) non-terminal nodes for multi-word units, and (3) discontinuities. Using the UCCA framework as a concrete instance of BSS, the authors are the first to propose and evaluate parsing methods for this type of representation. The paper's contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  **Pioneering the Task of BSS Parsing:** The primary contribution is the formalization and first attempt at parsing UCCA, a representation that combines challenges from dependency (DAGs), constituency (non-terminals), and discontinuous parsing. This work effectively opens up a new and interesting subfield of semantic parsing.

2.  **A Thorough Empirical Study with a Surprising Key Finding:** The paper presents two complementary parsing strategies: a novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed specifically for BSS, and a conversion-based approach that leverages existing high-performance parsers. The rigorous comparison between these yields a crucial insight: a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser, applied to a lossily converted version of the data, significantly outperforms the bespoke BSP parser on the core tree structure of the representation.

3.  **A Principled Novel Parser Design and Evaluation Framework:** The design of the BSP parser is a well-motivated synthesis of techniques from different transition-based parsing paradigms, extended with novel transitions (`LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE`) to handle DAG structures. Furthermore, the paper proposes a sensible evaluation metric based on edge yields, which is a necessary prerequisite for future work on this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and High Significance:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for BSS by identifying three key structural properties often lacking in other semantic representations. By positioning UCCA as a framework that satisfies these needs, the paper carves out a well-defined and important research problem. The work is timely, given the community's increasing interest in rich, broad-coverage semantic representations beyond SRL and bi-lexical dependencies.

2.  **Methodologically Sound and Comprehensive Approach:** The dual-strategy approach—building a novel parser while also assessing the limits of existing technology—is a major strength. The design of the BSP parser is elegant, combining known transitions (SHIFT, NODE, SWAP) with a clean extension for remote edges. The feature set is comprehensive and thoughtfully adapted from related successful parsers. The conversion-based approach provides an extremely strong baseline and contextualizes the performance of the new model.

3.  **Insightful and Honest Evaluation:** The experimental results are presented clearly and their implications are discussed frankly. The discovery that the converted LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest score on primary edges (69.2% F-score) is a significant finding. Instead of downplaying this, the authors correctly identify it as a promising direction for improving their own parser. This demonstrates scientific maturity and provides a clear path forward. The inclusion of conversion upper bounds and comparison to inter-annotator agreement (IAA) provides essential context for interpreting the scores.

4.  **Exceptional Clarity and Positioning:** The paper is very well-written. The background section expertly situates the work with respect to related formalisms like Semantic Dependency Parsing (SDP) and Abstract Meaning Representation (AMR), clearly articulating the trade-offs (e.g., groundedness vs. abstraction, bi-lexical vs. phrasal units). This makes the paper accessible and its contributions easy to appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The Main Technical Contribution (BSP) is Outperformed:** The most significant weakness is that the paper's main novel component, the BSP parser, is not the best-performing model on the primary task of recovering the core tree structure. It is beaten by a substantial margin (59.0% vs. 69.2% F1 on primary edges) by an off-the-shelf parser applied via a conversion pipeline. While BSP is the only model that attempts to predict remote edges, its performance is very low (13.3% F1), limiting its practical advantage. This raises the question of whether the simpler, higher-performing conversion-based approach is a more practical solution for now.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Remote Edge Errors:** The ability to predict remote edges is the key feature distinguishing BSP from the conversion-based methods. Given the extremely low F-score on this subtask, a more in-depth error analysis is warranted. The paper offers no insight into why this task is so difficult for the model. Is it a data sparsity issue (remote edges being rare)? Are the features insufficient to capture the long-distance dependencies involved? Or are there specific linguistic constructions that are systematically mishandled? This analysis would be invaluable for guiding future work.

3.  **Limited Discussion of Conversion Heuristics:** The dependency-to-constituency conversion procedure (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics to reintroduce non-terminal nodes and assign labels. While the upper-bound experiment quantifies the total information loss, the paper would be stronger with a qualitative discussion of the limitations. For example, what types of UCCA structures are irrecoverably lost in the dependency representation and incorrectly reconstructed by the algorithm? This would provide a clearer picture of the trade-offs involved in the conversion approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between your perceptron-based BSP and the LSTM-based dependency parser is striking. The paper suggests that integrating LSTMs into BSP is a promising future direction. Could you elaborate on what you believe is the primary source of the LSTM's advantage? Is it better handling of feature sparsity through dense representations, the ability to model sequential information in the stack/buffer, or both?

2.  Could you provide more detail on the failure modes for remote edge prediction? For instance, what is the breakdown between precision and recall errors? Does the parser tend to hallucinate incorrect remote edges, or does it simply fail to identify most of the gold ones? Is there a correlation between remote edge errors and other phenomena, such as sentence length or the presence of discontinuities?

3.  The novel `ratio` feature (terminals/nodes) is an interesting addition that seems quite effective. Could you offer more intuition on its role? Does it primarily act as a regularizer to prevent the oracle from generating an excessive number of `NODE` transitions, especially in complex sentences? Have you analyzed its impact on parser behavior beyond the final F-score?
