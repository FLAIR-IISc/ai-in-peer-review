### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the first parsing approaches for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of semantic representations that simultaneously feature (1) multiple parents (DAGs), (2) non-terminal nodes, and (3) discontinuous constituents. The work uses the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as its target representation. The authors propose two complementary strategies: first, a conversion-based approach that transforms UCCA structures into more standard formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers. Second, and more centrally, they develop a novel transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), specifically designed to directly generate all three properties of BSS. The methods are evaluated on the English UCCA corpora, establishing the first parsing benchmarks for this representation.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The design and implementation of BSP**, a novel transition-based parser that synthesizes techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing to directly model the complex properties of UCCA. This is the paper's core technical contribution.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical study establishing the first parsing results for UCCA.** This includes a valuable comparison between a direct parsing approach (BSP) and strong, conversion-based baselines using off-the-shelf parsers. This frames the difficulty of the task and provides a solid foundation for future research.
3.  **The development of conversion protocols between UCCA and other formalisms.** These protocols, which the authors promise to make public, are a useful practical contribution for the community, enabling comparison and interoperability.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackling a Novel and Challenging Problem:** The paper is the first to address the task of parsing UCCA, a rich semantic formalism whose structural properties (DAGs, non-terminals, discontinuities) pose a significant challenge for existing parsing paradigms. The problem is well-motivated, and the clear breakdown of BSS properties in the introduction provides excellent context for why a new approach is needed. This work opens up a new and interesting direction in semantic parsing.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Design:** The two-pronged experimental approach is a major strength. Instead of just presenting their novel parser, the authors first establish strong baselines by converting the data and applying existing high-performance parsers, including a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser. This provides crucial context for BSP's performance and reveals that a well-engineered conversion pipeline can be highly effective. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation further demonstrates the robustness of the methods.
3.  **Well-Designed Novel Parser Architecture:** The BSP parser is a thoughtful synthesis of existing, successful ideas in transition-based parsing. It correctly identifies and integrates the necessary components to handle the target structures: a `SWAP` transition for discontinuities (from Maier, 2015), non-reducing `EDGE` transitions for multiple parents (from Sagae and Tsujii, 2008), and standard transitions for constituency structures. The addition of novel features, particularly the `ratio` feature to regulate node creation, is a clever and useful contribution. The overall design is sound and directly addresses the defined problem.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly lays out the three motivating structural properties with helpful examples in Figure 1. The algorithms for the conversion procedures are presented clearly, and the transition system for BSP is well-defined in Figure 2. This clarity makes the paper's contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Main Contribution (BSP):** The paper's primary technical contribution, the BSP parser, is substantially outperformed on the main evaluation metric (primary edge F-score) by the conversion-based baseline using the LSTM dependency parser (59.9% F1 for BSP-Tree vs. 69.2% for LSTM). While BSP has the unique advantage of being able to predict remote edges, the nearly 10-point F1 gap on the core tree structure is significant. This raises questions about the efficacy of the proposed direct approach compared to a simpler pipeline that leverages more powerful classifiers (LSTMs). The authors acknowledge this and suggest incorporating LSTMs into BSP, but as presented, the results do not make a strong case for the superiority of the direct parsing model.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Remote Edge Parsing:** The ability to parse remote edges is a key feature of BSP, distinguishing it from the conversion-based methods. However, performance on this subtask is very low (13.3% F1 in-domain), and the paper offers no analysis of these errors. It is unclear whether these edges are simply sparse and difficult to learn, or if there are systematic error patterns that could be addressed. A qualitative error analysis would have greatly strengthened the paper by providing insight into the challenges of parsing DAG structures in this framework and suggesting paths for improvement.
3.  **Incremental Novelty of the Transition System:** While the *application* of the transition system to UCCA is novel, its core components are largely adaptations of existing work. The `SWAP`, non-reducing edge creation, and constituency-building transitions have been proposed before in separate lines of work. The contribution lies more in the successful engineering and integration of these components for a new task rather than a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough. This is a minor weakness, as the synthesis is non-trivial and well-executed, but it's worth noting that the novelty is primarily in combination and application.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between the LSTM dependency parser baseline (69.2 F1) and your BSP parser (59.9 F1) on primary edges is substantial, even though the dependency conversion is lossy. Beyond the known power of LSTMs, do you have any hypotheses about why the dependency-based factorization of the problem is so much more effective? Does this suggest that modeling head-to-head relationships is more critical than directly modeling constituent structure for this task?
2.  Could you provide more insight into the poor performance on remote edges (13.3 F1)? Is there a particular type of remote edge (e.g., those involved in argument sharing vs. others) that the model struggles with? Is the feature set for the `REMOTE-EDGE` transitions simply too impoverished to make accurate predictions? An error analysis here would be very helpful.
