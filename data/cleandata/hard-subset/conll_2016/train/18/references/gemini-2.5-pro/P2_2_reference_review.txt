### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of semantic representations that support three key properties often missing in other formalisms: multiple parents (DAG structures), non-terminal nodes for multi-word units, and discontinuous constituents. The authors introduce the UCCA annotation scheme as a prime example of BSS and present the first parsing results for it.

The paper's contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel transition-based parser (BSP) that is the first to directly model the combination of multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities.** This is the primary technical contribution, extending existing transition-based techniques from discontinuous constituency and DAG dependency parsing into a unified framework for this more complex semantic representation.
2.  **A thorough empirical study and benchmarking of UCCA parsing.** The authors wisely do not just present their own parser but also establish strong baselines by developing methods to convert UCCA structures to and from more standard formalisms (constituency and dependency trees). This allows them to leverage state-of-the-art parsers, providing a realistic assessment of where existing technology stands and a clear performance target for future work.
3.  **The introduction and formalization of the BSS parsing task.** By defining the problem, providing a concrete testbed (UCCA), and releasing code, the paper lays the groundwork for a new and interesting direction in semantic parsing research.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles an important and well-motivated problem.** The paper clearly articulates why all three properties of BSS are necessary for a comprehensive semantic representation. The examples provided (shared arguments, coordination, phrasal verbs) are convincing and highlight genuine limitations in many popular formalisms like dependency trees or AMR (which isn't grounded). This work is a valuable step towards parsers that can handle the full complexity of natural language semantics.

2.  **Excellent and sound experimental design.** The two-pronged approach—conversion-based baselines versus a direct parsing model—is a major strength. It provides a multi-faceted view of the problem.
    *   The conversion approach is not just a strawman; it leverages a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser, setting a very high bar for performance on the tree-like aspects of the structures.
    *   The inclusion of "Upper Bound" calculations for the conversion methods is crucial. It clearly quantifies the information loss inherent in the conversion process and helps contextualize the parser performance.
    *   The evaluation on both in-domain and out-of-domain data demonstrates the robustness of the proposed methods.

3.  **The proposed BSP parser is a principled and logical extension of existing work.** While not a radical departure, BSP sensibly combines techniques from successful parsers for related but simpler tasks. The transition set is a thoughtful amalgamation of transitions for constituency (NODE), DAGs (distinguishing primary vs. remote edges, not auto-reducing), and discontinuities (SWAP). The design is well-explained and justified.

4.  **Clear presentation and contribution to the community.** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The formal descriptions of the conversion algorithms and the transition system are clear. The authors' commitment to releasing their parsers and converters is a significant service that will undoubtedly spur further research in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The performance of the novel parser (BSP) is significantly lower than the best conversion-based baseline on the primary task.** As shown in Table 2, the LSTM-based dependency parser achieves 69.2% F-score on primary edges, while BSP only reaches 59.0% (or 59.9% for the tree-only version). This is a substantial gap of nearly 10 F1 points. While BSP is the only model that can predict remote edges, its performance there is very low (13.3% F-score). This raises the question of whether the complexity of directly modeling all three BSS properties at once harms the model's ability to learn the more fundamental tree structure, which a simpler, specialized model captures better.

2.  **The technical novelty of the BSP parser is somewhat incremental.** The parser is a clever combination of existing ideas: SWAP for discontinuities from Nivre (2009) and Maier (2015), separate edge creation and reduction for DAGs from Sagae & Tsujii (2008), and a standard constituency parsing transition system. The primary novelties appear to be the `REMOTE-EDGE` transitions and the `ratio` feature. While the synthesis is valuable for this new task, the core components are not new, which slightly diminishes the paper's technical contribution in terms of fundamental parsing algorithms.

3.  **The analysis of the results could be deeper.** The paper presents the numbers clearly, but a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. For example:
    *   Why does BSP perform so much worse than the LSTM parser on primary edges? Is it a feature engineering problem, a search problem, or a fundamental limitation of the perceptron-based model?
    *   What kinds of remote edges is BSP able to predict, and where does it fail? The 13.3% F-score is low, but is it concentrated on specific linguistic phenomena?
    *   The dependency-to-constituency back-conversion (Algorithm 2) seems heuristic ("always high-attaching," rule-based labels). Could this simplistic conversion be artificially limiting the performance of the dependency-based baselines?

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between the LSTM-based conversion parser and BSP on primary edges is substantial. You rightly suggest that integrating LSTMs into BSP would likely improve results. Could you elaborate on why the current feature-based model might be struggling so much in comparison? Does it have difficulty with long-range dependencies or lexical sparsity, which LSTMs are known to handle better?

2.  The F-score on remote edges is quite low (~13%). Could you provide some insight into this? Is this primarily due to data sparsity (remote edges being rare), a modeling limitation in capturing the necessary long-distance context, or both? An error analysis showing which types of remote edges are easier or harder to predict would be very informative.

3.  Regarding the dependency conversion, the back-conversion in Algorithm 2 reconstructs non-terminals heuristically. Have you analyzed how much performance is lost due to this step? Is it possible that a more sophisticated back-conversion could push the already strong dependency baseline even higher, further widening the gap with BSP?
