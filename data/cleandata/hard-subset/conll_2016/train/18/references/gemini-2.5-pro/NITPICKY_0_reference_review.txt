### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Broad-coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), focusing on the UCCA formalism, which represents sentence semantics as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) that allows for multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors propose two main approaches: 1) a conversion-based method that transforms UCCA structures into dependency or constituency trees to leverage existing parsers, and 2) BSP, a novel transition-based parser designed to directly produce UCCA structures. The work is evaluated on English UCCA corpora, providing the first parsing results for this scheme.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  The introduction and empirical evaluation of parsing for the UCCA semantic representation, establishing initial benchmarks on existing corpora.
2.  The design of BSP, a unified transition-based system that is, to my knowledge, the first to explicitly model and generate structures with the combination of multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities.
3.  An analysis of conversion-based methods, which provides an upper bound on this approach and demonstrates that strong existing parsers can be adapted to the task, achieving competitive results even with lossy conversions.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles a Novel and Challenging Problem:** The paper is the first to propose a parsing solution for the UCCA formalism. UCCA's properties (DAGs, non-terminals, discontinuities) pose a significant challenge that lies beyond the scope of most standard parsers. Establishing a foothold in this area is a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing.
2.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The core proposal, the BSP parser, is a principled extension of well-established transition-based parsing techniques. The authors thoughtfully combine mechanisms from discontinuous constituency parsing (SWAP), dependency DAG parsing (not immediately removing nodes after edge creation), and standard constituency parsing (NODE transition) into a coherent system. This is a non-trivial piece of system design.
3.  **Comprehensive Empirical Grounding:** The authors do not present their novel parser in a vacuum. The inclusion of conversion-based baselines using a variety of strong, publicly available parsers (MaltParser, UPARSE, Dyer et al.'s LSTM parser) is commendable. This provides a clear context for BSP's performance and reveals that a state-of-the-art LSTM-based parser, even on a lossy representation, sets a very high bar. The inclusion of out-of-domain evaluation is also a welcome addition.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Proposed Model:** The central contribution, the BSP parser, is substantially outperformed on the primary evaluation metric (Primary F-score) by a baseline system (LSTM parser on converted dependencies), scoring 69.2% vs. BSP's 59.9% (for the tree-only variant). While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on this front is very low (13.3% F-score), suggesting it has not effectively learned to model this phenomenon. The paper's conclusion frames the 69.2% result as a success for the task, but this was achieved by a baseline, not their proposed model.
2.  **Lack of Technical Rigor and Clarity in Key Details:** Several methodological details are either unclear, potentially incorrect, or underdeveloped.
    *   In Algorithm 1, the head-finding function `h(u)` is defined as `argmin_v Priority(lc(u, v))`, but the preceding text states it is the child with the "highest edge label". An `argmin` over priority would typically correspond to the *lowest* label. This is a confusing contradiction.
    *   The feature engineering for remote edges—a key capability of BSP—appears superficial. Figure 3 lists only four simple count-based features (`s0R, s0wR, b0R, b0wR`). It is difficult to believe that such limited features could capture the complex syntactic and semantic cues required for identifying non-local, multi-parent dependencies.
    *   The BSP parser uses a greedy decoder. This is a significant disadvantage compared to the UPARSE baseline, which uses a beam search of size 4. This confounding variable is not mentioned or discussed, making the comparison less direct than it appears.
3.  **Superficial Treatment of Limitations and Design Choices:** The paper makes several simplifying assumptions and design choices without adequate justification.
    *   Implicit nodes are discarded from the data, but the potential impact of this on the parsing task is not discussed.
    *   The inverse dependency-to-constituency conversion (Algorithm 2) uses a simple heuristic ("assume that attachments are always high-attaching") and a rule-based `Label(u)` function which is deferred to supplementary material (Footnote 5). This makes the procedure difficult to assess and reproduces.
    *   The reason for discarding non-projective MaltParser variants is given only as they "yielded lower scores", which is insufficient detail for a formal publication.
4.  **Poor Presentation and Lack of Attention to Detail:** The paper is marred by numerous, distracting formatting and typographical errors, most notably in the References section. The bibliography is riddled with BibTeX artifacts (`\.,?`, `\Q2008\E`), duplicate citations, and inconsistent formatting. This lack of careful proofreading reflects poorly on the submission's overall quality and undermines confidence in the rigor of the work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the definition of the head-finding function `h(u)` in Section 3? Is it selecting the child with the highest or lowest priority label, and how does this reconcile with the use of `argmin` in Algorithm 1?
2.  Given that the LSTM dependency parser baseline significantly outperforms BSP on primary edges, what do you see as the primary contribution of the BSP model itself? Should it be viewed as a framework that requires a stronger classifier (like an LSTM), or do you believe the current feature-based model can be made competitive with further engineering?
3.  The feature set for predicting remote edges appears quite limited. Did you experiment with more sophisticated features for this sub-task, such as path-based or structural features between the candidate parent and child? If not, why do you believe the current features are a sufficient starting point?
