### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces the task of parsing into Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) that support non-terminal nodes, discontinuous constituents, and nodes with multiple parents. The authors focus on the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of BSS, for which no parser currently exists. The paper proposes two main approaches: first, a conversion-based method where UCCA structures are lossily converted to constituency or dependency trees, parsed with established parsers, and then converted back. Second, the paper presents a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed to directly produce BSS graphs by extending existing transition systems. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that the conversion-based approach, particularly when using a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser, achieves the highest performance on the primary tree structure (69.2% F1). The proposed direct parser, BSP, performs substantially worse on this structure (59.9% F1) but is capable of predicting the multi-parent "remote" edges, albeit with very low F1-score (13.3%).

### Contributions
1.  **Establishment of UCCA Parsing as a Task with Strong Baselines:** The paper's most significant contribution is the formulation of parsing for the UCCA formalism and the thorough evaluation of strong baselines. By converting the structures to more standard dependency and constituency formats and applying powerful, established parsers (including the LSTM-based parser of Dyer et al., 2015), the authors provide a crucial first benchmark and a high-water mark for future research on this problem.
2.  **A Direct Transition-Based Parser for BSS:** The paper proposes the first direct parser (BSP) for a BSS formalism like UCCA. While its empirical performance is not state-of-the-art, it serves as a proof-of-concept for directly modeling the formalism's key properties (DAGs, discontinuities) within a single transition-based framework. Its ability to predict remote edges, which are entirely lost in the tree-based conversion methods, represents a functional advantage.

### Strengths
1.  **Methodologically Sound Baseline Comparisons:** A major strength of this work is its rigorous use of conversion-based methods as baselines. Rather than comparing their new model only against weak alternatives, the authors apply high-performing, well-established parsers to the task. The inclusion of the LSTM dependency parser provides a very strong point of comparison and grounds the results within the context of recent advances in parsing.
2.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Analysis:** The paper does an excellent job of defining the structural properties of BSS and motivating the need for parsers that can handle them. The analysis is thorough, including upper-bound calculations for the conversion methods, in-domain and out-of-domain experiments, and a feature ablation study that provides insight into the proposed BSP model.
3.  **Introduction of a New Parsing Task:** The work successfully introduces parsing for the UCCA scheme to the community. By providing data, evaluation metrics, and initial parsing results, it lays the groundwork for future research in broad-coverage semantic parsing beyond the more established dependency-based formalisms.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Proposed Method is Substantially Outperformed by a Baseline:** The central weakness of the paper is that its main novel contribution, the BSP parser, is not competitive with the best-performing baseline. An F-score of 59.9% on primary edges is nearly 10 points lower than the 69.2% achieved by converting to dependencies and using an existing parser. This performance gap is very large and raises significant questions about the value of the proposed direct approach. While BSP can predict remote edges, its performance on them is too low to compensate for the major deficit in parsing the primary structure.
2.  **Extremely Low Performance on the Differentiating Capability:** The key advantage of the BSP parser over the conversion baselines is its ability to produce a full DAG by predicting remote edges. However, with a labeled F-score of only 13.3%, this capability has not been convincingly demonstrated. Such low performance suggests the model struggles to learn the necessary signals for these constructions, making the contribution feel preliminary and not yet practically useful.
3.  **Limited Novelty in Parser Architecture:** The proposed BSP parser is a competent integration of techniques from prior work on transition-based parsing for discontinuous constituency trees and dependency DAGs. The transition set and features are largely adaptations of existing work (e.g., Maier, 2015; Sagae and Tsujii, 2008). While the engineering effort is noted, the conceptual novelty of the parser itself is limited. This is particularly relevant given that the final model is not empirically superior.
4.  **Unfair Comparison of Classifiers:** The best baseline (LSTM parser) uses a powerful non-linear classifier, while the proposed BSP uses a linear perceptron. The authors themselves acknowledge that the classifier is likely the source of the performance difference between the LSTM parser and MaltParser. A more compelling evaluation would involve implementing BSP with a comparable LSTM-based classifier. As it stands, the results may simply reflect the superiority of the LSTM classifier rather than any inherent weakness in the direct transition-based approach itself.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The performance gap between the LSTM-based dependency parser baseline and your proposed BSP parser on primary edges is significant (~10 F1 points). What is the primary motivation for developing the direct BSP model when a conversion-based approach using established technology is so much more effective on the core structure? Is there a clear path to improving the remote edge prediction (currently at 13.3 F1) to a level that would justify this trade-off?
2.  The results strongly suggest that the choice of classifier (LSTM vs. perceptron) is a dominant factor in parser performance. Have you considered implementing BSP with an LSTM-based scoring model to create a more direct comparison between the underlying transition systems (i.e., your direct BSS system vs. the dependency conversion system)? Such a comparison would be critical to validating the design of the BSP system itself.
3.  The inverse conversion from dependency trees to UCCA-style constituency trees (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics, such as a high-attachment preference. Could you provide an analysis of the types and frequency of errors introduced by this inverse conversion step alone? This would help clarify how much of the performance ceiling for the dependency conversion method is due to structural information loss versus parser errors.
