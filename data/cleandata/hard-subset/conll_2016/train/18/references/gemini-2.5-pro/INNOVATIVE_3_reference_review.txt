**Summary and Contributions**
This paper introduces the task of Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), targeting semantic representations like UCCA that simultaneously exhibit three challenging structural properties: multiple parents (forming DAGs), non-terminal nodes over word spans, and discontinuous constituents. The authors present two main lines of attack. The first is a conversion-based approach, which assesses the viability of existing technology by converting UCCA graphs into more standard (but lossy) formalisms like dependency trees and discontinuous constituency trees, and then applying off-the-shelf parsers. The second, and more significant, contribution is a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed from the ground up to directly produce BSS graphs by integrating mechanisms for handling all three structural properties. The work is evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, establishing the first parsing results for this representation.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **Defining and providing the first-ever solution for a new, challenging semantic parsing task.** This work is foundational in that it formalizes the problem of parsing UCCA/BSS structures and provides the first concrete systems and benchmarks, opening up a promising new direction for research in semantic parsing.
2.  **The proposal of a novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP) that elegantly synthesizes existing techniques to handle the combined complexities of BSS.** The system's ability to natively model DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities in a single, coherent framework is a significant conceptual advance over pipeline or conversion-based methods.
3.  **A valuable comparative study of direct versus conversion-based parsing.** By benchmarking their novel parser against strong existing parsers applied via conversion, the authors provide a clear and insightful view of the problem's landscape, the limitations of current formalisms, and the potential of modern neural methods.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Novelty and Ambition:** The paper's greatest strength is its boldness. It moves away from incremental improvements on well-trodden tasks and instead tackles a new, structurally rich, and arguably more adequate semantic representation. The willingness to define a new problem and provide a first solution is precisely the kind of work that pushes the field forward. UCCA's combination of properties presents a unique challenge, and this paper is the first to meet it head-on.
2.  **A Principled and Conceptually Complete Model:** The proposed BSP parser is a direct and well-motivated solution. Instead of forcing the rich BSS structures into a restrictive formalism, the authors design a system to handle them natively. The transition system, which combines standard shift-reduce operations with specific transitions for creating non-terminals (NODE), handling discontinuities (SWAP), and forming multiple parent relations (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE), is a conceptually elegant contribution. Crucially, BSP is the only method presented that can actually produce the full target BSS graphs, including the remote edges that form DAG structures.
3.  **Insightful and Thorough Experimental Design:** The authors' decision to pursue two complementary parsing strategies is highly effective. The conversion-based approach provides a strong set of baselines and yields a fascinating result: a state-of-the-art stack-LSTM dependency parser, despite operating on a lossily converted representation, sets a very high bar for performance on the primary (tree) structure. This not only grounds the performance of the novel BSP parser but also clearly delineates the path for future improvements—namely, integrating more powerful neural classifiers into the direct parsing framework.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Performance Gap on Primary Edges:** The main empirical weakness is that the novel BSP parser is substantially outperformed on the primary edge F-score by the conversion-based LSTM parser (e.g., 59.9 F1 for BSP-Tree vs. 69.2 F1 for the LSTM). While BSP is the only system capable of predicting the full DAG, its lower performance on the core tree backbone is a limitation. However, this is a forgivable flaw in a first-of-its-kind system. The gap seems likely attributable to the difference in classifier power (perceptron vs. LSTM) rather than a fundamental flaw in the transition system, a point the authors themselves acknowledge. I see this not as a reason for rejection, but as a clear and exciting direction for immediate future work.
2.  **Modest Performance on Remote Edges:** While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, the performance is still quite low (13.3 F1). This indicates that capturing these long-distance, non-local dependencies that create the DAG structure is an especially hard problem that the current feature set struggles with. This is not unexpected for a first attempt, but it highlights a key area for improvement.
3.  **Novelty is in Synthesis, not Components:** The individual mechanisms used in BSP (e.g., SWAP for discontinuities, separate transitions for remote edges) have been explored before in other contexts. The novelty here lies in the skillful integration of these components into a single parser for the unique combination of challenges posed by UCCA. While a critic might argue this reduces the technical novelty, I believe the successful synthesis to solve a new and difficult problem is a substantial contribution in its own right.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The performance difference between BSP and the stack-LSTM parser is significant. To what extent do you believe this gap is due to the representational power of the classifier (perceptron vs. LSTM) versus potential search or modeling limitations in the proposed transition system for handling the tree-like backbone of UCCA structures?
2.  The novel `ratio` feature is shown to be quite impactful in the ablation study. Could you offer more intuition on its effect? For example, does it mainly prevent the spurious creation of non-terminal nodes via the NODE transition, and have you analyzed its effect on the distribution of transition choices during parsing?
3.  Given the low F-score on remote edges, do you have an analysis of the types of errors the parser makes? Are the errors concentrated on specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., control structures, arguments shared across clauses), or are they more diffuse? This could provide insight into what is needed to better model these dependencies.
