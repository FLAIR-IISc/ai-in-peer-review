**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces the task of Broad-coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), defined as parsing into semantic representations that support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors use the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instantiation of BSS. The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  The introduction and evaluation of a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed to directly produce BSS graphs by incorporating transitions for remote edge creation (for multiple parents) and swapping (for discontinuities).
2.  A comparative analysis using a conversion-based approach, where UCCA structures are lossily converted to dependency or discontinuous constituency trees. This allows for the application of strong existing parsers (e.g., MaltParser, UPARSE, and an LSTM-based parser), providing strong baselines for the new task.
3.  An empirical evaluation of these methods on the English UCCA corpus, demonstrating that while the conversion-based approach with an LSTM parser achieves the highest F-score on primary tree edges (69.2%), the proposed direct parser (BSP) is the only method capable of recovering remote edges, albeit with low performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Task Novelty and Importance:** The paper is, to my knowledge, the first to formalize and tackle the problem of parsing into UCCA structures. This is a valuable contribution, as UCCA represents a challenging and linguistically motivated semantic formalism. Addressing the joint problem of non-terminals, discontinuities, and re-entrancies (DAGs) in a single parsing model is a significant research direction.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The authors wisely pursue two complementary strategies: a direct parsing approach and an indirect conversion-based one. The inclusion of several strong, publicly available parsers in the conversion-based track, particularly the high-performing LSTM-based parser, provides a solid empirical grounding and a realistic assessment of where the proposed BSP model stands.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written. The problem is clearly motivated in the introduction, the UCCA formalism is explained sufficiently for the reader to understand the task, and the high-level descriptions of the parsing methods are easy to follow. The provision of algorithms for the conversion procedures is also a plus.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novelty of the task, the paper is marred by a significant number of methodological ambiguities, presentation flaws, and formatting errors that undermine the confidence in the work.

1.  **Methodological Opacity:** Several key technical details are either missing or insufficiently explained.
    *   **Conversion Algorithms:** The conversion from constituency to dependency in Algorithm 1 relies on `argminv Priority(lc(u, v))`, which uses a "linear order over possible edge labels". This priority order is a crucial hyperparameter of the conversion process, yet it is never defined or justified. Similarly, the inverse conversion in Algorithm 2 uses a function `Label(u)` which is deferred to supplementary material. This function is critical for reconstructing non-terminals and its logic should be explained in the main paper. The heuristic that "attachments are always high-attaching" is a strong assumption whose impact is not analyzed.
    *   **Transition System Conditions:** The conditions for applying transitions in Figure 2 are not fully specified. The condition `y 6;G x` for `LEFT-EDGE` is cryptic; I assume it means "y is not a descendant of x," but this should be stated explicitly. The `i(x) < i(y)` condition for `SWAP`, meant to handle discontinuities, is not explained beyond a brief note in the caption; the reader should not have to consult external papers (e.g., Maier, 2015) to understand the core mechanics of the proposed system.
    *   **Classifier Details:** The claim that the LSTM parser differs from MaltParser "only in its classifier" is a significant oversimplification. The models differ fundamentally in their feature representation (sparse indicators vs. dense, compositional representations). Furthermore, reporting the strong result from the LSTM parser without providing any details on its training setup (hyperparameters, how the converted data was fed into it) is insufficient for a scientific contribution.

2.  **Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness in its presentation, which is concerning.
    *   **Figure 1 is Flawed:** The figure is central to illustrating the core phenomena, yet it is inaccurate. The caption refers to a "remote edge (dashed)", but no dashed edge appears in Figure 1a. The legend defines a category 'F' which is never used in the examples. This is a critical error in a core figure.
    *   **Inconsistent and Unprofessional Referencing:** The bibliography is riddled with errors. Multiple entries contain stray question marks (e.g., "Abend and Rappoport.,? 2013") or formatting artifacts (e.g., "\Q2008\E"). The entry for Goldberg and Elhadad (2011) is truncated and merged with another reference. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Vague Definitions and Statements:** The definition of "grounded" in footnote 2 is circular ("tokens are directly annotated as part of the representation"). The paper's main argument hinges on BSP's ability to handle remote edges, but this capability results in a mere 13.3% F-score, which is not a strong practical advantage over the much higher-performing conversion methods. The conclusion that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement of 80-85% "underscores" the quality of a 69.2% F-score result is a stretch; it could equally be interpreted as showing a very large performance gap yet to be closed.

3.  **Incomplete Analysis:**
    *   **Evaluation Metric:** A new evaluation metric is proposed, but its properties are not discussed. How does it behave in corner cases? How does it compare to other potential graph-matching metrics? Without this analysis, it is difficult to fully interpret the reported scores.
    *   **Feature Ablation:** The description of the feature ablation states that "Almost all feature sets have a positive contribution... or otherwise to the prediction of remote edges". The phrasing "or otherwise" suggests that some features may have hurt primary edge performance. This potential trade-off is an interesting finding that should have been discussed explicitly rather than glossed over.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the `Priority` function used in Algorithm 1 and the `Label(u)` function from Algorithm 2? Understanding these is essential to replicating your conversion-based baselines.
2.  The core illustrative figure (Figure 1) appears to be incorrect regarding the depiction of remote edges. Can you confirm this and provide the correct visualization?
3.  The main takeaway seems to be that a carefully adapted existing parser (Dyer et al., 2015) significantly outperforms your novel direct parser (BSP) on the primary structure, while BSP's unique ability to handle remote edges is very weak (13.3 F1). Could you elaborate on why the direct approach should be pursued further, given this large performance gap? Why was an LSTM-based classifier not integrated into BSP for this submission, as it appears to be the most promising direction?
