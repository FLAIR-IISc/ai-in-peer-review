### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of Broad-coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), defined as parsing into a grounded semantic representation that supports three key properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The paper uses the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instantiation of BSS. The authors' main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Transition-Based Parser (BSP):** The primary contribution is the development of BSP, the first parser designed to directly produce BSS graphs. It combines and extends existing transition-based techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing to handle all three properties of BSS, notably by introducing new transitions to create "remote" edges for handling multiple parents.
2.  **A Rigorous Conversion-Based Baseline Study:** The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis by adapting existing state-of-the-art parsers to the BSS task. This is achieved by developing lossy conversion protocols from UCCA to dependency trees and discontinuous constituency trees. This study serves to benchmark the new task and contextualize the performance of the proposed BSP model.
3.  **Task and Metric Definition:** The paper effectively introduces and formalizes a new semantic parsing task. As part of this, it proposes a suitable evaluation metric for these complex graph structures based on the yield of labeled edges, which correctly collapses to standard PARSEVAL for tree structures.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for BSS. It clearly articulates the limitations of existing semantic representations (e.g., bi-lexical dependencies in SDP, lack of grounding in AMR) and justifies the necessity of supporting multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities with clear examples. This successfully frames UCCA parsing as a well-defined and important new research direction.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength. Rather than simply presenting their new parser in isolation, the authors first establish strong baselines by exploring a conversion-based approach. This is a very insightful way to proceed, as it allows them to leverage powerful existing models (like the stack-LSTM parser) and quantify the information loss inherent in the conversion (via the upper bound analysis). The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments further demonstrates the robustness of the findings.
3.  **First Complete Solution for a New, Challenging Task:** The proposed BSP parser is the first system capable of handling the full complexity of UCCA parsing, including the prediction of remote edges which create the DAG structure. While its components are adapted from prior work, their combination into a single, coherent system for this new task is a significant engineering and research achievement. The fact that it is made publicly available is a valuable contribution to the community, enabling future research in this area.
4.  **Honest and Insightful Analysis of Results:** The authors are transparent about the fact that their direct parsing approach (BSP) is outperformed on primary edges by a conversion-based baseline (the LSTM parser). This is an interesting and important finding, suggesting that the power of modern neural classifiers (LSTMs) can overcome the information loss from a lossy conversion. This honest reporting adds to the paper's credibility and provides a clear direction for future work: integrating more powerful classifiers into the direct parsing model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underperformance of the Main Proposed Model:** The most significant weakness is that the headline result (69.2% F-score) is achieved not by the novel BSP parser, but by a baseline using existing technology. The BSP parser lags behind by nearly 10 F-score points on primary edges (59.9%). While the paper suggests that using an LSTM classifier in BSP would likely improve results, this remains speculation. The current results leave the reader wondering about the efficacy of the direct transition-based approach itself, as the simpler dependency representation appears easier to learn, even after a lossy conversion.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Remote Edge Prediction:** The BSP parser's unique capability is predicting remote edges, but its performance on this subtask is very low (13.3% F-score in-domain). The paper does not provide a detailed error analysis for this crucial aspect of the model. It is unclear what types of remote edges are being missed, or what makes this task so difficult for the current model. A more in-depth analysis would be beneficial for understanding the remaining challenges and guiding future work. For instance, are the features for `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX` transitions insufficient?
3.  **Simplicity of the Dependency Conversion:** The conversion from constituency to dependency trees (Algorithm 1) and back (Algorithm 2) is heuristic and relatively simple. While this is acceptable for a baseline, the paper could have strengthened its analysis by discussing the impact of these specific conversion choices. For example, the inverse conversion (Algorithm 2) uses a rule-based function to re-introduce non-terminals and their labels, which seems likely to introduce a specific pattern of errors. It's unclear if a more sophisticated conversion scheme could have improved the upper bound for the dependency-based methods.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between the LSTM-based dependency parser and your perceptron-based BSP is substantial. Beyond the obvious difference in the classifier (LSTM vs. linear perceptron), do you believe there are other factors at play? For example, could the transition system for BSS be fundamentally more complex, leading to more error propagation than the simpler arc-standard system for dependencies?
2.  Given the very low performance on remote edges (13.3% F1), have you considered a pipeline approach as an alternative to a fully integrated parser? For instance, a first-stage parser could predict the primary tree structure (for which the LSTM model is very effective), and a second-stage model could then predict remote edges on top of this fixed tree. Do you have intuitions on whether such an approach might be more effective?
3.  Regarding the novel `ratio` feature (terminals-to-nodes ratio), this is an interesting idea for regularizing the creation of non-terminals. Could you provide a bit more intuition or analysis on its impact? Table 3 shows it improves recall significantly at the cost of precision. Does this suggest the baseline model is too conservative in creating `NODE` transitions, and this feature helps correct that?
