### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of Broad-coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), focusing on the UCCA formalism, which uniquely combines three challenging structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes for multi-word units, and discontinuous constituents. The authors make three primary contributions:

1.  **Introduction and Benchmarking of UCCA Parsing:** This is the first work to present a parsing system for the UCCA semantic representation. The authors establish the task, provide baseline results on existing UCCA corpora, and define a suitable evaluation metric. By promising to release their code and data converters, they lay the foundation for future research in this area.
2.  **A Novel Transition-Based Parser for Complex DAGs:** The paper proposes the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), a new transition-based system specifically designed to directly generate BSS structures. It synthesizes and extends techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing to handle the full complexity of UCCA, including the prediction of "remote" edges that create the DAG structure.
3.  **A Comparative Analysis via Conversion-Based Methods:** The authors cleverly use existing high-performance parsers as strong baselines by developing conversion protocols from UCCA to dependency and discontinuous constituency trees. This comparative framing provides valuable context for their direct parser's performance and offers insights into what aspects of the task are solvable with current technology. The analysis of the upper bounds of these conversions is particularly insightful.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Problem Motivation and Framing:** The paper does an outstanding job of motivating the need for semantic representations that support the three properties of BSS. The introduction clearly explains why each property is important, using illustrative examples (Figure 1), and situates the work effectively with respect to related formalisms like SDP and AMR. This clarity strongly establishes the importance and novelty of the problem being tackled.
2.  **Methodologically Sound Direct Parser Design:** The proposed BSP parser is a well-reasoned and logical extension of state-of-the-art transition-based parsing techniques. The transition set is a principled combination of actions for creating non-terminals (NODE), handling discontinuities (SWAP), and creating both primary (LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE) and remote (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX) edges. The feature set is comprehensive, drawing from successful parsers for related tasks, and the inclusion of the novel `ratio` feature is an interesting contribution.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. Comparing a novel direct parser against strong baselines derived from state-of-the-art systems (including an LSTM-based parser) is the right approach. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain test sets demonstrates the robustness of the findings. Furthermore, calculating and reporting the upper bounds for the conversion-based methods is a crucial piece of analysis that quantifies the information loss and helps interpret the results of the parsers that rely on them.
4.  **High Clarity and Potential for Impact:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The algorithms, transition system, and feature templates are all clearly presented. The commitment to releasing the systems and converters significantly lowers the barrier to entry for other researchers, which will likely spur further work on UCCA and other complex semantic representations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Sub-SOTA Performance of the Main Technical Contribution:** The primary weakness is that the paper's main novel contribution, the BSP parser, is significantly outperformed on primary edges by a baseline (59.9% vs. 69.2% F1). While BSP is the only system capable of predicting remote edges, its performance on them is very low (~13% F1), limiting its practical utility for capturing the full DAG structure at present. The authors rightly point to the success of the LSTM parser as a path forward, but as presented, the results show that a clever adaptation of existing technology is superior to the novel direct approach for the core tree structure of the representation.
2.  **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper provides a solid quantitative analysis but would be strengthened by a qualitative error analysis. What kinds of constructions or UCCA categories are most difficult for the parsers? Where does BSP fail in comparison to the conversion-based LSTM parser on primary edges? For the remote edges, what is the nature of the errors? Is the parser failing to identify any remote links (low recall), or is it proposing many incorrect ones (low precision)? Such an analysis would provide deeper insight into the remaining challenges and offer more specific guidance for future work.
3.  **Potential Underestimation of Conversion-Based Baseline:** The dependency-to-constituency back-conversion procedure (Algorithm 2) appears quite heuristic, particularly in its reconstruction of non-terminal nodes and the assignment of their labels via a rule-based `Label(u)` function. It is possible that a more sophisticated, perhaps learned, back-conversion model could further improve the already strong performance of the LSTM dependency parser. This raises the question of whether the performance gap between the conversion-based approach and the direct BSP parser is even larger than reported.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance difference between your direct BSP parser and the conversion-based LSTM parser on primary edges is the key result of the paper. You suggest that incorporating LSTMs into BSP is a promising next step. Could you elaborate on how you envision this? Would you primarily use LSTMs to replace the manual feature engineering with learned representations of the parser state (stack, buffer, etc.), as in Dyer et al. (2015), or do you foresee unique challenges in applying LSTMs to your more complex state representation, which includes non-terminal nodes?
2.  The F1-score for remote edges is quite low (~13%). Could you provide a breakdown of precision and recall for these edges? Is the primary challenge in identifying that a remote edge should exist (recall) or in correctly identifying its parent, child, and label (precision)? Is this poor performance mainly due to the sparsity of remote edges in the training data, or do you believe it points to a more fundamental issue with the model's ability to capture these long-distance, non-local dependencies?
3.  Regarding the dependency conversion pipeline: how sensitive are the final results of the dependency-based parsers to the heuristics in your back-conversion algorithm (Algorithm 2)? Specifically, how much impact do the rules within the `Label(u)` function and the high-attachment assumption have on the final F-score?
