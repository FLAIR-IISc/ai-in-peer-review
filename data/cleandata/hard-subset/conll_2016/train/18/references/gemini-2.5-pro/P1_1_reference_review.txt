### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces the task of Broad-coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), which aims to produce semantic representations that are grounded in text and support three key structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes for multi-word units, and discontinuous units. The paper focuses on UCCA, a semantic annotation scheme that embodies these properties but currently lacks a parser. The authors propose two main approaches: a conversion-based method that transforms UCCA graphs into more restricted formalisms (constituency or dependency trees) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers, and a direct approach with a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed specifically to produce BSS graphs. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a conversion-based approach using an LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest performance on the core tree structure (69.2% F-score on primary edges), while the direct BSP parser, though scoring lower on primary edges, is the only method capable of predicting the remote edges that create the full DAG structure.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Defining and tackling a new parsing task:** This is the first paper to formally define and provide a parsing solution for UCCA, a complex, broad-coverage semantic representation. By doing so, it opens up a new and challenging research direction in semantic parsing.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical study with strong baselines:** The authors provide a very strong benchmark for future work by not only proposing a new parser but also thoroughly evaluating how existing, powerful parsers perform on the task via lossy conversions. This contextualizes the difficulty of the problem and the performance of their proposed model.
3.  **A novel transition-based parser for complex graph structures (BSP):** The paper presents a complete design for a transition-based parser that natively handles DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities. While building on previous work, the integration of these capabilities into a single system for a grounded semantic formalism is a solid engineering and research contribution.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for parsers that can handle the three properties of BSS. The introduction clearly explains why existing formalisms like SDP or AMR are insufficient, thereby carving out a well-defined and important research gap that this work begins to fill.
2.  **Sound and Thorough Experimental Design:** The two-pronged approach—conversion-based vs. direct parsing—is a major strength. It allows the authors to leverage a very strong, state-of-the-art LSTM parser as a baseline, providing a realistic assessment of where the field stands. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation demonstrates the robustness of the methods, and the feature ablation study offers valuable insights into the proposed BSP model.
3.  **Novelty in Application and System Integration:** While the components of the BSP transition system draw from prior work on discontinuous and DAG parsing, their synthesis to parse a grounded semantic representation like UCCA is novel and well-executed. The system is thoughtfully designed to handle the specific properties of UCCA, such as distinguishing primary vs. remote edges.
4.  **Contribution to the Community:** The authors' commitment to release their parsers and converters is a significant contribution. It will lower the barrier to entry for other researchers to work on UCCA parsing, ensuring the work has a lasting impact and can be built upon directly.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The Proposed Direct Parser (BSP) is Outperformed by the Baseline:** The most significant weakness is that the novel BSP parser is substantially outperformed on primary edges (the core tree structure) by the baseline that converts to dependencies and uses an existing LSTM parser (59.0% vs. 69.2% F-score). While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on that subtask is very low (13.3% F-score), making its practical advantage over the much stronger baseline questionable at this stage. The paper acknowledges this, but it remains a central issue with the results.
2.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The BSP parser is primarily an integration of existing techniques. The transition system combines ideas from discontinuous constituency parsing (e.g., SWAP) and dependency DAG parsing (e.g., separating edge creation from REDUCE). The main new transitions (`LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX`) are an intuitive but incremental extension. The work's novelty lies more in the application and engineering than in fundamental new parsing algorithms.
3.  **Uncertainty Around the New Evaluation Metric:** The paper proposes a new, yield-based evaluation metric for BSS. While it seems reasonable and has the nice property of collapsing to PARSEVAL for trees, its properties are not deeply analyzed. As the first work on this task, establishing a robust and well-understood evaluation protocol is critical. It is unclear how sensitive this metric is to minor span errors or how it compares to other potential graph-matching metrics.
4.  **Key Details of Conversion Baselines are Underspecified:** The conversion algorithms, which are crucial for the strong baselines, lack some key details in the main paper. For example, Algorithm 1 depends on a pre-defined "linear order over possible edge labels" to select heads, and Algorithm 2 relies on a `Label(u)` function to create non-terminal labels. These heuristics, relegated to supplementary material, are critical for understanding the information loss during conversion and for reproducing the baseline results.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Given that the LSTM dependency parser baseline is over 10 F-score points better on primary edges, what is the main argument for pursuing the direct BSP approach, besides its current, low-performing ability to predict remote edges? Is there a theoretical ceiling on the conversion-based approach (e.g., due to information loss during conversion) that you believe a direct model could overcome with further improvements?
2.  The performance on remote edges is very low at 13.3% F-score. Could you provide a brief error analysis? For instance, does the parser fail to propose remote edges (recall issue), or does it propose them for the wrong nodes/with the wrong labels (precision issue)? Understanding the nature of these errors would be helpful for assessing the feasibility of improving this aspect of the model.
3.  The upper bound for the dependency conversion is 88.4% F-score, indicating a significant information loss of over 11 points even with perfect parsing. Could you elaborate on the main sources of this loss? Is it primarily from the loss of non-terminal nodes, the head-selection heuristics in Algorithm 1, or the non-terminal reconstruction heuristics (`Label(u)`) in Algorithm 2?
