### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations that can model multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents. The work focuses on the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of BSS. The authors present a two-pronged approach. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted to dependency or constituency trees, parsed with existing state-of-the-art tools, and then converted back. This serves to establish strong baselines. The second is a novel direct parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), which uses a transition system specifically designed to produce BSS graphs by combining techniques from dependency DAG, discontinuous constituency, and standard shift-reduce parsing. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a conversion-based approach using an LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest performance on the primary tree structure, while the direct BSP parser is the only system capable of recovering the full DAG structure, including remote edges.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **Defining the UCCA Parsing Task and Providing Strong Baselines:** This is the first work to tackle parsing for the UCCA formalism. By defining the task, developing an evaluation metric, and showing that a conversion to dependencies coupled with a state-of-the-art parser achieves nearly 70 F1 on primary edges, the paper provides an invaluable starting point and a high-bar baseline for all future research in this area.
2.  **A Novel, Unified Parser for Complex Semantic Structures:** The proposed BSP parser is a significant contribution as a proof-of-concept. It is the first system to integrate transitions for handling non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents within a single, coherent transition-based framework. This provides a clear and extensible model for parsing graph-structured, grounded semantic representations.
3.  **A Thorough Empirical Investigation:** The paper's empirical evaluation is comprehensive. Comparing a direct approach against multiple conversion-based methods (arc-standard, arc-eager, LSTM-based dependency; discontinuous constituency) is insightful and provides a clear picture of the current landscape. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation demonstrates the robustness of the methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper does an outstanding job of motivating the need for parsers that can handle the three core properties of BSS. The introduction clearly explains, with linguistic examples, why each property (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) is crucial for broad-coverage semantic representation, and correctly identifies the lack of a parser for UCCA as a major research gap.
2.  **Sound and Clever Experimental Design:** The decision to pursue both a conversion-based and a direct approach is a major strength. It not only allows for the creation of strong baselines by leveraging powerful existing technology (like LSTM parsers) but also cleanly isolates the unique challenge of parsing all BSS properties jointly. This dual approach makes the results much more informative than presenting the novel parser in isolation.
3.  **Clarity and High Potential for Impact:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the conversion algorithms (Algorithms 1 & 2) and the BSP transition system (Figure 2). The authors’ commitment to releasing all code and converters is commendable and significantly increases the paper's potential for impact, as it will enable other researchers to easily build upon this work.
4.  **Novelty in the Direct Parser Design:** While the individual transition types in BSP are inspired by previous work, their synthesis into a single system is novel and non-trivial. The system is thoughtfully designed to handle the specific constraints of UCCA (e.g., distinguishing primary vs. remote edges). The introduction of the `ratio` feature is a nice, simple idea that could prove useful in other transition-based constituency parsers.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Novel Parser:** The primary weakness is the performance gap between the main technical contribution (BSP) and the conversion-based baseline. BSP is outperformed by the LSTM dependency parser by nearly 10 F1 points on primary edges (59.9 vs. 69.2). While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on that subtask is very low (13.3 F1). This result, while honest, slightly undermines the argument for the more complex direct approach over a simpler, more effective pipeline/conversion method, at least for the primary structure.
2.  **Lack of In-depth Error Analysis:** The paper reports overall scores but would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed error analysis. For instance, what kinds of structures are difficult for BSP? Does it struggle with coordination, long-distance dependencies, or deeply nested non-terminals? Crucially, why is remote edge prediction performance so low? An analysis of precision vs. recall for remote edges, and examples of common errors, would provide invaluable insight for guiding future work.
3.  **Potentially Suboptimal Back-Conversion:** The conversion from dependency trees back to UCCA-style structures (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics (e.g., assuming high attachment, using a rule-based function for labels). The upper bound for this conversion is 88.4 F1, indicating that over 11 F1 points are lost solely due to the representation conversion. This is a substantial loss and may unfairly penalize the dependency-based parsers. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the impact of these heuristics or explored more sophisticated reconstruction techniques.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance difference between your direct BSP parser and the conversion-based LSTM parser on primary edges is quite large. Do you attribute this gap primarily to the power of the LSTM classifier versus your feature-based perceptron, or do you believe there are inherent search or learning challenges in BSP's more complex transition space that make it harder to train effectively?
2.  Your parser's ability to predict remote edges is a key advantage, but the F-score is very low. Could you provide a precision/recall breakdown for remote edges? Is the main issue that the parser is too conservative and misses most remote edges (low recall), or that it proposes many incorrect ones (low precision)?
3.  The upper bound of 88.4 F1 for the dependency conversion pipeline suggests the back-conversion step is a major source of error. Have you experimented with alternative, perhaps learned, reconstruction models instead of the heuristic-based Algorithm 2? Do you believe a better reconstruction module could significantly close the gap between the upper bound and the LSTM parser's performance?
