### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of broad-coverage semantic parsing, defined as parsing into structures that support (1) multiple parents (DAGs), (2) non-terminal nodes, and (3) discontinuous units. The authors use the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instantiation of such a formalism. The paper presents a two-pronged approach to tackle this new task. First, it explores a conversion-based method, where UCCA structures are lossily converted to more restricted formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees), parsed with existing state-of-the-art parsers, and then converted back. Second, it proposes a novel transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), which is designed to directly produce the full range of UCCA structures. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a conversion-based approach using an LSTM-based dependency parser achieves the highest F-score (69.2%) on the core tree structure (primary edges), while the novel BSP parser, though scoring lower on primary edges, is the only method capable of predicting the non-tree "remote" edges that create the DAG structure.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Introduction and formalization of a new parsing task.** The paper is the first to address parsing for the UCCA formalism. By defining the properties of Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), it clearly articulates the challenges involved and provides a valuable framework for future research in this area. It effectively establishes a new benchmark task for the community.
2.  **A rigorous and insightful experimental design.** The use of a conversion-based approach alongside a novel direct parser is a major strength. This methodology not only establishes strong baselines using existing SOTA technology but also effectively quantifies the information loss inherent in simplifying the representation. This provides a clear picture of what can be achieved by re-framing the problem versus what requires a new architectural solution.
3.  **The development of the first end-to-end parser for UCCA.** The Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) is a solid and principled first attempt at a dedicated parser for this complex task. While its performance is not yet state-of-the-art, it serves as a crucial proof of concept and an extensible foundation for future work, being the only model presented that can handle all three properties of BSS.

### Strengths

1.  **High-impact problem definition and novelty.** The paper tackles a well-motivated and challenging problem. The need for semantic representations that can handle re-entrancy (multiple parents), phrasal units (non-terminals), and non-local phenomena (discontinuities) is well-established in linguistics and computational semantics. This work is the first to propose and evaluate concrete parsing methods for a formalism (UCCA) that supports all three. This is a significant step towards parsing richer and more expressive semantic graphs.
2.  **Excellent and thorough evaluation.** The experimental setup is comprehensive and well-thought-out. The authors compare their novel parser against multiple strong baselines derived from different formalisms (constituency vs. dependency) and different parsing technologies (perceptron, SVM, LSTM). Including upper bounds for the conversion methods is particularly insightful, as it cleanly separates parser error from representation-conversion error. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments demonstrates the robustness of the findings.
3.  **Methodological transparency and scientific honesty.** The authors are transparent about the performance of their models. It is commendable that they prominently feature the result that a baseline (the LSTM dependency parser) outperforms their own novel parser (BSP) on the primary evaluation metric. This finding is valuable in itself, as it suggests that powerful sequence models can overcome some of the information loss from the conversion and that future work should focus on integrating such models into the direct parsing approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Sub-optimal performance of the primary methodological contribution.** The main novel system proposed, BSP, is significantly outperformed on primary edges by the conversion-based LSTM parser (59.9% vs. 69.2% F-score). While BSP is the only system that can predict remote edges, its performance on that subtask is very low (13.3% F-score). This raises the question of whether, for practical purposes, one would be better off using the stronger parser for the backbone structure and developing a separate, dedicated model for predicting remote edges on top of it. The paper doesn't explore such a pipeline alternative.
2.  **Limited novelty in the parser's architecture.** The BSP parser is a competent integration of existing techniques rather than a breakthrough in parsing methodology. The transition system largely combines known elements from discontinuous constituency parsing (e.g., SWAP from Maier, 2015) and DAG dependency parsing (e.g., decoupling edge creation from REDUCE, as in Sagae and Tsujii, 2008). The novel features and transitions are straightforward extensions for UCCA. While this is perfectly acceptable for introducing a new task, the methodological contribution is incremental.
3.  **Lack of clarity in the back-conversion algorithm.** The dependency-to-constituency back-conversion procedure (Algorithm 2) is crucial for the dependency-based baselines, but its description is somewhat opaque. The mechanism for creating intermediate nodes and the rule-based function `Label(u)` are not fully specified. The text notes that `Label(u)` "mostly selects the UCCA label Center", which is vague. Since the quality of this heuristic-based reconstruction directly impacts the final scores of the strongest baselines, a more detailed explanation (perhaps in an appendix) would be necessary to fully understand the results and ensure reproducibility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the impressive performance of the LSTM-based dependency parser on primary edges, it seems clear that the classifier is a key component. What are the primary obstacles to replacing the structured perceptron in your BSP parser with a stack-LSTM or similar neural architecture? Do you believe this would be the most promising path toward improving performance on both primary and remote edges simultaneously?
2.  The performance on remote edges is currently very low. Based on your analysis, what makes these edges so much harder to predict than primary edges? Is the difficulty primarily due to their sparsity in the training data, a lack of informative features, or inherent linguistic ambiguity that is difficult to resolve from local context?
3.  Could you provide more detail on the `Label(u)` function and the logic for inserting an intermediate node (`u'`) in Algorithm 2? Understanding the heuristics of this back-conversion is important for interpreting the upper bound and the performance of the dependency-based systems. For example, are there specific dependency labels or configurations that trigger the creation of a `Center` node?
