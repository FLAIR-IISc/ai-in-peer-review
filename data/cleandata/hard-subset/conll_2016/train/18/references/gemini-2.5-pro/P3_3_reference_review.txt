### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of grounded semantic representations that are directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) allowing for non-terminal nodes and discontinuous constituents. The paper presents the first parsing solutions for Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA), a concrete BSS framework that previously had no available parser. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The first parser for the UCCA formalism:** The paper presents the first systems capable of parsing text into UCCA structures. This is a significant contribution that opens the door to using this rich, cross-linguistically motivated semantic representation in downstream NLP applications.
2.  **A novel, direct transition-based parser (BSP):** The authors develop a new parser that can directly generate BSS graphs. This parser, BSP, capably synthesizes and extends state-of-the-art techniques from transition-based parsers for discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing into a unified model.
3.  **A rigorous, conversion-based evaluation framework:** The paper proposes a clever and insightful method for benchmarking their new parser. By converting UCCA structures to and from more standard formalisms (dependency and constituency trees), they are able to leverage powerful existing parsers as strong baselines. This not only contextualizes the performance of their novel system but also provides a valuable analysis of the capabilities of current technology on this new task.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a novel and important problem. As the field moves towards richer semantic representations, the ability to parse structures with DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities is crucial. By being the first to tackle UCCA parsing, this work represents a substantial and timely contribution.
2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The two-pronged approach—developing a direct parser while also creating strong baselines via conversion—is excellent scientific practice. It allows for a nuanced understanding of the problem's difficulty and the performance of the proposed solution. The direct parser, BSP, is a well-motivated and technically sound integration of relevant prior work, with a clearly defined transition system and a comprehensive feature set.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. Comparing against multiple strong parsers, including a state-of-the-art stack-LSTM dependency parser, provides a robust assessment. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain test sets demonstrates the generalizability of the findings. Furthermore, the calculation of upper bounds for the conversion methods and the feature ablation study are highly informative, adding depth to the analysis.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The properties of BSS are well-illustrated, and the technical details of both the conversion algorithms and the BSP parser are explained with sufficient clarity for reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Proposed BSP Parser:** The central novel contribution, the BSP parser, is significantly outperformed on its primary task (parsing primary edges) by a baseline that relies on conversion to dependencies and an off-the-shelf LSTM-based parser (59.0% F1 vs. 69.2% F1). While BSP has the unique capability of predicting remote edges, its performance on that front is very low (13.3% F1), making its practical advantage questionable. This result suggests that the proposed direct model, at least with a perceptron classifier and greedy search, is not yet competitive with a simpler, stronger alternative.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Key Parsing Challenges:** While the paper successfully reports performance on primary and remote edges, it misses an opportunity for deeper analysis. The performance on remote edges is extremely low, but there is little discussion as to why. An error analysis exploring which types of re-entrant structures are most difficult to capture, or whether the failures are due to data sparsity or feature limitations, would have been very valuable. Such an analysis would provide clearer directions for future work on this challenging aspect of the problem.
3.  **Potentially Weak Conversion Baselines:** The procedure for converting dependency trees back into UCCA constituency structures (Algorithm 2) relies on simple heuristics to re-introduce non-terminal nodes and their labels. The fact that the upper bound for this conversion is only 88.4% indicates that a substantial amount of information is lost. This lossiness may unfairly penalize the dependency-based parsers, meaning that the true performance gap between them and BSP could be even larger than reported. A more sophisticated or learned back-conversion model could have provided a stronger baseline.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The largest performance gap is between your BSP parser and the LSTM-based dependency parser baseline. Do you believe this ~10 F1-point gap is primarily due to the power of the classifier (LSTM vs. perceptron), or could there be more fundamental reasons? For example, is the search space for the direct BSP parser significantly larger and more complex, making greedy search more prone to error propagation compared to the dependency parsing task?
2.  The `ratio` feature is an interesting, novel contribution. The ablation study shows it provides a modest 0.5% F1 improvement on primary edges by increasing recall at the expense of precision. Could you elaborate on its effect? Does it primarily encourage the creation of more non-terminal nodes, and is there a risk that it learns a corpus-level statistic that may not generalize well?
3.  Could you provide a brief qualitative analysis of the errors on remote edges? Are there specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., control structures, relative clauses) where argument sharing is consistently missed? Understanding these errors seems critical for improving performance on the DAG-aspect of UCCA parsing.
