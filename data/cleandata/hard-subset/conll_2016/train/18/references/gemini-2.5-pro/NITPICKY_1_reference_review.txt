### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the task of Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), which aims to parse sentences into graph structures that accommodate multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. The work uses the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instantiation of BSS. The authors propose two main approaches: a conversion-based method that transforms UCCA structures into dependency or constituency trees to leverage existing parsers, and a direct method using a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed to handle all three properties of BSS. Experiments are conducted on UCCA-annotated corpora, with the highest performance achieved not by the novel BSP parser, but by a conversion-based approach using a state-of-the-art LSTM dependency parser.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **Task Formulation and Empirical Baseline Establishment:** The primary contribution is the introduction and formalization of the UCCA parsing task. The paper provides a valuable service by establishing the first parsing results on this corpus, including a set of strong baselines using conversion-based methods. The finding that an adapted state-of-the-art dependency parser significantly outperforms a purpose-built model is an important empirical result.
2.  **A Novel (Albeit Underperforming) Transition System:** The paper proposes BSP, a transition-based parser that combines techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and DAG dependency parsing. While its components are not entirely new, their synthesis into a single system for BSS is a novel contribution.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Tackles a Novel and Important Problem:** The paper addresses the parsing of complex, graph-structured semantic representations that capture phenomena often simplified or ignored by more common tree-based schemes. Broad-coverage semantic parsing is a crucial direction for the field, and this work represents a serious first attempt at parsing UCCA.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The authors rightly compare their direct approach not only to a simple upper bound but also to strong baselines created by adapting existing high-performance parsers. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation provides a good measure of the robustness of the proposed methods.
3.  **Methodological Transparency:** The paper clearly lays out its conversion algorithms and the transition system for the BSP parser. While there are issues with clarity (see Weaknesses), the intent to provide a reproducible methodology is commendable. The authors' promise to release code and converters is a welcome contribution to the community.

### **Weaknesses**

The paper, while addressing an interesting problem, is undermined by a substantial number of methodological gaps, presentation flaws, and experimental results that question its primary claims.

1.  **Serious Methodological Ambiguities and Errors:** Several core components of the methodology are either undefined or appear to be flawed.
    *   The constituency-to-dependency conversion (Algorithm 1) relies on a `Priority` function that defines a "linear order over possible edge labels L". This function is crucial for determining head-child relationships but is never defined, leaving a critical step of the methodology entirely unspecified.
    *   The reverse conversion (Algorithm 2) seems structurally incorrect. The line `p ← Parentc(t′)` assigns the pre-terminal of the head token `t'` to `p`. The subsequent attachment `Ec ← Ec ∪ {(p, u′)}` makes the new node `u'` a child of a pre-terminal node. This is highly unorthodox for constituency structures and suggests a potential flaw in the algorithm's logic.
    *   In the description of the BSP parser (Section 4), the text states that the `NODE_X` transition creates a new node "on the buffer", while Figure 2 clearly shows the new node being pushed onto the stack. This is a direct and confusing contradiction.

2.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Proposed Model:** The central technical contribution, the BSP parser, performs very poorly. It achieves an F-score of 59.9% on primary edges (BSPTree), which is nearly 10 points lower than the 69.2% F-score achieved by the baseline LSTM dependency parser. The paper's conclusion misleadingly highlights the baseline's score ("UCCA can be parsed with 69.2% primary F-score") without properly contextualizing that this result invalidates the effectiveness of their own proposed model. The performance on remote edges (13.3% F-score) is also extremely low.

3.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Flaws:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulous preparation, which detracts from its professional quality.
    *   The bibliography is replete with errors: random question marks (`Abend and Rappoport.,?`), inconsistent formatting (`Ambati et al\.,?`), apparent BibTeX artifacts (`Sagae and Tsujii., \Q2008\E`), and broken entries (the Goldberg and Elhadad, 2011 citation seems garbled with text from another reference). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable.
    *   Figure 1's legend defines a label 'F' (function unit) which does not appear in any of the accompanying examples.
    *   Footnote 1 is simply a raw URL, which is improper formatting.
    *   The condition `y 6;G x` for the `LEFT-EDGE_X` transition in Figure 2 is not explained.

4.  **Limited Novelty of the BSP Parser:** The BSP transition system is largely a combination of existing ideas: SWAP for discontinuities from Nivre (2009) and Maier (2015), and separate REDUCE actions for DAGs from Sagae and Tsujii (2008). The set of novel features specific to UCCA is minimal, and while the "ratio" feature is interesting, it represents a very minor contribution.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a precise, formal definition of the `Priority` function used in Algorithm 1 to establish a linear order over UCCA edge labels? On what linguistic or empirical basis was this ordering determined?
2.  Please clarify the logic of Algorithm 2. Is it intended for the parent of a new node (`u'`) to be the pre-terminal of its head (`Parentc(t')`)? If so, could you justify this unconventional structure? If not, could you provide the corrected algorithm?
3.  Please resolve the contradiction in the description of the `NODE_X` transition. Does it place the new node on the buffer or the stack?
4.  The main technical contribution of this paper, the BSP parser, is outperformed by the conversion-based LSTM baseline by a very large margin (~10 F1 points). In light of this, what is the primary argument for the utility of the BSP model as presented? Does this result not suggest that future work should focus exclusively on improving conversion-based methods?
