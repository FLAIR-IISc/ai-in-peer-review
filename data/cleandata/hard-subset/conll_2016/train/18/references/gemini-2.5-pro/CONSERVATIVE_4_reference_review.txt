**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), exemplified by the UCCA annotation scheme. These structures are challenging as they can be DAGs (allowing multiple parents), contain non-terminal nodes, and feature discontinuities. The authors propose two approaches: first, converting UCCA structures to more restricted formalisms (constituency and dependency trees) to leverage existing parsers; second, a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed to handle all three properties of BSS directly.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  Establishing strong initial benchmarks for the novel task of UCCA parsing. The use of conversion methods to apply powerful, established parsers like the stack-LSTM dependency parser provides a valuable and high-performing baseline for future work.
2.  The introduction and evaluation of a new transition-based parser (BSP) for this task, which serves as a proof-of-concept for directly parsing such complex structures, even if its performance is not state-of-the-art.
3.  A clear articulation of the parsing challenges posed by semantic schemes like UCCA, which helps frame the problem for the community.

**Strengths**

1.  **Thorough Baselines:** The authors are to be commended for the conversion-based approach, which allows for a direct comparison against several well-established parsers. In particular, the inclusion of the stack-LSTM parser (Dyer et al., 2015) is crucial, as it represents the state of the art for transition-based dependency parsing and sets a high bar for performance. This rigorous comparison provides an essential sanity check for their novel approach.
2.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The introduction provides a well-motivated and clear description of the specific structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) that make this parsing task difficult and distinct from standard dependency or constituency parsing. This effectively frames the problem and justifies the need for new research in this area.
3.  **Publicly Available Resources:** The experiments are conducted on publicly available corpora, and the authors promise to release their parsers and converters. This is good scientific practice and will facilitate reproducibility and future work on this task.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Proposed Method is Substantially Outperformed by a Baseline:** The most significant weakness of this work is that the central novel contribution, the BSP parser, is not competitive with the strongest baseline. The stack-LSTM parser, a well-established method applied via a straightforward conversion, achieves a primary edge F-score of 69.2%. In contrast, the authors' BSP parser (even in its tree-only configuration, BSPTree, for a fair comparison) achieves only 59.9%. A performance gap of nearly 10 F-score points is very large and strongly suggests that the proposed transition system and feature set are not as effective as existing technology. This undermines the argument that a new, specialized parser is currently the best path forward for this problem.
2.  **Unconvincing Performance on the Core Differentiating Task:** The main advantage of BSP over conversion-based methods is its ability to predict "remote" edges, which form the DAG structure. However, the reported performance is extremely low, at 13.3 F-score. This result is difficult to interpret as a success. While technically non-zero, it is not clear if this level of performance is practically meaningful or substantially better than what a simple heuristic might achieve. The paper presents a system that can, in principle, handle DAGs, but fails to demonstrate that it can do so effectively.
3.  **Incremental Novelty of the Proposed Parser:** The BSP parser is largely a careful combination of techniques from prior work on transition-based parsing for different phenomena: discontinuous constituency parsing (Maier, 2015) and dependency DAG parsing (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008). The primary novelties appear to be new transitions for remote edges and a handful of UCCA-specific features. While assembling existing components to tackle a new task is a valid research contribution, the limited novelty combined with the inferior performance makes the contribution less impactful.
4.  **Misleading Framing of Results:** The conclusion states that "UCCA can be parsed with 69.2% primary F-score". While this statement is factually correct, it is misleading because this result was achieved by the baseline system (the converted stack-LSTM parser), not the authors' proposed BSP parser. This phrasing obscures the main empirical finding of the paper: that an established method significantly outperforms their novel one.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The comparison between the perceptron-based MaltParser (60.1 F1) and the LSTM-based parser (69.2 F1), both using an arc-standard system, strongly suggests that the neural classifier is the primary source of the performance gain, not the transition system itself. Your proposed BSP parser uses a linear perceptron classifier. Given this, isn't the comparison between BSP and the LSTM baseline confounded? A more direct comparison would be to implement your BSP transition system with an LSTM-based classifier. Without this, it seems the main conclusion is simply that LSTMs are better classifiers than perceptrons, which is already well-established.
2.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis for the remote edge prediction? With an F-score of only 13.3, are there specific types of remote relations that the parser fails on? Is this low score due to recall (never proposing remote edges) or precision (proposing many incorrect ones)? This would help in assessing whether the proposed mechanism is fundamentally flawed or simply needs better features/training.
3.  Given that a simple conversion plus a state-of-the-art parser works so well, what is the primary motivation for pursuing a more complex, direct transition-based approach that currently performs much worse? Could the research effort not be more fruitfully directed at improving the conversion/reconstruction process to recover remote edges, rather than designing a new parser from scratch?
