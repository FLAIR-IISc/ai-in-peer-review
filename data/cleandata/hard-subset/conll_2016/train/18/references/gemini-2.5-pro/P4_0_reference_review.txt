**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of grounded semantic representations that are directed acyclic graphs (DAGs), can have non-terminal nodes, and can represent discontinuous constituents. The UCCA annotation scheme is presented as a concrete instance of BSS. The authors propose and evaluate two main approaches to this task. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted into more standard representations (discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees), parsed with existing state-of-the-art parsers, and then converted back. The second approach is a novel, direct parser called Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), which uses a transition-based system specifically designed to produce BSS graphs by combining and extending techniques from discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing. The methods are evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, with a conversion-based approach using an LSTM dependency parser achieving the highest F-score (69.2%) on the core tree structure, while the direct BSP parser is the only method capable of predicting the graph's remote (re-entrant) edges, albeit with lower accuracy.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **Formulation of the BSS Parsing Task and First Solution for UCCA:** The paper is the first to formalize and provide a parsing solution for the UCCA semantic representation. By defining the problem and establishing strong initial baselines, it opens up a new and challenging parsing task for a promising, broad-coverage semantic formalism, laying the groundwork for future research in this area.
2.  **A Novel Transition-Based Parser for Complex Semantic Graphs:** The paper presents BSP, a technically sound transition-based parser that integrates mechanisms for handling non-terminal nodes, discontinuities, and multiple parents within a single framework. The system's design, which includes novel transitions for distinguishing primary vs. remote edges, is a non-trivial extension of existing parsing technology tailored to the specific challenges of BSS.
3.  **A Thorough Empirical Comparison of Direct and Conversion-Based Methods:** The work provides a valuable comparative study, evaluating the proposed direct parser against strong baselines that leverage state-of-the-art constituency and dependency parsers. This analysis, which includes upper-bound calculations for the conversion process, offers clear insights into the trade-offs between the approaches and demonstrates that existing high-performance models can be effectively adapted to parse the core tree-like structure of UCCA.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Problem:** The paper addresses a novel and important parsing problem. The task of parsing grounded semantic representations that are simultaneously constituency-based, non-projective, and DAGs is under-explored. The authors provide clear motivation for why these structural properties are necessary for broad-coverage semantics, making a strong case for the relevance of the task.
2.  **Sound and Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The evaluation is methodologically rigorous. Comparing a novel, direct parser against strong, conversion-based baselines using multiple state-of-the-art systems (UPARSE, MaltParser, Dyer et al. 2015) provides a robust assessment of the proposed method. The inclusion of upper-bound scores for the conversion methods is particularly insightful, as it cleanly separates parsing errors from information loss due to the representation conversion. The use of both in-domain and out-of-domain test sets further strengthens the empirical claims.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for BSS is clearly articulated with illustrative examples (Figure 1). The technical details of both the conversion algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) and the BSP transition system (Figure 2) are presented with sufficient clarity to allow for reproducibility. The overall structure of the paper is logical, building a compelling narrative from problem definition to evaluation.
4.  **Public Release of Resources:** The authors' commitment to making all converters and parsers publicly available is a significant strength, as it will lower the barrier to entry for other researchers and facilitate future work on UCCA parsing.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Proposed BSP Parser:** The main technical contribution, the BSP parser, is significantly outperformed on its primary task—predicting primary edges—by a baseline method (59.0% F1 for BSP vs. 69.2% F1 for the LSTM parser on converted dependencies). While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on this task is very low (13.3% F1). As presented, the results suggest that a simpler, high-performing conversion-based pipeline is currently the superior approach for parsing the main UCCA structure, which somewhat diminishes the impact of the more complex, direct parser.
2.  **Lack of In-Depth Error Analysis:** The paper presents aggregate performance scores but provides little analysis of the errors made by the BSP parser. It is unclear why BSP underperforms the LSTM baseline on primary edges or what types of structures cause it to fail. For the low-performing remote edge prediction, an analysis of precision vs. recall, common error patterns (e.g., specific labels, long-distance attachments), or the impact of data sparsity would be invaluable for understanding the parser's limitations and would provide a clearer path for future improvements.
3.  **Complexity of the BSP System without Clear Justification for All Components:** The BSP parser combines a large number of features and a complex transition set from various previous works. While the feature ablation study is useful, it is not entirely clear which specific design choices in BSP are critical innovations for UCCA parsing, versus which are simply inherited from prior work on related but distinct tasks. For example, the set of novel features for remote edge prediction is very small, which may contribute to its poor performance. A more focused discussion on the novel challenges posed by UCCA and how the new components of BSP specifically address them would strengthen the paper's technical contribution.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The LSTM dependency parser baseline achieves a primary F-score that is over 10 points higher than BSP. Given this large performance gap, could you elaborate on the argument for pursuing the direct parsing approach? Are there specific types of structures in UCCA that are fundamentally unrecoverable through a dependency conversion, even if a more sophisticated inverse conversion algorithm were used?
2.  The performance on remote edges is very low. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of this result? For example, what are the precision and recall, and are errors concentrated on particular edge labels or across specific distances? Is the primary bottleneck data sparsity, or do you suspect limitations in the model's features or transition system for this sub-task?
3.  The dependency conversion procedure (Algorithm 1) relies on a predefined priority over edge labels to identify a head child (`h(u)`). Could you specify this priority order and explain the linguistic or empirical motivation behind it? Furthermore, how sensitive is the performance of the high-scoring LSTM dependency parser to this specific head-finding heuristic?
