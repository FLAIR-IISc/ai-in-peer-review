### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the first parsing methods for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations that are directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) allowing non-terminal nodes and discontinuities. The work is framed around UCCA, a specific BSS scheme for which no parser previously existed. The authors pursue two complementary strategies: 1) A conversion-based approach, where UCCA structures are lossily converted into simpler formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers. 2) A direct approach, for which they propose the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), a novel transition-based parser that natively handles all three properties of BSS. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a conversion-based approach using a stack-LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest performance on primary (tree) edges, while the proposed BSP is the only method capable of recovering remote (non-tree) edges, albeit with low accuracy.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **The first parser and parsing methodology for the UCCA formalism.** This is a significant contribution, as it makes a rich, cross-linguistically motivated semantic representation scheme computationally accessible for the first time. The work establishes strong baselines and provides a publicly available tool for the community.
2.  **A systematic comparison of direct vs. conversion-based parsing for complex semantic DAGs.** By evaluating both approaches, the paper provides valuable insights into the trade-offs involved. It demonstrates that existing technology can be surprisingly effective after a lossy conversion, setting a high bar for bespoke parsers.
3.  **The proposal of BSP, a transition-based parser unifying techniques for DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities.** While building on prior work, BSP represents a novel synthesis of transition system components specifically tailored to BSS parsing, demonstrating the feasibility of a direct approach.

### Strengths

1.  **High-impact problem:** The paper tackles a novel and important problem. Broad-coverage semantic parsing is a key goal in NLP, and existing formalisms often make simplifying assumptions (e.g., bi-lexical dependencies in SDP, non-grounding in AMR). UCCA offers an appealing, linguistically-grounded alternative, but its lack of a parser has been a major barrier to adoption. This work directly removes that barrier.
2.  **Thorough and insightful experimental design:** The decision to explore both conversion-based and direct parsing methods is a major strength. It provides a much richer picture than simply presenting a single new model. Using very strong off-the-shelf parsers, particularly the stack-LSTM dependency parser, establishes a robust and challenging baseline. This contextualizes the performance of the proposed BSP model and clearly highlights areas for future work. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation further strengthens the empirical results.
3.  **Clarity and quality of writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly motivates the need for the three properties of BSS and situates UCCA within the landscape of semantic representations. The proposed methods, including the conversion algorithms and the BSP transition system, are explained clearly and precisely. The results are presented transparently and the conclusions are well-supported by the evidence.
4.  **Contribution to reproducible research:** The authors' commitment to making their converters and parsers publicly available is commendable and significantly increases the value of their contribution. This will undoubtedly spur further research on UCCA and other complex semantic representations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The proposed BSP parser is significantly outperformed by a baseline.** The paper's main novel technical contribution, the BSP parser, achieves a primary edge F-score of 59.0%, which is more than 10 F-score points behind the 69.2% achieved by the conversion-based approach using the Dyer et al. (2015) parser. While BSP is the only model that can predict remote edges, its performance on them is very low (13.3% F-score). This raises the question of whether the direct approach, as implemented, is the most promising path forward. The authors rightly conclude that incorporating LSTMs into BSP is a key next step, but as it stands, the results suggest that improving conversion/reconstruction methods might be an equally, if not more, fruitful direction.
2.  **The novelty of the BSP parser's design is incremental.** The authors are transparent that BSP combines and extends existing techniques from transition-based parsing for dependency DAGs (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008), discontinuous constituency (Maier, 2015), and standard constituency (Zhang and Clark, 2009). The primary novelties are the specific combination for the UCCA task, the addition of `REMOTE` transitions, and a new `ratio` feature. While this is a solid piece of engineering, the core technical ideas in the parser design are not themselves new. The contribution lies more in the novel application and system integration than in fundamental algorithmic advances.
3.  **Lack of a detailed error analysis.** The paper reports aggregate precision, recall, and F-scores, but provides little insight into the *types* of errors each model makes. For instance, how do the conversion-based parsers and BSP compare in handling specific phenomena like coordination or multi-word expressions? Which UCCA edge labels are the most difficult to predict? An error analysis could have provided a more nuanced understanding of the relative strengths and weaknesses of the direct vs. indirect approaches and offered clearer guidance for future work. For example, it would be interesting to know if the 11.6% F-score gap in the dependency conversion upper bound is concentrated on particular structures.

### Questions to Authors

1.  You convincingly argue that incorporating a stack-LSTM-style classifier into BSP is a promising direction for future work. Could you elaborate on the potential challenges in this integration? Given that BSP's state includes a partially built DAG and its transitions create non-terminals (unlike the dependency parser), would a straightforward replacement of the perceptron with an LSTM be sufficient, or would it require a more fundamental redesign of the state representation?
2.  The performance on remote edges is very low (13.3% F-score). What is your main hypothesis for this difficulty? Is it primarily a data sparsity issue (remote edges being rare), an issue with the feature set being too local for these long-distance dependencies, or a fundamental limitation of a greedy, transition-based approach for such phenomena?
3.  The dependency conversion upper bound (88.4%) shows a significant information loss compared to the constituency conversion (100% on primary edges). Your dependency-to-constituency conversion algorithm (Alg. 2) uses heuristics like assuming high-attachment. Could you quantify how much of the drop in the upper bound is attributable to this heuristic versus other aspects of the conversion, such as the initial head-finding procedure (Alg. 1)? Have you considered a learnable model for the back-conversion, which might be able to recover more of the original structure?
