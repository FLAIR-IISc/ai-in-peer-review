### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of broad-coverage semantic parsing into UCCA, a representation scheme that supports DAG structures (multiple parents), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. The authors propose two distinct approaches. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted to constituency or dependency trees, allowing off-the-shelf parsers to be trained. The second is a novel, direct transition-based parser, BSP, which is specifically designed to produce the full range of UCCA structures by integrating techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing. The methods are evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, establishing the first parsing results for this representation.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Pioneering the task of UCCA parsing:** This is the first work to formalize and provide a computational solution for parsing into the UCCA semantic representation. By defining the task, creating strong baselines, proposing a suitable evaluation metric, and releasing code, the paper lays a crucial foundation for future research in this area.
2.  **A comprehensive and insightful comparative study:** The paper presents a rigorous comparison between a direct parsing model and several strong conversion-based baselines. The inclusion of state-of-the-art dependency parsers (including a neural stack-LSTM model) provides a clear and valuable picture of the challenges of the task and demonstrates that existing technology can be surprisingly effective, setting a high bar for future models.
3.  **The design of a novel and versatile transition-based parser (BSP):** The proposed BSP parser is a well-motivated synthesis of modern transition-based parsing techniques. It is the only presented model capable of directly handling all core properties of the target formalism, most notably the prediction of remote edges to form DAG structures.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a novel and well-motivated problem. By targeting a grounded semantic representation that handles phenomena like coordination and multi-word expressions more gracefully than bi-lexical dependency schemes, this work addresses a clear gap in the semantic parsing landscape. It is a foundational piece of research that opens up a new and promising direction for the community.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is excellent. The authors not only propose a new model but also compare it against a variety of strong baselines derived from high-performing, publicly available parsers. The analysis is multifaceted, including upper-bound calculations for the conversion methods, in-domain and out-of-domain experiments, and a feature ablation study. This comprehensive approach gives a clear sense of the task's difficulty and the relative merits of different strategies.
3.  **Clarity and Quality of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly motivates the need for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) with illustrative examples. The proposed methods, including the conversion algorithms and the transition system for the BSP parser, are described in sufficient detail to be understood and reproduced. The results are presented clearly and interpreted thoughtfully.
4.  **Sound and Well-Informed Methodology:** The design of the BSP parser is technically sound, intelligently combining established techniques from related but distinct parsing paradigms (discontinuous constituency, DAG dependencies) to meet the requirements of the UCCA formalism. The feature set is comprehensive and borrows effectively from prior work, while also introducing novel ideas like the `ratio` feature.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Novel Parser:** The primary weakness is that the proposed BSP parser is significantly outperformed on its main task (predicting primary edges) by the conversion-based baseline using the stack-LSTM dependency parser (59.9% F-score for BSPTree vs. 69.2% for the LSTM parser). While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on that subtask is very low (13.3% F-score). As presented, the results suggest that a simpler, indirect approach is currently more effective, which somewhat diminishes the practical impact of the more complex, direct model.
2.  **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper provides a strong quantitative evaluation but would benefit greatly from a qualitative error analysis. What are the most common types of errors made by the best-performing models? Are there specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., coordination, complex argument structures, specific edge labels) that pose the biggest challenges? An analysis of the errors made in remote edge prediction would be particularly valuable for understanding why performance is so low and how it might be improved.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Remote Edge Prediction:** The ability to predict remote edges is the key advantage of the BSP parser over the conversion-based methods. However, this aspect is analyzed in relatively little depth. The 13.3% F-score is difficult to interpret without context. It is unclear what kinds of multi-parent phenomena the model successfully captures and where it fails. A more detailed breakdown or discussion of these results would strengthen the paper's claims about the utility of the direct approach.
4.  **Potential Simplism in Back-Conversion:** The dependency-to-constituency back-conversion procedure (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics to re-introduce non-terminal nodes, notably a `Label(u)` function that defaults to the "Center" label. While the conversion upper bound is high, this heuristic approach could still be a source of error, potentially masking the true performance of the dependency parsers. A brief discussion of the potential impact of this heuristic or alternatives would be welcome.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given that the stack-LSTM dependency parser baseline is over 9% F-score better on primary edges, what is the strongest argument for pursuing the direct BSP model at this stage? While it is the only model that handles remote edges, are there specific structural configurations (beyond the presence of remote edges) that you believe a direct model is fundamentally better equipped to handle, which may not be fully captured by the current evaluation?
2.  Could you provide more insight into the very low performance on remote edge prediction (13.3% F-score)? For example, are errors concentrated on specific edge labels or structural patterns (e.g., participants shared across distant clauses)?
3.  Regarding the dependency back-conversion, the `Label(u)` function for re-introducing non-terminal nodes seems quite simple. Have you investigated how sensitive the final dependency-based parsing scores are to this heuristic? For instance, what is the impact if a different default label is chosen?
