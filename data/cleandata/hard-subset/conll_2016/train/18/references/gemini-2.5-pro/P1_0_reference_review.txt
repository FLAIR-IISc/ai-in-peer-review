### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces the task of broad-coverage semantic parsing for representations that support three key structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. Using the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a case study, the authors are the first to propose and evaluate parsing methods for such structures. They pursue two complementary strategies. First, they establish strong baselines by converting the UCCA structures into more restrictive formalisms (constituency and dependency trees) and applying state-of-the-art parsers. Second, they develop a novel, direct transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), which integrates mechanisms from discontinuous constituency and dependency DAG parsing to handle all three properties natively. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a conversion-based approach using a stack-LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest F-score (69.2%) on the primary tree structure, but cannot predict the re-entrant (remote) edges that create the DAG. The direct BSP model performs less well on the primary structure (59.0% F-score) but is the only model presented that can recover remote edges, achieving a 13.3% F-score on them.

### Main Contributions
1.  **Introduction of the UCCA Parsing Task and Baselines:** The paper's foremost contribution is defining and tackling the novel task of parsing UCCA, a grounded semantic formalism with a challenging combination of structural properties. By developing conversion methods and applying strong existing parsers, the authors provide the first-ever parsing results for this representation and establish a comprehensive set of baselines that will be crucial for future work in this area.
2.  **A Novel, Integrated Parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures:** The design and implementation of the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) is a significant contribution. It successfully integrates and extends existing transition-based techniques for discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing into a single, unified system capable of directly producing these complex graph structures. This provides a solid foundation for future direct parsing approaches.
3.  **A Thorough Empirical Evaluation and Analysis:** The paper presents a well-designed empirical study, including in-domain and out-of-domain experiments that demonstrate the robustness of the methods. The comparison of the direct parser against strong conversion-based baselines, including a state-of-the-art stack-LSTM model, provides a clear picture of the current trade-offs. The feature ablation study further adds value by giving insights into the model's components.

### Strengths
1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Problem:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem. As the field moves towards richer, more expressive semantic representations, developing parsers that can handle properties like re-entrancy, phrasal nodes, and discontinuities in a grounded fashion is a critical research direction. This work is a pioneering effort on this front.
2.  **Systematic and Methodologically Sound Approach:** The authors tackle the problem in a very systematic way. The dual approach of conversion-based baselines and a direct parser is excellent. The conversion baselines are not strawmen; they leverage state-of-the-art technology and provide a realistic assessment of what can be achieved by simplifying the problem. This clear-eyed comparison provides important context for the performance of the novel BSP parser and justifies the need for direct modeling.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The conversion algorithms and the transition system for BSP are described with sufficient clarity. The authors' commitment to releasing their code and converters is commendable and will be a great service to the community, lowering the barrier to entry for other researchers to work on this important problem.
4.  **Thorough and Insightful Experiments:** The experimental setup is strong. The use of a stack-LSTM parser as a baseline is a key strength, as it represents the state of the art in transition-based parsing and sets a high bar. The inclusion of out-of-domain evaluation demonstrates the practical applicability of the parser. Furthermore, the upper-bound analysis for the conversion methods clearly quantifies the information loss inherent in those approaches, reinforcing the motivation for a direct parser.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Performance Gap of the Direct Parser:** The primary weakness is that the novel BSP parser is significantly outperformed on primary edges by the conversion-based LSTM dependency parser (59.0% vs. 69.2% F-score). While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on them is very low (13.3% F-score), with recall being particularly poor (11.8%). This makes it hard to argue for the superiority of the direct approach in its current form. A simpler pipeline model (e.g., predict the tree with the LSTM parser, then add remote edges with a second-stage classifier) might prove more effective in practice.
2.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty in the Parser Design:** While the integration is novel and well-executed, the core components of the BSP transition system are largely adapted from previous work on discontinuous parsing (e.g., SWAP from Maier, 2015) and dependency DAG parsing (e.g., delaying REDUCE from Sagae and Tsujii, 2008). The main additions—the `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE` transitions and the UCCA-specific features—are relatively straightforward extensions. The contribution lies more in the successful engineering and application to a new, complex task rather than in a fundamental advance in parsing algorithms.
3.  **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper presents a strong quantitative analysis but would benefit greatly from a qualitative error analysis. What kinds of structures or phenomena does BSP struggle with most? For example, are errors concentrated on coordination, long-distance dependencies, or specific semantic roles? Why is the performance on remote edges so low? An analysis of common error patterns would provide much deeper insight into the remaining challenges of UCCA parsing and offer more specific directions for future work beyond the general suggestion of incorporating LSTMs.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The performance of the conversion-based LSTM parser on primary edges is impressive. Have you considered a pipeline model where you first predict the primary tree structure with this strong parser, and then use a separate model to predict remote edges on this fixed tree structure? This could potentially combine the strengths of both approaches.
2.  The recall on remote edges is very low (11.8% in-domain). Could you provide some insight into why these edges are so difficult for the parser to identify? Is this primarily a data sparsity issue, or are there specific linguistic characteristics of remote dependencies (e.g., distance, syntactic configuration) that make them hard to capture with the current feature set?
3.  The `ratio` feature is an interesting contribution. The ablation study shows it gives a significant boost to recall at the cost of precision. Could you elaborate on the intuition behind this feature and why it has this particular effect? Does it primarily act as a regularizer to encourage the creation of an appropriate number of non-terminal nodes?
