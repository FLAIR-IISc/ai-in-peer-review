### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of parsing into Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of graph-based representations that feature multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. Using the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete formalism, the authors present two parsing strategies. The first is a conversion-based approach, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted into constituency or dependency trees to be parsed by established parsers. The second is a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed to directly generate BSS graphs. Experiments are conducted on the English UCCA corpus.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical benchmark for the task of parsing the UCCA representation. The work provides a valuable service by establishing strong baselines using existing, well-understood parsing technologies.
2.  A clear demonstration that a state-of-the-art, established method (a stack-LSTM dependency parser) adapted via a simple conversion pipeline provides the strongest performance on this new task.
3.  The introduction of a direct, transition-based parser (BSP) for BSS, though its empirical results are not competitive with the best-performing baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong and Informative Baselines:** The authors have done an excellent job of grounding their work by first exploring conversion-based approaches. This is a methodologically sound way to assess the difficulty of the task and leverage powerful, existing parsers. The inclusion of multiple dependency parsers, particularly the high-performing stack-LSTM model, provides a very strong point of comparison and contextualizes the performance of the authors' proposed system.
2.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges inherent in BSS parsing, systematically outlining the three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) that distinguish it from more standard parsing tasks. This framing effectively motivates the need for new or adapted parsing technology.
3.  **Sound Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is conducted rigorously, with both in-domain and out-of-domain test sets, which demonstrates the robustness of the parsing approaches. The calculation of upper bounds for the conversion methods is particularly helpful, as it quantifies the information lost in the conversion process and helps interpret the final parser performance.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The Proposed Method (BSP) is Substantially Outperformed:** The most significant weakness is that the paper's primary technical novelty, the BSP parser, fails to achieve competitive performance. The stack-LSTM dependency parser, a baseline using established technology, achieves a primary edge F-score of 69.2%. In contrast, the authors' direct parser achieves only 59.0% (or 59.9% for the tree-only variant). This is a substantial performance gap of nearly 10 F-score points. The results strongly suggest that the more promising research direction is to improve the conversion pipeline or adapt existing tree parsers, rather than develop the proposed bespoke transition system. The paper's claim that BSP could be improved by incorporating LSTMs is speculative and does not mitigate the weakness of the currently presented model.
2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty in BSP:** The BSP parser is presented as a novel system, but it appears to be a fairly straightforward combination of existing techniques from the transition-based parsing literature. The use of NODE transitions for creating non-terminals, SWAP transitions for handling discontinuities, and separate transitions for primary vs. remote edges to build DAGs all draw heavily on prior work in constituency, discontinuous, and DAG parsing. The feature set is likewise adapted from previous systems. While the application to UCCA is new, the work overstates its methodological contribution.
3.  **Unconvincing Performance on Remote Edges:** The main justification for the additional complexity of the BSP parser is its ability to predict remote edges, which are discarded by the conversion-based approaches. However, the reported F-score for these edges is extremely low (13.3%). This level of performance is difficult to interpret as a success. It is not clear from the paper whether this is meaningfully better than a simple heuristic baseline (e.g., a rule-based post-processing step that adds common remote edges). Without stronger results, the primary motivation for the direct parsing approach is undermined.
4.  **Lack of In-depth Analysis:** The paper presents results but offers little insight into *why* the proposed BSP parser performs so much worse than the LSTM-based baseline. An error analysis comparing the outputs of the two systems would be critical for understanding the deficiencies of the new transition system. Is the greedy search the main culprit? Does the feature model fail to capture important dependencies? Without such analysis, the paper's conclusion is simply that a new, custom-built system is inferior to an existing one, which is not a particularly insightful outcome.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given that the established stack-LSTM parser with a simple conversion pipeline is the best-performing system by a wide margin, what is the core argument for pursuing the direct BSP approach? Is there a qualitative advantage of the BSP outputs (e.g., better structural coherence) that is not captured by the evaluation metric?
2.  The 13.3% F-score on remote edges is very low. Could you provide some analysis of these errors? What are the most common error types? Furthermore, have you considered a simple post-processing baseline that attempts to add remote edges to the output of the much stronger LSTM parser? It would be important to demonstrate that BSP's performance, while low, is superior to such a heuristic.
