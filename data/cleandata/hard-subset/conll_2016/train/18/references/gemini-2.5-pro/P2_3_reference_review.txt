### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of semantic representations that are grounded in the text and support three key properties: multiple parents (forming a DAG), non-terminal nodes (for phrasal units), and discontinuous constituents. The authors focus on the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of BSS. The paper presents a two-pronged approach: first, it establishes strong baselines by converting UCCA structures into more restrictive formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees) and applying state-of-the-art parsers. Second, it proposes a novel transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), specifically designed to handle all three properties of BSS directly.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The first parser for the UCCA formalism:** The paper introduces BSP, the first-ever parsing system capable of directly generating UCCA structures, or any BSS combining the three specified properties. This is a significant contribution as it opens up a promising semantic representation to downstream NLP applications.
2.  **A comprehensive benchmarking of UCCA parsing:** By developing conversion methods to dependency and constituency formats, the authors are ableto apply existing high-performance parsers to the task. This provides a set of strong baselines and an upper-bound analysis, which effectively situates the difficulty of the task and the performance of their proposed model.
3.  **A novel integration of transition-based parsing techniques:** The BSP parser cleverly synthesizes techniques from different subfields of transition-based parsing—including transitions for constituency parsing (NODE), discontinuous parsing (SWAP), and DAG dependency parsing (REMOTE-EDGE)—into a single, unified system for this new, complex task.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles a well-motivated and novel problem:** The paper clearly articulates the need for semantic representations that can handle multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities. It correctly identifies UCCA as a formalism that meets these criteria and notes the absence of any existing parser for it. Addressing this gap is a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing.
2.  **Thorough and insightful experimental design:** The decision to pursue two parallel strategies—conversion-based baselines and a direct parser—is a major strength. It provides a much richer picture than simply presenting the novel parser in isolation. This allows for a clear-eyed assessment of where existing technology succeeds and fails. The inclusion of an LSTM-based dependency parser as a baseline is particularly important, as it represents the state-of-the-art in transition-based methods and reveals crucial insights about the problem.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivations are clear, the UCCA formalism is explained sufficiently, the conversion algorithms are explicitly provided (Algs 1 & 2), and the transition system for BSP is clearly detailed (Fig 2). The authors’ commitment to making their code and converters publicly available is commendable and enhances the paper’s potential impact.
4.  **Honest and Transparent Reporting of Results:** The authors do not shy away from the fact that their novel BSP parser is outperformed on primary edges by the conversion-based LSTM baseline. They report this result clearly and use it to draw the sound conclusion that incorporating more powerful classifiers (like LSTMs) into their direct parsing framework is a promising direction for future work. This transparency adds significant credibility to the research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming performance of the main contribution (BSP):** The central novel component of this work, the BSP parser, performs significantly worse on the primary task of identifying primary edges than a baseline that uses a lossy conversion to dependencies (59.9% vs. 69.2% F-score). While BSP is the only system that attempts to parse remote edges, its performance on them is very low (13.3% F-score), limiting its practical utility for recovering full DAGs. This performance gap makes the direct parsing approach seem less compelling than the simpler, higher-performing conversion-based method, at least for the tree-backbone of the structures.
2.  **Limited Technical Novelty in Parser Design:** While the integration is novel for the UCCA task, the individual components of the BSP parser are largely adopted from previous work. The transition set is a combination of standard transitions from constituency (NODE), discontinuous constituency (SWAP), and DAG dependency parsing (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE). The feature set is also explicitly adapted from several prior systems. The primary novelty is in the application and synthesis, rather than in the development of fundamentally new parsing techniques or model architectures.
3.  **Lack of Error Analysis for Remote Edges:** The paper establishes that predicting remote edges is very difficult, but it does not offer much insight as to why. An error analysis would have been highly beneficial. For instance, are the errors concentrated on particular edge labels or specific syntactic configurations? Is the problem primarily one of recall (failing to propose remote edges) or precision (proposing incorrect ones)? Such an analysis would provide a clearer path forward for improving this aspect of the parser.
4.  **Potential Simplification in Baselines:** The conversion from dependencies back to constituency structures (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics, particularly for creating non-terminal nodes and assigning their labels. It's possible that a more sophisticated reconstruction method could further improve the strong dependency-based baseline, potentially widening the gap with BSP. While the current approach is reasonable, its heuristic nature is a potential source of unanalyzed error in the baseline pipeline.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between the LSTM-based dependency parser and your feature-based BSP is striking. You suggest that incorporating LSTMs into BSP is the logical next step. Beyond the general representational power of LSTMs, do you have a more specific hypothesis for why the linear model struggles? For example, is it failing to capture long-distance dependencies that are crucial for UCCA's non-local relations, even for primary edges?
2.  Given the very low performance on remote edges (13.3 F-score), could you elaborate on the main challenges? Is it primarily a data sparsity issue (remote edges are rare), a feature engineering problem (the features don't capture the necessary information), or a search problem (the greedy parser makes early mistakes from which it cannot recover to form a valid remote attachment)?
3.  You introduce a novel real-valued `ratio` feature, which you suggest is a useful regularizer. However, its impact is not isolated in the feature ablation study (Table 3). Could you provide some detail on its standalone contribution to the model's performance?
