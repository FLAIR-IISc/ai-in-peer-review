**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of broad-coverage semantic parsing for formalisms that support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents, which the authors term Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS). The work focuses on the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors propose two approaches: (1) a conversion-based method that transforms UCCA structures into dependency or constituency trees, allowing the use of existing parsers, and (2) a novel, direct transition-based parser, BSP, designed to handle all three properties of BSS.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  The first empirical study of parsing the UCCA semantic representation, establishing benchmarks on the available English corpora.
2.  The development of BSP, a transition-based parser that synthesizes techniques for discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing to directly produce BSS graphs.
3.  A comparative analysis showing that a conversion-based approach using a state-of-the-art LSTM dependency parser currently yields the best performance on the primary tree-like structure of UCCA graphs.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a valuable and challenging parsing problem. Moving beyond tree-structured representations is crucial for capturing a wider range of semantic phenomena, and UCCA is a promising formalism in this space. This work is a timely and necessary first step towards developing parsers for such representations.
2.  **Principled Direct Model:** The proposed BSP parser is a sound integration of existing, powerful transition-based techniques. It logically combines mechanisms for handling non-terminals (NODE), discontinuities (SWAP), and multiple parents (separating EDGE and REDUCE transitions, adding REMOTE_EDGE) into a unified system.
3.  **Thorough Baseline Comparison:** The inclusion of conversion-based approaches provides strong and realistic baselines. Comparing BSP against parsers like UPARSE and the high-performing LSTM dependency parser of Dyer et al. (2015) provides crucial context for the results and transparently shows the performance gap that future direct models must close. The robustness of the parsers, demonstrated in the out-of-domain experiment, is also a positive finding.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Clarity:** The paper suffers from several omissions that hinder reproducibility and understanding. For instance, Algorithm 1 for constituency-to-dependency conversion relies on a `Priority` function over edge labels, but this function is never defined. The inverse conversion in Algorithm 2 uses a `Label(u)` function which is relegated to supplementary material, despite being a key heuristic component. The novel real-valued `ratio` feature is mentioned, but its precise calculation and application (e.g., binning) are not described. Furthermore, the `NODEX` transition's behavior of placing a new node on the buffer rather than the stack is unconventional for a constituency-style parser and is presented without justification.
2.  **Inaccurate Claims and Statements:** The paper contains some misleading or incorrect statements. The conclusion prominently highlights the 69.2% F-score, but this result was achieved by a baseline conversion method using an external parser, not the authors' primary contribution, BSP, which scored nearly 10 F-points lower on the same task. This framing is misleading. Additionally, the evaluation section claims the proposed metric "collapses to the standard PARSEVAL constituency evaluation measure" for trees. This is incorrect; PARSEVAL is based on matching labeled constituent spans (i.e., (label, start_index, end_index) tuples), whereas the authors' metric matches labeled edges based on the set of terminals they yield, which is a different evaluation paradigm.
3.  **Subpar Performance of the Primary Contribution:** The main technical novelty, the BSP parser, does not perform competitively. It is significantly outperformed on its primary task (parsing primary edges) by the LSTM-based dependency parsing baseline (59.9% vs. 69.2% F-score). While BSP is the only model presented that can predict remote edges, its performance is extremely low (13.3% F-score), making its practical utility for capturing re-entrancy questionable at this stage.
4.  **Insufficient Attention to Detail and Formatting:** The manuscript is marred by a substantial number of formatting errors, particularly in the bibliography. Multiple references have question marks in place of publication years (e.g., "Abend and Rappoport.,? 2013"), strange backslashes and punctuation (e.g., "Artzi et al\.,?"), and at least one entry appears completely corrupted (the Goldberg and Elhadad, 2011 citation in the text and its malformed entry in the reference list). This sloppiness detracts significantly from the paper's professionalism and raises concerns about the overall care taken in the research.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the definition of the label `Priority` used in Algorithm 1, as it is essential for reproducing the dependency conversion?
2.  The citation for Goldberg and Elhadad (2011) in Section 4 appears corrupted, referencing both their 2011 paper and the Goldberg and Nivre (2012) paper in the same sentence. Can you please correct this and ensure all other citations and references are properly formatted?
3.  The performance of your novel BSP parser is substantially below that of the conversion-based LSTM baseline. While you suggest LSTMs could be integrated into BSP, could you elaborate on why the current BSP model struggles so much in comparison, even on the primary tree structure? Is it the feature set, the greedy search, or a fundamental limitation of the transition system?
