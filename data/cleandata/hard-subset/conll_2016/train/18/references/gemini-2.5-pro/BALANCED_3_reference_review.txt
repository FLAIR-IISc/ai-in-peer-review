### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), which aims to produce semantic graphs that simultaneously support multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents. The paper frames this task around the UCCA annotation scheme and presents the first parsing results for this representation. The authors propose two complementary approaches: (1) a conversion-based method that transforms UCCA graphs into more standard formalisms (dependency or discontinuous constituency trees) to leverage existing state-of-the-art parsers, and (2) a novel, direct transition-based parser, BSP, designed specifically to produce BSS structures. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a conversion-based approach using an LSTM dependency parser achieves the best results on the primary tree structure (69.2 F1), while the proposed BSP parser is the only method capable of predicting remote edges, albeit with low performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Introducing and benchmarking the UCCA parsing task:** This is the first work to formalize and provide parsing results for the UCCA semantic representation. By establishing strong baselines using conversion methods and proposing a novel direct method, the paper lays a solid foundation for future research in this important and challenging area of semantic parsing.
2.  **A novel transition system for complex semantic graphs (BSP):** The paper presents a new transition-based parser that integrates mechanisms for handling non-terminal nodes, discontinuities, and multiple parents within a single, unified framework. This is a non-trivial methodological contribution that extends the capabilities of transition-based parsing.
3.  **A thorough empirical evaluation and analysis:** The paper conducts a well-designed set of experiments, including in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation, an analysis of conversion upper-bounds, and a feature ablation study. This rigorous evaluation provides valuable insights into the challenges of the task and the relative merits of different approaches.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a novel and ambitious task. While various semantic representations exist, few parsers attempt to handle the combined challenges of DAG structures, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. By tackling UCCA parsing, this work pushes the boundaries of semantic parsing towards more expressive and cross-lingually applicable representations. This is a significant and timely contribution.
2.  **Sound and Comprehensive Methodology:** The dual-approach strategy is very effective. Using conversion-based methods provides strong, realistic baselines and contextualizes the performance of the novel parser. The design of the direct parser, BSP, is well-motivated, thoughtfully extending established principles from discontinuous constituency and DAG dependency parsing. The description of the transition system, features, and conversion algorithms is clear and detailed.
3.  **Rigorous and Insightful Experiments:** The evaluation is thorough and provides a clear picture of the current state of UCCA parsing. Comparing against multiple modern parsers, including the very strong LSTM-based dependency parser, makes the results credible. The inclusion of upper-bound calculations for the conversion methods is particularly insightful, as it quantifies the information loss inherent in those approaches and motivates the need for direct parsing. The out-of-domain evaluation further demonstrates the robustness of the methods.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem is clearly motivated with helpful examples, and the proposed methods are explained with sufficient detail to be understood and likely reproduced. The authors' commitment to releasing their code and converters is commendable and will be a great asset to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Performance of the Proposed Method:** The primary weakness is that the novel BSP parser is significantly outperformed on its core task—predicting primary edges—by a baseline that uses existing technology (59.0% F1 for BSP vs. 69.2% F1 for the LSTM dependency parser). While BSP's unique ability to predict remote edges is a key selling point, its performance on this front is very low (13.3% F1). This performance gap undermines the practical advantage of the proposed direct approach over a simpler, stronger conversion-based pipeline, at least for the primary tree structure.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Remote Edge Prediction:** The ability to predict remote edges is the main justification for BSP over the tree-based conversion methods. However, given the very low F-score, the paper would be much stronger with a detailed error analysis for these predictions. It is unclear what kinds of structures or relations are being correctly or incorrectly predicted. This analysis is crucial for understanding the current limitations of the model and for guiding future work on this particularly challenging aspect of BSS parsing.
3.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison due to Greedy Search:** The proposed BSP parser uses a greedy inference algorithm. In contrast, at least one of the baselines (UPARSE) uses beam search, and it is a standard technique for improving transition-based parsers. Given the increased search space and complexity of parsing BSS structures, it is highly likely that incorporating beam search would improve BSP's performance. Without this, the comparison to other parsers may not be entirely fair, and the reported performance of BSP might be an underestimate of its potential.
4.  **Simplicity of the Inverse Dependency Conversion:** The procedure for converting dependency trees back to UCCA-style structures (Algorithm 2) is heuristic and relatively simple. While this highlights the difficulty of the task, it may also unfairly handicap the dependency-based approach. It is possible that a more sophisticated, perhaps machine-learned, reconstruction model could improve the dependency parser's upper bound and final performance, potentially widening the performance gap with BSP even further. A brief discussion of this possibility would have been welcome.

### Questions to Authors

1.  You note that the LSTM-based dependency parser baseline is very strong and suggest that incorporating a similar classifier into BSP would likely improve its performance. Could you comment on why this was not explored in the current work? Understanding the potential of a stronger BSP model is key to assessing whether the direct approach is ultimately more promising than conversion-based ones.
2.  The F-score for remote edges is very low at 13.3%. Could you provide any qualitative insights from an error analysis? For instance, are the errors primarily related to attachment (choosing the wrong second parent) or labeling? Are there particular linguistic constructions (e.g., control verbs, relative clauses) that are proving especially difficult?
3.  Did you experiment with or consider using beam search for the BSP parser? Given the complexity of the output structures and the significant improvements it often brings to transition-based systems, it seems like a natural extension that could substantially boost performance on both primary and remote edges.
