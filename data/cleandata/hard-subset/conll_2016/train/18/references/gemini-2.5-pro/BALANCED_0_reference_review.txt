### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces and tackles the task of Broad-coverage Semantic Parsing (BSS), aiming to produce grounded semantic representations that are directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) rather than trees. This allows them to naturally support three key linguistic phenomena: shared arguments (multiple parents), multi-word units without a clear head (non-terminal nodes), and discontinuous constituents. Using the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of BSS, the authors propose two main approaches. The first is a conversion-based method that transforms UCCA graphs into constituency or dependency trees, allowing off-the-shelf parsers to be used as strong baselines. The second is a novel, direct transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), specifically designed with a new transition set and features to produce BSS graphs. Experiments on the UCCA corpus show that a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser, used in the conversion approach, achieves the highest score (69.2% F1) on the primary, tree-like structure of the graphs. The proposed BSP, while achieving a lower score on the primary structure, is the first parser capable of directly predicting the non-tree "remote" edges that form the full DAG.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **The formulation of a new, challenging parsing task and the development of the first direct parser for it.** The paper clearly defines the properties of Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) and makes a compelling case for their necessity. The proposed BSP parser is a novel and principled extension of transition-based methods to directly address this new task, representing a significant first step in this research direction.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation establishing a new benchmark for UCCA parsing.** The authors provide a strong and well-designed experimental setup. By creating strong baselines via conversion to other formalisms, they ground their results against state-of-the-art technology. This methodology, along with the proposed evaluation metrics and public release of code, provides a solid foundation and a clear benchmark for future work on parsing UCCA and other similar semantic schemes.
3.  **An insightful analysis that effectively decomposes the problem.** By evaluating primary (tree) and remote (DAG) edges separately, the paper highlights that existing powerful tree-parsing technology excels at the core predicate-argument structure, while the more complex cross-cutting dependencies remain a distinct and unsolved challenge. This finding clearly frames the problem for future research, suggesting that hybrid or multi-stage approaches may be fruitful.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear motivation and problem definition.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for semantic representations that go beyond simple trees. The introduction clearly explains the limitations of existing formalisms and why the three properties of BSS are crucial for broad-coverage semantic analysis. This clarity makes the paper accessible and its contributions easy to appreciate.
2.  **Sound and thorough experimental design.** The decision to include conversion-based baselines is a major strength. It allows for a direct comparison with powerful, established parsers (like the LSTM dependency parser) and provides a realistic measure of what can be achieved with current technology. The calculation of upper bounds for these conversion methods is particularly valuable, as it quantifies the inherent information loss and properly contextualizes the performance of the direct approach. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments further demonstrates the robustness of the proposed methods.
3.  **Principled design of the direct parser (BSP).** The BSP's transition system is a thoughtful combination and extension of ideas from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing. The explicit distinction between primary edge transitions (`LEFT-EDGE`) and remote edge transitions (`LEFT-REMOTE`) is a simple but elegant mechanism for learning to produce DAGs. The introduction of the novel `ratio` feature is also a clever, generalizable idea for regularizing the creation of non-terminal nodes in transition-based constituency parsers.
4.  **Constructive analysis and clear future directions.** The authors provide a sober assessment of their results. They correctly identify that the success of the LSTM-based baseline on primary edges strongly suggests that replacing BSP's linear classifier with a more powerful neural model is the most promising path for improvement. This self-awareness and clear articulation of next steps makes the paper's contribution feel more solid and forward-looking.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Low performance on the core challenge of remote edges.** While BSP is pioneering in its ability to predict remote edges, the F-score of 13.3% is very low. This significantly limits the practical utility of the parser for capturing the full DAG structure that motivates the work. The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth error analysis of these predictions. For example, are the errors concentrated on specific semantic relations, long-distance dependencies, or other structural configurations? Without this analysis, it is difficult to know whether the limitation lies with the greedy search, the feature set, or the fundamental transition system design.
2.  **Indirect comparison of parsing models.** The paper's strongest result comes from an LSTM-based parser, while its novel contribution, BSP, uses a linear perceptron. This setup compares a more powerful classification model (LSTM) against a less powerful one (perceptron). This makes it difficult to fully disentangle the contributions of the transition system from the power of the underlying classifier. The conclusion that LSTMs would improve BSP is well-founded, but the current results do not allow for a direct comparison of the *parsing paradigms* (conversion-based vs. direct) on a level playing field.
3.  **The dependency conversion seems lossy and heuristic.** The back-conversion from dependency trees to UCCA structures (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics like "assume that attachments are always high-attaching" and a rule-based function for labeling new non-terminals which "mostly selects the UCCA label Center". This raises concerns that the high performance of the LSTM parser is achieved despite working with a significantly impoverished representation, and that the upper bound of 88.4% may optimistically understate the information lost in the conversion. More detail on the impact of these heuristics would be helpful.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the low performance on remote edges, could you provide a brief error analysis? Are there common patterns in the remote edges that BSP fails to predict (e.g., related to sentence length, syntactic structure, or specific UCCA labels)? Do you believe the primary bottleneck is the greedy search decoding, or is the feature representation insufficient for capturing the necessary non-local information?
2.  The LSTM dependency parser is very strong on the primary tree structure. Have you considered a pipeline or two-stage model as an alternative to your integrated BSP parser? For instance, one could first use the best-performing tree parser to establish a primary structure, and then run a second model dedicated solely to identifying and labeling remote edges over this fixed scaffold. This might allow you to combine the strengths of both approaches.
3.  Could you elaborate on the heuristics in the dependency-to-constituency back-conversion (Algorithm 2)? Specifically, how much structural information is lost due to the "always high-attaching" assumption, and how does this affect the upper bound calculation? A more detailed characterization of the information lost during the dependency conversion would help clarify how much of the performance gap between the LSTM parser and the upper bound is attributable to parsing errors versus conversion artifacts.
