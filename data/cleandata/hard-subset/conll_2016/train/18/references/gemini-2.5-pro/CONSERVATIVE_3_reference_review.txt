### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces the task of broad-coverage semantic parsing (BSS), specifically targeting the UCCA annotation scheme, which produces DAG structures that can include multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents. The authors propose two main approaches. First, a conversion-based method that transforms UCCA graphs into more standard tree formats (constituency and dependency) to leverage existing, high-performance parsers. Second, they develop a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed to directly produce the full BSS graphs by extending existing transition systems with new actions for remote edges and UCCA-specific features. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser, adapted via the conversion method, achieves the highest performance on the primary tree structure (69.2% F-score). The novel BSP parser performs less well on this core structure (~60% F-score) but is uniquely capable of predicting remote edges, albeit with low performance (~13% F-score).

### Contributions
1.  **Establishment of Baselines for UCCA Parsing:** The most significant contribution is the systematic evaluation of established parsing technologies on the new task of UCCA parsing. By developing conversion protocols to dependency and constituency trees, the authors provide a methodologically sound set of baselines, demonstrating that existing state-of-the-art parsers (particularly the LSTM dependency parser) can effectively capture a large portion of the semantic structure. This is a valuable contribution that grounds a new parsing problem in the context of existing, well-understood methods.
2.  **A Direct Transition-Based Parser for DAGs with Discontinuities:** The paper presents BSP, a novel integrated parser for the complex BSS formalism. While its performance is not state-of-the-art, it serves as an important proof of concept for a direct parsing approach and is the first system, to my knowledge, that combines transitions for discontinuous constituency parsing with those for dependency DAG creation in a single model.

### Strengths
1.  **Methodological Rigor in Establishing Baselines:** The decision to first explore conversion-based approaches is a major strength. Rather than simply presenting a new model in isolation, the authors provide a clear and compelling comparison against strong, established systems. This approach not only contextualizes the difficulty of the task but also reveals that a significant portion of the parsing problem can be solved by adapting existing, powerful machinery.
2.  **Significant Finding Regarding Existing Technology:** The high performance of the LSTM-based dependency parser (Dyer et al., 2015) after conversion is a key result. Achieving nearly 70% F-score demonstrates the robustness and adaptability of modern dependency parsing techniques. This finding is valuable for the community, as it suggests a very strong and readily available baseline for any future work on UCCA parsing.
3.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of defining the challenges of BSS parsing, motivating the need for representations that handle multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities. By formalizing this as a parsing task and providing the first set of benchmark results, the authors have laid important groundwork for future research.

### Weaknesses
1.  **The Proposed Novel Parser is Substantially Outperformed:** The primary weakness of this work is that the central technical contribution, the BSP parser, is substantially outperformed by one of the baselines on the core task of predicting the primary tree structure. The LSTM conversion-based approach achieves a 69.2% F-score on primary edges, while the best-performing BSP variant (BSPTree) reaches only 59.9%. A performance gap of nearly 10 F-score points undermines the argument for the new parser's effectiveness.
2.  **Incremental Novelty of the BSP Parser:** The BSP model is presented as novel, but it is largely a careful combination of existing techniques. The transition system integrates ideas from discontinuous constituency parsing (Maier, 2015) and dependency DAG parsing (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008). The primary additions are the `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX` transitions and a few UCCA-specific features. While this integration is non-trivial, the conceptual novelty is limited, which makes the subpar performance more problematic.
3.  **Very Low Performance on the Novel Aspect of the Task:** The main advantage of BSP over the conversion-based methods is its ability to predict remote edges. However, with an F-score of only 13.3% (in-domain), its performance on this front is not compelling. This suggests that the model struggles significantly with the very phenomena it was specifically designed to handle. While this is acknowledged as a difficult problem, the results are too low to claim that the proposed approach is effective.
4.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The paper presents results but lacks a detailed error analysis that could justify the direct parsing approach despite its lower scores. Why does BSP lag so far behind the LSTM parser on primary edges? What specific structures does it fail on? Is the bottleneck the transition system, the feature set, or the linear classifier? The conclusion that an LSTM-based classifier would improve BSP seems plausible, but without analysis, it remains speculative. A more thorough analysis comparing the output of BSP and the LSTM baseline would have strengthened the paper considerably.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis comparing the output of the LSTM-based conversion parser and your BSP parser on primary edges? What are the most common error types for BSP, and on which constructions does the stronger LSTM-based model succeed where BSP fails? This would help clarify whether the performance gap is due to the classifier or more fundamental limitations of the transition system.
2.  The upper bound for the dependency conversion is 88.4%, indicating a significant loss of information. Can you quantify how much of this loss is attributable to the removal of non-terminal nodes versus the heuristic nature of the back-conversion algorithm (Algorithm 2)?
3.  Given the very low performance on remote edges, have you analyzed whether the parser learns any meaningful patterns for their prediction? For instance, does it successfully identify common re-entrancy phenomena like argument sharing, or are the few correct predictions scattered randomly?
