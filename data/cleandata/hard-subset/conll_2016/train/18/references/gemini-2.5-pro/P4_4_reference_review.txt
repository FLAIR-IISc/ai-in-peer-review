### Summary of the paper

This paper introduces the task of parsing for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), defined as grounded, directed acyclic graphs that support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. Using the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instantiation of BSS, the authors present the first parsing approaches for this formalism. The work follows two main strategies. First, a conversion-based approach is used to establish strong baselines by converting UCCA structures to and from more standard formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees), allowing the use of existing state-of-the-art parsers. Second, the authors propose a novel, direct parser called the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), which uses a transition-based system designed specifically to produce all three properties of BSS. The methods are evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, with the conversion-based approach using a stack-LSTM dependency parser achieving the highest F-score (69.2%) on primary (tree) edges, while the direct BSP parser is the only method capable of predicting remote (non-tree) edges, albeit with a lower F-score.

### Main Contributions

1.  **The first parser for the UCCA semantic representation scheme.** The primary contribution is the introduction and implementation of the first parser for UCCA, a broad-coverage semantic formalism. The proposed direct parser, BSP, is the first system capable of generating the full range of UCCA structures, including multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical benchmark for UCCA parsing.** The paper establishes a solid baseline for the new task by adapting several state-of-the-art parsers through lossy conversion protocols. This thorough evaluation, including both dependency and constituency-based systems and an analysis of conversion upper bounds, provides a valuable point of reference and a clear measure of the task's difficulty for future research.
3.  **A novel transition system for direct BSS parsing.** The paper develops a new transition-based parser (BSP) that synthesizes and extends techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing. It introduces a specific set of transitions to handle primary vs. remote edges and incorporates a novel feature to regulate the creation of non-terminal nodes.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Task:** The paper addresses a novel and well-motivated problem: parsing into a semantic representation that handles a wider range of linguistic phenomena (coordination, multi-word expressions, shared arguments) than many standard formalisms. By developing the first parser for UCCA, the work opens up a promising research direction and provides a critical tool for leveraging this cross-lingually applicable semantic scheme in downstream applications.
2.  **Thorough and Sound Experimental Design:** The methodology is technically sound. The use of a conversion-based approach to create strong baselines is an excellent design choice. It effectively leverages existing high-performance parsers (like the stack-LSTM model) to contextualize the performance of the novel direct parser. The analysis is strengthened by the inclusion of upper-bound scores for the conversions, which transparently quantifies the information loss inherent in the indirect approach. The evaluation covers both in-domain and out-of-domain data, demonstrating the robustness of the proposed methods.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation and Analysis:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for BSS and UCCA is clearly articulated with illustrative examples. The conversion algorithms and the transition system for the BSP parser are described in sufficient detail. The feature ablation study provides useful insights into the contributions of different components of the BSP model. The results are presented clearly, and the discussion rightly points to promising avenues for future work, such as integrating more powerful classifiers into the direct parser.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Proposed Direct Parser:** The central novel technical contribution, the BSP parser, is significantly outperformed on its primary task by the conversion-based baseline. BSP achieves a labeled F-score of 59.0% on primary edges, whereas the baseline using an existing stack-LSTM dependency parser reaches 69.2%. While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on this subtask is very low (13.3% F-score). This performance gap raises questions about the practical utility of the proposed direct parser in its current form, as a simpler, indirect method proves more effective for the core structural prediction task.
2.  **Limited Technical Novelty in the Parser Design:** While the BSP parser is the first of its kind for UCCA, its underlying architecture is a straightforward combination of previously proposed techniques. The transition set and features are largely adapted from prior work on discontinuous constituency parsing (Maier, 2015) and dependency DAG parsing (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008; Tokgöz and Eryiğit, 2015). The principal novel components are the explicit `REMOTE-EDGE` transitions and a `ratio` feature. The work is thus more of a successful application and integration of existing methods to a new problem rather than a fundamental advance in parsing algorithms.
3.  **Evaluation Metric is Underexplained:** The paper defines a new evaluation metric based on matching edges that have identical yields and labels. While this seems plausible, the metric's properties are not analyzed. It is unclear how this metric compares to other potential evaluation schemes for labeled DAGs or how sensitive it is to minor boundary errors in the yields. The lack of this analysis makes it harder to fully interpret the reported scores. Additionally, reporting only labeled scores makes it difficult to disentangle attachment errors from labeling errors. An unlabeled score would have provided a more complete picture of parser performance.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between your direct BSP parser and the stack-LSTM conversion-based approach is over 10 F-score points on primary edges. You suggest this is primarily due to the classifier. Could you elaborate on why you expect the classifier to be the main source of this difference, as opposed to limitations in the transition system's expressiveness or error propagation in the greedy search procedure?
2.  The inverse conversion from dependencies back to UCCA (Algorithm 2) is heuristic and lossy, with an upper bound of 88.4 F1. Have you analyzed the primary error types this conversion introduces? Do you believe a more sophisticated, perhaps machine-learned, inverse conversion model could further improve the strong dependency-based baseline?
3.  The F-score for remote edges in the BSP parser is very low (13.3%). Could you provide a brief error analysis? Are the errors primarily due to a failure to propose any remote edge, incorrect attachment points, or incorrect edge labels? Does the model often confuse primary and remote edges for the same attachment?
