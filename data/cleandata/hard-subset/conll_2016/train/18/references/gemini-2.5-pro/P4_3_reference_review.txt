### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of grounded semantic representations that simultaneously support multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The UCCA scheme is presented as a concrete instance of BSS. The authors pursue two complementary strategies: first, a conversion-based approach that adapts existing constituency and dependency parsers to the task, providing strong baselines; and second, a novel, direct transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), designed specifically to handle the full range of BSS properties. Experiments are conducted on UCCA-annotated corpora, and a new evaluation metric based on edge yields is proposed. The results demonstrate that a conversion-based approach using an LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest accuracy on the primary tree structure, while the proposed BSP is the only parser capable of predicting the full DAG structure, albeit with modest performance on non-tree edges.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The formulation of the BSS parsing task and a novel transition-based parser (BSP) to address it.** The paper is the first to systematically tackle parsing for a grounded semantic representation that combines DAG structures, phrase structure, and discontinuities. The BSP parser is a technically sound contribution that integrates and extends existing transition-based techniques to handle this complex new task.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical benchmark for UCCA parsing.** The work establishes the first parsing results for the UCCA formalism. By evaluating both conversion-based and direct methods, including several state-of-the-art parsers, it provides a thorough analysis and a strong set of baselines that will be valuable for future research.
3.  **A principled evaluation metric for BSS.** The authors define a new labeled precision/recall metric based on matching the yields (the set of terminal descendants) of edges. This is a necessary and well-motivated contribution for evaluating parsers on this new type of graph-structured representation.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Task.** The paper addresses a new and challenging parsing problem. While prior work has focused on semantic dependency parsing (which lacks non-terminal nodes) or ungrounded representations like AMR, this work is the first to target a grounded representation with the combined properties of BSS. This broadens the scope of semantic parsing and has the potential to support applications that benefit from richer, grounded structural information. The choice of UCCA is well-justified by its typological grounding and demonstrated cross-linguistic applicability.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Design.** The evaluation is well-conceived and provides a multifaceted view of the problem. The decision to benchmark a direct approach against conversion-based methods is particularly effective. Using strong, state-of-the-art parsers (MaltParser, UPARSE, and especially the stack-LSTM parser) for the baselines sets a high standard and provides a clear context for the performance of the novel BSP parser. The inclusion of upper-bound calculations for the conversion methods transparently quantifies the information loss inherent in those pipelines.
3.  **Technical Soundness of the Proposed Parser.** The design of the BSP parser is technically solid, logically extending established transition-based parsing principles. The transition set is a well-motivated synthesis of operations from discontinuous constituency parsing (e.g., `SWAP`), dependency DAG parsing (e.g., `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE`, decoupling of edge creation and `REDUCE`), and standard constituency parsing (e.g., `NODE`). The feature set is comprehensive, and the addition of the novel `ratio` feature is an interesting idea for regularizing the creation of non-terminals.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation.** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation for the three properties of BSS is clearly articulated in the introduction with helpful diagrams. The conversion algorithms and the BSP transition system are described precisely and are easy to understand. The results are presented clearly and support the paper's main conclusions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Novel Direct Parser.** The paper's primary technical contribution, the BSP parser, is substantially outperformed on the core tree structure by a conversion-based baseline. The stack-LSTM dependency parser achieves a primary edge F-score of 69.2%, over 10 F-score points higher than BSP's 59.0%. While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on them is very low (13.3% F-score). The authors correctly suggest that integrating LSTMs into BSP would likely help, but as presented, the results do not make a strong case for the superiority of the direct approach over a simpler, higher-performing conversion-based pipeline for capturing the bulk of the semantic structure.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Remote Edge Prediction.** The prediction of remote edges (enabling multiple parents) is the key feature that distinguishes BSP from the baseline models. However, the analysis of this capability is superficial. The paper reports a single aggregate F-score for remote edges but provides no further breakdown or error analysis. It is unclear what types of remote edges are being captured, what the most common failure modes are, and why the performance is so low. A more in-depth analysis would be necessary to understand the current limitations and to chart a path for future improvements, which would strengthen the contribution of the direct parsing approach.
3.  **Potential Underestimation of Conversion-Based Approaches.** The inverse conversion from dependency trees back to UCCA (Algorithm 2) is heuristic-based and relatively simplistic. For example, it makes a fixed assumption about high attachment and uses a rule-based function to label newly created non-terminal edges. This simple reconstruction process may not reflect the full potential of a conversion-based pipeline. A more sophisticated, possibly machine-learned, reconstruction model might recover more of the lost information and further improve the already strong results of the dependency-based approach. The current design may therefore unfairly disadvantage the conversion-based methods in comparison to the direct approach.
