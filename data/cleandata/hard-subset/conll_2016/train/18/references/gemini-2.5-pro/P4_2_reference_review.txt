### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of grounded semantic representations that simultaneously support multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes for multi-word expressions, and discontinuous units. Using the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of BSS, the authors are the first to propose and evaluate parsing methods for this formalism.

The paper explores two complementary strategies. The first is a conversion-based approach, which assesses the ability of existing parsers to handle the task by converting UCCA structures into more restrictive formalisms like discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees. State-of-the-art parsers are trained on these converted structures, and their outputs are converted back to UCCA for evaluation. The second strategy introduces a novel, direct parser called the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP). BSP is a transition-based parser specifically designed to generate BSS graphs by extending existing systems with new transitions and features to handle multiple parents and other complexities of UCCA.

Experiments are conducted on the English UCCA-annotated Wikipedia corpus for in-domain evaluation and the "Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea" corpus for out-of-domain evaluation. The authors also propose a new evaluation metric based on matching the yields of labeled edges. Results show that a conversion-based approach using a state-of-the-art LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest performance on the primary tree structure of the graphs (69.2% F-score). In contrast, the direct BSP parser achieves a lower F-score on primary edges but is uniquely capable of predicting the non-tree "remote" edges that create the DAG structure.

### Main Contributions

1.  **The first parser for the UCCA formalism and the general class of Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS).** The primary contribution is the design, implementation, and evaluation of BSP, a novel transition-based parser that can directly generate grounded semantic graphs with multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. This work introduces and formalizes a new semantic parsing task.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical benchmark for UCCA parsing.** The paper establishes a solid empirical foundation for future work by comparing the proposed direct parsing method against strong conversion-based baselines that leverage state-of-the-art constituency and dependency parsers. The inclusion of upper-bound analyses for the conversions provides a clear picture of the trade-offs and challenges inherent in the task.
3.  **A principled evaluation metric for BSS parsing.** To facilitate evaluation, the paper defines a labeled precision/recall metric based on matching the terminal yields of predicted edges. This metric is a necessary contribution for this new task and correctly generalizes the standard PARSEVAL metric for constituency trees to DAG structures.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a novel and well-motivated problem.** The paper identifies a clear gap in the semantic parsing literature: the lack of parsers for grounded representations that simultaneously handle DAG structures, non-terminal phrasal nodes, and discontinuities. By formalizing this task and using UCCA as a concrete framework, the work opens up a new and valuable research direction. The motivation for needing all three structural properties is well-argued and supported by examples.
2.  **Technically sound parser design.** The proposed BSP parser is a thoughtful and technically sound integration and extension of established transition-based parsing techniques. It logically combines components from parsers for discontinuous constituency and dependency DAGs and introduces novel transitions (e.g., `LEFT-REMOTEX`, `RIGHT-REMOTEX`) specifically to handle the primary vs. remote edge distinction in UCCA. The transition system is clearly specified and appears to be a robust foundation for this parsing task.
3.  **Thorough and insightful experimental evaluation.** The experimental design is a major strength. The authors do not merely present their novel parser in isolation; they create a challenging benchmark by adapting several state-of-the-art systems via conversion. This comparison, particularly with the high-performing LSTM dependency parser, provides critical context for the results. The inclusion of upper-bound calculations for the conversion methods and an out-of-domain test set further strengthens the empirical analysis and demonstrates the robustness of the findings.
4.  **High degree of clarity and reproducibility.** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the problem, the proposed methods, and the experimental results. The provision of detailed algorithms for the conversion procedures and a clear table defining the transition system significantly aids understanding. The authors' commitment to making their code and converters publicly available is commendable and will be a great benefit to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Modest empirical performance of the proposed direct parser.** A key weakness is that the novel BSP parser is significantly outperformed on its core tree-parsing task (primary edges) by a baseline method that relies on conversion to dependencies (a 59.9% F-score for BSPTree vs. 69.2% for the LSTM parser). While BSP is the only method presented that can predict remote edges, its performance on that sub-task is very low (13.3% F-score). The results, as they stand, suggest that a pipeline approach (parse to dependencies, then convert) is currently more effective for the bulk of the parsing task than the more complex, integrated direct model.
2.  **Limited analysis of remote edge prediction.** The primary advantage of the BSP parser is its ability to predict remote edges, which form the DAG structure. However, the paper provides little analysis beyond the aggregate F-score for this capability. An error analysis detailing the types of remote relations that are learned successfully versus those that are missed would be highly valuable. Without this, it is difficult to assess the current limitations of the model in handling multi-parent phenomena or to understand the specific challenges that future work should address.
3.  **Potential for stronger conversion-based baselines.** The inverse conversion from dependency trees back to UCCA-style structures (Algorithm 2) relies on simple, rule-based heuristics for re-introducing non-terminal nodes and their labels. While the upper-bound analysis correctly identifies information loss in the forward conversion as a major issue, it is possible that a more sophisticated, perhaps learned, model for the inverse conversion could further improve the performance of the dependency-based baseline. The simplicity of the current back-mapping might understate the full potential of the conversion-based approach.
