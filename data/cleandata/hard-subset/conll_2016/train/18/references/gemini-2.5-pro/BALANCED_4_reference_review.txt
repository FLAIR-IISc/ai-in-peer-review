### **Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper introduces the task of parsing into Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations that support multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. The work is framed around the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme, which exhibits these properties but for which no parser currently exists. The authors propose two main approaches: 1) a conversion-based method that transforms UCCA graphs into more standard dependency or constituency trees, allowing the use of existing state-of-the-art parsers, and 2) a novel, direct transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), specifically designed to handle all three properties of BSS. These methods are evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, establishing the first parsing results for this representation.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Formulation of the UCCA parsing task and a dedicated parser.** The paper is the first to formally define and tackle the problem of parsing into the rich UCCA representation. The development of the BSP parser, a transition-based system capable of directly producing the required DAG structures with non-terminals and discontinuities, represents a significant and foundational contribution to this new area.
2.  **A thorough empirical investigation using a conversion-based methodology.** Beyond proposing a new model, the authors provide a strong set of baselines by developing conversion protocols to and from standard syntactic formalisms. This allows them to leverage powerful existing parsers (including a state-of-the-art LSTM-based model) and provides a clear picture of the task's difficulty and the relative performance of different strategies.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Task.** The paper addresses a novel and important problem. As the field moves towards richer semantic representations, parsing formalisms like UCCA that are grounded, cross-linguistically motivated, and structurally flexible is a crucial research direction. This work takes the first concrete step, establishing a new task, providing datasets, an evaluation metric, and initial baselines, which will undoubtedly spur future research.
2.  **Comprehensive and Methodologically Sound Approach.** The dual-pronged approach of developing both a direct parser (BSP) and a conversion-based method is a major strength. The conversion-based approach provides powerful and realistic baselines, grounding the performance of the novel BSP parser in the context of existing state-of-the-art technology. This comparative framework is much more insightful than simply presenting a new parser in isolation.
3.  **Insightful Experimental Analysis.** The experiments are well-designed and the results are analyzed thoughtfully. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation demonstrates the robustness of the methods. The finding that a conversion-based approach using an LSTM parser achieves the highest F-score on primary edges is a key result. The authors' candid discussion of this fact provides a clear and promising direction for future work: integrating neural classifiers into the direct parsing framework. The feature ablation study also provides valuable insights into the components of the BSP model.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The transition system, feature sets, and conversion algorithms are described clearly. The commitment to release the code for the parsers and converters is commendable and greatly enhances the paper's value to the community by ensuring the work is reproducible and extensible.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Sub-optimal Performance of the Main Technical Contribution.** The primary weakness is that the proposed novel parser, BSP, is significantly outperformed on its main task—parsing primary edges—by a baseline that uses existing technology (59.0% F1 for BSP vs. 69.2% for the LSTM-based dependency parser). While BSP has the unique capability to parse remote edges, its performance on them is very low (13.3% F1). This performance gap raises questions about the immediate utility of the proposed transition system and features compared to the simpler and more effective conversion-based approach. While the paper correctly identifies a path for improvement, the current results show that the main technical novelty is not yet the state of the art for the task it introduces.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Remote Edge Parsing.** The ability to handle remote edges (multiple parents) is a key motivation for the BSP parser. However, performance on this subtask is low, and the paper lacks a deep analysis of why this is the case. An error analysis would be highly beneficial. For example, is the issue low recall (failing to predict remote edges) or low precision (predicting incorrect ones)? Are certain types of remote edges (e.g., those involved in argument sharing) harder than others? Without this analysis, it is difficult to understand the specific challenges and how to address them in future work.
3.  **Insufficient Discussion of the Novel `ratio` Feature.** The paper introduces a novel real-valued `ratio` feature (terminals-to-nodes ratio) intended to regularize the creation of non-terminals. This is an interesting idea, as it introduces a global graph property into a local decision-making process. However, its effect is not analyzed beyond the feature ablation table, which shows it provides a significant boost (mostly in recall). A more detailed discussion of the intuition behind this feature and how it helps the parser would strengthen the paper. For instance, does it primarily prevent the parser from creating overly complex, deep structures?
4.  **Heuristics in the Dependency Back-Conversion.** The conversion from dependency trees back to UCCA structures relies on heuristics, such as a rule-based function for labeling new non-terminals and a fixed high-attachment strategy. The upper bound for this conversion path is 88.4%, indicating a non-trivial information loss. While this primarily affects the baseline, it does mean the comparison between the direct and conversion-based approaches is not perfectly controlled. A brief discussion of the impact of these heuristics on the baseline's performance would be welcome.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance gap between your perceptron-based BSP and the LSTM-based dependency baseline is substantial (~10 F1 points on primary edges). Could you elaborate on the expected challenges in integrating an LSTM-based classifier into the BSP transition system? Given that your system has a more complex state and transition set (e.g., NODEX, SWAP, remote edges) than a standard dependency parser, do you foresee any specific difficulties beyond implementation effort?
2.  The F-score for remote edges is quite low (13.3%). Could you provide a brief error analysis? For instance, what is the precision vs. recall on these edges? Is the main problem failing to propose a remote edge at all, or proposing it with the wrong label or for the wrong nodes? This would help clarify whether the issue lies with the features, the search, or the inherent difficulty and sparsity of the phenomenon.
