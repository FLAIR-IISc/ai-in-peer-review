**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations that support multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents. The authors use the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of BSS. They propose two main approaches to this new task. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted into more restricted formalisms (constituency trees or dependency trees) to be parsed by existing state-of-the-art parsers, with the results converted back. The second is a novel, direct approach using a transition-based parser, named BSP, which is designed to handle all three properties of BSS by extending existing transition systems for discontinuous and DAG parsing. The methods are evaluated on the English UCCA corpus, establishing the first parsing results for this representation.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **Problem Formulation and Baselines:** The paper is the first to formalize and tackle the UCCA parsing task. It provides a comprehensive baseline evaluation by cleverly adapting existing parsers through conversion, which also yields insightful upper-bound analyses on the information loss incurred by such conversions.
2.  **A Novel Transition-Based Parser (BSP):** The authors design and implement a complete transition system capable of directly producing the complex graph structures of UCCA. While its components are adaptations of prior work, their synthesis into a single system for this new task is a solid engineering contribution.
3.  **Introduction of Evaluation Metrics:** The paper proposes a labeled precision/recall/F-score metric for BSS graphs based on matching edges by their labels and terminal yields. This provides a necessary foundation for future work on this task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Task:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and challenging new problem. The limitations of existing semantic formalisms are clearly articulated, and the choice of UCCA as a target representation that overcomes them is well justified. Establishing the first parsing results for this rich, cross-linguistically motivated scheme is a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The inclusion of conversion-based approaches is a significant strength. It grounds the performance of the novel BSP parser in the context of what is achievable with existing, highly optimized tools. The finding that an LSTM-based dependency parser achieves the best performance on primary edges, despite the lossy conversion, is a particularly important result that provides clear direction for future work.
3.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The design of the BSP parser is logical and well-grounded. It correctly identifies and integrates techniques from prior work on transition-based parsing for related complex structures (discontinuous constituents, DAGs). The extension with `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE` transitions is a straightforward and effective way to handle multiple parents within this framework.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Algorithmic Clarity and Reproducibility:** Several key algorithmic details are either ambiguous or entirely omitted, severely hindering reproducibility.
    *   **Algorithm 1:** The head-finding procedure relies on a predefined "linear order over possible edge labels L". This ordering is never specified. Since head selection is fundamental to the dependency conversion, this is a critical missing detail. The definition of `n(t)` is also unnecessarily convoluted.
    *   **Algorithm 2:** The inverse conversion from dependencies to constituencies is highly heuristic. It relies on a rule-based function, `Label(u)`, which is deferred to "Supplementary Material" that was not provided with the paper. This makes the entire dependency-based baseline difficult to interpret or reproduce, as the reconstruction of non-terminal nodes is a black box.
    *   **BSP Transition System:** The condition for the `SWAP` transition in Figure 2 is given as `i(x) < i(y)`, where `i(x)` is a "running index for the created nodes". This notation is non-standard and ambiguous. It is unclear if this refers to a node creation timestamp or an index based on the span of terminal nodes.

2.  **Insufficient Technical Detail and Justification:**
    *   The paper introduces a "novel feature" called `ratio` and claims it "should be beneficial for other transition-based constituency parsers too." This is a strong claim supported only by a single entry in an ablation table. Without further analysis or experimentation, this remains pure conjecture.
    *   The training procedure mentions using a dynamic oracle, but the implementation details for such a complex target structure (DAGs with discontinuities) are glossed over. The process of generating the set of optimal transitions at each state is non-trivial and warrants a more detailed explanation.

3.  **Inconsistent and Unprofessional Formatting:** The paper is marred by a significant number of formatting errors, particularly in the bibliography.
    *   The reference list is inconsistently formatted and contains numerous errors, such as stray question marks ("Abend and Rappoport.,? 2013"), backslashes ("Ambati et al\.,?"), and strange escape codes ("\Q2008\E"). The entry for Goldberg and Elhadad (2011) incorrectly merges the title of another paper (Goldberg and Nivre, 2012) into its abstract.
    *   There are typos in the main text, such as the test set range being specified as "passages 701–695".
    *   This lack of attention to detail undermines the credibility of the scientific content and is not acceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference.

4.  **Weakness in Comparative Analysis:** The comparison between BSP (using a perceptron) and the LSTM-based dependency parser is not entirely fair. The results in Table 2 clearly show that the choice of classifier (LSTM vs. perceptron/SVM) accounts for a massive 9.1% F-score difference between two otherwise identical arc-standard parsers. While the authors correctly note this as a path for future work, it also means that the current BSP parser's performance is likely limited more by its classifier than its transition system design. The direct comparison to MaltParser (59.0 vs. ~60 F1) is more appropriate and shows BSP is competitive but not superior.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding Algorithm 1, what is the precise "linear order over possible edge labels" used to determine the head child `h(u)`? How does the performance of the dependency conversion baseline change if this order is varied?
2.  Regarding Algorithm 2, can you please provide the full definition of the rule-based function `Label(u)` that was omitted from the paper?
3.  For the `SWAP` transition in the BSP system, please clarify the meaning of the condition `i(x) < i(y)`. Does the index `i(·)` refer to the order in which nodes were created, or an index derived from the sentence's original token order (e.g., the leftmost terminal in a node's yield)?
