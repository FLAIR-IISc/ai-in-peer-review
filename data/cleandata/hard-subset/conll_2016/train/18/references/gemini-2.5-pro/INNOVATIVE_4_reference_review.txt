### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the task of broad-coverage semantic parsing (BSS), which aims to produce semantic representations that are DAGs, accommodating multiple parents, non-terminal nodes for multi-word units, and discontinuous constituents. Using the UCCA annotation scheme as a concrete example of BSS, the authors present the first parsing systems for this formalism. The work follows two distinct strategies: first, a conversion-based approach that maps UCCA structures to simpler formalisms (dependency and constituency trees) to leverage existing high-performance parsers. Second, and more importantly, the paper proposes a novel, direct transition-based parser called BSP, specifically designed to generate all three structural properties of BSS in a unified framework.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Defining and tackling a novel, ambitious parsing problem.** This is, to my knowledge, the first work to attempt parsing for a representation like UCCA that synthesizes the challenges of non-local dependencies (DAGs), phrasal structure (non-terminals), and non-projectivity (discontinuities). This is a significant step towards parsing richer, more expressive semantic representations and opens a new avenue for research.
2.  **The proposal of BSP, a novel and principled transition-based parser.** The key novelty lies in its transition system, which elegantly integrates mechanisms from discontinuous constituency parsing (SWAP), constituency parsing (NODE), and DAG dependency parsing (separate LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE transitions and decoupling REDUCE from edge creation). This provides a single, coherent model for a highly complex output structure, which is a substantial conceptual and engineering contribution.
3.  **A thorough empirical grounding for a new task.** The paper establishes the first benchmarks for UCCA parsing, providing not only a novel parser but also strong baselines via the conversion method. This analysis, including the upper-bound calculations, carefully delineates the challenges of the task and provides a solid foundation for future work.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Vision.** The paper's primary strength is its boldness. Instead of making incremental improvements on existing tasks, it charts a new course by tackling a complex, multi-faceted parsing problem that has been previously unaddressed. The goal of building a single parser for structures with DAGs, phrases, and discontinuities is highly ambitious and forward-looking. This is precisely the kind of work that can push the field in new directions.
2.  **Elegant and Unified Model Design.** The proposed BSP parser is a well-conceived system. Rather than a complex pipeline that handles different structural properties separately, BSP provides a unified transition system. The design choices, such as distinguishing primary vs. remote edge creation and decoupling reduction to allow multiple parents, are principled and directly motivated by the properties of the target representation. This makes the model more than just a combination of existing tricks; it is a thoughtful synthesis tailored to the problem.
3.  **Strong and Illuminating Experimental Setup.** The inclusion of the conversion-based approach is a major strength. It grounds the performance of the novel BSP parser in the context of what is achievable with current state-of-the-art technology (like the LSTM dependency parser). This comparison is extremely valuable, as it simultaneously demonstrates the feasibility of parsing UCCA's tree-like backbone to a reasonable accuracy while also highlighting the inherent limitations of formalisms that cannot represent remote edges. It provides a clear picture of both the solved and unsolved parts of the problem.
4.  **Honest and Insightful Analysis.** The authors are commendably transparent about their results. They openly acknowledge that their novel parser is outperformed on primary edges by the conversion-based LSTM parser. This self-criticism strengthens the paper, and their conclusion—that BSP's classifier is a prime candidate for improvement (e.g., with LSTMs)—is both convincing and provides a clear roadmap for future work. The main goal of a foundational paper like this is to demonstrate feasibility and map out the territory, which this work does admirably.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Modest Empirical Performance of the Novel Model.** The primary weakness is that the main technical contribution, the BSP parser, does not achieve the best performance on the task's backbone (primary edges). Furthermore, while it is the only system capable of predicting remote edges, its F-score of ~13% is very low. While this does not detract from the novelty of the approach, it does suggest that the proposed model in its current form is more of a proof-of-concept than a practical, high-performance system. Given the ambition of the project, this is an acceptable trade-off, but a weakness nonetheless.
2.  **Incremental Nature of the Learning Component.** While the transition system is novel, the learning machinery (averaged perceptron) and the feature sets are largely adapted from prior work. The performance gap between the perceptron-based MaltParser and the LSTM-based parser in their own experiments suggests that the choice of classifier is critical. While it is understandable to start with a simpler model, the paper would have been even more compelling if it had integrated a more powerful learning component into the novel BSP framework from the start.
3.  **Heuristics in Back-Conversion.** The dependency-to-constituency conversion algorithm (Algorithm 2) relies on heuristics for re-introducing non-terminal nodes (e.g., the `Label(u)` function, assuming high attachment). The impact of these heuristics on the high dependency-based baseline scores is not fully analyzed. While the upper-bound calculation provides a ceiling, a more detailed discussion of how these choices affect the final performance would have been beneficial.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap on primary edges between BSP and the LSTM-based dependency parser is quite large (nearly 10 F-score points). You convincingly argue this is likely due to the power of the LSTM classifier. Is it also possible that the greedy search of your transition-based system is a limiting factor, and that the dependency parser, despite being greedy, operates on a simpler search space that is easier to navigate?
2.  The F-score for remote edges is a promising start but still very low. Could you provide a brief error analysis? Are there particular categories of remote edges (e.g., those involved in argument sharing across clauses vs. local coordination) that are particularly difficult for BSP to identify? Understanding the nature of these errors would be invaluable for guiding future research on this challenging aspect of the problem.
