### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the first parsing methods for Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA), a grounded semantic representation scheme notable for supporting three key properties simultaneously: multiple parents (DAG structures), non-terminal nodes for multi-word expressions, and discontinuous units. The authors frame this as the first work on "Broad-coverage Semantic Structure" (BSS) parsing. They pursue two distinct strategies. The first is an indirect, conversion-based approach that transforms UCCA graphs into more restricted formalisms (dependency or constituency trees), allowing the use of existing state-of-the-art parsers. The second is a direct approach, presenting a novel transition-based parser, BSP, specifically designed to produce BSS graphs by incorporating transitions for creating non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents (via "remote" edges). Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that a conversion-based approach using a state-of-the-art LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest accuracy (69.2 F1) on the primary tree structure, while the direct BSP parser, though less accurate on the primary structure, is the only method capable of predicting the full DAG, including remote edges.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Pioneering the Task of UCCA Parsing:** This is the first paper to present a parser for the UCCA formalism. By doing so, it opens up a rich, cross-linguistically motivated semantic representation for use in NLP applications. This is a significant step in moving beyond tree-structured semantic parsing.
2.  **A Comprehensive Empirical Study with Strong Baselines:** The authors provide a rigorous evaluation that not only introduces their novel parser (BSP) but also establishes its performance relative to very strong baselines. The use of conversion to leverage a state-of-the-art LSTM-based dependency parser is a key strength, providing a realistic assessment of where the new model stands. The analysis of the conversion's theoretical upper bound is also highly insightful.
3.  **A Novel Transition System for Full BSS Parsing:** The proposed BSP parser, which synthesizes techniques from discontinuous, constituency, and DAG parsing, is a solid technical contribution. Its transition set, particularly the explicit `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX` actions, provides a clean and direct mechanism for handling the multiple parent phenomenon central to UCCA.

### Strengths

1.  **High-Impact Problem Formulation:** The paper successfully identifies and motivates a gap in semantic parsing: the lack of parsers for grounded representations that simultaneously handle DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities. By tackling parsing for UCCA, the work addresses this gap directly and introduces a challenging but valuable new task to the community.
2.  **Technically Sound and Well-Motivated Direct Parser (BSP):** The design of the BSP parser is a thoughtful integration of established transition-based parsing techniques. It combines the `SWAP` transition for discontinuities, the `NODE` transition for non-terminals, and a non-reducing edge creation policy for DAGs in a principled way. The addition of dedicated transitions for remote edges is an elegant solution to the multi-parent challenge.
3.  **Rigorous and Transparent Experimental Design:** The comparison between the direct and conversion-based approaches is excellent. Using a top-performing parser like the Dyer et al. (2015) LSTM parser as a baseline sets a high bar and makes the results much more informative. The separate reporting of scores for primary and remote edges, along with the calculation of upper bounds for the conversion methods, allows for a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs involved.
4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly explains the core linguistic phenomena with helpful examples and diagrams (Figure 1). The technical descriptions of the conversion algorithms and the transition system are clear and easy to follow. This high level of clarity makes the paper's contributions accessible and convincing.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Substantial Performance Gap on Primary Structure:** The primary weakness of the results is the performance of the direct BSP parser on primary edges (59.0 F1), which is over 10 F1 points lower than the best conversion-based baseline (69.2 F1). While BSP is the only system that can predict remote edges, this large gap on the core tree structure suggests that its current model (linear classifier, greedy search) is not yet competitive with state-of-the-art parsing technology for the foundational part of the task. The authors note this and suggest LSTMs as future work, but it remains a significant limitation of the presented system.
2.  **Very Low Performance on Remote Edges:** The F1-score for remote edges is only 13.3%. While this is the first system to even attempt this, such a low score indicates that the model has great difficulty learning to predict these challenging, non-local dependencies. This limits the current practical utility of the parser's full DAG-parsing capability. An error analysis would have been valuable here to diagnose the sources of these errors and suggest more targeted modeling improvements.
3.  **Incremental Novelty of the Transition System:** The BSP system is primarily a clever synthesis of existing components from different lines of transition-based parsing research (e.g., `SWAP` from discontinuous parsing, non-reducing attachments from DAG parsing). The main novelties are the remote edge transitions and a few features. While this is effective engineering, the paper could be slightly more explicit in distinguishing between adapted and entirely novel components to more accurately frame its algorithmic contribution.
4.  **Potential Weakness in the Dependency Back-Conversion:** The algorithm for converting dependency trees back to UCCA-style constituency graphs (Algorithm 2) appears heuristic and relatively simple. It is possible that a more sophisticated, perhaps learned, back-conversion method could improve the dependency-based baseline's performance, potentially widening the performance gap with BSP. This makes it slightly difficult to fully attribute the baseline's performance loss (relative to its upper bound) between the parser and the back-conversion step.

### Questions to Authors

1.  You convincingly argue that the performance gap between BSP and the LSTM-based conversion parser on primary edges is likely due to the classifier. However, could other architectural differences, such as the search algorithm (greedy in BSP vs. beam search used by UPARSE) or the transition system itself, contribute to this gap? For example, is an arc-standard system inherently better at finding the primary tree structure than your more flexible system?
2.  The F1 score for remote edges is very low. Could you provide a brief qualitative error analysis? For instance, what are the most common types of remote relations the parser fails to predict? Are failures correlated with the linear distance between the parent and child, or with particular UCCA categories?
3.  The ablation study shows that your novel `ratio` feature has a large impact, primarily on recall. This is interesting, as it's a global graph property used to inform local decisions. Could you elaborate on your intuition for why this feature is so effective and why it specifically boosts recall of primary edges?
