Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing (BSS) that supports three properties: multiple parents (remote edges), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. It focuses on UCCA as the target representation.
- Two approaches are presented: (i) conversion-based parsing by mapping UCCA to constituency or dependency trees, training existing parsers, then converting back; (ii) a novel transition system (BSP) that directly constructs UCCA-style DAGs with discontinuities and remote edges.
- An evaluation on English UCCA (Wikipedia as in-domain; 20K Leagues as out-of-domain) shows best primary-edge F1 ≈ 69% via a converted dependency approach with a stack-LSTM parser; BSP achieves ≈59% primary F1 and low remote-edge F1 (~13%).

As I see it, the core contributions are:
1) A unified transition system (BSP) that handles non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents for grounded semantic parsing, plus a feature set and dynamic-oracle training.
2) A practical study of lossy conversions between UCCA and tree formalisms (constituency/discontinuity and dependencies), including explicit algorithms for both directions, with empirical upper bounds.
3) An evaluation protocol for UCCA parsing based on labeled yield matching, distinguishing primary and remote edges.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the representational desiderata (non-terminals, discontinuities, multiple parents) and why UCCA is an appropriate testbed. The motivation is timely and compelling.
- The transition system is well-scoped and operationalized: it integrates NODE, EDGE (primary vs remote), SWAP, and FINISH, and enforces core constraints (e.g., only one primary parent). The condition x ∉ w1:n to prevent terminals as parents is a sensible design choice.
- Conversion algorithms are described concretely (Algorithms 1 and 2), with a principled head-determination using label priority and an explicit high-attachment heuristic in the inverse mapping. Reporting upper bounds is valuable and shows the information loss from conversions (notably, 88.4 F1 cap for dependency).
- Diverse baselines: projective MaltParser (arc-standard/eager), a strong stack-LSTM dependency parser, and a discontinuous constituency parser (UPARSE). Showing the LSTM gap over Malt underlines classifier-driven gains and suggests a viable path to improve BSP.
- Out-of-domain evaluation without retuning (20K Leagues) demonstrates some robustness; performance degradation is modest on primary edges.

Weaknesses
- The proposed BSP underperforms substantially against the converted stack-LSTM baseline (≈59 vs 69.2 primary F1). Given the primary claim is a new direct parser for BSS, failing to match stronger tree-conversion baselines weakens the methodological contribution. The paper itself suggests switching BSP’s classifier to an LSTM; not doing so leaves the main contribution empirically underdeveloped.
- Critical implementation details are underspecified, impeding reproducibility and interpretability: (i) the linear order over labels L (which determines h/h* and thus heads) is not given; (ii) the Label(u) function for inverse dependency→constituency conversion is relegated to supplementary material; (iii) feature engineering relies on h/h* yet the label order is unspecified; (iv) POS tagging source/tagset and whether tags are gold or predicted are not stated. These choices likely affect results and fairness of comparisons.
- Evaluation metric design needs more justification and analysis. Using labeled yield equivalence ignores node height and much of internal topology; different BSS may collapse under this metric. Remote and primary are reported separately, but it is unclear whether “remote” scores are computed only on remote edges or combined; the text is ambiguous. No per-phenomenon analysis (coordination, phrasal verbs, MWEs) is provided, missing an opportunity to evidence that BSP actually addresses the three hallmark phenomena better than conversions.
- The dynamic oracle and transition-system soundness/completeness are not proved or empirically stress-tested. For a novel DAG+discontinuity+non-terminal system, guarantees of acyclicity, termination, and reachability (especially with remote edges) need at least a sketch of correctness. Conditions in Figure 2 contain apparent typesetting errors (e.g., “y 6;G x”) and rely on creation indices for SWAP (i(x)<i(y)) without explanation of invariants.
- Data splits and preprocessing are error-prone and opaque. The test range “701– 695” is reversed; linkage nodes/edges and implicit units are dropped (and 0.18% inter-sentence edges), but the impact on task difficulty is not quantified (e.g., how many remote edges remain, distributional shift). Hyperparameter tuning is not discussed. No runtime or significance testing is reported. Default settings for baselines (e.g., Malt) may disadvantage them or, conversely, be non-optimal for UCCA.
- The related work and baselines are somewhat dated or incomplete. For discontinuous constituency, there exist multiple transition-based systems beyond UPARSE. For DAG parsing, code unavailability is stated as reason to omit DAG parsers; stronger effort to compare against more recent or better-tuned systems would strengthen the case.

Questions to Authors
- Please specify the exact label priority order over L used to define h/h*, and provide the Label(u) rules used in Algorithm 2. How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Clarify the evaluation: are “remote” scores computed over remote edges only? Provide per-phenomenon breakdowns (coordination, phrasal verbs, PP attachment, MWEs) and remote-edge recall by type.
- Detail POS tagging (tagger, tagset, predicted vs gold), embeddings, and feature preprocessing. For the LSTM baseline, specify training settings (embeddings, dropout, etc.).
- Provide a proof sketch or formal argument for termination, acyclicity, and oracle optimality/reachability in the presence of remote edges and SWAP. What constraints prevent cycles among non-terminals with multiple parents?
- Report statistics on the proportion of remote edges and discontinuous units after preprocessing; include ablations for remote transitions and SWAP on those subsets. Can BSP learn to avoid over-generation of nodes without the ad hoc ratio feature?
- Why not integrate an LSTM classifier into BSP, given the large gains observed for dependencies? Any preliminary results?

Recommendation
While the problem is important and the transition system is interesting, the accumulation of methodological gaps, underperforming main model, and numerous presentation/formatting issues (typos, broken references, index ranges, symbol rendering) lead me to recommend rejection at this time. A revision addressing the empirical gap (e.g., LSTM-based BSP), providing missing technical details and proofs, and adding deeper analyses would substantially strengthen the work.
