Summary and Claimed Contributions
The paper targets Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) that require support for (i) multiple parents (DAGs), (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuous units. Using UCCA as the grounded semantic scheme, the authors (a) propose conversion protocols from UCCA to constituency and dependency formats and back, to leverage existing parsers; and (b) introduce a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly builds UCCA-style DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities. They define an evaluation measure based on labeled yields and report in-domain and out-of-domain experiments, feature ablations, and upper bounds for the conversion pipelines.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A transition system and implementation (BSP) that jointly supports non-terminals, discontinuity (via SWAP), and multi-parent edges (remote edges) for grounded semantic parsing. This is the clearest novelty.
2) Practical conversion pipelines (with algorithms) between UCCA and constituency/dependency representations, enabling the use of off-the-shelf parsers and providing a lens on representational trade-offs.
3) An evaluation protocol for BSS (labeled yield matching) and empirical study on UCCA, including out-of-domain results and feature ablations.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and task formulation: The paper convincingly argues why trees and bi-lexical dependencies are insufficient for broad-coverage grounded semantics and motivates UCCA as a suitable testbed. The three desiderata are well exemplified and tied to realistic phenomena (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs).
- Technically sound transition system: BSP extends standard transition-based parsing with NODE, remote-edge creation, and SWAP, without forced reductions, enabling multi-parent structures and discontinuities. The use of a dynamic oracle for training is appropriate and increases the plausibility of learning effective policies in a large local decision space.
- Useful conversion protocols with explicit algorithms: The constituency↔dependency procedures are well specified, simple, and reveal how label priorities substitute for head-percolation. Providing upper bounds quantifies how much structure is lost by each conversion, which is informative when interpreting downstream performance.
- Empirical breadth and baselines: The paper evaluates three plausible routes—discontinuous constituency, dependencies, and direct BSS parsing—plus out-of-domain testing. The strong LSTM dependency baseline (69.2 primary F) shows that conversion can be competitive, while BSP’s ability to predict remote edges (albeit modestly) demonstrates a capability tree-based pipelines lack.
- Feature ablation and a novel regularizer: The ratio feature is a simple, effective regularizer for non-terminal creation, improving recall; the ablation study helps readers understand which feature groups matter.

Weaknesses
- Direct parser underperforms a strong conversion baseline on primary edges: BSP achieves 59.0 primary F (tree variant 59.9), well below the LSTM-dependency conversion (69.2). Given the paper’s central claim about the necessity of direct BSS parsing, this performance gap weakens the practical case, especially since remote edges are rare and the gains there are small (13.3 F in-domain; 13.6 OOD).
- Remote-edge performance and analysis are limited: While BSP can predict remote edges, F-scores remain low. There is little error analysis isolating which remote phenomena are captured or missed, how often remote edges occur, and how mispredictions interact with discontinuities and non-terminal structure. Without this, it is hard to assess whether BSP’s unique capability is practically impactful.
- Evaluation metric may mask structural errors: The labeled-yield measure ignores parent identity beyond the yield and label. Different topologies sharing yields receive credit, potentially overstating structural faithfulness for DAGs. Complementary metrics (e.g., edge/node matching with alignment, or AMR-style Smatch adapted to grounded DAGs) would provide a fuller picture.
- Incomplete comparisons and fairness concerns: The paper notes inability to obtain DAG dependency parsers’ code, but the absence of stronger DAG baselines (even reimplementations or controlled reproductions) limits comparative insight. Moreover, conversion pipelines zero out remote edges by construction, effectively creating an apples-to-oranges comparison on that dimension; a clearer separation of objectives or a remote-edge-free evaluation subset would help.
- Modeling choices lag behind the strongest baseline: BSP uses a linear averaged perceptron without beam search, while the best baseline uses an LSTM classifier. The paper itself suggests this as a future direction; however, given the substantial gap, integrating a neural classifier and/or beam search would be necessary to substantiate the promise of the direct approach.
- Scope restrictions and task simplification: The experiments discard linkage nodes, inter-sentential edges, and implicit units, narrowing “broad-coverage.” It would help to quantify the proportion and impact of the removed phenomena and to discuss how BSP would generalize when they are included.

Questions to Authors
- Coverage and completeness: Is the transition set complete for the class of UCCA graphs you target (under the single-root, acyclic, grounded constraints)? Can you formalize soundness/completeness guarantees or provide oracle coverage statistics?
- Remote edges: What proportion of edges in the corpora are remote? Can you break down remote-edge performance by phenomenon (e.g., shared arguments across Scenes vs. control/raising vs. MWEs)?
- Metric sensitivity: How correlated is the labeled-yield measure with a topology-sensitive metric on a development subset? Do gains/losses persist under an alternative evaluation?
- Conversion details: How was the edge-label priority order determined for h(u)? How sensitive are the dependency conversions and upper bounds to this ordering?
- Training/oracle: How do you construct the dynamic oracle in states with multiple valid derivations involving SWAP and NODE operations? Any statistics on oracle ambiguity or error propagation?
- Efficiency: What are training and decoding speeds for BSP vs. the baselines? Any practical constraints for longer sentences with many discontinuities?

Suggestions for Improvement
- Integrate a neural local classifier (e.g., stack-LSTM or BiLSTM-based features) and/or beam search within BSP; the gap to the LSTM-conversion baseline suggests this is likely to yield sizable gains.
- Add topology-sensitive evaluation and report breakdowns by phenomenon (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, shared arguments) and by continuity to make the advantages of direct BSS parsing more concrete.
- Strengthen baselines: attempt a simple DAG extension to the LSTM dependency parser to allow multi-head attachments, or compare to a reimplemented transition-based DAG parser with dynamic oracles.
- Provide error analysis for remote edges and discontinuous structures; quantify how often the direct parser recovers structures that conversion pipelines necessarily lose.
- Clarify and release the Label(·) heuristic and priority scheme, and provide an analysis of how much each conversion step contributes to loss (with examples).
- Explore multi-task or pipeline variants (e.g., leveraging syntactic parses, or jointly predicting primary and remote edges) and semi-supervised pretraining to improve robustness, particularly OOD.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and technically solid paper that introduces an interesting transition system for grounded semantic DAGs and offers useful conversion tools. However, the empirical advantage of direct BSS parsing is not yet demonstrated on primary structure, and remote-edge gains are modest without deeper analysis. Strengthening the modeling and evaluation would substantially improve the submission.
