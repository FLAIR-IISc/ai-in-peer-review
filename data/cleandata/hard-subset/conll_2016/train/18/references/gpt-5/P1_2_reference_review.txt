Summary and main contributions
This paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing for grounded structures that simultaneously allow (i) multiple parents (reentrancy), (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuities. The work focuses on the UCCA scheme and proposes two complementary strategies: (a) conversion-based parsing via reductions to (discontinuous) constituency and dependency formalisms, and (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that natively builds UCCA-style DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuous units. Experiments on in-domain (Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) English UCCA show primary-edge F1 up to 69.2 via conversion using an LSTM dependency parser, and 59.0/13.3 F1 (primary/remote) for the direct BSP approach.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A practical transition system (BSP) that supports the combination of multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities in a grounded semantic formalism, with a dynamic oracle and tailored feature set.
2) Bidirectional conversion procedures between UCCA graphs and dependency/constituency trees, yielding informative upper bounds and enabling the reuse of existing parsers (including neural LSTMs).
3) A first empirical benchmark for UCCA parsing with in- and out-of-domain results and analysis, showing that strong local classifiers substantially improve primary-edge parsing via conversion.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem and target representation: The paper convincingly argues that grounded semantic parsing must handle reentrancy, non-terminals (for headless structures like coordination), and discontinuities. UCCA is an appropriate testbed and fills a gap left by bi-lexical dependency-only approaches that struggle with headless constructs.

- Clear, implementable transition system for BSS: The transition set (NODE, LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE vs. LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE, SWAP, REDUCE, FINISH) is a clean synthesis of ideas from discontinuous constituency parsing and DAG dependency parsing, adapted to UCCA constraints (single primary parent). The dynamic oracle and the “ratio” feature are sensible engineering choices that improve practicality.

- Thoughtful conversion-based baselines and upper bounds: The paper does more than introduce a new parser; it tests whether off-the-shelf parsers can be leveraged via conversion. The conversion algorithms are principled (e.g., label-priority for head selection) and the explicit upper bounds help interpret results and information loss (100% for primary edges in constituency conversion; 88.4% in dependency conversion).

- Solid experimental setup and cross-domain evaluation: In-domain and out-of-domain evaluations with clear, appropriate metrics (yield-based labeled precision/recall for DAGs, remote vs. primary edges) provide a balanced view of robustness. The out-of-domain drops are moderate, indicating some generalization.

- Insightful empirical takeaway: The finding that a strong LSTM local classifier markedly outperforms classical Malt (69.2 vs. ~60 F1) strongly suggests a straightforward path to improving BSP—plug in a neural scorer—thus making the work actionable.

Weaknesses
- Limited performance on remote edges and modest direct parsing quality: While BSP supports reentrancy, the remote-edge F1 is low (13.3 in-domain; 13.6 OOD), and its primary-edge F1 (~59) trails the LSTM conversion approach by ~10 points. This leaves unclear whether the proposed direct approach is preferable, especially given that conversions are simple and performant.

- Novelty is largely compositional: Individually, most technical components (SWAP, NODE, remote vs. primary edges, dynamic oracle) have precedents in discontinuous constituency and DAG dependency parsing. The main novelty is their combination for UCCA. The new “ratio” feature is helpful but minor. A stronger claim would be supported by demonstrating phenomena that previous parsers truly cannot model even via conversion.

- Comparability and fairness of baselines: The best-performing baseline relies on a modern neural parser, whereas BSP uses a greedy perceptron without beam or neural features. This confounds “approach” vs. “classifier” effects. It would be more convincing to (i) show BSP with beam/neural scoring, or (ii) compare to a non-neural conversion baseline of comparable capacity.

- Missing qualitative/error analyses by phenomenon: The paper would benefit from breakdowns (e.g., coordination, MWE, PP attachment, phrasal verbs, discontinuity rates), error typology, and the specific contribution of REMOTE transitions. Without this, it’s hard to diagnose where BSP helps vs. conversion degrades.

- Reproducibility gaps around conversion details: The dependency conversion depends on a label-priority ordering and a rule-based Label(u) function; both are deferred to supplementary material. Since conclusions hinge on conversions and their upper bounds, more in-paper detail or ablations (e.g., priority sensitivity) would strengthen credibility.

Questions to authors
- Remote edges: What are the main sources of errors for remote edges? Are they due to data sparsity, features, or transition search? Any statistics on how often the oracle proposes REMOTE vs. EDGE transitions and how often they are chosen?

- Conversion sensitivity: How sensitive are dependency conversion results to the label-priority order and the Label(u) heuristic? Did you try alternative priorities or learned heuristics?

- Beam/neural BSP: Did you attempt beam search or a neural classifier for BSP? If not, can you estimate potential gains given the LSTM improvement in the conversion setting?

- Acyclicity and constraints: How does the system ensure DAG acyclicity during parsing? Are there constraint checks to prevent cycles or overgeneration of remote edges?

- Phenomenon-level results: Can you provide a breakdown of performance by key UCCA phenomena (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, PP attachments, discontinuities), and by unit size/height?

Additional comments
- Reporting speed/memory and average number of transitions per sentence would help gauge practicality.
- Since UCCA is cross-linguistic, even small-scale experiments on another language would strengthen generality claims.
- Code/data release is mentioned; please include exact commit/version identifiers for converters and BSP to ease replication.

Overall, this paper tackles an important and underexplored parsing target with a thoughtful combination of methods. While the direct parser lags the best conversion baseline and the novelty is largely integrative, the work establishes a useful benchmark, shows promising avenues (neural scoring) to close the gap, and provides reusable conversions. I lean toward acceptance, conditional on clarifying conversion details and adding finer-grained analyses.
