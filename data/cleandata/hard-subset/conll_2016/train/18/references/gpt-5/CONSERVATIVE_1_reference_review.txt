Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing for UCCA, a grounded DAG representation that allows multiple parents, non-terminal units, and discontinuities. The authors (i) propose BSP, a transition-based parser supporting these three properties through an extended transition set and feature design; (ii) develop lossy conversions between UCCA and (discontinuous) constituency and dependency trees to leverage existing parsers; and (iii) provide empirical results on UCCA English corpora (in-domain Wikipedia and out-of-domain 20K Leagues), including ablations and upper bounds.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A concrete transition system and implementation for UCCA/BSS (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities), with a dynamic oracle and feature set.
2) Conversion protocols between UCCA and constituency/dependency formats, with quantification of conversion upper bounds.
3) An empirical evaluation establishing initial baselines for UCCA parsing, including out-of-domain results and feature ablations.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing on a grounded, broad-coverage semantic formalism. UCCA’s combination of multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities is important and underexplored; providing a first parser and evaluation pipeline fills a gap and may catalyze subsequent work.
- Concrete, reproducible transition design. The transition inventory, constraints (primary vs. remote edges), and state definition are clearly spelled out, with attention to discontinuity (SWAP) and non-terminals (NODE). The dynamic oracle design—while concise—is thoughtful and aligns with prior work.
- Sensible conversion baselines with quantified upper bounds. The conversion procedures are explicit, and the upper-bound analysis usefully shows where information is lost (notably non-terminals and remotes in dependency conversion). This helps interpret the empirical results.
- Initial out-of-domain evaluation. Applying models trained on Wikipedia to 20K Leagues is a welcome test of robustness. The primary-edge F-scores being comparable across domains is encouraging.
- Diagnostic analyses. The feature ablation (including the novel ratio feature) and the observation that the LSTM-based dependency parser substantially outperforms Malt provide practical insights and clear avenues for improvement (e.g., stronger classifiers for BSP).

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty and weak empirical case for the new parser. The transition set and features largely combine prior ideas from discontinuous constituency and DAG dependency parsing with modest UCCA-specific adjustments. Crucially, the strongest primary-edge results come from an established LSTM dependency parser via conversion (69.2 F), not from BSP (≈59 F). The unique capability of BSP—remote edges—is realized with very low F (≈13), offering limited evidence that the proposed architecture actually delivers on the key BSS properties in practice.
- Baselines and comparisons are incomplete. The paper does not compare to strong DAG parsers from the SDP literature or contemporary AMR parsers adapted via grounding, citing code availability. Given the central claim (first broad-coverage parser supporting all three properties), stronger empirical positioning is needed. At a minimum, a more competitive BSP variant (beam search and/or a neural classifier) should be included to establish whether the architecture itself is competitive when fairly tuned.
- Evaluation design raises concerns. The custom metric based on yield matching is reasonable but nonstandard; its behavior for remote edges and shared units deserves more justification (e.g., ambiguity when multiple nodes share yields). The evaluation excludes linkage and implicit units—phenomena that stress BSS representations—weakening the claim of “broad coverage.” Remote-edge recall is especially low; more analysis of error types and category-wise performance is needed to demonstrate that the core BSS distinctions are learned.
- Conversion quality and analysis are shallow. The dependency conversion discards non-terminals and reconstructs them with a simple Label heuristic (often Center), yielding a sizable gap between the upper bound (88.4 F) and best achieved (69.2 F). The paper cites more sophisticated methods (e.g., Kong et al., 2015) but does not implement them. A deeper analysis of what structures are lost and how this impacts tasks (e.g., coordination, MWEs) is needed to validate conclusions about the viability of conversion-based approaches.
- Methodological choices are under-explored. BSP is trained with a greedy perceptron and MINUPDATE but no beam/global inference; SWAP updates are doubled yet other rare decision types (e.g., REMOTE) are not explicitly reweighted. There is no statistical significance testing, no discussion of cycle-prevention in the DAG, limited hyperparameter exploration, and scant detail on class imbalance for remote vs. primary edges.

Questions to authors
1) How do you ensure acyclicity during BSP parsing? Are there explicit cycle checks or constraints beyond “EDGE transitions disallowed where the child already has a primary parent”?
2) What proportion of edges in your datasets are remote, and how does BSP performance break down by remote edge label? Any oracle upper bound on recoverability of remote edges given your transition system?
3) Can you provide a detailed specification of Label(u) for the inverse dependency→constituency conversion and quantify how often each label is chosen?
4) Why not add beam search or a stronger (e.g., BiLSTM) classifier to BSP, given the large margin observed for the LSTM dependency parser? Did you try beam sizes >1, and what was the effect on primary and remote edges?
5) How sensitive are results to the removal of linkage nodes and implicit units? Please report the proportion of removed structures and, if possible, results including them.
6) Can you report per-category F-scores (e.g., for coordination, MWEs, prepositional phrases), especially those motivating non-terminals and discontinuities?

Additional comments
- Please include statistical significance tests for key comparisons (e.g., BSP vs. Malt/LSTM baselines).
- Provide an error analysis emphasizing the three BSS properties: multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities.
- Clarify whether punctuation and separators used in features are also excluded in evaluation, and discuss any mismatch effects.
- Code and data availability at submission time would substantially improve reproducibility.
