Summary and claimed contributions
- Introduces Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) parsing and presents the first parser (BSP) that jointly supports the three key properties needed for grounded semantic parsing: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal units, and discontinuities. This includes a novel transition system (with primary vs. remote edges, discontinuities via SWAP, and node-creation) and UCCA-oriented features (including a useful ratio regularizer).
- Proposes practical, lossy conversion protocols between UCCA and two mainstream formalisms—discontinuous constituency and dependency trees—with explicit algorithms and upper bounds, enabling reuse of existing parsers and a clear empirical comparison across formalisms.
- Provides an initial empirical study on English UCCA (in- and out-of-domain), showing that conversion to a strong dependency parser (stack-LSTM) reaches 69.2 F1 on primary edges, and that BSP is the only approach predicting remote edges at all; defines simple DAG-sensitive evaluation metrics and conducts feature ablations.

Strengths
- Clear, novel problem framing and bold scope: Addressing grounded semantic parsing that simultaneously needs DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities is a meaningful, underexplored direction. Being the first to tackle UCCA parsing with all three properties is a significant conceptual step likely to influence future work.
- A principled, unified transition system for BSS: The extension of transition-based parsing to support remote (non-primary) edges (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX), non-terminals (NODEX), and discontinuities (SWAP) is well-motivated and self-contained. It offers a reusable blueprint for other semantic DAG formalisms beyond UCCA.
- Bridging formalisms via explicit conversion algorithms: The constituency↔dependency conversion grounded in UCCA edge labels (h(·), h*(·)) is elegant and simpler than traditional head-percolation. Providing upper bounds quantifies conversion loss and helps interpret downstream results. This is a valuable service to the community and clarifies relationships between representations.
- Solid empirical protocol with both in- and out-of-domain tests: Results demonstrate that conversion-based baselines are competitive for primary edges, while BSP uniquely captures remote edges. The modest OOD degradation suggests robustness. The feature ablation (highlighting the impact of unigrams/bigrams and the ratio feature) is informative.
- Practical contributions: The intent to release converters and parsers increases impact. The proposed evaluation measure (yield- and label-based) is straightforward and collapses to PARSEVAL on trees, easing adoption.

Weaknesses
- Performance gap and limited remote-edge accuracy: BSP’s primary F1 (~59–60) lags the best conversion-based baseline (69.2), and remote-edge F1 is still low (∼13). Given the paper’s emphasis on BSS, more attention to improving remote-edge prediction (e.g., better negatives, class balancing, global constraints) would strengthen the case.
- Underpowered learning setup for BSP: The greedy averaged perceptron without beam or neural features likely bottlenecks performance. Since the authors already show a large jump from SVM to LSTM in the dependency baseline, integrating a neural classifier (or beam search) into BSP seems essential for a fair comparison.
- Evaluation and conversion limitations: The inverse conversion from dependencies assumes high attachment and inserts heuristic labels, which can bias results and complicate interpretation. Remote edges are always dropped in conversion, making “zero” remote scores unavoidable and lowering the relevance of those baselines for BSS. Some discussion on how much of the UCCA phenomena are actually testable under the conversions would help.
- Missing comparisons and analysis: Although code for prior DAG parsers was unavailable, the paper would benefit from a broader qualitative error analysis (e.g., coordination, MWEs, PPs, phrasal verbs), breakdowns by phenomenon (including discontinuities), and ablations of the transition inventory (e.g., effect of removing REMOTE or SWAP).
- Presentation nitpicks: Some references are malformed, and important implementation details (e.g., Label(·) function specifics, label priority order, hyperparameters and feature counts) are relegated to supplementary material or only briefly described. Clarity could be improved to aid reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Dynamic oracle: How is optimality defined in states involving remote edges and multiple incoming edges? Are there cases where the oracle must choose between competing remote vs. primary edges, and how are ties broken?
- Class imbalance and search: Remote edges are sparse; did you try cost-sensitive training, loss scaling, or biased sampling to address this? Did beam search help, and if not, why?
- Neural BSP: Given the strong gains of the LSTM dependency parser, do you foresee any obstacles to swapping BSP’s local classifier with an LSTM-based model? Any preliminary results?
- Phenomenon-level analysis: Can you provide breakdowns for key UCCA phenomena (coordination, MWEs, PPs, phrasal verbs, Scenes/linkers) for both BSP and the conversion baselines?
- Conversion sensitivity: How sensitive are dependency upper bounds to the priority order over labels and the high-attachment heuristic? Any alternative heuristics tried?

Overall evaluation
This paper is high on novelty and ambition: it defines and operationalizes BSS parsing and delivers the first UCCA parser with all three required properties. While the empirical performance of BSP trails a strong converted baseline on primary edges and remote-edge accuracy remains modest, the conceptual and methodological contributions are impactful and open a promising direction. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the oracle, improving presentation, and (ideally) committing to a stronger learning setup.
