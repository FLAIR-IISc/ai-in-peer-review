Summary and main contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing into UCCA graphs, emphasizing three structural properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. It proposes two approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing via constituency and dependency reductions, and (2) a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that natively supports the above properties. Experiments on in-domain (UCCA Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) English data report up to 69.2% labeled F1 on primary edges (via an LSTM dependency parser + conversion) and 59.0% with BSP, with 13.3% F1 on remote edges.

Contributions (as assessed, strongest first):
1) A transition system (BSP) for UCCA/BSS parsing that jointly supports non-terminals, discontinuities (SWAP), and multiple parents (primary vs. remote edges), with a dynamic oracle and tailored feature set, including a novel global “ratio” feature to regularize node creation.
2) Practical conversion protocols between UCCA and (possibly discontinuous) constituency trees and CoNLL-style dependencies, with upper bounds and baselines using existing parsers (UPARSE, Malt, LSTM).
3) An empirical benchmark for UCCA parsing with in-domain/out-of-domain evaluation, feature ablations, and analysis of conversion loss.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-motivated gap: UCCA is one of few grounded schemes supporting all three BSS properties; prior parsers typically lack at least one. The transition system is clearly specified (Figure 2), with sound constraints (e.g., at most one incoming primary edge) and explicit handling of remote edges. This constitutes a concrete, implementable parsing framework for UCCA.
- Comprehensive experimental design and baselines: The paper evaluates both direct parsing and conversion-based routes, reports upper bounds for conversions (100% primary for constituency, 88.4% for dependency), and includes out-of-domain tests. It demonstrates that a strong dependency parser with LSTMs reaches 69.2% primary F1 after inverse conversion, giving a useful performance reference for the field.
- Technical soundness of the parsing approach: The transition inventory extends established systems for discontinuous constituency and DAG dependencies. Training uses an averaged perceptron with a dynamic oracle and MINUPDATE; transitions for remote edges are explicitly modeled. The feature inventory is extensive (Figure 3), and ablation shows the impact of core sets (e.g., unigrams/bigrams) and the ratio feature (improved primary recall).
- Clear, grounded evaluation protocol: The labeled yield-matching metric is defined for DAGs and reduces to PARSEVAL on trees. The paper distinguishes primary vs. remote edges and reports both precision and recall. Conversion loss is quantified via upper bounds, clarifying how much performance is inherently unattainable in the conversion baselines.

Weaknesses
- Limited accuracy of the direct parser relative to baselines and upper bounds: BSP attains 59.0% primary F1 and 13.3% remote F1 (in-domain), substantially below the LSTM dependency baseline’s 69.2% primary F1 and far from the dependency upper bound (88.4%). This suggests that the current classifier (linear perceptron, greedy inference) underfits the task’s complexity, especially for remote edges (low recall).
- Simplistic and under-specified conversion details: The dependency inverse conversion relies on a rule-based Label() function “mostly” choosing Center; the exact rules and their empirical effect are deferred to supplementary material. There is little phenomenon-level analysis of what structures are lost by conversion and how that affects downstream performance. The study would benefit from finer-grained error analysis per label and construction (coordination, PPs, MWEs, Scenes).
- Evaluation granularity and diagnostics: The yield-based metric does not capture internal attachment structure beyond child yields and labels; for DAGs, different parent configurations may collapse to the same yield match. There is no statistical significance testing, limited per-category breakdown, and minimal analysis of errors on remote edges or discontinuities, which limits interpretability of where BSP succeeds/fails.
- Reproducibility and clarity gaps: Some critical components are incomplete or referenced externally (Label() definition; dynamic oracle specification and guarantees; preprocessing such as tokenization/POS tagging; exact hyperparameters for baselines). Runtime/complexity and beam-search considerations are not reported. The results of non-projective variants of Malt are mentioned but not detailed.
- Novelty is primarily integrative: While the combination of properties for UCCA parsing is new, most algorithmic components (SWAP, DAG edge modeling, dynamic oracle training) are adapted from prior work. Beyond the remote-edge transitions and the ratio feature, methodological novelty is moderate.

Questions to authors
- Dynamic oracle: Is the oracle exact (optimal) for the full transition set, including remote edges and SWAP, or approximate? Please provide a proof sketch or cite a result establishing reachability and optimality.
- Conversion specifics: How is Label() defined in the dependency-to-constituency inverse conversion, and what proportion of reconstructed non-terminals receive Center vs. other labels? Can you quantify the errors attributable to this heuristic?
- Preprocessing: Which tokenization and POS tagger were used? Are tags predicted or gold? How sensitive are results to tagging errors?
- Remote edges: What is the distribution of remote labels in the corpora, and per-label precision/recall? Which phenomena drive the low recall? How effective are the UCCA-specific remote features in ablation?
- Search and model capacity: Did you experiment with beam search and/or neural classifiers for BSP? Given the large gap to the LSTM baseline, can you report preliminary results with a neural local model?
- Discontinuities: How frequent are discontinuous units in the datasets? What is the contribution of SWAP (ablation without SWAP)?

Additional comments
- Provide per-category (label-wise) scores for primary and remote edges to better characterize strengths/weaknesses.
- Include runtime and memory profiles, and the effect of beam size on BSP.
- Clean up references (typos/incomplete entries) and ensure all algorithms referenced in the text are fully specified or linked in the supplementary material.
- Releasing code and converters upon publication will materially aid reproducibility; consider adding scripts for exact data splits and preprocessing.
