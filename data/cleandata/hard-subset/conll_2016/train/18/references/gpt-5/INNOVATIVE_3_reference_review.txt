Summary and main contributions
- A unified transition-based parsing framework (BSP) for grounded semantic graphs that simultaneously support multiple parents (reentrancies), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. This is, to my knowledge, the first parser to target this combination for a concrete, grounded scheme (UCCA).
- A conversion-based framework that maps UCCA graphs to (discontinuous) constituency and dependency trees and back, with explicit algorithms and upper-bound analyses, enabling the reuse of existing parsers. Alongside, the paper proposes a simple, general evaluation metric for BSS based on labeled yields.
- An empirical study on UCCA parsing (in-domain and out-of-domain), showing feasibility: conversion+neural dependency parsing reaches 69.2% primary F1; BSP directly predicts some remote edges (reentrancies), and exhibits robustness across domains. Code/resources are promised.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and scope: The paper squarely tackles broad-coverage grounded semantic parsing with three hard structural properties at once. The transition set (including explicit primary vs. remote edge creation, SWAP for discontinuity, and NODE for non-terminals) and the dynamic-oracle training for DAGs are substantive innovations. The “ratio” feature is a simple but neat regularizer for node creation.
- Bridging representations: The conversion pipelines (with clear, reproducible algorithms and stated information loss) are a pragmatic and insightful way to leverage mature parsers while illuminating what is gained/lost across formalisms. The upper bounds quantify representational mismatch rather than burying it.
- Evidence that the task is tractable: Results on UCCA, including out-of-domain tests, show the problem is solvable to a useful degree. Notably, a standard stack-LSTM dependency parser (via conversion) attains 69.2% primary F1, suggesting headway can be made with modern classifiers. BSP, while modest in aggregate F1, is the only system here predicting any reentrancies at all.
- Relevance and timeliness: UCCA is cross-linguistically motivated and increasingly used. Providing the first parsing baselines and toolchain (including converters) is valuable infrastructure for the community and opens a new line of grounded, broad-coverage semantic parsing research.

Weaknesses
- Modeling underpowered for the direct parser: BSP uses a greedy averaged-perceptron classifier without beam or neural features. Primary-edge F1 lags the neural baseline by a large margin, and remote-edge F1 (~13%) is low. Given that the LSTM classifier clearly helps in the conversion route, the core claim that BSP is a strong direct approach is undercut by the implementation choice rather than the transition system per se.
- Comparability and evaluation framing: Much of the headline performance (69.2% F1) comes from a conversion-to-dependency setup that necessarily deletes non-terminals and all remotes; remote F1 is therefore 0 by design. Comparing this to BSP (which predicts remotes) is partly apples-to-oranges; the evaluation would benefit from disentangling phenomena (e.g., report per-phenomenon scores for coordination, phrasal verbs, reentrancies). Excluding linkage and inter-sentential edges simplifies the task and should be quantified.
- Conversion losses and baselines: The dependency conversion upper bound (88.4% primary F1) shows substantial representational loss; the inverse reconstruction relies on a heuristic Label function and “high-attachment” assumptions. There’s little exploration of alternatives (e.g., learned head orders, structured reconstruction as in Kong et al., 2015), which could meaningfully raise the ceiling and strengthen the conversion story.
- Limited analysis: There is no proof or clear argument for soundness/completeness of the transition system for UCCA DAGs (without implicits). Cycle prevention with remote edges is only informally handled. Error analysis is sparse: no breakdown by label, unit type, or discontinuity/reentrancy phenomenon; no speed/memory analysis; no dev-tuning details. The new ratio feature is introduced but not deeply examined beyond an ablation line.
- Presentation polish: Several references have formatting errors and missing details; some algorithmic components (e.g., the Label function) are deferred to supplementary material and deserve at least a brief intuition in the main text.

Questions to authors
- Can you state (or sketch) a soundness/completeness argument for the transition system with respect to UCCA graphs without implicit units? How do you guarantee acyclicity when adding remote edges?
- What are the corpus statistics per phenomenon (percent of remote edges, discontinuities, coordination, MWEs)? Please report per-phenomenon precision/recall to substantiate where BSP helps most.
- How sensitive are the conversion results to the linear priority order over labels when selecting heads? Could learning this order improve the (currently 88.4% F1) upper bound?
- Have you tried a neural local classifier (e.g., stack-LSTM or BiLSTM features) within BSP? Any preliminary results or obstacles?
- What POS/lexical features were used (gold vs. predicted)? How do results change with predicted tags?

Overall assessment
This paper proposes a bold and timely direction: a unified, grounded semantic parsing framework handling reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuity, with concrete algorithms and the first UCCA parsing results. Despite modest direct-parser metrics and some underpowered modeling choices, the conceptual contribution and the bridging infrastructure are significant. I am inclined to accept, provided the authors clarify the transition system’s properties, expand analysis, and discuss a path toward stronger classifiers within BSP.
