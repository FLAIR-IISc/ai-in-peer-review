Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing for UCCA by (i) proposing a transition-based parser (BSP) that supports three key structural properties: multiple parents (remote edges), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities; (ii) benchmarking conversion-based approaches that map UCCA graphs into (discontinuous) constituency trees or CoNLL-style dependency trees for training with existing parsers, then converting back; (iii) defining an evaluation protocol for BSS based on labeled yield matching and reporting in- and out-of-domain results on UCCA corpora.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A concrete transition system and feature set that together enable parsing UCCA graphs with remote edges, non-terminals, and discontinuities, including a dynamic oracle and a novel “ratio” feature.
2) Empirical comparison of direct BSP vs. conversion-based pipelines (to constituency/dependency), showing a strong LSTM dependency baseline via conversion and modest remote-edge prediction by BSP.
3) Practical conversion procedures between UCCA and trees, along with measured “upper bounds” due to lossy conversion.

Strengths
- Well-motivated task and representation: UCCA’s support for multi-headed DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities makes it a compelling testbed for broad-coverage semantics. The paper clearly articulates why bi-lexical approaches struggle with headless constructions (e.g., coordination), and why grounding in text is useful.
- Transition set design and coverage: The parser combines elements from discontinuous constituency parsing and DAG dependency parsing, extending them with explicit primary vs. remote edges. The inclusion of SWAP for discontinuities, NODE for non-terminals, and distinct REMOTE transitions is principled.
- Dynamic oracle training with comprehensive features: The system uses a dynamic oracle and a reasonably rich feature set drawn from related work, plus a novel regularizer (ratio) to control node creation. An ablation (though insufficiently reported; see weaknesses) suggests many templates contribute.
- Clear baselines, including a strong neural one: The conversion-based dependency setting includes a stack-LSTM arc-standard parser, which reaches the best primary F1 (69.2), giving readers a realistic performance bar and showing headroom for improving BSP (e.g., swapping its classifier).
- Out-of-domain test: Applying models trained on Wiki to 20K Leagues provides a minimal generalization check; BSP’s primary F1 is relatively stable and remote-edge F1 does not collapse entirely.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility gaps: Critical details are deferred or missing. The label priority order over L used in Algorithm 1 is unspecified; the rule-based Label(u) for inverse conversion is only said to “mostly select Center” and is in “Supplementary Material” (not provided). There is no description of cycle-prevention for remote edges, although acyclicity is a core well-formedness property; do LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE transitions check for cycles? Termination and well-formedness guarantees are not established. Source of POS tags and preprocessing are not specified. These omissions hinder reproduction and assessment of design choices.
- Evaluation design and fairness: The “upper bound” for constituency conversion is trivially 100 F1 on primary edges because conversion just deletes remotes; this is not informative. Comparisons confound search regimes (UPARSE with beam vs. others greedy) and classifier strength (LSTM vs. linear perceptron). Hyperparameters are defaulted for baselines, potentially under-tuning them (especially Malt), while BSP is hand-tuned (e.g., doubling SWAP updates). No significance testing or variance is reported.
- Limited empirical depth: Remote-edge performance is low (~13 F1) without analysis of error types, frequency of remotes in the corpora, or per-label breakdown. No ablations over REMOTE-specific features vs. primary performance trade-offs; no study of discontinuity rates/errors (despite a SWAP-oriented design). No speed/efficiency comparisons, nor beam-search experiments for BSP, even though the paper itself suggests classifier improvements could help.
- Dataset and split issues: The split description appears erroneous (“passages 701– 695” as test). The paper states that linkage nodes/edges and inter-sentence edges are removed, but this meaningfully changes the target structure; the implications for coverage are not analyzed. Table 1 and Table 3 are referenced but not present in the provided text; key corpus statistics and ablation results are missing.
- Writing and formatting quality: Numerous reference list errors (question marks, malformed citations, duplicate author listings, “Q2008,” stray backslashes, incomplete venues), inconsistent hyphenation (“Broadcoverage”), awkward figure/pseudocode formatting, and raw URL artifacts. Such issues reflect a lack of polish and complicate verification.

Questions to Authors
1) How do you guarantee acyclicity when adding remote edges? Are there explicit cycle checks in LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX? If not, can cycles arise, and how are such states handled?
2) What is the exact priority order over UCCA labels used in Algorithm 1, and how sensitive are results and “upper bounds” to this order?
3) Please provide the precise rule set for Label(u) in Algorithm 2. How often does it predict labels other than Center, and how does this affect inverse-conversion quality?
4) What POS tagger/tokenizer are used, and are gold vs. predicted POS employed? Are embeddings or lexicon normalization applied anywhere?
5) Can you report per-label precision/recall, the frequency of remote edges, and error analysis for remote/discontinuous predictions?
6) Is beam search for BSP feasible, and if so, how does it impact primary and remote F1? Likewise, what happens if you replace the perceptron with an LSTM classifier?

Additional comments
- Please fix the test split range, include the missing tables, and add variance/significance tests. Ensure fair search settings across baselines or justify differences.
- Consider reporting unlabeled scores and combined (primary+remote) metrics.
- Clarify whether punctuation is removed from training or only excluded from evaluation.
- The claim of being “first” to propose techniques for BSS parsing should be carefully qualified, given prior DAG/discontinuous parsing work; the novelty seems to lie in the specific combination tailored to UCCA with remote edges.
