Summary and contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing with three key structural requirements: multiple parents (remote edges), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. It focuses on UCCA as the only grounded scheme supporting all three. The authors propose (i) conversion pipelines between UCCA DAGs and two more restrictive formalisms (discontinuous constituency and dependency trees) to leverage existing parsers, and (ii) a new transition-based DAG constituency parser (BSP) that directly supports all three properties (via NODE, REMOTE-EDGE, and SWAP transitions), trained with a perceptron and a dynamic oracle. They also define a simple, DAG-compatible, yield-based labeled evaluation and conduct in-domain and out-of-domain experiments on English UCCA.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A transition system and parser (BSP) for grounded semantic DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including explicit handling of remote edges; plus a dynamic-oracle training regime and a small set of novel features (notably the “ratio” feature).
2) Conversion procedures (and inverse procedures) between UCCA DAGs and constituency/dependency trees with clear algorithms, enabling reuse of strong syntactic/semantic parsers and establishing informative upper bounds.
3) An empirical evaluation on UCCA with both direct and conversion-based approaches and a DAG-compatible labeled yield matching metric, including OOD testing and feature ablation.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and scope: The paper crisply argues why a grounded semantic formalism needs multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, and it situates UCCA as the natural testbed. This makes the problem setup compelling and relevant to broad-coverage semantics beyond SRL and AMR.
- Technically sound and well-specified transition system: BSP extends known transition-based parsing ideas to UCCA’s requirements with a clean set of transitions (NODE, LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE vs LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE-EDGE, SWAP, FINISH) and constraints (single primary parent). The design choices are reasonable and align with prior work on discontinuous parsing and DAG dependencies.
- Conversion algorithms and analysis: The forward and inverse conversions are spelled out algorithmically, including an intuitive head selection via edge-label priority and a heuristic inverse reconstruction. The explicit upper bounds (100 for constituency conversion on primary, 88.4 for dependency conversion) quantify information loss and make the comparison to conversion-based baselines more interpretable.
- Solid empirical study and baselines: The paper evaluates both in-domain and OOD, compares against UPARSE, Malt (arc-eager/standard), and the Stack-LSTM dependency parser, and reports precision/recall/F1 on primary vs remote edges. The finding that a strong LSTM dependency parser plus conversion tops primary F1 (69.2) is useful and honest, and it points to a concrete path (neural scoring) for improving BSP.
- Useful features and ablations: The feature set is carefully assembled from related work, enriched with UCCA-specific remote-edge features and the new “ratio” feature, which demonstrably improves recall in ablation. The ablation gives insight into which components matter.

Weaknesses
- Limited performance of the direct parser relative to conversion-based neural baselines: BSP lags the LSTM conversion baseline on primary edges by ~10 F1 (59.0 vs 69.2), and its remote-edge F1 is low (~13). While this still shows feasibility, the gap raises questions about practical utility at present. Greedy inference and linear models are likely bottlenecks; the paper largely defers this to future work rather than demonstrating even modest gains via beam or neural scoring.
- Sparse details and guarantees for the dynamic oracle: The oracle is central to training but is only sketched. There is no proof sketch or completeness analysis for the DAG+discontinuity setting (with remote vs primary edges and SWAP), and no empirical ablation comparing static vs dynamic oracle. This under-specification makes it hard to assess learning stability and the oracle’s optimality.
- Evaluation limitations and analysis depth: The yield-based metric is reasonable, but the paper lacks breakdowns by phenomenon (coordination, MWEs, PP attachment, phrasal verbs), by discontinuity/remote frequency, or by edge label. There is little error analysis. Given the focus on structural properties, per-phenomenon scores would substantiate the claims. Also, statistical significance is not reported.
- Conversion fairness and reconstruction heuristics: The inverse dependency→constituency conversion relies on heuristic high-attachment and a rule-based Label() (often Center). While upper bounds are reported, the main results hinge on this reconstruction, and alternative, stronger reconstruction (e.g., learned or grammar-based) is not explored. This could bias the comparison among conversion-based systems.
- Scope of empirical validation: Despite claims of cross-linguistic applicability, experiments are only on English; linkage edges and implicit nodes are removed; and inter-sentential edges are discarded. This narrows the demonstrated coverage and leaves out challenging UCCA aspects that arguably matter in practice.

Questions to authors
- Dynamic oracle: Can you clarify the oracle’s optimality conditions and provide a brief argument (or reference) that the set of “optimal transitions” is complete and leads to a gold-reachable state in the presence of both REMOTE edges and SWAP? How do you avoid misleading oracle choices when multiple parents must be created in different orders?
- Label priority and inverse Label(): How is the edge-label priority order chosen for head selection, and how sensitive are results to this order? Could you detail the Label(u) rule set used in Algorithm 2 and its empirical impact (e.g., ablating it or varying heuristics)?
- Error analysis: What proportion of errors in BSP stem from missing remote edges vs wrong primary structure vs wrong discontinuity handling (SWAP usage)? Do you have per-label F1 or phenomenon-based results (coordination, MWEs, PP relators)?
- Remote edges: What fraction of edges in the data are remote, and how often do they involve long-distance reentrancy? Have you tried cost-sensitive training or curriculum strategies to improve remote-edge recall?

Additional comments
- It would strengthen the paper to include a small beam or a neural scoring variant of BSP to verify the hypothesized gains, even if only on dev data.
- Reporting unlabeled scores and per-length breakdowns would aid comparability with other parsers.
- Please clarify whether gold POS tags or predicted tags are used, and whether pre-trained embeddings were used in any baseline.
