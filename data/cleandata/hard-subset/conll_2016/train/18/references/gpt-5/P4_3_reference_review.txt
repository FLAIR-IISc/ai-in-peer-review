Summary and Claimed Contributions
The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing where representations require (i) multiple parents (DAGs), (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuous units. Using UCCA as the testbed, the work presents two complementary strategies: (a) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA graphs to (discontinuous) constituency trees and to dependency trees so that existing parsers can be reused and then mapped back; and (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly produces UCCA-style graphs supporting all three structural properties. The paper also defines an evaluation measure for labeled edges via yield matching, reports in-domain and out-of-domain results on UCCA corpora, and conducts feature ablations.

Main contributions (as assessed here), in order of strength:
1) A transition system (BSP) that directly parses to DAG structures with non-terminals and discontinuities, including explicit REMOTE-edge transitions for multiple parents, SWAP for discontinuity, and NODE for non-terminals, trained with a dynamic oracle and an averaged perceptron.
2) Bidirectional conversion procedures between UCCA graphs and both dependency and (possibly discontinuous) constituency trees, enabling the use of off-the-shelf parsers and establishing upper bounds for lossy conversions.
3) An experimental framework for UCCA parsing, including a yield-based labeled evaluation for primary and remote edges, in-/out-of-domain results, and feature ablation highlighting contributions of specific feature sets (e.g., the “ratio” regularizer).

Strengths
1) Technically sound transition system for BSS: The parser’s transition inventory systematically covers the three needed properties: NODE for non-terminals, SWAP for discontinuity, and explicit LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX transitions for multiple parents without violating single-primary-parent constraints. The use of a dynamic oracle for training improves robustness to search errors, and conditions on transitions (e.g., forbidding a second primary parent) preserve graph well-formedness.

2) Clear and implementable conversion algorithms: The paper specifies concrete forward and inverse conversions, including an algorithm from constituency to dependency based on prioritized edge labels (Algorithm 1), and a reverse procedure recreating non-terminals with heuristic attachment (Algorithm 2). These procedures are sufficiently detailed to reproduce, and the reported upper bounds quantify information loss (e.g., dependency conversion upper bound 88.4 F1 on primary edges; remote edges unrecoverable), providing useful ceilings for the conversion approach.

3) Empirical coverage and robustness: Experiments on both in-domain (Wikipedia) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) data, with the same model and no retuning, show comparable primary-edge F1 for BSP (59.0 in-domain, 57.1 out-of-domain) and non-trivial remote-edge F1 (13.3/13.6), demonstrating some generalization across domains. The comparison to strong baselines (Malt, LSTM stack parser, UPARSE) contextualizes progress and indicates where improvements may come from (e.g., better local classifiers).

4) Comprehensive evaluation design: The labeled yield-based metric cleanly reduces to PARSEVAL for trees, isolates primary vs. remote edges, and excludes punctuation consistently. Reporting both precision/recall and upper-bound scores for conversions clarifies where performance is limited by representation loss versus modeling.

5) Feature and training choices are justified: The inclusion of UCCA-specific features for REMOTE edges and a global “ratio” feature to regularize node creation are motivated and evaluated via ablations, indicating tangible contributions (e.g., improved recall on primary edges). The dynamic-oracle training and MINUPDATE help control feature sparsity and training stability.

Weaknesses
1) Limited absolute performance on remote edges: While BSP is the only model predicting remote edges, remote F1 remains low (≈13%). Given that multiple-parent structures are a central motivation, the current recall/precision on remotes limits practical utility. No qualitative error analysis is provided to identify failure modes (e.g., confusion between primary vs. remote, long-distance sharing).

2) Greedy decoding and non-neural classifier limit primary-edge performance: BSP’s averaged perceptron with greedy inference underperforms the neural LSTM arc-standard baseline after conversion (69.2 F1 primary). Since the paper itself highlights the classifier gap, the direct approach’s competitiveness is currently constrained, and no experiments with beam search or neural scoring are reported to close this gap.

3) Conversion inverses are lossy and heuristic: The dependency-to-constituency inverse relies on high-attachment heuristics and a rule-based Label() function (mostly assigning Center). This induces substantial loss (upper bound 88.4 F1 vs. 100 for constituency), potentially biasing comparisons in favor of the conversion-to-constituency route. Details of Label() are deferred to supplementary material, reducing clarity and reproducibility in the main paper.

4) Evaluation scope is restricted: Linkage nodes, implicit units, and cross-sentence edges are excluded, which simplifies the task but reduces coverage of phenomena UCCA was designed to represent. The impact of these exclusions on general semantic coverage is not quantified. Results lack statistical significance tests and detailed per-phenomenon breakdowns (e.g., coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs), making it hard to assess where gains occur.

5) Clarity and presentation issues: Some minor inconsistencies/typos (e.g., test split index “701–695”), partial reliance on supplementary material (Label function), and absence of error analysis reduce clarity. The reference list contains formatting issues, and justification of certain design choices (e.g., default parser settings, non-use of non-projective Malt variants beyond a brief note) could be more explicit.

Questions to Authors
1) Remote edges: What are the predominant error types for REMOTE predictions (missed edges vs. wrong labels vs. wrong endpoints)? Are there specific categories (e.g., shared participants) where performance is higher or lower?

2) Decoding/search: Did you experiment with beam search for BSP, and if so, how did it affect primary and remote edges? If not, is there a reason (e.g., efficiency constraints)?

3) Classifier choice: Can you share preliminary results replacing the perceptron with a neural classifier (e.g., BiLSTM features), or plans to do so? Given the LSTM gains in the conversion pipeline, this seems critical.

4) Conversion heuristics: Please provide the full Label() specification and any sensitivity analysis. How often are attachments misreconstructed due to high-attachment assumptions?

5) Excluded phenomena: What fraction of edges are linkage/implicit/cross-sent in the datasets, and how might including them affect both conversion procedures and BSP?

6) Reproducibility: Will the released code include the dynamic oracle and feature ablation settings, and the exact data splits used in the paper?
