Summary
The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing where representations must support three structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal units, and discontinuities. It uses UCCA as the grounded semantic scheme exemplifying these requirements. The work pursues two strategies: (1) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA graphs to constituency or dependency trees, applies existing parsers, and maps outputs back; and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs UCCA-style DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities. The authors define a yield-based labeled F-score for evaluation, provide algorithms for conversion in both directions, and evaluate on UCCA English corpora (Wikipedia in-domain; 20K Leagues out-of-domain). The best primary-edge F-score is achieved by a dependency conversion plus a stack-LSTM parser (69.2), while the direct BSP achieves 59.0 with limited ability to predict remote edges (≈13 F). Ablations show several feature groups are beneficial, notably unigram/bigram and a novel node-to-terminal ratio feature.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A transition set and training procedure for a transition-based parser that jointly supports multi-parent DAGs, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities in a grounded semantic formalism (UCCA). This includes dedicated transitions for primary and remote edges and SWAP for discontinuities, plus a dynamic oracle.
2) Practical, explicit conversion procedures between UCCA and both constituency and dependency trees, with corresponding upper bounds and an evaluation protocol for BSS based on labeled yields.
3) An empirical study establishing baselines for UCCA parsing, including in-domain and out-of-domain results, comparison to strong dependency parsers, and feature ablations.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and motivation for BSS: The paper articulates why multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities are essential for grounded broad-coverage semantics, and why UCCA embodies these requirements. This framing justifies the need for methods beyond standard bi-lexical dependencies.
- Technically sound transition system and oracle: The proposed transition inventory is well grounded in prior shift-reduce work (including discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing) and extends it to UCCA’s requirements (primary vs. remote edges without forced reductions, SWAP, NODE). Use of a dynamic oracle and structured perceptron training is appropriate and standard for this paradigm.
- Explicit conversion algorithms and analysis: The constituency/dependency conversion algorithms are fully specified, including the use of label priorities and high-attachment heuristics in inverse conversion. Reporting of conversion upper bounds quantifies the information loss, aiding interpretation of results.
- Comprehensive experimental setup: The paper evaluates multiple baselines (UPARSE, Malt variants, stack-LSTM), reports both precision and recall, includes out-of-domain testing, and performs feature ablations. The LSTM dependency baseline provides a strong reference point, showing the promise of neural classifiers for this task family.
- Practical evaluation measure for BSS: The yield-based labeled F-score naturally generalizes PARSEVAL and is applicable to DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities. While simple, it is a reasonable first metric and is reproducible.

Weaknesses
- Direct parser underperforms conversion with a strong classifier: The best-performing system is the dependency conversion with an LSTM classifier (69.2 F on primary), notably higher than the direct BSP (59.0). This weakens the empirical case that directly modeling BSS is preferable with the current learning setup. It also suggests the core contribution’s practical impact depends on upgrading the local classifier/decoder.
- Low performance on remote edges: BSP’s remote-edge F-score is about 13 in-domain and similar out-of-domain, indicating limited success in modeling multiple parents. Given that supporting remote edges is a central motivation, the empirical gains are modest and deserve deeper analysis.
- Evaluation metric limitations: The yield-based metric ignores parent identity beyond the child’s yield and label, potentially overestimating quality when internal structure above spans differs. Remote edges are scored via yields as well, but the metric does not assess graph-level properties such as acyclicity constraints, edge multiplicity accuracy, or attachment depth, which may mask errors.
- Heuristic assumptions in conversion not fully explored: The dependency inverse conversion assumes high attachment and relies on a rule-based Label(u), and the constituency-to-dependency step depends on a label priority order. The paper does not analyze sensitivity to these choices or provide ablations/alternatives; yet these heuristics substantially affect upper bounds and baseline results.
- Methodological gaps: Inference for BSP is greedy without beam search; no statistical significance testing is reported; hyperparameters largely use defaults; and comparisons to contemporary DAG parsers are omitted due to code availability. Together, these make it harder to fairly assess the ceiling of the direct approach under stronger decoding or neural classifiers.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Label priority and Label(u): How is the linear order over edge labels determined? Is it learned or manually specified? Please detail the Label(u) rule set and its impact (e.g., an ablation or sensitivity study).
- Dynamic oracle: Can you formalize the oracle’s optimality for the full transition set including remote edges and SWAP? Are there cases where the gold graph becomes unreachable despite oracle guidance?
- Decoding and classifier: What is the expected gain from beam search for BSP? Have you tried neural local classifiers (e.g., BiLSTM features) within BSP, and how do they compare?
- Error analysis: What are the dominant error types for BSP (e.g., failure to create necessary non-terminals, wrong edge labels, missed remote edges, discontinuity handling)? A breakdown by structure type (coordination, phrasal verbs, PP attachment) would be helpful.
- Metric robustness: Do conclusions hold under alternative graph evaluation (e.g., exact match of parent sets per yield, or Smatch-style alignment adapted to grounded graphs)?

Overall, the paper is clear and technically sound, with moderate novelty: a concrete, first direct parser for UCCA/BSS and practical conversion methods. The empirical results show feasibility but also highlight that stronger classifiers/decoders are needed for the direct approach to surpass conversion-based baselines.
