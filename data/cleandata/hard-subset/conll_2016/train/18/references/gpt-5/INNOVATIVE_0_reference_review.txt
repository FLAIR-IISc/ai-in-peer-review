Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage grounded semantic parsing where outputs must support (i) multiple parents, (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuous units. Using UCCA as the testbed, the authors pursue two complementary strategies: (a) lossy conversions to standard formalisms (discontinuous constituency and dependency) to leverage existing parsers, and (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including remote edges. They also propose an evaluation protocol for such structures.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A unified transition system (BSP) for grounded semantic parsing that jointly supports DAGs (multiple parents), non-terminals, and discontinuities, with a dynamic oracle and specialized transitions for remote edges. This is a meaningful new direction.
2) Practical conversion protocols between UCCA and constituency/dependency formats, with corresponding upper-bound analyses and competitive baselines that illuminate where information is lost and how far existing technology can go.
3) An empirical study on UCCA (in- and out-of-domain), including feature ablations and an evaluation measure for BSS.

Strengths
- Novel and bold problem formulation plus method: The unified transition system that simultaneously handles DAG structure, non-terminals, and discontinuities in a grounded setting is genuinely new and valuable. Prior work typically omits at least one of these aspects; bringing them together in a single parsing framework is a clear conceptual advance.
- Strong and informative baselines via conversion with upper bounds: The conversion-based experiments (to discontinuous constituency and to dependencies) are well thought-out and provide a meaningful picture of what’s achievable with existing parsers, where information is lost (notably remote edges and non-terminals), and why. The upper-bound analyses are particularly useful to calibrate expectations.
- Direct modeling of remote edges: Only the proposed BSP predicts remote edges at all, which are central to DAG semantics. Even if F-scores are modest, showing feasibility and providing a concrete transition design for remotes is an important step that others can build on.
- Clear path to improvement: The comparison to a strong LSTM classifier in the dependency conversion setting (69.2% primary F) suggests that swapping BSP’s local perceptron for a neural classifier and/or adding beam search could yield sizable gains. The paper is explicit about this, which increases its value as a springboard for follow-up work.
- Practicality and breadth: In/out-of-domain results (Wiki → 20K Leagues) are relatively stable, suggesting robustness. The public release of converters and parsers (promised) would further amplify impact.

Weaknesses
- Direct model underperforms on primary edges and has low remote-edge accuracy: BSP primary LF (≈59%) lags the best conversion baseline (69.2%). Remote edges are only ≈13% LF. While novelty is strong, the current empirical performance is limited. The choice of greedy inference and a linear perceptron (vs. beam/global/neural alternatives) is a likely factor.
- Uneven comparison due to lossy conversions: Conversion baselines necessarily drop remote edges, so the head-to-head comparison of overall semantic coverage is incomplete. The paper could strengthen fairness by (a) reporting primary-only vs. primary+remote aggregate metrics, (b) exploring conversions that retain some remotes (e.g., via pseudo-edges or node duplication), or (c) analyzing how much of the remote-edge performance gap is due to modeling vs. conversion loss.
- Missing implementation details hinder reproducibility: The linear order (priority) over UCCA labels used in conversion, the Label() heuristic for inverse conversion, and details of the dynamic oracle (especially with REMOTE and SWAP) are only sketched. Without precise definitions, independently reproducing the conversions and oracle is difficult.
- Limited analysis and scope: Only English is evaluated despite UCCA’s cross-linguistic motivation. Phenomenon-level error analysis (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, long-range dependencies) is minimal; remote-edge errors are not broken down by category. The decision to discard linkage and implicit units reduces the “broad-coverage” claim in practice; more quantification and discussion would help.
- Modeling choices not fully justified: No ablation on beam search vs. greedy; no experiments with neural features or embeddings in BSP; no discussion of runtime/complexity or memory footprint, which matter for practical adoption.

Questions to authors
- Label priority: How is the linear order over edge labels chosen in Algorithm 1? Is performance sensitive to this ordering? Please provide the exact order used and any tuning procedure.
- Oracle and constraints: How is acyclicity enforced when adding remote edges? Beyond “only one primary parent,” what constraints ensure graph well-formedness? Could the oracle produce spurious multiple primaries, and how is this prevented?
- Conversion heuristics: Can you share the exact Label() function and statistics on its choices? Did you try learning the inverse conversion (e.g., via a small classifier) instead of rule-based attachment?
- Error breakdown: What proportion of remote edges are intra- vs. inter-sentential in your data slice, and how does BSP perform by remote label? Similarly, can you report per-phenomenon F-scores for discontinuities (e.g., phrasal verbs vs. MWEs vs. coordination)?
- Search and modeling: Did you try beam search for BSP, or replacing the perceptron with a neural classifier (e.g., BiLSTM features)? If tried, what was the effect; if not, what do you expect quantitatively given the LSTM baseline results?
- Generalization: Any preliminary results on another language/layer to support the cross-linguistic ambition of UCCA/BSP? If not, what domain/language properties do you anticipate will challenge BSP most?

Overall, despite methodological imperfections and modest remote-edge accuracy, this submission opens a new, worthwhile direction by defining and tackling grounded DAG-with-discontinuity semantic parsing and by operationalizing it with a concrete transition system and baselines. I value the ambition and would be inclined to accept, contingent on clarifying the missing technical details and committing to releasing code/converters.
