Summary and main contributions
- A transition-based parser (BSP) for grounded broad-coverage semantic structures (UCCA) that simultaneously supports non-terminal nodes, discontinuities, and reentrancies via a carefully designed transition set (including SWAP and primary/remote edge creation) and a dynamic oracle. This appears to be the first parser to meet all three requirements in a grounded setting.
- A pair of conversion protocols between UCCA DAGs and (i) discontinuous constituency trees and (ii) dependencies, with empirical assessment including upper bounds and competitive baselines (Malt, UPARSE, stack-LSTM), showing that conversion-based approaches can be surprisingly strong for primary structures while inherently losing remote edges.
- An evaluation framework for BSS (a yield+label matching criterion) and practical feature/learning insights (e.g., a novel ratio feature that regularizes node creation). Code and converters are promised for release.

Strengths
- Novel and foundational problem formulation and method. The paper squarely targets a gap: grounded semantic parsing that supports non-terminals + discontinuity + multiple parents. The BSP transition system (NODEX, LEFT/RIGHT-EDGEX vs LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX, SWAP, FINISH) is clearly specified, principled, and extends prior work in discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing. The dynamic oracle is appropriate and well-motivated for training.
- Comprehensive and honest empirical framing through conversions. The constituency/dependency conversion pipelines, with explicit upper bounds, allow the community to leverage strong existing parsers while quantifying what representational loss costs. Notably, the stack-LSTM dependency parser achieves 69.2 F1 on primary edges, substantially above Malt, and the dependency upper bound (88.4 F1) clarifies remaining head-reconstruction limits.
- Unique ability to predict reentrancies. While conversion approaches necessarily zero out remote edges, BSP predicts them (13.3 F1 in-domain; 13.6 OOD). This is important: it validates the transition design for multiple parents and shows feasibility on a hard phenomenon entirely inaccessible to the conversion baselines.
- Rigorous evaluation setup and cross-domain check. The work evaluates on UCCA Wiki and out-of-domain 20K Leagues without retuning, showing robustness (primary F1 drop is modest; remote F1 is stable). The ablation identifies impactful features (e.g., unigrams/bigrams, “ratio” regularizer), giving practical guidance.
- Clear path to improvement is identified. The striking gain of the LSTM classifier over Malt in the conversion track (69.2 vs ~60 F1) strongly suggests that swapping BSP’s perceptron for a neural classifier and/or adding beam search could close much of the gap, making this a promising direction rather than a dead end.

Weaknesses
- Primary-edge accuracy of BSP lags the best conversion baseline. BSP attains 59.0 F1 on primary edges (in-domain), behind the stack-LSTM conversion approach (69.2). While the setup is harder (full DAG vs tree), the headline number can underwhelm readers focused on accuracy. A small beam or a stronger classifier would strengthen the empirical case and better reflect BSP’s architectural potential.
- Limited analysis of reentrancies and discontinuities. Remote-edge F1 (~13) demonstrates feasibility but remains low; the paper lacks per-category diagnostics (e.g., which edge labels, constructions, or graph motifs are learned), error typology, or oracle studies quantifying how much is due to search vs feature representation vs data sparsity.
- Baselines omit modern DAG graph parsers. The paper notes code availability issues and therefore excludes other DAG parsers. Still, even a simplified reimplementation or a strong graph-based decoder over UCCA trees (to add remote edges) would help position BSP against a more competitive DAG baseline.
- Evaluation measure and structural fidelity. The yield+label matching metric is reasonable and reduces to PARSEVAL for trees, but risks overlooking certain structural confounds (e.g., multiple attachments vs attachment height). Complementing it with edge-level matching on terminal spans or intrinsic reentrancy metrics would strengthen claims.
- Under-explored cross-lingual promise of UCCA. Given the cross-linguistic motivation, experiments remain English-only. Even small-scale cross-lingual tests or zero-shot transfer via the conversion pipelines would bolster the “broad-coverage” claim.

Questions to authors
- Do you have preliminary results with a neural classifier (e.g., stack-LSTM) or beam search for BSP? Even development-set numbers would be informative.
- Can you provide a breakdown of remote-edge performance by label and by construction type (e.g., control/raising/coreference/coordination sharing)? How frequent are remote edges in the corpora?
- What is the oracle (upper bound) performance of BSP under perfect classification, and how much is lost to search errors vs representational constraints?
- For the dependency-to-constituency inverse conversion, can you detail Label(u)? How sensitive are results to this heuristic, and could a learned labeler close part of the gap to the 88.4 upper bound?
- Could multi-task or pipeline approaches help (e.g., first predict primary trees with LSTM, then add remote edges with a DAG layer)? Any early evidence?

Overall evaluation
This paper is bold and timely: it defines and operationalizes a grounded, broad-coverage semantic parsing target that truly requires non-terminals, discontinuities, and reentrancies, and provides the first transition system and learning setup for it. Despite BSP’s primary-edge F1 lagging the best conversion baseline, the ability to predict remote edges, the clarity of the formalization, and the conversion framework’s usefulness make this a valuable contribution. The path to significant gains (neural classifier/beam) is compelling and feasible. I lean toward acceptance for its conceptual novelty and for laying a foundation the community can build on.
