Summary and main contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing with three key structural properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. It argues that existing grounded schemes do not support the full combination and focuses on UCCA as the only scheme that does. The authors propose two complementary strategies: (i) conversion-based parsing via lossy mappings between UCCA and constituency/dependency trees; and (ii) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly produces DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including remote edges.

Contributions (as I see them), strongest first:
1) A novel, end-to-end transition-based parser (BSP) that natively supports the full BSS triad: multiple parents (remote edges), non-terminals, and discontinuities. This is, to my knowledge, the first grounded parser with this coverage for UCCA.
2) Simple, concrete conversion procedures between BSS and constituency/dependency trees (with inverse mappings), which enable reuse of existing parsers and clarify representational relationships.
3) An empirical benchmark for UCCA parsing, including evaluation measures tailored to primary vs. remote edges, with in-domain and out-of-domain results and feature ablations.

Strengths
1) Conceptual novelty and scope: The paper squarely addresses a long-standing gap—grounded semantic parsing that jointly supports multi-headed DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities. Introducing BSP and framing “BSS” as the target is a bold and timely direction with clear implications for semantic parsing beyond UCCA.
2) Methodological contribution via transition design: Extending transition-based parsing to cover remote edges and discontinuities in a unified system (with NODE, REMOTE, and SWAP transitions, plus dynamic oracle training) is non-trivial. The setup is principled and compatible with known strengths of transition-based paradigms.
3) Bridging via conversion protocols: The constituency/dependency conversions, while lossy, are simple, reproducible, and useful. They provide an avenue to leverage strong off-the-shelf parsers and to study what is lost when collapsing BSS to trees. This clarifies how far existing infrastructure can go and exposes concrete upper bounds.
4) Empirical grounding and robustness: The experiments cover both in-domain (Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) data; BSP’s performance is relatively robust. Importantly, BSP predicts remote edges (F≈13), where tree-based conversions cannot, highlighting genuine added modeling capacity.
5) Clear path to improvement: The finding that an LSTM-based arc-standard parser achieves 69.2 F on primary edges after conversion strongly suggests that simply swapping BSP’s local classifier for a neural encoder (and/or beam search) could yield substantial gains. The paper demonstrates feasibility and provides actionable next steps.

Weaknesses
1) Modest absolute accuracy on full BSS and limited remote-edge performance: While BSP is novel, its primary-edge F-scores (~59) lag the converted LSTM baseline (~69.2). Remote edges (F≈13) are a start but remain weak in recall. The paper would be stronger with more targeted techniques for remote edges (e.g., specialized features/constraints, global training, or auxiliary losses).
2) Limited baselines and fairness questions: The paper could not compare against existing DAG dependency parsers (code unavailable). This leaves a gap: are there stronger DAG-capable baselines for remote/multi-parent edges? Similarly, the conversion-based comparisons remove remote edges entirely; while illustrative, they don’t provide a like-for-like baseline on the full BSS target.
3) Underpowered inference and modeling choices: BSP uses greedy inference and a linear perceptron, whereas UPARSE uses a beam and the LSTM dependency parser is much stronger. Given the clear gains of neural encoders and beams for transitions, it’s hard to disentangle algorithmic novelty from classifier capacity. A minimal neural/beam ablation for BSP would strengthen the case.
4) Evaluation design and analysis depth: The yield-based labeled matching metric is reasonable, but more analysis would help: error breakdowns for discontinuities vs. coordination vs. phrasal verbs; per-label confusion; how many errors stem from SWAP vs. REMOTE choices; and sensitivity to the ratio feature. Also, excluding linkers and implicit units limits claims about “broad coverage.”
5) Reproducibility details: Some key conversion heuristics (e.g., Label function) are deferred to supplementary material; training/tagging pipeline details (POS source, tokenization) are not fully specified. Given the reliance on conversion and feature engineering, more explicit detail would aid reuse.

Questions to authors (optional)
1) What fraction of BSP errors are due to discontinuities vs. remote edges vs. non-terminal structure? Any per-phenomenon error analysis?
2) Did you try beam search for BSP, or a simple neural scoring function (e.g., BiLSTM features)? How much does performance move with either?
3) For remote edges, which labels benefit most from the UCCA-specific features? Any targeted ablations for LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE transitions?
4) How sensitive are results to the ratio regularizer? Could a learned penalty or node-creation prior help more?
5) Can you clarify the POS tagging setup (gold vs. predicted) and its impact?
6) Are the code and conversion scripts released, and do they reproduce the exact splits and settings?

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. Despite moderate accuracy, the paper introduces a genuinely new and important capability—direct parsing into grounded semantic DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities—along with practical conversion bridges that clarify relations to existing syntactic/semantic parsers. The work opens a promising line of research and provides clear avenues for substantial improvement (neural scoring, beam/global inference) that could rapidly raise accuracy. Given the novelty and potential impact, I’m willing to overlook the current performance limitations.
