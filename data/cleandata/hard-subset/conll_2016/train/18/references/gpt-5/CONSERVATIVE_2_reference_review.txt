Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets parsing into Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) as instantiated by UCCA, emphasizing support for multiple parents (remote edges), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. The work presents:
1) A transition-based parser (BSP) designed to produce DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including new transitions for remote edges and a dynamic-oracle training setup.
2) Conversion protocols between UCCA and two established formalisms (discontinuous constituency and CoNLL-style dependency), enabling reuse of off-the-shelf parsers as baselines; associated evaluation measures for DAGs via labeled yield matching.
3) An empirical study on in-domain (UCCA Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) data, with feature ablations and upper bounds for conversion-based methods.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and scope: UCCA is a grounded semantic formalism with structures that challenge standard parsers. The paper articulates the need for supporting multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities and motivates why bi-lexical SDP or non-grounded AMR do not fully suffice.
- Methodological contribution in parser design: The transition inventory extends prior work on discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing to cover the UCCA phenomena, with explicit handling of primary vs. remote edges, and a dynamic oracle. The feature set is carefully assembled from prior work with some UCCA-specific additions; the ratio feature is a sensible regularizer.
- Conversions and baselines: The conversion pipelines and explicit upper bounds help quantify what is lost by mapping UCCA to trees and back. This is a useful diagnostic and provides a way to leverage established parsers.
- Empirical breadth beyond a single test: Results on both in-domain and out-of-domain corpora (without retuning) are reported, and feature ablations are provided, which is good practice.
- Relevance and potential impact: UCCA is gaining traction as a cross-linguistic, grounded semantic layer. Providing an initial parser and evaluation setup is valuable to the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited strength of empirical validation and baselines: The main new model (BSP) performs modestly (≈59 F on primary edges; ≈13 F on remote), substantially below the conversion+LSTM baseline (≈69 F primary). The study relies on default settings for strong baselines (Malt, stack-LSTM) and cannot compare against strong DAG/SDP parsers (code “not available”). This leaves open whether the proposed innovation is actually competitive when baselines are properly tuned or when stronger DAG models are included. Given the skepticism towards unproven approaches, I am not convinced the new transitions and perceptron-based BSP deliver enough evidence of superiority.
- “Broad-coverage” claim undercut by remote edge performance: Remote edges are central to the BSS motivation, yet the BSP’s labeled F on remotes is ≈13% (with very low recall), and conversion-based methods necessarily score 0 on remotes. There is no phenomenon-level breakdown (e.g., coordination remotes, shared arguments) to demonstrate that the method meaningfully advances these cases. As reported, the approach does not yet compellingly solve the hallmark aspects of UCCA (multi-parent structures).
- Evaluation design and analysis are thin: The labeled-yield metric collapses to PARSEVAL for trees but may not sufficiently probe DAG-specific correctness (e.g., partial matching for remote subspans, penalties for spurious non-terminals). There is no sensitivity analysis of the label-priority order used in conversion, nor of the Label(u) heuristic in inverse conversion, although the dependency conversion upper bound shows substantial information loss (LF 88.4). The paper concludes conversions are “effective” but does not analyze which structures are lost or distorted, weakening the conclusions.
- Training/inference choices limit the demonstration: BSP uses greedy decoding with a linear perceptron while baselines include beam search (UPARSE) and a neural stack-LSTM. The authors themselves note that replacing the classifier could help; however, the current evidence does not isolate the contribution of the transition system from the weaker learning/decoding regime. Without showing beam/neural BSP or more competitive tuning of baselines, it’s hard to attribute observed differences to modeling rather than learning/search choices.
- Dataset scope and simplifications: Inter-sentential linkages and implicit units are dropped, reducing the challenge of UCCA’s full layer. The best primary LF (69.2) after conversion remains well below UCCA’s reported IAA (80–85). There is no significance testing, no variance reports, and minimal error analysis, so it’s difficult to assess robustness.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Please provide a fine-grained breakdown of performance by phenomenon: discontinuous units, coordination, multi-word expressions, and especially remote edges by label type. Which remote structures are recovered, and which are missed?
- How is the label-priority order over L (used to define h(·) in the constituency→dependency conversion) determined? Is it hand-crafted or learned? How sensitive are results to this order?
- How sensitive are the results to the Label(u) heuristic in inverse conversion? Can you report alternative heuristics and their impact on the upper bound and on end-to-end results?
- Did you attempt beam search or a neural local classifier for BSP? If so, what is the delta on primary and remote edges? If not, can you justify the choice of greedy perceptron given the comparisons to strong neural baselines?
- Can you report significance tests and/or multiple runs (with different random seeds) for BSP and the LSTM baseline? Also, which POS tagger and preprocessing pipeline were used?

Additional comments
- The claim “first parser that supports multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities” is plausible within grounded semantic parsing, but please contextualize against prior discontinuous constituency/DAG work more explicitly, and clarify the novelty in transitions vs. prior DAG parsers.
- Releasing code and converters is essential for impact; if possible, include an anonymized link for the rebuttal.
- An expanded error analysis (particularly for remote edges and discontinuities) would substantially strengthen the paper’s diagnostic value.

Recommendation: borderline reject. The problem is important and the paper is well-written, but the empirical support for the new parser is not yet compelling relative to established, well-tuned baselines, and the key phenomena (multi-parent structures) remain weakly handled. A stronger evaluation (tuned baselines, beam/neural BSP, phenomenon-level analysis) could change this assessment.
