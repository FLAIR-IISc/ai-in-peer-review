Summary
The paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing for grounded representations that require (a) multiple parents (DAGs), (b) non-terminal units, and (c) discontinuous units. Using UCCA as the target formalism, the authors propose two complementary strategies: (1) a conversion-based approach that maps UCCA graphs to (discontinuous) constituency trees or dependency trees, trains existing parsers, and maps outputs back; and (2) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three structural properties via an extended transition system (including remote edges and SWAP for discontinuity), with a perceptron classifier trained with a dynamic oracle. They define an evaluation measure for DAGs based on labeled yields, run in-domain and out-of-domain experiments on UCCA corpora, and provide ablations. Conversion plus a neural dependency parser yields the best primary-edge F-score (~69.2), while the direct BSP achieves ~59 F on primary edges and modest performance on remote edges; conversion approaches cannot predict remote edges.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A transition-based parser (BSP) that directly parses grounded semantic DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, introducing remote-edge transitions and features. This appears to be the first parser to support the full combination of these properties for UCCA-like structures.

2) Practical conversion protocols between UCCA DAGs and (discontinuous) constituency and dependency trees, including inverse mappings and quantified upper bounds, enabling reuse of existing parsers and showing competitive performance despite lossy conversion.

3) Task setup for UCCA parsing with evaluation metrics for labeled DAGs, an ablation study, and out-of-domain evaluation; minor technical additions include a ratio regularizer feature.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and formalization: The paper crisply articulates why grounded semantic parsing needs multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, and why existing representations/parsers fall short. UCCA is a suitable testbed, with clear examples and formal graph definitions.

- Technically sound transition system for BSS: The transition set extends standard shift-reduce parsing with NODE, SWAP, and explicit primary/remote EDGE transitions without auto-reduce, which correctly enables multiple parents and discontinuities. Constraints on primary parents and use of SWAP mirror prior, well-understood techniques, adapted coherently to semantic DAGs.

- Comprehensive, reproducible conversion baselines: The paper gives explicit forward and inverse algorithms (including pseudo-code) for constituency↔dependency conversions, a clear evaluation protocol with upper bounds, and shows that a strong existing dependency parser (LSTM) performs best on primary edges after conversion. This provides a meaningful baseline and indicates what information is preserved/lost.

- Sensible evaluation for DAGs and analysis: The labeled-yield metric generalizes PARSEVAL to DAGs and isolates remote-vs-primary edges. Reporting both in-domain and out-of-domain results and a feature ablation (showing, e.g., the importance of n-grams and the ratio regularizer) contributes to clarity and diagnostic value.

- Resource impact and baselines for a new task: First systematic results on UCCA parsing, code release promise, and empirical signals (e.g., 69.2 F primary F-score via conversion) establish a baseline for future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited direct performance and remote edges: The direct BSP underperforms conversion with a strong neural dependency parser on primary edges (59 vs 69.2 F). Remote-edge prediction, while a key novelty, remains low (e.g., 13.3 F in-domain, 13.6 F OOD), limiting practical gains from direct DAG parsing. No detailed error analysis of remote edges is provided.

- Incomplete comparison to relevant baselines: There is no empirical comparison to SDP/DAG parsers from SemEval (even if code was unavailable), nor to more recent neural transition systems on constituency parsing. The strongest baseline (stack-LSTM) is only used post-conversion; BSP itself uses a perceptron and greedy inference, which may understate the potential of the direct approach.

- Reproducibility gaps in conversion details: The dependency conversion relies on an unspecified priority order over labels and a rule-based Label(.) function, with details deferred to supplementary material. The inverse conversion assumes high attachment heuristics. As these choices critically affect both upper bounds and results, more explicit specification in the main text would improve clarity and reproducibility.

- Oracle and learning details not fully formalized: While a dynamic oracle is mentioned, the definition for the DAG+discontinuity+primary/remote setting is sketched informally. Given the complexity of constraints (single primary parent, remote edges, SWAP decisions), a more precise specification or proof of oracle optimality/reachability would strengthen technical soundness.

- Missing architectural variants: The paper does not evaluate beam search or neural classifiers within BSP, despite showing that classifier choice is decisive in the conversion setting (LSTM vs Malt). This weakens the claim that transition-based BSS parsing is competitive in practice, beyond the proof-of-concept.

Questions to authors (optional)
- What is the exact priority order over UCCA labels used in the dependency conversion, and how sensitive are results to this order? Can you quantify its impact?

- Please detail the Label(.) heuristic used in the inverse conversion. How often does it choose Center vs other labels, and how does this affect the upper bound gap?

- Can you provide a formal definition of the dynamic oracle for BSP, including how it handles remote-edge creation and the single-primary-parent constraint? Is the oracle optimal under your transition system?

- What proportion of edges in the datasets are remote, and how does BSP performance vary by remote label/type? Any common error patterns?

- Did you try beam search or a neural classifier within BSP? If so, what were the outcomes; if not, what are the expected engineering challenges?

- What are the parsing speeds for BSP and conversion pipelines, and how do they scale with sentence length/discontinuity rate?

Overall, the paper is clear and technically sound as a first step toward broad-coverage grounded semantic DAG parsing, with credible novelty in the direct transition system and valuable conversion baselines. The main limitations are empirical (direct system underperformance and weak remote handling) and in comparative/implementation details. The work establishes a useful task and baseline and suggests concrete, feasible paths to improvement (neural classifiers, beam), making it a meaningful contribution.
