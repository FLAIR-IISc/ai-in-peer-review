Summary and contributions
This paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing for grounded representations that require three structural properties simultaneously: reentrancies (multiple parents), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. Using UCCA as the testbed, the authors propose two strategies: (1) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA graphs to (discontinuous) constituency or dependency trees, parses with existing parsers, and converts back; and (2) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly builds UCCA-style DAGs with discontinuities, adding transitions for non-terminals and remote edges and training with a dynamic oracle.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Problem formulation and first practical treatment of grounded broad-coverage semantic parsing that jointly supports reentrancies, non-terminals, and discontinuities; including a concrete direct parser and evaluation setup on UCCA. This is the main novelty.
2) Simple, explicit conversion protocols between UCCA graphs and constituency/dependency formats, with quantification of information loss via upper bounds; these enable leveraging strong existing parsers as baselines.
3) Empirical findings: competitive primary-edge F-scores via conversion (69.2% with LSTM dependency parser) and a direct transition-based model that additionally predicts remote edges (albeit at low F), with ablations and out-of-domain evaluation.

Strengths
- Novel and relevant task definition with grounded semantics: Supporting all three properties in a single parser for a grounded scheme is non-trivial and not met by prior grounded systems. This fills a concrete gap between semantic dependency parsing (no non-terminals) and non-grounded AMR parsing (alignment problem). The choice of UCCA provides a linguistically motivated, cross-lingual target with existing corpora.

- Sound methodology with dual strategy and clear upper bounds: The conversion-based route is well-justified to gauge how far current technology can go; the paper carefully defines forward and inverse conversions and reports upper bounds (e.g., 88.4 F for dependency conversion), contextualizing achieved numbers. This makes the empirical comparisons meaningful and transparent.

- Direct parser design grounded in prior art, extended to UCCA needs: BSP extends discontinuous constituency and DAG dependency parsing with NODE and remote-edge transitions, SWAP for discontinuities, and a dynamic oracle. The inclusion of UCCA-specific “remote” transitions and features is a principled way to model reentrancies.

- Comprehensive evaluation including OOD and ablation: Results are reported in-domain and out-of-domain (20K Leagues), showing robustness (primary F within ~2 points). The ablation shows which feature groups matter (e.g., unigrams/bigrams, the novel ratio feature improving recall), supporting the design choices.

- Reproducibility: The conversion algorithms are explicitly specified; transition set, features, training regimen (perceptron with MINUPDATE, IMPORTANCE), and evaluation metric are described. The commitment to release code/converters further strengthens impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited absolute performance on the defining property (reentrancies): While BSP can predict remote edges, the reported labeled F on remote edges is low (13.3 in-domain; 13.6 OOD). This undercuts the practical benefit of “supporting multiple parents.” The paper should analyze causes (data sparsity? feature design? search) and provide targeted error analysis or techniques (e.g., global training, neural features, structured loss) to improve this crucial aspect.

- Fairness and completeness of baselines: The strongest primary-edge result (69.2 F) comes from a neural LSTM dependency parser after lossy conversion, whereas BSP is trained with a linear perceptron and greedy inference. This obscures whether the gap is due to model class or problem formulation. A stronger direct baseline (e.g., BSP with a neural classifier and/or beam search) would better isolate the value of the transition system. Conversely, some state-of-the-art DAG parsers (e.g., SDP systems) are not compared even via conversion, though the authors note code availability constraints; nevertheless, this leaves an open question about competitiveness.

- Evaluation metric may not fully capture structural quality: The yield-based labeled edge matching collapses height/attachment distinctions within the same yield. For BSS with many non-terminals and reentrancies, this can overestimate structural correctness (e.g., correct span but wrong parent unit). This is partly acknowledged (it reduces to PARSEVAL), but a complementary metric (e.g., node/edge precision at the graph level, remote edge attachment accuracy, or fine-grained category-wise scores) would provide a more nuanced picture. Error analysis is minimal.

- Conversions are simplistic in places and may bias outcomes: The dependency inverse conversion uses a heuristic Label(u) and always high attachment, losing structural distinctions; this yields a relatively low upper bound (88.4 F), and success of the LSTM baseline hinges on this pipeline. The constituency conversion trivially reaches 100 F on primary edges by definition (dropping remotes), making it less informative. More sophisticated conversion (e.g., learned head rules or neural reconstruction of non-terminals) could strengthen conclusions.

- Search/training choices limit the direct model: BSP uses greedy decoding without beam and a linear model, despite discontinuities and reentrancies making the search space complex. Prior work on discontinuous parsing often benefits from beam/global training. The paper itself suggests an LSTM classifier could help; its absence weakens the empirical case for BSP beyond proof-of-concept.

Questions to authors
- What proportion of edges in the datasets are remote? Please report remote edge prevalence and per-category remote performance. Are low remote F-scores driven by very low recall or precision?

- Did you try beam search or global training for BSP? If so, what was the trade-off in speed versus accuracy, particularly for remote edges and discontinuities?

- Can you provide an error analysis highlighting the dominant failure modes (e.g., coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, reentrancy types)? Which UCCA categories are hardest?

- How sensitive are the dependency inverse-conversion results to the Label(u) heuristic? Could learned labeling improve the 88.4 upper bound and downstream F?

- There is a likely typo in the split description (“passages 701–695”). Please clarify the in-domain test split and whether any passages overlap across splits.

Overall: This is a timely and well-motivated paper with clear technical contributions and careful experimental framing. Despite modest absolute accuracy on reentrancies, it opens an important line of work. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying evaluation and strengthening discussion of remote edge performance.
