Summary and main contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage, grounded semantic parsing with three core structural properties: multiple parents (reentrancy), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuity. The authors (i) introduce a transition-based parser (BSP) designed to produce UCCA-style DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including explicit transitions for remote (reentrant) edges; (ii) propose conversion protocols between UCCA DAGs and both discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees to reuse existing parsers; and (iii) define a simple, yield+label evaluation for BSS, reporting in-domain and out-of-domain results on English UCCA corpora.

In my view, the primary contributions are:
1) A transition system for grounded semantic DAG parsing that jointly supports non-terminals, discontinuities, and reentrancy, with a dynamic oracle and tailored features.
2) Practical conversion pipelines to leverage off-the-shelf constituency/dependency parsers for UCCA, with explicit upper bounds quantifying information loss.
3) An empirical study showing that a strong neural dependency parser plus lossy conversion can outperform a direct symbolic BSP on primary (non-remote) edges, while BSP uniquely predicts remote edges.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation. The paper articulates why grounded broad-coverage semantics needs all three properties and situates UCCA as a testbed where these properties are required and annotator-agreement is high.
- Novel transition system with explicit primary vs. remote edge creation and SWAP for discontinuities. The separation of primary and remote edges and the decision not to auto-REDUCE to permit multiple parents are well-motivated design choices; incorporating a dynamic oracle is also appropriate.
- Careful comparison via conversion upper bounds. The proposed conversions are simple and transparent, and reporting upper bounds (e.g., 88.4 F1 for dependency conversion on primary edges) helps interpret downstream numbers and the representation loss due to removing non-terminals and remote edges.
- Empirical breadth and robustness. The paper evaluates multiple families of baselines (discontinuous constituency parsing, transition-based dependency parsing including an LSTM stack parser) and shows stable out-of-domain behavior on 20K Leagues. The result that the stack-LSTM dependency parser reaches 69.2 F1 on primary edges highlights the potential of stronger classifiers for BSP.
- Evaluation design is sensible and representation-aware. The yield+label matching generalizes PARSEVAL to DAGs with non-terminals, and separating primary and remote edges makes explicit the current strengths and limitations of different approaches.

Weaknesses
- Limited performance and analysis on reentrancy. BSP’s remote edge F1 is low (13.3 in-domain; 13.6 out-of-domain), and there is little qualitative error analysis. Given that reentrancy is a headline property motivating BSS, more insight into what remote edges are recovered (e.g., shared participants across Scenes, control, coref-like links) and where errors arise would strengthen the contribution.
- Training/inference are dated and underpowered for the task. BSP uses a greedy averaged perceptron with sparse features and no beam, while the best conversion-based model uses a neural stack LSTM. This confounds conclusions: is the main bottleneck the transition system or the local classifier? Without a neural classifier (e.g., stack-LSTM/biLSTM features) or at least beam/global training for BSP, it is hard to assess the true potential of the direct approach.
- Evaluation incompleteness and comparability concerns. The primary and remote scores are reported separately, but there is no unified score reflecting the full DAG quality. The best-performing conversion pipelines get 0 for remotes by construction; an aggregate measure (e.g., micro-averaged over all edges, or a weighted composite) would contextualize the trade-off. Unlabeled F1 and per-phenomenon breakdowns (coordination, MWEs, PPs, phrasal verbs) are also missing.
- Conversion design choices are rather heuristic on the inverse direction. For dependency-to-constituency, high-attachment heuristics and Label(·) defaulting to Center are strong assumptions; it would help to quantify their impact (ablate high vs. low attach, error types introduced) beyond the single upper bound. Likewise, the choice and linear order over labels that drive head selection deserve sensitivity analysis.
- Experimental details and clarity issues. The split description appears to contain a typo (“701–695” for test). Hyperparameters for baselines are mostly “default,” and no significance testing is reported. Some references are incomplete/misformatted. The feature ablation is mentioned but not fully detailed in the provided text. The decision to remove linkage edges is only briefly justified; it reduces task scope and may overstate general BSS coverage.

Questions to the authors
- Can you report an aggregate DAG score that combines primary and remote edges (and optionally unlabeled), so that conversion-based systems can be compared fairly to BSP on the full task?
- What types of remote edges does BSP recover? Please provide a breakdown by UCCA category and common error patterns.
- How crucial is the dynamic oracle? Do you have results with/without it, or with beam search? Any attempt to plug in a neural classifier for BSP?
- How sensitive are the conversion results to the label priority order and to the “always high-attach” heuristic in the inverse conversion?
- Please clarify the train/dev/test split indices and confirm that no document-level leakage occurs when sentence boundaries are used.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Strongly consider a neuralized BSP (biLSTM features or a stack-LSTM controller) to close the gap with the conversion+LSTM baseline; this seems low-hanging fruit given your findings.
- Provide an error analysis focused on the three hallmark properties (discontinuity, non-terminals, reentrancy) to diagnose where each approach wins/loses.
- Report speed/complexity and memory footprint; transition systems for discontinuous DAGs can have larger search spaces, which matters for practical adoption.
- Release code and converters as stated; this would be valuable for the community exploring UCCA and cross-representation parsing.

Overall, this paper addresses an important and underexplored task and makes a credible first attempt with a principled transition system and useful conversion baselines. The main limitations are the relatively weak learning setup for BSP and limited analysis of reentrancy. With stronger modeling and deeper error analysis, this work could have substantial impact. I lean toward acceptance if improvements to experimental clarity and evaluation can be addressed in the final version.
