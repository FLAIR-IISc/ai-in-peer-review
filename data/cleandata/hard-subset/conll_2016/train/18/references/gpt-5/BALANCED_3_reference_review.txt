Summary and contributions
This paper addresses broad-coverage, grounded semantic parsing with three key structural properties: multiple parents, non-terminal units, and discontinuities. It targets UCCA as the only existing grounded scheme with all three and presents two approaches: (i) conversion of UCCA graphs into more restrictive formalisms (dependencies and discontinuous constituency) to leverage existing parsers, and (ii) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly builds DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities. Experiments on English UCCA (in-domain and out-of-domain) show that a dependency parser with an LSTM classifier achieves the best primary-edge F1 after conversion (69.2), while BSP is the only method predicting remote edges (13.3 F1).

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A transition system and implementation (BSP) for grounded semantic DAG parsing that jointly supports multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, including dynamic oracle training and UCCA-specific features.
2) A practical conversion pipeline between UCCA and (discontinuous) constituency and CoNLL-style dependency trees, together with empirical evidence that modern dependency parsers are strong baselines for primary edges after conversion.
3) An evaluation framework for BSS/UCCA graphs (yield-based labeled precision/recall with and without remotes) and feature analysis (including a useful “ratio” regularization feature).

Strengths
- Problem significance and scope: The paper tackles a timely and under-served setting—grounded semantic parsing with the three structural properties. UCCA is a sensible testbed; the work has potential broader impact for semantic parsing tasks that need shared arguments, MWEs, and discontinuities.
- Novel transition system that can predict remote edges: BSP supports multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities in a single framework and is the only evaluated system that predicts remotes. The transition inventory (including NODE, REMOTE edges, SWAP) is clear and well motivated by prior work.
- Comprehensive empirical study with informative baselines: The conversion-based analysis is valuable. It establishes realistic upper bounds and shows that strong dependency models (stack-LSTM) materially outperform traditional classifiers on primary edges after conversion, suggesting a clear path to strengthen BSP via neural classifiers.
- Cross-domain evaluation and ablation: The out-of-domain test shows robustness, and the feature ablation highlights which elements matter (unigrams/bigrams and the ratio feature). This increases confidence in the findings and provides practical guidance.
- Clarity of motivation and connection to prior work: The paper situates itself well within SDP, AMR, discontinuous constituency, and dependency DAG parsing, and makes a compelling case for grounding and non-terminals (e.g., coordination).

Weaknesses
- Empirical advantage of direct parsing is not yet demonstrated: The best primary-edge F1 is achieved by the conversion-based LSTM dependency parser (69.2), while BSP lags behind on primary edges (~59–60 F1) and achieves low remote-edge F1 (~13). As a result, the key claim—direct BSS parsing—does not yet translate into superior overall accuracy or a tangible downstream advantage.
- Limited baseline coverage and fairness: Conversion baselines cannot predict remotes by construction; stronger DAG parsers or multi-root/multi-head baselines are absent (“could not obtain code”), and no attempt is made to extend the conversion pipeline to support remotes (e.g., via post-hoc reattachment or multi-graph decoding). This makes it hard to assess whether BSP’s remote-edge gains are due to modeling or baseline limitations.
- Technical gaps and clarity: Details on ensuring acyclicity and well-formedness (especially with REMOTE edges) are sparse; Figure 2’s constraints are underspecified (e.g., how cycles are prevented for remote edges). The dynamic oracle is described informally without proof of optimality/completeness for the full transition set with SWAP and REMOTEs. Time/space complexity and runtime are not reported.
- Conversion and evaluation limitations: The dependency-to-constituency inverse conversion is simplistic (always high-attach; Label mostly Center), likely inflating the gap between conversion upper bound (88.4) and achieved scores. There is no fine-grained error analysis (by label, construction type, or discontinuity), no statistics on the frequency of remote edges, and no significance testing of improvements.
- Reproducibility and missing setup details: It is unclear which tokenizer/POS tagger is used, whether pretrained embeddings are used anywhere, exact dataset sizes for splits, and how punctuation is handled in preprocessing. Code is promised but not available at review time.

Questions to authors
- How do you enforce DAG acyclicity with REMOTE edge creation? Are there explicit cycle checks or invariants beyond forbidding a second primary parent?
- What is the distribution and label-wise breakdown of remote edges in the corpora? How does a simple heuristic baseline perform on remote edges (e.g., attach shared participants to nearest predicates)?
- What POS tagging and tokenization were used? Are features lowercased or normalized? Any pretrained embeddings?
- Can you report significance tests and per-label F1 (especially for P/A/R and coordination)?
- Did you try beam search or global training for BSP? What is the effect on primary and remote edges?
- Could you strengthen the inverse conversion (e.g., learned attachment height, more nuanced Label rules) and report how much of the conversion upper bound gap is algorithmic vs model?

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace the perceptron classifier in BSP with a neural stack-LSTM/bi-LSTM-based classifier; add beam search and global training. Your own results with the LSTM dependency parser strongly suggest substantial gains.
- Provide formalization and proofs (or at least clear algorithms) for the dynamic oracle with SWAP and REMOTE, and for cycle prevention; report complexity and runtime.
- Strengthen conversion baselines: attempt a DAG-capable dependency parser or a post-conversion module for remotes; improve inverse conversion with learned attachment height and richer label prediction.
- Add detailed error analysis by category (Scenes, coordination, MWEs, P/A roles), (dis)continuity, and remote labels; report remote-edge frequency and difficulty.
- Include significance testing and more thorough reporting of experimental settings; release code and conversion scripts upon camera-ready.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper tackles an important problem with a thoughtful formulation and provides useful baselines and a first direct parser. However, the main technical contribution (BSP) underperforms strong conversion-based baselines on primary edges, and remote-edge performance remains low. With clearer technical guarantees, stronger classifiers/decoding, and improved baselines and analysis, this work could be a solid contribution.
