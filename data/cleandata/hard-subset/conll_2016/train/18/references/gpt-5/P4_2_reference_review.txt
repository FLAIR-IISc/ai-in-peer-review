Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing for grounded representations that require (i) multiple parents, (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuous units. Using UCCA as the target formalism, the authors (a) define a parsing setting (BSS) supporting these three properties, (b) propose two strategies: lossy conversions from UCCA to (dis)continuous constituency and dependency trees to leverage existing parsers, and (c) introduce a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly builds UCCA-style DAGs with discontinuities and remote edges. Experiments on UCCA English (in-domain and out-of-domain) compare conversion-based baselines (Malt, LSTM stack-based) and the proposed BSP. The best primary-edge F-score is 69.2 (LSTM via conversion); BSP achieves 59.0 on primary edges and 13.3 on remote edges. The paper also presents an evaluation metric for BSS, upper bounds for conversions, and feature ablation, including a “ratio” feature.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength
1) A direct, transition-based parser (BSP) for grounded semantic DAGs that simultaneously supports multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, with concrete transition inventory (including remote edges and SWAP) and a dynamic oracle. This is novel for UCCA and broadly for grounded semantics.

2) Conversion protocols between UCCA DAGs and (dis)continuous constituency and dependency trees, with corresponding inverse conversions and quantified upper bounds, enabling reuse of existing parsers and a principled comparison against the direct approach.

3) An empirical evaluation suite for UCCA parsing, including in-/out-of-domain tests, ablation of feature sets (highlighting the impact of unigrams/bigrams and the novel ratio feature), and analysis via conversion upper bounds.

Strengths
- Novel direct parsing capability for grounded semantic DAGs: The transition set extends established shift-reduce paradigms to handle both remote (multiple-parent) edges and discontinuities, while maintaining non-terminals. This fills a gap where existing grounded semantic parsers typically miss at least one of these properties.

- Methodological clarity and reproducibility: The conversion algorithms are specified (including priority-based head selection and inverse reconstructions). The BSP transition system and feature templates are clearly enumerated; training uses a dynamic oracle and standard averaged perceptron, and hyperparameters are reported. The paper promises public release of converters and parser.

- Rigorous comparative setup with upper bounds: By quantifying information loss due to conversion (e.g., 88.4 primary F upper bound for dependency conversion; 0 for remote edges), the paper contextualizes the achievable performance of conversion-based baselines and fairly positions BSP’s scope.

- Empirical breadth: Results include both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation without retuning, showing robustness (BSP primary F-scores degrade modestly). The feature ablation is informative: unigrams/bigrams dominate; the ratio feature substantially boosts primary-edge recall; discontinuity-related features contribute positively.

- Insight into future directions: The LSTM dependency baseline (69.2 primary F) far outperforms classical Malt, suggesting that replacing BSP’s classifier with an LSTM could yield immediate gains. This provides a concrete path for improvement grounded in evidence.

Weaknesses
- Performance of the direct parser lags strong baselines on primary edges: BSP’s 59.0 primary F is substantially lower than the best conversion-based result (69.2). While BSP uniquely predicts some remote edges (13.3 F), overall accuracy on the core (primary) structure is behind the strongest baseline.

- Limited comparison to other DAG-capable parsers: The paper notes unavailability of code for DAG parsers, but the absence of comparisons to alternative graph-based or transition-based DAG methods (even if reimplemented or via reported numbers) weakens the empirical positioning of BSP.

- Evaluation scope and analysis: The yield-based metric is reasonable and reduces to PARSEVAL for trees, but deeper analysis is limited. There is no detailed error analysis by phenomenon (e.g., coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, long-distance dependencies) or by edge type, making it hard to assess where BSP’s design helps/hurts most. No statistical significance testing is reported.

- Training/inference choices likely limit performance: BSP uses a linear perceptron with greedy decoding; no beam or neural features are employed, despite evidence that an LSTM local classifier substantially improves results for conversion-based parsing. The dynamic oracle is described at a high level with no formal proof of optimality/reachability for the full transition set (including remote edges and SWAP), leaving technical soundness partially unsubstantiated.

- Dataset and preprocessing constraints: Inter-sentential edges and linkers are removed; this simplifies the task but also reduces coverage of UCCA relations. Only English is evaluated, despite UCCA’s cross-linguistic motivation. Details on dataset sizes and distributional properties are limited in the presented text.

Questions to authors
- Dynamic oracle: What are the formal reachability guarantees of the oracle given the transition system, especially with remote edges and SWAP? Can you provide a proof sketch or empirical oracle optimality rate?

- Cycle prevention: What constraints ensure the constructed graph is acyclic? Are cycle checks enforced during EDGE/REMOTE transitions?

- Conversion details: How is the priority order over edge labels (for head selection) determined? How sensitive are results to this ordering? For the inverse dependency-to-constituency conversion, can you quantify errors attributable to the Label() heuristic?

- BSP classifier choices: Did you try beam search or neural features (e.g., BiLSTM encoders) within BSP? If not, what prevented this, and do you foresee any complications due to discontinuities/remote edges?

- Phenomenon-level performance: Can you report F-scores by major UCCA categories (e.g., coordination, relators, linkers, MWEs) and by continuity/discontinuity to pinpoint where BSP gains over conversion-based approaches?

- Remote edges: What proportion of gold edges are remote, and how does BSP’s recall vary by remote edge type? Are there features or transitions that especially drive remote-edge prediction?

Overall, this is a clear and technically grounded paper introducing a novel direct parser for grounded semantic DAGs and providing useful conversion baselines and tooling for UCCA. While the direct method currently trails a strong conversion-based neural baseline on primary edges, it uniquely addresses remote edges and lays a solid foundation for future improvements with stronger classifiers.
