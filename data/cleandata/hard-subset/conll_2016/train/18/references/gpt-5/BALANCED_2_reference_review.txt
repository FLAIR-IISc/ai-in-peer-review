Summary
This paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing for grounded representations that require (i) multiple parents, (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuities. The authors focus on UCCA as the only existing scheme that natively supports all three properties, and propose two approaches: (a) conversion-based parsing by mapping UCCA to constituency or dependency trees, using existing parsers and back-converting; and (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly builds DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities. On English UCCA data, the strongest primary-edge F1 is achieved by the conversion-to-dependencies plus a stack-LSTM parser (69.2 F1), while BSP achieves 59.0 F1 on primary edges and 13.3 F1 on remote edges (which conversion methods cannot predict). Out-of-domain results are similar. The paper also defines evaluation metrics over labeled edge yields and presents feature ablations for BSP.

Main contributions (as I see them; ordered by significance)
1) A novel transition system (BSP) for grounded semantic DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including specialized transitions for remote edges; accompanied by a dynamic-oracle training regime and UCCA-oriented features.
2) A conversion-based evaluation of UCCA parsing via constituency and dependency formalisms, including explicit conversion procedures and upper bounds quantifying the loss from conversion.
3) The first empirical study (to my knowledge) of UCCA parsing at sentence level, including in- and out-of-domain results and a simple, consistent evaluation protocol.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and relevance: The paper motivates why broad-coverage grounded semantics needs all three properties (multi-parent, non-terminals, discontinuity), and positions UCCA well among related formalisms (SDP, AMR). This framing is timely and important.
- Technically coherent transition system: BSP integrates established ideas (SHIFT/REDUCE, NODE, SWAP) with new remote-edge transitions and constraints for UCCA’s primary vs. remote edges. The design is sensible and extendable, and the dynamic oracle is appropriate.
- Thorough conversion baselines with quantified upper bounds: The conversions are described algorithmically, and the back-and-forth upper bounds make the trade-offs transparent (e.g., perfect primary score for constituency conversion; substantial loss for dependency conversion). This helps interpret where information is lost.
- Solid experimental protocol and robustness: In-domain and out-of-domain results are both reported; feature ablations illuminate what matters (e.g., unigram/bigram features, the ratio regularizer, and discontinuity features).
- Practical insights for future work: The stark gap between Malt and the stack-LSTM parser under identical conversions highlights that stronger neural local classifiers are likely to substantially improve BSP. This is a useful, actionable takeaway.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical strength of the direct parser: BSP’s primary-edge F1 (59) lags far behind the best conversion baseline (69.2), and its remote-edge F1 (≈13) is still very low. As the central contribution, BSP would benefit from stronger decoding (beam), better features (neural), or global training to close this gap.
- Restricted comparisons and missing baselines: The evaluation omits comparisons against other graph parsers for semantic structures (e.g., SDP parsers or recent DAG parsers), even via additional conversions. This leaves open whether BSP is competitive beyond the chosen baselines.
- Simplistic inverse conversion to non-terminals: The dependency-to-constituency inverse conversion relies on heuristic attachment (always high) and a rule-based Label() that “mostly selects Center.” This weakens the dependency path and may understate what is possible with learned head rules or more principled reconstruction (as in Kong et al., 2015).
- Evaluation metric limitations and analysis: The yield-based labeled matching abstracts away from attachment height and internal topology (beyond labels), potentially inflating scores for some structures and obscuring common error patterns (e.g., coordination vs. PP-attachment). The paper would benefit from a breakdown by phenomenon (coordination, MWEs, scenes, remotes).
- Scope and generality: Experiments are English-only and exclude linkage and cross-sentence edges, with no exploration of languages with more discontinuity (e.g., German) or richer remote structures. Given the cross-linguistic motivation, broader evidence would strengthen the case.

Questions to Authors
1) Can you report an error analysis by phenomenon (e.g., coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, PP modifiers, scenes) for BSP vs. conversion baselines? Where does BSP help/hurt most?
2) What fraction of edges are remote in the datasets, and how does the 13 F1 on remotes translate to overall structure quality? Are there common patterns of false positives (spurious remotes)?
3) How difficult would it be to replace BSP’s perceptron classifier with a stack-LSTM (or biaffine) encoder while keeping the same transition system? Any preliminary results?
4) For the inverse dependency conversion, did you try learned head rules or the method of Kong et al. (2015) to better reconstruct non-terminals? Could that close the gap to the upper bound?
5) How sensitive are results to beam search and global normalization for BSP? Did you attempt even small beams to mitigate greedy errors (especially for SWAP and remotes)?
6) Could you report unlabeled scores and primary vs. remote breakdown across sentence lengths? Is performance disproportionately poor on longer or more discontinuous sentences?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Clarify Label() and the priority ordering over labels; these choices affect the conversions and may encode UCCA-specific biases.
- Consider reporting inter-annotator agreement on the exact splits used, to contextualize scores per phenomenon.
- Minor: reference formatting is inconsistent; some citations have placeholders or typos.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is important and the paper is well-motivated and carefully executed. However, the main technical contribution (BSP) underperforms strong conversion baselines and lacks stronger decoding/classification to demonstrate clear advantages. With improved modeling (e.g., neural local classifier, beam/global training), richer comparisons, and deeper analysis of remote/discontinuous phenomena, this work would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
