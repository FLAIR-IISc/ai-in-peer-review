Overall evaluation against criteria
- Originality: Good. The paper is, to my knowledge, the first to present a transition-based parser that simultaneously supports multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities in a grounded semantic scheme (UCCA). The conversion protocols are less novel but useful.
- Soundness/correctness: Reasonably sound but some gaps. The transition system is well specified; training uses a dynamic oracle and perceptron. However, details/proofs for the dynamic oracle’s optimality in the DAG+discontinuity setting and for label-priority selection are not fully specified in the main text.
- Significance: Moderate to high. UCCA is a growing semantic framework; providing a practical parser and conversion pipelines could enable research and downstream applications. The direct parser’s ability to predict remote edges, albeit modestly, is an important capability not offered by conversion baselines.
- Empirical evaluation: Adequate but not comprehensive. Two corpora (in-domain and OOD), multiple baselines via conversion, upper bounds, and ablations are provided. However, analysis of error types, transition ablations, and stronger decoding/classification variants are missing.
- Clarity: Generally clear, with concrete algorithms and feature sets; some implementation details deferred to supplementary material make replication harder from the main paper alone.
- Reproducibility: Good. Uses public corpora; promises code/converters release; baselines are standard toolkits with default settings.

Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
1) A transition-based Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) for UCCA that natively supports multiple parents (remote edges), non-terminal units, and discontinuities (via SWAP), with a defined transition set, features (including a novel node-creation regularizer), and dynamic-oracle training.
2) Conversion-based pipelines between UCCA DAGs and both discontinuous constituency and dependency formats, including practical inverse conversions and quantification of the information loss (upper bounds), enabling use of mature parsers for this task.
3) An empirical study on English UCCA corpora (Wiki in-domain, 20K Leagues OOD), including a simple yet principled evaluation metric for BSS (labeled yield matching), demonstrating that conversion+strong dependency parsing achieves 69.2% primary F, while the direct parser achieves comparable primary F and uniquely recovers some remote edges.

Strengths
- First end-to-end parser for UCCA with full structural coverage: Prior semantic parsers or SDP formalisms typically lacked non-terminals or grounded spans; BSP directly targets these needs, demonstrating feasibility. This fills a gap for UCCA resources and can spur further research.
- Solid empirical framing with informative upper bounds: The conversion pipelines and their measured upper bounds (e.g., 88.4% primary F for dependency conversion) contextualize achievable performance and illuminate where information is lost (notably remote edges and non-terminals).
- Competitive baselines and a strong finding: The stack-LSTM dependency parser on converted data substantially outperforms Malt variants (69.2% vs ~60% primary F), highlighting that classifier choice matters and pointing to a clear path to improve BSP by adopting neural classifiers.
- Out-of-domain robustness and ablation: The OOD results are close to in-domain for primary edges, and feature ablations (e.g., the ratio feature improving recall) provide insight into what matters, which is valuable for reproducibility and future improvements.
- Practical evaluation design: The labeled-yield metric is simple, reduces to PARSEVAL for trees, and reasonably extends to DAGs and discontinuities; reporting primary vs remote edges separately makes the trade-offs explicit.

Weaknesses
- Direct model underperforms conversion baseline on primary edges and recovers few remotes: BSP’s primary F (59–60%) lags the best conversion approach by ~9–10 F points, and remote edge F is low (~13%). While predicting remotes is unique, the absolute performance is modest and lacks deeper analysis (e.g., how many remotes exist; baseline of predicting none).
- Modeling choices are dated and decoding is greedy: BSP uses a linear perceptron with hand-engineered features and greedy inference. Given the clear gains from LSTM classifiers in the conversion setting and beam search gains in related work, not trying neural scoring or beam search weakens the methodological contribution.
- Insufficient detail on key algorithmic components: The dynamic oracle for a transition system that includes DAG edges and SWAP is nontrivial; the paper does not provide enough detail to assess optimality or correctness. Similarly, the label-priority order for head selection and the Label(u) heuristic in inverse conversion are only sketched; their impact could be large.
- Limited analysis and diagnostics: The paper lacks error analysis (e.g., breakdown by construction types like coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs), ablations of transition inventory (e.g., impact of REMOTE and SWAP), learning curves, or statistical significance tests. This makes it harder to interpret where the system struggles and how to improve it.
- Scope constrained to English and a single layer: While understandable, the claims about cross-linguistic applicability (a key UCCA motivation) would be stronger with preliminary results on another language or at least cross-lingual transfer discussion. Implicit units/linkage are excluded, further narrowing coverage.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- How many remote edges are present in the datasets (proportion of edges/graphs), and what is the precision/recall separately for remote parents? How does BSP compare to a trivial baseline (predict no remote edges)?
- Please clarify the dynamic oracle: What is the loss function it optimizes, and can you guarantee that the gold graph remains reachable under the oracle’s suggested transitions for states reachable by arbitrary mistakes? Any empirical evidence (e.g., oracle accuracy)?
- How is the label-priority ordering for head selection determined (fixed, linguistically motivated, or tuned on dev)? How sensitive are dependency conversion results to this ordering?
- Did you try beam search or a neural local classifier for BSP? Even small-beam or MLP features might meaningfully reduce the gap to conversion+LSTM; any preliminary numbers?
- Can you provide statistics on discontinuities (percentage of nodes/edges requiring SWAP) and the observed effect of the SWAP transition (e.g., ablate SWAP)?

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. Despite moderate absolute performance and missing analysis, the paper provides the first parser with full structural coverage for UCCA, introduces useful conversion pipelines with quantified upper bounds, and presents a careful empirical comparison including OOD testing. The work is a valuable step toward broad-coverage grounded semantic parsing and should be of interest to the CoNLL community. Strengthening the algorithmic details and experiments (oracle, analysis, stronger classifiers/decoding) would further improve the paper.
