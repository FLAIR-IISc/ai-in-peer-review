Summary and main contributions
- The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing where representations must support (i) multiple parents (reentrancy), (ii) non-terminal units, and (iii) discontinuities. It frames this under “Broad-coverage Semantic Structures” (BSS) and uses UCCA as the grounded scheme.
- Contribution 1 (primary): A transition-based parser (BSP) that extends standard shift-reduce parsing with transitions to handle non-terminals, reentrancies (primary vs. remote edges), and discontinuities (SWAP), plus a dynamic oracle and a small set of novel features (including a global “ratio” regularizer).
- Contribution 2: Lossy conversion protocols between UCCA graphs and (i) discontinuous constituency trees and (ii) dependency trees, enabling the use of existing parsers; includes algorithms for conversion and inverse conversion.
- Contribution 3: An evaluation on English UCCA data (Wikipedia in-domain; 20K Leagues out-of-domain), reporting primary-edge F1 up to 69.2 via the dependency-conversion + LSTM classifier, and direct BSP results including remote-edge prediction.

Strengths
- Clearly motivated structural targets: The paper argues convincingly why a grounded semantic parser should support reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuity, and positions UCCA as a realistic testbed. This is a relevant and timely problem.
- Parser design that unifies three properties: The transition set cleanly composes known mechanisms (NODE, SWAP, remote vs. primary edge creation) into one system capable of producing DAGs with discontinuities and non-terminals. This integration is non-trivial and of potential use beyond UCCA.
- Conversion baselines and upper bounds: Providing both constituency- and dependency-based conversion pipelines with upper bounds helps calibrate how much information is lost, and illustrates the trade-offs of leveraging existing technology.
- Dynamic oracle and feature engineering: Use of a dynamic oracle and tailored features (including remote-specific features and a global ratio regularizer) shows technical care and yields measurable gains on dev in ablation (as claimed).
- Domain robustness check: Applying the same model to 20K Leagues without retuning and obtaining broadly similar primary F1 suggests some robustness across domains.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and reproducibility gaps in critical components:
  - The label-priority order for head determination in Algorithm 1 is unspecified, and the rule-based Label(u) function for inverse conversion is deferred to supplementary material. These two choices can strongly affect both conversion quality and reported scores; without details, results are not reproducible.
  - The SWAP precondition uses creation indices i(x) < i(y) without a proof or discussion of soundness/completeness for generating all required discontinuities under the proposed state design. Termination/acyclicity guarantees are not discussed.
  - The dynamic oracle is only sketched; edge cases with remote vs. primary constraints and multiple parents are not fully described.
- Evaluation design concerns:
  - The evaluation metric matches edges by yield and label only, ignoring node identity and attachment height. This can conflate structurally different analyses with identical yields, and can inflate or deflate scores in the presence of non-terminals and reentrancies. There is no sensitivity analysis or alternative metric (e.g., node/edge alignment-based measures).
  - No statistical significance testing or variance reporting; improvements (e.g., LSTM vs. Malt; BSP variants) are not substantiated statistically.
  - The ablation study is reported only on development data; the actual table is not present in the main text here and quantitative impacts are not fully shown.
- Comparisons and baselines:
  - The strongest primary-edge result (69.2 F1) comes from the dependency-conversion with an LSTM classifier, not from BSP; thus the main proposed method is not competitive with a conversion-based pipeline. The paper acknowledges but does not address this, e.g., by upgrading BSP’s classifier.
  - UPARSE uses beam search while BSP is greedy; this mismatch can confound comparisons. Conversely, the authors do not report beam experiments for BSP or speed/accuracy trade-offs.
  - The paper does not compare to other DAG parsers (even if code was unavailable, controlled re-implementations or alternative strong non-DAG baselines could help). No simple baselines (e.g., majority label or flat chunkers) are provided to contextualize absolute F1.
- Remote edges remain poorly handled: Remote-edge F1 is very low (≈13% in-domain; ≈14% OOD), with limited analysis of error types or ablations specific to reentrancy prediction. Since reentrancy is one of the central motivations, the current results undercut the main claim.
- Data and presentation issues:
  - Train/dev/test partition has an apparent typo: “passages 701– 695 as in-domain test set.” This casts doubt on the split. Dataset sizes per split are not reported.
  - The paper discards inter-sentential, linkage, and implicit nodes; while practical, the impact is not quantified beyond a single percentage for inter-sentence edges, and this narrows “broad coverage.”
  - Numerous formatting/citation errors throughout (e.g., missing years, “?”, stray LaTeX artifacts \Q2008\E, misspellings like “Transitionbased dependeny,” inconsistent hyphenation/spaces “broadcoverage,” “UCCAannotated,” misnumbered/inline footnotes). Figures are referenced but seem partially unavailable here. This reduces readability and polish.

Questions to Authors
- Please precisely specify:
  - The linear priority order over UCCA edge labels used in Algorithm 1, and how it was chosen.
  - The Label(u) function used in Algorithm 2 (full rules), and any tuning.
  - The dynamic-oracle formal definition for all transitions, especially when children already have primary parents and in the presence of remote edges.
- Can you provide theoretical guarantees (or counterexamples) on completeness/soundness of the transition system with respect to UCCA DAGs with discontinuities? Under what conditions does SWAP + NODE + REMOTE suffice?
- Why was BSP trained and decoded greedily? What happens with small beams, or with an LSTM/MLP classifier in BSP? Given the strong gains from LSTM in the conversion setup, this seems crucial.
- How sensitive are results to the yield-based evaluation choice? Can you report an alternative metric that accounts for node identity/attachment height, or at least an analysis of ambiguous-yield cases?
- Please correct and clarify the data splits (passage indices and sizes). Also report the impact of excluding linkers/implicit nodes on metrics.
- Runtime/memory: training and decoding speeds for BSP vs. conversion pipelines?

Overall, while the problem is important and the integration of transitions to support all three structural properties is interesting, the accumulation of methodological and presentation issues, limited reentrancy performance, and weaker results than a conversion pipeline make this submission below the bar in its current form.
