Summary and main contributions
- The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing for grounded representations that require (i) multiple parents, (ii) non-terminal units, and (iii) discontinuities. It uses UCCA as a concrete BSS instance.
- Two complementary strategies are proposed: (a) conversion-based parsing, mapping UCCA to (possibly discontinuous) constituency trees and to dependency trees to leverage existing parsers and then converting back; (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that natively supports DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities via an extended transition system, dynamic oracle training, and tailored features.
- The work introduces simple, label-driven tree↔dependency conversion algorithms, an evaluation protocol for BSS via yield- and label-matched edges with a separation of primary vs. remote edges, and reports in- and out-of-domain experiments on UCCA English corpora.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem and clear definition of representational targets. The paper articulates why bi-lexical dependencies or purely abstract (non-grounded) formalisms are insufficient for capturing coordination, MWE/discontinuity, and shared arguments, and frames UCCA as a demanding but practical testbed for grounded BSS parsing.
- Novel transition system for DAG-with-non-terminals and discontinuities. The BSP transition inventory (NODEX, LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE for primary, LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE for remotes, SWAP for gaps) is coherent and extends prior work on discontinuous constituency and dependency DAG parsing. The explicit separation of primary vs. remote edges is sensible for UCCA constraints.
- Dynamic-oracle, perceptron training with UCCA-oriented features. The feature design shows awareness of UCCA structure (e.g., counts of parents/children and remote children, head proxies via h* derived from labels, and the simple ratio regularizer to disincentivize gratuitous node creation). The oracle is task-appropriate and enables learning under spurious ambiguity.
- Complementary conversion baselines with upper bounds and a strong neural baseline. The label-priority constituent→dependency mapping (Algorithm 1) is elegant for UCCA (labels encode roles, obviating head percolation). Evaluating both UPARSE (discontinuous constituency) and LSTM arc-standard (Stack-LSTM) parsers provides a realistic picture; reporting upper bounds quantifies conversion loss (e.g., 88.4 F1 UB for dependency conversion).
- Empirical scope and robustness. Results cover in-domain Wikipedia and out-of-domain 20K Leagues without retuning; BSP’s primary F1 degrades modestly and retains some ability to predict remotes across domains. The study also quantifies that remote edges are currently not recovered by conversion methods (0 F1), whereas BSP does (albeit low F1), highlighting the value of direct modeling.

Weaknesses
- Primary-edge accuracy of the direct parser lags a strong conversion baseline. The Stack-LSTM via dependency conversion achieves 69.2 primary F1 vs. 59.0 for BSP (and 59.9 when disallowing remotes). While BSP is first to target all three phenomena jointly, the central modeling contribution underperforms a relatively straightforward conversion plus strong classifier. The paper argues that using an LSTM classifier in BSP could close the gap, but this remains speculative.
- Remote-edge performance remains very low. BSP’s remote F1 is 13.3 (in-domain) and 13.6 (OOD). The paper shows that conversion methods cannot predict remotes by design, but the absolute remote performance is low enough to question practical utility for the targeted multi-parent phenomenon. There is no detailed error analysis of remote-edge types, labels, or structural contexts that fail.
- Evaluation design and analysis are limited. The yield+label matching metric is reasonable and reduces to PARSEVAL on trees, but it collapses distinctions about attachment heights and may be insensitive to some structural errors in non-terminals. There is no per-label breakdown, no confusion analysis between role labels, no breakdown by phenomenon (coordination, phrasal verbs, PP attachment), and no quantification of remote/discontinuous prevalence, which hampers interpretability.
- Incomplete baselines and search/training settings. BSP uses greedy inference; related work often benefits from beam search or global normalization. The comparison to prior DAG parsers is limited because code was unavailable, but the paper could have implemented a competitive graph-based baseline or at least ablated the impact of beam size for UPARSE vs. BSP. The training choice (averaged perceptron) is dated relative to the neural baseline; this exacerbates the performance gap on primary edges.
- Conversion details and heuristics under-specified in the main text. Critical components (label priority order, the Label(u) function for constituency reconstruction) are relegated to supplementary material; their design can materially affect the upper bounds and empirical performance. The choice to remove linkage nodes and inter-sentential edges is practical, but the impact on label distribution and difficulty is not quantified.

Questions to authors
- What proportion of edges in your datasets are remote vs. primary, and what is the distribution across labels for remotes? Can you provide per-label F1 and a breakdown by construction (e.g., coordination, argument sharing, MWEs) to contextualize the 13% remote F1?
- How sensitive are your results to the label priority order used in Algorithm 1? Could you report UB and downstream performance under alternative priority schemes or learned priorities?
- Could you provide an ablation on search: does adding a small beam (e.g., 4–8) to BSP improve primary and especially remote edges? Similarly, how does global normalization (e.g., early updates with beam) affect results?
- Did you attempt to swap the perceptron with a neural classifier within BSP (e.g., bi-LSTM feature encoders over stack/buffer terminals)? If so, what performance did you see; if not, what are expected engineering hurdles?
- What is the runtime/complexity of BSP compared to UPARSE and the Stack-LSTM baseline (tokens/sec), and how does SWAP frequency impact parsing time?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide qualitative error examples for remote edges and discontinuities to guide future modeling.
- Report per-label precision/recall and phenomenon-level scores; this will clarify where BSP helps beyond conversion.
- Consider simple neural features (character/word embeddings, POS/lemma embeddings) and biaffine scoring of EDGE transitions; this may close the primary-edge gap and help remote decisions.
- Explore joint training with converted trees (multi-task or distillation) to transfer strengths of the LSTM baseline into BSP.
- Clarify the effect of removing linkage and implicit nodes on data statistics and evaluation.

Overall recommendation: a solid and timely paper that defines a challenging grounded semantic parsing target, proposes a principled transition system, and establishes informative baselines. Despite lower primary-edge accuracy than a strong conversion+neural baseline and limited remote-edge performance, the paper opens an important direction and provides tools and datasets. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening analysis and clarifying key design choices.
