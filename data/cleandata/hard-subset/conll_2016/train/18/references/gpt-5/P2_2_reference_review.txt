Summary and contributions
- The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing where outputs may have multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units (BSS), using UCCA as the grounded formalism. It proposes: (1) a transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs UCCA-style DAGs with discontinuities and remote edges; (2) lossy but principled conversions from UCCA DAGs to discontinuous constituency trees and dependency trees (and inverse conversions), enabling reuse of existing parsers as strong baselines; (3) an empirical study on UCCA corpora with in- and out-of-domain evaluation, a new evaluation metric for BSS (yield+label matching), ablations, and upper-bound analyses.
- In my view, the primary contribution is the transition system and training setup that explicitly handle the three BSS properties within a single parser. Secondary contributions are the conversion protocols with quantified information loss and the empirical evaluation that positions UCCA parsing within the broader parsing ecosystem.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated problem and representation choice. The paper convincingly argues the need for supporting multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities in grounded semantics. UCCA is an appropriate testbed because it natively exhibits the three properties and has demonstrated cross-linguistic applicability and semantic stability.
- Technically sound transition system for DAGs with discontinuities. The system extends established transition-based designs (SHIFT/REDUCE/NODE/SWAP) with primary vs. remote edge creation and constraints on primary in-degree, enabling multiple parents without collapsing to trees. The dynamic oracle is a strong choice for training; the inclusion of SWAP for discontinuity is standard and justified.
- Useful conversion baselines with quantified upper bounds. The constituency and dependency conversions are specified algorithmically, including a simpler head selection via edge labels and an inverse reconstruction heuristic with an explicit upper bound on recoverable structure. This makes comparisons interpretable; notably, the dependency conversion loses non-terminals, and the measured upper bound (LF≈88.4) contextualizes the downstream results.
- Thorough empirical setup for an emerging task. The paper reports in-domain and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) results, includes strong baselines (UPARSE, Malt, and an LSTM-based dependency parser), reports remote-edge scores separately, and provides feature ablations showing the contribution of unigram/bigram features and the novel ratio feature. The out-of-domain results are reasonably robust, and the best primary F1 (69.2) is close to but below UCCA IAA, giving a realistic sense of progress.
- Clarity and reproducibility. The transition set, features, and algorithms are clearly described. The evaluation metric is simple and reduces to PARSEVAL on trees. Promising to release code and converters increases impact.

Weaknesses
- Direct approach underperforms a strong conversion baseline on primary edges. The best primary F1 is achieved by the LSTM dependency parser on converted trees (69.2), compared to BSP at 59–60 on primary edges. This weakens the case that a direct BSS parser is currently advantageous for the core structure, despite its expressivity. The paper argues that BSP can predict remote edges, but remote F1 remains low (≈13), which limits the practical advantage at present.
- Limited modeling choices for BSP. BSP uses a greedy averaged perceptron with hand-crafted features and no beam search, despite citing neural LSTM parsing as highly effective. The paper itself notes that using an LSTM-style classifier could help. As a result, the main technical contribution is not evaluated with state-of-the-art learning, making it hard to assess the potential of the transition system independently of the classifier capacity. Including even small-beam or neural variants would strengthen the claim.
- Evaluation metrics and analysis are somewhat coarse. The yield+label metric is appropriate and connects to PARSEVAL, but it does not distinguish attachment height errors or structural choices beyond spans. Given the centrality of non-terminals and discontinuity, a more fine-grained error analysis (e.g., coordination, MWEs, discontinuous phrasal verbs, remote edges by label) would help diagnose where BSP succeeds or fails. Statistical significance and runtime/efficiency numbers are also absent.
- Baselines and comparisons constrained by tool availability. The paper notes inability to obtain code for DAG dependency parsers and thus does not compare to them. While understandable, this leaves a gap: BSP should be contrasted to a DAG parser (even a reimplementation or a simplified variant) to isolate the gains from supporting non-terminals versus purely bi-lexical DAGs. Similarly, the inverse conversion heuristic (Label(u)) is underspecified in the main text; some choices could influence results.
- Scope limitations of the dataset. The experiments discard linkage nodes, implicit units, and inter-sentence edges. Although the paper quantifies the proportion of inter-sentence edges (0.18%), the practical impact of ignoring linkage/implicit nodes is not analyzed. Reporting their frequency and potential upper bounds would clarify how far the current system is from full foundational-layer coverage.

Questions to authors
- What is the distribution and label breakdown of remote edges in the test sets, and how does BSP’s performance vary by label and construction (e.g., control/raising, argument sharing)?
- Have you tried adding a small beam or a neural classifier (e.g., MLP or LSTM over the same features) to BSP? Any preliminary numbers?
- Can you provide error analysis for discontinuous constructions and coordination? Where does BSP gain/lose versus the conversion baselines?
- What is the average number of transitions per sentence and runtime compared to UPARSE and the dependency baselines? How many SWAPs per sentence?
- How sensitive are the results to the inverse conversion heuristic (Label(u)) and to the priority order over UCCA labels?

Overall assessment
- Novelty: High for the direct parser covering all three BSS properties within a grounded scheme; moderate for the conversion protocols.
- Soundness: Good; algorithms and training are well specified and consistent with prior art.
- Clarity: Good; the paper is readable and concrete.
- Significance: Moderate. The topic is important, and the direct parser is a meaningful step, but primary-edge quality lags a strong conversion baseline and remote-edge performance is low. Still, given the first attempt at full BSS parsing for UCCA and the thorough evaluation, I lean toward borderline accept, contingent on strengthening the modeling and analysis.
