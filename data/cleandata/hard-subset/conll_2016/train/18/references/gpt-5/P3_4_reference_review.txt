Summary and main contributions
This paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing into UCCA graphs, which are grounded DAG structures that allow multiple parents (remote edges), non-terminal units, and discontinuities. The paper proposes two complementary strategies:
- Conversion-based parsing: lossy conversion of UCCA DAGs to (possibly discontinuous) constituency trees and to dependency trees, training existing parsers (UPARSE, Malt, Stack-LSTM), and converting outputs back to UCCA.
- A direct transition-based parser (BSP): a novel transition system that natively supports non-terminals, discontinuities (via SWAP), and multiple parents (via primary vs. remote edge transitions), trained with a dynamic oracle and a perceptron classifier.

Evaluation on UCCA English corpora (in-domain Wikipedia, out-of-domain “20K Leagues”) uses a labeled yield-based precision/recall metric (with/without remotes). The dependency conversion plus Stack-LSTM achieves 69.2 primary F1; BSP (greedy perceptron) achieves ~59–60 primary F1 and is the only approach to predict remote edges (F1 ≈ 13).

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A transition system and training oracle for UCCA/BSS parsing that simultaneously supports non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents.
2) A careful, reproducible conversion-based assessment (with upper bounds) that situates UCCA parsing within existing syntactic/semantic parsing technology and defines a practical evaluation protocol for UCCA graphs.
3) Empirical insights (feature ablations, in/out-of-domain results) and public release of converters and parsers.

Strengths
- Problem significance and scope: UCCA/BSS target a level of semantics that standard dependency and SRL schemes struggle with (coordination without clear heads, discontinuities, shared arguments). A method that directly handles all three structural properties is timely and broadly relevant for semantic parsing.
- Methodological clarity and principled design: The transition inventory is well-motivated (NODE for non-terminals, SWAP for gaps, primary vs. remote edge creation without forced REDUCE to allow multiple parents). The dynamic oracle is thoughtfully constructed to keep gold reachability and is appropriate for a greedy parser.
- Conversion methodology and baselines: The paper presents explicit constituency↔dependency conversion algorithms, including a simple but transparent head selection by label priority and an inverse reconstruction heuristic, and reports upper bounds quantifying information loss. This makes the comparison interpretable rather than opaque pipeline baselines.
- Empirical breadth: Results cover in-domain and out-of-domain settings; they separate primary from remote edges; they include multiple strong baselines (UPARSE, Malt arc-std/eager, Stack-LSTM), and they provide feature ablations. The cross-domain robustness (small drop) is encouraging.
- Practical insights: The finding that the Stack-LSTM (even through lossy conversion) outperforms Malt by a large margin suggests a clear path to improve BSP by upgrading the local classifier; the novel ratio feature provides a simple, portable regularizer for non-terminal creation.

Weaknesses
- Absolute performance and remote edges: BSP’s primary F1 (~59–60) is substantially below the best conversion baseline (69.2), and remote-edge F1 is very low (~13). Since remote edges are a central motivation for UCCA/BSS, this limits practical impact. The paper would benefit from targeted modeling for remotes (frequency, distance, types) and stronger architectural choices to raise recall.
- Baseline comparability and fairness: The conversion to dependencies necessarily loses non-terminals and remote edges; the inverse reconstruction (high-attachment heuristic, Label function mostly assigning Center) is simplistic. While upper bounds are reported, the comparison remains skewed: conversion baselines cannot produce remotes (zero by design), and their primary F1 is capped by reconstruction errors. A stronger constituency baseline (with learned inverse reconstruction or joint latent non-terminal induction) would strengthen claims.
- Modeling choices are dated/limited: BSP uses a greedy averaged perceptron without beam search or neural features, despite demonstrating that an LSTM classifier improves conversion baselines dramatically. Without trying at least beam search or a neural scoring model within BSP, it is hard to assess the transition system’s true potential.
- Evaluation scope and analysis: The proposed yield-based metric is reasonable, but the paper lacks a deeper breakdown by linguistic phenomena (coordination, MWEs, PPs, phrasal verbs, Scenes) and by structural properties (degree of discontinuity, number of parents). There is limited error analysis, no statistical significance testing, and no qualitative discussion of typical remote-edge errors.
- Missing or underspecified details: The priority order over edge labels (central to Algorithm 1) and the Label(·) rule set for inverse conversion are deferred to supplementary material; cycle-prevention and well-formedness constraints are not discussed; POS source (gold vs. predicted) is not specified. These affect reproducibility and interpretation of results.

Questions to authors
- Label priority and reconstruction: Please provide the exact label priority order used to define h(u) and the full Label(·) mapping for inverse conversion. How sensitive are the dependency conversion results to these choices?
- Data and inputs: Are POS tags gold or predicted? Do you use any pre-trained embeddings? What is the distribution of remotes and discontinuities (e.g., percentage of sentences with remotes; average discontinuity length)?
- Constraints: How do you enforce acyclicity and UCCA well-formedness during decoding, especially with remote edges and SWAP? Can the transition system generate invalid graphs?
- Search and modeling: Did you try beam search, cost-augmented training, or neural feature representations inside BSP? If so, what was the effect on primary and remote edges?
- Error breakdown: Which edge types (e.g., Center, Participant, Relator, Linker) dominate the errors? How much of the primary F1 gap vs. Stack-LSTM is due to label mistakes versus span/yield mismatches?
- Remote edges: What proportion of remote edges relates Scenes vs. intra-Scene sharing? Did the remote-specific features you added (e.g., s0R) shift precision vs. recall? Any ablations focused on remotes?

Additional comments
- Reporting speed and memory, plus significance tests, would help contextualize the trade-offs.
- Consider a stronger tree-to-graph reconstruction (e.g., learned attachment height, non-terminal induction), or a joint decoding that preserves more non-terminal structure during conversion, to sharpen the baseline comparison.
- A per-phenomenon evaluation (coordination, MWEs, Scene linking) would highlight where BSP’s structural advantages pay off and where modeling is still lacking.
