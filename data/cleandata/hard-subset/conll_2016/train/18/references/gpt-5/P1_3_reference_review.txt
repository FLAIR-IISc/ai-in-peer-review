Summary and contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing of grounded representations that jointly support (i) multiple parents (reentrancy), (ii) non-terminal nodes, and (iii) discontinuous units—properties that many existing semantic formalisms and parsers do not simultaneously support. The work focuses on UCCA as a representative scheme and proposes two approaches:

1) Conversion-based parsing: lossily map UCCA DAGs into (possibly discontinuous) constituency trees or dependency trees, train existing parsers, then convert back.

2) A new transition-based parser (BSP) that natively supports all three BSS properties via an extended transition set (including REMOTE edges, NODE, SWAP) and tailored features (notably a novel “ratio” feature), trained with a dynamic oracle and greedy averaged perceptron.

Experiments on the UCCA English Wikipedia corpus (in-domain) and 20K Leagues (out-of-domain) show: the best primary-edge F1 (69.2) comes from a dependency conversion with an LSTM stack-based parser; BSP is competitive with Malt/UPARSE on primary edges and uniquely predicts some remote edges (13.3 F1 in-domain), with decent OOD robustness. Converters and parsers will be released.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
- A first end-to-end parser that jointly handles reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuities for grounded semantic structures (BSP).
- A practical conversion framework establishing baselines for UCCA/BSS via constituency and dependency parsers, with quantified upper bounds that expose information loss.
- An empirical benchmark for UCCA parsing (in- and out-of-domain), with analyses (feature ablations, conversion upper bounds) that reveal concrete paths for improvement.

Strengths
1) Addresses an important gap: Joint support for reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuities is underexplored in grounded semantic parsing. Framing UCCA as a BSS target and providing both direct and conversion-based approaches is timely and useful.

2) Solid empirical setup with multiple baselines and upper bounds: Comparing UPARSE, Malt, and an LSTM stack parser under conversion, alongside the direct BSP, gives a well-triangulated empirical picture. The upper bounds quantify how much is lost by converting DAGs to trees, which is informative for the community.

3) First parser with all three BSS properties: The transition system is a sensible synthesis of prior work (discontinuous constituency parsing, DAG dependency parsing) augmented with remote-edge handling. The inclusion of SWAP and explicit primary/remote edge transitions is methodologically sound.

4) Out-of-domain evaluation and analysis: Showing similar trends on 20K Leagues is valuable. The feature ablation (especially the impact of unigrams/bigrams and the “ratio” feature) provides insight and practical takeaways.

5) Reusable artifacts: Releasing converters and parsers can catalyze further research on BSS/UCCA, conversions, and comparability across semantic formalisms.

Weaknesses
1) The strongest results come from conversion plus a neural dependency parser, not from the proposed BSP. BSP lags the LSTM conversion model by ~10 F1 on primary edges and achieves relatively low remote-edge F1 (~13). The paper’s central technical contribution underperforms the best baseline, limiting the method’s empirical impact.

2) Limited methodological novelty: The transition system largely combines known ingredients (NODE, SWAP, primary vs. remote edges) and dynamic oracle training; the “ratio” feature is minor. While integrating these for UCCA is valuable engineering, the algorithmic novelty feels incremental.

3) Evaluation scope and choices: The evaluation removes linkage and implicit units, which are part of UCCA’s semantics and could be critical for applications. The metric reduces edges to yields, which is reasonable, but the paper lacks label-wise analyses or error typology to show where the system struggles (e.g., coordination vs. MWE vs. reentrancy). Remote-edge performance is reported but not deeply analyzed; and comparison to broader SDP systems (or conversion to bi-lexical DAGs) is not attempted.

4) Search and learning likely limit BSP performance: Greedy perceptron with no beam/global inference is known to underperform neural/beam methods in transition parsing. The claim that neuralizing the classifier would help is plausible but remains speculative without experiments.

5) Missing or underspecified details: It is unclear whether POS tags are gold or predicted, what preprocessing is used, and the frequency/distribution of remotes and discontinuities in the datasets (to contextualize the 13 F1). The rule-based Label(·) function for inverse conversion is only described as “mostly Center,” with no concrete rules in the main text. Some reference formatting is inconsistent.

Questions to authors
- Data characteristics: What proportions of edges/units are (a) remote (reentrant) and (b) discontinuous in the training/test sets? How many sentences contain each phenomenon? This would help interpret the remote F1 and the impact of SWAP.
- POS and preprocessing: Which tagger/tokenizer did you use? Are tags gold or predicted? Do you use any pretrained embeddings for the LSTM baseline, and are there differences in resources across systems?
- Error analysis: Which UCCA categories contribute most to errors, for primary and remote edges respectively? Can you provide per-label F1 and a brief confusion analysis?
- Beam/neural BSP: Did you try beam search or a neural classifier for BSP? Even small beams often help transition systems. If not, can you comment on expected computational trade-offs?
- Inverse conversion details: Please specify the Label(·) rules used in dependency-to-constituency reconstruction and their empirical impact (e.g., ablating to always Center).

Additional comments
- The constituency-conversion upper bound of 100% on primary edges is expected because the conversion simply drops remotes; clarifying this explicitly in the main text would prevent misinterpretation.
- A brief qualitative example contrasting a correct remote edge vs. nearest-tree baseline would be instructive.
- References have formatting issues; please standardize.

Overall, this paper defines a useful task framing, provides baselines and tools, and offers a first direct parser for BSS/UCCA. The technical novelty is moderate and the proposed parser underperforms the best conversion-based model, but the work is solid and likely to be valuable to the community, especially with released code and converters.
