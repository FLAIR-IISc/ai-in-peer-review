Summary and contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing of grounded graph structures that allow multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities, using UCCA as the testbed. It presents two complementary strategies: (i) lossily converting UCCA DAGs to tree formalisms (constituency and dependency) to leverage existing parsers, and (ii) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly builds such DAGs with explicit support for remote edges and discontinuities. Evaluation on in-domain UCCA Wiki and out-of-domain 20K Leagues shows up to 69.2 F1 on primary edges via dependency conversion with an LSTM parser, and 59.0 F1 primary (+13.3 F1 remote) for the direct BSP approach.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first):
1) A transition system (BSP) for grounded semantic DAGs that jointly supports non-terminals, multiple parents (remote edges), and discontinuities, with a dynamic oracle and targeted feature design.
2) Practical conversion protocols between UCCA DAGs and dependency/constituency trees, with quantification of information loss via upper bounds and competitive baselines using UPARSE, Malt, and an LSTM dependency parser.
3) An evaluation protocol for BSS over UCCA with primary vs. remote edge scoring and a yield/label matching metric, plus an initial cross-domain evaluation and feature ablation.

Strengths
- Novel and clearly scoped problem formulation: The paper articulates three key structural requirements for broad-coverage grounded semantics and positions UCCA as the only scheme meeting them. This motivates why standard dependency or tree-only solutions are insufficient, and justifies the design goals of BSP.

- Direct modeling of key UCCA phenomena: BSP extends transition-based parsing to support (a) multiple parents via explicit REMOTE edge transitions, (b) non-terminals via NODE transitions, and (c) discontinuities via SWAP. The transition inventory and constraints are concrete, implementable, and in line with prior work on discontinuous parsing and DAG dependencies.

- Thoughtful baselines and upper-bound analysis: The conversion-based approach is a sensible stress test of “how far can we get with existing tech,” with clearly measured losses (e.g., perfect primary recovery under constituency conversion vs. 88.4 F1 upper bound for dependency conversion). Including a strong LSTM dependency baseline (69.2 F1) is important and informative.

- Empirical breadth and robustness: Results are given in-domain and out-of-domain without retuning, indicating reasonable robustness (e.g., BSP primary F1 drops from 59.0 to 57.1, remote F1 13.3 to 13.6). The feature ablation is useful; e.g., the ratio feature’s strong effect on recall is a good design insight.

- Transparent evaluation design: The edge yield/label matching metric is principled and reduces to PARSEVAL in trees. Separating primary vs. remote edges is appropriate and makes the challenges clear (remote edges are indeed hard).

Weaknesses
- Modest direct-model performance and limited analysis of errors: BSP’s primary F1 (59.0) substantially trails the conversion-based LSTM (69.2) and predicts remote edges with relatively low F1 (13.3). The paper would benefit from a detailed error analysis by phenomenon (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, remotes), and a breakdown by discontinuity type and distance.

- Greedy inference and classic linear features constrain performance: BSP trains a linear perceptron with greedy decoding, while the strongest baseline uses an LSTM. This likely under-exploits available signal and context, especially for remote edges and long-range discontinuities. No experiments with beam search, global training, or neural features are reported.

- Incomplete comparison to related DAG parsers: The paper notes inability to run prior DAG parsers, but omits any reimplementation or alternative comparisons (e.g., graph-based or factor-graph approaches). This leaves open whether BSP’s transition inventory offers practical advantages over other DAG strategies on UCCA when equipped with similar classifiers.

- Evaluation metric and task scope limitations: Linkage and implicit units are discarded, which narrows the target task and may simplify structures. The metric treats primary and remote edges disjointly but does not report their distribution or impact on overall graph quality (e.g., connectivity, Scene completeness). No statistical significance testing is provided.

- Conversion procedures are lossy and rely on heuristics but overshadow direct parsing: The dependency conversion drops non-terminals and uses a heuristic Label(u). While the paper is honest about information loss, the headline best result comes from this lossy path. More nuanced inverse conversions (e.g., learned head percolation or neural tree-to-graph reconstruction) could better contextualize BSP’s contribution.

Questions to authors
1) Dynamic oracle and reachability: Is the oracle complete for the proposed transition system (i.e., guarantees gold reachability when possible)? Are there gold graphs that are unreachable due to transition constraints (e.g., acyclicity, primary-parent uniqueness)?
2) Remote edge distribution: What proportion of edges are remote, and how does remote F1 break down by label and construction (shared arguments, control/raising, coreference-like remotes)?
3) Discontinuities: What percentage of nodes are discontinuous? How does BSP performance vary with the number/length of gaps? How crucial is SWAP frequency to errors?
4) Beam search and neuralization: Did you try beam decoding or a neural classifier (e.g., BiLSTM over buffer/stack terminals) for BSP? Given the strong LSTM baseline, this seems a promising path.
5) Conversion heuristics: Can you elaborate on the Label(u) rule and its error profile? Any attempts to learn it or to reconstruct non-terminals with a neural tree transducer?
6) Upper bounds: Constituency conversion shows 100/100 primary by construction. Could you report an upper bound for recovering both primary and remote edges (e.g., with a learned remote reconstruction step) to better contextualize the remote results?
7) Runtime and memory: What are parsing speeds for BSP vs. conversion-based pipelines?

Additional comments
- Reproducibility: Please release code, trained models, and splits. Clarify full hyperparameters (feature hashing, regularization, learning rates, early stopping).
- Significance: Add statistical testing across runs or dev experiments.
- Figures and algorithms: Improve readability of Figure 2 (transition table) and ensure all constraints are precisely defined (e.g., i(x) indexing, conditions preventing cycles).
- Related work: Expand comparison to SDP systems and recent neural discontinuous parsing where applicable; discuss whether bi-lexical SDP augmented with latent non-terminals could mitigate the non-terminal limitation.
- References: Several citation entries have formatting issues (question marks, escapes). Please clean.

Overall, this is a timely, well-motivated paper with a concrete transition system and careful baseline framing. However, in its current form the core direct parser underperforms strong conversion-based methods and lacks deeper analysis and stronger modeling. Strengthening BSP with neural features/beam search, adding thorough error analysis and broader comparisons, and improving remote-edge handling would significantly improve the paper.
