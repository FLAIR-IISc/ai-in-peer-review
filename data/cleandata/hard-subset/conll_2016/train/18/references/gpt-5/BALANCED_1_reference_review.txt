Summary and claimed contributions
The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing into grounded graphs that jointly support: (i) multiple parents (reentrancy), (ii) non-terminal units, and (iii) discontinuities. The authors focus on UCCA, the only grounded scheme that natively exhibits all three, and propose two approaches: (a) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA to trees (constituency or dependency), applies existing parsers, and converts back; and (b) a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that extends shift–reduce parsing with operations for non-terminals, remote (reentrant) edges, and SWAP for discontinuities. They define a span-based labeled evaluation, report results on English UCCA (in-domain and out-of-domain), analyze feature contributions, and discuss upper bounds for the conversions.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A transition system (BSP) that handles in one framework the trio of requirements: reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuity, with a dynamic oracle and tailored features. This is novel for grounded semantic parsing.
2) A careful empirical study via conversion pipelines (to discontinuous constituency and to dependencies), including upper bounds and comparisons to strong off-the-shelf parsers, thereby establishing baselines and highlighting trade-offs between direct and conversion-based approaches.
3) An initial benchmark for UCCA parsing on English, including in/out-of-domain results, an evaluation protocol, and ablations.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated problem and positioning. The paper convincingly argues why grounded, broad-coverage semantics needs all three structural properties, and why existing formalisms (e.g., SDP, AMR) fall short in at least one dimension. The UCCA focus is appropriate and timely.
- Novel transition system covering all required structures. The extension with NODE, explicit primary vs. remote edge creation, and SWAP is principled. The design is consistent with prior work (discontinuous constituency and DAG parsing), yet adapted to UCCA’s specifics, e.g., primary vs. remote constraints.
- Solid experimental protocol with multiple baselines and upper bounds. Evaluating both direct and conversion strategies, and reporting conversion-induced upper bounds, is informative. The results show BSP is competitive on primary edges with traditional parsers, while uniquely recovering some remote edges; the LSTM-based dependency pipeline reaching 69.2 F on primary edges offers a strong reference point.
- Generalizable evaluation and analysis. The span-based labeled precision/recall is simple and reduces to PARSEVAL on trees; feature ablations are instructive (e.g., the “ratio” feature boosts recall), and the out-of-domain evaluation demonstrates some robustness.
- Relevance and potential impact. Being first to provide a parser for UCCA-like structures fills a gap. The release of converters and parsers should benefit future work on grounded semantics and applications (MT, summarization).

Weaknesses
- Performance gap of BSP vs. best conversion baseline on primary edges, and low remote-edge F1. BSP reaches ~60 F1 on primary edges vs. 69.2 F1 for the LSTM dependency pipeline; remote edges remain low (~13 F1 in-domain). While novelty is in the modeling, effectiveness is limited by the greedy perceptron classifier without beam or neural features. Suggestion: upgrade BSP’s scorer (e.g., stack-LSTM or BiLSTM features), add beam search or global training, and consider specialized features/loss for remote edges; report results to demonstrate that the transition design, not the classifier, is the bottleneck.
- Limited comparison to alternative DAG/semantic parsers and conversions. While code availability is an issue, prior DAG dependency parsers (e.g., shift-reduce DAG variants) and more sophisticated tree↔graph conversions (e.g., enriched head rules, latent non-terminals, or methods like Kong et al., 2015) are not explored. Suggestion: incorporate a stronger inverse conversion for dependencies, assess more head-priority schemes, and include ablations on conversion choices; where possible, reimplement a representative DAG baseline.
- Sparse analysis of errors and structure-specific performance. The paper lacks detailed breakdowns: which UCCA categories or phenomena (coordination, phrasal verbs, MWEs) fail most; where remote edges are missed; how discontinuity is handled (frequency and accuracy). Suggestion: add per-category F1, discontinuity-specific metrics, and qualitative error analysis to guide modeling improvements.
- Reproducibility and setup details. Critical components are underspecified: POS tagging/tokenization used for features, training hyperparameters for baselines, and the precise Label(u) heuristic for the inverse dependency→constituency conversion are only in supplementary or omitted. Suggestion: include full preprocessing details, parameter settings (including tuning), and a precise definition of Label(u) in the paper; release scripts to replicate conversions and evaluation.
- Presentation and editorial issues. There are typos (e.g., passage indices “701–695”), inconsistent references, and some notation dense sections. These detract from clarity. Suggestion: fix typos, standardize references, and add intuitive examples to illustrate key transitions and the evaluation metric.

Questions to authors
- Dynamic oracle details: How are optimal transitions defined when both primary and remote edges are pending? Does the oracle prioritize NODE vs. EDGE vs. SWAP in ambiguous states? Any differences for remote edges?
- Classifier and search: Did you try beam search for BSP, or neural scoring (e.g., stack-LSTMs) akin to Dyer et al.? If yes, what was the impact on remote-edge recall/precision?
- Preprocessing: Which POS tagger/tokenizer were used? Are tags gold or predicted? How sensitive are results to POS quality?
- Conversions: How was the priority order over UCCA labels chosen for h(u)? Did you experiment with alternative orders, and how much do they affect the dependency upper bound and performance?
- Remote edges: What is their frequency and label distribution in the data? Can you report per-label remote F1 and typical error patterns?
- Generalization: Have you tried BSP on other UCCA languages, or cross-lingual transfer? Any challenges anticipated for languages with higher discontinuity rates (e.g., German)?

Overall, this is a meaningful step toward broad-coverage grounded semantic parsing. Strengthening the learning component of BSP and expanding the analysis would substantially improve the paper’s impact.
