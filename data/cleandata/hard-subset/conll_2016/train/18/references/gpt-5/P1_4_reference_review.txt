Summary
The paper targets broad-coverage grounded semantic parsing where structures may have multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. It focuses on UCCA as the target formalism and proposes two complementary strategies: (i) conversion-based parsing by mapping UCCA graphs into (discontinuous) constituency or dependency trees, applying standard parsers, and converting back; and (ii) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly builds UCCA graphs with operations for node creation, primary and remote edges, and discontinuity via SWAP. Training uses an averaged perceptron with a dynamic oracle; inference is greedy. Experiments on UCCA Wikipedia (in-domain) and 20K Leagues (out-of-domain) show that conversion to dependency paired with an LSTM dependency parser yields the best primary F1 (69.2), while BSP is the only approach predicting remote edges (∼13 F1). Feature ablations and upper bounds from conversions are provided.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A transition system and parser for grounded semantic structures supporting multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, instantiated for UCCA, with dynamic-oracle training and an explicit REMOTE-edge mechanism.
2) A systematic conversion framework between UCCA and tree formalisms (constituency and dependency), with algorithms, upper-bound analysis, and strong baselines (e.g., stack-LSTM dependency parser), providing a clear context for what is lost by conversion.
3) Empirical analysis including out-of-domain evaluation and feature ablations (notably the useful “ratio” regularization feature), giving initial evidence that full BSS parsing is feasible and suggesting concrete paths for improvement.

Strengths
- Novel capability: First transition-based parser that jointly supports the three key BSS properties (DAGs with multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities). This fills a gap left by existing grounded semantic parsers and distinguishes the work from SDP (no non-terminals) and AMR (not grounded).
- Thoughtful comparative setup: The conversion pipelines are carefully specified (with algorithms) and paired with upper bounds, quantifying exactly how much information is discarded in each conversion. This makes the empirical comparisons interpretable and valuable for the community.
- Solid baselines and surprising finding: The stack-LSTM dependency baseline, after conversion, achieves 69.2 primary F1, significantly outperforming Malt variants, highlighting the value of modern neural local classifiers. This result directly informs future BSP improvements.
- Cross-domain robustness: Out-of-domain results are close to in-domain, suggesting the approach (and UCCA categories) generalize reasonably across genres, which is important for broad-coverage semantics.
- Training and features: Use of a dynamic oracle with a rich, well-motivated feature set and a new “ratio” feature that regularizes node creation. Feature ablations are informative and show which components contribute.

Weaknesses
- Performance gap and limited modeling: BSP’s primary F1 (∼59) lags far behind the best conversion-based baseline (69.2), and remote-edge F1 remains low (~13). The choice of a greedy averaged perceptron without beam or neural representations likely limits performance; this undermines the main claim of the direct approach’s effectiveness.
- Limited evaluation depth: There is little qualitative or fine-grained error analysis. No breakdown by key UCCA phenomena (coordination, MWEs, prepositions, discontinuous structures, remote edges), nor by label. It is thus hard to assess where BSP helps most or what bottlenecks exist.
- Comparisons to related DAG parsers: The paper states code for dependency DAG parsers was unavailable, but this leaves a gap in comparisons to the closest line of work (e.g., SDP DAG parsers). Even partial reimplementations or reported numbers on related datasets would strengthen the case.
- Metric and analysis limitations: The yield-based labeled P/R is reasonable but might obscure attachment-height errors and overemphasize constituent boundaries. No statistical significance tests are reported. Also, remote-edge evaluation is very limited (zero for conversions by design), making it hard to contextualize the 13 F1.
- Incomplete details and clarity issues: The Label() function for inverse dependency→constituency conversion is deferred to supplementary material, and the priority ordering over labels that drives head selection is not detailed. Some references and footnotes are malformed. Runtime/complexity and swap frequency statistics are missing.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a label-wise breakdown and phenomenon-level analysis (e.g., coordination, PP structures, phrasal verbs) to show where BSP helps or fails? How do remote-edge predictions distribute across categories?
- What is the priority ordering over UCCA labels used in the constituency→dependency conversion, and how sensitive are results to this ordering? Similarly, can you describe Label() in more detail and its impact on the dependency→constituency inverse conversion?
- How often does SWAP fire, and what fraction of gold discontinuities does BSP recover? Do you have statistics on discontinuity-related errors?
- Have you tried beam search or neural features (e.g., BiLSTM encoders over tokens and stack/buffer summaries) inside BSP? Given the large gain of the LSTM baseline, what performance do you expect from a neuralized BSP?
- Could you compare to SDP parsers on an SDP-style conversion of UCCA (even if imperfect), or at least discuss how BSP would adapt to bi-lexical DAGs?
- Are there significance tests between BSP and baselines, and between in-domain and out-of-domain results?
- What is the runtime profile of BSP versus baselines (throughput, memory), and how does SWAP impact complexity on long sentences?

Additional comments
- The promise to release converters and parser is important for impact and should be emphasized. Including a small qualitative error analysis and a per-label score table in the final version would greatly strengthen the paper.
