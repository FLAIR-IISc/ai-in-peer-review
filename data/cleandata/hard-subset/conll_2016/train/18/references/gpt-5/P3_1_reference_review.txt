Summary
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing with three key structural properties: multiple parents (reentrancies), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. It focuses on UCCA as the only grounded scheme supporting all three and proposes two strategies: (i) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA graphs to (discontinuous) constituency trees or bi-lexical dependency trees, trains existing parsers, then maps back; and (ii) a new transition-based DAG-and-discontinuity parser (BSP) that introduces dedicated transitions (including remote edges) and features (including a regularization “ratio” feature). On English UCCA, the best primary-edge F1 (~69.2) comes from the dependency conversion with an LSTM-based dependency parser, while BSP reaches ~59 F1 on primary edges and ~13 F1 on remote edges; out-of-domain performance is comparable.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A concrete transition system for grounded DAG parsing with non-terminals and discontinuities, including explicit support for UCCA remote edges and a practical feature set and dynamic oracle.
- A systematic conversion methodology between UCCA DAGs and two restrictive formalisms (discontinuous constituents, bi-lexical dependencies), with measured upper bounds and empirical performance using standard parsers.
- A simple, span-based evaluation measure for labeled edges in DAGs (primary and remote), along with the first reported results on UCCA parsing across in-domain and out-of-domain settings.

Strengths
- Problem formulation and scope: The paper clearly motivates why tree-only or bi-lexical dependency representations are insufficient for semantic phenomena such as shared arguments, coordination without clear heads, and MWEs with discontinuities. Framing UCCA parsing as “BSS” is crisp and persuasive.
- Methodological clarity: The transition inventory is carefully designed to cover reentrancy (remote edges), non-terminals (NODEX), and discontinuities (SWAP), and constraints (single primary parent) are explicitly enforced. The dynamic oracle and perceptron setup are standard and appropriate for this setting.
- Conversion baselines and upper bounds: The paper does not rely solely on a novel parser. Instead, it makes a fair attempt to probe “how far we can get” with off-the-shelf technology via lossy conversions, reporting meaningful upper bounds and strong baselines (notably, the stack-LSTM dependency parser).
- Empirical scope: Both in-domain (Wikipedia) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) experiments are run. BSP’s out-of-domain degradation is modest, suggesting some robustness. The feature ablations are informative; the “ratio” feature is a useful regularizer for non-terminal creation.
- Practical relevance: UCCA is grounded and cross-linguistic; enabling its parsing opens the door to downstream applications (MT, summarization) that can benefit from stable, semantically meaningful spans.

Weaknesses
- Limited strength of the core contribution: The proposed BSP parser underperforms the best conversion-based baseline on primary edges by a sizable margin (~10 F1), and its remote-edge recall remains low. While the paper suggests adopting a stronger classifier (e.g., LSTM), the current results do not demonstrate superiority of the bespoke transition system over conversion-based alternatives.
- Evaluation metric may be too forgiving: Matching edges solely by the yield of the child and the edge label ignores the identity (or span) of the parent. In DAGs with reentrancies, this can conflate distinct attachments that share the same child span and label, potentially overestimating performance, especially for remote edges. A more discriminative metric (e.g., requiring both parent and child anchoring, or node-to-node alignment) would better reflect structural accuracy.
- Incomplete details for conversions: Key decisions in the conversions (e.g., the linear priority over labels for head selection; the Label(u) function for reconstructing non-terminals) are only sketched. Given that the best reported results come from a conversion pipeline, these design choices merit fuller specification and sensitivity analysis; the dependency upper bound (88.4 F1) leaves a wide gap to the achieved 69.2 F1.
- Limited analysis of error sources: The paper lacks per-label or per-phenomenon breakdowns (e.g., coordination vs. phrasal verbs vs. PP structures), qualitative analyses of remote-edge errors, or cycle/ill-formedness diagnostics. Without this, it is hard to assess whether weaknesses stem from search (greedy decoding), features, data sparsity, or transition design.
- Scope and generality claims: Although UCCA is argued to be cross-linguistic, experiments are only in English; linkage edges and implicit units are excluded, which removes some of the hallmark UCCA phenomena. Claims of “broad-coverage” would be stronger with results on additional languages or with a discussion of how BSP handles typical cross-linguistic divergences.

Questions to authors
- Evaluation: Can you quantify how often the current metric would count as correct an edge that is attached to an incorrect parent (same child yield and label, different parent)? Could you report an alternative metric that conditions on both parent and child anchoring?
- Conversions: Please specify the exact label-priority order used in Algorithm 1 and the full definition of Label(u) in Algorithm 2. How sensitive are the final numbers to these choices?
- Constraints: How do you ensure DAG acyclicity, especially with remote edges? Are cycles possible under the current transition system, and if not, why?
- Remote edges: What is the distribution of remote-edge labels, and which ones are most frequently missed? Could you provide per-label F1 and an error analysis?
- Oracle and search: Did you try beam search for BSP, or larger-margin (or globally normalized) training? If not, can you comment on the expected gains vs. the dynamic-oracle with greedy inference?

Additional comments
- The “ratio” feature is interesting and possibly useful beyond UCCA; an ablation showing its effect on over- or under-generation of non-terminals per sentence length would be helpful.
- Reporting significance tests and per-category scores (e.g., P/A/C/R etc.) would strengthen the empirical section.
- There are minor typos and formatting issues (e.g., “Transitionbased,” “dependeny”); a careful proofreading pass is recommended.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important and well-motivated; the paper provides a first concrete transition system for UCCA-like DAGs with discontinuities, plus strong conversion baselines. However, the bespoke parser currently lags the best conversion pipeline, the evaluation has limitations, and some methodological details are under-specified. Strengthening the analysis and clarifying the conversion design would make this a solid contribution.
