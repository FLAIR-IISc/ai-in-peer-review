Summary and contributions
This paper tackles broad-coverage semantic parsing for grounded representations that require (i) multiple parents (reentrancy), (ii) non-terminal units, and (iii) discontinuities. The authors focus on UCCA as the target scheme and propose two complementary strategies: (a) conversion-based parsing using existing constituency/dependency parsers through lossy conversions, and (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs UCCA-style DAGs with discontinuities and remote edges. They design transitions, features, and a dynamic oracle; define evaluation for DAGs based on yield+label matching; and provide experiments on UCCA Wikipedia (in-domain) and 20K Leagues (out-of-domain).

As I see it, the main contributions (in order):
1) A transition system (BSP) that directly parses into grounded DAG structures with non-terminals, reentrancies, and discontinuities, including new transitions for remote edges and a dynamic oracle.
2) Simple bidirectional conversion protocols between UCCA and constituency/dependency formalisms to leverage existing parsers, with empirical findings that a strong dependency parser (stack-LSTM) plus conversion reaches the best primary-edge F-score.
3) An evaluation protocol for BSS/UCCA via labeled yield matching, and an empirical benchmark on UCCA (including out-of-domain) establishing initial baselines.

Strengths
- Problem framing and scope: The paper clearly motivates why a grounded semantic parser must support all three structural properties (reentrancy, non-terminals, discontinuity). UCCA is an appropriate testbed, with prior evidence of cross-linguistic applicability and semantic stability. Positioning the work as the first to handle all three simultaneously for a grounded semantic scheme is compelling.

- Methodological contribution (BSP): The transition inventory is well thought out: NODE for non-terminals, SWAP for discontinuity, and explicit LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE vs LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE for controlling primary vs remote edges. The constraint that primary in-degree is one is encoded. The dynamic oracle and perceptron training are appropriate, and the novel ratio feature is a simple, effective regularizer. The system design is clear and draws from related paradigms while extending them for UCCA’s needs.

- Empirical benchmark and insights: The experiments provide useful, comparable baselines under both conversion regimes and the direct approach, including in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation. A key empirical insight is that a strong neural dependency parser followed by reconstruction achieves the best primary-edge F-score (69.2), suggesting that classifier strength matters more than transition set in this setting and pointing a clear path to improve BSP by adopting neural representations.

- Evaluation definition and transparency: The labeled yield-based matching measure is reasonable for DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities and reduces to PARSEVAL on trees. The authors fairly separate primary and remote edges, acknowledging conversion methods cannot recover remotes. Upper bounds for lossy conversions are provided, illuminating how much structure is lost.

- Reproducibility intent: The paper specifies hyperparameters and training details (e.g., MINUPDATE, IMPORTANCE, iterations) and promises code release, which is important for a new task/representation.

Weaknesses
- Performance gap of the proposed parser: BSP underperforms the best conversion-based baseline on primary edges (59.0 vs 69.2 F1), and its remote-edge F1 is low (~13). While the paper argues neural classifiers could close the gap, the current main novel method lags behind a relatively simple pipeline, weakening the case for immediate practical utility. A neuralized BSP or at least a beam-search variant would strengthen the contribution.

- Lossy conversion and evaluation implications: The dependency conversion discards non-terminals and reentrancies, and the reconstruction relies on a simple Label heuristic that “mostly selects Center.” This may bias constituent structure and label distributions and makes the comparison between direct and conversion-based parsers partially apples-to-oranges (especially since conversion gets a perfect primary upper bound in the constituency regime and zero for remote edges in both regimes). More analysis on the structural errors introduced by conversion (e.g., category-wise, attachment height, coordination/PPs) would contextualize the results.

- Limited analysis of remote edges: Remote edge prediction is a core motivation (multiple parents), yet the paper provides only aggregate scores and no error analysis, frequency, or breakdown by phenomenon (control/raising, shared participants, etc.). It is difficult to assess whether low recall stems from data sparsity, transition design, features, or oracle/training dynamics.

- Completeness and correctness guarantees: The paper does not provide a formal proof or argument that the transition system is sound and complete for the class of UCCA graphs considered (with primary/remote edges, discontinuities, and the single-primary-parent constraint). A brief justification or citation would increase confidence that no admissible structures are excluded or spurious ones allowed.

- Baseline coverage and training choices: BSP uses greedy decoding and sparse features, whereas some baselines (UPARSE) use beam search and the best dependency baseline uses neural representations. This exacerbates the perceived gap. Including a beam for BSP, or swapping in a neural classifier, would make the comparison more balanced. Also, lack of comparison to prior DAG dependency parsers is attributed to unavailability of code; a reimplementation of a representative method or stronger ablations might help.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a proof sketch or argument for the soundness and completeness of the transition system for the targeted UCCA graphs (under the constraints you enforce)? Are there UCCA structures that your system cannot produce?

- What is the prevalence of remote edges in the datasets, and how does BSP performance vary by remote-edge type (e.g., shared participants across Scenes vs other reentrancies)? Any diagnosis of the main sources of remote-edge errors?

- How sensitive is BSP to beam size and to adopting a neural scoring model (e.g., BiLSTM features)? Did you attempt beam search or a neural classifier, and if so, what were the trends?

- In the dependency conversion, how often does the Label heuristic pick non-Center categories, and how does this affect label accuracy downstream? Could a learned reconstruction improve the upper bound materially?

- How much of the primary-edge error for BSP is due to discontinuities? Does IMPORTANCE=2 for SWAP materially change results (ablation)?

Additional comments
- Some references have typos/incomplete metadata; please clean up. Please add more qualitative/error analysis (esp. coordination, PPs, MWEs), and report parsing speed. Clarify whether linkage nodes’ removal materially changes graph connectivity in single sentences.
