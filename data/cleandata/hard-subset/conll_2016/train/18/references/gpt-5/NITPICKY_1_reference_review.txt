Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper targets broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS) that require multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, focusing on UCCA. It proposes: (1) a transition-based parser (BSP) that supports all three properties; (2) lossy conversion protocols between UCCA and (possibly discontinuous) constituency trees and CoNLL-style dependencies, enabling reuse of existing parsers; (3) an evaluation protocol for BSS and experiments on in-domain (UCCA Wikipedia) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) data.

My view of the main contributions, in order of strength:
1) A concrete, reproducible transition system and feature set for UCCA parsing that allows non-terminals, discontinuities (via SWAP), and multiple parents (via REMOTE edges).
2) Empirical comparison via conversion-based pipelines that quantifies how much information is lost when forcing UCCA into trees, plus an informative upper-bound analysis.
3) A simple yield-based labeled evaluation for BSS, and a small ablation study highlighting feature importance (notably the ratio feature).

Strengths
- Relevance and scope: The paper squarely addresses a real, under-served need: grounded semantic parsing to a graph formalism with non-terminals and discontinuities. UCCA is a concrete testbed, and this work is, to my knowledge, the first end-to-end attempt at UCCA parsing.
- Technical clarity of the transition system: The transition inventory extends well-known shift-reduce machinery with NODE, SWAP, and primary/remote LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE variants. The constraints (e.g., only one primary parent) and the separation of primary vs. remote edges are sensible and clearly motivated by UCCA’s design.
- Dynamic oracle training and out-of-domain evaluation: Using a dynamic oracle with a greedy perceptron is a reasonable starting point; including out-of-domain results on a different genre (20K Leagues) adds value and shows some robustness.
- Useful conversion baselines and bounds: The two conversion pipelines (to constituency and dependency) are carefully constructed, and the upper-bound analysis highlights exactly where information is inevitably lost (notably remote edges and non-terminals). The result that an off-the-shelf LSTM dependency parser yields the best primary F (69.2) is an important empirical finding for the community.
- Feature ablation: Although limited, the ablation reveals that simple lexical features and the proposed ratio regularizer materially contribute, which is practically useful for future work.

Weaknesses
- Core method underperforms a baseline: The proposed BSP lags the conversion + LSTM baseline by ~10 absolute F1 on primary edges (59.0 vs. 69.2 in-domain), and its one clear advantage—predicting remote edges—remains weak (13.3 F1 in-domain; 13.6 OOD). The main selling point (direct BSS parsing) is not yet competitive on the primary edges that dominate the structure.
- Evaluation design and reporting gaps: The yield-set matching metric collapses different structures that share the same descendant set and label, potentially overstating correctness in DAGs (e.g., multiple parents with identical yields). There is no unlabeled score, no analysis of continuity vs. discontinuity errors, no remote-edge breakdown by label, and no statistical significance testing. Excluding punctuation from scoring but including it in modeling is fine, but this should be justified quantitatively (does it affect baselines differently?). Also, runtime, memory, and sentence-length robustness are not reported.
- Reproducibility concerns: Several crucial components are unspecified or deferred. The linear order over edge labels (which determines heads in conversion) is not given; the Label(u) function for inverse dependency-to-constituency conversion is relegated to “Supplementary Material” not included; POS tagging source is not specified; hyperparameters beyond MINUPDATE and IMPORTANCE are not detailed; no beam search or early update comparisons; no code link (only “upon publication”). The choice to train on sentence-internal edges only removes 0.18% of edges; the impact is stated but not analyzed.
- Incomplete/fragile novelty: The transition set largely recombines known operations (NODE/REDUCE/SHIFT/SWAP) and DAG edges (primary/remote) akin to prior DAG dependency parsing. The distinctive novelty is mainly the UCCA-tailored details and features. However, there’s no comparison to recent graph parsing frameworks beyond a brief SDP/AMR discussion. The claim of being the first “BSS” parser is semantically defined to exclude formalisms without non-terminals; this should be tempered.
- Presentation and formatting issues: Numerous typos and reference problems: inconsistent author-year formatting (question marks, duplicated author names), spelling errors (“Transitionbased dependeny”), mismatched punctuation, and missing diacritics; dangling footnote markers; “passages 701– 695” (reverse range); unexplained symbols in Figure 2 conditions (e.g., “y 6;G x”), undefined i(x) initialization for tokens; and the linear order over labels L is mentioned but not specified. These undermine polish and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
1) Evaluation metric: How sensitive are conclusions to using (a) unlabeled yield matching; (b) edge-level precision/recall over terminal pairs for remote edges; or (c) a structure-aware metric that distinguishes multiple parents with the same yield? Please provide a small-scale comparison.
2) Conversion details: What exact linear order over labels L was used, and how was it chosen? Please include the full Label(u) function and quantify how different plausible orderings affect upper bounds and system performance.
3) Features and inputs: What POS tagger (and other preprocessing) was used? Are embeddings used anywhere? Have you tried simple pre-trained embeddings in BSP?
4) Training and search: Did you try beam search for BSP (given UPARSE uses beam 4)? What is the effect on primary and remote edges? Any results with a neural local classifier (e.g., the stack-LSTM used in your best baseline)?
5) Error analysis: What fraction of errors are due to discontinuities vs. multiple-parent edges? Which labels are most error-prone for remote edges?
6) Data splits: Please clarify the in-domain split (“701– 695” appears to be a typo). Also provide sentence-length histograms and learning curves.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is important and the paper provides useful baselines and a first-pass transition system for UCCA, but the proposed method is not yet competitive; evaluation/reporting and reproducibility have notable gaps; and the paper requires significant polishing and clarification. A strengthened revision with a neural classifier for BSP, beam search, fuller evaluation, and corrected presentation could be impactful.
