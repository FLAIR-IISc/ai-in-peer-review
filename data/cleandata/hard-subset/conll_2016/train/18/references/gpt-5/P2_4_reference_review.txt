Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper targets “broad-coverage semantic structures” (BSS) that simultaneously support multiple parents (reentrancy), non-terminal units, and discontinuities, using UCCA as the grounded semantic formalism.
- It proposes two approaches: (i) conversion-based parsing by mapping UCCA to constituency and dependency trees, training off-the-shelf parsers, and mapping back; and (ii) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly builds UCCA DAGs with non-terminals, remote edges, and discontinuities.
- It introduces explicit conversion algorithms (with upper-bound analyses), a transition inventory including remote-edge and SWAP operations, a dynamic oracle, and feature design including a novel global “ratio” feature.
- Experiments on in-domain (UCCA Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) show: best primary-edge F1 via conversion + stack-LSTM dependency parser (69.2%); BSP achieves comparable primary-edge F1 to Malt/UPARSE but uniquely recovers some remote edges (≈13% F1); out-of-domain results are similar.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1) A first end-to-end parser that natively supports all three BSS properties on a grounded semantic scheme (UCCA), with a concrete transition system and dynamic oracle.
2) Well-specified, reproducible conversion procedures with informative upper bounds, enabling the use of existing parsers for UCCA as a strong baseline.
3) An empirical study across in-/out-of-domain data, including a useful feature ablation and comparisons across parser families.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and scope. The paper crisply motivates the need to support reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuity in grounded semantics, arguing why bi-lexical dependencies alone are insufficient (e.g., coordination, MWEs). Using UCCA is well-justified.
- First direct parser for UCCA/BSS with all three properties. The transition system is thoughtfully adapted from prior work (discontinuous constituency and DAG dependencies) and extended with remote-edge transitions; the dynamic oracle and SWAP usage are appropriate. This is a meaningful technical step.
- Solid conversion baselines and upper-bound analysis. The forward/inverse conversion algorithms are explicit and reasonable; reporting the information loss (e.g., 100% primary upper bound for constituency conversion; 88.4% for dependency) contextualizes the achievable performance and fairly shows the trade-offs.
- Competent experimental design. Multiple parsers (UPARSE, Malt variants, stack-LSTM) provide a broad baseline comparison; both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation is included; the evaluation measure is defined and relates to PARSEVAL; punctuation and non-sentence-level edges are transparently handled.
- Feature ablation and insights. The ablation shows which feature families matter (e.g., unigrams/bigrams, ratio feature), providing guidance for future work. The result that the stack-LSTM dramatically outperforms Malt (same transitions, different classifier) is a useful empirical takeaway and suggests a direct neural upgrade path for BSP.

Weaknesses
- Proposed method underperforms the strongest baseline on the main metric. The best primary-edge score (69.2% F1) is achieved by conversion + stack-LSTM dependency parsing; BSP’s primary-edge F1 is ≈59–60. While BSP uniquely recovers some remote edges, their F1 remains low (~13%). The paper would be stronger if BSP were competitive on primary edges, especially given its greater modeling capacity.
- Limited modeling choices for BSP. BSP uses a linear averaged perceptron with greedy inference, no beam, and no neural features, despite clear evidence (in this very paper) that neural classifiers substantially help. This makes it difficult to assess the full potential of the proposed transition system; at minimum, a beam or a neural classifier would provide a fairer comparison.
- Sparse analysis of remote edges and discontinuities. Remote-edge F1 is low and there is little qualitative or quantitative error analysis to identify dominant failure modes (e.g., which labels, which constructions, data sparsity vs. search errors vs. feature insufficiency). Given that remote edges motivate the entire BSS setting, more insight is warranted.
- Evaluation metric limitations and reporting. The yield+label matching is a reasonable generalization of PARSEVAL, but for DAGs it can conflate structures that share yields but differ in internal configuration or parentage; discussion of potential ambiguities or alternative metrics (e.g., node/edge alignment or anchored graph F1) would increase confidence. Statistical significance and learning curves are not reported.
- Missing or deferred baselines and clarity issues. The paper notes inability to obtain DAG parsers; while understandable, it leaves out a pertinent comparison class. Some details (e.g., Label() heuristic, feature sparsity handling) are relegated to supplementary material; references have formatting problems; minor clarity issues persist.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Why not equip BSP with a beam or a neural classifier, given the demonstrated gains with the stack-LSTM baseline? Do you expect similar improvements if BSP used an LSTM-based scoring function?
- Can you provide an error analysis for remote edges and discontinuities? Which labels or constructions dominate the errors, and are they due to search, data sparsity, or representation?
- How sensitive are results to the Label() heuristic in the dependency-to-constituency inverse conversion? Could a learned inverse conversion improve the upper bound and actual performance?
- What preprocessing is used (e.g., POS tagging)? Are gold POS used? How does this affect results?
- Could you comment on metric robustness for DAGs (e.g., cases with identical yields but different parents), and whether alternative graph matching metrics change conclusions?

Overall assessment
This paper tackles an important and under-explored problem: grounded semantic parsing that truly accommodates reentrancy, non-terminals, and discontinuities. It provides the first direct UCCA parser with these capabilities, along with clear conversion baselines and informative upper bounds. The empirical section is thoughtfully constructed and the results, while modest, set a credible starting point for the task. The main drawbacks are that the proposed parser is not yet competitive with the strongest conversion-based neural baseline and that analysis of remote/discontinuous cases is limited. With a stronger classifier for BSP or deeper analysis, this would be a clear accept; as is, it remains a valuable contribution establishing the task and baseline methodology.
