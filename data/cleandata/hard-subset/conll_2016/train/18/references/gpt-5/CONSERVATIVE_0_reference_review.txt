Summary and claimed contributions
- A conversion-based study showing that UCCA Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) can be approximated by converting to (possibly discontinuous) constituency trees and to dependency trees, training established parsers, and converting back; the strongest primary-edge result (69.2 F1) comes from a stack-LSTM dependency parser.
- A new transition system (BSP) for UCCA/BSS that supports non-terminals, remote edges (multiple parents), and discontinuities (via SWAP), trained with a perceptron and a dynamic oracle; BSP attains 59.0 primary F1 and modest remote-edge F1 (13.3).
- Converters between UCCA and trees (with stated upper bounds), a simple evaluation measure, and experiments on in-domain and out-of-domain data.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and scope. The paper targets a representation that requires multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, and articulates why bi-lexical dependencies and AMR do not fully meet these needs. The focus on UCCA—one of the few grounded semantic formalisms with these properties—is timely.
- Solid, informative conversion baselines with upper bounds. The conversion protocols are well specified, and the inclusion of back-and-forth upper bounds helps quantify information loss. The finding that a strong dependency parser (stack-LSTM) reaches 69.2 primary F1 is valuable and pragmatic for practitioners who may not need remote edges.
- A concrete transition system for UCCA. The transition inventory (NODE, LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE vs. REMOTE variants, SWAP) is sensible and draws from established work on discontinuities and DAG parsing. The dynamic oracle and MINUPDATE training are appropriate choices.
- Out-of-domain evaluation. Applying models trained on Wiki to 20K Leagues without retuning and reporting both primary and remote scores adds credibility on robustness (albeit with modest absolute performance).
- Reproducibility intent. Converters and parsers are to be released. The description of algorithms and features is reasonably detailed.

Weaknesses
- The proposed parser underperforms strong established baselines on the main metric. The best primary-edge performance is achieved by an off-the-shelf neural dependency parser after conversion (69.2 F1), while BSP lags by ~10 F1. The paper itself suggests that using an LSTM classifier for BSP would likely help; however, that experiment is not conducted. As a result, the main methodological contribution (BSP) is not empirically validated as competitive.
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior transition-based DAG and discontinuous parsing. Most design choices (SWAP for discontinuity, separate REMOTE transitions, non-terminal creation via NODE, dynamic oracles) closely follow established parsers. The work is framed as “first to support all three properties,” but functionally combines known ingredients with UCCA-specific labels and a small set of novel features. Without stronger empirical gains or theoretical guarantees (e.g., completeness/soundness proofs for generating all valid UCCA DAGs), the novelty feels incremental.
- Evaluation scope and metric limitations. The evaluation removes inter-sentential links and implicit units, and remote-edge F1 remains low (≈13), leaving a core property of UCCA under-modeled. The proposed “edge yield + label” metric collapses to PARSEVAL on trees, but its behavior on DAGs with discontinuities is not analyzed (e.g., multiple edges with identical yields, impact of non-terminal reconstruction). No calibration against UCCA’s native evaluation or alternative graph metrics is provided.
- Comparisons and training choices are dated/uneven. BSP is trained with a linear perceptron and greedy inference, while some baselines use beam search (UPARSE) or neural models (stack-LSTM). There is no beam or global training for BSP, no strong graph/DAG parsers (e.g., higher-order or max-margin/CRF variants), and no contemporary neural ablations. The fairness of the comparison is thus unclear, and the conclusion that “transition-based methods are effective for BSS” is mainly supported by the converted dependency baseline rather than BSP itself.
- Analysis depth is limited. The paper mentions a feature ablation but reports only qualitative takeaways; detailed numbers are missing. There is little error analysis (e.g., where remote edges fail, what types of discontinuities require SWAP, where conversions lose structure), and limited per-phenomenon results (coordination, PPs, MWEs, phrasal verbs).

Questions to authors
- Can you formally characterize the coverage of your transition system? Is it sound and complete for the class of UCCA DAGs considered (single primary parent per node plus any number of remote parents, arbitrary discontinuities)? If not, what classes of structures cannot be generated?
- How does the evaluation metric behave on DAGs with overlapping yields or multiple parents? Do you deduplicate edges with the same yield/label pairs, and how are ties broken? Any sensitivity analysis?
- What is the impact of beam search or a neural classifier on BSP? Even small-scale experiments (beam size 4–8, BiLSTM features) would help assess whether the gap to the converted stack-LSTM baseline is due to modeling or search.
- Can you report the concrete numbers for feature ablations (Table 3) and provide per-category results (e.g., coordination, PPs, MWEs, remote edges by label)?
- How much of the remote-edge gold annotations are intra-sentential vs. inter-sentential after your filtering? Remote F1 is low; which labels dominate errors?
- For the dependency-to-constituency inverse conversion, the Label heuristic “mostly selects Center.” How sensitive are results to this choice, and can you provide ablations or improved heuristics?

Additional comments
- Please release code and conversion scripts at submission or camera-ready to facilitate verification.
- Clarify whether punctuation is excluded only at evaluation or also during training; detail tokenization and POS tagging resources.
