Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles parsing into broad-coverage grounded semantic structures (UCCA), emphasizing three structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminals, and discontinuities. It offers:
1) A transition-based parser (BSP) extending standard shift-reduce parsing with transitions for non-terminals, remote (re-entrant) edges, and discontinuities.
2) Two conversion protocols between UCCA and more established formalisms (discontinuous constituency and dependencies), allowing use of existing parsers; with explicit upper bounds to quantify conversion loss.
3) Empirical evaluation on English UCCA (in-domain and out-of-domain), showing the strongest results come from conversion to dependencies + LSTM parser (~69.2 primary F1), while BSP predicts remote edges (low F1) that conversions cannot.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and principled conversions with upper bounds. The conversion algorithms are specified, and the paper quantifies information loss via “gold→converted→inverse-converted” upper bounds. This provides a useful reference for researchers interested in leveraging established parsers on UCCA-like graphs.
- Establishes initial baselines for UCCA parsing. The work reports multiple baselines (UPARSE, Malt, stack-LSTM), a direct transition-based model, and out-of-domain tests (20K Leagues). The evaluation protocol (yield- and label-based comparison; separate reporting for primary vs. remote edges) is transparent and reproducible.
- Sound learning setup for BSP relative to its design. The use of a dynamic oracle with averaged perceptron and a feature ablation study demonstrates due diligence in training a transition system with non-trivial constraints (single primary parent, remote edges, discontinuities).
- Useful empirical finding: established dependency parsers with conversion outperform the bespoke direct parser on primary edges. This is important for practitioners: until stronger direct models exist, conversion to dependencies with a strong classifier is a reasonable baseline.
- Out-of-domain robustness analysis. Reporting results on a different text type without retuning is appreciated and relatively rare in early-stage semantic parsing work.

Weaknesses
- Core method underperforms a simple conversion baseline. The best primary F1 (69.2) is achieved by conversion to dependencies plus a standard stack-LSTM parser, while the proposed BSP lags (~59 F1). The headline capability of predicting remote edges is only weakly realized (remote F1 ≈ 13), raising questions about the practical advantage of the direct approach at this stage. The paper suggests adopting an LSTM classifier for BSP, but does not implement it; thus the main contribution remains unvalidated against the strongest reasonable baseline.
- Limited novelty beyond combination and adaptation. The transition set largely extends known mechanisms (NODE, EDGE, REMOTE, SWAP) from prior constituency/DAG/discontinuous parsing work. Without a stronger empirical advantage or deeper theoretical insight, the incremental algorithmic novelty is modest.
- Evaluation scope and analysis are insufficient. Experiments are confined to English UCCA, with relatively small and domain-specific splits. There is no phenomenon-level breakdown (e.g., coordination, phrasal verbs, degree of discontinuity, frequency of remotes), no category-wise performance, and no statistical significance testing. The very low remote-edge F1 is not analyzed (e.g., how many remotes exist; precision/recall tradeoffs; typical error types).
- Methodological concerns and fairness. Inference for BSP is greedy with a linear perceptron, whereas the strongest baseline uses an LSTM; UPARSE uses beam search while others are greedy. This makes it hard to attribute performance differences to representation vs. learning capacity. No attempt is made to control for search (e.g., beam for BSP) or classifier strength. Moreover, the dependency→constituency inverse conversion uses a heuristic Label function; the impact of this heuristic on final scores is not dissected.
- Metric and presentation issues. The yield-based scoring is reasonable, but for DAGs with multiple parents, yield identity may still mask structural differences; the paper should justify this choice or add complementary metrics (e.g., unlabeled, remote-only, attachment accuracy to specific parents). There are several clarity and editorial issues (e.g., test split “701–695”, incomplete/erroneous citations), which reduce confidence in polish and reproducibility. Code/data are promised “upon publication” rather than provided, limiting verification.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- What proportion of edges in the corpora are remote? Please report primary vs. remote counts and per-phenomenon distributions. How does BSP perform specifically on the most frequent remote categories?
- Can you provide a breakdown by key phenomena (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, degree of discontinuity)? Where does BSP help over conversion, if anywhere?
- How much of the BSP–LSTM gap is due to classifier capacity vs. representation? Do you have preliminary results with a neural (biLSTM/stack-LSTM) local classifier or beam search for BSP?
- For the dependency conversion, how sensitive are final scores to the heuristic Label function in the inverse step? Can you report per-label precision/recall after inverse conversion to identify systematic reconstruction errors?
- Please clarify the dataset splits (the test range “701–695” seems a typo). Also confirm whether linkage and implicit units were removed consistently in both training and evaluation for all methods and conversions.
- Could you add significance testing across systems, and release code/converters during the rebuttal to facilitate verification?

Overall, this submission opens a valuable line for UCCA parsing and provides practical conversion baselines. However, the proposed direct method currently underperforms established approaches and lacks rigorous analysis of the challenging phenomena it aims to address. Strengthening the empirical validation (stronger classifier/search for BSP, phenomenon-wise diagnostics, significance, broader datasets) would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
