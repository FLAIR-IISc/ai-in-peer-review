Summary and contributions
This paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing for UCCA by (i) proposing lossy conversions from UCCA DAGs to more restrictive structures (constituency and dependency) to leverage existing parsers, and (ii) introducing a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports three structural properties: multiple parents (remote edges), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities (via SWAP). Experiments on English UCCA (Wikipedia; 20K Leagues OOD) report primary-edge F1 up to 69.2 via conversion + LSTM dependency parsing, and 59.0 with BSP; remote-edge F1 with BSP is ~13.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An initial parsing baseline for UCCA that shows existing transition-based technology (especially neural dependency parsing) can reach ~69% primary F1 after conversion.
2) A transition system for UCCA-style DAGs with discontinuity, including remote-edge transitions and a dynamic oracle, plus a small set of representation-specific features.
3) A simple evaluation protocol and conversion toolkit with reported upper bounds quantifying information loss.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: The paper articulates why UCCA/BSS require support for multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, and why off-the-shelf dependency/constituency parsers are insufficient. The conversion-based setup is reasonable as a first step and useful for the community.
- Solid use of established baselines and methodology: Leveraging UPARSE, MaltParser, and a stack-LSTM dependency parser to ground results in known technology is appropriate. Reporting upper bounds for conversions helps interpret what is lost structurally vs. by the parser.
- Direct parser design is technically sound at a high level: The transition set extends standard systems with NODE and REMOTE transitions; SWAP handles discontinuities; dynamic-oracle perceptron training is a reasonable established choice. The “ratio” feature to regularize node creation is a pragmatic idea.
- Some robustness evidence: Out-of-domain evaluation on 20K Leagues without retuning is welcome, even if gains are modest.
- Reproducibility intent: Stated plan to release converters and parsers is valuable for a relatively under-resourced representation like UCCA.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation on the distinctive aspects: The hallmark property—multiple parents—is handled poorly in practice (remote-edge F1 ≈ 13). There is no phenomenon-level breakdown (remote vs. discontinuous vs. complex non-terminals) to demonstrate that the proposed transitions actually help where UCCA differs from trees. The best overall primary F1 comes from a conversion plus a powerful neural dependency baseline that cannot predict remotes at all.
- Methodological choices are dated relative to strongest baselines: BSP uses a greedy averaged perceptron with hand-crafted features. Given the strong gains observed with the LSTM dependency parser, a comparable neural classifier for BSP seems essential to fairly assess the transition system. No beam search or global learning is considered. This weakens the main claim that BSP is a practical parser for BSS.
- Comparisons do not cover related strong formalisms: There is no comparison to state-of-the-art DAG parsers from SDP (even if they lack non-terminals) or to competitive discontinuous constituency parsers beyond UPARSE defaults. The paper argues SDP’s bi-lexicality is limiting, but empirical comparison (e.g., via conversions) would strengthen the case.
- Conversion procedures and evaluation design are simplistic: The dependency-to-constituency inverse conversion relies on a heuristic Label(u) (often Center), which is ad hoc; alternative head rules or learned reconstruction are not explored. The evaluation metric reduces to yield-based edge matching; while simple, it gives zero credit to converted trees on remotes by construction, making across-system comparison less informative. No statistical significance testing is reported.
- Scope and clarity issues: Only English is evaluated, despite cross-lingual claims; OOD test is still English. Important implementation details are missing or unclear (e.g., ensuring acyclicity with REMOTE edges). There are minor typos/indices (e.g., “701–695”) and incomplete references.

Questions to authors
1) How do you prevent cycles in the DAG when creating remote edges? Are there explicit constraints or checks?
2) Can you provide a breakdown of performance by phenomenon: (a) continuous vs. discontinuous units, (b) nodes with multiple parents vs. single-parent nodes, and (c) coordination/MWE structures?
3) What proportion of errors in BSP are attachment vs. labeling? Any confusion matrix over labels?
4) How sensitive are BSP results to beam search and/or a neural classifier (e.g., BiLSTM features over buffer/stack tokens)? Do you have preliminary numbers?
5) For the inverse dependency→constituency conversion, did you try learned reconstruction or alternative head/attachment heuristics? How does the upper bound change?
6) How large is the set of discontinuous cases in your corpora, and what is BSP’s accuracy specifically on edges requiring SWAP?
7) Can you clarify the train/dev/test split indices and provide exact sentence/token counts per split?

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is timely and the conversion toolkit plus initial baselines are useful. However, the core novelty—the direct BSS parser—falls short empirically on the very properties it is designed to capture (remote/discontinuous), and the methodology lags behind established neural baselines. A stronger neural instantiation of BSP, phenomenon-level analysis, and broader comparisons would be needed to justify acceptance.
