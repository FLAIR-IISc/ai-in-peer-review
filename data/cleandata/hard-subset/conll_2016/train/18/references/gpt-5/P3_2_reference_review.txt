Summary
This paper targets “Broad-coverage Semantic Structures” (BSS) that simultaneously require (i) reentrancies (multiple parents), (ii) non-terminal units, and (iii) discontinuities. Using UCCA as a concrete grounded semantic formalism, the authors: (a) propose lossy conversions from UCCA DAGs to (discontinuous) constituency trees and CoNLL-style dependency trees, enabling reuse of existing parsers; and (b) introduce BSP, a transition-based parser that directly builds UCCA DAGs with explicit support for remote edges, non-terminals, and discontinuities. They define a yield-based labeled evaluation for primary and remote edges and show that a strong conversion pipeline with a Stack-LSTM dependency parser achieves 69.2 F on primary edges, while BSP achieves 59.0 primary F and 13.3 F on remote edges.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Problem formulation and target representation: A clear articulation of BSS desiderata and positioning UCCA as a grounded, cross-linguistically motivated target that meets them.
2) Direct transition-based parser for DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities (BSP): A concrete transition system, dynamic oracle training, and feature design, including explicit remote-edge transitions and SWAP for discontinuities.
3) Conversion protocols and empirical comparison: Practical conversion to constituency/dependency trees with upper bounds, enabling reuse of off-the-shelf parsers and a useful empirical reference point for UCCA parsing.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated target. The paper convincingly argues for the three structural properties needed for broad semantics and why UCCA is a good testbed. The examples highlight where bi-lexical dependencies and head-driven choices fall short (coordination, MWEs, PP attachment), validating the need for non-terminals and discontinuity support.
- Concrete, reproducible methodology. The transition inventory, training regime (averaged perceptron, dynamic oracle), and feature templates are described in detail. The conversion algorithms are explicit and the reported upper bounds clarify what information is lost. The announced release of converters and parsers will likely catalyze research on UCCA/BSS.
- Solid baselines and analysis scaffolding. The conversion-based setups let the community leverage well-understood syntactic parsers (UPARSE, Malt, Stack-LSTM), and the upper-bound analysis makes the degree of loss through conversion transparent. The result that a strong neural dependency parser is surprisingly competitive on UCCA (after conversion) is a useful empirical finding.
- Direct handling of reentrancies and discontinuities. BSP’s explicit LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX transitions and use of SWAP are principled adaptations of prior transition-based work to UCCA’s needs. The novel “ratio” feature is a sensible regularizer against over-creation of non-terminals.
- Robustness across domains. Comparable in-domain and out-of-domain results suggest that both the target representation and approach are not overly tuned to Wikipedia, which is promising for downstream applications.

Weaknesses
- Limited accuracy of the direct parser and low remote-edge F. BSP underperforms the best conversion pipeline on primary edges (59.0 vs 69.2 F) and achieves relatively low remote-edge F (13.3 in-domain, 13.6 OOD). This weakens the central claim that direct modeling of BSS yields substantial benefits. Without a stronger direct model (e.g., neural features, beam/global training), it is hard to argue for BSP over conversion pipelines.
- Incomplete exploitation of modern modeling. The choice of a greedy averaged perceptron with hand-engineered features limits performance. Given the strong gains observed with Stack-LSTMs on the conversion pipeline, a neural local classifier for BSP (or even a neural transition system end-to-end) seems essential. Beam search or globally normalized training could also help mitigate local errors and better trade off primary vs remote edges.
- Acyclicity and well-formedness constraints are underspecified. While the target is a DAG, it is not explained how cycles are prevented during parsing. The transition conditions ensure single primary parents but do not obviously rule out cycles via remote edges. Clarifying whether cycle checks or structural constraints are enforced at decode time (and their computational impact) is necessary.
- Conversion details and evaluation design leave gaps. The inverse dependency-to-constituency conversion relies on a rule-based Label(·) function with details deferred to supplementary material; its impact is non-trivial given the 88.4 F upper bound. The priority order over labels in determining heads is not described, yet it is central to the conversion. The yield-based metric is reasonable but not standard; a discussion of alignment with the UCCA community’s evaluation and a breakdown by phenomenon (coordination, MWEs, PP, phrasal verbs) are missing.
- Baseline coverage and comparisons. The paper does not compare to semantic dependency parsers from SemEval SDP (which support reentrancies), even as reference points. If code was unavailable, a controlled reimplementation or a literature-based contrast would strengthen claims. Also, no graph-based/neural DAG baselines are considered for remote edges. Finally, the removal of linkage and inter-sentential edges simplifies the task; quantifying their impact would help interpret scores.

Questions to authors
- How is acyclicity guaranteed during BSP decoding? Do you perform cycle checks when adding remote edges, or are there structural constraints that provably prevent cycles?
- What is the label priority order used in the constituency-to-dependency conversion, and how sensitive are the results to this ordering? Can you provide the full Label(·) specification and an estimate of how much error it induces?
- What proportion of UCCA edges in the datasets are remote, and what is the distribution of phenomena contributing to remote edges? Could you provide an error analysis for remote edges (e.g., shared arguments vs control/raising vs appositions)?
- How often is SWAP used, and what is the rate of discontinuous units? Do disco features materially help on discontinuities in English; have you tried German (where discontinuity is more frequent)?
- Did you experiment with beam search or neuralized features for BSP? If not, can you estimate oracle F-score or exact-oracle reachability to bound potential gains from improved search/classification?
- Can you report statistical significance for the main comparisons and include per-label F-scores (e.g., P/A/C/R/N) to assess where the parser struggles most?

Overall, this is a timely and valuable step toward broad-coverage grounded semantic parsing with UCCA. The problem setup and tooling are strong, but the direct parser lags the best conversion baseline and remote-edge performance is still low. Strengthening the direct model with neural features/beam, clarifying well-formedness constraints, and providing deeper error/phenomenon analyses would substantially improve the paper’s impact.
