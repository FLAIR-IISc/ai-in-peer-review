

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a novel approach for parsing "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" (BSS), which support three key structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex units like coordination), and discontinuous units (for phrasal verbs). The authors introduce the first parser for BSS, called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser), using a transition-based approach. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:
1. The first parser supporting all three structural properties of BSS
2. A conversion-based approach demonstrating how existing parsers can be adapted to BSS (with lossy conversion)
3. The first experimental evaluation of UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation) parsing, which is a semantic representation scheme supporting BSS

## Strengths

1. **Significant Novelty in the Problem Space:** The paper identifies a clear gap in the literature - no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for BSS. The authors correctly argue that while AMR and SDP support some properties, they fail to address the full set of requirements. This is a meaningful contribution to semantic parsing research.

2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides comprehensive experiments comparing BSP with conversion-based approaches (using UPARSE, MaltParser, and LSTM parsers) on both in-domain (Wikipedia) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) data. The ablation study on feature sets provides valuable insights into what contributes to performance.

3. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper effectively explains why BSS is important, with clear examples of each structural property (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) and why they matter for semantic representation. The comparison with related work (AMR, SDP) is well-structured.

4. **Practical Contribution:** The authors make their code publicly available, which is a significant service to the research community. The paper also demonstrates that UCCA can be parsed with 69.2% primary F-score, which is promising given UCCA's inter-annotator agreement of 80-85%.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of the Parsing Approach:** While the paper claims to be the first to support BSS, the actual parsing approach (transition-based with new transitions) is largely built upon existing work in dependency DAG parsing and discontinuous constituency parsing. The core parsing methodology isn't particularly novel - the main contribution is the adaptation to BSS, not the parsing algorithm itself. The paper would benefit from more clearly articulating how the new transitions specifically address the unique challenges of BSS.

2. **Ad-Hoc Evaluation Metrics:** The authors define their own evaluation metrics for BSS, which is necessary given the lack of standard metrics. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why these metrics are appropriate for semantic parsing tasks or how they relate to downstream applications. The connection to practical utility isn't sufficiently established.

3. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides strong quantitative results but lacks qualitative analysis of the parser's behavior. Without examples of correct and incorrect parses, it's difficult to understand the nature of the errors and where the approach succeeds or fails. This would greatly strengthen the paper's contribution.

4. **Limited Comparison with Alternative Semantic Parsing Approaches:** The paper focuses on comparing with conversion-based approaches but doesn't adequately compare with other semantic parsing frameworks that might support some of the BSS properties. For instance, how does BSP compare to AMR parsers that have been developed for similar tasks?

5. **Scalability and Language Coverage Concerns:** The evaluation is limited to English, with relatively small training data (only 650 sentences). The paper doesn't address how well the approach scales to larger datasets or different languages, which is a significant limitation for a work claiming to support "broad-coverage" parsing.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims BSP is the first parser to support all three BSS properties. How does it specifically handle coordination structures compared to other semantic parsers that might support some aspects of BSS?

2. The evaluation metric is novel and ad-hoc. How does the paper's metric correlate with practical downstream semantic parsing tasks, and why is it superior to existing metrics for related structures?

3. The paper mentions that UCCA has inter-annotator agreement of 80-85%. How does the parser's performance compare to this upper bound, and what does this suggest about the potential for improvement?

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid contribution that fills a clear gap in semantic parsing research by providing the first parser for BSS. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluates its approach, and makes a practical contribution by releasing code. However, the novelty is somewhat limited to the adaptation of existing parsing techniques to a new problem space, rather than introducing fundamentally new parsing methods. The ad-hoc evaluation metrics and lack of qualitative analysis are significant weaknesses that need addressing.

While the results (69.2% primary F-score) are promising, the paper would be significantly strengthened by more thorough comparison with alternative semantic parsing approaches, qualitative analysis of errors, and clearer justification for the evaluation metrics. The authors should also address the scalability concerns and language coverage limitations.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, but the authors should address the weaknesses regarding novelty of the parsing approach, evaluation metrics, and qualitative analysis to strengthen the paper's contribution. The work has clear value for the semantic parsing community, but needs to better articulate the specific innovations beyond adapting existing techniques to a new problem.
