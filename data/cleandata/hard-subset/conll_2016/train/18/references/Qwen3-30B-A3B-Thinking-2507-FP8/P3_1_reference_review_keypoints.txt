- Introduces BSP, a transition-based parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) and the first parser for UCCA supporting multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuous units.
- Explores conversion-based approaches from constituency and dependency formalisms to assess adapting existing parsers to BSS.
- Proposes a novel evaluation metric for BSS that distinguishes primary and remote edges and evaluates both in- and out-of-domain performance.

- Clearly identifies a gap: no existing parser supports all three BSS properties simultaneously, illustrated with motivating examples.
- Provides a comprehensive evaluation framework including in/out-of-domain tests and upper bounds for conversion approaches.
- Details well-motivated conversion procedures between UCCA and other formalisms that clarify representation relationships.
- Introduces UCCA-specific parser features (remote-edge handling and ratio regularizer) as useful extensions to transition-based parsing.

- Overstates novelty by claiming to be first for BSS parsing despite prior SDP work supporting multiple parents and discontinuities, and mischaracterizing SDP’s handling of non-terminals.
- Insufficiently compares UCCA to related formalisms (AMR, SDP) and fails to justify why UCCA is superior for broad-coverage parsing.
- Modest empirical results (best 69.2% F1 on primary edges) are lower than other frameworks and lack analysis explaining the gap to state-of-the-art.
- Omits discussion of limitations, notably handling of implicit units, scalability to more complex phenomena, and language variation beyond English.
- Raises concerns about the new evaluation metric’s advantages over standard measures and lacks comparisons demonstrating practical benefits.

- Clarify the claim that SDP “disallows non-terminal nodes” given SDP’s application to coordination structures.
- Explain how UCCA specifically addresses challenges unmet by AMR or SDP and provide evidence for UCCA’s claimed advantages for broad-coverage parsing.
- Provide a rigorous analysis of the gap between parser performance and theoretical/maximal performance rather than relying on IAA as an upper bound.
- Justify the treatment and emphasis of remote-edge results (15.3% F1) and whether this should be a highlighted contribution.

- Overall: a valuable first parser for UCCA with strong evaluation and conversion work, but novelty claims are overstated and empirical/significance shortcomings limit impact.
- Paper would be strengthened by clearer comparisons to related formalisms, deeper error/limitation analysis, and justification of the new evaluation metric.
