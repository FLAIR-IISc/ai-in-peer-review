

# Peer Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of semantic parsing that supports "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" (BSS), defined by three key properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units. The authors present two complementary approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing that transforms BSS into more restricted representations (dependency trees or constituency trees) and uses existing parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly handles BSS.

The main contributions, in order of strength, are:
1. The first parser supporting all three properties of BSS simultaneously (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units)
2. A comprehensive evaluation framework for BSS parsing, including a novel evaluation measure that distinguishes between primary and remote edges
3. A systematic comparison of conversion-based approaches versus direct BSS parsing, providing insights into the trade-offs between representation formats

## Strengths

1. **Significant gap addressed**: The authors correctly identify a critical gap in semantic parsing literature. While prior work has addressed one or two of the BSS properties, no existing parser supports all three simultaneously. The paper's claim that "to our knowledge, no existing parser for a grounded semantic annotation scheme supports the combination of these criteria" is well-supported, making this a genuine contribution to the field.

2. **Clear evaluation framework**: The paper introduces a meaningful evaluation measure that accounts for the unique properties of BSS, particularly distinguishing between primary and remote edges. This is essential for meaningful comparison, as standard parsing metrics (like PARSEVAL) are inadequate for DAG structures. The authors' measure collapses to standard metrics for tree structures, showing appropriate theoretical grounding.

3. **Practical demonstration**: The authors demonstrate that BSS parsing is feasible, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score on the UCCA English corpus. This is particularly significant given that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85%, suggesting the approach is approaching human-level performance for this task. The paper provides a solid foundation for future work.

4. **Comprehensive experimental comparison**: The paper thoroughly compares conversion-based approaches (to constituency and dependency trees) with direct BSS parsing. This comparison reveals important insights about the trade-offs between representation formats and parsing approaches, which is valuable for the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison with alternative semantic representations**: The paper mentions AMR and other representations but fails to provide a substantial comparison. Given that AMR is the most widely used semantic representation, it's surprising the authors don't compare their approach to AMR parsing techniques or discuss why BSS is superior for their intended applications. This weakens the paper's contribution.

2. **Conversion-based approach outperforms the novel parser**: The results show that conversion to dependency trees with the LSTM parser achieves 69.2% F-score, while the direct BSP approach achieves only 59% for primary edges. The conversion-based approach is simpler and yields better results, which undermines the main motivation for developing the novel BSP parser. The paper doesn't adequately explain why the authors believe BSP is valuable despite this.

3. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to English, despite UCCA's stated cross-linguistic applicability. The authors claim UCCA has been applied to multiple languages, yet they don't test their approach on non-English data. This limits the perceived significance of the contribution.

4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper provides overall F-scores but no detailed error analysis. Without understanding where the parser fails (e.g., specific constructions like coordination or discontinuous units), it's difficult to assess the limitations of the approach or guide future improvements.

5. **Novelty of BSP is overstated**: While BSP is novel for BSS parsing, its core approach builds heavily on existing work (e.g., Maier 2015 for discontinuous parsing). The paper doesn't sufficiently highlight what's truly new about BSP compared to prior transition-based parsers for DAGs or discontinuous structures.

## Questions to Authors

1. Given that conversion to dependency trees with LSTM achieves better results than BSP, what is the primary advantage of developing a specialized parser like BSP rather than using conversion-based approaches?

2. How does the performance of BSP compare to direct parsers for other semantic representations (like AMR) that might achieve similar coverage with different representation choices?

3. The paper mentions UCCA's applicability to multiple languages, yet evaluation is limited to English. Could you provide an estimate of how much performance might degrade on other languages, and what additional challenges might arise?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a solid paper that addresses a meaningful gap in semantic parsing. The core contribution of supporting all three BSS properties simultaneously is valuable, and the evaluation framework is well-constructed. However, the paper's main claim is weakened by the fact that conversion-based approaches outperform the novel parser, and the lack of comparison with alternative semantic representations. With better justification for the BSP approach and more comprehensive evaluation, this would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, it makes a useful but incremental contribution to semantic parsing.

The paper's value is primarily in demonstrating that BSS parsing is feasible and providing a clear evaluation framework, rather than in the specific parser implementation. With the authors addressing the weaknesses noted above, this could be a strong contribution to CONLL.
