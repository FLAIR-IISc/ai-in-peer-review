

# Peer Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel transition-based semantic parser (BSP) designed to handle Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which require three key structural properties: multiple parents (to represent shared arguments between predicates), non-terminal nodes (for complex units like coordination), and discontinuous units (for phrasal verbs and multi-word expressions). The authors argue that no existing parser supports all three properties simultaneously, with UCCA being the only scheme that does so but lacking a parser. The paper introduces BSP as the first parser directly handling BSS, while also exploring conversion-based approaches to simpler formalisms (dependency trees and constituency trees) for comparison.

## Main Contributions
1. **First parser directly supporting all three BSS properties** without lossy conversion to simpler formalisms (previously, conversion-based approaches would lose remote edges or non-terminal nodes)
2. **Novel transition-based framework** specifically designed for BSS with new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features (e.g., ratio feature to regularize node creation)
3. **Comprehensive evaluation** on UCCA-annotated data showing BSP achieves 69.2% F1 on primary edges (vs. 69.2% for conversion-based approaches) while uniquely handling remote edges (13.3% F1)

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant gap in semantic parsing literature**: The authors convincingly argue that no existing parser supports all three structural properties simultaneously, with the UCCA representation being the only scheme that does so. Their examples of multiple parents (shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (coordination), and discontinuous units (phrasal verbs) are well-chosen and demonstrate a genuine need for the proposed approach.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper provides a thorough justification for UCCA as the appropriate representation, citing cross-linguistic applicability, rapid annotation capability, and semantic stability across translations. This makes a compelling case for why UCCA is a suitable target for broad-coverage semantic parsing.

3. **Well-executed evaluation**: The comparison between direct parsing (BSP) and conversion-based approaches (to constituency/dependency trees) is comprehensive. The paper demonstrates that BSP uniquely handles remote edges (which are lost in conversion), achieving 13.3% F1 on these edges compared to 0% for conversion-based methods. The out-of-domain results (BSP achieving 13.6% F1 on remote edges) further demonstrate robustness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient justification for the three structural properties**: While the authors provide examples of the three properties, they don't sufficiently demonstrate that these properties are *necessary* for broad-coverage semantic parsing. Without showing how these structures improve performance on downstream tasks (e.g., machine translation, summarization), it's unclear whether the added complexity of BSS is justified.

2. **Evaluation metric limitations**: The authors define a new metric for comparing DAG structures, but don't adequately justify why it's superior to existing metrics. The metric essentially collapses to PARSEVAL when graphs are trees, but the paper doesn't discuss how it handles the DAG nature of UCCA (e.g., how it accounts for multiple parents in the evaluation). This weakens the claimed performance gains.

3. **Lack of comparison to alternative representations**: The paper doesn't compare UCCA with other representations that might support the three properties (e.g., SDP with modifications for non-terminal nodes). Without this comparison, it's unclear whether UCCA is the optimal representation for the task, or whether the problem could be solved more simply with another formalism.

4. **Underreported human performance benchmark**: The authors mention UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80-85% F1) but don't clearly state how BSP's 69.2% F1 compares to this upper bound. Is the parser at 80% of human performance? This context is critical for understanding the significance of the results.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims UCCA is "the only such scheme supporting them" (all three properties), but SDP (SemEval 2015 task) also supports multiple parents and discontinuous units. How does UCCA differ from SDP in a way that makes it uniquely suitable for a direct parser, and why couldn't SDP be extended to handle non-terminal nodes?

2. The evaluation metric is novel but not well-justified. How does your metric handle the case when a predicted edge has the correct label and yield but the wrong parent (which would be impossible in a tree but possible in a DAG)? And how does it compare to other DAG evaluation metrics used in the literature?

3. The paper states BSP achieves 69.2% F1 on primary edges, but doesn't provide the human upper bound for this specific metric. Given that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85% F1, what percentage of the human upper bound does BSP achieve?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing the first parser that directly handles all three structural properties of BSS. The technical approach is sound, and the evaluation provides useful insights about the limitations of conversion-based approaches. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the justification for the three structural properties and the evaluation metric. With these improvements, the work would represent a substantial contribution to the field of semantic parsing.
