

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the first parser for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" (BSS), which support three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units (for semantic units spanning non-contiguous text). The authors present two complementary approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing (converting BSS to dependency trees or constituency trees, then using existing parsers), and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly handles all three properties. The paper's primary contributions are:

1. The first parser that directly supports all three properties of BSS through a novel transition-based approach.
2. A thorough evaluation of conversion-based approaches to assess the feasibility of existing technology for BSS parsing.
3. The first experimental work on parsing UCCA-annotated data, providing a benchmark for future research.

## Strengths

1. **Significant theoretical contribution**: The paper identifies a clear gap in the semantic parsing literature - no existing parser supports all three structural properties of BSS simultaneously. The authors successfully address this gap with BSP, which is a meaningful contribution to the field. The paper's thorough explanation of the three structural properties and their importance provides valuable context for the work.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The authors provide a detailed comparison between conversion-based approaches and their novel BSP parser, including ablation studies of different feature sets. The out-of-domain testing demonstrates robustness, and the upper bound analysis for conversion approaches provides important context for interpreting results. The results (nearly 70% F-score) are reasonable given the challenging nature of the task.

3. **Practical value**: The paper makes all converters and parsers publicly available, which will be valuable for the community. The paper also provides a clear roadmap for future work, including potential improvements through LSTM-based classifiers, which could significantly boost performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in the parsing approach**: While the application to BSS is novel, the transition system itself is largely adapted from existing work (Maier 2015, Tokgöz and Eryiğit 2015). The paper does not sufficiently distinguish between the existing parsing techniques and the novel aspects specific to BSS. The contribution appears more like an application of existing methods to a new domain rather than a methodological innovation.

2. **Questionable evaluation metric**: The authors define a custom evaluation metric based on edge yields, which is not standard in semantic parsing. The paper does not adequately justify why this metric is better than existing measures (e.g., those used in AMR parsing), nor does it compare with standard metrics. This makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the reported 70% F-score.

3. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with only a few parsers (MaltParser, UPARSE, LSTM), but doesn't include more recent state-of-the-art semantic parsers (e.g., AMR parsers that achieve over 80% F-score). This omission makes it hard to gauge how competitive BSP is with the current best approaches for semantic parsing.

4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper provides no detailed error analysis to understand where the parser fails. Without this, it's difficult to identify specific weaknesses or provide targeted suggestions for improvement. The paper mentions that "the quality of the results is underscored by UCCA's inter-annotator agreement of 80-85%," but doesn't analyze the gap between parser performance and human agreement.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the quality of the results is underscored by UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80-85% F-score." However, the paper doesn't explicitly compare the parser's performance with the human agreement rate on the same test set. Could the authors clarify how the parser's F-score compares to the human agreement rate on their test data?

2. The authors mention that "applying a similar approach to BSP is likely to improve results" by using an LSTM-based classifier. Could they provide more specific details about how this would be implemented and what performance gains might be expected?

3. The paper claims that their conversion-based approaches lose information, but doesn't provide examples of the specific structures that are lost. Could the authors provide a few concrete examples from their dataset showing where conversion to trees causes information loss?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing the first parser for BSS, addressing an important gap in semantic parsing research. The work is well-executed, with thorough evaluation and clear presentation. However, the novelty is somewhat limited to the application of existing techniques to a new domain rather than the development of new parsing methods. The evaluation methodology also requires further justification.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the evaluation metric justification, strengthen the comparison with state-of-the-art parsers, and include a more detailed error analysis. These improvements would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and make it more compelling for the CONLL audience.
