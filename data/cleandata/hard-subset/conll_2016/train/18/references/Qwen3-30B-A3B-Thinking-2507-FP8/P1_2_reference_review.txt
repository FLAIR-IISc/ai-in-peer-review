

# Peer Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to semantic parsing that supports Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), defined by three key properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments/relations), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units (for constructions like phrasal verbs). The authors identify that no existing parser supports all three properties simultaneously. They propose two complementary approaches: (1) a conversion-based approach that transforms BSS into dependency trees or constituency trees and uses existing parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser) that directly handles all three properties.

The main contributions I identify, in order of strength, are:
1. The first parser that directly supports all three structural properties of BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units), which the authors convincingly argue is a significant gap in current semantic parsing research.
2. A comprehensive evaluation framework for BSS, including a new evaluation metric that distinguishes between primary and remote edges, which is necessary for proper assessment of these structures.
3. A detailed analysis of the trade-offs between conversion-based approaches and direct parsing, demonstrating that while conversion-based methods lose information (particularly regarding remote edges), direct parsing can recover some of this information.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Identification of a Significant Gap**: The paper effectively demonstrates that no existing parser supports all three structural properties of BSS simultaneously. This is a substantial contribution to the field, as most existing semantic parsers either avoid grounding (like AMR) or fail to support one or more of these properties (like SDP, which lacks non-terminal nodes). The authors' argument is well-supported with examples and references to related work.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation and Analysis**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of both conversion-based methods and their novel BSP parser across multiple settings (in-domain, out-of-domain). The authors carefully analyze the trade-offs between different approaches, including the information loss in conversion-based methods. The results showing that BSP can predict some remote edges (which conversion methods cannot) is particularly significant.

3. **Practical Implementation and Availability**: The authors make all converters and parsers publicly available upon publication, which will greatly benefit the community. The paper also provides sufficient technical details for reproducibility, including the transition set, feature templates, and training procedures.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Justification for the Novel Parser Approach**: While BSP achieves 69.2% F-score on primary edges, the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why a direct transition-based approach is better than simply using a more sophisticated classifier with conversion-based methods. The results show that the LSTM parser (which uses a different classifier) outperforms BSP by 9.1% F-score, suggesting that the main bottleneck is the classifier rather than the parsing approach itself. The authors should clarify why they believe BSP is the right direction rather than improving the classifier for conversion-based approaches.

2. **Ad Hoc Evaluation Metric**: The authors define their own evaluation metric for BSS, which is not standard in the field. While they justify it based on the need to handle remote edges and discontinuous units, it's not clear how this metric relates to human judgments or other evaluation frameworks. The paper would be stronger if they could demonstrate that their metric correlates with human annotation quality or other established metrics.

3. **Lack of Comparison with Alternative Representations**: The paper mentions related frameworks like AMR and SDP but doesn't provide a thorough comparison of how UCCA (and thus BSP) relates to these other representations. It would be valuable to understand whether the three properties of BSS are unique to UCCA or shared with other representations, and whether BSP could be adapted to parse other frameworks with similar properties.

4. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications**: The paper reports 69.2% F-score but doesn't discuss what this means for real-world applications. For many NLP tasks, this might be insufficient, and the authors should address the practical utility of their approach. They also don't discuss computational complexity or scalability to larger datasets.

5. **Insufficient Treatment of Domain Adaptation**: The out-of-domain results (20K Leagues corpus) are presented but not thoroughly analyzed. The authors should discuss why the performance drops (e.g., from 69.2% to 57.1% F-score) and what specific challenges the out-of-domain data presents for BSS parsing.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that UCCA has inter-annotator agreement of 80-85% F-score, which the authors suggest is an upper bound. How does this compare to the inter-annotator agreement for other semantic representation schemes like AMR or SDP? Is this higher agreement a key advantage of UCCA that justifies the development of BSP?

2. The LSTM parser achieves 9.1% higher F-score than the arc-standard MaltParser, differing only in the classifier. Why not use the LSTM classifier with the BSP parser (rather than the simpler classifier used in BSP)? Would this lead to significantly better results, and if so, why wasn't this explored?

3. The paper mentions that UCCA has been demonstrated to be cross-linguistically applicable. Have the authors tested their parser on non-English UCCA-annotated data to validate this claim? If not, what would be the main challenges in adapting BSP to other languages?

4. The conversion to dependency trees loses considerable information due to the loss of non-terminal nodes. Have the authors considered using more sophisticated conversion procedures (e.g., as in Kong et al., 2015) to better preserve this information, rather than relying on their simple rule-based inverse conversion?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper addresses a significant gap in semantic parsing research and provides a well-motivated, technically sound approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures. While the results are promising, the paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the justification for their direct parsing approach and the evaluation metric. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address these concerns.
