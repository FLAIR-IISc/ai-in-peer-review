

# Peer Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the first parser that directly supports Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which require three key properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments between predicates), non-terminal nodes (for complex semantic units without clear heads), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors propose a transition-based parser called BSP that directly handles UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation) structures, which support all three properties. The paper also explores conversion-based approaches that transform UCCA structures into constituency trees or dependency trees for parsing with existing tools.

The main contributions, in order of importance, are:
1. First direct parser for BSS that supports all three structural properties without conversion to more restrictive representations
2. Novel transition-based parsing framework (BSP) with specialized transitions and features for UCCA parsing
3. Comprehensive evaluation showing BSP achieves ~70% labeled F-score on UCCA structures, demonstrating the feasibility of direct BSS parsing

## Strengths

1. **Significant Problem Identification**: The paper correctly identifies a critical gap in semantic parsing research - the lack of parsers that support all three structural properties of BSS. Most existing semantic parsers either avoid grounding (like AMR) or fail to support non-terminal nodes (like SDP), making this a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors provide a clear and compelling motivation for BSS through well-chosen examples.

2. **Well-Motivated Technical Approach**: The transition-based approach is well-chosen given the success of this paradigm in syntactic and semantic parsing. The authors' extension of transition-based parsing with new transitions (LEFT/RIGHT-EDGEX, LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features (especially the ratio feature) is well-designed and directly addresses the challenges of BSS parsing. The conversion-based experiments provide a useful baseline for understanding the limitations of existing technology.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The evaluation is thorough, with careful comparison of conversion-based approaches, in-domain and out-of-domain testing, and feature ablation studies. The authors provide clear metrics for evaluating BSS structures, which is particularly valuable since no standard evaluation measures exist for these representations. The results show that BSP can predict remote edges (unlike conversion-based approaches), which is a significant advantage.

4. **Clear Positioning in the Field**: The paper does an excellent job of situating itself within the broader landscape of semantic parsing research, distinguishing between UCCA, AMR, and SDP, and explaining why BSS is important. The discussion of UCCA's cross-linguistic applicability and semantic stability in translation provides strong motivation for their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Performance Comparison**: The paper lacks a meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art semantic parsers. While BSP achieves ~70% F-score, it doesn't compare with the best AMR parsers (which typically achieve 70-75% F-score on their own evaluation metrics). The paper mentions that the LSTM parser outperforms MaltParser by 9.1%, but doesn't provide a direct comparison with the best semantic parsers. Without this context, it's difficult to assess how much progress the paper represents.

2. **Lack of Error Analysis**: The paper provides no detailed error analysis, which would be valuable for understanding the specific challenges of BSS parsing. For example, it would be useful to know whether the parser struggles more with remote edges, non-terminal nodes, or discontinuous units. This analysis would help identify the most promising directions for future improvement.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Out-of-Domain Performance**: While the paper reports out-of-domain results, it doesn't analyze why the parser performs differently on the 20K Leagues corpus. The paper states that out-of-domain results are "largely comparable" but doesn't discuss the specific challenges of the out-of-domain data or how the parser might be adapted to handle them better.

4. **Overlooked Related Work**: The paper claims UCCA is the only scheme supporting all three BSS properties, but doesn't consider other semantic frameworks that might also support these properties. For instance, Meaning-Text Theory and the Prague Dependency Treebank handle some of these properties, and their relationship to UCCA could be clarified.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the BSP parser's performance compare to the best existing semantic parsers (e.g., AMR parsers) on their respective evaluation metrics? Is the 70% F-score competitive with the state-of-the-art for semantic parsing?

2. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, particularly regarding the types of errors made on remote edges and discontinuous units, to help identify the most promising directions for future improvement?

3. The paper mentions that conversion-based approaches lose information when converting to trees, but doesn't quantify how much information is lost. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the information loss during conversion, especially regarding remote edges?

4. The paper states that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85% F-score, which is higher than the parser's 70% F-score. Could you discuss whether the parser is approaching the human performance ceiling or if there's significant room for improvement?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing the first parser that directly supports all three structural properties of BSS. The technical approach is well-motivated, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the work has clear potential for application in semantic parsing tasks. However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art semantic parsers and the absence of detailed error analysis are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. With these issues resolved, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field of semantic parsing.

The paper demonstrates that BSS parsing is feasible and provides a solid foundation for future work in this area, particularly as the authors note the potential for LSTM-based improvements to the local classifier. The work also makes a valuable contribution by clarifying the relationship between different semantic representation schemes and highlighting the importance of grounding in semantic structures.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above).
