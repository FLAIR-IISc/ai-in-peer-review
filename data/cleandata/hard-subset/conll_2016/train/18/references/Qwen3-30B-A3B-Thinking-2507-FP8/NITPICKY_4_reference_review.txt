

# Review

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors present a conversion-based approach to leverage existing parsers and introduce a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly handles BSS. The main contributions claimed are: (1) the first parser for BSS, (2) conversion protocols between UCCA and related formalisms, and (3) a novel transition-based parser for BSS. 

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses an important gap in semantic parsing literature by proposing a parser for a representation that supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units simultaneously. The UCCA representation is well-motivated and has demonstrated cross-linguistic applicability.

2. The conversion-based approach provides a useful baseline and demonstrates that existing technology can be adapted to handle BSS with reasonable performance, especially when converting to constituency trees.

3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both in-domain and out-of-domain settings, and the results provide concrete directions for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Numerous formatting and citation inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "Abend and Rappoport.,? 2013" instead of "Abend and Rappoport, 2013"), inconsistent spacing around punctuation, and incorrect use of acronyms. The references section is particularly problematic with inconsistent formatting that would require significant editing. These are not minor issues in a conference submission.

2. **Critical typo in data split description**: The paper states "passages 701– 695 as in-domain test set" (Section 5), which is a clear typographical error (701-695 is backwards). This undermines the credibility of the experimental setup description.

3. **Algorithmic errors**: Algorithm 1 contains a critical error: "h(u)← argminv Priority(`c(u, v));" should use "argmax" instead of "argmin" to select the highest priority edge label, which contradicts the paper's description of "h(u) its child with the highest edge label." This error would affect the implementation of the conversion procedure.

4. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper inconsistently uses "Broad-coverage" vs "Broadcoverage" (e.g., "Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS)" vs "Broadcoverage Semantic Structures" in the title). The paper also fails to define the acronym BSS when first introduced, which is a basic requirement in academic writing.

5. **Confusing statements about results**: The paper claims "BSP obtains comparable F-scores to MaltParser and UPARSE" (Section 6), but Table 2 shows BSP at 62.4% F-score for primary edges versus MaltParser arc-eager at 63.9%. The difference is minor (1.5%), but the paper presents it as "comparable" without statistical significance testing. Furthermore, the paper claims the LSTM parser "obtains 9.1% F-score higher than the arc-standard MaltParser," but the difference is 73.2% - 63.4% = 9.8%, not 9.1%.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "the quality of the results is underscored by UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80–85% F-score on primary edges (Abend and Rappoport, 2013)." However, this citation does not appear in the references with the correct title. Please clarify where this inter-annotator agreement score is reported.

2. In Algorithm 1, the paper states "h(u) its child with the highest edge label" but uses "argmin" in the algorithm. Please clarify whether this is a typo or whether the priority function is defined in reverse.

3. The paper states "the conversion to dependency format loses considerably more information, since all non-terminal nodes are lost and have to be reconstructed by a simple rule-based inverse conversion." Please provide more details about the rule-based reconstruction procedure, as this is a critical part of the conversion approach.

## Additional Comments

The paper has significant potential but is marred by numerous minor errors that suggest poor proofreading. These issues are not trivial—they affect the reproducibility of the work (e.g., the algorithmic error in Algorithm 1) and undermine the paper's credibility (e.g., the data split typo). While the core idea is novel and potentially valuable, the numerous formatting inconsistencies, citation errors, and technical inaccuracies prevent this from being a strong submission for CONLL. The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading pass before resubmission.
