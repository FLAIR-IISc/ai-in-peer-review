

# Peer Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the first parser supporting all three structural properties of Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS): multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units. The authors claim this is a significant gap in semantic parsing literature, as existing parsers either support some properties or avoid grounding. The paper proposes two complementary approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing (converting BSS to dependency trees or constituency trees for existing parsers), and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) directly supporting BSS properties.

The main contributions I see are:
1. First parser that simultaneously supports all three BSS properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units)
2. Clear formalization of the three structural properties and their importance for broad-coverage semantic parsing
3. Demonstration that conversion-based approaches can serve as effective baselines for BSS parsing

## Strengths

1. **Correct identification of a significant gap**: The paper correctly identifies a genuine limitation in current semantic parsing literature. Most semantic parsers (including AMR, SDP, and grounded semantic dependency parsers) fail to support all three BSS properties simultaneously. The authors' claim that "no existing parser for a grounded semantic annotation scheme supports the combination of these criteria" is well-supported by their thorough related work analysis.

2. **Clear formalization of requirements**: The paper provides a precise definition of the three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) and demonstrates their necessity with compelling examples (Figures 1a-c). This formalization is valuable for the field as it provides a clear benchmark for evaluating semantic parsers.

3. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper includes comprehensive experiments comparing conversion-based approaches with the novel BSP parser, both in-domain and out-of-domain. The ablation study on features (Table 3) provides valuable insights into what features contribute to performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the BSP parser**: The main contribution is the BSP parser, but the approach appears to be a straightforward extension of existing transition-based parsing techniques. The new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) are logical extensions of existing work (e.g., Maier, 2015) rather than significant methodological innovations. Crucially, the conversion-based approach using LSTM achieves higher performance (73.2% F-score) than the BSP (62.4% F-score) on primary edges, suggesting the novel parser is not necessary for good results. The paper fails to convincingly argue why the direct approach is needed when conversion-based methods outperform it.

2. **Ad-hoc evaluation metric**: The authors define a new metric based on edge yields (y(e) = set of terminals in descendants), which is not standard in semantic parsing. While they argue this is necessary for evaluating BSS, they don't demonstrate how this metric better captures semantic accuracy than standard measures. The metric collapses to standard PARSEVAL for tree structures, but the paper doesn't show how it improves evaluation for DAG structures.

3. **Inadequate comparison with DAG parsers**: The paper mentions dependency DAG parsers (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008; Tokgöz and Eryiğit, 2015) but doesn't compare with them directly, despite their ability to handle multiple parents. This omission weakens the claim of novelty, as the BSP's ability to handle multiple parents might not be as novel as presented.

4. **Modest results**: The 69.2% F-score for primary edges (BSP) is not significantly better than the 73.2% achieved by the conversion-based LSTM parser. Given that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85%, the results leave considerable room for improvement, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of error patterns to guide future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims BSP is the first parser supporting all three BSS properties, but how does it compare with other DAG-based parsers that handle multiple parents? Could the BSP's improvements be achieved by adapting existing DAG parsers rather than creating a new transition set?

2. The conversion-based approach with LSTM achieves higher primary edge F-score (73.2%) than BSP (62.4%). Why is this the case, and why is the direct approach necessary? The authors should clarify whether their direct approach offers advantages beyond the ability to predict remote edges (which the conversion-based approaches cannot do).

3. The evaluation metric is defined as "labeled precision and recall" based on edge yields, but how does this metric correlate with semantic accuracy? Could the authors provide examples where this metric identifies errors that standard measures would miss?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a useful contribution by formally identifying the three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing and demonstrating the first parser that supports all three. However, the novelty of the BSP parser is limited, the results are modest, and the evaluation metric is not well-justified. The conversion-based approach (particularly with LSTM) outperforms the novel parser, raising questions about the necessity of the direct approach. While the paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature, the technical contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The work would be strengthened by a more compelling argument for the necessity of the direct approach and a better justification for the evaluation metric.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions above, particularly regarding the necessity of the BSP parser given the superior performance of conversion-based approaches. The paper has strong theoretical foundations but needs to better demonstrate the practical value of the proposed parser.
