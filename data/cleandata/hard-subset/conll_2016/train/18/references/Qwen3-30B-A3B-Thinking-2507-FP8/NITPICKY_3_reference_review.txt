

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper claims to present the first parser supporting all three structural properties required for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS): multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors propose a transition-based approach called Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) that directly handles these properties. They also present conversion-based approaches to adapt existing parsers (to dependency trees and constituency trees) for BSS parsing. As I see it, the main contributions are: (1) the BSP parser that supports all three structural properties, (2) conversion protocols between UCCA and other formalisms, and (3) experimental evaluation on UCCA-annotated corpora.

## Strengths
1. The paper correctly identifies an important gap in semantic parsing: the lack of parsers that support all three structural properties simultaneously (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units). This is a meaningful contribution to the field.
2. The conversion-based approach provides a reasonable baseline for assessing the feasibility of using existing technology for BSS parsing, which is a pragmatic first step.
3. The experimental results are promising (69.2% F-score on primary edges), suggesting that BSS parsing is viable and providing a baseline for future work.

## Weaknesses
1. **Unjustified novelty claim**: The paper states "to our knowledge, no existing parser for a grounded semantic annotation scheme supports the combination of these criteria" and claims "the only such scheme supporting them is UCCA." However, the paper fails to provide a thorough comparison of other semantic frameworks (AMR, MRS, DRT) to justify this claim. For instance, AMR supports multiple parents (in the form of multiple arguments) and discontinuous units (through complex relations), though it doesn't use non-terminal nodes. This lack of comparative analysis undermines their novelty claim.

2. **Non-standard evaluation metrics**: The authors define their own metrics for evaluating BSS parsing, which "collapse to the standard PARSEVAL constituency evaluation measure if Gp and Gg are trees." However, they don't justify why these metrics are appropriate for BSS, nor do they compare their metrics to standard semantic parsing metrics. This makes it difficult to assess the significance of their results.

3. **Critical citation and formatting errors**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies in the references (e.g., "Abend and Rappoport.,? 2013" vs. "Abend and Rappoport, 2013"), inconsistent citation styles, and a clear typo in the data split ("passages 701–695" instead of "701–750"). The figure caption for Figure 1a contains a typo ("After graduation P H ," instead of "After graduation P L ,"). These are not trivial issues and reflect poor attention to detail.

4. **Missing justification for key claims**: The paper states "UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80–85% F-score on primary edges" without providing a citation. This is a significant oversight, as this claim is central to their evaluation. Without a citation, it's impossible to verify this claim, which undermines their results.

5. **Incomplete technical justification**: The paper states that "the conversion to dependency format loses considerably more information, since all non-terminal nodes are lost and have to be reconstructed by a simple rule-based inverse conversion." However, they don't quantify how much information is lost or provide evidence that the rule-based reconstruction is reasonable. This lack of detail makes it hard to assess the validity of their conversion-based approach.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide a citation for the claim that "UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80–85% F-score on primary edges"? This is a crucial claim for evaluating your results.

2. How did you justify the specific conversion procedures (Algorithms 1 and 2) for converting between UCCA and other formalisms? Why were these specific procedures chosen over others?

3. In Table 2, the out-of-domain test set is described as "20K Leagues," but the paper states "passages 701–695" for the in-domain test set. This is clearly a typo (695 < 701). What is the correct test set range?

## Final Recommendation
While the paper addresses an important gap in semantic parsing, the numerous minor flaws, including critical citation errors, formatting inconsistencies, and unjustified claims, are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues before it can be considered for publication. The lack of comparative analysis with other semantic frameworks, the non-standard evaluation metrics without justification, and the formatting inconsistencies undermine the paper's credibility and the significance of its contributions.
