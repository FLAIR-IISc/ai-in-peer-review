

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing that directly supports all three structural properties required for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS): multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex units with no clear head), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors present two complementary approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing, where UCCA structures are converted to constituency trees or dependency trees and parsed with existing tools; and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed specifically for BSS.

The primary contribution is the first parser that directly supports all three BSS properties without relying on conversion to other representations. The secondary contribution is a thorough exploration of conversion-based approaches to assess the viability of existing technology for BSS parsing. The tertiary contribution is the detailed evaluation framework for BSS parsing, including a novel evaluation metric that accounts for the unique properties of BSS.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness of the Proposed Parser:** The authors provide a well-motivated extension of transition-based parsing to handle BSS. The BSP parser introduces appropriate transitions (NODE, LEFT-EDGEX, RIGHT-EDGEX, LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX, SWAP, FINISH) and features specifically designed for UCCA's structure. The parser is built on solid foundations from existing work in dependency and constituency parsing. The evaluation is comprehensive, including in-domain and out-of-domain testing, and the authors provide detailed ablation studies of their feature sets, demonstrating the importance of specific feature categories (e.g., disco features for discontinuous units).

2. **Novelty and Problem Identification:** The paper correctly identifies a significant gap in the literature: no existing parser supports all three BSS properties simultaneously. While some previous work has addressed subsets of these properties (e.g., SDP supports multiple parents and discontinuous units but not non-terminal nodes), the authors present the first parser that handles all three. This is a clear and meaningful contribution to semantic parsing.

3. **Clear Evaluation Framework:** The authors define appropriate evaluation metrics for BSS, which is crucial since standard metrics for tree structures don't directly apply. Their metric (based on edge yield) properly accounts for the unique properties of DAGs and discontinuous structures. The upper bounds for conversion-based approaches provide important context for interpreting the results, showing that conversion to dependency trees loses significant information (88.4% F-score upper bound for primary edges versus 69.2% achieved by BSP).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Existing BSS Work:** While the paper correctly identifies UCCA as the only scheme supporting all three properties, it lacks a thorough comparison with other BSS-like approaches. For instance, the paper mentions SDP (Broad-coverage Semantic Dependency Parsing) but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of how BSP handles specific SDP challenges (e.g., coordination structures like "John and Mary went home"). This weakens the claim of novelty by not fully situating the work within the broader BSS landscape.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Remote Edges:** The paper reports results for both primary and remote edges but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how the prediction of remote edges affects the semantic representation quality. The authors note that BSP achieves 13.3% F-score on remote edges (compared to 0% for conversion-based approaches), but don't explain how these remote edges contribute to the overall semantic understanding or whether they are necessary for downstream applications.

3. **Limited Domain and Language Coverage:** The paper only evaluates on English data (UCCA Wikipedia corpus and 20K Leagues corpus), despite the authors' claim that UCCA is "cross-linguistically applicable." This limits the scope of the contribution, as the paper doesn't demonstrate whether the approach generalizes to other languages with different syntactic structures or morphological complexity.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the BSP parser specifically handle coordination structures (e.g., "John and Mary went home") compared to the SDP approach mentioned in the background section, which faces difficulties with such constructions?

2. The paper reports 13.3% F-score on remote edges. Could the authors provide examples where the prediction of remote edges significantly improves the semantic representation, and discuss whether these edges are essential for practical applications of semantic parsing?

3. The authors claim UCCA is "cross-linguistically applicable" but only evaluate on English. What are the key challenges for extending BSP to other languages, and how might these be addressed in future work?

## Conclusion
This paper makes a meaningful contribution by presenting the first parser that directly supports all three structural properties required for BSS. The technical approach is sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the problem identification is correct. While the paper could strengthen its contribution by providing more detailed comparisons with existing BSS work and analyzing the impact of remote edges, the core contribution remains novel and technically sound. The work provides a solid foundation for future research in broad-coverage semantic parsing.
