1. Introduces BSP, the first parser supporting all three BSS properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units.  
2. Evaluates conversion-based approaches to quantify how much information existing parsers lose for BSS.  
3. Analyzes UCCA's suitability for broad-coverage semantic parsing.  
4. Clearly identifies a literature gap and uses examples to illustrate existing parsers' limitations.  
5. Proposes rigorous evaluation metrics (mutual edges, yields, and primary vs. remote edges).  
6. Strong practical contribution: code and data released; shows conversion methods lose remote and other information.  
7. Core method offers limited novelty—mainly extensions of prior transition-based parsers with minor new transitions.  
8. Comparison to prior work is insufficient, missing newer semantic parsers and lacking conversion-process detail.  
9. Lacks detailed error analysis—unclear which BSS properties (remote edges, discontinuities, non-terminals) cause most errors.  
10. Omits discussion of scalability and computational complexity for complex or long sentences.  
11. Requests clarification on the claim that AMR is “not grounded” and why AMR parsing fails to support the three BSS properties.  
12. Asks how BSP’s 69.2% primary F-score compares to UCCA inter-annotator agreement (80–85%) and whether the gap is parsing or annotation noise.  
13. Seeks evidence for how well inverse-conversion heuristics recover non-terminal structure versus chance.  
14. Asks how to incorporate an LSTM-based classifier into BSP and what improvements to expect.  
15. Overall: valuable and well-evaluated contribution addressing a clear gap, but incremental novelty and needs clearer comparisons and deeper error analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
