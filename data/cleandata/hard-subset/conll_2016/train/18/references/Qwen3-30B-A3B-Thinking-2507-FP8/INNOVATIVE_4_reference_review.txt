

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary
This paper introduces the concept of Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), defined by three key properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors claim this is the first parser to support all three properties simultaneously, presenting a transition-based parser called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser) that handles these structures. The paper also explores conversion-based approaches to leverage existing parsers by converting BSS to more standard representations (dependency or constituency trees).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Conceptual framework for broad-coverage semantic parsing** - The paper clearly defines BSS as a novel class of semantic structures that addresses important linguistic phenomena not supported by existing semantic parsing approaches. This conceptual contribution is significant because it identifies a gap in the field that has been overlooked.

2. **First parser supporting all three BSS properties** - The authors present BSP, the first transition-based parser designed to handle multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units simultaneously. This represents a genuine first-of-its-kind contribution to the field.

3. **Practical implementation and evaluation** - The paper provides a concrete implementation of BSP with detailed transition set and feature templates, along with thorough experiments on UCCA-annotated corpora, demonstrating that their approach can achieve reasonable performance (nearly 70% F-score) while supporting all three structural properties.

## Strengths

1. **Novel conceptual framework with clear gap identification**: The paper successfully identifies and defines a meaningful gap in semantic parsing that has not been addressed by previous work. The authors clearly articulate why BSS is important, providing concrete linguistic examples and demonstrating how existing approaches (like AMR or SDP) fall short on one or more of the three required properties. This conceptual contribution is exactly the kind of bold, novel direction I value as a reviewer.

2. **First-of-its-kind implementation**: The paper correctly claims to be the first to propose techniques for BSS parsing. Given that UCCA (the representation they use) has no parser, this is a genuine first. This is the kind of significant conceptual contribution that justifies acceptance even if the performance isn't state-of-the-art.

3. **Clear articulation of limitations and future work**: The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach, particularly the lossy nature of conversion-based methods and the performance ceiling (they note that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85% F-score, suggesting room for improvement). They also clearly outline paths for future work, including applying LSTM-based classifiers to their parser.

4. **Strong evaluation approach**: The authors define appropriate evaluation metrics for BSS, distinguishing between primary and remote edges, which is crucial for evaluating their novel structure. Their comparison with conversion-based approaches provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between different parsing strategies.

## Weaknesses

1. **Performance not state-of-the-art**: While the paper's conceptual contribution is strong, the actual performance (62.4% primary edge F-score) is not particularly impressive compared to state-of-the-art semantic parsing. However, as a reviewer who values novelty over methodological perfection, I'm willing to overlook this given the paper's conceptual contribution.

2. **Conversion-based approach is fundamentally limited**: The paper acknowledges that converting BSS to other representations is lossy, and the conversion-based methods achieve zero scores on remote edges. This limitation is inherent to the conversion approach rather than their parser, so it's not a major weakness for the main contribution.

3. **Limited language coverage**: The paper only evaluates on English data, despite UCCA being presented as cross-linguistically applicable. However, as a first step, this is understandable, and the authors correctly note that future work should explore multilingual applications.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The conceptual contribution of defining BSS and presenting the first parser to support all three required properties is significant and represents a genuine new direction in semantic parsing. While the performance is not state-of-the-art, this is a reasonable trade-off for the novelty and conceptual contribution. The paper clearly articulates the gap it's addressing, provides a concrete implementation, and offers a clear path for future improvement.

As a reviewer who values novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper to be exactly the kind of contribution that advances the field in meaningful ways. The authors have identified an important gap, defined a clear conceptual framework, and provided the first implementation to address it. This represents a significant step forward in semantic parsing, even if the current implementation has room for improvement.

I'm willing to overlook the relatively modest performance numbers (compared to state-of-the-art) because the conceptual contribution is so significant. The paper clearly demonstrates a new direction for semantic parsing that could influence future work in the field.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you expect the performance of your parser to improve with more sophisticated classifiers (like the LSTM-based classifier you mention as a potential improvement)? What would you expect the F-score to be with such a classifier?

2. Given that UCCA has inter-annotator agreement of 80-85% F-score, how do you expect your parser to approach this ceiling, and what would be the most promising directions for improvement?

3. The paper mentions that the conversion to dependency trees loses considerable information. What are your thoughts on whether a more sophisticated conversion procedure could preserve more of the BSS information?
