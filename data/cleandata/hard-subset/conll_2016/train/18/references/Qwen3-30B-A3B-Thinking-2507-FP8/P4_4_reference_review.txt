

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper introduces the first parser that directly supports all three structural properties required for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS): multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units (for constructions like phrasal verbs). The authors propose a transition-based parser called Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) that extends existing transition-based parsing techniques with new transitions and features specifically designed for UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation) structures. They also explore conversion-based approaches to assess how well existing parsers can handle BSS by converting UCCA structures to dependency or constituency trees.

The main contributions, in order of strength, are:
1. First parser that directly supports all three structural properties of BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units)
2. Novel transition-based parser (BSP) with new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX, SWAP) and features specifically for UCCA
3. Comprehensive evaluation showing that direct parsing achieves 69.2% labeled F1-score on primary edges, with 13.3% on remote edges

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant gap in the literature**: The paper effectively establishes that no existing parser supports the combination of multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors provide strong motivation by clearly illustrating the three structural properties with concrete examples (Figure 1) and showing how current semantic parsing approaches fail to handle these cases.

2. **Technical contribution to transition-based parsing**: The BSP parser introduces novel transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) that specifically address the need to distinguish between primary and remote edges. The feature set (Figure 3) is well-designed, incorporating both standard features from related work and novel UCCA-specific features (like the remote edge features). The authors demonstrate that their approach can predict remote edges (13.3% F1), which conversion-based methods cannot.

3. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation with both in-domain and out-of-domain testing, ablation studies of feature sets, and careful comparison with conversion-based approaches. The authors clearly define appropriate evaluation metrics for DAG structures (which differ from standard tree evaluation measures), and the results (Table 2) show the direct approach achieves competitive performance while handling the full BSS properties.

4. **Practical significance and clear future directions**: The results (69.2% F1 on primary edges) are promising, particularly since they approach the inter-annotator agreement (80-85%), suggesting the approach is viable. The authors correctly identify that applying LSTM-based classifiers to BSP (as done in the LSTM parser) would likely improve performance, providing a clear path for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core parsing approach**: While the BSP parser is novel in its specific application to UCCA and the three structural properties, the core transition-based parsing framework is not new (it builds on Nivre, 2003; Dyer et al., 2015; etc.). The technical contribution is primarily in the adaptation to UCCA rather than the parsing algorithm itself. This limits the paper's novelty claim.

2. **Performance not significantly better than conversion approaches**: The best direct approach (BSP) achieves 69.2% F1 on primary edges, which is comparable to the LSTM parser on dependency conversion (69.2% F1). The direct approach only adds the ability to predict remote edges (13.3% F1), which is a small fraction of the overall performance. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the direct approach is superior to conversion-based methods for practical applications.

3. **Lack of comparison with alternative semantic parsing approaches**: The authors don't compare with other semantic parsing approaches that might handle some BSS properties (e.g., approaches that handle discontinuous units or multiple parents). The paper claims "no existing parser" supports all three properties, but doesn't address whether other approaches handle some properties better than their conversion-based methods.

4. **Evaluation measure limitations**: The authors' evaluation measure based on edge yields is ad-hoc and not standard in semantic parsing. While they justify it by noting it collapses to PARSEVAL for trees, they don't provide evidence that it effectively captures semantic accuracy. A more detailed error analysis would have strengthened the evaluation.

5. **Insufficient discussion of failure modes**: The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the parser's errors, which would help understand the limitations of the approach and where improvements are most needed. This is particularly important given that the results (69.2% F1) are below the inter-annotator agreement (80-85%).

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the LSTM parser achieves the highest primary F-score (69.2%) when applied to dependency conversion. How much of this performance gain is due to the LSTM classifier versus the dependency conversion? Could a similar LSTM-based classifier applied to BSP (with remote edges) achieve similar gains?

2. The authors note that the inter-annotator agreement for UCCA is 80-85%, yet their best parser achieves 69.2% F1. What specific types of errors are most common in the parser's output that prevent it from reaching the inter-annotator agreement level?

3. The paper mentions that the conversion to dependency format "loses considerably more information" than constituency conversion. Could the authors elaborate on what specific information is lost in dependency conversion beyond the loss of remote edges and non-terminal nodes, and how this impacts semantic interpretation?

This paper makes a clear contribution to the field by introducing the first parser that directly supports all three structural properties of broad-coverage semantic structures. While the technical novelty is somewhat limited by building on existing transition-based parsing frameworks, the specific adaptation to UCCA and the demonstration that the approach is viable (approaching inter-annotator agreement levels) make it a valuable contribution to the field of semantic parsing. The main limitations are the modest performance gain over conversion-based methods and the lack of comparison with alternative semantic parsing approaches.
