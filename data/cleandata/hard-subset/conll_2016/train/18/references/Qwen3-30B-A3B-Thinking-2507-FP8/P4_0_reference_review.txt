

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing that supports "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" (BSS), defined by three structural properties: multiple parents (to represent shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (to represent complex semantic units), and discontinuous units (where semantic units span non-contiguous text spans). The authors argue that UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation) is the only semantic representation that supports all three properties, but lacks a parser. The paper presents two complementary approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing, where UCCA structures are converted to dependency trees or constituency trees for processing by existing parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three properties of BSS.

The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1. First parser that directly supports all three structural properties required for BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units).
2. A detailed technical description of the BSP transition-based parser with new transitions specifically designed for handling remote edges and non-terminal nodes.
3. A comprehensive evaluation showing that BSP achieves nearly 70% labeled F-score on primary edges, demonstrating the feasibility of direct BSS parsing.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant gap in the literature**: The paper convincingly identifies a gap in semantic parsing research—the lack of parsers that support all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing. The authors provide a thorough comparison with existing semantic representations (AMR, SDP) and clearly demonstrate why they fall short of supporting all three properties simultaneously. This is a well-motivated and significant contribution to the field.

2. **Technical soundness of the BSP approach**: The paper provides a detailed technical description of the BSP parser, including the new transition set, feature templates, and conversion procedures. The extension of transition-based parsing to handle BSS properties is technically sound and well-justified. The authors provide clear algorithmic descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) for converting between representations, which adds to the technical rigor of the work.

3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The evaluation is thorough, with both in-domain and out-of-domain testing on the UCCA Wikipedia corpus. The comparison between the direct BSP approach and conversion-based approaches (to constituency and dependency trees) provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between these methods. The results show that BSP achieves comparable performance to conversion-based methods on primary edges while also handling remote edges, which conversion methods cannot.

4. **Feature ablation study**: The paper includes a detailed feature ablation study that demonstrates the relative importance of different feature sets for BSP performance. This analysis provides valuable insights for future work and helps validate the authors' design choices.

5. **Practical contribution**: The authors plan to make their converters and parsers publicly available, which is a significant practical contribution to the community. This will allow other researchers to build upon their work and further develop BSS parsing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core technical approach**: While the authors present a novel parser for UCCA/BSS, the underlying transition-based parsing approach builds heavily on existing techniques for dependency parsing and constituency parsing. The key innovations (new transitions for remote edges, the ratio feature) are incremental rather than transformative. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why these specific extensions to existing transition-based parsing techniques are novel or non-trivial.

2. **Lack of standard evaluation metrics**: The authors define their own evaluation metrics for comparing BSS structures, which makes it difficult to compare their results with other semantic parsing systems that use standard metrics (e.g., AMR parsing metrics). While the authors provide a reasonable definition of their metrics, the absence of comparison to standard evaluation frameworks weakens the significance of their results.

3. **Insufficient corpus details**: The paper mentions using the "UCCA Wikipedia corpus" and "UCCA Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea English-French parallel corpus" but provides minimal details about these corpora. The size, domain, annotation quality, and distribution of the data are not sufficiently described, making it difficult to assess the significance of the reported results.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach. For example, it doesn't analyze how well their parser handles the most challenging cases (e.g., complex coordination structures, highly discontinuous units) or how performance varies with sentence length and complexity. This omission weakens the paper's contribution.

5. **Overstated claims about novelty**: The paper claims to be "first in proposing techniques for BSS parsing," but doesn't adequately address whether other existing semantic parsing approaches might implicitly support BSS properties. The comparison with AMR and SDP is limited, and the paper doesn't explore whether other grounded semantic representations might have similar capabilities.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "to our knowledge, no existing parser for a grounded semantic annotation scheme supports the combination of these criteria," but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with other parsers for grounded semantic representations. Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with parsers for other grounded semantic representations (e.g., for SDP)?

2. The evaluation metrics are specific to the UCCA representation. How would the results compare if evaluated using more standard semantic parsing metrics (e.g., those used for AMR parsing)?

3. The paper mentions UCCA's inter-annotator agreement of 80-85% F-score as an upper bound, but doesn't discuss how the parser's performance compares to this bound in more detail. Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison between their parser's performance and the inter-annotator agreement?
