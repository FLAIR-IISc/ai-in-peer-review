

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the first parser capable of handling Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which support three critical properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units (for non-contiguous phrases). The authors present two complementary approaches: a conversion-based method that transforms BSS into more restricted representations (dependency trees or constituency trees), and a novel transition-based parser called BSP that directly handles BSS structures.

The primary contributions are: (1) the first parser for BSS that supports all three structural properties; (2) the BSP parser with carefully designed transitions and features specifically for UCCA structures; and (3) a comprehensive evaluation demonstrating the feasibility of BSS parsing.

## Strengths

1. **Significant problem identification**: The paper correctly identifies a critical gap in semantic parsing literature—no existing parser supports all three properties of BSS. The authors provide clear examples (Figure 1) and literature review showing that UCCA (the only representation supporting all three) has no parser, while other approaches either lack grounding or fail to handle one or more structural properties. This is a strong justification for the work's necessity.

2. **Well-motivated and technically sound approach**: The BSP parser is well-designed, with a carefully constructed transition set (Figure 2) that addresses the unique challenges of BSS. The authors thoughtfully extend existing transition-based parsers with new transitions for remote edges and discontinuous units. The feature set (Figure 3) combines standard features with novel UCCA-specific features (e.g., remote edge features), demonstrating thorough understanding of the task.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The authors develop a new evaluation metric for BSS structures that distinguishes between primary and remote edges, providing a clear way to assess performance. The evaluation includes both in-domain and out-of-domain testing, conversion-based baselines, and feature ablation studies. The results (Table 2) show that BSP achieves 69.2% F-score on primary edges, with 13.3% on remote edges, significantly outperforming conversion-based approaches that achieve 0% on remote edges.

4. **Strong technical execution**: The paper provides clear algorithms for conversion procedures (Algorithms 1-2) and detailed experimental setup. The authors demonstrate that their approach is robust to domain shifts (comparable in-domain and out-of-domain results), and they provide a thorough analysis of the impact of different feature sets (Table 3).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with related semantic parsing work**: While the paper discusses AMR and SDP, it fails to directly compare with existing semantic parsers that might handle similar structures. For example, AMR parsers like Wang et al. (2015) handle non-terminal nodes and discontinuities (though with different representation), and the authors don't explain why their approach is superior or how it relates to these systems. This weakens the argument for the novelty of their contribution.

2. **Insufficient error analysis**: The paper reports quantitative results but lacks qualitative analysis of errors. Understanding why BSP fails (e.g., whether errors cluster around remote edges, discontinuous units, or specific grammatical constructions) would be valuable for future work. Without this, it's difficult to assess the practical limitations of the approach.

3. **Limited discussion of practical impact**: While the paper mentions potential applications (machine translation, summarization), it doesn't provide concrete examples of how BSS parsing would improve these tasks. The claim that "UCCA's merits in providing a cross-linguistically applicable, broad-coverage annotation will support ongoing efforts" lacks empirical support, making the practical significance less clear.

4. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper claims to be "first in proposing techniques for BSS parsing," but Oepen et al. (2015) in SemEval 2015 task 18 already addressed broad-coverage semantic dependency parsing, which supports discontinuous units and multiple parents (though it lacks non-terminal nodes). The paper should clarify how BSS differs from SDP and why their approach is needed despite this prior work.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the BSP parser specifically handle implicit units (mentioned in Section 2) that the paper states "are deferred to future work"? What would be the technical challenges in incorporating these?

2. The paper reports that the LSTM parser achieves 73.2% F-score on primary edges, significantly higher than BSP's 62.4%. Why didn't the authors explore incorporating LSTM into BSP, and how might this affect performance on remote edges?

3. The paper claims that "UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80-85% F-score" suggests their results (69.2%) are good. However, this upper bound might be misleading for a different task. Could you clarify how UCCA's inter-annotator agreement relates to the specific evaluation metric used in this paper?

4. The paper states that "no existing parser for a grounded semantic annotation scheme supports the combination of these criteria." How does this differ from the SDP work in SemEval 2015 (Oepen et al., 2015), which supports multiple parents and discontinuous units?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a significant gap in semantic parsing literature. The BSP parser is well-designed and demonstrates that BSS parsing is feasible. The work provides a solid foundation for future research in broad-coverage semantic parsing. While the novelty claim could be better justified and the practical impact could be more clearly articulated, the technical contribution is substantial and well-executed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above.

The paper meets CONLL's standards for technical contribution, evaluation, and clarity. It presents a clear problem, a thoughtful solution, and thorough evaluation, making it a strong contribution to the field.
