

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing that directly supports all three structural properties required for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS): multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors propose a transition-based parser called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser) that directly handles these properties, rather than relying on conversion from other representations. The main contributions are:

1. First parser that directly handles all three structural properties of BSS using a transition-based approach, addressing a gap in the literature where no existing parser supports all three criteria simultaneously.

2. A novel transition set and feature set specifically designed for UCCA semantic structures, including new transitions for handling remote edges (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) and discontinuous units (SWAP), along with UCCA-specific features.

3. Comprehensive evaluation demonstrating the limitations of conversion-based approaches (to dependency trees and constituency trees) and showing that a direct parsing approach can achieve reasonable performance on BSS parsing.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant gap in the literature:** The paper correctly identifies that no existing parser supports all three structural properties of BSS simultaneously, making this a genuinely novel contribution. The authors effectively demonstrate that while other semantic parsing approaches support some of these properties, none support all three together.

2. **Well-motivated technical approach:** The authors provide a thorough and detailed description of the transition set and feature templates specifically designed for BSS parsing. The transition set extends standard parsing transitions with new operations for handling remote edges and discontinuous units, while the feature templates incorporate both standard parsing features and novel UCCA-specific features. The technical descriptions are precise and well-justified.

3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, including in-domain and out-of-domain testing, and provides labeled precision, recall, and F-score for both primary and remote edges. The comparison with conversion-based approaches (to constituency trees and dependency trees) effectively demonstrates the limitations of indirect methods due to information loss during conversion.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited performance compared to human annotation:** The best results reported (69.2% labeled F-score for primary edges) fall significantly below the reported inter-annotator agreement (80-85%). While the authors acknowledge this gap, they do not provide sufficient analysis of why this gap exists or what specific types of errors are most common. This raises questions about the practical utility of the approach.

2. **Lack of qualitative analysis:** The paper focuses exclusively on quantitative metrics without providing any qualitative analysis of the parser's errors. Understanding the nature of the parser's mistakes would be valuable for assessing the approach's limitations and potential improvements.

3. **Insufficient comparison with related work:** The paper mentions SDP (Broad-coverage Semantic Dependency Parsing) as supporting some of the properties of BSS but does not provide a detailed comparison of how the authors' approach differs fundamentally from SDP. This weakens the claim that their approach is the first to support all three properties.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that UCCA has inter-annotator agreement of 80-85% for primary edges, but the best parser achieves only 69.2% F-score. What specific types of errors are most common in the parser's output that cause it to fall short of human agreement? 

2. The paper claims BSP is the first parser supporting all three properties of BSS, but SDP (Oepen et al., 2015) is mentioned as supporting "some of these properties." Could you clarify why SDP does not qualify as a BSS parser, and how the authors' approach differs fundamentally from SDP in handling all three properties?

3. The paper mentions that the LSTM-based parser achieves the highest primary F-score (73.2%), differing from MaltParser only in its classifier. Why not use the LSTM classifier for BSP directly, rather than the linear classifier? How much improvement would this likely yield, and why wasn't this explored in the paper?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with a clear presentation of the problem and a reasonable approach to addressing it. The conversion procedures between UCCA and other representations are well-described and theoretically sound, though the information loss during conversion is significant (as evidenced by the zero remote edge scores in conversion-based approaches). The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach and suggest concrete paths for improvement, particularly through the use of LSTM classifiers. The work makes a clear technical contribution to the field of semantic parsing, though the performance gap between the parser and human annotation remains a notable concern.
