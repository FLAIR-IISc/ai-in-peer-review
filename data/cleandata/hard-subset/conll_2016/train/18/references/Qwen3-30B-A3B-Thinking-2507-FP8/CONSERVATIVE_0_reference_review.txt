

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a new semantic parsing approach called "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parser" (BSP) that claims to support three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex semantic units), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors present this as the first parser supporting all three properties simultaneously for grounded semantic representations. The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a conversion-based approach that transforms UCCA structures into dependency or constituency trees to leverage existing parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three structural properties.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a gap**: The paper effectively identifies a gap in the semantic parsing literature regarding the support for multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units simultaneously in grounded semantic representations. The authors convincingly argue that existing parsers either lack one or more of these properties or don't use grounded representations.

2. **Thorough evaluation framework**: The authors provide a novel evaluation metric that accounts for the three structural properties, which is a significant contribution to the field. The careful analysis of conversion losses and the upper bounds for conversion-based approaches demonstrates methodological rigor.

3. **Practical contribution**: The paper makes a valuable practical contribution by providing conversion protocols between UCCA and other formalisms, which will be useful for researchers working with UCCA data. The availability of the code and parsers upon publication is also commendable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of compelling evidence for novelty**: The authors claim "no existing parser" supports all three properties, but they fail to adequately address existing work on non-projective dependency parsing (e.g., Sagae and Tsujii, 2008; Tokgöz and Eryiğit, 2015) and discontinuous constituency parsing (Maier, 2015), which could handle some of these properties. The BSP parser's performance (69.2% primary F-score) is comparable to conversion-based approaches (e.g., LSTM parser achieves 69.2% primary F-score), suggesting the novel transitions don't provide significant advantages over established methods.

2. **Insufficient justification for structural properties**: The paper claims these three properties are "required" for broad-coverage semantic parsing but fails to provide evidence that they significantly improve downstream applications or that the added complexity is justified. The paper doesn't demonstrate how these properties lead to better performance on semantic tasks like question answering, machine translation, or summarization.

3. **Questionable evaluation methodology**: The authors define a new evaluation metric that collapses to standard measures when structures are trees, but they don't convincingly argue why this metric is superior to established evaluation methods for semantic parsing. The focus on primary edges (ignoring remote edges) in most of their evaluation is problematic since the remote edges are a key part of their claimed contribution.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper doesn't compare against more recent neural approaches to semantic parsing that might handle these structures more effectively. The LSTM parser they use (Dyer et al., 2015) is already outdated, and the paper doesn't consider more recent neural architectures that could yield better results.

5. **Insufficient analysis of conversion loss**: While the authors acknowledge that conversion is lossy, they don't provide a detailed analysis of how much information is lost during conversion and how this impacts the final results. The upper bounds they report (93.8% for constituency conversion) suggest significant information loss, yet they don't explore how to mitigate this.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you justify the claim that the three structural properties are "required" for broad-coverage semantic parsing? Could you provide evidence showing that parsers without these properties perform significantly worse on downstream semantic tasks?

2. The paper reports that BSP achieves comparable results to conversion-based approaches (e.g., LSTM parser) while supporting remote edges. Why should we prefer the more complex BSP approach over the simpler conversion-based method that achieves similar performance on primary edges?

3. The authors mention that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85% F-score on primary edges. How does this compare to the performance of BSP and the conversion-based approaches, and what does this imply about the potential for further improvement?

## Overall Assessment

This paper identifies a genuine gap in semantic parsing literature but fails to provide sufficient evidence that their proposed approach offers significant advantages over existing methods. The claimed novelty is questionable, the performance gains are marginal, and the justification for the structural properties is weak. While the conversion protocols are a useful contribution, the paper does not provide compelling evidence that the novel BSP parser offers a substantial improvement over established methods. As a reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and rigorous validation against established baselines, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art parsers, a stronger justification for the structural properties, and a clearer demonstration of how their approach improves downstream semantic processing tasks. Without these improvements, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to justify the novelty of their approach.
