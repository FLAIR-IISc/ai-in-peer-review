

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in semantic parsing literature by proposing the first parser that supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing: multiple parents (for shared arguments between predicates), non-terminal nodes (for complex units like coordination), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors introduce a transition-based parser called Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) that directly supports these properties. They also explore conversion-based approaches to assess how existing parsers could be adapted to this task.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. First parser supporting all three required properties for broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS)
2. Novel transition set and features specifically designed for BSS parsing
3. Comprehensive evaluation framework for BSS parsing

## Strengths

1. **Significant Gap Filled:** The paper correctly identifies a clear and important gap in semantic parsing literature. The authors provide a compelling argument for why current approaches fall short: while AMR avoids grounding (complicating parsing), SDP supports multiple parents and discontinuities but lacks non-terminal nodes, and UCCA (the only scheme supporting all three properties) has no parser. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as comprehensive semantic parsing requires all three properties.

2. **Technical Innovation:** The BSP parser represents a substantial technical contribution. The authors extend transition-based parsing to handle all three properties through carefully designed transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX, SWAP) and features. The inclusion of the "ratio" feature as a regularizer for node creation is particularly novel and well-motivated. The parser achieves 69.2% labeled F-score on primary edges, which is promising given the lack of prior work in this space.

3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare their direct approach with conversion-based methods (to constituency and dependency trees), provide upper bounds for conversion-based methods, and test both in-domain and out-of-domain. The evaluation metrics are well-defined for BSS structures, and the results clearly demonstrate the trade-offs between different approaches. The out-of-domain results (60.6% F-score) show robustness to domain variation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparative Analysis with Alternative Representations:** While the paper correctly states that UCCA is the only representation supporting all three properties, it doesn't adequately explain why UCCA was chosen over other semantic representations. The authors mention AMR and SDP as alternatives but don't clearly justify why UCCA is superior for this specific task. A more thorough comparison with alternative representations would strengthen the paper's foundation.

2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper reports good performance numbers but lacks detailed error analysis. Without understanding the specific types of errors (e.g., errors in predicting remote edges, coordination structures, or discontinuous units), it's difficult to assess the parser's limitations and guide future improvements. The paper states that "results suggest concrete paths for further improvement" but doesn't elaborate on what those paths might be.

3. **Limited Discussion of Practical Applications:** The paper mentions potential applications like machine translation and summarization but doesn't provide evidence that this parser would actually improve these tasks. The authors don't discuss how the parser would integrate into real-world NLP pipelines or how it would scale to larger corpora. The connection between parsing performance and downstream application benefits is underdeveloped.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses a meaningful gap in semantic parsing with a novel approach that has promising results. The main weaknesses are the lack of comparative analysis with alternative semantic representations, insufficient error analysis, and limited discussion of practical applications. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why was UCCA chosen as the representation scheme over other semantic representations that might support some of the required properties? What specific advantages does UCCA offer for this task?
2. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, particularly regarding errors in predicting remote edges and handling discontinuous units?
3. How would you envision integrating this parser into practical NLP pipelines, and what evidence do you have that it would improve downstream tasks like machine translation or summarization?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to semantic parsing by addressing an important gap in the literature. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, it would be a strong addition to the field.
