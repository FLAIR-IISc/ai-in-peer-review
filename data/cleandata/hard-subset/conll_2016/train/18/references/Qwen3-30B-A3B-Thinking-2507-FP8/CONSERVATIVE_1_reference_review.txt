

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a new parser (BSP) for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors claim this is the first parser to support all three properties simultaneously. They present two approaches: a conversion-based approach (converting BSS to constituency/dependency trees and using existing parsers) and a novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed specifically for BSS. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A clear definition and justification for BSS parsing
2. Conversion protocols between UCCA structures and dependency/constituency trees
3. A novel transition-based parser (BSP) with new transitions and features for BSS parsing

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a well-structured definition of BSS and its three required properties, effectively contrasting this approach with related work (SDP and AMR). The authors clearly articulate why these properties are necessary for a "broad-coverage" semantic representation.

2. The conversion protocols between UCCA and other formalisms are well-described and provide a useful baseline for comparison. The authors transparently acknowledge the lossy nature of these conversions, which is important for proper evaluation.

3. The paper makes the data and code publicly available, which is a significant strength for reproducibility and future work in this area.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of convincing novelty claim:** The paper claims to be "first in proposing techniques for BSS parsing," but this claim is not well-supported. The authors cite that UCCA has no parser, yet their conversion-based approach already demonstrates that existing parsers can handle the task (with loss), which undermines the novelty of the direct approach. The paper fails to explain why previous work (including their own conversion approach) isn't sufficient for the task, making the novelty claim appear overstated.

2. **Weak results and lack of comparison to state-of-the-art:** The results are modest (69% F-score for primary edges), and the paper doesn't compare against the most recent state-of-the-art methods. The authors mention that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85%, suggesting that current results are significantly below the upper bound. The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze why the performance gap exists or how it might be closed, which is concerning for a paper claiming to present the first solution to this problem.

3. **Insufficient evidence for novel components:** The feature ablation study (Table 3) is minimal and doesn't provide strong evidence for the value of the novel features. The paper claims the "ratio" feature serves as a regularizer, but doesn't show how much it contributes to the results. The authors don't provide a thorough analysis of why their novel transitions (LEFTREMOTEX and RIGHTREMOTEX) are necessary, compared to the conversion-based approach.

4. **Lack of thorough error analysis:** The paper mentions that the conversion-based approach is lossy but doesn't quantify how much error is introduced by the conversion process. They briefly mention this in Section 6 but don't provide a detailed analysis of how conversion errors affect the results, making it difficult to assess the true performance of the direct approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that no existing parser supports all three properties of BSS, but the conversion-based approach already uses existing parsers that don't support these properties. How do you justify that the conversion-based approach isn't sufficient for the task, and why is a direct approach necessary?

2. The results (69% F-score) are significantly below UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80-85%). What are the main sources of error in the parser, and how might they be addressed in future work?

3. The paper mentions that the LSTM parser outperforms the arc-standard MaltParser by 9.1% F-score. Given this, why didn't the authors use an LSTM-based classifier for BSP instead of a linear classifier? How would this affect the results?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper provides a clear definition of BSS and a reasonable conversion-based baseline, the claims of novelty and the modest results are concerning. The paper presents a new parser but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the proposed approach offers significant advantages over existing methods. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their novelty claim and provide more thorough validation against established baselines to justify the paper's contribution to the field. The current evidence does not meet the high standards of rigor expected in semantic parsing research.
