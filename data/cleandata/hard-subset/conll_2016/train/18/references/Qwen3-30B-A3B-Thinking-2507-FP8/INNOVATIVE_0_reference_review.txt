

# Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a novel semantic representation framework supporting three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex units like coordination), and discontinuous units (for phrasal verbs and multi-word expressions). The authors claim this is the first work to propose a parser for BSS, which is significant because existing semantic annotation schemes like UCCA (the only one supporting all three properties) lacks a parser. The authors present two approaches: a conversion-based method to assess existing parsers and a novel transition-based parser called BSP that directly handles BSS.

The main contributions, ranked by significance, are:
1. The conceptual framework of BSS, defining a new semantic representation that addresses a fundamental gap in semantic parsing research.
2. The first parser for BSS, specifically designed to handle multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units.
3. The BSP parser with novel transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features tailored for UCCA's unique representation.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Novelty**: The paper identifies a critical gap in semantic parsing that has been overlooked for years—existing semantic representations either avoid grounding (like AMR) or lack one or more of the three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units). The authors' conceptual framework of BSS is genuinely novel and represents a bold direction for semantic parsing research. This conceptual contribution alone justifies publication, as it opens up a new research space that has been unexplored.

2. **First Parser for BSS**: The authors correctly note that UCCA is the only existing semantic representation supporting all three properties of BSS, but it has no parser. By being the first to propose a parser for this representation, they've created a new research direction that will likely influence future work in semantic parsing. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that CONLL should prioritize.

3. **Technical Innovation**: The BSP parser introduces specialized transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) for handling remote edges in UCCA, which is a technically sound contribution to transition-based parsing. The novel "ratio" feature that regularizes node creation is particularly clever and could benefit other transition-based parsers. The paper demonstrates that their approach can predict remote edges (which conversion-based methods cannot), showing the value of a direct parsing approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Evaluation**: While the authors achieve 69.2% F-score on primary edges (comparable to state-of-the-art dependency parsers), the evaluation is limited to English UCCA-annotated corpora with no error analysis or comparison to recent semantic parsing approaches like AMR. The paper could have provided more insight into where the parser fails, which would be valuable for future work.

2. **Conversion-Based Approach Lacks Novelty**: The conversion-based approach (converting BSS to constituency trees or dependency trees) is a standard technique in parsing, not a novel contribution. While the authors acknowledge this as a baseline, it doesn't represent a significant contribution to the field.

3. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently compare their approach to recent advances in semantic parsing (e.g., neural semantic parsers for AMR). While the authors note that LSTM-based approaches could improve their results, they don't demonstrate this or discuss how their framework relates to these newer techniques.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you envision BSS and UCCA fitting into the broader landscape of semantic representations, especially compared to AMR, which has achieved strong results in semantic parsing?

2. The paper mentions that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85% F-score. How does this compare to the performance of your parser, and what does this suggest about the room for improvement?

3. Given that UCCA has been shown to work across multiple languages, how do you expect your parsing approach to scale to other languages, particularly those with more complex discontinuous structures?

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The conceptual framework of BSS is a major novelty that addresses a fundamental gap in semantic parsing research, and the authors are the first to propose a parser for this representation. While the evaluation could have been more comprehensive, the novelty of the core contribution outweighs these limitations. This paper establishes a new research direction that will likely influence future work in semantic parsing, making it a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have presented a bold, novel approach that aligns perfectly with CONLL's interest in innovative semantic parsing research.
