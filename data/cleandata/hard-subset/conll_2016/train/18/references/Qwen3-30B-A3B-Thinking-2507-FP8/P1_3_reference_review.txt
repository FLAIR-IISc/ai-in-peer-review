

# Peer Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a significant gap in semantic parsing: the lack of parsers that support all three structural properties required for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS): multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex units with no clear head), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors propose two complementary approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing, where BSS structures are converted to dependency or constituency trees and parsed using existing tools, and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly handles BSS.

The main contributions in order of strength are:

1. **The first parser directly supporting all three structural properties of BSS** - This represents the primary contribution of the paper, as no existing parser handles all three requirements simultaneously.
2. **Formal definition of BSS and identification of the three structural properties** - The paper clearly articulates what constitutes a broad-coverage semantic structure, providing a useful framework for evaluating semantic representations.
3. **Conversion-based approach to assess existing technology** - The authors develop a principled method for converting BSS to more standard representations, allowing them to evaluate existing parsers on this challenging task.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem definition and strong motivation**: The paper excels at defining the problem space with compelling examples and a thorough analysis of how existing semantic representations (AMR, SDP, etc.) fall short. The identification of the three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) provides a clear framework for evaluating semantic parsing approaches. The authors effectively demonstrate why these properties are necessary for a truly broad-coverage semantic representation.

2. **Well-structured methodological approach**: The authors present a balanced approach with two complementary strategies. The conversion-based method provides a useful baseline to assess the current state of the art, while the BSP parser represents a genuine contribution to the field. The transition set and feature engineering are well-motivated, drawing on established techniques from related parsing tasks while adapting them for the specific requirements of BSS.

3. **Appropriate evaluation framework**: The authors define a meaningful evaluation metric that distinguishes between primary and remote edges, and they provide upper bounds for the conversion-based methods. The evaluation on both in-domain and out-of-domain data demonstrates the robustness of their approach. The comparison with conversion-based methods provides valuable context for understanding the challenges of the task.

4. **Practical contribution**: The paper includes detailed algorithms for conversion between representations, and the authors commit to making their tools publicly available. This will be valuable for the community, enabling future work on BSS parsing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with relevant prior work**: The paper mentions AMR and SDP but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with parsers that handle some of the BSS properties. For instance, the paper states that "AMR has no grounding," but doesn't compare with AMR parsers that might handle some of the structural properties of BSS. A more comprehensive comparison with existing semantic parsing approaches would strengthen the paper's claims about the novelty of their contribution.

2. **Lack of qualitative analysis**: The paper focuses heavily on quantitative results but provides no qualitative examples of the parser's output, especially for cases involving multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. A few well-chosen examples would help readers understand the strengths and limitations of the approach. Without this, it's difficult to assess how well the parser handles the specific challenges of BSS.

3. **Limited discussion of error analysis**: The paper reports an F-score of 69.2% for the best parser, which is significantly below the inter-annotator agreement (80-85%). The authors don't provide a detailed analysis of the error types or where the parser fails most often. A more thorough error analysis would help identify specific challenges and suggest concrete paths for improvement.

4. **Underdeveloped discussion of practical impact**: While the paper mentions potential applications in machine translation and summarization, it doesn't provide concrete examples of how BSS parsing would improve these tasks. A more thorough discussion of the practical implications of this work would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "no existing parser for a grounded semantic annotation scheme supports the combination of these criteria." However, the UCCA scheme itself is grounded, and the authors cite Abend and Rappoport (2013) as the source for UCCA. Could you clarify if there have been any previous attempts to parse UCCA, and if so, why they don't meet the criteria of supporting all three BSS properties?

2. The paper mentions that the LSTM parser achieves 9.1% F-score higher than the arc-standard MaltParser. Given that the BSP parser is also transition-based, could you elaborate on how the LSTM approach could be integrated with BSP, and what specific modifications would be needed to apply it to the BSP framework?

3. The authors note that their best parser achieves 69.2% F-score for primary edges, while inter-annotator agreement is 80-85%. What do you believe is the primary source of this gap? Is it due to the complexity of the parsing task, limitations in the parser design, or other factors?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing the first parser that directly supports all three structural properties of Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures. The work is well-motivated, methodically executed, and provides a useful framework for future research in semantic parsing. While the performance is not groundbreaking, the problem definition and methodology represent a significant step forward. The paper would be strengthened by more comprehensive comparisons with existing work, qualitative examples, and a deeper error analysis. With these improvements, this work could become a valuable resource for the semantic parsing community.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
