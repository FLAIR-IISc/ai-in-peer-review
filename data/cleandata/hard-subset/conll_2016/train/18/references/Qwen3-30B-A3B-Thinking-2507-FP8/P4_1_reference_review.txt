

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), defined as semantic representations supporting three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex units without clear heads), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors claim to be the first to propose a parser that supports all three properties simultaneously, as existing parsers either support some of these properties or avoid grounding semantic units.

The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1. Formalization of BSS as a well-defined semantic parsing target that addresses a significant gap in the literature (no existing parser supports all three properties)
2. Development of a novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports all three BSS properties through new transitions and features
3. Demonstration of a conversion-based approach that adapts existing parsers to handle BSS, providing a baseline for comparison

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant research gap**: The paper effectively demonstrates that no existing parser supports all three BSS properties simultaneously. The background section provides a thorough comparison of existing semantic representations (AMR, SDP, UCCA), showing that each has limitations regarding one or more of the three structural properties. This gap is well-motivated and represents a meaningful advancement in semantic parsing.

2. **Technical soundness of the BSP parser**: The paper builds on established transition-based parsing techniques with careful extensions for BSS properties. The transition set (Figure 2) and feature templates (Figure 3) are well-justified and adapted from relevant prior work. The parser correctly handles the complexities of multiple parents (via remote edges), non-terminal nodes (via NODE transitions), and discontinuous units (via SWAP transitions). The training procedure with dynamic oracle and MINUPDATE is standard practice for transition-based parsing.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation methodology**: The evaluation includes both in-domain and out-of-domain experiments, comparison with conversion-based approaches, and upper bounds for the conversion methods. The authors define appropriate evaluation measures for BSS (considering both primary and remote edges), which is essential given the lack of standard metrics. The results show BSP achieves comparable performance to conversion-based methods on primary edges while being able to predict some remote edges, demonstrating the value of the direct parsing approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty claim**: The paper claims to be the first to propose a parser for BSS, but the literature review doesn't provide a thorough comparison of all relevant parsers across the three structural properties. For example, the paper mentions that SDP supports multiple parents and discontinuous units but not non-terminal nodes, but doesn't provide a detailed table comparing all existing parsers against the three BSS properties. This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Limited performance results**: The best parser achieves only 69.2% F-score on primary edges, which is significantly below UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80-85%). The paper doesn't adequately address why this gap exists or provide sufficient analysis of the error patterns. This low performance makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the approach, especially given the complexity of the task.

3. **Insufficient analysis of challenging cases**: While the paper provides examples of BSS in Figure 1, it doesn't analyze how the parser performs on the most challenging cases (e.g., complex coordination structures or highly discontinuous units). The evaluation results don't distinguish between different types of errors, making it hard to assess where the parser fails and why.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first to propose a parser for BSS, but doesn't provide a detailed comparison table of existing parsers against all three BSS properties. Could the authors provide such a table to clarify the novelty of their approach?

2. The paper reports 69.2% F-score for BSP on primary edges, while UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85%. What are the main sources of error in the parser that prevent it from reaching higher performance, and how might these be addressed in future work?

3. The conversion to dependency format loses information when converting to trees, resulting in zero scores for remote edges. How would the authors suggest addressing this limitation in future conversion-based approaches?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution by formalizing BSS as a well-defined parsing target and proposing the first parser to support all three required structural properties. The BSP parser is technically sound and demonstrates the feasibility of direct BSS parsing. However, the limited performance results and lack of thorough comparison with existing approaches weaken the claim of novelty and practical significance. The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis and clearer justification for why the current results represent a meaningful step forward given the high inter-annotator agreement. The work is a solid technical contribution to semantic parsing, but its impact would be enhanced with stronger empirical results and more comprehensive comparison with the literature.
