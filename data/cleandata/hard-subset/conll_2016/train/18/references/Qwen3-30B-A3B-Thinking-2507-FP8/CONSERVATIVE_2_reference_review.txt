

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary of the Paper and Contributions

The paper proposes a transition-based parser (BSP) for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" (BSS), claiming to be the first parser supporting three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units. The authors evaluate their approach on UCCA-annotated English data, comparing against conversion-based approaches (converting UCCA to constituency trees or dependency trees) and existing parsers.

My assessment of contributions (not as claimed by authors):

1. The identification of a gap in semantic parsing literature (no parser supporting all three structural properties) is a modest but useful contribution.
2. The conversion protocols between UCCA and other formalisms are practical and provide useful baselines for future work.
3. The BSP parser itself is a minor extension of existing transition-based parsing techniques rather than a significant methodological advance.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a genuine gap**: The paper correctly identifies that no existing parser supports all three structural properties simultaneously. This is a valid observation that addresses a meaningful limitation in current semantic parsing approaches. The UCCA annotation scheme is well-motivated and has demonstrated cross-linguistic applicability, making this gap relevant to the field.

2. **Practical conversion protocols**: The conversion procedures between UCCA and constituency/dependency trees are well-executed and provide useful baselines. These protocols allow the authors to leverage existing parsers and demonstrate that the conversion process is effective in practice, which is a valuable contribution for future work.

3. **Good empirical evaluation**: The authors provide thorough experimental results, including out-of-domain testing and feature ablation studies. The results are presented clearly, and the authors have made their code and data publicly available, which is commendable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the main contribution**: The BSP parser is not a significant methodological advance but rather a minor extension of existing transition-based parsing techniques. The transition set is largely based on prior work (Sagae & Lavie, 2005; Maier, 2015), with only incremental additions for remote edges. The paper fails to adequately distinguish their approach from prior work, and the novelty claim is overstated.

2. **Results are not compelling**: The best reported F-score (69.2%) is not strong enough to justify the complexity of the approach. This falls short of state-of-the-art results in other semantic parsing tasks (e.g., AMR parsing often achieves 70-80% F-score on standard benchmarks), and the authors don't explain why their approach would be preferable to simpler alternatives. The paper claims their approach is "first" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that their results are substantially better than conversion-based approaches.

3. **Lack of thorough comparison with relevant approaches**: The paper mentions SDP (Broad-coverage Semantic Dependency Parsing) but doesn't provide a direct comparison with it, despite SDP addressing similar challenges. The authors also don't adequately justify why their approach is necessary when SDP (which supports discontinuous units and multiple parents) already exists. This weakens the paper's claim of being the first to address the problem.

4. **Non-standard evaluation metrics**: The authors define their own evaluation metric for comparing DAG structures, which is not justified or related to standard semantic parsing metrics. This makes it difficult to assess the significance of their results in the broader context of the field. The metric collapses to standard PARSEVAL only when considering trees, which undermines the evaluation for the core contribution (supporting DAG structures).

5. **Insufficient justification for structural properties**: The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why these three structural properties are necessary for practical applications. They don't provide examples where supporting these properties leads to measurable improvements in downstream tasks, which is essential for justifying the added complexity of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide a more thorough literature review to confirm your claim that BSP is the "first" parser supporting all three properties, particularly regarding SDP and other related work?

2. How do you justify the additional complexity of your approach given that your best F-score (69.2%) is lower than state-of-the-art in other semantic parsing tasks (e.g., AMR parsing)?

3. Your evaluation metric is non-standard. How does this metric relate to standard semantic parsing evaluation metrics, and how would it be useful for practitioners evaluating semantic parsers?

4. The paper claims to support all three structural properties, but the results for remote edges (the key indicator of multiple parents) are quite low (11.8% F-score). How do you plan to improve this aspect of the parser, and why is this important for practical applications?

## Final Recommendation

This paper identifies a valid gap in semantic parsing literature and presents a well-executed conversion-based approach. However, the main contribution (BSP) is not novel enough, the results are not compelling, and the paper lacks sufficient justification for the added complexity. While the work has some merit as a baseline for future research, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim of being a significant contribution to the field. I recommend rejection due to the lack of methodological novelty and the absence of compelling results that justify the proposed approach.
