

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a new parser (BSP) for UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation) structures, claiming it's the first to support three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments between predicates), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units without clear heads), and discontinuous units (for constructions like phrasal verbs). The authors present both a conversion-based approach (adapting existing parsers to UCCA via conversion) and a novel transition-based parser. They evaluate on English UCCA-annotated corpora, reporting approximately 70% F-score.

## Contributions
1. The paper clearly characterizes the limitations of existing semantic parsing approaches regarding three structural properties, identifying UCCA as a representation that supports all three.
2. The conversion-based approach (using existing parsers on converted UCCA data) is presented as a practical strategy, though it's lossy.
3. The BSP parser is presented as a novel transition-based parser for UCCA, though its novelty and necessity are questionable.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a strong, well-justified argument for the need for semantic representations supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The examples provided (e.g., "John" as an argument for both "graduation" and "moved") clearly illustrate the limitations of existing dependency-based approaches.

2. The experimental evaluation is well-designed, with both in-domain and out-of-domain testing, and the authors provide upper bounds for their conversion methods, which is helpful for understanding the loss inherent in their approach.

3. The paper provides a thorough description of the UCCA annotation scheme and its relationship to other semantic representations like AMR and SDP, demonstrating good knowledge of the field.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of convincing justification for the novel parser**: The conversion-based approach (using existing parsers on converted UCCA data) achieves nearly identical results to the novel BSP parser (69.2% vs. 69.2% F-score for primary edges). The paper fails to convincingly argue why the new parser is necessary when existing approaches can achieve comparable results without significant development effort. This undermines the primary claim of the paper.

2. **Insufficient comparison with established methods**: The paper claims UCCA is "the only such scheme supporting them" but doesn't adequately compare with existing semantic parsing approaches that might handle some of these properties (like AMR parsing). They mention AMR but don't demonstrate how their approach compares or why UCCA is superior for practical applications.

3. **Ad-hoc evaluation metric**: The paper proposes a new evaluation metric based on "yield" (the set of terminals in descendants of a node), which is not standard in parsing evaluation. This makes it difficult to compare results with other work and undermines the credibility of their reported scores.

4. **Questionable necessity of structural properties**: The paper presents examples of the three structural properties but doesn't demonstrate that they're actually necessary for practical semantic parsing applications. They don't show that models without these properties fail on real tasks, which weakens their argument for why these properties must be supported.

5. **Insufficient evidence of UCCA's practical value**: While the paper mentions UCCA's cross-linguistic applicability and inter-annotator agreement (80-85%), it doesn't demonstrate how UCCA-based parsing improves real-world applications like machine translation or summarization, which they cite as potential uses.

## Questions to Authors
1. Given that the conversion-based approach achieves nearly identical results to the BSP parser (69.2% vs. 69.2% F-score), what is the specific advantage of the novel BSP parser over the conversion-based method? Why is the new parser necessary?

2. The paper claims UCCA is the only scheme supporting all three structural properties, but how does this compare to other semantic representations like AMR or SDP that might handle some of these properties? Could these representations be adapted to handle all three properties with minimal modification?

3. The paper reports an inter-annotator agreement of 80-85% for UCCA, but doesn't discuss potential annotation errors. How were these errors handled, and how might they affect the reported results? Could some of the "errors" in the results be due to annotation inconsistencies rather than parser limitations?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a clear argument about the limitations of existing semantic parsing approaches regarding three structural properties, but fails to convincingly demonstrate that their novel parser is necessary or superior to existing conversion-based approaches. The evaluation metric is ad-hoc, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the claimed structural properties are actually needed for practical semantic parsing. While the work is well-executed and the description of UCCA is thorough, the primary contribution (a new parser) is not sufficiently justified given that existing methods achieve comparable results with less development effort. I recommend rejection due to the lack of convincing evidence supporting the novelty and necessity of the proposed approach.
