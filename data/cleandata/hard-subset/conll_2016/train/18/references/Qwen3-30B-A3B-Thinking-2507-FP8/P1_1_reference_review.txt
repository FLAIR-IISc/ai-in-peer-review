

# Peer Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of parsing natural language into semantic representations that support three key structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments between predicates), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word expressions without clear heads), and discontinuous units (for semantic units that span non-contiguous text). The authors propose a transition-based parser called "Broad-coverage Semantic Parser" (BSP) that directly supports all three properties, rather than relying on conversion from more restrictive representations.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. **First parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS)**: The authors present the first parser that directly supports all three structural properties required for BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units), addressing a gap in the literature where no existing parser supports the combination of these criteria.

2. **Novel transition-based parsing approach for UCCA**: The paper introduces a new transition system specifically designed for UCCA's semantic representation, extending existing transition-based parsers with new transitions and features to handle the unique requirements of BSS.

3. **Systematic evaluation of conversion-based approaches**: The authors provide a thorough analysis of conversion-based parsing approaches (converting BSS to constituency trees or dependency trees), which establishes baseline performance for existing technology on this task.

## Strengths

1. **Significant conceptual contribution addressing a clear gap**: The paper identifies a genuine gap in semantic parsing literature: no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic structures. The authors correctly note that while some representations (like AMR) support some of these properties, none support all three in a grounded semantic representation. The paper's conceptual contribution is substantial as it establishes a new task definition (BSS parsing) and provides the first method to address it.

2. **Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their proposed BSP parser against conversion-based baselines on a standard UCCA corpus. They carefully measure both primary and remote edges (which is critical for their task), and they report results on both in-domain and out-of-domain data. The evaluation design is well thought out and directly addresses the paper's claims.

3. **Clear technical contribution with practical value**: The BSP parser introduces several novel transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features (including the ratio feature) specifically designed for BSS parsing. These additions are well-motivated and demonstrate clear improvements over conversion-based baselines, especially in predicting remote edges. The paper's practical contribution is significant as it enables semantic parsing for applications requiring these structural properties (e.g., machine translation, summarization).

4. **Strong connection to real-world annotation scheme**: The authors ground their work in UCCA, which has demonstrated cross-linguistic applicability and semantic stability across translations. This connection to an existing, well-motivated semantic representation scheme gives the work greater practical relevance than if it were based on a hypothetical representation.

5. **Clear presentation of limitations and future work**: The paper acknowledges the limitations of the conversion-based approaches (particularly the loss of remote edges) and provides concrete paths for improvement (e.g., using LSTM-based classifiers), which makes the work more credible and forward-looking.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with other semantic parsing approaches**: While the paper compares with conversion-based approaches, it doesn't compare with other semantic parsing approaches that might handle similar structural properties. For instance, the authors mention AMR parsing (Wang et al., 2015), but don't compare with it directly. Given that AMR parsing has been extensively studied, a comparison would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty.

2. **Limited analysis of the "remote edge" concept**: The paper treats remote edges as a key innovation, but doesn't sufficiently justify why they are necessary or how they improve downstream applications. The paper would be stronger with a concrete example showing how remote edges enable better semantic parsing for a specific application (e.g., coreference resolution or machine translation).

3. **Questionable upper bound calculation**: The paper reports an upper bound of 93.8% F-score for dependency tree conversion, but the method for calculating this upper bound is unclear. If the conversion procedure is lossy (as the authors acknowledge), the upper bound might not reflect the true potential of the conversion approach. A more detailed explanation of how this upper bound was calculated would strengthen the paper.

4. **Insufficient discussion of domain adaptation**: The out-of-domain results are reported but not deeply analyzed. The paper states that BSP shows "robustness to domain variation," but doesn't explore why the performance drops (e.g., from 69.2% to 57.1% F-score) or what specific challenges the out-of-domain data presents. A deeper analysis would provide more valuable insights.

5. **Lack of qualitative analysis**: The paper provides quantitative results but no qualitative examples of the parser's output. Showing specific examples of successful and failed parses would help readers better understand the strengths and limitations of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you justify the need for remote edges specifically in UCCA? The paper states that they're necessary for shared arguments, but could this functionality be achieved through other means (e.g., by modifying the annotation scheme or using different parsing strategies)?

2. The paper mentions that UCCA has "semantic stability in translation" (Sulem et al., 2015), but doesn't elaborate on how this stability translates to the parsing task. Could you provide specific examples of how the BSP parser's output would be more consistent across languages compared to other semantic parsers?

3. The paper reports that removing remote edge transitions from BSP improves primary edge F-score by 0.9% in the in-domain setting but reduces it by the same amount out-of-domain. Could you elaborate on why this discrepancy exists and what it implies about the nature of remote edges?

4. The paper states that the conversion to dependency trees "loses considerably more information" than conversion to constituency trees, but doesn't quantify this loss. Could you provide more detailed statistics on the information loss (e.g., percentage of edges that cannot be recovered through the inverse conversion)?

5. The paper mentions that "UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80–85% F-score on primary edges" (Abend and Rappoport, 2013). How does this upper bound compare to the upper bound reported for the conversion-based methods (93.8% for dependency trees)? If the UCCA inter-annotator agreement is the true upper bound, why is the conversion-based upper bound higher?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant conceptual contribution to semantic parsing by identifying and addressing a clear gap in the literature. The proposed BSP parser is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates promising results. While the paper has some weaknesses in comparison to related work and analysis of specific concepts, these are relatively minor compared to the substantial contribution it makes to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions raised above.
