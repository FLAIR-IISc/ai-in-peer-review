

# Peer Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a significant gap in semantic parsing: the lack of parsers supporting all three structural properties required for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" (BSS) - multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units (for phrasal verbs). The authors propose the first parser, BSP, that supports all three properties simultaneously, based on extending transition-based parsing techniques. They also evaluate conversion-based approaches to assess how well existing parsers can handle BSS properties when converted to more restrictive representations.

The main contributions I see (in order of importance) are:
1. The first parser explicitly designed for BSS, supporting all three structural properties simultaneously
2. A thorough evaluation of conversion-based approaches to understand the limitations of existing parsers for BSS
3. A detailed analysis of the UCCA annotation scheme's applicability to broad-coverage semantic parsing

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant gap in the literature:** The paper effectively demonstrates that no existing parser supports all three BSS properties simultaneously, while prior work either supports some properties (e.g., SDP supports multiple parents and discontinuities but not non-terminal nodes) or avoids grounding (e.g., AMR). The examples provided (e.g., "John and Mary went home" requiring coordination structures) compellingly illustrate the limitations of existing approaches.

2. **Rigorous evaluation framework:** The paper establishes appropriate evaluation metrics for BSS by defining "mutual edges" that consider yield (the set of terminals descended from a node) and edge labels. This is a significant contribution as the field lacks standard evaluation measures for such structures. The distinction between primary and remote edges is well-motivated and critical for evaluating the BSS properties.

3. **Strong practical contribution:** The authors make their code and data publicly available, which will facilitate future work in this area. The paper also provides a thorough comparison between conversion-based approaches and their direct BSP parser, demonstrating that while conversion to constituency trees preserves primary edges, it loses remote edges, while conversion to dependency trees loses even more information.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in the core approach:** While the paper claims to be the first to support all three BSS properties, the core transition-based approach is largely an extension of existing work (Maier 2015, Sagae and Tsujii 2008). The novel transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX/RIGHT-REMOTEX) are relatively minor additions to the existing transition set. The paper would benefit from a more detailed justification of why these specific transitions are necessary rather than a more general approach to handling multiple parents.

2. **Inadequate comparison to relevant prior work:** The paper compares to UPARSE and MaltParser, but fails to compare to more recent state-of-the-art semantic parsers that might handle some of these structures better. Notably, the paper states that "the only such scheme supporting them is UCCA (Abend and Rappoport, 2013), which has no parser," but doesn't discuss why previous work on AMR parsing (e.g., Wang et al. 2015) doesn't address these properties, given that AMR is grounded. The paper also doesn't provide sufficient detail on the conversion process to fully assess the information loss.

3. **Lack of detailed error analysis:** The paper reports overall F-scores but doesn't provide a meaningful error analysis. For instance, it would be valuable to know whether the parser struggles more with remote edges, discontinuous units, or non-terminal nodes. This is particularly important given the paper's claim about the importance of all three properties.

4. **Limited discussion of scalability and computational complexity:** The paper doesn't address how the parser scales with sentence length or complexity, especially for sentences with many shared arguments (multiple parents) or highly discontinuous units. This is a critical gap, as the computational cost of supporting these properties could be prohibitive for real-world applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims AMR is "not grounded" but AMR is actually a grounded semantic representation where words are directly annotated as part of the representation. Can you clarify this apparent contradiction and explain why AMR parsing doesn't support the three BSS properties?

2. The paper reports 69.2% primary F-score for BSP. Given that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85% F-score, what does this suggest about the task's difficulty and the potential for improvement? Is the gap primarily due to parsing limitations or annotation inconsistencies?

3. The conversion to dependency trees loses non-terminal nodes, and the inverse conversion uses a heuristic to recover them. How much of the original structure is actually recovered in practice? Can you provide any evidence that the heuristic performs better than random guessing?

4. The paper mentions that the LSTM parser outperforms other parsers significantly. How would you adapt the BSP architecture to incorporate the LSTM-based classifier, and what specific improvements would you expect?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a clear gap in semantic parsing - the lack of parsers supporting all three BSS properties. The thorough evaluation framework and conversion-based analysis are significant strengths. However, the core approach lacks sufficient novelty to be considered a major advance, and the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to relevant prior work and a deeper error analysis.

The paper is well-written and makes a solid contribution to the field, but the novelty is somewhat incremental. The authors should clarify the distinction between grounded representations (like UCCA and AMR) and the specific limitations of existing parsers for handling the three BSS properties. With these clarifications and a more thorough error analysis, this work would be a stronger contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to address the novelty claims and provide more detailed analysis of the results.
