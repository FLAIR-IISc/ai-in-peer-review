- Paper proposes BSP, a transition-based semantic parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units.
- Authors claim BSP is the first parser to directly support all three BSS properties without lossy conversion and compare to conversion-based approaches.
- Main contribution: first direct parser for all three properties, avoiding loss of remote edges and non-terminals.
- Main contribution: novel transition-based framework with new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features (e.g., ratio feature).
- Main contribution: comprehensive evaluation on UCCA showing 69.2% F1 on primary edges and 13.3% F1 on remote edges (vs. 0% for conversion-based).
- Strength: clearly identifies a gap in semantic parsing and gives well-chosen examples motivating the three structural properties.
- Strength: strong theoretical justification for using UCCA (cross-linguistic applicability, annotation speed, semantic stability).
- Strength: well-executed evaluation showing BSP uniquely handles remote edges and robustness in out-of-domain tests.
- Weakness: insufficient justification that the three structural properties are necessary for downstream tasks or worth added complexity.
- Weakness: new evaluation metric is not well-justified for DAGs and its handling of multiple parents is unclear.
- Weakness: lacks comparison to alternative representations (e.g., SDP variants) that might also support the three properties.
- Weakness: human performance benchmark is underreported; unclear how 69.2% F1 compares to UCCA inter-annotator agreement.
- Question: how does UCCA differ from SDP and why couldn't SDP be extended to handle non-terminal nodes?
- Question: how does the new metric handle correct label/yield but wrong parent in DAGs, and how does it compare to existing DAG metrics?
- Question: what percentage of the human upper bound (80–85% IAA) does BSP’s 69.2% F1 represent?
- Overall: valuable contribution as the first direct BSS parser with sound technical approach, but needs stronger justification for properties and evaluation choices to be a substantial contribution.
