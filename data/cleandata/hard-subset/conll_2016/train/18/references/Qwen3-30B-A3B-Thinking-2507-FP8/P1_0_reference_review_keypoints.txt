- Defines BSS as requiring multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units.  
- Proposes two methods: conversion-based parsing (to trees) and a novel transition-based BSP that directly handles BSS.  
- Claims the first parser supporting all three BSS properties simultaneously.  
- Introduces a comprehensive evaluation framework with a new measure distinguishing primary vs remote edges.  
- Provides a systematic comparison of conversion-based versus direct BSS parsing, analyzing trade-offs.  
- Fills an important gap: prior work handled at most two of the BSS properties.  
- Evaluation metric is well-grounded and reduces to standard metrics for tree-structured outputs.  
- Demonstrates feasibility with ~70% labeled F-score on the UCCA English corpus, near human IAA.  
- Experimental comparison is thorough and yields useful insights about representation choices.  
- Lacks meaningful comparison with other semantic formalisms (e.g., AMR) or discussion of BSS advantages.  
- Conversion-based approach outperforms BSP (69.2% vs 59% primary F), calling BSP’s value into question.  
- Evaluation is limited to English despite UCCA’s cross-linguistic claims.  
- No detailed error analysis is provided to pinpoint systematic weaknesses (e.g., coordination, discontinuities).  
- BSP’s novelty is overstated given its reliance on prior transition-based/discontinuous parsing techniques.  
- Reviewers ask authors to justify BSP given conversion success and to compare BSP performance with parsers for other semantics like AMR.  
- Request estimates or discussion of expected degradation and challenges for non-English evaluation.  
- Overall: solid and useful demonstration plus evaluation framework, but incremental; needs stronger justification for BSP and broader evaluation to be a major contribution.
