

# Peer Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing that supports three key structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments between predicates), non-terminal nodes (for representing multi-word units without clear heads), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions like phrasal verbs). The authors call this representation "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" (BSS) and present the first parser that supports all three properties together.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1. The formalization of BSS as a semantic representation framework that supports all three structural properties, filling a significant gap in the literature.
2. The development of the first transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports BSS, with novel transitions to handle multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units.
3. The creation of conversion protocols between UCCA structures and standard representations (dependency trees and constituency trees), enabling the use of existing parsers as baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem definition and motivation**: The paper effectively identifies a critical gap in semantic parsing literature by defining BSS and demonstrating why all three structural properties must be supported together. The examples provided (e.g., "John" as an argument of both "graduation" and "moved") are compelling and clearly illustrate the limitations of existing approaches.

2. **Well-motivated approach**: The transition-based parsing approach is well-chosen, as it builds on successful techniques in syntactic parsing and aligns with UCCA's predicate-argument structure. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the new transitions they've added to handle BSS, including the novel LEFT-REMOTEX and RIGHT-REMOTEX transitions.

3. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The evaluation includes both conversion-based baselines (using UPARSE and MaltParser) and their direct BSP parser. The out-of-domain results on the 20K Leagues corpus demonstrate robustness to domain variation. The authors also provide upper bounds for the conversion-based methods, which is a thoughtful touch that helps contextualize the results.

4. **Practical contribution**: The authors plan to make their code and data publicly available, which will be valuable for the community. The paper also includes a detailed description of their conversion protocols, which could be useful for others working with UCCA.

5. **Clear comparison with related work**: The paper provides a good overview of related work in semantic parsing, including SDP and AMR, and clearly explains how their approach differs and advances the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited performance**: The reported results (69.2% F-score on primary edges) are not particularly strong compared to the state of the art in other semantic parsing tasks. For example, AMR parsing has achieved over 80% F-scores on some benchmarks. The authors acknowledge this and suggest that using an LSTM-based classifier could improve results, but they don't provide these results in the paper, which weakens their main claim.

2. **Lack of qualitative analysis**: The paper lacks a qualitative analysis of the parser's errors. A few examples of what the parser gets right and wrong would provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of their approach, and help readers understand where improvements are needed.

3. **Conversion-based approach is lossy**: The conversion to dependency trees inherently removes remote edges and non-terminal nodes, which are key components of BSS. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide enough detail on how much information is lost in the conversion process or how this affects the evaluation. For instance, they report 0% F-score on remote edges for conversion-based methods, which is expected but not discussed in terms of its impact on the overall evaluation.

4. **Comparison with other DAG parsers**: The authors mention they could not obtain code for existing dependency DAG parsers, but this is a significant oversight. There are several dependency DAG parsers (e.g., Sagae and Tsujii 2008; Tokgöz and Eryiğit 2015) that could have provided a more direct comparison and strengthened their evaluation.

5. **Evaluation metrics**: The edge yield-based metrics they define are unconventional and might not be directly comparable to standard metrics in semantic parsing. While they provide a reasonable justification, the paper could have provided more evidence that these metrics are appropriate for evaluating BSS.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that UCCA has an inter-annotator agreement of 80-85% F-score on primary edges. How does this compare to the performance of their parser? Does this suggest that the performance gap between the parser and human annotation is significant?

2. The authors mention that using an LSTM-based classifier could improve results. Could they provide preliminary results or at least a more detailed analysis of why they believe this would be the case?

3. The paper states that the conversion to dependency format loses considerable information. Could they provide more details about the information loss (e.g., percentage of edges lost) and its impact on the evaluation?

4. The authors mention they could not obtain code for existing dependency DAG parsers. Could they elaborate on why they couldn't obtain this code and whether they plan to make their parser available to facilitate future comparisons?

5. The paper discards linkage nodes and edges as they often express inter-sentence relations. How does this affect the evaluation, and what is the impact of discarding these edges on the overall performance of their parser?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that addresses an important gap in semantic parsing. The formalization of BSS and the development of the first parser for this representation are significant contributions. While the results are not groundbreaking, the problem definition and the practical contribution of the parser are valuable. With more detailed analysis of the results and a stronger evaluation, this work could make a substantial contribution to the field. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to readers familiar with semantic parsing.
