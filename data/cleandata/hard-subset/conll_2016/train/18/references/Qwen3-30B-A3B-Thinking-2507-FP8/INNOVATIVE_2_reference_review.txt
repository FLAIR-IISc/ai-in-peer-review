

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the concept of Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which require three structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex semantic units with no clear head), and discontinuous units (for semantic units spanning non-contiguous text). The authors claim that no existing semantic parser supports all three properties simultaneously, and they present the first parser (BSP) that does so. The paper makes three significant contributions in order of strength: (1) defining the BSS concept as a novel framework for broad-coverage semantic parsing, (2) proposing the first parser architecture (BSP) capable of handling all three properties, and (3) exploring conversion-based approaches to assess how existing parsers could be adapted to handle BSS.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Novelty of BSS**: The paper identifies and clearly articulates three structural properties that are collectively necessary for broad-coverage semantic parsing. This conceptual framework is a significant contribution that defines a new direction for semantic parsing research. Previous work either supported some properties (e.g., AMR supports non-terminal nodes but not multiple parents) or avoided grounding (e.g., AMR), while UCCA (which supports all three) lacked a parser. The authors' ability to precisely define the problem space is exceptional and provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for future work.

2. **First Parser for BSS**: The paper presents the first parser that can handle all three properties simultaneously. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a genuinely novel approach to semantic parsing. The authors' transition-based parser (BSP) introduces new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX, etc.) specifically designed to handle remote edges and non-terminal nodes. This is a bold technical contribution that addresses a clear gap in the literature.

3. **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement**: The paper excels at explaining why existing approaches fall short. The examples of coordination structures, multi-word expressions, and discontinuous units are compelling and well-chosen. The authors effectively demonstrate that standard dependency parsing approaches are fundamentally limited in handling these phenomena without arbitrary head selection rules.

4. **Well-Defined Evaluation Framework**: The authors propose a novel evaluation metric that accounts for both primary and remote edges, which is essential for evaluating BSS parsing. This is a thoughtful contribution that enables meaningful comparison of different approaches to broad-coverage semantic parsing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Performance Gains**: While BSP achieves 69.2% F-score on primary edges (compared to 65.4% for conversion-based UPARSE), the improvement is marginal. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that BSP is substantially better than conversion approaches for practical applications, especially given that the conversion-based methods have the advantage of leveraging existing high-performing parsers.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Other Semantic Parsing Approaches**: The paper focuses almost exclusively on UCCA, without comparing BSP to other semantic parsing approaches that might handle some aspects of BSS (e.g., AMR parsing approaches). This limits the significance of the claim that "no existing parser supports the combination of these criteria."

3. **Insufficient Justification for the Novel Parser Design**: While the new transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) are introduced, the paper doesn't clearly explain why these specific transitions are necessary rather than adapting existing transitions. The authors claim these transitions are needed to distinguish primary vs. remote edges, but it's not clear how this is fundamentally different from existing DAG parsing approaches.

4. **Limited Exploration of the "Broad-Coverage" Claim**: The paper demonstrates that BSP can handle BSS properties, but doesn't convincingly show that it achieves "broad-coverage" in the sense of handling a wide range of semantic phenomena. The evaluation is limited to the UCCA corpus, which may not represent the full range of linguistic phenomena.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the BSP parser specifically handle cases where multiple semantic units share the same argument (e.g., "John and Mary went to Paris and London")? Does it properly handle the shared argument "John" in both predicates?

2. The paper claims UCCA has no parser, but the authors mention "UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80–85% F-score." How do you reconcile the 69.2% parser performance with this 80-85% upper bound? Does this suggest the BSS framework is overly restrictive?

3. The conversion-based approach to dependency trees loses information (with 0% remote edge scores), while BSP achieves 13.3% remote edge F-score. Why is this improvement so modest, and what would be the most promising path to significantly improving remote edge prediction?

## Additional Comments

I appreciate the paper's bold conceptual framing and its clear articulation of the limitations of existing semantic parsing approaches. The authors have identified a genuine gap in the field, and their proposed framework for BSS is both conceptually sound and practically relevant. While the empirical results aren't groundbreaking, the novelty of the conceptual framework and the first parser for BSS are significant enough to warrant acceptance. The paper makes a clear case for a new direction in semantic parsing research that could have substantial long-term impact.

I'm willing to overlook the marginal performance improvements and limited evaluation scope because the paper introduces a novel conceptual framework that defines a new research direction. This is precisely the kind of bold contribution that advances the field, even if the initial implementation isn't perfect. The authors have established a clear problem space and a promising path forward for broad-coverage semantic parsing.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as it makes a significant contribution to defining a new research direction in semantic parsing. The conceptual framework and first parser for BSS are novel and important, and the paper clearly articulates why this direction matters for future semantic parsing research.
