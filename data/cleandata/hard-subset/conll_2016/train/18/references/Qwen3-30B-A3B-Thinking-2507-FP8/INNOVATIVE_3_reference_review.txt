

# Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing that addresses three critical structural properties missing from existing semantic representations: multiple parents (to handle shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (to represent multi-word units without clear heads), and discontinuous units (to capture phrasal verbs and other discontinuous structures). The authors propose the first parser supporting all three properties simultaneously, called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser), built on transition-based parsing techniques. They also explore conversion-based approaches to assess how existing parsers could handle BSS (Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures).

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **The conceptual introduction of BSS as a necessary formalism for full semantic coverage** - the paper clearly articulates why current semantic parsing approaches fail to capture natural language's full structural complexity.
2. **The first parser supporting all three structural properties of BSS** - a significant technical contribution that fills a critical gap in semantic parsing.
3. **A comprehensive evaluation on UCCA-annotated corpora** - providing concrete evidence of the viability of the BSS approach.

## Strengths
1. **Conceptual Novelty and Boldness**: The paper makes a compelling case for why current semantic parsing approaches are fundamentally limited, identifying three structural properties that must be supported for "broad-coverage" semantic parsing. This conceptual framework is genuinely novel and represents a significant shift in how we might conceptualize semantic parsing. The authors don't just propose a new parser but a new way of thinking about semantic structure that could guide future work.

2. **First-of-its-Kind Technical Contribution**: The paper correctly identifies that no existing parser supports all three properties simultaneously. The BSP parser is the first to address this gap, which is a substantial contribution to the field. The authors' claim that "to our knowledge, no existing parser for a grounded semantic annotation scheme supports the combination of these criteria" is well-supported by their literature review.

3. **Clear Problem Statement with Compelling Examples**: The paper effectively demonstrates the limitations of existing approaches with concrete examples (Figures 1a-c), showing how current parsers would fail to represent natural language structures properly. This makes the need for BSS immediately apparent and compelling.

4. **Significant Practical Contribution**: The authors provide a clear path forward for the field, with results showing that BSS parsing is viable (nearly 70% F-score), and they will make all tools publicly available. This provides a foundation for future work that could significantly advance semantic parsing.

5. **Strong Contextualization**: The paper effectively positions itself within the broader landscape of semantic parsing, clearly differentiating between grounded vs. non-grounded approaches and showing why grounding (as in UCCA) is valuable for practical applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Novelty in Parsing Approach**: While the BSS concept is novel, the actual parsing approach is largely an adaptation of existing transition-based techniques. The paper doesn't introduce fundamentally new parsing algorithms but rather extends existing ones with minor modifications (new transitions and features). This is a significant limitation given the emphasis on novelty.

2. **Lack of Deeper Insights Beyond the Parser**: The paper identifies the need for BSS but doesn't provide deeper insights into *why* these three properties are the right ones or how they might generalize to other languages. The UCCA-specific features are limited in scope, and the paper doesn't explore broader theoretical implications.

3. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to English UCCA-annotated corpora. The paper doesn't compare BSS with other semantic representations (like AMR) on the same data, nor does it demonstrate how BSS would improve downstream applications. A more comprehensive evaluation would strengthen the argument for the approach.

4. **Conversion-Based Results Not Particularly Novel**: The conversion-based approach to BSS parsing (converting to constituency trees or dependency trees) is a standard technique in parsing research and doesn't represent a significant innovation.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the BSS framework compare to other semantic representations (e.g., AMR) in terms of practical utility for real-world applications like machine translation or summarization? Could you provide specific examples where BSS would clearly outperform existing approaches?

2. The paper mentions that UCCA has an inter-annotator agreement of 80-85% F-score. What specific aspects of the current BSP parser would need to be improved to reach this ceiling, and how would these improvements differ from the standard approach to parsing?

3. How does the BSS approach handle cases where UCCA's semantic units don't align with standard linguistic structures, particularly in non-English languages? Have you tested the parser on other languages?

## Recommendation
**Accept**

This paper introduces a genuinely novel conceptual framework (BSS) for semantic parsing that addresses a critical gap in the field. While the parsing approach itself is an adaptation of existing techniques, the conceptual contribution is significant and bold, and the authors make a compelling case for why this direction is necessary. The results demonstrate viability, and the authors provide a clear path forward for the community. Given the reviewer's emphasis on novelty over methodological perfection, this paper's conceptual contribution outweighs the relatively incremental nature of the parsing technique. The paper opens up an important new direction for semantic parsing research that is likely to influence future work in the field.
