

# Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

## Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a significant gap in semantic parsing: no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for "broad-coverage semantic structures" (BSS): multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex units like coordination), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors propose a novel transition-based parser called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser) that directly supports all three properties. They also explore a conversion-based approach, converting UCCA structures to dependency trees or constituency trees, and using existing parsers on the converted structures. The paper evaluates both approaches on the English UCCA corpus, reporting approximately 70% labeled F-score for primary edges, with BSP showing the ability to predict some remote edges (representing multiple parents).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **The first parser that directly supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing** (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units).
2. **A novel transition-based parsing framework specifically designed for UCCA structures**, with new transitions and features to handle BSS requirements.
3. **A thorough empirical evaluation** that establishes baseline performance for BSS parsing and demonstrates the feasibility of the approach.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Identification of a Significant Gap**: The paper compellingly identifies and justifies the need for a parser supporting all three structural properties, with concrete examples illustrating the limitations of existing approaches. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as previous semantic parsing work has largely focused on either grounded representations without full structural coverage or non-grounded representations that avoid the challenges of text alignment.

2. **Thorough Technical Approach**: The authors provide a detailed description of their transition-based parser, including a carefully designed transition set (with LEFT/RIGHT-EDGEX, LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX, and SWAP transitions) and relevant features. The inclusion of the "ratio" feature as a regularizer for node creation is a thoughtful technical contribution that could benefit other transition-based parsers.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The evaluation compares both the direct BSP approach and conversion-based approaches, with careful attention to primary vs. remote edges. The paper includes upper bounds for conversion-based methods, providing a clear picture of the information loss inherent in conversion. The out-of-domain results demonstrate reasonable robustness to domain variation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Semantic Parsers**: The paper does not compare with state-of-the-art semantic parsers for other representations (like AMR parsers), which is a significant omission. Since the authors claim to be the first for BSS, they should demonstrate how their results compare to the best possible approaches for semantic parsing in general, not just conversion-based approaches.

2. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to the English UCCA corpus, with no cross-linguistic or cross-domain testing. UCCA was designed to be cross-linguistically applicable, but the paper doesn't explore this aspect. The authors also don't test on other semantic parsing benchmarks, making it difficult to assess the relative performance of their approach.

3. **Insufficient Error Analysis**: The paper reports overall F-scores but provides no detailed analysis of error patterns. Understanding where the parser fails (e.g., for specific types of coordination structures or remote edge types) would be valuable for future work. The authors mention "concrete paths for further improvement" but don't provide any insights into what these might be.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims BSP is the first parser supporting all three structural properties. However, there are other semantic parsing approaches (like AMR parsing) that might implicitly handle some of these properties. Could you clarify why these approaches don't qualify as supporting all three properties, and how your work differs from them?

2. The paper reports an F-score of 69.2% for primary edges, which is lower than the inter-annotator agreement (80-85%) for UCCA. How do you account for this gap, and what specific improvements would be most likely to close it?

3. The authors mention that the LSTM-based parser achieves higher scores than MaltParser. Could you discuss the potential benefits of incorporating LSTM or other neural network approaches into the BSP framework, and why this wasn't implemented in the current work?

## Additional Comments

- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the conversion-based approach, particularly regarding the loss of information when converting to dependency trees.
- The relationship between this work and previous semantic parsing approaches (beyond the brief background) could be more thoroughly discussed.
- The evaluation metrics are defined, but it would be helpful to include more context about how these metrics compare to those used in other semantic parsing work.

## Decision

This paper addresses a meaningful gap in semantic parsing with a clear technical contribution. While the evaluation is thorough in its scope, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art semantic parsing approaches and limited evaluation scope weaken the significance of the contribution. The paper would benefit from additional experiments comparing with other semantic parsers, cross-linguistic evaluation, and a detailed error analysis.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses noted above in the camera-ready version)
