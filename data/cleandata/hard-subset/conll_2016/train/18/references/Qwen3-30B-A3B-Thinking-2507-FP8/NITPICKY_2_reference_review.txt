

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary
This paper introduces a transition-based parser (BSP) for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which support three structural properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors propose two approaches: conversion-based parsing (converting BSS to dependency/constituency trees) and a novel BSP parser. They evaluate on UCCA-annotated English corpora, reporting 69.2% F1 on primary edges.

## Main Contributions
1. First parser supporting all three structural properties of BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units)
2. Conversion protocols between UCCA structures and dependency/constituency trees
3. Detailed technical description of a transition-based parser for BSS

## Strengths
1. The paper correctly identifies a gap in existing semantic parsing approaches with respect to structural properties, and the BSS framework is well-motivated. The UCCA representation is clearly explained and the three structural properties are well-justified with examples.

2. The conversion-based approach is well-considered and provides a useful baseline for comparison. The authors demonstrate that existing technology can be adapted to BSS parsing with reasonable results, which is valuable for the field.

3. The BSP parser is well-described with a clear transition set and feature templates. The inclusion of novel features like the ratio feature (number of terminals to nodes) shows thoughtful design for the specific parsing task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Terminology and notation inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent terminology, switching between "BSS" (the representation) and "BSS parsing" (the task) without clear distinction. In Figure 2, the transition condition "x 6;G x" appears to be a typo (likely meant "x ∉ V"). The paper also inconsistently refers to "UCCA" versus "UCCA scheme" and has ambiguous descriptions of the head function h(u) as "the child with the highest edge label" without defining what "highest" means.

2. **Inadequate description of conversion procedures**: The paper claims UPARSE is "the only transition-based constituency parser... able to parse trees with discontinuous constituents" but provides no reference. Algorithm 1 references a Priority() function that's never defined, and the head function h(u) is described without clear implementation details. The conversion from constituency to dependency trees is described as "simpler than head percolation" but without justification for why this is the case.

3. **Evaluation metric inconsistencies**: The evaluation metric for BSS parsing is defined as "the set of mutual edges" based on yield sets, but the paper doesn't explain how to handle cases where yield sets are identical but edge labels differ (which could lead to overestimation of performance). The paper also reports "upper bounds" for conversion-based methods but doesn't clarify how these were calculated or why they differ from the gold standard.

4. **Insufficient justification for novel features**: The "ratio" feature is described as "novel" but without evidence that it actually improves performance beyond vague claims. The "remote" features are labeled as "UCCA-specific" but the paper doesn't explain why these features are necessary for UCCA specifically rather than being general to any DAG parsing task.

5. **Formatting and minor errors**: Several typos and formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper, including "UCCAannotated" (should be "UCCA-annotated"), inconsistent citation formatting (e.g., "Banarescu et al\.,? 2013"), and the out-of-domain test set described as "passages 701– 695" (which is clearly a typo). The paper claims to discard "linkage nodes and edges" but doesn't explain why these are discarded given they're part of the UCCA annotation scheme.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please clarify the meaning of "highest" in the definition of h(u) (the head function) and provide a specific ordering of edge labels.

2. The paper states "we discard linkage nodes and edges" but doesn't explain why these are discarded when they're part of the UCCA annotation scheme. Please clarify this decision and its impact on the evaluation.

3. The "ratio" feature is claimed to be novel, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that it actually improves performance. Please provide ablation results demonstrating its contribution.

4. The out-of-domain test set is described as "passages 701– 695" which appears to be a typo. Please clarify the actual test set boundaries.

5. The paper claims UPARSE is "the only transition-based constituency parser... able to parse trees with discontinuous constituents" but provides no reference. Please cite the source for this claim.

## Overall Recommendation
While the paper presents a novel approach to semantic parsing that addresses important structural properties, the numerous methodological inconsistencies, terminological ambiguities, and unexplained design decisions significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The minor but pervasive issues with technical details, descriptions, and formatting suggest a lack of thoroughness in the preparation of the manuscript. Given the stringent standards for CoNLL, these issues collectively warrant rejection, as they prevent readers from fully understanding and evaluating the proposed method.
