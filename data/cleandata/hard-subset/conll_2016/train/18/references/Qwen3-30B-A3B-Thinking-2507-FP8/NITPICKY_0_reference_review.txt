

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes a transition-based parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which the authors define as semantic representations supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The main contributions claimed are: 1) the first parser for BSS, 2) conversion protocols between UCCA structures and dependency/constituency trees, and 3) a novel transition-based broad-coverage parser (BSP) supporting all three BSS properties.

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear case for the importance of BSS properties, demonstrating how existing semantic representations fail to support all three. The conversion-based approach provides a useful baseline for comparison, and the BSP parser represents a reasonable extension of existing transition-based methods. The evaluation on UCCA data is appropriate for the task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent Terminology and Definitions**: The paper introduces "BSS" as a new term but inconsistently defines it. The paper states "We call formal representations supporting all three properties Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS)" but later uses "BSS" as if it's a standard term. The definition of "remote edges" is unclear - the paper states "UCCA marks one of the incoming edges for each non-root as 'primary' and the others as 'remote' edges," but doesn't explain how this is determined or how it differs from standard dependency parsing. The term "non-terminal nodes" is also used inconsistently (sometimes hyphenated, sometimes not).

2. **Methodological Ambiguity in BSP**: The transition set in Figure 2 is poorly explained. The description states "LEFT-EDGEX and RIGHT-EDGEX create a new primary X-labeled edge," but doesn't clarify what "X" represents (is it a label? a category?). The paper claims "the parser to be able to determine whether an edge is a primary or a remote one" but doesn't explain the mechanism for this determination in the transition set.

3. **Inadequate Evaluation Metrics**: The evaluation metric defined in Section 5 is problematic. The authors claim it "collapses to the standard PARSEVAL constituency evaluation measure" when Gp and Gg are trees, but they don't justify why this metric is appropriate for DAG structures. The metric considers "yield" (set of terminals in descendants) which is problematic for semantic structures where the same terminal might be shared across multiple subtrees. The evaluation does not account for the fact that remote edges are fundamentally different from primary edges.

4. **Inconsistent Results Reporting**: The paper claims "nearly 70% labeled F-score" but Table 2 shows 69.2% for the LSTM parser, which is not "nearly 70%" (it's 69.2%, closer to 69% than 70%). The paper also reports results for "BSPTree" which is a variant of BSP trained on UCCA trees (without remote edges), but doesn't explain why this variant is included in the main results rather than in a separate ablation study.

5. **Reference Formatting Inconsistencies**: The references section has inconsistent formatting. Some entries have "et al." while others don't, some have "Proc. of ACL" while others have "Proc. of ACL, pages...". The reference for "UCCA" has an incomplete citation ("Abend and Rappoport.,? 2013" instead of "Abend and Rappoport, 2013").

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "BSP obtains comparable F-scores to MaltParser and UPARSE" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests. How do the differences between BSP and the baselines compare statistically?

2. The conversion procedure from dependency trees to constituency trees (Algorithm 2) assumes "attachments are always high-attaching." How does this heuristic affect the quality of the reconstructed UCCA structures, and are there cases where this assumption fails?

3. The paper states "UCCA's foundational layer" but doesn't clearly define what the "foundational layer" is or how it differs from other layers. What specific semantic phenomena does this layer cover that would be excluded in other layers?

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses an interesting problem and makes a reasonable attempt at proposing a solution, the numerous minor methodological inconsistencies, unclear definitions, and formatting issues significantly undermine the paper's technical quality. The paper claims to be the first to propose techniques for BSS parsing, but the lack of clarity in the proposed method and evaluation makes it difficult to assess whether this claim is valid. The paper's minor flaws, while individually small, collectively prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance.

The authors should address the terminology inconsistencies, clarify the transition set details, provide a more rigorous justification for the evaluation metric, and fix the reference formatting before reconsideration. Without these changes, the paper does not meet the standards of a CONLL publication.
