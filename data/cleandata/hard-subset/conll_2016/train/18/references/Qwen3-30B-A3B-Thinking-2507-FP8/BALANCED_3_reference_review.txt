

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a significant gap in semantic parsing by proposing the first parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which support three critical properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units without clear heads), and discontinuous units (for phrasal verbs and multi-word expressions). The authors introduce a novel transition-based parser called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser) that directly supports all three properties, rather than relying on conversion to more restrictive representations. The main contributions are:

1. The first parser specifically designed for BSS structures that directly supports all three required properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units).
2. A thorough exploration of conversion-based approaches to BSS parsing, with careful analysis of the trade-offs between conversion to constituency trees versus dependency trees.
3. A detailed evaluation of the BSP parser on UCCA-annotated data, with both in-domain and out-of-domain testing.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation for a significant gap in the field**: The paper clearly identifies a critical limitation in existing semantic parsing approaches - no parser supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation. The authors provide compelling examples (coordination structures, phrasal verbs) where standard dependency or constituency parsing fails, making a strong case for the need for BSS parsing. This is a well-motivated problem that has been largely overlooked in the literature.

2. **Novel evaluation metrics for BSS**: The authors define appropriate evaluation measures for BSS structures that account for both primary and remote edges, as well as discontinuous units. Their metric, which evaluates edges based on the set of terminals they yield, is well-justified and allows for meaningful comparison of structures with different topological properties. The inclusion of upper bounds for conversion-based methods is particularly valuable for understanding the limitations of their approach.

3. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper conducts comprehensive experiments, including both in-domain and out-of-domain testing, with careful comparison between conversion-based approaches and direct parsing. The feature ablation study provides valuable insights into the relative importance of different features for BSS parsing. The results demonstrate that BSP can predict remote edges (which conversion-based approaches cannot), while achieving competitive primary edge F-scores.

4. **Practical contribution and reproducibility**: The authors plan to make all converters and parsers publicly available, which is a significant contribution to the research community. The detailed description of the transition set, feature templates, and training procedure would allow others to replicate and build upon their work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the transition-based approach**: While the paper correctly identifies the need for a BSS parser, the transition-based approach itself builds heavily on existing work (e.g., Maier, 2015 for discontinuous parsing). The specific additions to the transition set (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) are reasonable but not particularly innovative. The paper would benefit from more clearly articulating how BSP differs from existing transition-based parsers for related tasks.

2. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art semantic parsers**: The paper compares with conversion-based approaches (UPARSE, MaltParser, LSTM), but fails to compare with other semantic parsing approaches that handle some BSS properties (e.g., SDP parsers or AMR parsers). The LSTM parser achieves 73.2% primary F-score, but it's unclear how this compares to state-of-the-art semantic parsers like those for AMR or SDP. This omission weakens the significance claim of the work.

3. **Insufficient analysis of error types**: The paper presents overall F-scores but lacks detailed analysis of error patterns. It would be valuable to know whether the parser struggles more with remote edges, discontinuous units, or coordination structures. Such analysis would help identify specific areas for improvement and provide more actionable insights for future work.

4. **Unclear relationship to other semantic representations**: The paper mentions UCCA's applicability to multiple languages and semantic stability in translation, but doesn't sufficiently compare UCCA to other semantic representations (AMR, SDP) in terms of coverage, complexity, or practical utility. This is particularly important given the growing interest in semantic parsing for applications like machine translation and summarization.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims BSP is the first parser supporting all three BSS properties. How does BSP compare to the approaches used in SemEval 2015 task 18 (Oepen et al., 2015), which also addresses broad-coverage semantic dependency parsing? Do those approaches handle the same structural properties?

2. The paper states that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85% F-score on primary edges. How does this compare to the inter-annotator agreement for other semantic representations like AMR or SDP? How does this upper bound compare to the performance of your parser on remote edges?

3. The conversion to dependency format loses "considerably more information" than conversion to constituency trees. Could you provide specific examples of semantic structures that are lost in this conversion and how this affects downstream applications?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to semantic parsing by addressing a significant gap in the literature - the lack of parsers supporting all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation. The BSP parser is the first to directly support these properties, and the evaluation demonstrates promising results (nearly 70% labeled F-score for primary edges). 

However, the paper would be strengthened by more clearly distinguishing its contribution from existing work, providing a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art semantic parsers, and offering more detailed error analysis. The limited novelty of the transition-based approach and the lack of comparison with other semantic parsing frameworks are the most significant weaknesses.

With careful revision to address these concerns, particularly by better contextualizing the work within existing semantic parsing research and providing more detailed error analysis, this paper could make a meaningful contribution to the field. As it stands, the work is interesting and well-executed, but the novelty and significance claims need to be more strongly supported. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the weaknesses identified above.
