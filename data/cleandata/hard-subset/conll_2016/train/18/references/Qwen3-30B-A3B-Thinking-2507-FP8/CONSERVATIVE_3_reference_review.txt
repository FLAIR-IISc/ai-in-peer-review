

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes a transition-based parser (BSP) that claims to be the first to support three structural properties for semantic parsing: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word expressions without clear heads), and discontinuous units (for phrasal verbs and other discontinuities). The authors present two approaches: a conversion-based method (converting UCCA structures to constituency/dependency trees and using existing parsers) and their novel BSP parser. They report results on the UCCA Wikipedia corpus, claiming up to 69.2% F1 for primary edges.

The authors claim their main contributions are:
1. First parser supporting all three structural properties of Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS)
2. A novel transition-based parser (BSP) that directly handles all three properties
3. A conversion-based approach to assess the ability of existing technology for this task

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a genuine gap in semantic parsing research: no existing parser supports all three structural properties simultaneously. This is a meaningful observation about the limitations of current semantic parsing frameworks.
2. The conversion-based approach provides a useful baseline for comparison and demonstrates how existing parsers could be adapted to handle more complex semantic structures.
3. The technical details of the transition set and feature templates are well-described and build logically on existing work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient validation against established baselines**: The paper claims to be "first in proposing techniques for BSS parsing," but fails to properly compare against the most relevant established semantic parsing approaches. The authors dismiss AMR parsing as "not grounding the representation in the text," but provide no comparison to AMR parsers (e.g., Wang et al., 2015) that have been extensively validated on semantic parsing tasks. The conversion-based approach uses UPARSE and MaltParser, which are primarily syntactic parsers, not semantic parsing systems. Without comparison to semantic parsing baselines, the paper cannot demonstrate that their approach actually solves the problem better than existing methods.

2. **Non-standard evaluation metric**: The authors define a novel evaluation metric for DAG structures that collapses to standard constituency evaluation when applied to trees. This metric is not justified, and without comparison to standard semantic parsing evaluation metrics (e.g., AMR evaluation), it's impossible to assess how the results compare to existing semantic parsing work. The paper claims "UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80–85% F-score," but doesn't provide comparable upper bounds for other semantic parsing frameworks.

3. **Overstated novelty**: The BSP parser is presented as a novel contribution, but the transition set (LEFT-EDGEX, RIGHT-EDGEX, etc.) is a minor extension of existing work on transition-based DAG parsing (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008; Tokgöz and Eryiğit, 2015). The paper doesn't demonstrate that these extensions provide significant performance improvements over existing DAG parsers, which should have been included as baselines. The claim of being "first" is questionable given that transition-based DAG parsing has been explored in other contexts.

4. **Limited out-of-domain testing**: The out-of-domain results (20K Leagues corpus) show only slightly worse performance (57.1% vs 59.0% primary F1), but this is not a meaningful demonstration of robustness. The out-of-domain test set is only 1.8% of the total corpus, and the paper doesn't explain why this corpus was chosen or how representative it is of domain variation. A proper out-of-domain evaluation would require multiple diverse domains and larger test sets.

5. **Lack of ablation studies on core innovations**: The paper presents feature ablation in Table 3, but this doesn't evaluate the core innovation of supporting all three structural properties. The comparison between BSP and BSPTree (BSP without remote edge transitions) shows minimal difference (0.9% F1 improvement), suggesting that the remote edge transitions are not significantly contributing to performance. The paper fails to demonstrate that the three structural properties are actually necessary for good performance.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why did you not compare your approach to AMR parsing systems, which are the most widely used framework for semantic parsing? How would your results compare to state-of-the-art AMR parsers?
2. Can you provide a more rigorous justification for your evaluation metric? How does it relate to standard semantic parsing evaluation metrics?
3. The paper claims UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85%, but this is not a standard upper bound for semantic parsing tasks. What is the upper bound for semantic parsing tasks that use similar structures to UCCA?
4. Given that the conversion-based approach yields higher primary edge F1 (64-73.2%) than BSP (62.4%), why should we prefer the more complex BSP approach over the simpler conversion-based method?

## Conclusion
This paper identifies a meaningful gap in semantic parsing research, but fails to provide sufficient evidence that its approach is actually superior to existing methods. The claims of novelty are overstated, the evaluation is inadequate compared to standard semantic parsing practices, and the paper does not properly validate its approach against relevant baselines. While the conversion-based approach is interesting, the paper does not provide compelling evidence that the direct BSP approach offers significant advantages. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison to established semantic parsing frameworks and a more standardized evaluation approach. As it stands, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to warrant acceptance at CoNLL.
