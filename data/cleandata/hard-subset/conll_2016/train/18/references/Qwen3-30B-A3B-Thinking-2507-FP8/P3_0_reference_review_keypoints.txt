- Introduces the first parser supporting all three BSS properties: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units.  
- Proposes two approaches: conversion-based parsing (to dependencies/constituency) and a novel transition-based BSP parser that directly models BSS.  
- Claims main contributions: first simultaneous support of BSS properties, formalization of the properties, and demonstration that conversion-based methods are strong baselines.  

- Strength: correctly identifies a genuine gap in existing semantic parsing literature.  
- Strength: provides clear formalization and motivating examples for the three structural properties.  
- Strength: includes thorough experimental evaluation and useful feature ablation.  

- Weakness: BSP parser shows limited novelty—extensions to existing transition systems rather than a major methodological advance.  
- Weakness: Conversion-based LSTM outperforms BSP on primary edges (73.2% vs. 62.4%), undermining necessity of the direct approach.  
- Weakness: Introduces an ad-hoc edge-yield evaluation metric without sufficient justification or demonstration of its benefits over standard metrics.  
- Weakness: Fails to adequately compare with existing DAG parsers that handle multiple parents, weakening novelty claims.  
- Weakness: Overall results are modest relative to human agreement and lack detailed error analysis to guide improvement.  

- Question: How does BSP compare to other DAG-based parsers—could existing DAG parsers be adapted instead of introducing new transitions?  
- Question: Why does the conversion-based LSTM outperform BSP, and what advantages does the direct approach actually provide beyond predicting remote edges?  
- Question: How does the proposed edge-yield metric correlate with semantic accuracy—can examples show it detects errors standard metrics miss?  

- Overall: Useful formal and practical contribution but technically incremental; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to justify BSP’s necessity and the evaluation metric.
