

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing that supports "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" (BSS), defined by three key properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units (for structures like phrasal verbs). The authors present two complementary approaches: a conversion-based method that maps BSS to dependency/constituency trees and uses existing parsers, and a novel transition-based parser called BSP that directly handles all three BSS properties.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. The first parser that directly supports all three properties of BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units), which the authors correctly identify as a significant gap in the literature.

2. A comprehensive evaluation of conversion-based approaches to BSS parsing, which demonstrates their limitations and provides valuable insights into the challenges of this task.

3. The development of a practical conversion protocol between UCCA structures and other formalisms, along with a novel transition-based parser that supports BSS.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant gap in semantic parsing**: The paper effectively demonstrates that no existing parser supports all three properties of BSS simultaneously. The authors provide compelling linguistic examples (e.g., "John" as an argument of both "graduation" and "moved" in the first example) to illustrate why these properties are essential for broad-coverage semantic parsing. This is a well-motivated and important contribution to the field.

2. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments on the UCCA corpus, including both in-domain and out-of-domain testing. They provide upper bounds for conversion-based methods and carefully evaluate both primary and remote edges. The results (nearly 70% F-score for primary edges) are promising and suggest that BSS parsing is feasible.

3. **Practical contribution with public release**: The authors commit to making all converters and parsers publicly available upon publication, which will significantly benefit the semantic parsing community. This is a strong practical contribution that will enable further research and application of BSS parsing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the parser approach**: While the problem is novel, the solution (a transition-based parser with extensions for remote edges and discontinuous units) is largely incremental. The paper builds on existing transition-based parsing work (Nivre, 2003) and existing DAG parsers (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008; Tokgöz and Eryiğit, 2015), but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from these prior works. The authors claim BSP is the "first" parser for BSS, but fail to compare it to existing DAG parsers that already handle multiple parents.

2. **Lack of meaningful comparison to existing semantic parsing work**: The paper doesn't provide a comparison to other semantic representations (e.g., AMR or SDP) that might have different evaluation metrics. Without this context, it's difficult to assess how good the reported 70% F-score is compared to other semantic parsing approaches. The authors mention UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80-85% F-score), but don't clearly position their results relative to this upper bound.

3. **Insufficient analysis of failure cases**: The paper provides limited analysis of where the parser fails, which is crucial for understanding the limitations of the approach. The authors mention that "results suggest concrete paths for further improvement," but don't elaborate on what these paths might be or what specific structures the parser struggles with most.

4. **Limited discussion of linguistic coverage**: While the paper claims to support "broad-coverage" semantic parsing, it doesn't provide detailed analysis of how well the parser handles specific challenging linguistic phenomena (e.g., complex coordination, multi-word expressions, or discontinuous units across different sentence types). A more thorough analysis of coverage would strengthen the paper's claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your BSP parser specifically differ from existing dependency DAG parsers (e.g., Sagae and Tsujii, 2008; Tokgöz and Eryiğit, 2015) that also handle multiple parents? Why were these parsers not compared to in your experiments?

2. Given that your evaluation metrics are non-standard, how do you compare your results to other semantic parsing approaches (e.g., AMR parsing)? What would be a reasonable benchmark for comparison?

3. Could you provide more detailed analysis of where the parser fails, particularly on complex constructions like coordination structures or discontinuous units? This would help identify concrete paths for improvement.

4. The paper mentions UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80-85% F-score on primary edges). How does your parser's performance compare to this upper bound, and what does this imply about the potential for improvement?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by identifying a significant gap in semantic parsing and presenting the first parser that supports all three properties of BSS. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the commitment to public release of tools is commendable. However, the novelty of the parser approach is limited, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed comparison to existing DAG parsers and a deeper analysis of the parser's limitations.

The paper addresses an important problem in semantic parsing, but the proposed solution is more of an incremental extension of existing work than a fundamentally new approach. The main value of the paper lies in the identification of the problem and the comprehensive evaluation of conversion-based approaches, rather than the BSP parser itself.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the comparison to existing DAG parsers and the analysis of failure cases. The paper has strong potential to influence future work in semantic parsing, but needs to more clearly position itself within the existing literature.
