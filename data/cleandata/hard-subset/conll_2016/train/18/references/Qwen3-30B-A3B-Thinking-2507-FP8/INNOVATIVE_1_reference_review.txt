

# Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing that supports three critical structural properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units without clear heads), and discontinuous units (for phrasal verbs and multi-word expressions). The authors claim this is the first parser that supports all three properties simultaneously, as existing semantic parsing work either supports some properties or avoids grounding semantic units (like AMR). They propose two complementary approaches: a conversion-based method that adapts existing parsers to UCCA structures, and a novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed specifically for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS).

## Main Contributions
1. **First parser supporting all three structural properties of BSS** - This is the most significant contribution, addressing a critical gap in semantic parsing research.
2. **Novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed for BSS** - Extending transition-based parsing to handle multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units through new transitions and features.
3. **Conversion protocols between UCCA and other formalisms** - Enabling use of existing parsers on UCCA structures through lossy but effective conversion.

## Strengths

1. **Groundbreaking Conceptual Contribution**: This is the strongest strength. The paper identifies and addresses a fundamental gap in semantic parsing: no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic structures. The authors convincingly argue that previous work either supports some properties (e.g., SDP supports multiple parents and discontinuities but not non-terminal nodes) or avoids grounding (e.g., AMR). This is a genuinely novel direction that opens up new possibilities for semantic parsing.

2. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation**: The paper excels in clearly defining what broad-coverage semantic structures are and why they're necessary, with excellent examples (Figure 1) that demonstrate the three structural properties. The motivation is compelling - natural language contains structures that cannot be adequately represented by existing semantic parsing approaches.

3. **Promising Initial Results**: While not state-of-the-art (nearly 70% F-score), the results are significant for a first attempt at this challenging task. The fact that BSP can predict remote edges (which conversion-based approaches cannot) while achieving comparable primary edge performance to existing parsers demonstrates the viability of their approach. The authors rightly note that these results suggest concrete paths for improvement (e.g., using LSTM-based classifiers).

4. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation with well-defined formalisms (BSS, UCCA) and a principled extension of transition-based parsing to handle the three structural properties. The transition set and features are well-motivated and logically derived from the problem requirements.

5. **Potential Impact**: If successful, this work could significantly impact semantic parsing by enabling representations that better reflect the complexity of natural language semantics. The authors correctly emphasize that grounding semantic units in text (as UCCA does) is useful for many applications, and their approach supports this while handling complex structures.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to a single English corpus (UCCA Wikipedia), with only limited out-of-domain testing. A more comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and semantic representations would strengthen the claim of broad applicability.

2. **Incomplete Comparison to Relevant Work**: The paper compares primarily to conversion-based approaches but doesn't provide a thorough comparison to other semantic parsing methods that handle some of the BSS properties (e.g., SDP). This weakens the argument that their approach is truly novel and necessary.

3. **Lack of Error Analysis**: The paper reports overall F-scores but doesn't analyze error patterns, which would help understand where the parser struggles with the three structural properties. This is especially important for a paper claiming to solve a fundamental problem.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper. The core contribution - the first parser supporting all three structural properties of broad-coverage semantic structures - is genuinely novel and addresses a significant gap in semantic parsing. While the results are preliminary, they are promising for a first attempt at this challenging task, and the paper clearly outlines paths for improvement.

The novelty of the conceptual contribution far outweighs the methodological simplicity of the BSP parser. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's conceptual contribution to be significant and worthy of publication. The authors have made a clear and compelling case for why this direction is important, and they've provided the first steps toward solving this problem.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does BSP's performance compare to other semantic parsing approaches that handle some of the BSS properties (e.g., SDP), beyond just conversion-based methods?

2. What are the main error types that BSP makes, and how do they relate to the three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units)?

3. The paper mentions that UCCA has inter-annotator agreement of 80-85% F-score. How does BSP's performance compare to this upper bound, and what is the gap that could be closed with further improvements?

4. Have you tried applying the LSTM-based approach mentioned in the paper to the local classifier? If so, what results did you obtain?

This paper represents a bold and necessary direction for semantic parsing, and I believe it will be an important contribution to the field.
