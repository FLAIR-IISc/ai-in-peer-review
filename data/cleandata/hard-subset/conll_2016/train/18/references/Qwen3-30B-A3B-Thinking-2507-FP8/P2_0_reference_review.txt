

# Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing that supports three structural properties required for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures" (BSS): multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex semantic units), and discontinuous units (for phrasal verbs and multi-word expressions). The authors claim this is the first parser to support all three properties simultaneously. They propose a transition-based parser called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser) for UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation), a semantic representation scheme that supports all three properties. They also explore conversion-based approaches where UCCA structures are converted to dependency trees or constituency trees, then parsed with existing tools.

My assessment of the main contributions (in order of strength):
1. The first parser to support all three structural properties of BSS (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, discontinuous units) - this is the most significant contribution
2. The development of BSP, a transition-based parser specifically designed for UCCA structures
3. The conversion protocols between UCCA and other formalisms (dependency trees, constituency trees)

## Strengths

1. **Significant Gap Addressed**: The paper correctly identifies a clear gap in semantic parsing literature. Prior work either supported some of the three properties or avoided grounding semantic units (as in AMR). The authors provide a convincing argument for why these three properties are necessary for broad-coverage semantic parsing, making their contribution highly relevant to the field.

2. **Clear Evaluation Framework**: The authors define a novel evaluation measure for DAG structures that accounts for the three properties of BSS. This is crucial because existing evaluation metrics for semantic parsing are designed for tree structures. The paper's approach to evaluating both primary and remote edges provides a more complete picture of parser performance.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The paper provides a concrete implementation of the first parser for BSS, which is a significant contribution to the community. The authors make all converters and parsers publicly available, which will enable future research in this area. The implementation details are thorough, including the transition set and feature templates.

4. **Competitive Results**: The results are promising, with BSP achieving 69.2% labeled F-score on primary edges, which is comparable to state-of-the-art parsers on converted structures. The out-of-domain results (60.6% F-score) demonstrate reasonable robustness to domain variation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Parser Architecture**: While the paper claims to be the first to support all three properties, the parser itself builds on existing transition-based parsing techniques. The authors adapt existing methods (e.g., from discontinuous constituency parsing) rather than introducing fundamentally new parsing techniques. The novelty is more in the application to a new problem rather than in the parsing approach itself.

2. **Inadequate Comparison with Relevant Work**: The paper mentions that SDP supports discontinuous units and multiple parents but not non-terminal nodes, yet it doesn't compare with SDP parsers that might have addressed similar challenges. The paper also doesn't discuss how their approach differs from other parsers that might handle some of these properties (e.g., the work by Maier on discontinuous constituency parsing).

3. **Evaluation Metrics Not Fully Justified**: The paper's evaluation metrics for DAG structures are novel but not sufficiently justified in comparison to existing metrics. It's unclear why these metrics are superior to simply evaluating the primary edges (which form a tree) and treating remote edges separately. The paper could better demonstrate why their metrics provide a more accurate assessment of semantic parsing quality.

4. **Lossy Conversion Impact Not Quantified**: The conversion-based approaches necessarily lose information (remote edges in constituency conversion, non-terminal nodes in dependency conversion). The paper acknowledges this but doesn't sufficiently quantify how much of the performance drop is due to the conversion rather than the parser itself. The upper bounds for conversion-based methods (e.g., 93.8% F-score for dependency conversion) suggest that conversion loss accounts for a significant portion of the performance gap.

5. **Lack of Analysis on Remote Edges**: The paper shows that BSP can predict remote edges (15.3% F-score), but it doesn't analyze why the remote edge prediction is so low. This is a key aspect of BSS, so a deeper analysis of remote edge prediction would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims UCCA has no parser, but Sulem et al. (2015) mentions UCCA's inter-annotator agreement. Have there been any previous attempts at UCCA parsing that you're aware of that weren't published?

2. How does your parser differ from SDP parsers (which support discontinuous units and multiple parents) in terms of handling non-terminal nodes? The paper mentions SDP avoids non-terminal nodes, but doesn't detail how your approach specifically addresses this limitation.

3. Could you provide more details on why the LSTM-based parser (which achieves 73.2% F-score for primary edges) isn't used for the BSP parser? The paper mentions it would likely improve results but doesn't explain the technical challenges.

4. How much of the performance difference between BSP and conversion-based approaches is due to the parser itself versus the lossy conversion? The paper shows upper bounds for conversion-based methods, but doesn't clearly separate these two factors.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing the first parser that supports all three structural properties of broad-coverage semantic parsing. While the technical novelty of the parser itself is somewhat limited (it builds on existing transition-based parsing techniques), the application to a previously unaddressed problem is valuable. The paper is well-written, the evaluation is thorough, and the results are promising. The main weaknesses are the limited novelty in the parsing approach and insufficient comparison with relevant work. The paper would be strengthened by better quantification of the conversion loss and a deeper analysis of remote edge prediction.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly the comparison with relevant work and the evaluation of conversion loss.
