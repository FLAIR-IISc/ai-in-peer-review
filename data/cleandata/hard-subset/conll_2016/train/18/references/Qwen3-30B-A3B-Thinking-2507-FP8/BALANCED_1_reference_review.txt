

# Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper makes the following key contributions:
1. **First parser supporting all three structural properties of Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS)**: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, which the authors argue are essential for a truly broad-coverage semantic representation.
2. **A novel transition-based parser (BSP)** designed specifically for UCCA semantic structures, which addresses the limitations of conversion-based approaches to BSS parsing.
3. **Comprehensive analysis of conversion-based parsing** for BSS, demonstrating how existing parsers can be adapted (with loss) to the task and establishing meaningful upper bounds for these approaches.

## Strengths
1. **Clear identification of a significant gap in semantic parsing**: The paper effectively argues that no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for BSS, while most semantic representations either lack grounding (like AMR) or fail to support key structural properties (like coordination structures). This is a well-motivated contribution to the field.

2. **Thorough theoretical foundation for BSS**: The paper provides a clear, well-structured definition of BSS with concrete examples (Figures 1a-c) and a formalization of the three structural properties. The authors' explanation of UCCA's annotation scheme and its relation to BSS is particularly strong, making a compelling case for why this representation matters.

3. **Insightful conversion-based analysis**: The paper's exploration of conversion-based approaches (to constituency and dependency trees) is methodologically sound, with well-designed conversion procedures (Algorithms 1-2) and meaningful upper bounds that clarify the inherent limitations of these approaches. The analysis of why dependency conversion loses more information than constituency conversion is especially valuable.

4. **Practical relevance**: The paper demonstrates that UCCA has shown cross-linguistic applicability and semantic stability across translations, suggesting real-world utility for the proposed parsing approach. The authors also make their tools publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

## Weaknesses
1. **Ad-hoc evaluation metrics**: The paper defines "mutual edges" based on yield sets, but doesn't convincingly argue why this metric is better than standard semantic parsing metrics. The evaluation doesn't clearly demonstrate how BSP's performance translates to improved downstream applications, making it difficult to assess the practical significance of the results.

2. **Limited performance context**: The paper reports BSP's primary edge F-score (69.2%) but doesn't adequately contextualize this against the UCCA inter-annotator agreement (80-85%). Without understanding the gap between human performance and the parser, it's hard to gauge how significant the results are. The modest improvement over conversion-based approaches (e.g., 15.3% remote edge F-score) isn't convincingly argued as a major advance.

3. **Insufficient comparison with related work**: The claim that BSP is "first in proposing techniques for BSS parsing" lacks sufficient justification. The paper mentions UCCA has no parser but doesn't thoroughly survey whether other semantic frameworks might have parsers that could be adapted to BSS (e.g., SDP parsers). A more comprehensive literature review would strengthen the novelty claim.

4. **Limited empirical validation**: The evaluation is restricted to English UCCA-annotated corpora with no cross-linguistic testing. The paper doesn't provide examples of challenging BSS structures where BSP outperforms conversion-based approaches, making it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the new parser design.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper reports BSP achieves 69.2% primary edge F-score, but UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85%. How do the authors explain this gap, and what do they believe are the main sources of error (parser limitations vs. annotation inconsistencies)?

2. The paper claims BSP is the first parser supporting all three BSS properties, but conversion-based approaches also attempt to handle these properties. Can the authors clarify why their direct approach is fundamentally different from conversion-based approaches in terms of handling the three properties?

3. The paper uses a simple rule-based inverse conversion for dependency trees. How much does this affect the results, and would a more sophisticated conversion procedure improve the conversion-based baselines?

## Overall Assessment
This is a well-written, theoretically sound paper that identifies a genuine gap in semantic parsing literature. The authors provide a clear definition of BSS, a thorough analysis of conversion-based approaches, and a novel parser design that addresses the limitations of existing methods. However, the modest performance gains (around 10-15% on remote edges) and limited evaluation context weaken the impact of the contribution. The paper would be significantly strengthened by better contextualization of results against human performance, more compelling evidence of practical utility, and a more comprehensive literature review.

While the contribution is important, the paper currently falls short of demonstrating that this approach represents a substantial advance over existing methods. With more thorough evaluation and better contextualization of results, this could be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper makes a solid theoretical contribution but doesn't convincingly demonstrate its practical significance.

**Decision Recommendation**: Weak Accept (with major revisions). The paper addresses a meaningful gap, but needs to strengthen its evaluation, better contextualize results, and provide more compelling evidence of practical impact.
