- Paper proposes BSP, the first parser directly supporting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units for broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS).
- Introduces a novel transition-based parsing system for UCCA with new transitions and features tailored to BSS.
- Systematically evaluates conversion-based baselines (constituency and dependency conversions) to establish comparisons.
- Significant conceptual contribution filling a clear gap: no prior parser supports all three BSS properties jointly.
- Rigorous experimental evaluation on UCCA with careful measurement of primary and remote edges, in-domain and out-of-domain.
- Practical technical advances (e.g., LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX, ratio feature) that improve remote-edge prediction.
- Strong grounding in the real-world UCCA annotation scheme, enhancing cross-linguistic and practical relevance.
- Clear acknowledgement of limitations and plausible directions for future improvement (e.g., LSTM classifiers).
- Lacks comparison with other semantic parsing approaches (e.g., AMR parsers) that might handle similar structures.
- Limited justification and analysis of the necessity and downstream impact of remote edges.
- Unclear or questionable method for computing the reported 93.8% F-score upper bound for dependency conversion.
- Insufficient analysis of domain adaptation and why out-of-domain performance drops substantially.
- Absence of qualitative examples showing parsed outputs and failure cases.
- Question: Why are remote edges specifically necessary in UCCA versus alternative annotation or parsing strategies?
- Question: How does UCCA's claimed semantic stability in translation translate to more consistent BSP outputs across languages?
- Question: Why does removing remote-edge transitions improve in-domain primary F-score but harm out-of-domain performance by a similar amount?
- Question: Can you quantify the information loss in conversions (e.g., percentage of unrecoverable edges) between BSS and dependency/constituency conversions?
- Question: How do UCCA inter-annotator agreement upper bounds (80–85% F) reconcile with the higher conversion-based upper bound (93.8%)?
- Overall recommendation: Strong, novel, and well-motivated work recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address analyses and comparisons.
