

# Peer Review: "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a novel parser for Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which support three key properties: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for multi-word units), and discontinuous units. The authors claim to be the first to propose techniques for BSS parsing, introducing a transition-based approach called BSP that supports all three properties. They also explore conversion-based approaches to existing parsers (dependency and constituency trees) to assess the feasibility of adapting current technology for BSS parsing. The primary contribution is the first parser for UCCA, a semantic representation scheme that has previously lacked parsing tools.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant gap in semantic parsing**: The paper convincingly argues that no existing parser supports all three BSS properties simultaneously. The authors provide well-chosen examples (Figure 1) that illustrate why these properties are necessary for broad-coverage semantic parsing, particularly for handling coordination, multi-word expressions, and discontinuous units. This is a genuine contribution to the field.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The authors develop a novel evaluation metric specifically for BSS that distinguishes between primary and remote edges, which is necessary given UCCA's DAG structure. Their evaluation includes both in-domain and out-of-domain testing, and they provide meaningful upper bounds for conversion-based approaches. This careful evaluation design significantly strengthens the paper's contribution.

3. **Well-motivated conversion procedures**: The detailed conversion protocols between UCCA and other formalisms (constituency and dependency trees) are a valuable contribution in themselves. These procedures help clarify relationships between different semantic representations, which is an important contribution to understanding the landscape of semantic parsing.

4. **Novel parser features**: The BSP parser incorporates UCCA-specific features, particularly the "remote" features for handling remote edges, and the ratio feature as a regularizer for node creation. These are well-justified extensions to standard transition-based parsing features.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper claims to be "first in proposing techniques for BSS parsing," but this is inaccurate. While they're the first to propose a parser for UCCA specifically, the SDP work (Oepen et al., 2015) also supports multiple parents and discontinuous units, and the paper's claim that SDP "disallows non-terminal nodes" is misleading. SDP has been shown to handle coordination structures (which require non-terminal nodes) in practice. This weakens the paper's primary contribution claim.

2. **Insufficient comparison with related work**: The paper mentions AMR and SDP but doesn't adequately explain how UCCA differs from these representations or why UCCA is better suited for broad-coverage parsing. The authors claim UCCA has "semantic stability in translation" (Sulem et al., 2015), but don't provide evidence that this translates to better parsing performance. Without clear justification for why UCCA is superior to other representations, the paper's focus on UCCA seems arbitrary.

3. **Modest results**: The best F-score of 69.2% for primary edges is notably lower than state-of-the-art in other semantic parsing frameworks (e.g., AMR parsing typically achieves >75% F-score). The authors don't adequately address why their results are lower, which undermines the significance of their contribution. Their claim that "the quality of the results is underscored by UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80-85% F-score" is problematic since IAA is not a direct upper bound for parsing performance.

4. **Lack of discussion on limitations**: The paper doesn't address critical limitations of their approach, particularly how it handles implicit units (mentioned in Section 2 as deferred to future work). The authors also don't discuss how their parser would scale to more complex semantic phenomena or handle language variation beyond English.

5. **Evaluation metric concerns**: While the authors' evaluation metric is novel, it's not clear why it's superior to standard measures used in semantic parsing (e.g., for AMR). The paper doesn't compare their metric to existing ones or demonstrate its advantages for practical applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims SDP "disallows non-terminal nodes" but SDP has been applied to coordination structures (which require non-terminal nodes). Can you clarify why the authors believe SDP doesn't support this property?

2. How does the UCCA representation specifically address the challenges of semantic parsing that other representations (like AMR) don't, given that AMR has achieved higher parsing performance? What evidence supports UCCA's superiority for broad-coverage parsing?

3. The authors claim their results are "underscored" by UCCA's IAA of 80-85%, but IAA is not a direct upper bound for parsing performance. Can you provide a more rigorous analysis of the gap between their parser's performance and the theoretical maximum?

4. The paper mentions that BSP with remote edge transitions achieves 15.3% F-score on remote edges. Given that these edges are important for representing semantic relationships, why does the paper not emphasize this as a key contribution rather than just a secondary result?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by providing the first parser for UCCA, a promising semantic representation that has previously lacked parsing tools. The conversion-based approach is well-executed and provides useful insights into the relationships between different semantic representations. However, the paper's claims of novelty are overstated, and the results are modest compared to state-of-the-art in semantic parsing. While the work is technically sound and addresses a genuine gap in the literature, the significance of the contribution is somewhat diminished by these issues.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more nuanced discussion of the relationship between UCCA and other semantic representations, a clearer justification for UCCA's advantages, and a more rigorous analysis of the results. With these improvements, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid but not groundbreaking contribution to semantic parsing.
