**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces the first parsing methods for Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations that feature multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. Using the UCCA formalism as a case study, the authors explore two complementary, transition-based strategies. The first is an indirect, conversion-based approach that transforms UCCA graphs into constituency or dependency trees, allowing the use of existing state-of-the-art parsers. The second is a novel, direct parser (BSP) with a new transition system designed to handle all three properties of BSS natively. Experiments show that a conversion-based approach using an LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest accuracy on the primary tree structure, while the direct parser is the only method capable of recovering non-tree (remote) edges, albeit with low accuracy.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The introduction and comprehensive benchmarking of the novel task of UCCA parsing. The paper establishes the first strong results on this task, providing both a direct parsing model and competitive conversion-based baselines that will be crucial for future research.
2.  A valuable empirical comparison of direct versus indirect parsing strategies for a complex semantic formalism. The finding that a strong, existing parser applied to a lossy tree representation can outperform a bespoke direct parser on the core structure provides important insights into the trade-offs involved.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper pioneers parsing for UCCA, a representation designed to capture a wider range of semantic phenomena than many standard formalisms. By defining the problem, proposing multiple solutions, and providing the first empirical results, this work opens a new and promising area of research in semantic parsing.
2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The dual-strategy approach is a key strength. The authors wisely leverage existing high-performance parsers through a conversion-based method to establish a strong performance ceiling, while also proposing a more principled, direct parser (BSP) that can handle the full representational complexity.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing the direct parser against several strong dependency and constituency parsers under the conversion framework. The inclusion of upper-bound analysis for the conversions, feature ablation for the direct parser, and both in-domain and out-of-domain testing provides a robust and informative evaluation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Sub-optimal Performance of the Novel Parser:** The central technical contribution, the BSP parser, is significantly outperformed on primary edges by a conversion-based method using an existing LSTM parser (59.0% vs. 69.2% F-score). While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance here is very low (13.3% F-score), which limits the practical utility of the direct model in its current form.
2.  **Inconclusive Comparison of Parsing Strategies:** The performance gap between the best conversion-based method and BSP may stem from the difference in classifier technology (stack-LSTM vs. linear perceptron) rather than the inherent superiority of the parsing strategy. Because the direct parser was not equipped with a comparable state-of-the-art classifier, it is difficult to draw firm conclusions about the relative merits of the direct versus indirect approaches.
3.  **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper reports aggregate scores but would be strengthened by a qualitative error analysis. It is unclear which specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., coordination, discontinuous MWEs, structures requiring multiple parents) are most challenging for each model, and why the lossy dependency conversion works so well in practice. Such an analysis would provide deeper insight and better guide future work.
