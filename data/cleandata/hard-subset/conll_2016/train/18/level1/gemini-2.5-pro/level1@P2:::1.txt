### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations that simultaneously feature multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous constituents. The authors focus on the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme, the only grounded representation that exhibits all three properties. The paper proposes and evaluates two distinct transition-based approaches. The first is an indirect, conversion-based method that transforms UCCA structures into more standard formalisms (discontinuous constituency or dependency trees), allowing the use of existing state-of-the-art parsers. The second is a direct approach, for which the authors develop a novel transition-based parser, BSP, designed from the ground up to handle the full complexity of BSS. Experiments on UCCA-annotated corpora show that a conversion-based approach using a modern LSTM-based dependency parser achieves the highest performance on the core tree structure (primary edges), while the direct BSP parser, though currently less accurate, is the only method capable of parsing the full DAG structure by predicting remote edges.

### Main Contributions

1.  **Introduction of a New Parsing Task:** The primary contribution of this work is the formal introduction and first comprehensive attempt at parsing UCCA structures. By tackling a grounded semantic representation that combines DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities, the paper brings a novel, challenging, and well-motivated parsing problem to the NLP community. This work effectively establishes the task and provides the first set of baselines.

2.  **A Novel, Direct Parser for Broad-Coverage Semantics:** The paper presents BSP, a novel transition-based parser specifically designed to handle the combined complexities of UCCA. By integrating and extending techniques from discontinuous constituency parsing and DAG dependency parsing, BSP provides a unified framework for the task. Although its performance is not yet state-of-the-art, it serves as a crucial proof of concept and an extensible foundation for future research in direct BSS parsing.

3.  **A Thorough Empirical Comparison of Direct vs. Indirect Methods:** The authors provide a valuable empirical study comparing their direct parser against indirect, conversion-based methods. This comparison cleverly leverages existing high-performance parsers to set a strong performance ceiling. The results offer important insights, demonstrating that even lossy conversions combined with powerful models (like LSTMs) can be highly effective, while also clearly delineating the aspects of the representation (i.e., remote edges) that necessitate a direct approach.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of significant importance. As the field moves towards richer, broad-coverage semantic representations, the need for parsers that can handle structures more complex than trees becomes paramount. UCCA is a promising representation in this space, and this paper is the first to develop a parser for it. The clear motivation for why existing parsers are insufficient for the BSS criteria is a strong point.

2.  **Comprehensive and Methodologically Sound Approach:** The dual-pronged investigation—using both conversion-based and direct methods—is a major strength. The conversion approach is a pragmatic and insightful way to benchmark the task's difficulty using established, powerful tools. The design of the direct parser, BSP, is well-grounded, systematically extending known transition-based parsing techniques. This comprehensive methodology provides a much richer picture of the problem than a single approach would have.

3.  **Rigorous and Insightful Evaluation:** The experimental design is excellent. The inclusion of in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation demonstrates the robustness of the proposed methods. The definition of a new evaluation metric tailored to BSS is a necessary and well-executed contribution. A particularly strong aspect of the evaluation is the calculation of upper bounds for the conversion-based methods, which neatly disentangles the information loss inherent in the conversion from the errors made by the parsers themselves. The feature ablation for BSP further adds to the paper's analytical depth.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The problem, proposed solutions, and experimental results are all presented logically. The formal descriptions of the transition system (Figure 2) and feature sets (Figure 3) are detailed and unambiguous. The authors’ commitment to releasing the parsers and converters is a commendable contribution that will lower the barrier to entry for future research on this important new task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Underwhelming Performance of the Novel Direct Parser:** The primary weakness is that the main technical novelty of the paper, the BSP parser, is significantly outperformed on its core task of predicting primary edges. It achieves a 59.9% F-score, while a conversion-based approach using an off-the-shelf LSTM parser reaches 69.2%. While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on that front is also very low (13.3% F-score). This performance gap raises questions about the current utility of the direct model and whether the proposed transition system is the right approach.

2.  **Confounding Factors in the Core Comparison:** The paper frames a key comparison as "direct method vs. indirect method". However, the comparison is confounded by the choice of classifier. The direct BSP uses a linear perceptron model, whereas the best indirect method relies on a much more powerful and modern LSTM-based classifier. The authors acknowledge this, suggesting LSTMs would improve BSP. While a fair point, this makes it difficult to conclude whether the performance gap is due to the superiority of the conversion *approach* or simply the superiority of the LSTM *classifier*. The paper would have been stronger if it had used comparable classifier technology for both approaches to allow for a more direct comparison of the parsing strategies.

3.  **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper presents overall performance metrics but offers little qualitative analysis of the errors. It is unclear what types of semantic or syntactic structures are challenging for each model. For instance, how do the parsers handle complex coordination, long-distance dependencies, or constructions with no clear head? A more detailed error analysis would provide deeper insight into the current limitations of BSS parsing and offer more concrete directions for future work beyond just "use LSTMs".

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the significant performance gap between the perceptron-based BSP and the LSTM-based dependency parser, could you elaborate on the decision to use a perceptron for your novel parser? Was this primarily a decision based on implementation complexity, or are there theoretical reasons to believe a linear model is a good starting point for this particular transition system?

2.  The back-conversion from dependency trees to UCCA-style structures in Algorithm 2 appears to rely on heuristics to reintroduce non-terminal nodes. Could you provide some analysis of the errors or structural artifacts introduced by this back-conversion? How confident are you that the high scores from the LSTM parser aren't partially inflated by producing simple, easy-to-convert structures that align well with the heuristic?

3.  The F-score for remote edges is very low. Could you provide some examples of the types of remote attachments BSP successfully identifies versus those it fails on? Is there a pattern to these errors (e.g., specific semantic roles, long-distance attachments) that could inform the design of better features or transitions for this subtask?
