**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations that feature non-terminal nodes, discontinuities, and multiple parents (DAG structures). The authors use the Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation (UCCA) scheme as a concrete instance of BSS. They propose and evaluate two distinct transition-based approaches to this novel parsing task. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted into more standard formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees or dependency trees), parsed with existing state-of-the-art parsers, and then converted back. The second approach is a novel, direct parser called BSP (Broad-coverage Semantic Parser), which uses a custom transition system to directly generate the full BSS, including remote edges that create the DAG structure. Experiments on UCCA-annotated corpora show that a conversion-based approach using a state-of-the-art LSTM dependency parser achieves the highest performance on the primary tree structure, while the direct BSP parser, though currently scoring lower, is uniquely capable of predicting the multi-parent remote edges.

**Contributions**

1.  **Introduction and Benchmarking of a New Parsing Task:** The primary contribution of this work is the formulation of the UCCA parsing task and the establishment of the first strong baseline results. By tackling a semantic representation that simultaneously requires handling constituency, discontinuity, and DAG structures, the paper carves out a novel and challenging problem space for the parsing community. The release of code and converters provides an essential foundation for future research in this area.
2.  **A Novel Integrated Transition System (BSP):** The paper presents BSP, a transition-based parser that unifies techniques for parsing discontinuous constituency structures (e.g., SWAP) and dependency DAGs (e.g., non-reducing edge actions, separate primary/remote edge transitions). While the components are inspired by prior work, their integration into a single, coherent system designed for a grounded, constituent-based DAG representation is novel and constitutes a solid technical contribution.
3.  **A Comparative Analysis of Direct vs. Conversion-Based Parsing:** The paper provides a valuable empirical comparison between adapting existing technology via conversion and building a new, specialized parser. The finding that an off-the-shelf LSTM dependency parser outperforms the specialized parser on the tree backbone is an important result, highlighting the power of modern classifiers and suggesting clear directions for improving the direct approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty of the Task:** The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature. While semantic representations like AMR and SDP have received significant attention, UCCA offers a unique, linguistically-motivated, and grounded alternative. Providing the first parsing methodology for this scheme is a timely and important contribution that can enable its use in downstream applications. The authors clearly motivate the need for the three properties of BSS (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities).
2.  **Thorough and Methodical Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength. The two-pronged approach (direct vs. conversion) is well-conceived. Comparing their novel parser not just to one baseline but to a range of strong parsers (UPARSE, MaltParser, Dyer et al.'s LSTM parser) provides a robust picture of the current state-of-the-art. The inclusion of upper-bound calculations for the conversion methods is particularly insightful, as it cleanly disentangles parsing error from information loss due to the conversion process itself. The evaluation on both in-domain and out-of-domain data further strengthens the empirical claims.
3.  **Principled Parser Design:** The design of the BSP parser is well-motivated, building logically on a mature line of research in transition-based parsing. The authors clearly explain how they adapt and combine transitions from discontinuous constituency parsing (Maier, 2015) and DAG dependency parsing (Sagae and Tsujii, 2008) to meet the demands of UCCA. The introduction of novel features, such as the `ratio` feature to regulate node creation, is a nice, simple addition.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Underperformance of the Main Technical Contribution (BSP):** The novel BSP parser is substantially outperformed on the primary evaluation metric (primary edges F-score) by the conversion-based approach using the LSTM parser (59.0 vs 69.2 F-score). While BSP is the only system that can predict remote edges, its performance on that subtask is very low (13.3 F-score), limiting its practical advantage. The paper acknowledges this gap but could benefit from a deeper discussion of *why* this gap exists. Is it purely the superiority of the LSTM classifier, or are there limitations in BSP's transition system or feature set that prevent it from modeling the tree structure as effectively? This performance gap somewhat diminishes the impact of the proposed novel parser in its current form.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Remote Edge Prediction:** The ability to predict remote edges is the key selling point of BSP over the stronger conversion-based methods. However, the analysis of this capability is limited to reporting a single low F-score. The paper would be much stronger with an error analysis of the predicted remote edges. What kinds of multi-parent phenomena is the parser capturing, and which is it failing on? Are the errors related to attachment ambiguity, long-distance dependencies that are hard for the feature model to capture, or something else? Without this analysis, it is difficult to assess the true promise of the direct approach or understand the path to improving its most unique feature.
3.  **Simplicity of the Dependency-to-Constituency Conversion:** The inverse conversion from dependency trees back to UCCA-style constituency structures (Algorithm 2) appears to rely on a simple, rule-based heuristic for re-introducing non-terminal nodes and their labels (e.g., mostly choosing "Center"). It is not clear how much information is lost or how many errors are introduced by this heuristic. A more detailed justification or an ablation study on the impact of this heuristic would be valuable. It is possible that a more sophisticated reconstruction method could push the dependency-based baseline even higher.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance gap between your BSP parser and the LSTM-based dependency parser on primary edges is over 10 F-score points. You suggest that incorporating an LSTM-based classifier into BSP is a promising direction. Beyond the classifier itself, do you have any hypotheses about whether aspects of your transition system or feature model might also be contributing to this gap? For instance, does the arc-standard system used by the LSTM parser have inherent advantages over your system for this task?
2.  Could you provide more insight into the 13.3 F-score performance on remote edges? For instance, what are the most common linguistic phenomena associated with the remote edges that BSP successfully predicts, and what are the phenomena it struggles with the most?
3.  Regarding the dependency-to-constituency conversion (Algorithm 2), how sensitive are the final reported scores for the dependency-based systems to the heuristic function `Label(u)` used for creating intermediate nodes? Did you experiment with any alternative reconstruction strategies?
