- Task: parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS) — DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities — using UCCA as the target formalism.  
- Methods: two approaches — conversion-based (lossy convert to trees/deps, parse with existing parsers, back-convert) and a direct transition-based parser (BSP).  
- Main experimental finding: conversion-based stack-LSTM achieves highest primary-edge performance; BSP alone can predict remote edges but with very low accuracy.  
- Contribution 1: formulates BSS parsing and proposes the first direct transition system (BSP) handling multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities jointly.  
- Contribution 2: provides a systematic empirical comparison between direct parsing and strong conversion-based baselines, establishing a benchmark.  
- Contribution 3: defines a quantitative evaluation metric for BSS graphs based on matching edges by terminal yields and labels.  
- Strength 1: tackles a novel, well-motivated parsing problem that pushes semantic parsing toward richer, broad-coverage representations.  
- Strength 2: thorough experimental design with multiple strong baselines and both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation.  
- Strength 3: BSP is technically well-designed, combining transitions from prior work sensibly and adding mechanisms for primary vs. remote edges and new features.  
- Strength 4: clear presentation and commitment to releasing code/converters, aiding reproducibility and future work.  
- Weakness 1: BSP is substantially outperformed on primary edges by a conversion-based LSTM parser, and its remote-edge performance is very low, limiting practical gains.  
- Weakness 2: limited qualitative/error analysis comparing BSP and conversion baselines; missing detailed failure-mode analysis for phenomena like coordination or MWEs.  
- Weakness 3: transition system novelty is incremental—mainly an engineering combination of existing techniques with minor additions.  
- Question 1: what explains the large primary-edge gap — LSTM classifier power vs. the conversion producing an easier tree-like target?  
- Question 2: why is BSP recall for remote edges so low — data sparsity, insufficient features, or limits of greedy left-to-right transitions?  
- Question 3: have you tried learned or more sophisticated back-conversion methods, and how much of the gap is due to irreversible information loss vs. parsing errors?
