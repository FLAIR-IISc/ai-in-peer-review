### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), which are defined as grounded semantic representations that accommodate (1) multiple parents (DAGs), (2) non-terminal nodes, and (3) discontinuous constituents. The paper uses the UCCA formalism as a concrete instance of BSS. The authors propose and evaluate two transition-based approaches to this task. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA structures are lossily converted into more standard formats (discontinuous constituency trees or dependency trees) to be parsed by existing state-of-the-art parsers. The second is a novel, direct transition-based parser, named BSP, specifically designed to produce BSS graphs by combining and extending transition systems from constituency, discontinuous, and DAG parsing. Experiments on the English UCCA corpus show that while the conversion-based approach using an LSTM-based dependency parser achieves the highest performance on the core tree structure, the proposed direct parser is the only method capable of handling all BSS properties, including multiple parents (remote edges).

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **Formulating the BSS parsing task and developing the first direct parser for it.** The paper clearly defines a challenging and important parsing problem that unifies several structural complexities often handled in isolation. The proposed BSP parser, with its novel transition system and features, is a significant first step toward directly modeling these structures and serves as a strong foundation for future work.
2.  **A thorough empirical investigation and benchmarking of UCCA parsing.** By exploring both direct and conversion-based methods, the paper provides the first comprehensive empirical results on parsing this semantic formalism. The use of conversion establishes strong baselines, highlights the information loss involved in simplification, and provides a valuable comparative context for the novel direct parser.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Problem Definition and High Significance.** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem. The introduction clearly explains why the combination of multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities is necessary for broad-coverage semantic representation, using intuitive examples. By formalizing this as the BSS parsing task and grounding it in an existing annotated resource (UCCA), the authors are effectively establishing a new, challenging, and valuable direction for the parsing community.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Comprehensive Approach.** The authors' decision to explore two distinct strategies—conversion-based and direct parsing—is a major strength. This dual approach provides a much richer analysis than proposing a single method in isolation. It allows for a fair comparison against strong, existing systems and contextualizes the performance of their novel parser (BSP). The inclusion of upper-bound calculations for the conversion methods is particularly insightful, as it cleanly separates the error introduced by the conversion from the error introduced by the parser.
3.  **Solid and Novel Parser Design.** The proposed BSP parser is a well-engineered system that thoughtfully combines elements from several successful transition-based parsing paradigms to tackle the novel task. The transition set (Figure 2) is a principled extension of existing systems to handle the specific requirements of UCCA, such as distinguishing primary vs. remote edges. The feature set is also comprehensive, adapting proven features from related work and introducing a novel, potentially generalizable `ratio` feature to regulate node creation.
4.  **Well-Designed and Reproducible Experiments.** The experimental setup is thorough. The use of both in-domain and out-of-domain test sets demonstrates the robustness of the proposed methods. The feature ablation study provides valuable insight into the contributions of different components of the BSP model. The authors also promise to release their code and converters, which is a great service to the community and will facilitate future research on this task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The Main Novel Contribution (BSP) is Outperformed by a Baseline.** The strongest argument against acceptance is that the paper's primary novel contribution, the BSP parser, achieves a primary edge F-score (59.9%) that is nearly 10 points lower than a conversion-based baseline using an existing LSTM dependency parser (69.2%). While BSP is the only system that can predict remote edges, its performance on them is very low (13.3% F-score). This performance gap raises a critical question: is the complexity of a direct parsing approach justified if a simpler, more effective method (conversion + off-the-shelf parser) exists for the bulk of the structure? The paper would be stronger if it either improved BSP's performance or provided a more compelling argument for why the direct approach is superior despite the current results (e.g., by showing a clearer path to improvement or analyzing structures it handles better).
2.  **Lack of In-Depth Error Analysis.** The paper presents results but misses an opportunity for a deeper analysis of the parsers' outputs. For instance, why does BSP perform so much worse than the LSTM parser on primary edges? Is the bottleneck in the greedy search, the feature representation, or the transition system? An error analysis comparing the types of mistakes made by BSP versus the conversion-based systems would be highly illuminating. Similarly, it would be useful to analyze the few remote edges that BSP *does* predict correctly—are they of a particular type or length? This analysis would provide crucial insights for future work.
3.  **The Impact of the LSTM Classifier is Understated.** The results show a massive performance gap between MaltParser (60.1% F-score) and the LSTM parser (69.2% F-score), even though both use an arc-standard transition system. The paper attributes this simply to the classifier. This is a very significant finding in itself and suggests that the choice of the local classifier is perhaps more important than the choice of parsing strategy (conversion vs. direct). The paper should place more emphasis on this finding and discuss its implications for their own BSP parser, which currently uses a linear perceptron model.
4.  **Details of the Dependency Conversion are Opaque.** The dependency-to-constituency inverse conversion (Algorithm 2) is a critical component for the best-performing system, yet it relies on heuristics that are not fully explained. The paper states that the `Label(u)` function, which determines labels for reconstructed nodes, is defined in supplementary material. Given the importance of this step (the dependency conversion upper bound is only 88.4%, indicating significant information loss), a more detailed justification of these heuristics in the main body of the paper would improve clarity and rigor.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gap between the perceptron-based MaltParser and the LSTM-based parser is striking. Have you considered replacing the linear classifier in your BSP parser with a more powerful neural classifier, such as the stack LSTM used by Dyer et al. (2015)? Do you believe this would be the most direct path to closing the performance gap on primary edges?
2.  Could you provide a brief qualitative error analysis? What are the most common types of errors BSP makes on primary edges compared to the LSTM-based conversion parser? For the remote edges, what distinguishes the few that are correctly identified from the many that are missed?
3.  The `ratio` feature is an interesting and novel contribution. The ablation study shows it primarily boosts recall. Could you elaborate on the intuition here? Why would regularizing the creation of non-terminals relative to the number of terminals specifically prevent recall errors?
