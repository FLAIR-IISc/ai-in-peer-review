**SUMMARY AND CONTRIBUTIONS**

This paper addresses the task of parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), a class of representations characterized by three key properties: multiple parents (DAG structures), non-terminal nodes (constituency-like), and discontinuous units. The authors use the UCCA annotation scheme as a concrete instance of BSS. The paper proposes and evaluates two transition-based approaches to this novel parsing problem. The first is a conversion-based method, where UCCA graphs are lossily converted to more standard formalisms (discontinuous constituency trees or dependency trees), parsed with existing state-of-the-art parsers, and converted back. The second is a direct approach, for which the authors design a new transition-based parser, the Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), capable of generating all three BSS properties directly. Experiments are conducted on UCCA-annotated data, showing that a conversion-based approach using a stack-LSTM parser achieves the highest performance on the core tree structure (primary edges), while the proposed BSP is the only model capable of predicting the graph-based phenomena (remote edges), albeit with low performance.

The main contributions are:
1.  The formulation of the BSS parsing task and the proposal of the first direct parsing model (BSP) that jointly handles multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities in a single transition system.
2.  A systematic empirical study comparing the proposed direct parsing method against strong conversion-based baselines that leverage existing state-of-the-art parsing technology. This provides a clear benchmark for a new parsing task.
3.  The definition of a quantitative evaluation metric for BSS graphs, based on matching edges by their terminal yields and labels, which is necessary for evaluating this type of structure.

**STRENGTHS**

1.  **Addresses a novel and well-motivated parsing problem.** The paper tackles a new parsing task for semantic representations (UCCA) that combine structural properties not jointly handled by existing parsers. By aiming to parse structures that include DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities, the work pushes the boundaries of semantic parsing towards more comprehensive and flexible representations of meaning. This is a timely contribution given the community's growing interest in broad-coverage semantic analysis.
2.  **Thorough experimental design and strong baselines.** The authors evaluate two distinct and sensible strategies: adapting existing technology via conversion and building a novel, specialized parser. The use of multiple, strong, and varied baselines for the conversion approach—including standard tools like MaltParser and a state-of-the-art stack-LSTM parser—provides a rigorous context for evaluating the new BSP model. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain test sets further strengthens the empirical evaluation by demonstrating the robustness of the methods.
3.  **Technically sound design of the direct parser (BSP).** The proposed BSP parser is a well-reasoned synthesis of established techniques from related transition-based parsing literature. It logically combines mechanisms for constituency parsing (e.g., NODE transition), discontinuous parsing (SWAP), and DAG parsing (separating edge creation from REDUCE). The extension with specific transitions to distinguish primary from remote edges is a direct and appropriate solution for the target formalism. The feature set is similarly well-grounded in prior work, with the addition of a novel and intuitive `ratio` feature.
4.  **Clarity and potential for future work.** The paper is clearly written, motivating the problem well and describing the proposed methods, including algorithms for the conversion procedures, in sufficient detail. The authors' commitment to releasing their code and converters is a significant strength, promoting reproducibility and enabling the community to build upon this foundational work in BSS/UCCA parsing.

**WEAKNESSES**

1.  **The proposed novel parser (BSP) is significantly outperformed by a baseline.** The primary contribution, the BSP parser, achieves a labeled F-score of 59.9% on primary edges. This is nearly 10 F-score points lower than the 69.2% achieved by a conversion-based method using an existing LSTM parser. While BSP is unique in its ability to predict remote edges, its performance on that subtask is very low (13.3% F-score), limiting the practical advantage of this capability. The results suggest that, for parsing the core tree structure of UCCA, a simpler conversion-based approach is currently superior.
2.  **Limited qualitative analysis of results.** The paper presents aggregate precision, recall, and F-score metrics but lacks a deeper analysis of the parser outputs. An error analysis comparing the types of mistakes made by BSP versus the conversion-based methods would be highly informative. For example, it is unclear how the different approaches handle specific phenomena like coordination, multi-word expressions, or structures that are particularly affected by the lossy conversions. Such an analysis would provide more valuable insight into the remaining challenges of the task.
3.  **The novelty of the transition system is incremental.** While the application of the transition system to UCCA parsing is novel, its core components are adapted directly from prior work on discontinuous constituency parsing and dependency DAG parsing. The primary architectural novelty lies in the combination of these existing mechanisms and the addition of `LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTE` transitions. The work is a strong piece of engineering and application, but the technical novelty in terms of the underlying parsing algorithm is limited.

**QUESTIONS TO AUTHORS**

1.  The performance gap on primary edges between the LSTM-based dependency parser and your direct BSP model is substantial. What are your thoughts on the primary source of this gap? Do you attribute it mainly to the representational power of the LSTM classifier over the linear perceptron, or do you suspect that the simplified, tree-like dependency representation created by the conversion is an easier target for the parser to learn?
2.  The recall for remote edges in the BSP model is extremely low (11.8%). Could you provide some analysis as to why this is the case? Is it primarily a data sparsity issue, a failure of the feature set to capture the necessary non-local information, or an inherent limitation of the greedy, left-to-right nature of the transition-based model in predicting these long-distance dependencies?
3.  Your upper-bound analysis shows that the dependency conversion itself results in a significant information loss (max F-score of 88.4%). The back-conversion from dependencies to UCCA in Algorithm 2 seems to rely on simple heuristics. Have you considered or experimented with more sophisticated back-conversion models, perhaps a learned model, and do you have a sense of how much of the performance gap is due to parsing errors versus irreversible information loss during the round-trip conversion?
