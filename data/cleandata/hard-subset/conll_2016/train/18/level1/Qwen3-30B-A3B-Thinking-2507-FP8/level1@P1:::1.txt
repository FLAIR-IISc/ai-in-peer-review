

# Peer Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a critical gap in semantic parsing: the lack of parsers supporting all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representations (BSS): multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex units with no clear head), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors present two complementary approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing, where UCCA structures are converted to dependency or constituency trees and processed with existing parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) directly supporting all three properties. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. The first parser (BSP) that directly supports all three structural properties of BSS, addressing a significant gap in the literature.
2. The first comprehensive evaluation of UCCA as a semantic representation for broad-coverage parsing, demonstrating its viability as a standard for semantic parsing research.
3. A thorough exploration of conversion-based approaches that reveals the trade-offs between using existing parsers versus developing new ones for BSS.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a critical gap in semantic parsing**: The paper compellingly argues that no existing semantic parser supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation. The authors provide strong evidence from the literature and concrete examples (Figures 1a-c) to demonstrate this gap, making a strong case for the necessity of their work.

2. **Well-justified approach to UCCA parsing**: The paper provides a solid foundation for UCCA as a semantic representation scheme, highlighting its cross-linguistic applicability, semantic stability across translations, and comprehensive coverage of linguistic phenomena. The authors demonstrate that UCCA is the only representation supporting all three BSS properties, making it the ideal test case for their work.

3. **Strong empirical results with meaningful comparisons**: The authors present comprehensive experiments comparing conversion-based approaches with their direct BSP parser. The results (Table 2) show that BSP achieves nearly 70% labeled F-score for primary edges, with reasonable performance on remote edges (15.3% F-score), which is impressive given the task's difficulty. The out-of-domain results demonstrate robustness, suggesting practical applicability.

4. **Novel transition set and features for BSP**: The paper introduces a well-motivated set of transitions (including LEFT/RIGHT-EDGEX, LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features specifically designed for BSS parsing. The ratio feature (number of terminals to nodes) is particularly innovative and could benefit other transition-based parsers.

5. **Clear roadmap for future work**: The paper effectively identifies directions for improvement, particularly suggesting that applying LSTM-based classifiers (as in Dyer et al., 2015) could significantly boost performance, which is supported by their results showing the LSTM parser outperforms other dependency parsers by 9.1% F-score.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient justification for evaluation metrics**: The authors define custom evaluation metrics for comparing BSS structures, but don't adequately justify why these are superior to existing metrics (e.g., those used for AMR or SDP). The paper should provide more evidence that their metrics correlate with human judgment or better capture semantic accuracy than standard approaches.

2. **Limited discussion of conversion-based approach limitations**: The paper acknowledges that conversion to dependency trees loses significant information (remote edges), but doesn't sufficiently explore how this loss affects downstream applications. The authors should clarify whether the conversion-based approach is merely a baseline or has practical value beyond that.

3. **Inadequate comparison with related work**: The authors claim their parser is the first to support all three properties, but don't adequately compare with related work like SDP (Oepen et al., 2015), which also supports multiple parents and discontinuous units. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty.

4. **Limited discussion of scalability and language coverage**: The evaluation is limited to English UCCA corpora, with no exploration of how the approach scales to other languages or larger datasets. Given UCCA's claimed cross-linguistic applicability, this is a significant omission that weakens the paper's broader claims.

5. **Underdeveloped discussion of remote edge handling**: While the paper introduces LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX transitions, it doesn't sufficiently discuss why remote edges are so challenging to predict (only 15.3% F-score) or how the parser might be improved to better handle them. This is particularly important since remote edges represent the most distinctive aspect of BSS.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that UCCA is the only semantic representation supporting all three BSS properties. Can you clarify how UCCA differs from other representations like AMR or SDP in terms of these properties, and why the other representations cannot be adapted to support all three properties?

2. The authors claim that the conversion-based approach "is effective in practice" (Section 3), but the results show that dependency conversion yields 0% remote edge F-score. How do the authors reconcile this with their claim of effectiveness, especially given that remote edges are a key part of BSS?

3. The paper mentions that BSP "reaches nearly 70% labeled F-score for the highest scoring parser." Can you provide more details on the specific parser configuration that achieved this result, and whether the performance was consistent across different UCCA layers?

4. The paper states that "UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (often taken as an upper bound) of 80-85% F-score on primary edges." How do the authors account for the gap between this upper bound and their best result of 73.2%? Is this gap primarily due to the parser's limitations, or is it partly due to annotation inconsistencies?
