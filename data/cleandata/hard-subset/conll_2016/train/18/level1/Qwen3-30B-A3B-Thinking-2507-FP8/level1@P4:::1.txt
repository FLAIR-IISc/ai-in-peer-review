

# Review: Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper addresses the problem of parsing semantic structures that support three key properties: multiple parents (to represent shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (to represent multi-word units), and discontinuous units (to represent phrasal verbs and multi-word expressions). The authors argue that no existing parser supports all three properties simultaneously, and they introduce the first parser that does so. The main contributions are:

1. The first parser (BSP) that supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing (BSS), which the authors demonstrate is a significant gap in the literature.
2. The introduction of UCCA as a test case for BSS parsing, establishing it as the only existing semantic annotation scheme that supports all three properties.
3. The development of two complementary parsing strategies: conversion-based approaches (mapping BSS to dependency trees or constituency trees) and a direct transition-based parser for BSS.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem definition and technical soundness**: The paper provides a precise and well-structured definition of the three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic parsing. The authors effectively demonstrate that no existing parser supports all three properties simultaneously, establishing a clear gap in the literature. The BSP parser is technically sound, with a carefully designed transition set and feature set that extends existing work on dependency DAG parsing and discontinuous constituency parsing. The paper's evaluation metrics are well-defined for the specific representation (BSS), and the results demonstrate that BSP achieves 69.2% F-score on primary edges, which is comparable to state-of-the-art conversion-based approaches.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation comparing both conversion-based approaches and the direct BSP approach. The evaluation includes both in-domain and out-of-domain testing, with a detailed feature ablation study (Table 3) that demonstrates the relative importance of different feature sets. The authors also provide upper bounds for conversion-based methods, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of the limitations of these approaches.

3. **Establishment of a benchmark for future work**: By using UCCA-annotated corpora as the test case, the paper establishes the first benchmark for broad-coverage semantic parsing. The paper's analysis of UCCA's applicability across multiple languages and its semantic stability in translation provides strong justification for the choice of this annotation scheme as a test case. This will likely serve as a foundation for future work in the field.

4. **Technical contribution to transition-based parsing**: The paper makes a valuable contribution to transition-based parsing by extending it to handle the specific challenges of BSS, particularly the handling of remote edges and discontinuous units. The novel "ratio" feature (used as a regularizer for node creation) and the UCCA-specific features (e.g., remote features) demonstrate thoughtful adaptation of existing techniques to the new problem space.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core approach**: While the paper is the first to develop a parser that supports all three structural properties, the core approach (transition-based parsing) is not novel. The paper builds on existing work (e.g., Maier, 2015; Tokgöz and Eryiğit, 2015) rather than introducing a fundamentally new parsing algorithm. The main novelty lies in adapting existing techniques to handle the specific structural properties of BSS, which is significant but does not constitute a major algorithmic innovation.

2. **Limited comparison with related semantic parsing approaches**: The paper compares with conversion-based approaches and a few dependency parsers, but it does not compare directly with other semantic parsing approaches that might have different representation schemes (e.g., AMR parsers). The paper acknowledges that AMR parsing is different due to lack of grounding, but a more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's position in the broader landscape of semantic parsing.

3. **Overly optimistic upper bounds for conversion methods**: The paper reports upper bounds for conversion-based methods as 100% for primary edges (constituency conversion) and 93.8% for primary edges (dependency conversion). However, these upper bounds are calculated by applying conversion and inverse conversion on the gold standard graphs, which may not reflect the true potential of conversion-based approaches as they don't account for errors introduced during the conversion process. This could mislead readers about the potential performance of conversion-based methods.

4. **Limited discussion of practical implications**: While the paper mentions applications like machine translation and summarization, it lacks concrete examples or analysis of how the proposed parser would improve these applications. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the practical impact of the proposed parser, particularly given the authors' claims about UCCA's merits in supporting "ongoing efforts to incorporate deeper semantic structures into a variety of applications."

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that SDP (Broad-coverage Semantic Dependency Parsing) supports discontinuous units and multiple parents but disallows non-terminal nodes. Could the authors clarify if SDP might support some non-terminal nodes through other means, or if it truly cannot represent coordination structures?

2. The paper reports that BSP achieves 69.2% F-score on primary edges, while the UCCA inter-annotator agreement is 80-85%. Could the authors provide more detail on the discrepancy between the parser performance and the inter-annotator agreement, and what factors might be contributing to this gap?

3. The paper mentions that the conversion to dependency format loses considerable information due to the loss of non-terminal nodes. Could the authors provide an example of a specific structure that is lost in this conversion and how it affects the parsing performance?
