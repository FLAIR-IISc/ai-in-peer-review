- Summary: Proposes a transition-based semantic parser (BSP) for UCCA that handles multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units—properties missing together in prior parsers.

- Contribution 1: First parser directly supporting all three structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities).

- Contribution 2: Conversion-based framework to map UCCA to dependency and constituency trees, enabling use of existing parsers as baselines.

- Contribution 3: First large-scale empirical evaluation on UCCA corpora, establishing baseline performance metrics.

- Strength 1: Addresses a clear gap in semantic parsing by supporting genuinely needed structural properties for broad-coverage semantics.

- Strength 2: Strong empirical results (69.2% primary F-score) close to UCCA inter-annotator agreement and robust in out-of-domain tests.

- Strength 3: Clear motivation and practical presentation of conversion procedures and parser design, linking to related work.

- Strength 4: Methodological innovations in transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features (remote edge, ratio regularizer) tailored to UCCA.

- Strength 5: Clear directions for future improvements (e.g., LSTM classifiers) and exploring conversions between semantic formalisms.

- Weakness 1: Core approach relies on existing transition-based parsing methodology; novelty is mainly in adapting it to BSS rather than inventing new parsing paradigms.

- Weakness 2: Incomplete comparisons to related work—lacks benchmarking against parsers handling subsets of the properties (e.g., SDP parsers).

- Weakness 3: Custom evaluation metric (edge-yield based) is not sufficiently justified against standard semantic parsing measures.

- Weakness 4: No error analysis provided, so common failure modes and limitations of the parser/BSS representation are unclear.

- Weakness 5: Observed 2–3 point F-score drop out-of-domain suggests limited generalization, but causes and remedies are not discussed.

- Question 1: Why does dependency-tree conversion show a large performance gap (88.4% upper bound vs. 69.2% LSTM) compared to constituency conversion?

- Question 2: Can the authors analyze how parser errors overlap with UCCA inter-annotator disagreements to contextualize the 69.2% F-score?

- Question 3: What semantic phenomena do remote edges capture, and why are remote edges so challenging (BSP 15.3% vs. conversion 0%)?

- Question 4: How well would BSP generalize to other languages—would the transition set and features transfer or need language-specific changes?
