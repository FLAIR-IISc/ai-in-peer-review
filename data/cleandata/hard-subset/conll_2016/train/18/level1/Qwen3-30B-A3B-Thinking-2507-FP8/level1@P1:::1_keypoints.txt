- Proposes two approaches (conversion-based and a novel transition-based BSP) to parse UCCA, targeting three BSS properties: multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities.
- Claims three main contributions: first parser supporting all BSS properties (BSP), first broad evaluation of UCCA for semantic parsing, and a thorough study of conversion-based trade-offs.

- Clearly identifies a critical gap in existing parsers and motivates the need for a BSS-capable parser with concrete examples.
- Justifies UCCA as a suitable representation due to cross-linguistic applicability, semantic stability, and support for all three BSS properties.
- Presents strong empirical comparisons showing BSP ≈70% labeled F-score on primary edges and 15.3% on remote edges, with robust out-of-domain performance.
- Introduces a novel transition set (including LEFT/RIGHT-EDGEX and -REMOTEX) and useful features (e.g., terminals-to-nodes ratio) for BSS parsing.
- Outlines clear future directions, notably applying LSTM-based classifiers to improve performance.

- Custom evaluation metrics are insufficiently justified and lack evidence of correlating with human judgment or outperforming standard metrics.
- Conversion-based approach limitations are under-explored, especially the practical impact of losing remote-edge information.
- Inadequate comparison with related work (e.g., SDP), weakening the novelty claim of being the first to support all three properties.
- Evaluation is limited to English UCCA corpora with no analysis of scalability or multilingual applicability.
- Remote-edge handling is underdeveloped: low 15.3% F-score is not sufficiently analyzed nor are clear improvements proposed.

- How exactly does UCCA differ from AMR or SDP regarding the three BSS properties, and why can't those formalisms be adapted?
- How do the authors justify calling the conversion-based approach "effective" given dependency conversion yields 0% remote-edge F-score?
- Which parser configuration achieved the nearly 70% labeled F-score, and was performance consistent across UCCA layers?
- Why is there a gap between UCCA inter-annotator agreement (80–85% F) and the best parser result (73.2%) — parser limitations or annotation noise?
