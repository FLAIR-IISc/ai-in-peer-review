- BSP: first parser that supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units simultaneously.  
- UCCA introduced as the test case for broad-coverage semantic parsing (BSS), claimed to be the only scheme with all three properties.  
- Two parsing strategies developed: conversion-based approaches (to dependency/constituency) and a direct transition-based parser (BSP).  
- Clear problem definition and technical soundness; precise property definitions, well-designed transition/feature sets, and BSP attains 69.2% F on primary edges.  
- Comprehensive evaluation: in-domain and out-of-domain tests, detailed feature ablation, and upper bounds for conversion methods.  
- Establishes a benchmark using UCCA corpora and argues for UCCA’s cross-lingual stability and suitability for future work.  
- Technical contributions to transition-based parsing: handling remote edges and discontinuities, novel "ratio" regularizer and UCCA-specific features.  
- Limited novelty: core approach adapts existing transition-based methods rather than introducing a fundamentally new algorithm.  
- Limited comparison with other semantic parsing frameworks (e.g., AMR and other representation schemes).  
- Upper bounds for conversion methods may be overly optimistic because they are computed via conversion+inverse on gold graphs and ignore conversion-induced errors.  
- Limited discussion of practical implications and concrete examples of how the parser helps applications like MT or summarization.  
- Question: Can SDP represent any non-terminal or coordination structures by other means, or does it truly disallow non-terminals?  
- Question: What explains the gap between BSP’s 69.2% F and UCCA inter-annotator agreement (80–85%)?  
- Question: Can authors give a concrete example of a structure lost in dependency conversion and how that loss affects parsing performance?
