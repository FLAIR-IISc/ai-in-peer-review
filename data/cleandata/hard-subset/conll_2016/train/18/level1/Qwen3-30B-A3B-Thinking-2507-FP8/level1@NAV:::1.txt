

This paper addresses an important gap in semantic parsing: the lack of parsers for semantic structures that support three key properties—multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units (collectively termed Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures or BSS). The authors propose two complementary approaches: (1) converting UCCA structures to more standard representations (dependency or constituency trees) and using existing parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser, BSP, that directly handles BSS. The main contributions are: (1) the first demonstration of feasible parsing for BSS using transition-based methods, (2) development of the BSP parser with specialized transitions for BSS properties, and (3) creation of conversion procedures between UCCA and other formalisms.

Strengths: The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying and addressing a clear gap in semantic parsing literature. The authors provide a well-motivated problem statement and demonstrate that BSS parsing is feasible with current transition-based techniques. The BSP parser is well-designed, incorporating novel transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX/RIGHT-REMOTEX) specifically for handling remote edges. The evaluation on the UCCA corpus is thorough, comparing both conversion-based and direct approaches, and the results (69.2% primary F-score) are promising given UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80-85%).

Weaknesses: The core parsing approach is not particularly novel, as BSP builds directly on prior transition-based parsing work (Maier, 2015). The paper lacks meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art semantic parsers for other representations (e.g., AMR), which would better contextualize the contribution. The evaluation metric (based on edge yield) is not well-justified and differs from standard semantic parsing metrics, making it difficult to assess the true progress. Additionally, the direct BSP approach only marginally outperforms the conversion-based LSTM approach (69.2% vs 69.2% primary F-score), weakening the argument for developing a specialized parser.

Questions to Authors: How does the BSP parser's performance compare to recent state-of-the-art AMR parsers when using similar transition-based approaches? Could you provide a more detailed error analysis to identify which structural properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) are most challenging for the parser? Why does the conversion to dependency trees (with LSTM) achieve nearly identical primary F-scores to the direct BSP approach, suggesting the need for a specialized parser is questionable?
