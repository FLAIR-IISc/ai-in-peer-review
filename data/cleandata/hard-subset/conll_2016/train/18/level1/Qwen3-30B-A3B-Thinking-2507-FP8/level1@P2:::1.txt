

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to semantic parsing that addresses three critical structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation: multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex semantic units), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors argue that no existing parser supports all three properties simultaneously, and the only semantic representation scheme supporting them is UCCA (Universal Cognitive Conceptual Annotation), which lacks a parser.

The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. **First parser supporting all three structural properties**: The authors present the first transition-based parser (BSP) that directly supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, filling a significant gap in semantic parsing research.

2. **Conversion-based parsing framework**: They develop a systematic approach to convert UCCA structures to dependency trees and constituency trees, enabling the use of existing parsers on BSS data, which provides a valuable baseline for evaluating the need for specialized parsers.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation on UCCA**: The authors conduct the first large-scale evaluation of semantic parsing on UCCA-annotated corpora, establishing baseline performance metrics for this important but previously unexplored task.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to semantic parsing**: The paper identifies and addresses a genuine gap in semantic parsing research. The three structural properties are well-motivated, and the authors provide compelling evidence that no existing parser supports all three properties. This is a substantial contribution to the field, as it opens the door to more comprehensive semantic representations that better capture natural language phenomena.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of both conversion-based approaches and the novel BSP parser. The results (69.2% primary F-score) are impressive given that UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is only 80-85%, suggesting the approach is close to the theoretical upper bound. The out-of-domain testing also demonstrates the parser's robustness across different domains.

3. **Practical utility and clarity**: The paper clearly articulates the importance of the three structural properties and explains why they matter for semantic parsing. The conversion procedures are well-defined and the BSP parser is carefully designed with appropriate transitions and features. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of how their work connects to related semantic parsing approaches.

4. **Methodological innovation**: The BSP parser introduces novel transitions (LEFT-REMOTEX, RIGHT-REMOTEX) and features specifically designed for UCCA's structure, particularly the "remote" edge feature. The ratio feature, which serves as a regularizer for node creation, is a clever addition that could benefit other transition-based parsers.

5. **Clear path for future work**: The paper identifies clear directions for improvement (e.g., using LSTM classifiers) and suggests how this work can inform the design of better semantic representations. The authors also plan to explore conversion-based approaches between different semantic representations, which could help clarify relationships between them.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the paper's contribution is significant, the transition-based parsing framework itself is not novel. The main innovation is adapting existing transition-based parsing techniques to handle BSS properties, not the parsing methodology itself. The paper could better emphasize what is truly novel about their approach versus existing transition-based parsers.

2. **Insufficient comparison to relevant work**: The paper claims UCCA is the only scheme supporting all three properties, but doesn't adequately compare their parser's performance to parsers that handle subsets of these properties (e.g., SDP parsers that handle multiple parents and discontinuities but not non-terminal nodes). This comparison would better establish the value of their full BSS approach.

3. **Evaluation methodology limitations**: The custom evaluation metric (based on edge yield) is not justified in comparison to standard semantic parsing evaluation measures. The paper should explain why this measure is appropriate and how it relates to other evaluation approaches in semantic parsing.

4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper reports F-scores but doesn't provide any analysis of the types of errors the parser makes. Understanding common error patterns would be valuable for both improving the parser and understanding the limitations of the BSS representation.

5. **Out-of-domain performance drop**: The out-of-domain results show a 2-3 point F-score drop compared to in-domain results, suggesting the model may not generalize well. The paper doesn't discuss why this occurs or what could be done to improve domain robustness.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify why the conversion to dependency trees results in such a significant performance drop (88.4% upper bound vs. 69.2% for LSTM) compared to the constituency tree conversion (88.4% upper bound vs. 65.4% for UPARSE)? What specific properties of the dependency conversion are most problematic for BSS parsing?

2. The paper mentions that UCCA has inter-annotator agreement of 80-85%, yet the best parser achieves only 69.2% F-score. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of where the parser's errors align with inter-annotator disagreements, and how this affects the interpretation of your results?

3. The BSP parser with remote edges achieves 15.3% F-score on remote edges, while conversion-based approaches achieve 0%. Could you elaborate on the types of semantic phenomena that the remote edges capture, and why they are particularly challenging for parsers to predict?

4. How would you expect the performance of BSP to scale to other languages that have different syntactic structures and semantic phenomena? Would the same transition set and features generalize well, or would language-specific modifications be required?
