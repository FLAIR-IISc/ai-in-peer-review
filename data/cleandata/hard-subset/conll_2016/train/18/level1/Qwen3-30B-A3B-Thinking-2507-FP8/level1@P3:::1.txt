

# Peer Review for "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the gap in semantic parsing for representations supporting all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS): multiple parents (for shared arguments), non-terminal nodes (for complex semantic units), and discontinuous units (for multi-word expressions). The authors present two approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing, where UCCA structures are converted to dependency or constituency trees and parsed with existing parsers; and (2) a novel transition-based parser (BSP) directly supporting all three properties. The main contributions I identify are:

1. The first parser to directly support all three BSS properties, filling a significant gap in the literature.
2. The detailed development and analysis of conversion procedures between UCCA and more standard formalisms, along with a thorough evaluation of their trade-offs.
3. Demonstrating that transition-based methods can be effectively adapted for BSS parsing, with results showing promising performance (69.2% primary F-score).

## Strengths

1. **Significant theoretical contribution**: The paper identifies a clear gap in semantic parsing research—no existing parser supports all three structural properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation. The authors provide a compelling theoretical argument for why these properties are necessary (multiple parents for shared arguments, non-terminal nodes for complex constructions like coordination, and discontinuous units for phrasal verbs), and demonstrate that UCCA is the only annotation scheme supporting all three. This is a substantial contribution to the field's understanding of semantic representation requirements.

2. **Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments on the UCCA-annotated corpora, including both in-domain and out-of-domain testing. Their evaluation includes a detailed ablation study of feature sets, which provides valuable insights into what aspects of their approach are most effective. The comparison between conversion-based approaches and their direct BSP method is particularly valuable, showing that while conversion approaches lose information, they still achieve respectable performance.

3. **Practical utility and community contribution**: The authors make their converters and parsers publicly available, which is highly valuable for the community. The results (69.2% primary F-score) are competitive with the upper bound provided by UCCA's inter-annotator agreement (80-85%), suggesting that the approach is on the right track and has room for improvement. The paper also provides clear paths for future work, such as incorporating LSTM-based classifiers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the application to UCCA is novel, the transition-based parsing approach itself is not new. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate how their BSP differs from prior work on transition-based parsing for related semantic representations (e.g., Wang et al. 2015 for AMR, Maier 2015 for discontinuous constituency parsing). The key innovations are the adaptation of existing transition-based methods to handle all three BSS properties, but this is not clearly articulated as a distinct contribution.

2. **Questionable evaluation metric**: The authors introduce a novel evaluation metric based on "edge yields" that compares predicted and gold-standard edges based on the set of terminals they cover. This metric lacks clear justification and doesn't align with standard semantic parsing evaluation practices. The paper doesn't demonstrate how this metric correlates with practical application performance or how it compares to existing metrics used for similar representations.

3. **Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper compares primarily to conversion-based approaches and their BSP, but doesn't compare to the state-of-the-art semantic parsers for related representations (like AMR parsers). Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether their approach is competitive with the best existing methods for similar tasks. The claim that "the results suggest concrete paths for further improvement" is weakened by this lack of benchmarking.

4. **Limited scope of experiments**: The evaluation is restricted to English UCCA-annotated corpora, with only one out-of-domain test (20K Leagues). There's no analysis of how the approach might scale to other languages or more diverse domains. Given UCCA's cross-linguistic applicability (mentioned in the paper), this limitation significantly reduces the perceived impact of the work.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your evaluation metric (based on edge yields) relate to standard semantic parsing evaluation metrics (e.g., those used for AMR or SDP)? Why is this metric more appropriate for your task than existing metrics?

2. The paper claims to be the first to support all three structural properties, but how does your approach differ from prior work on dependency DAG parsing (e.g., Sagae and Tsujii, 2008; Tokgöz and Eryiğit, 2015)? What specific adaptations were needed to handle the full UCCA representation?

3. The authors note that the LSTM parser achieves higher scores than MaltParser. Why not use the LSTM-based approach for BSP directly, rather than training a separate parser for conversion-based approaches? How would this affect your results?

4. UCCA's inter-annotator agreement is 80-85% F-score. How does this compare to the inter-annotator agreement for other semantic representations (like AMR or SDP)? Is the lower performance of your parser (69.2% vs. 80-85%) primarily due to parser limitations or annotation inconsistencies?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of broad-coverage semantic parsing requirements and provides a promising first step toward parsing UCCA structures. With more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods, better justification of the evaluation metric, and broader experimental scope, the work would be significantly strengthened.
