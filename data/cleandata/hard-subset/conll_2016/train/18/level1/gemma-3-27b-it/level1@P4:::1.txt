This paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on UCCA annotation. The authors identify the need for parsers capable of handling multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, features absent in existing parsers tailored for UCCA. They propose two strategies: conversion to simpler formalisms (dependency and constituency trees) and direct parsing with a novel transition-based parser (BSP).

The primary contribution of this work is the first attempt at building a parser directly supporting the full set of properties required for BSS, as represented by UCCA. While conversion-based approaches exist, the core novelty lies in the design and evaluation of BSP, a transition-based parser extended to accommodate multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation on UCCA corpora, providing a baseline for future research in this area. Finally, the authors contribute a comparative analysis of conversion-based parsing as a stepping stone, which demonstrates the limitations of relying on existing, more restrictive parsing technologies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Clear Gap:** The paper clearly identifies a significant gap in existing parsing technology – the lack of support for BSS and the specific requirements of UCCA annotation. This justification provides strong motivation for the work.
2. **Novel Parser Design:** The proposed BSP parser represents a novel adaptation of transition-based parsing techniques to address the challenges of UCCA. The introduction of new transitions (LEFTREMOTEX, RIGHTREMOTEX) and features specifically designed for remote edges and non-terminal nodes is a solid engineering contribution.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation, including both in-domain and out-of-domain tests.  The comparison with conversion-based methods and baselines provides valuable insights into the performance of BSP and the effectiveness of the different approaches. The feature ablation study further strengthens the evaluation.
4. **Public Availability:** The commitment to making the converters and parsers publicly available fosters reproducibility and encourages further research in the area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Improvement over Conversion-Based Methods:** While BSP is a novel approach, the performance gains over conversion-based methods are relatively modest, especially considering the complexity of the parser.  The F-score differences are not substantial, suggesting that the conversion-based methods are already reasonably effective.
2. **Simplified Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation metrics, while clearly defined, are relatively simplistic and might not fully capture the nuances of BSS parsing quality. Relying solely on precision, recall, and F-score for edges can overlook structural errors in the generated graphs.
3. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by BSP (e.g., incorrect edge labels, incorrect attachment points) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.
4. **Limited Comparative Analysis with other Parsing Paradigms:** The comparison is limited to transition-based parsers and conversion-based approaches. Comparing against other parsing paradigms better suited for DAGs or discontinuous structures could provide more context and identify potential avenues for improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by BSP? Specifically, what are the most common types of incorrect predictions (e.g., wrong edge labels, incorrect attachment points)?
2. The “ratio” feature seems promising. Can you elaborate on its specific impact on performance, and its interaction with other features?
3. Did you experiment with beam search during inference for BSP? If so, what was the effect on performance? If not, why?
4. Could you briefly discuss the computational complexity of the BSP parser compared to the conversion-based methods?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive.
*   The figures and tables are clear and well-presented.
*   The abstract provides a concise summary of the work.
*   The notation is well-defined and consistent throughout the paper.
