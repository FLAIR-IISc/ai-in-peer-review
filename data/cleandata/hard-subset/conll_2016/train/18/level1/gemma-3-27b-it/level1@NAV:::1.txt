## Review of "Broad-Coverage Semantic Parsing: A Transition-Based Approach"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of parsing semantic structures that go beyond typical syntactic dependencies, specifically focusing on structures exhibiting multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities – which they term Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS). The authors identify a lack of existing parsers capable of handling these features and propose two transition-based approaches: conversion to simpler formalisms (dependency trees and constituency trees) followed by parsing, and direct parsing of BSS using a novel transition-based parser (BSP).

The primary contribution of this work lies in the **initial exploration of transition-based parsing for UCCA**, a semantic annotation scheme that *does* support the required BSS properties. While transition-based parsing is well-established for syntactic parsing, its application to structures with multiple parents and discontinuities is a valuable starting point. A second, more modest contribution is the **demonstration of a viable baseline using conversion-based parsing**.  This provides a useful comparison point for the more complex direct parsing approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Gap:** The paper correctly identifies a gap in existing parsing technology regarding BSS. The UCCA annotation scheme is a sensible choice for experimentation, as it uniquely embodies the required structural properties.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The use of transition-based parsing is a reasonable approach given its success in related tasks.  The introduction of new transitions to handle UCCA-specific features (remote edges) is well-motivated.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments, using both in-domain and out-of-domain data, offer a good assessment of the proposed methods.  Reporting precision, recall, and F-score for both primary and remote edges is commendable. 
4. **Public Availability:** The promise to release code and data is a significant strength, facilitating reproducibility and future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains:** While BSP achieves reasonable F-scores (around 62-63%), the gains over conversion-based methods are relatively modest, particularly considering the complexity of the direct parsing approach.  The performance isn't a significant leap beyond existing parsing technologies.
2. **Feature Engineering:**  The feature set, while extensive, relies heavily on features borrowed from other parsing paradigms. The novel "ratio" feature is interesting, but its impact doesn’t appear dramatically significant based on the presentation. Further investigation into UCCA-specific features could yield better results.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper would benefit from a comparison to more recent and sophisticated parsing models, particularly those utilizing neural networks (e.g., LSTM-based parsers). The comparison to the LSTM dependency parser demonstrates the potential for improvement, but the methodology is not directly replicated in the BSP model.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered during the development of the BSP parser that prevented achieving significantly higher performance compared to the conversion-based approaches?
2.  What is the computational cost of the BSP parser compared to the conversion-based methods? (e.g., training time, parsing speed).
3.  What is the distribution of the remote edges in the UCCA corpus? Could you provide more analysis on their properties and the challenges in predicting them accurately?
