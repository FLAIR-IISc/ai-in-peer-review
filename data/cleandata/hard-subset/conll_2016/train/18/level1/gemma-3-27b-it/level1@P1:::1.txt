This paper introduces a transition-based approach to parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The authors identify three key properties – multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units – that are crucial for broad semantic coverage, and which existing parsers struggle to handle jointly. They propose two strategies: converting UCCA structures into more readily parsable formalisms (dependency trees and constituency trees) and building a novel transition-based parser (BSP) directly supporting BSS. The experiments on UCCA corpora demonstrate the effectiveness of transition-based methods, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **First Transition-Based Parser for BSS:** The most significant contribution is the development and evaluation of BSP, the first transition-based parser capable of directly handling the complexities of BSS as defined by the authors. This addresses a clear gap in existing parsing technology.
2. **Empirical Evaluation on UCCA:** The paper provides a valuable empirical evaluation of parsing BSS using the UCCA dataset. This dataset is unique in supporting all three key properties identified and serves as a vital testbed for assessing parsing techniques. The results establish a baseline for future work in this area.
3. **Investigation of Conversion-Based Parsing:** While less novel, the investigation of converting UCCA structures to dependency and constituency trees provides a useful comparison point and insights into the information loss inherent in such transformations. This exploration highlights the limitations of relying on existing parsing tools for BSS.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of broad-coverage semantic parsing and the limitations of existing approaches. The identification of the three key properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuity) provides a strong rationale for the proposed work.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** Transition-based parsing is a logical choice given its success in dependency parsing and other semantic/syntactic tasks. The authors convincingly argue for its suitability to the UCCA structure.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, including both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluations, and comparison with conversion-based methods.  The inclusion of feature ablation studies is also a strength, demonstrating the impact of different feature sets.
4. **Availability of Resources:** The authors commit to making the converters and parsers publicly available, which will be a valuable resource for the research community.
5. **Solid Baseline Performance:** Achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score on a complex parsing task like UCCA is a good starting point and demonstrates the viability of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Transition System:** The proposed transition system, while extended to handle the properties of BSS, largely builds upon existing transition-based parsing techniques. The novelty lies more in the application to this specific task than in a fundamentally new parsing paradigm.
2. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Semantic Parsers:** The comparison is primarily focused on converting UCCA to formats handled by existing *syntactic* parsers. A direct comparison against existing semantic parsers, like those for AMR, even if not directly comparable due to grounding differences, would be beneficial to contextualize the performance.
3. **Feature Engineering:** While the feature ablation study is helpful, the feature set itself appears somewhat complex and relies on manually designed features. Exploring more automatic feature learning techniques (e.g., using neural networks) could potentially improve performance and reduce the need for extensive feature engineering.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The proposed evaluation metric, based on edge matching, may not fully capture the quality of the parse. A more holistic evaluation that considers the overall structure and semantic coherence of the parsed graph might be more informative.
5. **Limited Analysis of Errors:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by BSP (e.g., incorrect remote edges, missed non-terminals) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention a potential improvement by applying an LSTM-based approach to the local classifier. Could you elaborate on the planned implementation and expected performance gains?
2.  How sensitive is BSP’s performance to the MINUPDATE and IMPORTANCE parameters? Did you explore a wider range of values for these parameters during hyperparameter tuning?
3.  Can you provide more details about the specific types of errors made by BSP? Are there particular linguistic phenomena or UCCA categories that pose the greatest challenges?
4.  While you mention the limitations of comparing to AMR parsers due to grounding differences, could you briefly discuss how BSP’s performance compares qualitatively to the state-of-the-art on AMR parsing, and what the key challenges are in directly comparing these approaches?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are extensive and well-organized.
*   The figures are helpful in illustrating the concepts and examples.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between UCCA and other semantic representation schemes like AMR and SDP. This would help readers better understand the unique challenges addressed by the proposed work.
