This paper proposes two transition-based approaches for parsing Broad-coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), specifically focusing on UCCA annotations, which uniquely support multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. The authors first explore converting UCCA structures to dependency and constituency trees to leverage existing parsers, and then present a novel transition-based parser (BSP) designed to directly handle BSS. The experiments demonstrate the feasibility of transition-based methods for UCCA parsing, achieving nearly 70% labeled F-score.

The primary contribution of this work lies in *being the first to systematically address the problem of parsing UCCA structures with a transition-based approach*. While the individual components – transition-based parsing and UCCA – are well-established, their combination to tackle the complexities of BSS is novel and represents a significant step forward. The exploration of both conversion-based and direct parsing strategies provides a valuable comparison and highlights the potential of directly modeling BSS. A second, related contribution is the *empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of transition-based parsing on UCCA*. The reported results establish a strong baseline for future work and validate the suitability of this paradigm for handling the intricate structural properties of BSS. The ablation studies, while limited, provide some initial insight into feature importance. Finally, the authors contribute *a publicly available implementation of converters and a parser*, facilitating further research in this area. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Significance of the Problem:** The work tackles a genuinely challenging problem – parsing semantic representations that go beyond standard syntactic structures. UCCA is a valuable, under-utilized resource, and enabling its automated parsing is crucial for broader adoption. The limitations of existing parsers in handling BSS are clearly articulated, motivating the proposed approaches.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is well-defined, using established evaluation metrics and providing both in-domain and out-of-domain results. The use of the 20K Leagues corpus for out-of-domain evaluation demonstrates a degree of robustness testing. Reporting precision, recall, and F-score for both primary and remote edges is commendable and offers a granular view of performance.
3. **Comprehensive Approach:** The paper explores two distinct parsing strategies—conversion-based and direct parsing—providing a more complete understanding of the challenges and potential solutions. The conversion experiments provide a useful lower bound and a comparison point for the direct parser.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the motivation for the work, the key concepts, and the experimental results. The diagrams illustrating the structural properties of BSS are particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Improvement over Conversion-Based Approaches:** While the direct parser (BSP) achieves respectable results, the performance gain over the conversion-based approaches, especially the LSTM parser applied after conversion to dependencies, is not substantial. This raises questions about the benefits of the added complexity of a dedicated BSS parser, particularly given the resource limitations inherent in UCCA data. The authors acknowledge that a similar LSTM approach could improve BSP, suggesting this is a key area for future work, but the current results don't convincingly demonstrate the superiority of direct parsing.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Errors:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by BSP – whether related to multiple parents, discontinuous units, or non-terminal nodes – would provide valuable insights for future improvements.  The paper only briefly notes the challenge of predicting remote edges.
3. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set, while drawing from related work, feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a clear rationale for the inclusion of specific features. The impact of individual features is only partially explored through the ablation study. A more systematic feature selection or learning approach could potentially improve performance. The use of the “ratio” feature, while novel, isn’t thoroughly justified beyond being a regularizer.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Semantic Parsers:** The paper primarily compares against dependency and constituency parsers *after conversion*. A more direct comparison to state-of-the-art semantic parsers, even if they don't directly handle UCCA, would help contextualize the performance of BSP. Consider comparing against AMR parsers, acknowledging the differences in representation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing errors based on the specific structural properties of BSS (multiple parents, discontinuous units, non-terminal nodes)?
2.  Given the limited performance gain over the conversion-based LSTM parser, what specific modifications to BSP (e.g., architecture changes, richer features) do you believe would be most effective in closing the gap?
3.  The paper mentions a dynamic oracle for training. Was any experimentation done with a global oracle, and if so, what were the results?
4.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the “ratio” feature and provide more evidence of its effectiveness?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material mentioned in the feature descriptions should be made available during the review process.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the size and characteristics of the UCCA corpus (number of sentences, average sentence length, number of nodes, number of edges, etc.).
*   The notation used throughout the paper (e.g., the use of subscripts and superscripts) could be made more consistent and readable.
