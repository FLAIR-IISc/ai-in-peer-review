This paper introduces a transition-based approach for parsing Broad-Coverage Semantic Structures (BSS), focusing on the UCCA annotation scheme. The core idea is to address the lack of existing parsers for UCCA by proposing two strategies: converting UCCA to simpler, parseable formats (dependency and constituency trees) and building a novel parser directly supporting the complexities of UCCA (multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities).

**Main Contributions:**

1. **First Transition-Based Parser for BSS:** The paper presents the first attempt at directly parsing BSS using a transition-based framework. This is the strongest contribution, establishing a baseline and opening avenues for future research in this challenging area.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Conversion-Based Parsing:** The systematic evaluation of converting UCCA to dependency and constituency trees provides valuable insights into the information loss associated with these conversions and the effectiveness of leveraging existing parsers. This highlights the trade-offs between simplifying the task and preserving semantic information.
3. **Novel UCCA-Specific Features and Transitions:** The introduction of new transitions (LEFTREMOTEX, RIGHTREMOTEX) specifically designed for handling remote edges and novel features related to remote edges, ratio of nodes/terminals demonstrate a thoughtful adaptation of transition-based parsing to the unique characteristics of UCCA.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Gap:** The lack of parsers for UCCA is a genuine bottleneck in the field, and this paper directly tackles that issue. The UCCA scheme itself is a valuable resource for representing rich semantic information.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a robust experimental setup, including in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation, and detailed ablation studies. The comparison with both conversion-based parsers and a direct BSS parser offers a clear understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
3. **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The design of the new transitions and features is well-justified by the specific challenges posed by UCCA’s representation, demonstrating a clear understanding of the problem domain. The addition of a ratio feature as a regularizer is a particularly interesting idea.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts, methodology, and results. Figures are helpful in illustrating the UCCA representation and the parsing process.
5. **Publicly Available Resources:** The promise to release the code and models is a significant contribution to reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Performance Gaps Compared to SOTA:** While the parser achieves respectable F-scores (around 62-69%), there's a noticeable gap compared to the LSTM parser after conversion (73.2% primary edge F-score). This suggests that the transition-based approach, as implemented, is not yet fully exploiting the potential of UCCA or benefiting sufficiently from recent advances in neural parsing.
2. **Limited Ablation Study Depth:** While the ablation study is helpful, it could be more granular. For example, investigating the individual contribution of each component of the feature set (unigrams, bigrams, disco features) would provide a more fine-grained understanding of their impact.
3. **Simplistic Inverse Conversion:** The inverse conversion from dependency trees to constituency trees relies on a heuristic (high-attaching) which may introduce errors. A more sophisticated conversion process could potentially improve the performance of the conversion-based methods.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The defined evaluation metric, while necessary in the absence of standard measures, feels somewhat limited. It focuses heavily on edge matching and doesn't fully capture the structural quality of the parsed graphs. Considering metrics that assess graph similarity or semantic coherence could be beneficial.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis would reveal specific areas where the parser struggles and guide future improvements. For example, understanding the types of remote edges that are most frequently mispredicted could inform the development of more effective features or transitions.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the performance gap with the LSTM parser after conversion, what specific architectural or training improvements are planned for BSP to close this gap? Would exploring a neural transition-based framework be a promising direction?
2.  Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind the high-attaching heuristic used in the inverse dependency-to-constituency conversion? Have you considered alternative heuristics or learning-based approaches for this step?
3.  What are the limitations of the defined evaluation metric, and are there any alternative metrics that you considered or plan to explore?
4.  Could you provide some examples of common error cases identified during development? Specifically, what types of UCCA structures (e.g., coordination, discontinuous units) does the parser struggle with most?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive and provides helpful context for the research.
*   The motivation for the ratio feature is interesting, and it would be beneficial to see a more detailed analysis of its impact on performance.
*   The reference to the UCCA annotation guidelines would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the scheme.
