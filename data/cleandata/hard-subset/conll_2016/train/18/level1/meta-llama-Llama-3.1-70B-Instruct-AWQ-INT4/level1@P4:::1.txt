**Summary**

This paper proposes a transition-based approach for parsing broad-coverage semantic structures (BSS), which are required for representing complex semantic phenomena in natural language. The authors identify three structural properties necessary for BSS: multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units. They introduce two parsing strategies: (1) a conversion-based approach that maps BSS to existing formalisms and uses state-of-the-art parsers, and (2) a novel transition-based parser, Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), that directly supports the full set of properties. The authors experiment with UCCA-annotated corpora and demonstrate the effectiveness of transition-based methods for BSS parsing.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Introduction of BSS parsing**: The authors propose a novel task of parsing broad-coverage semantic structures, which is essential for representing complex semantic phenomena in natural language.
2. **Transition-based approach**: The authors introduce a transition-based approach for BSS parsing, which is a natural starting point for UCCA parsing due to the similarities between UCCA and dependency schemes.
3. **BSP parser**: The authors propose a novel transition-based parser, BSP, that directly supports the full set of properties required for BSS parsing.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective parsing results**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of transition-based methods for BSS parsing, achieving a labeled F-score of 69.2% on primary edges.
2. **Robustness to domain variation**: The authors show that BSP is robust to domain variation, achieving comparable results on out-of-domain data.
3. **Novel features**: The authors introduce novel features, such as the ratio feature, which improves recall on primary edges.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation metrics**: The authors only evaluate their parser using labeled precision, recall, and F-score, which may not capture all aspects of BSS parsing.
2. **Simple conversion procedures**: The authors use simple conversion procedures to map BSS to existing formalisms, which may not be optimal.
3. **Limited comparison with existing parsers**: The authors only compare their parser with a few existing parsers, which may not provide a comprehensive understanding of the parser's performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their parser to handle implicit nodes, which are currently deferred to future work?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the dynamic oracle used for training the local classifier?
3. How do the authors plan to evaluate their parser using more comprehensive evaluation metrics, such as semantic role labeling or question answering?
