This paper proposes a transition-based approach to broad-coverage semantic parsing, focusing on the Universal Conceptual Cognitive Annotation (UCCA) scheme. The authors introduce two techniques for parsing such semantic structures: converting UCCA structures into related formalisms and using existing state-of-the-art parsers, and constructing a parser that directly supports the full set of properties required for broad-coverage semantic representation.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. The introduction of a novel transition-based parser, Broad-Coverage Semantic Parser (BSP), that supports multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuous units, which are essential for broad-coverage semantic representation.
2. The exploration of a conversion-based parsing approach, which assesses the ability of existing technology to address the task by converting UCCA structures into related formalisms and using existing parsers.
3. The first experimentation on UCCA parsing, which demonstrates the effectiveness of transition-based methods for this task.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The authors provide a clear and comprehensive introduction to the problem of broad-coverage semantic parsing and the UCCA scheme, making it easy to follow for readers unfamiliar with the topic.
2. The proposed BSP parser is well-motivated and built upon recent advances in discontinuous constituency and dependency DAG parsing techniques, making it a strong contribution to the field.
3. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, with BSP obtaining comparable F-scores to existing parsers and outperforming them in predicting remote edges.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. The conversion-based parsing approach is somewhat limited, as it relies on existing parsers that may not be optimized for UCCA structures. This approach may not be able to capture the full range of semantic phenomena required for broad-coverage semantic representation.
2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the errors made by the BSP parser, which would provide insights into the challenges of parsing UCCA structures and areas for future improvement.
3. The authors mention that the UCCA scheme has been applied to multiple languages, but the paper only presents results for English. It would be interesting to see how the proposed approach performs on other languages and whether it can be adapted to different linguistic structures.

Questions to authors:

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of implicit units in UCCA structures, which are not currently handled by the BSP parser?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the feature templates used in the BSP parser and how they were selected?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the BSP parser to handle more complex semantic phenomena, such as coreference and anaphora?

Additional comments:

* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and experimental results.
* The use of tables and figures is effective in presenting the results and making them easy to understand.
* The authors provide a comprehensive list of references, which is helpful for readers interested in exploring the topic further.
