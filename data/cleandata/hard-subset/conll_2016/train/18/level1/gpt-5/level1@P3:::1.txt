Summary and claimed contributions
The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing where structures must support (i) multiple parents (remote edges), (ii) non-terminal units, and (iii) discontinuities—properties simultaneously present in UCCA. The authors propose two transition-based approaches: (1) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA DAGs into (discontinuous) constituency or dependency trees and leverages existing parsers, and (2) a direct transition system (BSP) that natively constructs DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including explicit transitions for remote edges. They also define an evaluation protocol for BSS/UCCA via yield-equivalence of labeled edges and provide experiments on in-domain (UCCA-Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) English corpora.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A novel transition system (BSP) for directly parsing DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including explicit handling of remote edges via LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX, NODE transitions, and SWAP for gaps, trained with a dynamic oracle.

2) A careful conversion-based evaluation framework with explicit algorithms for UCCA-to-constituency and UCCA-to-dependency and their inverses, quantifying information loss via upper bounds and benchmarking multiple parsers (UPARSE, Malt, stack-LSTM).

3) First comprehensive experimental study on UCCA parsing, including an out-of-domain evaluation and a feature ablation, establishing competitive baselines and demonstrating the promise of transition-based methods.

Strengths
- Technical novelty and clear formulation of a direct DAG parser: BSP extends transition-based parsing with a minimal, principled set of transitions to support all three target properties. The separation between primary and remote edges is explicit; the use of SWAP for discontinuities aligns with prior work, and dynamic oracles are used during training. This is the first concrete, reproducible framework to parse UCCA-style BSS directly.

- Solid comparative framework via conversions and upper bounds: The paper does not only propose a new parser but situates it against conversion-based approaches with explicit forward/inverse algorithms and measurable upper bounds (e.g., dependency conversion upper bound 88.4 F1 on primary edges, vs. 100 for constituency where only remotes are removed). This calibrates expectations and reveals where information is irretrievably lost.

- Empirical breadth and robustness: Experiments include multiple parsers (UPARSE, Malt arc-eager/arc-standard, stack-LSTM) and two domains. Results are coherent: the stack-LSTM dependency baseline achieves 69.2 primary F1; BSP achieves 59.0 primary F1 and 13.3 remote F1; out-of-domain performance is close (primary F1 57.1; remote F1 13.6), suggesting stable behavior under domain shift.

- Thoughtful feature engineering and analysis: The feature ablation shows clear contributions from unigrams/bigrams and a novel ratio feature that regularizes node creation, plus discontinuity-oriented features. This indicates the model’s behavior is well-understood and provides actionable insights for future improvements.

- Clarity and reproducibility: The transition set, training protocol (perceptron with MINUPDATE, dynamic oracle), conversion algorithms, and evaluation metric are all specified. The commitment to release code/converters is valuable for the community.

Weaknesses
- Direct parser underperforms on primary edges vs. best conversion baseline: BSP’s primary F1 (59.0) lags substantially behind the stack-LSTM dependency baseline (69.2). While BSP uniquely predicts remote edges, their F1 is still low (13.3), and it is unclear whether the current transition/classifier choices are the limiting factor. The comparison highlights that modern neural classifiers (stack-LSTM) offer large gains, yet BSP relies on a linear perceptron and greedy inference.

- Limited comparison to alternative DAG parsers and modeling choices: There is no head-to-head comparison with other DAG-capable transition systems beyond Malt variants, nor with graph-based/global models. The lack of beam search or neural features for BSP, despite demonstrating their benefits in the dependency baseline, weakens the empirical case for the direct approach.

- Evaluation measure and reporting limitations: The yield-equivalence metric is reasonable but collapses structural nuances, and it is sensitive to non-terminal reconstruction. There is no reporting of unlabeled scores, per-phenomenon breakdown (coordination, phrasal verbs, MWE), or detailed error analysis for remote edges vs. discontinuities. Given that constituency conversion has a perfect primary upper bound by definition, more qualitative analysis would help interpret where BSP wins/loses vs. conversions.

- Data scope and exclusions: Experiments are limited to English and exclude linkage nodes and implicit units. While justified, this omission conceals a class of semantic relations central to UCCA’s inter-scene modeling. The paper would be stronger with at least an analysis of how often such edges occur and whether their exclusion biases results.

- Some conversion/reconstruction heuristics are under-specified: The priority order over UCCA labels for head selection and the rule-based Label(u) used in the inverse dependency-to-constituency conversion are not fully detailed. These choices can materially affect both upper bounds and parser performance.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide the exact priority order over UCCA edge labels used in head selection (h and h*) and the full specification of Label(u)? How sensitive are the dependency conversion upper bounds to these choices?

- What is the distribution and frequency of remote edges and discontinuities in the training/test sets? Could you add per-phenomenon performance (e.g., recall on coordination, phrasal verbs, remote participants) to contextualize where BSP helps beyond conversions?

- How does BSP perform with beam search and/or neural features (e.g., stack-LSTMs) replacing the linear perceptron? Given the 9.1 F1 gap between Malt and the LSTM baseline, do you expect similar gains for BSP?

- Could you report unlabeled primary/remote scores and per-length/span metrics? This might disentangle labeling from structural attachment errors.

- What constraints ensure graph well-formedness (e.g., acyclicity and single primary parent) during decoding? Are there failure modes where constraints are violated, and how are they handled?

Overall, this is a timely and meaningful contribution: introducing a direct DAG parser for UCCA/BSS, grounding it with careful conversions and upper bounds, and providing initial baselines. The main limitations are empirical (the direct model’s accuracy and modeling choices) and analytical (lack of detailed phenomenon-level evaluation). Addressing these in the rebuttal or a revised version—especially by upgrading BSP’s classifier and providing deeper analysis—would significantly strengthen the paper.
