- Summary — Paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing with multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, proposing conversion-to-tree strategies and a direct transition-based BSP; conversion + LSTM parser gets best primary-edge F1 while BSP uniquely predicts remote edges.

- Contribution 1 — A direct transition-based Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) that natively supports multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities, remote-edge transitions, and a dynamic oracle.

- Contribution 2 — Well-specified conversion procedures between UCCA DAGs and constituency/dependency trees, with measured upper bounds quantifying information loss.

- Contribution 3 — An empirical benchmark and evaluation protocol for broad-coverage semantic structures on UCCA, including ablations and in/out-of-domain experiments.

- Strength — First end-to-end parser handling all three structural properties in a grounded setting; BSP uniquely recovers remote edges (∼13 F1).

- Strength — Rigorous experiments that clarify representational trade-offs (perfect constituency conversion vs. lower dependency upper bound) and show value of transition-based neural classifiers (best observed primary F1 69.2).

- Strength — Thorough technical specification (transition system, dynamic oracle, feature set) and ablation analysis; the novel ratio feature improves primary recall.

- Strength — Robustness across domains: primary-edge F1 degrades modestly out-of-domain, indicating limited overfitting.

- Weakness — Absolute accuracy is modest: BSP primary F1 (~59) lags best conversion baseline (69.2) and remote-edge F1 remains low, limiting practical multi-parent semantics.

- Weakness — Evaluation is confined to UCCA English; claims of broad coverage lack multilingual or cross-formalism evidence.

- Weakness — Incomplete comparisons: no direct head-to-head with contemporary DAG parsers or joint syntactic-semantic models, and some baseline code is unavailable.

- Weakness — Conversion pipeline discards remote edges and uses a simple high-attachment heuristic on inverse mapping, risking information loss.

- Weakness — Missing deeper error analysis (e.g., failures on coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs) and no reporting on computational efficiency or beam-search effects.

- Question — How would BSP perform with stronger neural classifiers (stack-LSTM/transformer features) and beam search?

- Question — Which remote-edge types cause low recall, and can targeted transitions or features improve them?

- Question — Do results transfer to other languages with higher discontinuity rates (e.g., German)?

- Question — Can conversion back to DAGs preserve remote edges or be improved with a learned recovery step?
