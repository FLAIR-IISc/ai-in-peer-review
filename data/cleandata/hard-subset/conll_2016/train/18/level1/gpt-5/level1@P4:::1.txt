Summary and claimed contributions
The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing where structures require (a) multiple parents (DAGs), (b) non-terminal nodes, and (c) discontinuous units. It focuses on UCCA, the only grounded scheme claimed to support all three. Two transition-based strategies are proposed: (1) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA graphs to trees (discontinuous constituency or dependency), applies existing parsers, and converts back; and (2) a direct transition-based parser (BSP) that natively supports all three properties, including remote edges (multiple parents) and discontinuities via SWAP. An evaluation measure for BSS, based on labeled yield identity, is proposed. Experiments on UCCA Wikipedia (in-domain) and 20K Leagues (out-of-domain) report primary-edge F1 up to 69.2% via conversion using an LSTM dependency parser, and 59.0% (primary) and 13.3% (remote) for the direct BSP; BSP shows similar performance out-of-domain. Feature ablations indicate key contributions from standard and novel features (e.g., a node-to-terminal ratio).

Contributions (as assessed)
- A direct, transition-based parser for grounded semantic DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities (BSP), including a transition inventory and dynamic-oracle training adapted to UCCA. This is the primary technical contribution and appears to be the first parser directly supporting the full UCCA structural criteria.
- A systematic conversion-based framework bridging UCCA to discontinuous constituency and to dependency trees, with explicit forward and inverse algorithms and quantified upper bounds. This provides a strong baseline and a lens on information loss.
- An evaluation protocol for BSS based on labeled yield identity, enabling direct comparison across tree and DAG outputs in the grounded setting.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: The paper precisely characterizes three structural requirements for broad-coverage grounded semantics and motivates why bi-lexical dependencies are insufficient for phenomena like coordination and MWEs. The choice of UCCA is justified by coverage of all three properties.
- Solid experimental comparison of direct versus conversion-based approaches: The study provides both approaches with detailed conversion algorithms (Algorithms 1–2), reports upper bounds for conversion losses, and demonstrates that conversion to dependencies loses substantial non-terminal information (upper bound 88.4 F1) whereas conversion to discontinuous constituents preserves primary edges (upper bound 100 F1) but loses remote edges—anchoring the empirical results.
- Technically sound transition system for BSS: The BSP transition inventory (NODEX, LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE with primary/remote distinction, SWAP for discontinuity, NO auto-reduce to allow multiple parents) reflects known best practices in discontinuous and DAG parsing and extends them to UCCA-specific needs. Use of a dynamic oracle and perceptron training is appropriate and reproducible.
- Comprehensive feature engineering and ablation: The model integrates features from prior work plus UCCA-specific features (remote counts) and a novel global ratio feature acting as a regularizer for node creation. Ablations attribute gains to unigrams/bigrams/disco features and quantify the ratio’s impact on recall.
- Robustness across domains: BSP maintains comparable primary-edge performance on 20K Leagues without re-tuning, and even improves remote-edge precision, supporting claims of generality for grounded representations.

Weaknesses
- Limited performance on multiple-parent (remote) edges: Remote-edge F1 is low (13.3% in-domain; 13.6% out-of-domain), indicating that the core challenge of predicting shared arguments remains largely unresolved. The paper lacks fine-grained error analysis to identify whether difficulty stems from data sparsity, features, search, or transition ambiguity.
- Fairness and breadth of baselines: The strongest conversion result leverages a neural LSTM classifier, while BSP uses a linear perceptron with greedy inference and no beam. This undercuts direct comparisons of architectures. No comparison to existing DAG parsers is provided (code unavailability is noted), and no neural variant of BSP is attempted despite clear benefits shown by LSTMs in the conversion setting.
- Evaluation scope and measures: The evaluation removes linkage and implicit nodes and restricts to sentence-level structures, limiting “broad-coverage” claims in practice. The proposed yield-based measure, while reasonable, is not contrasted with alternatives (e.g., alignment-based measures or DAG-specific metrics), and no analysis by phenomenon (e.g., coordination vs. MWEs vs. phrasal verbs) is reported.
- Heuristic inverse conversions may bias results: The dependency-to-constituency inverse conversion relies on heuristics (Label(u) predominantly Center and always high attachment). This could either understate or overstate the potential of conversion-based parsing; a sensitivity analysis or stronger reconstruction (e.g., learned head rules) is missing.
- Missing complexity/runtime and decoding details: The paper does not report parsing speed, memory, or transition counts, nor does it discuss acyclicity enforcement or constraints for remote edges. Absence of beam search or global training for BSP may hinder performance, but this is not assessed.

Questions to authors
- Dynamic oracle completeness: How is the oracle defined to guarantee optimality and reachability in the presence of multiple parents and discontinuities? Are there conditions ensuring the oracle remains sound and complete for the full transition set including REMOTE edges?
- Acyclicity and well-formedness: What constraints are enforced during EDGE/REMOTE transitions to prevent cycles in the DAG? Is there an explicit cycle check?
- Remote-edge analysis: What is the distribution of remote edges in the training data, and which labels/phenomena dominate remote errors? Do particular constructions (e.g., control/raising, shared arguments) account for most misses?
- Impact of search and modeling: What are BSP results with beam search and/or neural scoring (e.g., BiLSTM features), given the large gains observed for the LSTM dependency baseline? Did you attempt pretrained embeddings?
- Conversion heuristics: Can you detail Label(u) and the high-attaching assumption? Have you evaluated alternative inverse-conversion strategies or learned reconstructions (e.g., via a small supervised model) to narrow the dependency-conversion upper bound gap?
- Metric sensitivity: How do results change if evaluation considers unlabeled yields, or alternative DAG metrics (e.g., edge F1 over terminal alignments without yields)?

Additional comments
- Please include per-phenomenon breakdowns (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, discontinuities) to better illuminate where BSP helps over conversions.
- Reporting inter-annotator agreement on the specific splits used, and the proportion of discontinuous and remote edges, would contextualize scores.
- Minor clarity: ensure consistent reference formatting and correct hyphenation (e.g., transition-based).
