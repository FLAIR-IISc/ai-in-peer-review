- Paper addresses broad-coverage semantic parsing requiring DAGs with multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, focusing on UCCA.
- Proposes two strategies: conversion-based parsing (UCCA ↔ trees) and a direct transition-based parser (BSP) supporting all UCCA properties.
- Introduces an evaluation measure for BSS/BSP based on labeled yield identity to compare tree and DAG outputs.
- Reports conversion-based primary-edge F1 up to 69.2% (LSTM dependency parser) and BSP 59.0% primary, 13.3% remote in-domain; BSP shows similar out-of-domain performance.
- Feature ablations show standard and novel features (e.g., node-to-terminal ratio) materially improve performance.
- Primary technical contribution: first direct transition-based parser (BSP) supporting full UCCA structural criteria, with transition inventory and dynamic-oracle training.
- Provides a systematic conversion framework between UCCA and discontinuous constituency/dependency with forward/inverse algorithms and quantified upper bounds.
- Offers an evaluation protocol based on labeled yield identity enabling cross-form comparison in grounded semantics.
- Clear problem formulation motivating why bi-lexical dependencies are insufficient and why UCCA is an appropriate target.
- Solid experimental comparison between conversion and direct approaches, reporting conversion upper bounds and demonstrating information loss patterns.
- BSP transition system is technically sound, incorporating NODEX, LEFT/RIGHT-EDGE (primary/remote), SWAP for discontinuity, and NO auto-reduce for multiple parents.
- Uses a dynamic oracle and perceptron training, following reproducible, established practices.
- Comprehensive feature engineering and ablation, including UCCA-specific features and a global node-to-terminal ratio regularizer.
- BSP shows domain robustness, maintaining primary-edge performance and improved remote precision on out-of-domain data.
- Major weakness: very low remote-edge F1 (13.3% in-domain), with no fine-grained error analysis on causes.
- Baseline fairness issue: strongest conversion uses neural LSTM while BSP uses linear perceptron and greedy decoding, undermining architecture comparisons.
- No comparisons to existing DAG parsers and no neural variant of BSP explored despite LSTM gains in baselines.
- Evaluation scope limited: linkage/implicit nodes removed and only sentence-level structures considered, constraining “broad-coverage” claims.
- Yield-based measure not contrasted with alternative metrics, and no analysis by linguistic phenomena (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs).
- Heuristic inverse conversions (dependency→constituency) rely on strong assumptions (Label(u) as Center/high attachment) that may bias results and lack sensitivity analysis.
- Missing complexity/runtime details: no parsing speed, memory, transition counts, or discussion of acyclicity enforcement and remote-edge constraints.
- Questions: how is the dynamic oracle proven sound/complete with multiple parents and discontinuities?
- Questions: what constraints/checks prevent cycles during EDGE/REMOTE transitions?
- Questions: distribution and error patterns of remote edges — which labels/constructions drive misses?
- Questions: what are BSP results with beam search, neural scoring (BiLSTM), or pretrained embeddings?
- Questions: details of conversion heuristics (Label(u), high-attachment) and whether learned inverse strategies were tried.
- Questions: sensitivity of results to alternative metrics (unlabeled yields, DAG-specific measures).
- Additional requests: provide per-phenomenon breakdowns (coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, discontinuities).
- Additional requests: report inter-annotator agreement and proportions of discontinuous and remote edges in splits used.
- Minor clarity: fix reference formatting and hyphenation consistency (e.g., "transition-based").
