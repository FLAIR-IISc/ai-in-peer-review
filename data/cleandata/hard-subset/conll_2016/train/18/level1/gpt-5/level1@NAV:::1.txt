Summary
The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing where structures require multiple parents, non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. It proposes two transition-based strategies: (i) conversion of UCCA DAGs into constituency/dependency trees to leverage existing parsers and (ii) a direct transition-based Broad-coverage Semantic Parser (BSP) that natively supports the three properties. Experiments on UCCA English (in-domain Wiki and out-of-domain 20K Leagues) show strongest primary-edge F1 via conversion plus an LSTM dependency parser (69.2), while BSP uniquely predicts remote edges.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A direct transition-based parser (BSP) supporting multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities, including remote-edge transitions and a dynamic oracle.
2) Well-specified conversion procedures between UCCA and constituency/dependency formalisms, with measured upper bounds quantifying information loss.
3) An empirical benchmark and evaluation protocol for BSS on UCCA, including ablations (e.g., the ratio feature) and in/out-of-domain results.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful problem formulation and first end-to-end parser handling all three structural properties in a grounded setting; BSP is the only model here predicting remote edges (13.3 F1 in-domain; 13.6 out-of-domain).
- Rigorous experimental design that clarifies representational trade-offs: perfect primary F1 upper bound for constituency conversion vs. 88.4 upper bound for dependency conversion; best observed primary F1 of 69.2 with LSTM arc-standard emphasizes the value of transition-based neural classifiers.
- Thorough technical specification (transition system, dynamic oracle, feature set) plus ablation analysis; the novel ratio feature notably improves primary recall and is of independent interest to constituency-style transition parsers.
- Robustness across domains: primary-edge F1 degrades modestly out-of-domain, suggesting the approach is not overfitted to Wiki.

Weaknesses
- Absolute accuracy on the core task remains modest: BSP primary F1 ~59 trails the best conversion baseline (69.2), and remote-edge F1 is low, limiting practical utility for multi-parent semantics.
- Evaluation is limited to UCCA English; claims of broad coverage would be stronger with multilingual or cross-scheme evidence (e.g., SDP-style dependencies).
- Incomplete comparisons: no head-to-head with contemporary DAG parsers or joint syntactic-semantic models; reliance on “code unavailable” leaves uncertainty about relative merit.
- Conversion pipeline discards remote edges and uses a simple high-attachment heuristic in the inverse mapping; deeper error analysis of where information is lost or where BSP fails (e.g., coordination vs. MWEs vs. phrasal verbs) is missing. Computational efficiency and beam-search effects are not reported.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- How does BSP perform with a neural classifier (e.g., stack-LSTM or transformer features) and beam search?
- Which remote-edge types drive the low recall, and can targeted transitions/features help?
- Do results transfer to other languages with higher discontinuity rates (e.g., German)?
- Can the conversion back to DAGs preserve some remote edges, or be augmented with a learned recovery step?
