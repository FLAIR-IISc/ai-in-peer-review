Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper argues that broad-coverage semantic parsing requires three structural properties: multiple parents (DAGs), non-terminal nodes, and discontinuities. UCCA is presented as the only available grounded scheme exhibiting all three. The authors propose two complementary approaches: (i) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA graphs to (discontinuous) constituency trees or dependency trees, applies existing transition-based parsers, and converts back; (ii) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that natively supports DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities via new transitions and features. Experiments on UCCA English Wikipedia (in-domain) and 20K Leagues (out-of-domain) show primary-edge F1 up to 69.2 via dependency conversion with an LSTM parser, and 59.0 for BSP with additional ability to predict remote edges (≈13 F1).
- Main contributions (1–3, strongest first):
  1) A transition system (BSP) that directly supports all three BSS properties (multiple parents, non-terminals, and discontinuities), with a dynamic oracle and tailored feature set including a novel “ratio” feature.
  2) Practical, explicit conversion protocols between UCCA-style graphs and both constituency and dependency formalisms, with empirical assessment and upper bounds that quantify information loss.
  3) A concrete evaluation setup for UCCA parsing, including a labeled yield-based metric for DAGs, in-domain/out-of-domain experiments, and feature ablation.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and significance: The paper crisply motivates why DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities are necessary for grounded broad-coverage semantics, and positions UCCA as a stress test for parsers. This clarifies a gap in existing semantic parsing work (e.g., SDP lacks non-terminals; AMR is ungrounded).
- Technical novelty and soundness of BSP: The transition set extends prior work to simultaneously support DAG edges (primary vs. remote), node creation for non-terminals, and SWAP for discontinuities. The constraints (e.g., single primary parent) and distinction between primary/remote edge transitions are well-justified. The use of a dynamic oracle and structured perceptron is appropriate for this era and task.
- Thorough empirical comparison: The conversion-based route is not a strawman: the authors use competitive baselines (Malt arc-standard/arc-eager; stack-LSTM) and a discontinuous constituency parser (UPARSE), with reasonable defaults. Reporting conversion upper bounds (e.g., 93.8/83.7/88.4 primary LP/LR/LF for dependencies) quantifies head/non-terminal loss and makes results interpretable. Out-of-domain evaluation demonstrates robustness of trends.
- Evaluation design and transparency: The labeled-yield metric appropriately generalizes PARSEVAL to DAGs and separates primary vs. remote edges. The paper provides the conversion algorithms and training details (dynamic oracle behavior, MINUPDATE, IMPORTANCE, SWAP weighting), aiding reproducibility. Feature ablation shows which templates matter (e.g., unigrams/bigrams, ratio feature for recall).
- Insightful empirical takeaway: The finding that a strong dependency parser with conversion outperforms the direct model on primary edges (69.2 vs. 59.0 F1) is valuable to the community, indicating that conversion can be competitive while direct modeling is needed for remote edges.

Weaknesses
- Performance on remote (multi-parent) edges is low: BSP’s remote-edge F1 is ≈13 (even lower out-of-domain). Given the paper’s core motivation (supporting multi-parent structures), this is a key limitation. The paper lacks deeper analysis of why remote edges fail (data sparsity, feature inadequacy, transition errors, or search/training issues) and which remote relations are hardest.
- Direct model underperforms conversion baseline on primary edges: The flagship BSP trails the LSTM-based dependency-conversion approach by ~10 F1 on primary edges. While the authors note that swapping in an LSTM classifier could help, the current gap weakens the case for the direct approach as the primary practical solution.
- Limited scope of evaluation and analysis: Only English UCCA is evaluated; no cross-lingual or larger-domain tests despite UCCA’s cross-linguistic claims. Error analysis is minimal (no breakdown by constructions: coordination, MWEs, phrasal verbs, discontinuities). There are no significance tests, no speed/complexity discussion, and no ablation of critical design choices (e.g., beam vs. greedy).
- Heuristic choices in conversions not probed: The dependency→constituency inverse conversion assumes high-attachments and uses a rule-based Label function (often Center). The impact of these heuristics on upper bounds and downstream parsing is not quantified by alternatives or sensitivity analyses, potentially biasing comparisons.
- Baselines coverage gaps: The paper could not include existing DAG dependency parsers due to code availability; nonetheless, this leaves a hole in comparison (e.g., to better assess remote-edge handling). Also, UPARSE uses beam search while BSP is greedy, confounding fairness when concluding about transition-based methods’ effectiveness.

Questions to authors
- Remote edges: What proportion of edges in the corpora are remote? Can you provide per-label precision/recall for remote edges and an error typology? Did class imbalance strategies (cost-sensitive training, sampling) help?
- Search and learning: Did you try beam search for BSP or global training (e.g., early update/LaSO) to reduce exposure bias, especially for remote-edge transitions? Any preliminary results?
- Conversions: How is the linear order over edge labels determined for h(u)? Is it tuned on dev or fixed by the scheme? How sensitive are results/upper bounds to this ordering?
- Inverse conversion heuristics: Did you test low- vs. high-attachment variants or alternative Label(u) functions? How much do upper bounds vary?
- Resources and preprocessing: Which POS tagger and tokenization are used? Are gold tokens/tags assumed? Are the converters and BSP code/data splits released to ensure reproducibility?
- Evaluation: Any significance tests across systems? Can you report per-phenomenon scores (coordination, MWEs, discontinuities) to connect to the motivation?
- Dataset choices: You removed linkage and implicit nodes; how frequent are they, and how might their exclusion affect representational completeness and comparability to full UCCA?

Overall assessment
This paper addresses an important gap with a well-motivated, technically solid transition system and a careful conversion-based study. While the direct model’s accuracy lags the best conversion baseline on primary edges and remote-edge performance is low, the contributions (parser design, conversions, evaluation setup) are substantive and likely to catalyze further work. With stronger analysis of remote-edge errors, modest search/learning upgrades, and clearer probing of conversion heuristics, this would be a strong accept; as is, it meets CoNLL standards for novelty and empirical rigor, with some weaknesses in analysis and performance on the central phenomenon (remote edges).
