- Paper claims broad-coverage semantic parsing needs three properties (DAGs/multiple parents, non-terminals, discontinuities) and presents UCCA as a grounded scheme exhibiting all three.  
- Proposes two approaches: conversion-based parsing (UCCA↔constituency/dependency + existing parsers) and a new transition-based parser (BSP) that natively supports all three properties.  
- Reports experiments on in-domain (Wiki) and out-of-domain (20K Leagues) UCCA with best primary-edge F1 69.2 (dependency conversion + LSTM) and BSP primary F1 59.0, BSP remote-edge F1 ≈13.  
- Main contribution 1: BSP transition system supporting DAGs, non-terminals, and discontinuities, with dynamic oracle and a novel “ratio” feature.  
- Main contribution 2: Practical conversion protocols between UCCA graphs and constituency/dependency formalisms, with empirical upper bounds quantifying information loss.  
- Main contribution 3: Evaluation setup for UCCA parsing including a labeled yield-based DAG metric, in-/out-of-domain tests, and feature ablation.  
- Strength: Clear problem framing and motivation showing gaps in prior semantic formalisms (SDP, AMR) and positioning UCCA as a stress test.  
- Strength: Technical novelty and soundness of BSP—transitions handle primary vs. remote edges, node creation, and SWAP for discontinuities; constraints justified.  
- Strength: Use of a dynamic oracle and structured perceptron is appropriate for the task and era.  
- Strength: Thorough empirical comparison using competitive baselines (Malt, stack-LSTM, UPARSE) and reporting of conversion upper bounds for interpretability.  
- Strength: Evaluation design and transparency—labeled-yield metric, conversion/training details, and feature ablation provided to support reproducibility.  
- Strength/insight: Conversion-based dependency parsing outperforms the direct BSP on primary edges, suggesting conversion is competitive while BSP enables remote-edge prediction.  
- Weakness: Very low remote-edge performance (BSP ≈13 F1), with inadequate analysis of causes (data sparsity, features, transitions, search/training).  
- Weakness: BSP underperforms the conversion baseline on primary edges by ~10 F1, weakening the practical case for the direct model in its current form.  
- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope—only English, no cross-lingual tests, no detailed error breakdown by constructions (coordination, MWEs, discontinuities).  
- Weakness: No significance testing, no speed/complexity comparison, and no ablation for beam vs. greedy decoding, confounding some claims.  
- Weakness: Heuristic choices in conversion (e.g., high-attachment, Label function) are not sensitivity-tested and may bias upper bounds and comparisons.  
- Weakness: Baseline coverage gaps—couldn’t include existing DAG dependency parsers, and comparisons may be unfair (UPARSE beam vs BSP greedy).  
- Question: What proportion of edges are remote, per-label remote precision/recall, and which remote relations are hardest?  
- Question: Were class-imbalance strategies (cost-sensitive training, sampling) tried for remotes, and did they help?  
- Question: Did you try beam search or global training (early update/LaSO) for BSP to reduce exposure bias, especially for remotes? Any results?  
- Question: How is the linear order h(u) over edge labels determined—dev-tuned or scheme-fixed—and how sensitive are results to this ordering?  
- Question: Were inverse conversion variants (low- vs. high-attachment, alternative Label functions) tested and how much do upper bounds vary?  
- Question: What preprocessing (POS tagger, tokenization) is used; are gold tokens/tags assumed; and will code/converters/data splits be released?  
- Question: Any significance tests and per-phenomenon scores (coordination, MWEs, discontinuities) to better connect evaluation to the paper’s motivation?  
- Question: How frequent are linkage and implicit nodes that were removed, and how does their exclusion affect representational completeness and comparability to full UCCA?  
- Overall: Solid, well-motivated contributions (parser design, conversions, evaluation), but direct model lags and remote-edge handling is weak; with deeper remote-edge analysis and modest search/training improvements this would be a strong accept.
