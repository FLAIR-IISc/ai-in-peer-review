Summary and main contributions
- The paper targets broad-coverage semantic parsing with three key structural properties: multiple parents (reentrancy), non-terminal units, and discontinuities. It focuses on UCCA, a grounded semantic scheme that natively exhibits these properties.
- Two approaches are explored: (a) conversion-based parsing that maps UCCA graphs to (discontinuous) constituency trees or dependency trees, applies off-the-shelf parsers, and converts back; (b) a new transition-based parser (BSP) that directly constructs UCCA-style DAGs with non-terminals and discontinuities, including remote edges.
- Evaluation on English UCCA corpora (Wikipedia, and 20K Leagues out-of-domain) uses a yield- and label-matching metric for primary and remote edges.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) An empirical framework and baselines for UCCA parsing via conversion that quantify recoverability and show a strong neural dependency-based baseline (stack-LSTM) achieving 69.2 primary F1, substantially outperforming traditional Malt variants. This provides a practical and competitive reference point for the community.
2) A direct transition-based parser (BSP) that supports the full set of structural properties (non-terminals, discontinuities via SWAP, and reentrancies via remote edges). While primary F1 trails the conversion baseline, BSP is the only method to predict remote edges, achieving 13.3 F1 in-domain.
3) Tooling and evaluation protocol for UCCA (lossy converters, dynamic-oracle training, feature templates, and a simple graph evaluation), enabling future work on grounded broad-coverage semantic parsing.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and scope: The paper crisply identifies three structural needs for broad-coverage semantics and motivates UCCA as a grounded testbed. This fills a gap between SDP (no non-terminals) and AMR (not grounded).
- Solid and informative empirical study: The side-by-side comparison of constituency conversion (UPARSE), dependency conversion (Malt and stack-LSTM), and direct BSP is valuable. The result that a strong neural dependency parser post-conversion yields 69.2 F1 (vs. Malt ~60) is an actionable insight and sets a community baseline. The provision of upper bounds (e.g., 88.4 F1 for dependency conversion) is especially informative about conversion loss ceilings.
- First direct parser supporting the full structural set for UCCA: BSP is a concrete, working approach that handles non-terminals, discontinuities, and multiple parents with carefully designed transitions (LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX, SWAP, NODEX) and a dynamic oracle. The reported remote-edge F1, albeit low, demonstrates feasibility where tree-only methods necessarily score 0.
- Thoughtful feature engineering and training details: The inclusion of UCCA-specific features (e.g., remote-edge counts), the ratio feature acting as a regularizer on node creation, dynamic-oracle training, and SWAP upweighting display careful attention to the task’s idiosyncrasies. The ablation indicates meaningful contributions from unigram/bigram and discontinuity features.
- Robustness across domains: Out-of-domain results on 20K Leagues are close to in-domain for primary edges (e.g., BSP 57.1 vs. 59.0 F1), suggesting reasonable generalization under the chosen evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Primary-edge accuracy of BSP trails the best conversion-based baseline by a substantial margin: 59.0 (BSP) vs. 69.2 (stack-LSTM on dependency conversion). As the purported key contribution is a direct broad-coverage semantic parser, the gap weakens the case for BSP’s practical advantage today (beyond remote edges).
- Remote-edge performance remains low (13.3 F1 in-domain, 13.6 out-of-domain), with no deep error analysis. Given that reentrancies are a centerpiece of the motivation, readers need a breakdown of where the model succeeds/fails (e.g., control/raising, shared arguments in linked scenes) and which features/transitions limit performance.
- Conversion pipelines are lossy and under-specified in parts: The dependency inverse conversion relies on a simple high-attach heuristic and a Label(u) rule that “mostly selects Center.” The priority order over labels (for h(u)) is central to head selection yet not fully specified, hindering reproducibility and interpretability of errors vis-à-vis the 88.4 upper bound.
- Evaluation metric may obscure structural differences: Matching edges by yield and label collapses to PARSEVAL for trees and does not directly assess reentrancy structure beyond counting remote edges. No analysis of span/label confusions, attachment height errors introduced by inverse conversion, or sensitivity to discontinuities is provided. This limits diagnostic insight.
- Modeling choices limit BSP performance relative to contemporary parsers: BSP uses a linear averaged perceptron with greedy decoding, whereas the best baseline uses a stack-LSTM. The paper acknowledges this, but without providing even a modest neural or beam-search variant, it is hard to separate search/model limitations from representational challenges.

Questions to authors
- Please specify the exact priority order over UCCA labels used in Algorithm 1 (h(u)) and provide an analysis of how sensitive the dependency conversion and upper bound are to this ordering.
- How do you guarantee acyclicity when creating multiple parents with LEFT/RIGHT-REMOTEX? Are there explicit cycle checks or structural constraints?
- What proportion and types of remote edges are present in the datasets, and what are the main error types for BSP’s remote predictions (e.g., missing vs. spurious, label confusions)? Any per-label F1 for remote edges?
- What is the frequency and average distance of discontinuities, and how often is SWAP used per sentence? Does SWAP upweighting materially change error profiles?
- Have you tried beam search or a small neural scoring model for BSP? Even a modest stack-LSTM scorer or biaffine edge scorer might narrow the large gap with the conversion baseline.
- Could you include runtime and memory statistics (tokens/sec) for BSP vs. conversion pipelines?

Additional comments
- A qualitative comparison with SDP (where non-terminals are absent) on shared phenomena like coordination would further clarify the benefits/costs of non-terminals.
- An error analysis contrasting UPARSE vs. dependency conversion would help explain why dependency conversion + stack-LSTM is stronger despite larger information loss than constituency conversion.
- Releasing the converters and code is important for community uptake; please ensure label-priority lists, Label(u), and all hyperparameters are documented.
