This paper investigates the use of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, to improve sentiment analysis performance. The central hypothesis is that readers’ eye-movement patterns correlate with the cognitive processes involved in understanding nuanced language, and incorporating these patterns can enhance sentiment detection, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The project claims to achieve performance improvements of up to 3.7% and 9.3% on two datasets by augmenting traditional features with these cognitive signals. A key contribution is the exploration of gaze-based features in the context of sentiment analysis, a relatively novel approach. Another contribution lies in the careful analysis of feature significance, demonstrating that cognitive features do indeed contribute to improved performance, especially in challenging cases. Finally, the work provides a detailed experimental setup and evaluation, including a held-out dataset for testing the generalizability of the approach.

Strengths:

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The integration of cognitive features, specifically eye-tracking data, into sentiment analysis represents a novel and potentially valuable direction for research. While eye-tracking has been applied to related NLP tasks, its application to sentiment analysis, particularly in augmenting traditional methods, is a significant contribution.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation:** The paper employs a robust experimental methodology, utilizing two datasets and multiple classifiers (NB, SVM, and a multi-layer neural network). The use of 10-fold cross-validation and a held-out dataset strengthens the validity of the results. The inclusion of a statistical significance test (McNemar test) is commendable.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate considerable effort in feature engineering, going beyond simple eye-tracking metrics to develop more complex gaze-saliency-based features. The categorization and explanation of features (Sentiment, Sarcasm/Thwarting, Cognitive, Reading Difficulty) are clear and well-organized.
4. **Feature Significance Analysis:** The feature significance analysis provides valuable insight into the contribution of cognitive features. The observation that gaze-based features are prominent in the top-ranked features lends support to the core hypothesis of the paper.
5. **Practical Considerations & Discussion:** The paper thoughtfully addresses the feasibility of the approach, discussing the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios. This demonstrates an awareness of real-world limitations and potential impact.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Dataset Diversity:** While two datasets are used, they are both based on relatively short text snippets (tweets and reviews). The generalizability of the findings to longer or more formal text types is uncertain. Expanding the evaluation to a wider range of text genres would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Eye-Tracker Variability:** The datasets utilize eye-trackers with differing quality (500Hz vs 120Hz). The potential impact of this difference on the derived cognitive features is not fully addressed. A sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of the results across different eye-tracking technologies would be beneficial.
3. **Averaging of Gaze Data:** The method of averaging gaze data across participants to create saliency graphs may obscure individual variations in cognitive processing. Investigating individual-level patterns or employing more sophisticated aggregation techniques could reveal further insights.
4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Models:** The paper explicitly excludes comparison to deep learning approaches, citing the feature-based nature of their method. However, given the current dominance of deep learning in sentiment analysis, a brief discussion of how their approach might complement or compare to these models would be valuable.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper mentions error sources, a more detailed error analysis, including specific examples of misclassified instances, would provide a deeper understanding of the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for improvement.

Questions to Authors:

1. How sensitive are the results to the specific parameters used in the construction of the gaze-saliency graphs?
2. Could you elaborate on the potential for combining the proposed cognitive features with deep learning models for sentiment analysis?
3. Were there any significant differences in the eye-tracking data collected from the two datasets, and if so, how were these differences accounted for in the analysis?
4. Can you provide examples of sentences where the addition of cognitive features significantly improved the sentiment classification accuracy, and explain the rationale behind these improvements?

Additional Comments:

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The inclusion of related work is comprehensive, and the presentation of the experimental results is thorough. The discussion of feasibility and potential applications is a strong point of the paper. Overall, this is a promising contribution to the field of sentiment analysis, with the potential to inspire further research in the integration of cognitive and linguistic approaches.
