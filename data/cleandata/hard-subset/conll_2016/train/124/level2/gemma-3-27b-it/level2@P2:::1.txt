This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data, demonstrating improvements in F-score (up to 9.3%) on two datasets. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reveal cognitive processes that can aid in understanding nuanced sentiment, particularly sarcasm and irony.

The primary contribution is the demonstration of the utility of incorporating eye-tracking data as features for sentiment analysis, specifically showing performance gains over traditional methods. A secondary contribution is the curated datasets used for evaluation, which contain instances with complex linguistic constructs (sarcasm, irony, thwarted expectations). Finally, a contribution is the extensive feature engineering, combining traditional sentiment features with both basic and complex gaze-derived features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The exploration of cognitive features from eye-tracking for sentiment analysis is a relatively novel direction. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies of sentiment, its application *as features* in an automated sentiment analysis pipeline is a valuable contribution.
2. **Empirical Results:** The reported improvements in F-score, particularly the 9.3% gain on Dataset 2, are significant and provide strong evidence supporting the hypothesis that cognitive features are beneficial. The statistical significance testing further strengthens this claim.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of existing sentiment analysis features and expand upon them with a thoughtful selection of gaze-based features. The inclusion of both basic and complex (graph-based) features adds depth to the investigation.
4. **Detailed Analysis & Justification:** The paper provides a thorough background on the challenges in sentiment analysis, justifying the need for a more nuanced approach. The explanation of how the cognitive features relate to underlying cognitive processes (e.g., regressions indicating reanalysis) is helpful.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors proactively address the practical limitations of needing eye-tracking data and discuss the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers, strengthening the potential real-world applicability of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Specificity:** While the datasets are a strength in terms of containing complex sentiment expressions, their limited size (994 and 1059 samples) raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. Larger, more diverse datasets are needed to confirm the robustness of the approach.
2. **Over-Engineering of Features:** The sheer number of features (many gaze features, several combinations) introduces a risk of overfitting, even with the use of cross-validation. A more rigorous feature selection process or dimensionality reduction technique could have been employed to identify the most impactful features. The paper mentions PCA but doesn’t detail how feature selection was performed *across* all feature categories.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper mentions deep learning approaches for sentiment analysis but doesn't include a comparison against them. While the authors state their approach is feature-based and thus different, comparing against a strong baseline would better contextualize the performance gains. It's crucial to know how much performance is *lost* by avoiding end-to-end learning.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The error analysis section is brief and doesn’t provide specific examples of the types of errors the system makes. A more in-depth analysis of failure cases would help identify areas for improvement. What types of sarcastic or ironic sentences still confuse the system?
5. **Eye-Tracking Methodology Details:** The paper briefly mentions different eye-trackers used for the two datasets (high quality vs. low quality). However, it does not discuss the potential impact of this difference on the cognitive features extracted or the overall results. More detail on the data acquisition process is needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How was the optimal combination of features determined? Was a systematic feature selection method used (e.g., forward/backward selection, information gain)?
2. Could you provide more details on the impact of the different eye-tracker qualities (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) on the derived features? Did you attempt to normalize or account for differences in sampling rates and accuracy?
3. Can you provide some specific examples of sentences where the addition of cognitive features significantly improved sentiment classification accuracy? This would help illustrate the benefits of the approach.
4. Have you explored using more advanced machine learning models (e.g., deep learning) to learn feature representations directly from the combined traditional and cognitive feature set?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation in Table 1 (P, R, F) could be defined in the main text rather than only in the caption.
* Consider adding a discussion of the ethical implications of using eye-tracking data, especially regarding privacy and informed consent.
* The references are formatted inconsistently. Please ensure a uniform style.
