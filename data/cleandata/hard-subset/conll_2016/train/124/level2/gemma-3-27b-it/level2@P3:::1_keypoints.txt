- Central claim: incorporating eye-tracking–derived cognitive features can improve sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm/irony, with reported gains up to 3.7% and 9.3% on two datasets.  
- Novelty: using gaze-based cognitive features for sentiment analysis is a relatively new and valuable direction.  
- Experimental rigor: evaluation uses two datasets, NB/SVM/MLP classifiers, 10-fold CV, a held-out test set, and McNemar significance testing.  
- Feature engineering: detailed, well-categorized features including complex gaze-saliency metrics beyond basic eye-tracking measures.  
- Feature significance: analysis shows cognitive/gaze features rank highly and contribute to performance, particularly on challenging cases.  
- Practical discussion: authors address feasibility and real-world applicability, noting increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers.  
- Limited dataset diversity: both datasets are short texts (tweets/reviews), so generalizability to longer/formal text is unclear.  
- Eye-tracker variability: differing hardware (500Hz vs 120Hz) may affect features; sensitivity to tracker quality is not fully analyzed.  
- Gaze averaging concern: averaging across participants to build saliency graphs may mask individual cognitive differences.  
- No DL comparison: paper excludes comparisons to state-of-the-art deep learning models and lacks discussion of integration/compatibility with DL approaches.  
- Limited error analysis: more detailed error examples and analysis of failure cases are missing.  
- Question on parameters: how sensitive are results to choices in building gaze-saliency graphs?  
- Question on DL integration: can cognitive features be combined with deep learning models, and how?  
- Question on dataset differences: were there significant differences in eye-tracking signals between datasets and how were they handled?  
- Request for examples: provide sentences where cognitive features substantially improved classification and explain why.  
- Overall: paper is well-written, comprehensive in related work and experiments, and a promising contribution likely to inspire further research.
