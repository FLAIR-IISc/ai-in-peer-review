This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea is that readers' eye movements reveal information about the cognitive processes involved in understanding sentiment, particularly in nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate performance improvements on two datasets using this combined feature set, and provide feature significance analysis supporting the contribution of the cognitive features.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Feature Integration:** The primary contribution is the exploration and demonstration of the value of integrating cognitive features (derived from eye-tracking) with traditional sentiment analysis features. While eye-tracking has been used in related psycholinguistic tasks, applying it directly to enhance sentiment analysis is a novel approach.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides empirical evidence of the effectiveness of this integration, showing statistically significant improvements in F-score on two datasets. This is crucial, as the idea is not simply theoretical.
3. **Feature Significance Analysis:** The feature significance analysis lends support to the hypothesis that cognitive features are indeed useful for handling complex linguistic constructs like sarcasm and irony, providing insights into *why* the method works.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of sentiment analysis, particularly in dealing with subtleties in language. The introduction effectively motivates the use of cognitive features to address these challenges.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors consider a wide range of both traditional and cognitive features, demonstrating a thorough understanding of the existing literature. The detailed description of the cognitive features, including the gaze-saliency graphs, is commendable.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of two datasets, the implementation of multiple classifiers, and the 10-fold cross-validation provide a solid experimental foundation. The inclusion of a held-out dataset to specifically test performance on complex constructs is a valuable addition.
4. **Statistical Significance:** The paper doesn’t just report performance improvements, it also provides statistical significance testing (McNemar test) to demonstrate that the improvements are not due to chance.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors thoughtfully address the practical limitations of requiring eye-tracking data and discuss potential avenues for making the approach more feasible with the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge the relatively small size of the datasets, this remains a concern. The Fleiss kappa values, while reasonable, indicate moderate inter-annotator agreement, which could introduce noise into the results. A larger and more consistently annotated dataset would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Eye-tracker Quality Disparity:** Using two datasets with different eye-tracker qualities (high quality Eyelink vs. lower quality Tobii) introduces a potential confounding factor. It's unclear how much the difference in eye-tracker precision influences the cognitive feature extraction and subsequent model performance.
3. **Averaging of Gaze Data:** The authors acknowledge that averaging gaze data across participants might lead to a loss of information. Exploring alternative methods for incorporating individual eye-movement patterns (e.g., using participant-specific models) could potentially improve performance.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper focuses on comparing against simpler baselines (NB, SVM, Rule-based). While this is a reasonable starting point, a more comprehensive comparison to recent deep learning-based sentiment analysis models (mentioned briefly) would provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the proposed approach.
5. **Feature Redundancy:** The extensive feature set raises the possibility of redundancy between features. While feature significance analysis is performed, a more formal feature selection or dimensionality reduction technique could be employed to identify the most important features and simplify the model.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of using averaged gaze data and discuss potential alternative approaches for incorporating individual eye-movement patterns?
2.  How do you believe the difference in eye-tracker quality between Dataset 1 and Dataset 2 might have impacted the results? Did you perform any analysis to assess the influence of this factor?
3.  Given the increasing prevalence of deep learning models in sentiment analysis, do you have plans to explore integrating cognitive features into such models? If so, what challenges do you anticipate?
4.  Were any specific preprocessing or filtering steps applied to the eye-tracking data to remove artifacts or noise? If so, could you please describe these steps in more detail?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The inclusion of related work is comprehensive.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key statistics of the two datasets (e.g., number of instances, average sentence length, inter-annotator agreement).
*   A visual illustration of the gaze-saliency graph would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with this concept.
