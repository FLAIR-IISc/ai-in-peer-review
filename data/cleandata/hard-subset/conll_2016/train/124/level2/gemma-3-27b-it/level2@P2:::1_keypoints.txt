- Core idea: Augment traditional sentiment features with eye-tracking–derived cognitive features to capture readers’ processing patterns for improved sentiment analysis, especially on sarcasm/irony.  
- Primary contribution: Demonstrates that eye-tracking features improve F-score (up to 9.3%) on two curated datasets.  
- Secondary contribution: Provides curated datasets containing complex linguistic constructs (sarcasm, irony, thwarted expectations).  
- Additional contribution: Extensive feature engineering combining standard sentiment features with basic and graph-based gaze features.  

- Strength: Novelty — using eye-tracking as features in an automated sentiment pipeline is a fresh, valuable direction.  
- Strength: Empirical results — significant F-score gains (notably 9.3%) with statistical significance testing.  
- Strength: Comprehensive feature set — thoughtful inclusion of both basic and complex gaze-derived features.  
- Strength: Detailed analysis — good background and plausible cognitive justifications linking gaze patterns to reanalysis.  
- Strength: Feasibility discussion — authors acknowledge practical limits and discuss growing availability of mobile eye-trackers.  

- Weakness: Dataset size/specificity — small datasets (994 and 1059 samples) limit generalizability and robustness claims.  
- Weakness: Risk of overfitting — large number of features and combinations without rigorous cross-category feature selection; PCA mentioned but selection unclear.  
- Weakness: Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art — no evaluation against modern deep learning baselines to contextualize gains.  
- Weakness: Limited error analysis — lacks detailed failure examples showing what sarcastic/ironic cases still cause errors.  
- Weakness: Insufficient eye-tracking methodology detail — differences in tracker quality (Eyelink-1000 vs Tobii T120) and their impact on features aren’t analyzed.  

- Question: How was the optimal feature combination determined; was systematic feature selection (e.g., forward/backward, information gain) used?  
- Question: How did differences in eye-tracker quality/sampling rate affect derived features; were they normalized or accounted for?  
- Question: Can you provide specific sentence examples where cognitive features meaningfully improved classification?  
- Question: Have you tried advanced models (e.g., deep learning) to learn representations from combined traditional and cognitive features?  

- Additional comment: Define notation (P, R, F) in main text rather than only in table captions.  
- Additional comment: Discuss ethical implications of eye-tracking data use (privacy, informed consent).  
- Additional comment: Ensure references are formatted consistently.
