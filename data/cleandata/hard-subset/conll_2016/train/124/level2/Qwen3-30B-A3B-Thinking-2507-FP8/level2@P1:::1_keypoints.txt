- Introduces cognitive features from eye-tracking for sentiment analysis.
- Demonstrates significant classification gains, especially for sarcasm/irony (9.3% on one dataset).
- Shows cognitive features are most useful for linguistically nuanced texts via feature-significance analysis.

- Well-grounded psycholinguistic rationale linking eye movements to sentiment processing.
- Robust experimental design using two complex-sentiment datasets and a held-out irony test (6.1% vs 2.1% improvement).
- Thorough statistical testing (McNemar p=0.0001) and gaze features rank highly among top features (10/20, 7/20).

- Lacks detailed description of preprocessing and algorithms for extracting cognitive features, harming reproducibility.
- Superficial discussion of practical feasibility and user consent for eye-tracking; ignores privacy and device-availability evidence.
- Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning sentiment methods, weakening claims of superiority.
- Does not address potential biases from annotator demographics affecting eye-tracking signals.
- Scalability concerns due to reliance on specialized equipment and human annotators with no clear deployment path.

- Please provide preprocessing details, including handling of different sampling rates (500Hz vs 120Hz).
- What evidence supports user consent and current availability/accuracy of embedded eye-trackers for this use?
- How would cognitive features be integrated into deep learning models and what performance gains are expected?

- Promising, theoretically sound approach that needs more technical detail, practical discussion, and stronger comparisons to be fully convincing.
