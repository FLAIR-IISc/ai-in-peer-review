

Summary: The paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human readers. The main contributions are: (1) introducing cognitive features from eye-tracking data for sentiment analysis, (2) demonstrating their effectiveness on two datasets with complex sentiment constructs, and (3) showing their particular value for sarcasm/irony detection.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration of cognitive features' effectiveness, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony (6.1% improvement on held-out dataset with complex constructs). The feature significance analysis showing gaze-based features as top-ranked for both datasets (10/20 for Dataset 1, 7/20 for Dataset 2) is compelling evidence for their value.

2. The feature design is well-motivated by psycholinguistic research, with detailed explanations of how eye-movement patterns relate to cognitive processing of sentiment. The distinction between basic and complex gaze features is particularly insightful and well-justified.

3. The experimental setup is comprehensive, testing multiple classifiers and feature combinations with statistical significance testing (McNemar test). The held-out dataset experiment specifically targeting complex constructs is a strong point that directly supports the main claim about cognitive features' value for nuanced sentiment.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim is overstated. The paper states "our approach is the first of its kind to the best of our knowledge" but fails to properly contextualize with prior work using eye-tracking for related NLP tasks. Joshi et al. (2014) and Mishra et al. (2016) explicitly use eye-tracking data for sentiment annotation complexity and sarcasm understandability prediction, respectively. The paper should acknowledge and differentiate from these prior works to properly establish novelty.

2. The paper doesn't adequately contextualize its reported improvements (3.7% and 9.3%) against state-of-the-art methods. The authors dismiss deep learning approaches as "beyond the scope" but fail to mention that these are now the standard for sentiment analysis. Without comparison to current SOTA (e.g., Maas et al. 2011, dos Santos and Gatti 2014), the claimed improvements are difficult to interpret in the current landscape of sentiment analysis research.

3. The feasibility discussion (Section 7) is superficial and doesn't address major practical barriers. The paper mentions mobile eye-trackers but doesn't discuss the significant privacy concerns, user consent challenges, and cost barriers to widespread adoption. The examples (Samsung's Smart-scroll) are tangential to the sentiment analysis application and don't address the core feasibility issues for sentiment analysis systems.

4. The paper doesn't establish a causal relationship between cognitive features and improved performance. While the correlation is shown (cognitive features improve performance), it doesn't provide evidence that the cognitive features themselves are the reason for improvement rather than some confounding factor. The paper should include an ablation study showing the contribution of specific cognitive features.

5. The datasets' suitability for testing cognitive features isn't adequately justified. The paper claims the datasets are "ideal" because existing SA systems perform poorly on them (Table 1), but doesn't explain why these datasets specifically contain the linguistic nuances that cognitive features can address, rather than being difficult for general reasons.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do the cognitive features specifically address the lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic challenges mentioned in Section 1, rather than simply being correlated with difficulty?

2. Given that deep learning approaches have become dominant in sentiment analysis, how would the authors expect their cognitive feature approach to integrate with or complement these methods?

3. Can the authors provide more details about the ethical considerations and practical barriers to collecting eye-tracking data for sentiment analysis at scale, beyond what's mentioned in Section 7?
