- Proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking, showing effectiveness on two datasets and particular value for sarcasm/irony detection.

- Strong empirical evidence: cognitive (gaze-based) features improve performance (6.1% on held-out complex dataset) and rank highly in feature significance.

- Feature design is well-motivated by psycholinguistic theory, with a clear distinction between basic and complex gaze features.

- Comprehensive experiments: multiple classifiers, feature combinations, and statistical significance testing; held-out test focused on complex constructs strengthens the claim.

- Novelty claim is overstated; prior eye-tracking work (e.g., Joshi et al. 2014; Mishra et al. 2016) is not adequately acknowledged or differentiated.

- Reported improvements (3.7% and 9.3%) are not contextualized against current state-of-the-art/deep learning approaches, making their significance unclear.

- Feasibility discussion is superficial and omits major practical barriers like privacy, consent, and cost; cited mobile examples are tangential.

- No causal link established between cognitive features and performance gains; lacks ablation analyses to isolate contributions of specific cognitive features.

- Datasets’ suitability for evaluating cognitive features is not justified; unclear whether they capture the linguistic nuances cognitive features target or are just generally difficult.

- How do cognitive features specifically address lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic challenges rather than merely correlating with difficulty?

- How would cognitive features integrate with or complement dominant deep learning approaches in sentiment analysis?

- Can the authors provide more details on ethical considerations and practical barriers to large-scale eye-tracking data collection beyond Section 7?
