- Proposes augmenting traditional sentiment features with cognitive features from eye-tracking to improve detection of sarcasm and irony.  
- Claims up to 9.3% improvement over traditional feature sets on two datasets, with cognitive features especially effective for complex constructs.  
- Contribution 1: Introduces cognitive features from eye-tracking as a novel approach for sentiment analysis.  
- Contribution 2: Empirically demonstrates statistically significant improvements when combined with traditional sentiment and sarcasm features.  
- Contribution 3: Feature significance analysis shows cognitive features rank among the top predictors for sentiment classification.  
- Strength: Well-designed experimental setup with appropriate statistical validation (McNemar test).  
- Strength: Detailed feature significance analysis and a held-out experiment showing a 6.1% improvement for complex constructs like irony.  
- Strength: Thoughtful discussion of feasibility scenarios for mobile eye-tracking applications.  
- Weakness: Insufficient theoretical justification linking eye-movement patterns to sentiment processing.  
- Weakness: Practical feasibility concerns due to reliance on specialized eye-tracking hardware not widely available.  
- Weakness: Insufficient detail on the methodology for deriving cognitive features from raw eye-tracking data, hindering reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Lack of discussion on generalizability across languages, cultures, and domains beyond the used datasets.  
- Suggestion: Strengthen theoretical grounding for cognitive features and clarify the feature extraction pipeline.  
- Suggestion: Provide more thorough discussion of practical implementation, scalability, and alternatives to hardware-dependent solutions.  
- Questions for authors: How do you justify the cognitive link between eye movements and sentiment processing, detail the feature extraction pipeline, and address scalability without widespread eye-tracking hardware?
