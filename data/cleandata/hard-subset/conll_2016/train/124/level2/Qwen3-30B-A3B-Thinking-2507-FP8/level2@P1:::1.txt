

Summary and Contributions:
This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human readers. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) the development of a comprehensive set of cognitive features from eye-tracking data that capture readers' processing of sentiment-bearing text, (2) empirical demonstration that these features significantly improve sentiment classification performance, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony (with 9.3% improvement on one dataset), and (3) feature significance analysis showing cognitive features are most valuable for handling linguistically nuanced texts.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical foundation connecting eye-movement patterns to cognitive processing for sentiment analysis is well-supported by psycholinguistic literature. The authors effectively argue why eye-tracking data might capture linguistic nuances that traditional text-based features miss, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm where sentiment is not explicitly expressed.

2. The experimental design is robust, with careful selection of two datasets containing significant amounts of text with complex sentiment (including sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations). The held-out dataset experiment specifically targeting irony vs. non-irony texts provides compelling evidence that cognitive features are particularly valuable for the most challenging sentiment cases (6.1% vs. 2.1% improvement).

3. The statistical analysis is thorough, with proper significance testing (McNemar test with p=0.0001) and feature significance analysis showing that gaze-based features constitute a significant portion of the top-ranked features for both datasets (10/20 for Dataset 1, 7/20 for Dataset 2).

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient technical detail on how the cognitive features were extracted from the raw eye-tracking data. While the authors describe various gaze features (fixation duration, saccade length, etc.), they don't explain the preprocessing steps, the algorithms used to derive features from the raw gaze data, or how they handled variations in eye-tracking equipment (SR-Research vs. Tobii trackers). This makes it difficult to assess the reproducibility of their approach.

2. The practical feasibility discussion in Section 7 is superficial. The authors mention "inexpensive embedded eye-trackers" but provide no evidence of their current availability, accuracy, or adoption rates. The claim that "getting users' consent to record eye-movement patterns will not be difficult" lacks supporting evidence and ignores significant privacy concerns that would likely deter widespread adoption.

3. The comparison with deep learning approaches is inadequate. The authors dismiss deep learning methods as "beyond the scope" but don't provide a meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning sentiment analysis systems. Given that deep learning approaches have become dominant in sentiment analysis, this omission weakens the paper's claim of "significant improvement" by not showing how their feature-based approach compares to current best practices.

4. The paper doesn't address potential biases in the eye-tracking data. The authors mention the datasets were annotated by multiple participants but don't discuss how differences in annotator backgrounds (age, cultural background, expertise) might affect the cognitive features extracted. This could introduce systematic biases into their feature set.

5. The scalability of the approach is not adequately addressed. With the current requirement for specialized eye-tracking equipment and human annotators, the approach would be prohibitively expensive for large-scale sentiment analysis applications. The paper lacks any discussion of how this limitation might be overcome for practical deployment.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more details about the preprocessing pipeline used to derive cognitive features from raw eye-tracking data, including how you handled variations in sampling rates between the two eye-trackers (500Hz vs. 120Hz)?

2. What evidence do you have that users would consent to eye-tracking for sentiment analysis purposes, particularly considering privacy concerns and the current state of eye-tracking adoption in consumer devices?

3. How would you integrate your cognitive features into a deep learning framework, and what would be the expected performance gain compared to your current feature-based approach?

The paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound approach to sentiment analysis that addresses an important gap in handling complex sentiment constructs. With more technical detail on feature extraction, better discussion of practical limitations, and a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, this work could make a significant contribution to the field. The core idea is promising, but the current presentation doesn't fully convince that this approach is both novel and practically viable.
