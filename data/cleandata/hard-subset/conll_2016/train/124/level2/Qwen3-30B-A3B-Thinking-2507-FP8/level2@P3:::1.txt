

Summary: The paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns recorded during sentiment annotation. The authors claim that these cognitive features improve sentiment analysis performance, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony.

Main contributions:
1. Introduction of cognitive features from eye-movement patterns as a novel feature set for sentiment analysis, which the authors claim is the first of its kind.
2. Demonstration of improved performance (3.7% and 9.3% F-score improvements) on two datasets compared to traditional feature sets.
3. Feature significance analysis showing cognitive features are particularly effective for complex constructs like irony.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating cognitive features from eye-tracking data, which has not been previously explored in the sentiment analysis literature. This represents a significant conceptual contribution to the field.
2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with results on two different datasets showing consistent improvements across multiple classifiers. The statistical significance testing (McNemar test) adds credibility to the results.
3. The feature significance analysis (Table 3) provides strong evidence that cognitive features are indeed contributing to the improved performance, with gaze-based features ranking highly in the top 20 features for both datasets.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient theoretical grounding for why eye-movement patterns would correlate with sentiment processing. While the authors cite some psychological literature, they fail to establish a clear causal link between specific eye-movement patterns and cognitive processes relevant to sentiment analysis, particularly for complex constructs. This weakens the core hypothesis of the work.
2. The held-out dataset experiment (Section 5.1) demonstrating cognitive features' effectiveness for complex constructs is limited to 294 examples (131 with complex constructs), which is insufficient to draw robust conclusions. The small sample size for the complex construct category makes the claimed 6.1% improvement questionable without proper statistical validation.
3. The paper fails to address a critical practical limitation: the feasibility of collecting eye-tracking data for real-world sentiment analysis applications. While the authors mention mobile eye-trackers, they do not adequately address privacy concerns, user consent challenges, or the scalability of collecting such data across large populations, which are significant barriers to practical implementation.
4. The paper does not compare with state-of-the-art deep learning approaches (e.g., CNNs, RNNs) that have become dominant in sentiment analysis, despite acknowledging their existence in the related work. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed feature augmentation would still provide benefits when used with modern deep learning architectures.

Questions to authors:
1. How do the specific cognitive features (e.g., regression count, largest regression position) relate to the cognitive processes involved in sentiment analysis, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm? The paper cites psychological literature but does not establish a clear theoretical connection.
2. The held-out dataset for complex constructs contains only 131 examples. Given this small sample size, how can the claimed 6.1% improvement for complex texts be considered statistically significant? What additional experiments would be needed to validate this claim?
3. The paper acknowledges mobile eye-trackers as a potential solution but does not address the practical challenges of obtaining user consent for eye-tracking data collection in real-world applications. How would the approach handle privacy concerns and user consent requirements in commercial settings?

Additional comments:
The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the theoretical underpinnings connecting eye-movement patterns to sentiment processing. The authors should clarify why specific gaze features (e.g., regression count, largest regression position) are expected to correlate with sentiment complexity. A brief exploration of alternative explanations for the observed correlations would strengthen the paper.

The paper should also address the scalability issue more thoroughly. While the authors mention mobile eye-trackers, they should provide concrete evidence or case studies demonstrating that such data collection is feasible at scale in real-world sentiment analysis applications, including discussions of privacy, consent, and data collection costs.

The paper's claim that cognitive features are particularly effective for complex constructs is intriguing but currently under-supported. The authors should either conduct additional experiments with a larger held-out dataset containing complex constructs or temper their claims to reflect the limited evidence currently presented.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a comparison with modern deep learning approaches, even if only as a brief discussion of how the proposed cognitive features might complement such methods. This would help position the work within the current sentiment analysis landscape.
