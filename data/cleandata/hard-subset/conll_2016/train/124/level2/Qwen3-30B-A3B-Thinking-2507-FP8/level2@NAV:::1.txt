

This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns to improve sentiment detection, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The authors claim their approach improves polarity detection by up to 9.3% over traditional feature sets on two datasets, with cognitive features showing particular effectiveness for complex linguistic constructs.

The main contribution is the introduction of cognitive features from eye-tracking data as a novel approach for sentiment analysis, particularly for handling complex sentiment expressions. The second contribution is the empirical demonstration of statistically significant improvements when these features are combined with traditional sentiment and sarcasm features. The third contribution is the feature significance analysis showing cognitive features as top-ranked features for sentiment classification.

Strengths include a well-designed experimental setup with appropriate statistical validation (McNemar test), detailed feature significance analysis demonstrating cognitive features as top-ranked features, and a targeted held-out dataset experiment showing 6.1% improvement specifically for complex constructs like irony. The paper also provides a thoughtful discussion of feasibility scenarios for mobile eye-tracking applications.

Weaknesses include insufficient justification for why eye-movement patterns would correlate with sentiment processing. The paper fails to establish a strong theoretical link between cognitive processes and sentiment expression, making the core premise questionable. The practical feasibility is problematic as the approach requires specialized eye-tracking hardware that is not widely available in real-world applications. The paper also lacks sufficient detail on the exact methodology for deriving cognitive features from raw eye-tracking data, hindering reproducibility. Additionally, the paper does not adequately address how these features would generalize across different languages, cultures, or domains beyond the specific datasets used.

The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical grounding for the cognitive features, clearer explanation of the feature derivation process, and more thorough discussion of practical implementation challenges. While the experimental results are promising, the lack of theoretical justification and practical feasibility concerns significantly weaken the paper's contribution.

Questions to authors: How do you justify the cognitive link between eye-movement patterns and sentiment processing? Could you provide more details about the feature extraction pipeline from raw eye-tracking data? How would you address the scalability challenge of requiring eye-tracking hardware for sentiment analysis in real-world applications?
