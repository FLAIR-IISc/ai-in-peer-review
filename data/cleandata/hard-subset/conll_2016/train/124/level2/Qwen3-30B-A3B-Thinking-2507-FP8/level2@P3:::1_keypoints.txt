- Summary: Proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-movement patterns, claiming improved performance on sarcasm/irony.

- Contribution 1: Introduces eye-tracking–derived cognitive features as a novel feature set for sentiment analysis.

- Contribution 2: Reports F-score improvements of 3.7% and 9.3% on two datasets over traditional features.

- Contribution 3: Presents feature-significance analysis showing cognitive features help with complex constructs like irony.

- Strength 1: Novel conceptual contribution—using gaze data in sentiment analysis not previously explored.

- Strength 2: Comprehensive experiments on two datasets with multiple classifiers and statistical testing (McNemar).

- Strength 3: Feature analysis indicates gaze features rank highly among top predictive features.

- Weakness 1: Insufficient theoretical grounding linking specific eye-movement patterns to sentiment-related cognitive processes.

- Weakness 2: Held-out experiment for complex constructs is small (294 examples, 131 complex), limiting robustness of the 6.1% claim.

- Weakness 3: Practical feasibility concerns—privacy, consent, and scalability of collecting eye-tracking data are not adequately addressed.

- Weakness 4: No comparison with modern deep learning baselines (CNNs/RNNs), leaving unclear benefit over state-of-the-art methods.

- Question 1: How do specific gaze features (e.g., regression count) map to cognitive processes for sentiment and sarcasm?

- Question 2: Given only 131 complex examples, how is the 6.1% improvement for complex texts statistically validated and what further experiments are needed?

- Question 3: How would the approach handle privacy, consent, and large-scale data collection with mobile eye-trackers in real-world applications?

- Additional comment 1: Paper needs stronger theoretical justification linking gaze features to sentiment processing and alternative explanations for correlations.

- Additional comment 2: Provide concrete evidence or case studies on feasibility and costs of large-scale mobile eye-tracking, including privacy/consent issues.

- Additional comment 3: Either run comparisons with modern deep learning approaches or discuss how cognitive features would complement them, to better position the work.
