## Review of "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

This paper investigates augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The authors hypothesize that readers’ eye-movement patterns reveal insights into the nuances of sentiment, particularly in complex cases like sarcasm and irony. They demonstrate a performance improvement (up to 9.3% F-score increase) on two datasets by incorporating these cognitive features alongside standard linguistic features.

The primary contribution of this work is the novel application of eye-tracking derived cognitive features to the task of sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies related to sentiment, this paper is the first, to the authors’ knowledge, to integrate these features directly into a sentiment classification pipeline. A secondary contribution lies in the detailed feature engineering of both traditional and cognitive features, including the development of “gaze-saliency graphs” to capture more complex relationships within eye-movement data. Finally, the empirical validation on two datasets, including a held-out set specifically designed to contain complex constructs, provides evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The integration of cognitive features from eye-tracking into sentiment analysis is a genuinely novel approach.
*   **Empirical Results:** The reported performance improvements, particularly the 9.3% increase on Dataset 2, are significant and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.
*   **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors have clearly defined and justified a comprehensive set of features, including the innovative gaze-saliency graphs.
*   **Rigorous Evaluation:** The use of a held-out dataset with complex constructs strengthens the validity of the findings.
*   **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors proactively address the practical challenges of acquiring eye-tracking data and discuss potential solutions with the rise of mobile eye-trackers.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dataset Size:** The datasets used are relatively small, potentially limiting the generalizability of the results.
*   **Eye-tracker Quality:** The use of different quality eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) across datasets introduces a potential source of variability and may affect comparability.
*   **Feature Aggregation:** The averaging of gaze data across participants when constructing the saliency graphs may result in a loss of individual reader-specific information.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is primarily against relatively simple baseline systems. A comparison against state-of-the-art deep learning models would provide a stronger benchmark.
*   **Error Analysis:** While mentioned, the error analysis section lacks specific examples of misclassifications and a deeper dive into the reasons behind them.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the statistical significance of the improvements observed on the held-out dataset, beyond the mention of p < 0.05?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of parameters used in constructing the gaze-saliency graphs?
3.  Have you explored alternative methods for aggregating gaze data across participants, such as clustering or individual model training?
