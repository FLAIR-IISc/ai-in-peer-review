- Proposes augmenting text-based sentiment analysis with eye-tracking–derived cognitive features (fixations, saccades, gaze-saliency graph) and reports statistically significant F-score improvements on two datasets.
- Contribution: Introduces and validates a novel set of cognitive features, including basic gaze metrics and a graph-based gaze-saliency representation, integrated into a standard ML pipeline.
- Contribution: Shows targeted gains are larger for complex constructs (irony/sarcasm: 6.1% relative F-score) than for simpler texts (2.1%), supporting the method’s specific utility.
- Strength: Highly novel and well-motivated by psycholinguistic theory; gaze-saliency graph is a creative way to capture complex reading behavior.
- Strength: Rigorous experimental design with two datasets, multiple classifiers, ablation studies, and statistical testing; held-out experiments strengthen claims.
- Strength: Clear writing and reproducible setup using public datasets and standard toolkits.
- Strength: Thoughtful discussion of limitations and feasibility, acknowledging practical hurdles and suggesting future directions.
- Weakness: Requires eye-tracking data at test time, limiting practical deployment; needs strategies (e.g., predicted gaze) to generalize to new text.
- Weakness: Absolute performance gains from gaze features on full datasets are modest (0.6% and 1.5%), with sarcasm features providing much of the improvement.
- Weakness: Aggregating gaze metrics by mean across readers may lose informative variance; distributional statistics could be more informative.
- Question: Have you considered multi-task or transfer learning to predict gaze features from text to create deployable “pseudo-cognitive” features?
- Question: Did you explore using variance or higher-order moments of gaze feature distributions across annotators as additional signals?
