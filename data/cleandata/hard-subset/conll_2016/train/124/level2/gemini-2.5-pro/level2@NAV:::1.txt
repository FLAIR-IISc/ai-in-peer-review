**Summary and Contributions**
This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional linguistic feature sets with cognitive features derived from readers' eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, such as fixations and regressions, capture cognitive processes related to understanding linguistic nuances like sarcasm and irony, which are challenging for standard models. The authors demonstrate that incorporating these gaze-based features significantly improves F-scores for polarity classification on two datasets, particularly for text with complex semantic constructs.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **Novel Feature Modality for Sentiment Analysis:** The core contribution is the introduction and validation of cognitive features from eye-tracking as a new signal for sentiment classification. This provides a novel, empirically-backed link between psycholinguistic data and a classic NLP task.
2.  **Targeted Analysis of Complex Text:** The paper provides strong evidence that these cognitive features are particularly effective for correctly classifying difficult, non-literal texts (e.g., sarcastic/ironic statements), a known weakness of many sentiment analysis systems.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Novelty and Interesting Research Direction:** The paper's main strength is its originality. Fusing cognitive signals from human readers with textual features is an exciting and underexplored area. It opens up a promising new avenue for building more nuanced and human-aware language understanding systems.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive experimental setup. The authors test their method on two datasets with multiple classifiers, perform statistical significance testing (McNemar test), and conduct a feature importance analysis. The experiment on the held-out dataset, which explicitly isolates performance on complex versus simple text, provides compelling evidence for their central hypothesis.
3.  **Clarity and Acknowledgment of Limitations:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. Crucially, the authors proactively address the most significant limitation—the practical feasibility of their approach—in a dedicated section, discussing potential future scenarios. This demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the work's real-world applicability.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Practical Applicability:** The most critical weakness is the reliance on eye-tracking data during the inference phase. This makes the system impractical for real-world, large-scale sentiment analysis, as gaze data is not available for arbitrary online text. The work is better framed as a powerful analysis tool rather than a deployable system in its current form.
2.  **Lack of State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The study compares against traditional feature-based sentiment classifiers. However, it omits comparisons to modern deep learning architectures (e.g., Transformers), which are the current state-of-the-art. While the authors acknowledge this, including such a baseline would be necessary to fully contextualize the performance gains.
3.  **Information Loss in Feature Aggregation:** The cognitive features are derived by averaging eye-tracking data across multiple annotators. This approach may obscure valuable information contained in the variance of reader behaviors and individual reading patterns.

**Questions to Authors**
Have you considered a multi-task or privileged information learning framework where the goal is to predict sentiment from text alone, but the model uses gaze data as an auxiliary training signal? This could leverage the insights from cognitive data to build a more robust text-only model, thereby overcoming the practical limitation of needing eye-tracking data at inference time.
