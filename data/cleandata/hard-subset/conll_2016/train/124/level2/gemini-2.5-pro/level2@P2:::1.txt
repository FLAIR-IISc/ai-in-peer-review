**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The central hypothesis is that cognitive signals, as captured by eye-tracking, can help models better understand nuanced and complex linguistic phenomena such as sarcasm and irony, which are challenging for standard sentiment analysis systems. The authors define a set of "basic" (e.g., fixation duration, regression count) and "complex" (graph-based) gaze features. Through experiments on two datasets, they demonstrate that a feature set including these cognitive features improves classification F-score over and above strong textual baselines.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The novel proposal to use cognitive features from eye-tracking to improve a core NLP task like sentiment analysis. This introduces a new, promising research direction that integrates methods and insights from psycholinguistics into computational models of sentiment.
2.  Strong empirical evidence showing that these cognitive features are particularly beneficial for classifying complex texts containing sarcasm and irony. The authors support this claim with a targeted experiment on a held-out dataset, demonstrating a substantially larger performance gain on ironic texts compared to simpler ones.

**Strengths**

1.  **High degree of novelty and creativity.** The primary strength of this paper is its originality. The idea of using eye-tracking data to capture the cognitive processing of sentiment is a creative leap that pushes the boundaries of traditional feature engineering for sentiment analysis. The approach is well-motivated by the known limitations of text-only models in handling subtleties like sarcasm, and the link to cognitive effort is intuitive and well-grounded in psycholinguistic research.
2.  **Thorough and rigorous experimental validation.** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments to validate their claims. They use two distinct datasets, test three different classifiers, and perform detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each feature set. The inclusion of statistical significance testing (McNemar test) and a feature importance analysis lends credibility to their results. The experiment on the held-out dataset to specifically measure the impact on ironic vs. non-ironic text is particularly compelling and provides strong support for the central hypothesis.
3.  **Thoughtful feature engineering.** The paper presents a well-designed suite of cognitive features, moving from simple, interpretable metrics (like regression count and fixation duration) to more sophisticated graph-based representations of gaze patterns. Each feature is clearly described and motivated by cognitive science literature or intuitive reasoning about the reading process (e.g., linking long-range regressions to the detection of incongruity in sarcastic sentences).
4.  **Candid discussion of practical limitations.** The authors proactively address the most obvious criticism of their work—its dependence on specialized eye-tracking hardware—in a dedicated "Feasibility" section. While their outlook on the future availability of mobile eye-trackers is optimistic, this transparent discussion of the method's practicality and potential application scenarios is commendable and shows a mature understanding of their work's position in the field.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant questions of practicality and scalability.** The method's reliance on eye-tracking data for prediction makes it infeasible for any practical, real-world application at present. The system requires that for any new sentence to be classified, it must first be read by a human subject connected to an eye-tracker. The discussion in Section 7 is speculative and does not offer a path to deployment with current technology. The paper would be much stronger if it explored ways to remove this dependency at test time, for example, by training a model to predict cognitive features from text alone.
2.  **Modest overall performance gains.** While the improvements are statistically significant, the absolute gains from adding the novel cognitive features are quite small on the full datasets. For the best-performing SVM model, the F-score increases by only 0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2 over a strong baseline that already includes sarcasm-specific textual features. This raises concerns about the cost-benefit tradeoff, as collecting eye-tracking data is extremely expensive and time-consuming. The paper should address whether such a marginal gain justifies the immense data collection overhead.
3.  **Potential for confounding variables.** Eye-movement is affected by numerous linguistic factors beyond sentiment, including lexical properties (word frequency, predictability), syntactic complexity, and general reading difficulty. While the authors include features for readability and sentence length, it is not clear that their model has fully disentangled the cognitive signal for *sentiment processing* from the signal for *general linguistic complexity*. Sarcastic sentences are often longer or more syntactically complex than direct statements; the gaze features might simply be capturing this general complexity rather than a phenomenon unique to sentiment comprehension.
4.  **Lack of comparison with contemporary state-of-the-art models.** The authors explicitly state that they do not compare against deep learning approaches, which were already state-of-the-art for sentiment analysis in 2016. While their feature-based approach is the focus, the absence of such a comparison makes it difficult to assess the true impact of their contribution. A more powerful text encoder might capture some of the same nuances that the gaze features are picking up, potentially rendering the expensive cognitive data less impactful.

**Questions to Authors**

*   The primary barrier to adoption is the requirement of an eye-tracker at inference time. Have you considered a multi-stage approach where you first learn to *predict* the cognitive features from the text itself, and then use these predicted features in the final sentiment classifier? This would make the system fully deployable.
*   Given the high cost of collecting gaze data, how would you argue for the practical significance of the observed F-score improvements (0.6% and 1.5% on the full datasets) when adding gaze features on top of an already strong text-based baseline?
*   Your feature analysis shows that both textual features (e.g., lexical polarity) and cognitive features (e.g., regression count) are important. Can you elaborate on the specific cognitive phenomena you believe are being captured by your most effective gaze features? For instance, does the `RSF` feature (regressions from second to first half) specifically capture the detection of incongruity in sarcasm, as hypothesized?
