**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method to improve sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from human eye-movement data. The central hypothesis is that reading patterns, such as fixations and saccades, provide signals that can help models better understand complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, which are often challenging for systems relying solely on textual information. The work introduces a set of "basic" gaze features (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts) and more complex features derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph" representation. The proposed enhanced feature set is evaluated on two publicly available datasets containing sentiment labels and associated eye-tracking data, showing statistically significant improvements in F-score over baselines using only textual features.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The introduction and empirical validation of a novel set of cognitive features for sentiment analysis. This includes both well-motivated basic gaze metrics and a new graph-based method for extracting more complex relational gaze features, demonstrating that signals from human cognitive processing can be successfully integrated into a standard machine learning pipeline for a core NLP task.
2.  A targeted experimental analysis on a held-out dataset that provides strong evidence for the paper's core claim. The results show that the performance gain from cognitive features is substantially larger for texts containing complex constructs like irony (a 6.1% relative F-score improvement) than for simpler texts (2.1%), effectively demonstrating the specific utility of this approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive data from eye-tracking to inform a sentiment analysis model is highly novel. The approach is well-motivated by psycholinguistic research, with clear connections drawn between specific gaze behaviors (like regressions) and the cognitive load associated with processing complex or incongruous language. The proposed "gaze-saliency graph" is an interesting and creative method for capturing more complex reading patterns.

2.  **Rigorous Experimental Design:** The evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The use of two different datasets, multiple classifiers (SVM, Naïve Bayes, MLP), and statistical significance testing lends confidence to the results. The ablation study presented in Table 2 clearly breaks down the contribution of each feature set (sentiment, sarcasm, and gaze). Most importantly, the experiment on the held-out dataset (Table 4) provides compelling evidence that the gaze features are not just capturing general reading difficulty but are particularly effective at resolving the very linguistic challenges (irony/sarcasm) that motivated the work.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The features are described in sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is transparent. By using publicly available datasets and standard toolkits (Weka, LibSVM), the work facilitates reproducibility and provides a strong baseline for future research in this emerging area.

4.  **Thoughtful Discussion of Limitations:** The paper proactively addresses the most significant limitation of the approach—its reliance on specialized eye-tracking hardware and data—in a dedicated "Feasibility" section. This discussion, while speculative, demonstrates foresight and adds credibility by acknowledging the practical hurdles to widespread adoption and suggesting potential future pathways.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Practical Applicability:** The primary weakness is the requirement of eye-tracking data for each text instance at prediction time. This constraint severely limits the method's applicability to general, real-world scenarios where such data is unavailable. The system, as presented, is a powerful analysis tool for pre-collected datasets but not a deployable sentiment classifier for new, unseen text. The paper could be strengthened by discussing potential avenues to overcome this, such as a two-stage model where gaze features are first predicted from text and then used for the downstream sentiment task.

2.  **Modest Overall Performance Gain from Gaze Features:** While the results are statistically significant, the absolute F-score improvement from adding only the gaze features (`Gz`) to the strong `Sn+Sr` baseline is modest on the full datasets (0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2). The majority of the gain over the simple sentiment feature baseline comes from the inclusion of the sarcasm features (`Sr`). While the targeted experiment in Table 4 is a strong counterpoint, the paper's main results table could give the impression that the cognitive features have a smaller impact than they actually do on the specific phenomena they are designed to target. A more nuanced framing of these results in the main discussion could be beneficial.

3.  **Aggregation of Cognitive Features:** The cognitive features are derived by averaging the gaze metrics across all participants for a given sentence. This approach treats the readers as a monolithic group and may discard useful information. For instance, high variance in gaze patterns across different readers for the same sentence could itself be a powerful feature indicating semantic ambiguity or controversial content. Exploring the use of distributional statistics (e.g., variance, skew) of the gaze metrics across readers, rather than just the mean, could potentially yield a stronger signal.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given the practical limitation that eye-tracking data is required at test time, has consideration been given to a multi-task or transfer learning approach? For example, could a model be trained to predict gaze features directly from text, thereby creating "pseudo-cognitive" features that could make the sentiment analysis system applicable to any text, not just text with existing gaze data?

2.  The current feature engineering approach averages gaze metrics across all annotators for a given sentence. Could the variance or other moments of the distribution of these features across annotators provide an additional useful signal? For example, a sentence that elicits highly variable regression counts among readers might be inherently more ambiguous or sarcastic than one with uniform reading patterns. Was this type of feature explored?
