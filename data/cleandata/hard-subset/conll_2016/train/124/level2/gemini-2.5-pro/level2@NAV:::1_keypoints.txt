- Summary: Proposes augmenting linguistic features with eye-tracking–derived cognitive features (fixations, regressions) to better capture cognitive cues for sentiment, improving polarity classification especially on complex texts.  
- Contribution 1: Introduces and validates eye-tracking cognitive features as a novel modality for sentiment classification.  
- Contribution 2: Demonstrates these cognitive features particularly help classify complex, non-literal texts (e.g., sarcasm, irony).  
- Strength 1: High novelty—fusing human cognitive signals with text is an underexplored, promising research direction.  
- Strength 2: Thorough evaluation—tests on two datasets, multiple classifiers, statistical significance testing, feature importance analysis, and held-out complex-vs-simple experiments.  
- Strength 3: Clear presentation and explicit discussion of practical limitations and future scenarios.  
- Weakness 1: Limited practical applicability because the approach requires eye-tracking data at inference, making large-scale deployment infeasible.  
- Weakness 2: Lacks comparison to modern deep learning/Transformer baselines, leaving contextual performance unclear.  
- Weakness 3: Aggregating gaze features by averaging across annotators may discard useful variance and individual reading behaviors.  
- Question/Suggestion: Consider using gaze as auxiliary or privileged information (multi-task or PI learning) to train text-only models that retain cognitive signal benefits without needing gaze at inference.
