- Summary: Proposes augmenting text features with eye-tracking–derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis, showing improved F-score on two datasets and larger gains on ironic/sarcastic texts.

- Contribution 1: Novel idea to use eye-tracking cognitive signals to enhance NLP sentiment models, linking psycholinguistics to computational methods.

- Contribution 2: Empirical evidence that cognitive features especially help classify complex texts containing sarcasm and irony.

- Strength 1: Highly novel and creative integration of eye-tracking data into sentiment analysis, well-motivated by psycholinguistic theory.

- Strength 2: Thorough experimental validation using two datasets, three classifiers, ablations, significance tests, and targeted ironic-text evaluation.

- Strength 3: Thoughtful feature engineering from simple (fixation, regressions) to complex graph-based gaze features with cognitive motivation.

- Strength 4: Transparent discussion of practical limitations and feasibility concerns regarding eye-tracking hardware.

- Weakness 1: Practicality and scalability are unclear because the method requires human eye-tracking at inference time, with no concrete deployment path.

- Weakness 2: Absolute performance gains are modest (e.g., +0.6% and +1.5% F-score), raising cost–benefit concerns given expensive data collection.

- Weakness 3: Risk of confounding variables—gaze signals may reflect general linguistic complexity (length, readability) rather than sentiment-specific processing.

- Weakness 4: No comparison with contemporary deep-learning state-of-the-art, leaving unclear whether gaze features add value over powerful text encoders.

- Question 1: Have you considered predicting cognitive features from text (multi-stage approach) to avoid requiring eye-trackers at test time?

- Question 2: How do you justify the practical significance of small F-score improvements given the high cost of collecting gaze data?

- Question 3: Can you clarify which cognitive phenomena specific gaze features (e.g., RSF) capture and whether they directly reflect incongruity detection in sarcasm?
