- Proposes augmenting text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking to capture nuances like sarcasm and irony.  
- Engineers a broad set of gaze features from basic fixation/saccade stats to a novel "gaze-saliency graph."  
- Demonstrates statistically significant F-score improvements on two datasets, with larger gains on complex (ironic/sarcastic) texts.  
- Contribution: novel integration of eye-tracking cognitive signals into NLP for sentiment analysis.  
- Contribution: comprehensive, well-motivated gaze feature engineering including the gaze-saliency graph.  
- Contribution: targeted empirical validation showing substantially larger performance gains on ironic/sarcastic texts.  
- Strength: high novelty and potential impact at the intersection of NLP and cognitive science.  
- Strength: thorough evaluation using two datasets, multiple classifiers, ablation studies, and statistical tests.  
- Strength: feature design grounded in psycholinguistic theory (e.g., regressions → re-analysis, long saccades → surprisal).  
- Strength: held-out analysis shows a notable 6.1% F-score improvement on complex/ironic texts.  
- Weakness: limited practicality and generalizability because inference requires eye-tracking data, confining use to lab settings.  
- Weakness: modest overall absolute gains (0.6% and 1.5% F-score), raising cost–benefit concerns given data collection effort.  
- Weakness: averaging gaze features across readers may hide individual variation; unclear performance with single-reader data.  
- Weakness: no comparison to modern deep learning baselines (e.g., BERT), leaving unclear whether gaze adds value beyond text-only SOTA.  
- Question: How variable are gaze features across readers, and how does performance change when using a single reader instead of averaged data?  
- Question: Do results imply the approach is mainly useful as a sarcasm/irony detection component rather than for general sentiment analysis?  
- Question: Which gaze-saliency graph features were most informative, and did they outperform basic gaze features like regressions and fixation durations?
