### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that readers' eye-movement patterns can capture subtle linguistic nuances, such as sarcasm and irony, that are challenging for models relying solely on textual information. The authors engineer a comprehensive set of gaze features, ranging from basic fixation and saccade statistics to more complex features derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph". They test their enhanced feature set on two publicly available datasets using several classifiers, demonstrating that the inclusion of cognitive features leads to statistically significant improvements in F-score, particularly for texts containing complex constructs.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **Novel Integration of Cognitive Signals:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that cognitive signals from eye-tracking can be successfully used as features to improve a core NLP task. This presents a promising and novel direction for sentiment analysis, bridging psycholinguistics with computational modeling.
2.  **Comprehensive Gaze Feature Engineering:** The paper introduces a well-motivated and diverse set of cognitive features. The proposed "gaze-saliency graph" is an interesting method for capturing more complex interactions in reading patterns beyond simple aggregate statistics.
3.  **Targeted Empirical Validation:** The authors provide strong evidence for their hypothesis through a held-out experiment showing that the performance gains from cognitive features are substantially larger for ironic and sarcastic texts compared to simpler, non-ironic texts.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea of leveraging eye-tracking data for sentiment analysis is highly innovative. It moves beyond traditional textual analysis and opens up a new modality for understanding and modeling how humans process complex language. The work is well-positioned at the intersection of NLP and cognitive science, and it could inspire a new line of research into using physiological signals for language understanding tasks.

2.  **Thorough and Methodical Evaluation:** The experimental design is sound. The authors validate their approach on two different datasets (recorded with different quality eye-trackers), use multiple standard classifiers, and perform a systematic ablation study (Sn -> Sn+Sr -> Sn+Sr+Gz) that clearly isolates the contribution of each feature set. The inclusion of statistical significance testing and feature importance analysis strengthens the reliability of the conclusions.

3.  **Strong Motivation for Feature Design:** The cognitive features are not arbitrary; they are grounded in established psycholinguistic research. For instance, the authors link regressions to cognitive re-analysis and long-distance saccades to surprisal when encountering incongruity, which is characteristic of sarcasm. This thoughtful feature engineering adds credibility to the approach.

4.  **Targeted Analysis of Complex Phenomena:** The experiment on the held-out dataset (Section 5.1) is a key strength. By separating ironic and non-ironic texts, the authors provide compelling evidence that their method's primary benefit lies in tackling the exact linguistic challenges they set out to address. The 6.1% F-score improvement on complex texts is a significant finding.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Practicality and Generalizability:** The method's primary limitation is its reliance on eye-tracking data for inference on new, unseen text. While the authors discuss future feasibility with mobile eye-trackers, the current approach is confined to a laboratory setting and is not applicable to general sentiment analysis "in the wild." The model is trained and tested on instances that already have associated gaze data, which is not a realistic deployment scenario.

2.  **Modest Overall Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the absolute improvement from adding gaze features to the strong text-based baseline (Sn+Sr) is small: 0.6% F-score on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2. This raises questions about the cost-benefit trade-off. The significant effort required to collect and process eye-tracking data may not be justified for such a modest gain in a general sentiment analysis setting, even if the gains are larger on specific subsets like sarcastic text.

3.  **Aggregation of Reader Data:** The paper states that gaze features are averaged across the multiple participants who read each text snippet. This aggregation could obscure important signals by averaging out both noise and meaningful individual variations in reading patterns. It is also unclear how the system would perform if gaze data were available from only a single reader, which would be a more likely scenario in any practical application. An analysis of feature stability and performance with fewer readers would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern Baselines:** The authors explicitly decline to compare their method against deep learning approaches. While their system is feature-based, state-of-the-art NLP performance is now dominated by large pre-trained models (e.g., BERT). A comparison against such a model would provide a more realistic benchmark and help determine whether the nuances captured by gaze data can also be learned from text alone given a powerful enough model and sufficient data. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the true value of the cognitive features relative to the current state of the art.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The features from multiple readers (5-7 per snippet) are averaged. In a potential real-world application, data might only be available from a single user. Have you explored the variance of the gaze features across different readers for the same text? How does the model's performance change if features are derived from a single reader's data instead of an average?

2.  The held-out experiment shows a much larger F-score gain for ironic texts (6.1%) than for non-ironic texts (2.1%). Does this suggest that the primary utility of this approach is as a specialized component for a sarcasm/irony detection system, rather than as a general-purpose sentiment analyzer where the overall gains are more modest?

3.  In your feature significance analysis, which of the novel, complex gaze features derived from the "gaze-saliency graph" proved to be most informative? Was there a clear advantage to these graph-based features over the more straightforward "basic gaze features" like regression counts and fixation durations?
