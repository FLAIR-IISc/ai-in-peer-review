- Paper augments textual sentiment features with eye‑movement (fixations, saccades, regressions, graph-based saliency) and reports F‑score gains up to 3.7% and 9.3%, larger on ironic/sarcastic texts.  
- Main contributions: empirical evidence for gaze features helping polarity detection, a concrete basic+graph gaze feature set with ablations, and a feature‑importance analysis showing gaze features rank highly.  

Strengths
- Novel signal: using eye‑tracking to tackle pragmatic phenomena (irony/thwarting) is well motivated and novel.  
- Psycholinguistic grounding: gaze features are related to prior psycholinguistic findings, justifying choices.  
- Careful feature design and ablations: clear taxonomy (Sn, Sr, Gz, readability) and replicable finding that Sn+Sr > Sn and Sn+Sr+Gz improves further.  
- Challenging data: datasets contain substantial irony/sarcasm and show larger gains on ironic subset.  
- Statistical analysis: chi‑squared feature rankings and some McNemar tests provide partial statistical support.  
- Reproducibility: uses public eye‑tracking corpora and reports key preprocessing choices (e.g., PCA to 500 dims).  

Weaknesses
- Practicality/ecological validity: requires eye‑tracking at test time; unclear how many readers or what tracker rates are needed and no single‑reader/low‑rate evaluation.  
- Participant confounds: same reader pool for all sentences may introduce reader‑specific effects; no leave‑reader‑out validation and normalization details are incomplete.  
- Missing strong baselines: lacks contemporary neural baselines (CNN/RNN) and directly comparable state‑of‑the‑art sarcasm models.  
- Mixed standalone utility: gaze features alone sometimes underperform text (especially Dataset 2 with SVM), implying gaze helps mainly when combined with sarcasm cues and needs analysis.  
- Reporting clarity gaps: unspecified whether F‑scores are macro/micro, no class‑wise results, incomplete feature scaling/normalization description, and some graph‑feature definitions are confusing/redundant.  

Questions for authors
- How many readers and what sampling rate are minimally required for gains; can you report single‑reader and low‑rate (60–120 Hz) tracker results?  
- Can you provide leave‑one‑reader‑out or leave‑some‑readers‑out evaluations to rule out reader identity confounds?  
- How are gaze features normalized (per word/sentence/reader); are features z‑scored and are per‑reader speed differences controlled?  
- Are reported F‑scores macro‑averaged; please provide confidence intervals/significance tests and class‑wise performance for main gains on both datasets.  
- Can you include simple neural baselines (CNN/RNN with pre‑trained embeddings) and a sarcasm‑aware textual model trained under the same cross‑validation?  
- Which specific gaze features within Gz drive the gains (basic vs graph‑based); please provide an ablation within Gz.  
- Do gaze features trained on one dataset transfer to the other (cross‑dataset evaluation across devices like EyeLink vs Tobii)?  

Additional comments
- Clarify construction, weighting, and naming of saliency graphs and include a small schematic with numeric examples.  
- Specify unigram preprocessing (tokenization, vocab thresholds) and confirm PCA was fit only on training folds to avoid leakage.  
- Discuss privacy/consent, storage/anonymization, and potential demographic biases in gaze data.
