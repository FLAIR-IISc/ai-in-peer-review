Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper augments conventional, text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement (fixations, saccades, regressions), arguing that gaze signals capture lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic difficulties that standard features miss. It proposes both basic gaze features and new graph-based “gaze-saliency” features.
- On two small, publicly available eye-tracking datasets (D1 and D2), the combined feature set improves F1 over text-only baselines; the bulk of gains come from adding sarcasm/thwarting features, with additional but smaller gains from gaze. On a held-out subset rich in irony/sarcasm, gaze yields larger improvements, suggesting particular utility for complex constructs.
- Feature significance analysis shows multiple gaze features among the top-ranked predictors.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that gaze-derived features can (modestly) improve sentence-level sentiment classification, with larger benefits on sarcastic/ironic cases.
2) Introduction of gaze-saliency graph features as a structured way to summarize inter-word gaze transitions.
3) An analysis indicating where gaze signals matter (complex constructs), and initial discussion of feasibility.

Strengths
- Novelty and timeliness: Using eye-tracking as input to sentiment classifiers is original (distinct from prior use of gaze for annotation complexity/subjectivity), and the proposed saliency-graph features are a creative addition beyond standard gaze aggregations.
- Evidence across two datasets and devices: Results hold on two publicly available datasets with different eye-trackers (EyeLink-1000 vs. Tobii T120). Despite device differences and noise, gaze provides consistent, if modest, gains over strong text baselines augmented with sarcasm/thwarting features.
- Emphasis on difficult phenomena: The held-out analysis isolating irony/sarcasm is a strong point; the +6.1% absolute F1 improvement specifically on complex cases supports the central hypothesis that gaze helps where textual cues are sparse or misleading.
- Feature significance and statistical testing: The chi-squared ranking showing many gaze features among the top predictors, and McNemar tests (albeit limited) provide evidence that gains are not due to chance.
- Reusable resources and clarity of features: The work uses public datasets and clearly enumerates feature sets, aiding reproducibility in principle.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and task definition: The proposed deployment scenario requires real-time eye-tracking at inference, which is atypical for sentiment analysis and currently impractical for most applications. As framed, the task becomes “sentiment classification with access to a reader’s gaze,” which is a different problem than standard SA. The paper’s feasibility discussion is speculative for 2016 and does not address privacy, consent, and bias introduced by which reader’s gaze is used at test time.
- Modest overall gains; most improvement from text-based sarcasm features: On cross-validation, adding gaze to Sn+Sr yields small boosts (≈0.6% and 1.5% F1). The main improvements come from adding sarcasm/thwarting features (3.1% and 7.8%). Thus, the incremental value of gaze on average is limited; the strong case is largely restricted to complex constructs.
- Experimental design ambiguities and potential confounds:
  - It is unclear whether the “IMPLICIT PCA” phrases and the LP feature were trained exclusively on external data or learned using in-fold data, risking leakage. Similarly, the held-out experiment does not specify the training regime (trained on the remainder and tested on the held-out set, or CV within the held-out set?).
  - The baseline comparison to “existing SA systems” (Table 1) is cross-domain and not directly comparable to the in-domain CV results; its purpose is unclear and could mislead.
  - No hyperparameter tuning or stronger text-only baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN, recursive models) are included; in 2016 such baselines are common at sentence level and might reduce or erase the marginal gains from gaze.
- Limited analysis of what in gaze helps: While feature importance lists are provided, there is no ablation across subsets of gaze features (basic vs. graph-based) nor per-phenomenon error analysis beyond irony/non-irony. It is unclear which specific gaze signals drive the benefits.
- Generalization and robustness: Results are per-dataset CV with per-sentence gaze aggregated across the same annotators who labeled those sentences. How models trained with one cohort/device generalize to another cohort/device, or to new domains, remains untested.

Questions to authors
- Held-out setting: How exactly was the held-out subset evaluated? Was the model trained on the remainder of D1 and tested once on the held-out set, or was cross-validation performed within the held-out subset? Please report training data size, test protocol, and whether any model selection used held-out data.
- Sources of Sr features: For IMPLICIT PCA and LP, which corpora and labels were used to induce patterns/models? Were any target-dataset instances used (even indirectly) to derive these features? Please clarify to rule out leakage.
- Gaze feature aggregation: Do gaze features at test time require multiple readers? How does performance change when using a single reader’s gaze vs. aggregation? Is there high inter-reader variance?
- Ablations: What is the marginal contribution of basic gaze features vs. saliency-graph features? Are the graph-based features still helpful on the lower-quality Tobii data?
- Calibration to reading difficulty: Beyond RED and LEN, did you control for word frequency and surprisal? Could gaze gains be explained by (unmodeled) lexical frequency effects?
- Stronger baselines: Can you compare against a tuned linear SVM with n-grams/char-grams and a simple CNN/LSTM on the same CV splits? Even if data are small, this would contextualize the incremental value of gaze.

Additional comments
- Some tables contain formatting inconsistencies (Table 2 rows/columns for D2 Sn+Gz; Table 1 labels). Please verify and correct.
- Consider reporting per-class precision/recall and confusion matrices for irony vs. non-irony subsets.
- Ethical considerations around eye-tracking at scale deserve more discussion (privacy, consent, demographic bias in gaze behavior).
