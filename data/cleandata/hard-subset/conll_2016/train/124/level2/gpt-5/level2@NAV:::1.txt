Summary and Contributions
The paper augments standard sentiment-analysis features with cognitive signals derived from eye-tracking, aiming to better handle nuanced phenomena such as irony and thwarting. On two public datasets with reader gaze data, the best system (linear SVM) reports F-score gains up to 3.7% and 9.3% over a text-only baseline, with larger relative gains on an irony-heavy held-out subset. Main contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical evidence that aggregated gaze features yield consistent, statistically significant improvements for polarity classification, especially on texts with complex constructs.
2) A gaze-saliency graph representation that produces novel graph-based features beyond simple fixation/saccade aggregates.
3) A careful ablation showing the incremental utility of sarcasm/thwarting features and then gaze features, plus feature-importance analyses.

Strengths
- Focus on hard cases: The datasets are enriched for sarcasm/irony and demonstrably challenging for standard SA systems; the held-out analysis further isolates the benefit on complex texts.
- Methodical ablations and statistics: Clear feature-group ablations (Sn, Sr, Gz) and McNemar tests support the claims; chi-squared ranking highlights the contribution of gaze features.
- Novel feature design: The saliency-graph features are an interesting addition to cognitive-NLP, going beyond commonly used fixation counts/durations.
- Use of public data and transparent feature descriptions: Most ingredients (tools, lexicons, datasets) are accessible, facilitating replication in principle.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference: The method requires gaze at test time. The proposed mobile eye-tracking scenario is speculative; no experiments simulate low-fidelity/mobile signals or demonstrate deployment without per-instance gaze.
- Limited novelty relative to prior cognitive SA: Prior work (e.g., Mishra et al., Joshi et al.) already connected gaze and sentiment/subjectivity; the incremental novelty is mainly the specific feature set and end-to-end SA gains.
- Modest incremental gains from gaze: Most improvement comes from adding sarcasm/thwarting features; the added boost from gaze is 0.6–1.5 F-score on the main datasets.
- Outdated baselines: Excluding contemporary text-only neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN/transformers) weakens the claim that gaze adds value beyond strong textual models.
- Potential confounds: Different eye-trackers across datasets, averaging across readers, and possible fold leakage (same sentence with different readers) are not fully controlled.

Questions to Authors
- At inference, who provides gaze? Can you report results with leave-one-reader-out and per-sentence CV that prevents the same snippet appearing across folds with different readers?
- How robust are results across devices? Any cross-device normalization or subsampling to simulate low-rate trackers?
- Do gains persist against strong modern text-only baselines?
- Can you quantify performance when only a small fraction of test items have gaze, or with imputed/proxy gaze (e.g., reading-time estimates)?
