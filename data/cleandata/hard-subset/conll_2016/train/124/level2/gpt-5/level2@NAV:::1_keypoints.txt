- Paper augments sentiment-analysis features with eye-tracking signals, showing up to 3.7% and 9.3% F-score gains over text-only baselines, larger on an irony-heavy subset.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that aggregated gaze features yield consistent, statistically significant improvements for polarity classification, especially on complex texts.  
- Contribution: proposed gaze-saliency graph representation producing novel graph-based features beyond simple fixation/saccade aggregates.  
- Contribution: careful ablation study showing incremental utility of sarcasm/thwarting features and gaze, plus feature-importance analyses.  
- Strength: focuses on hard cases (sarcasm/irony) and uses a held-out analysis to isolate benefits on complex texts.  
- Strength: methodical ablations and statistical tests (McNemar, chi-squared) support claimed improvements.  
- Strength: novel saliency-graph features extend cognitive-NLP feature design beyond standard fixation counts/durations.  
- Strength: uses public datasets and provides transparent feature descriptions to facilitate replication.  
- Weakness: requires gaze at inference, with proposed mobile eye-tracking deployment speculative and untested.  
- Weakness: limited novelty relative to prior cognitive sentiment/subjectivity work; novelty mainly in specific features and empirical gains.  
- Weakness: modest incremental gains attributable to gaze (only ~0.6–1.5 F-score on main datasets).  
- Weakness: outdated baselines—lacks comparison to strong contemporary neural text-only models (BiLSTM/CNN/transformers).  
- Weakness: potential confounds from different eye-trackers across datasets, averaging across readers, and possible fold leakage (same sentence with different readers).  
- Question: at inference, who provides gaze and how is per-instance gaze obtained or simulated?  
- Question: can authors report leave-one-reader-out and per-sentence CV that prevents the same snippet appearing across folds with different readers?  
- Question: how robust are results across devices; any cross-device normalization or low-rate tracker simulation?  
- Question: do gains persist against strong modern text-only baselines?  
- Question: what is performance when only a small fraction of test items have gaze, or when using imputed/proxy gaze (e.g., reading-time estimates)?
