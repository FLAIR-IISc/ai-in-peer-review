Summary
The paper augments standard textual features for sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye movements. It builds basic and graph-based gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions; saliency graphs) and combines them with sentiment, sarcasm/irony/thwarting, and readability features. On two publicly available eye-tracking datasets (D1, D2), 10-fold cross-validation with linear SVM shows F-score improvements of up to 3.7% and 9.3% over textual features alone; a held-out subset with complex constructs (irony/sarcasm) shows larger relative gains (6.1%) than non-complex text (2.1%). Feature significance analysis (chi-squared) and McNemar tests are reported.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical demonstration that gaze-derived cognitive features improve supervised sentiment classification on short texts, particularly for complex phenomena such as sarcasm and irony.
2) A set of engineered gaze features, including a gaze-saliency graph with weighted-degree statistics, accompanied by an ablation illustrating complementary value over sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features.
3) Evidence that improvements are statistically significant and not solely explained by general reading difficulty (via inclusion of readability and length features).

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains with statistical testing: On two datasets with different eye-tracking hardware (Eyelink-1000 and Tobii T120), adding gaze features to sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features yields consistent improvements (up to +3.7% F on D1 and +9.3% on D2), with McNemar tests indicating significance in the closest comparison on D1 (p=0.0001). The held-out analysis further shows larger gains on ironic/sarcastic text, aligning with the motivation.
- Thoughtful feature design and analysis: The feature set spans lexical, syntactic/proxy, pragmatic (sarcasm/thwarting), and multiple gaze dimensions (basic and graph-based). The chi-squared ranking shows many gaze features among the top contributors (10/20 for D1; 7/20 for D2), supporting their utility beyond readability and length.
- Datasets and evaluation framing: Use of two public datasets with recorded gaze and known presence of complex phenomena strengthens external validity. The paper also benchmarks off-the-shelf SA systems trained on out-of-domain data to motivate that D1/D2 are challenging.
- Error analysis and feasibility discussion: The paper acknowledges limitations (aggregation across readers, device quality, linguistic tool noise) and discusses deployment scenarios and consent issues, indicating care about practical constraints.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference time: The approach requires eye-tracking signals for the target text at test time. While potential availability of mobile eye trackers is discussed, this remains speculative for most SA applications (e.g., batch analysis of reviews without readers present). A clear statement of the intended inference-time scenario and constraints is needed; otherwise the approach is best viewed as augmenting human-in-the-loop settings.
- Limited comparative baselines and fairness: Comparisons to existing SA systems use models trained on different corpora, while the proposed models are trained and evaluated in-domain via cross-validation. Stronger baselines trained on the same folds (e.g., competitive neural models or stronger feature-based SVMs) would clarify the incremental benefit of gaze. The paper explicitly sets aside deep models, but even a fastText/CNN/RNN baseline could be informative in 2016.
- Subject and device generalization: Gaze features are averaged across multiple readers; robustness to new readers is not examined (e.g., leave-one-subject-out or per-subject training/testing). Similarly, cross-device generalization (train on Eyelink, test on Tobii, or vice versa) is not evaluated. Without such tests, it is hard to assess how portable the features are.
- Novelty positioning relative to prior gaze work: Prior studies have used eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks (e.g., Mishra et al., 2014; Joshi et al., 2014; Mishra et al., 2016). The paper claims to be first to use gaze features for SA; it would help to delineate what is new (e.g., supervised polarity prediction with engineered gaze features vs. analysis/prediction of annotation complexity or subjectivity extraction) and cite any closely related classification efforts if they exist.
- Analysis depth and reporting details: Statistical significance is shown for the closest comparison on D1 and for the held-out subset, but not reported for D2 ablations. The definitions of some graph-based features (e.g., F1H/F1S vs. F2H/F2S; “left/source” vs. “right/target”) are hard to parse, and normalization choices (by words vs. characters) vary. More clarity would aid reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Inference scenario: What is the intended deployment mode? Is the model expected to classify sentiment for texts as readers consume them (with gaze available), or to classify texts offline without readers? If the latter, how would gaze be obtained?
- Subject generalization: How do results change under leave-one-subject-out cross-validation, where all gaze features for the test fold come from readers unseen during training? Similarly, what happens if features are computed from a single reader rather than averaged?
- Device/setting robustness: Can a train-on-D1 test-on-D2 (and vice versa) experiment be reported to evaluate cross-device and domain generalization of the gaze features?
- Significance on D2: Are the gains from adding gaze to Sn+Sr on D2 statistically significant under McNemar or bootstrap tests?
- Gaze vs. textual proxies: To what extent do gaze features add information beyond textual proxies of processing difficulty (e.g., LM surprisal, syntactic depth, dependency length, punctuation patterns)? A control replacing gaze with these proxies would strengthen the causal story.
- Feature extraction details: Please clarify the saliency graph weighting schemes (F1H/F2H, P(S)H/PSD(H), RS(H/D) etc.), whether degrees are normalized by sentence length, and whether regression amplitude is measured in words or characters consistently across datasets.

Suggestions for improvement (essential)
- Clarify and evaluate the intended inference-time use case; include subject- and device-generalization experiments.
- Add stronger in-domain baselines trained on the same folds (e.g., fastText/CNN/RNN) to contextualize gains.

Suggestions (nice-to-have)
- Compare gaze to textual complexity/surprisal proxies as a control.
- Report additional significance tests on D2; include confidence intervals.
- Release feature extraction code and detailed documentation for reproducibility.
