- Paper augments text-based sentiment analysis with reader eye-movement features (fixations, saccades, regressions) and novel graph-based “gaze-saliency” features.
- On two small public eye-tracking datasets (D1, D2) the combined feature set improves F1 over text-only baselines; sarcasm/thwarting features drive most gains, gaze adds smaller improvements.
- On a held-out subset rich in irony/sarcasm, gaze yields larger improvements (notably +6.1% F1), suggesting utility for complex constructs.
- Feature-significance analysis ranks multiple gaze features among the top predictors.
- Contribution: Empirical demonstration that gaze-derived features can modestly improve sentence-level sentiment classification, with larger benefits on sarcastic/ironic cases.
- Contribution: Introduction of gaze-saliency graph features to summarize inter-word gaze transitions.
- Contribution: Analysis indicating where gaze signals matter (complex phenomena) and initial feasibility discussion.
- Strength: Novel and timely idea—using eye-tracking as input to sentiment classifiers—distinct from prior uses of gaze for annotation.
- Strength: Creative saliency-graph features beyond standard gaze aggregations.
- Strength: Evidence is shown across two datasets and different eye-trackers (EyeLink-1000, Tobii T120), with consistent modest gains.
- Strength: Emphasis on difficult phenomena (irony/sarcasm) where gaze gives substantial improvement.
- Strength: Use of feature-importance rankings and statistical tests (chi-squared, McNemar) to support claims.
- Strength: Reusable resources and clear feature enumeration aiding reproducibility.
- Weakness: Practicality concerns—requiring real-time eye-tracking at inference reframes the task and is impractical for many applications.
- Weakness: Ethical/privacy/bias implications of using a reader’s gaze at test time are not addressed.
- Weakness: Overall gains are modest on average; most improvements stem from text-based sarcasm features, limiting incremental value of gaze.
- Weakness: Experimental ambiguities and potential data leakage (unclear training sources for IMPLICIT PCA and LP, unclear held-out protocol).
- Weakness: Baseline comparisons are unclear/cross-domain and stronger text baselines (CNN/RNN, tuned SVM) are missing.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of which specific gaze signals drive benefits—no ablation of basic vs. graph-based gaze features or detailed per-phenomenon error analysis.
- Weakness: Generalization and robustness untested across different reader cohorts, devices, or domains.
- Question: Clarify held-out evaluation protocol—training data size, test procedure, and model selection details.
- Question: Specify sources and training data for IMPLICIT PCA and LP features and whether target-dataset instances were used.
- Question: Do gaze features at test time require multiple readers? Report single-reader vs aggregated performance and inter-reader variance.
- Question: Provide ablation separating basic gaze features from saliency-graph features and test graph features on lower-quality Tobii data.
- Question: Control for lexical effects (word frequency, surprisal) to rule out alternative explanations for gaze gains.
- Question: Compare to stronger/tuned baselines (linear SVM with n-grams/char-grams, CNN/LSTM) on the same CV splits.
- Additional: Fix table formatting inconsistencies and report per-class precision/recall and confusion matrices for irony vs non-irony subsets.
- Additional: Discuss ethical considerations of scaling eye-tracking (privacy, consent, demographic bias).
