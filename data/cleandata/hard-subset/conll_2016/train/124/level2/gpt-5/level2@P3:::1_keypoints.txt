- Summary: The paper augments textual sentiment features with reader eye-movement (gaze) features—including basic and graph-based measures—and reports F-score improvements (up to +3.7% on D1, +9.3% on D2; +6.1% on complex held-out vs +2.1% on non-complex) using linear SVM with 10-fold CV.

- Contribution 1: Empirically shows gaze-derived cognitive features improve supervised sentiment classification, especially for sarcasm/irony.

- Contribution 2: Proposes engineered gaze features including a gaze-saliency graph with weighted-degree statistics and provides ablation showing complementarity to sentiment and sarcasm features.

- Contribution 3: Provides evidence that gains are statistically significant and not solely due to readability/length confounds.

- Strength: Consistent empirical gains across two public datasets recorded with different eye-trackers, with significance testing (e.g., McNemar p=0.0001 on a key comparison).

- Strength: Thoughtful multi-dimensional feature design (lexical, syntactic, pragmatic, basic and graph-based gaze) with chi-squared ranking showing many gaze features among top contributors.

- Strength: Use of public datasets with known complex phenomena and benchmarking against off-the-shelf SA systems to motivate dataset difficulty.

- Strength: Honest error analysis and discussion of practical/ethical deployment constraints (aggregation, device quality, consent).

- Weakness: Practicality—requires gaze at inference time, which is speculative for many real-world SA applications and needs a clarified intended deployment scenario.

- Weakness: Baselines—comparisons are uneven (off-the-shelf out-of-domain systems vs in-domain trained models); stronger in-domain baselines (fastText/CNN/RNN) are missing.

- Weakness: Generalization—no leave-one-subject-out or cross-device (Eyelink↔Tobii) experiments to test robustness to new readers or hardware.

- Weakness: Novelty positioning—insufficient distinction from prior eye-tracking work on sentiment/annotation complexity; clearer citation and positioning needed.

- Weakness: Analysis/reporting gaps—some significance tests (e.g., D2 ablations) not reported; some graph-feature definitions and normalization choices are unclear, hindering reproducibility.

- Question: What is the intended inference scenario—real-time with live gaze or offline without readers—and how would gaze be obtained in the latter case?

- Question: How do results change under leave-one-subject-out CV and when using single-reader vs averaged gaze features?

- Question: Can you report cross-device/domain experiments (train on D1 test on D2 and vice versa) to assess portability?

- Question: Are the gains on D2 statistically significant (McNemar or bootstrap)?

- Question: How much do gaze features add beyond textual processing proxies (surprisal, syntactic depth, dependency length, punctuation)?

- Question: Please clarify saliency-graph weighting schemes, normalization by sentence length, and regression amplitude units (words vs characters).

- Essential suggestion: Clarify intended inference-time use case and run subject- and device-generalization experiments.

- Essential suggestion: Add stronger in-domain baselines (e.g., fastText/CNN/RNN) trained on the same folds.

- Nice-to-have: Compare gaze features to textual complexity/surprisal proxies as a control.

- Nice-to-have: Report additional significance tests and confidence intervals for D2 results.

- Nice-to-have: Release feature extraction code and documentation for reproducibility.
