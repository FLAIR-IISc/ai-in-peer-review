Summary and contributions
This paper augments standard textual features for sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns (fixations, saccades, regressions, and graph-based “gaze-saliency” features). Using two publicly available eye-tracking datasets of short opinionated texts rich in irony/sarcasm, the authors report F-score gains up to 3.7% and 9.3% over textual baselines, with larger relative gains on ironic/sarcastic instances. Main contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived cognitive features can improve sentiment polarity detection, especially for linguistically complex (ironic/sarcastic) texts.
2) A concrete feature set of basic and graph-based gaze features and an ablation showing their contribution alongside sarcasm/thwarting textual features.
3) A feature-importance analysis indicating several gaze features rank highly.

Strengths
- Novel signal and motivation: Bringing eye-tracking into sentiment analysis is a fresh and well-motivated angle for handling pragmatic phenomena (irony, thwarting) where text-only systems often struggle. The paper relates gaze patterns to psycholinguistic findings, grounding the feature choices.
- Careful feature design and ablations: The feature taxonomy (Sn, Sr, Gz, readability) and ablation results are informative. The finding that Sn+Sr noticeably outperforms Sn, and that adding Gz on top of Sn+Sr further improves performance, is clear and replicable.
- Evidence on challenging data: The datasets contain substantial irony/sarcasm. The held-out analysis showing larger relative gains for the ironic subset (6.1% vs 2.1% for non-ironic) supports the core hypothesis.
- Feature significance and statistics: The chi-squared ranking shows gaze features among the most informative, and the use of McNemar tests (for at least one dataset and the held-out splits) provides some statistical backing.
- Public datasets and implementation transparency: The work uses publicly available eye-tracking corpora and states key preprocessing choices (e.g., PCA to 500 dims for unigrams), which aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and ecological validity: The approach requires eye-tracking at test time. While the paper discusses future availability of mobile eye-trackers, the present evaluation assumes multiple readers’ gaze per sentence and averages over them. This raises questions about deployment: How many readers are needed to obtain stable features? What happens with a single reader or noisy/low-rate trackers? A realistic usage scenario/evaluation is missing.
- Potential confounds and splits: Because all sentences were read by the same pool of participants, per-sentence features averaged across readers may still reflect participant-specific behaviors. A stronger validation would report participant-aware splits (e.g., leave-one-reader-out or leave-readers-out) and sentence-only splits to rule out reader identity confounds. Relatedly, normalization choices (per sentence vs per reader) are not fully detailed.
- Baselines and state of the art: The comparison omits competitive neural baselines (CNN/RNN) that were standard by 2016, including architectures known to help with sarcasm/irony. The external baselines trained on Pang & Lee/Sentiment140 and tested cross-domain are not directly comparable to the cross-validated in-domain results reported for the proposed features.
- Mixed results for gaze features alone: In Table 2, “Gz” or “Sn+Gz” sometimes underperform Sn (especially on Dataset 2 with SVM). This suggests gaze features are helpful primarily when combined with carefully engineered sarcasm/thwarting cues; the paper should analyze why gaze hurts alone and under what conditions it helps.
- Reporting clarity: Important details are underspecified, including whether F-scores are macro/micro, class-specific performance (given class imbalance), feature scaling/normalization for gaze measures, and complete statistical significance across all key comparisons (only one dataset’s top-two comparison is reported). Some graph-feature definitions and units (e.g., PSDH/PSDS vs RSDH/RSDS) are hard to follow and appear partly redundant.

Questions to authors
1) Test-time setup: How many readers and what sampling rate are minimally needed to obtain gains over Sn or Sn+Sr? Can you report results for a single-reader setting and for low-rate (e.g., 60–120 Hz) trackers only?
2) Participant-aware evaluation: Can you provide leave-one-reader-out (or leave-some-readers-out) results to demonstrate robustness to reader-specific patterns?
3) Normalization: How are gaze features normalized (per word, per sentence length, per reader)? Are features z-scored across sentences? Did you control for per-reader speed or accuracy differences?
4) Metrics and significance: Are F-scores macro-averaged over classes? Please report confidence intervals or significance for the main gains on both datasets, and class-wise performance.
5) Baselines: Can you include a simple neural baseline (e.g., CNN/RNN with pre-trained embeddings) and a modern sarcasm-aware textual model, trained and evaluated with the same cross-validation, to better situate the contribution of gaze?
6) Ablations: Which specific gaze features drive the gains when combined with Sn+Sr? An ablation within Gz (basic vs graph-based) would be helpful.
7) Generalization: Do gaze features trained on one dataset transfer to the other without re-estimating parameters (cross-dataset evaluation)? This would indicate whether the cognitive patterns are consistent across domains/devices (Eyelink vs Tobii).

Additional comments
- Clarify the construction and weighting of saliency graphs and ensure feature names/definitions are consistent. A small schematic with numeric examples would help.
- Please specify preprocessing for unigrams (tokenization, vocab thresholds) and whether PCA was fit only on training folds to avoid leakage.
- Consider a privacy/consent discussion beyond feasibility: storage, anonymization, demographic effects on gaze, and potential biases.
