**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by leveraging cognitive features derived from the eye-movement patterns of readers. The authors argue that traditional sentiment analysis methods often fail to capture the nuances of sentiment expressed in user-generated text, particularly at the lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic levels. To address this, they introduce a set of cognitive features that are derived from the eye-movement patterns of human annotators while they annotate short texts with sentiment labels. These features are combined with traditional sentiment features to improve the performance of sentiment analysis systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Introduction of cognitive features**: The paper introduces a novel set of cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of readers, which are shown to improve the performance of sentiment analysis systems.
2. **Improved sentiment analysis performance**: The authors demonstrate that the combination of traditional sentiment features and cognitive features leads to significant improvements in sentiment analysis performance, particularly for texts with complex constructs like irony and sarcasm.
3. **Feature significance analysis**: The paper presents a feature significance analysis that shows the importance of cognitive features in improving sentiment analysis performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis that leverages cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of readers.
2. **Improved performance**: The authors demonstrate significant improvements in sentiment analysis performance, particularly for texts with complex constructs like irony and sarcasm.
3. **Feature significance analysis**: The paper presents a thorough feature significance analysis that shows the importance of cognitive features in improving sentiment analysis performance.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited datasets**: The paper uses only two publicly available datasets, which may not be representative of all types of user-generated text.
2. **Limited feature set**: The paper focuses on a limited set of cognitive features, which may not capture all the nuances of sentiment expressed in user-generated text.
3. **Need for more robust evaluation**: The paper could benefit from more robust evaluation metrics, such as human evaluation of sentiment analysis performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend this approach to other types of user-generated text, such as longer texts or texts with multiple sentiment labels?
2. Can you provide more details on the eye-tracking technology used to collect the data, and how it was calibrated to ensure accuracy?
3. How do you plan to address the issue of data sparsity, particularly for texts with complex constructs like irony and sarcasm?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper could benefit from more discussion on the implications of this approach for real-world applications, such as sentiment analysis for customer reviews or social media posts.
2. The authors could consider using more advanced machine learning techniques, such as deep learning, to improve sentiment analysis performance.
3. The paper could benefit from more comparison with state-of-the-art sentiment analysis systems to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
