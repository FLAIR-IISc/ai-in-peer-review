- Novel approach: first to augment sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-movement data.  
- Shown improvements: statistically significant F-score gains (3.7% and 9.3%) over traditional-feature systems.  
- Contribution — integration: introduces eye-tracking as a new feature dimension for NLP.  
- Contribution — feature significance: demonstrates gaze-based features are important for complex constructs (e.g., sarcasm) via held-out tests.  
- Contribution — feasibility: discusses practical deployment and mobile eye-tracker use cases.  
- Strength — novelty: cognitive features offer a significant innovation for handling nuanced text.  
- Strength — empirical validation: experiments are well-designed and improvements are consistent across classifiers and datasets.  
- Strength — feature engineering: presents rich, psycholinguistically grounded basic and graph-based gaze features.  
- Strength — practical considerations: addresses real-world applicability and potential applications.  
- Weakness — small datasets: limited-size datasets (994 and 1059 snippets) raise generalizability concerns.  
- Weakness — hardware dependency: reliance on eye-tracking devices limits immediate applicability.  
- Weakness — missing SOTA comparison: excludes transformer/deep-learning baselines, weakening evaluation.  
- Weakness — shallow error analysis: lacks detailed investigation of failure cases and remedies.  
- Question — scalability: how to scale to larger datasets or settings without eye-tracking data?  
- Question — model fusion: have you tried combining gaze features with deep models (e.g., transformers)?  
- Question — overhead: what is the computational cost of gaze-based features, especially for real-time use?  
- Overall: promising and novel work with compelling results but constrained by dataset size and hardware reliance; recommend accept with minor revisions.
